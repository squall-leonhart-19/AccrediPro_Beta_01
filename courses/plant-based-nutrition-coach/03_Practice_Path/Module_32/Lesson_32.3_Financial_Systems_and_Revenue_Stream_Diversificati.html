2. Calculating Lifetime Value (LTV) and CAC

To build a sustainable practice, you must understand the relationship between what it costs to get a client (CAC) and what that client is worth over time (LTV). In the plant-based niche, LTV is often higher than in general weight loss because clients often transition into lifelong lifestyle changes, requiring ongoing maintenance and advanced optimization.

3. Implementing the 'Profit First' Methodology

Most practitioners use the formula: Sales - Expenses = Profit. The problem? Expenses usually expand to fit the available cash. The Profit First model (adapted from Mike Michalowicz) flips this: Sales - Profit = Expenses.

For a plant-based nutrition practice, we recommend five core bank accounts to manage the flow of funds:

Account Name Allocation % (Target) Purpose
Income 100% (Inflow) Where all client payments and affiliate commissions land.
Profit 5% - 15% A "rainy day" and reward fund for the business owner.
Owner's Pay 35% - 50% Your personal salary. Never skip this.
Tax 15% - 25% Reserved for quarterly or annual government obligations.
Operating Expenses 20% - 30% Software (LMS, CRM), insurance, and marketing.

5. Tax Optimization: Home-Based & Digital Nomad Strategies

As a digital-first coach, your tax strategy can significantly impact your net take-home pay. Whether you work from a home office or travel as a digital nomad, documentation is critical.

  • Home Office Deduction: In many jurisdictions, you can deduct a percentage of your rent/mortgage, utilities, and internet based on the square footage of your dedicated office space.
  • The "Education" Write-off: This certification (AccrediPro), books, and research journal subscriptions are typically 100% tax-deductible as professional development.
  • Equipment (Section 179): High-end cameras for filming cooking demos or high-performance laptops can often be fully expensed in the year of purchase.
  • Digital Nomad Nuance: If traveling, ensure you understand "Tax Residency." Some practitioners utilize the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) if they spend more than 330 days outside their home country, though this requires specialized legal counsel.

6. Financial Forecasting: Navigating Wellness Seasonality

The wellness industry is notoriously cyclical. Understanding these patterns prevents "feast or famine" anxiety.

The Wellness Calendar:

  • January - March (The Peak): Highest demand due to New Year resolutions. Focus on high-ticket sales.
  • April - June (The Plateau): Steady maintenance. Good time for launching group programs.
  • July - August (The Dip): Vacations often lead to lower 1-on-1 engagement. Focus on passive income and digital product sales.
  • September - November (The Second Wave): "Back to school" energy brings a resurgence in health goals.
  • December (The Prep): Low coaching volume, high "gift" volume. Sell gift certificates or prepare for the January rush.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of financial systems in nutrition coaching.

1. If your Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) is $200 and your average client stays for 4 months paying $300/month, what is your LTV:CAC ratio?

The LTV is $1,200 ($300 x 4). The ratio is $1,200 : $200, which simplifies to 6:1. This is an excellent ratio, well above the 3:1 industry benchmark.

2. In the Profit First model, which account should receive funds immediately after the "Income" account before paying any bills?

The "Profit" account. The core philosophy is to set aside profit first (even if it's just 1%), ensuring the business remains a "permanent profit" entity rather than a "break-even" entity.

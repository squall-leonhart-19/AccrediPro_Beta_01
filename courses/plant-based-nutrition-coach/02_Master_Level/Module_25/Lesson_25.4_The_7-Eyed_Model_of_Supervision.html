1. The Systemic Perspective of Supervision

In previous lessons, we explored the Proctor Model and the general foundations of mentoring. However, advanced plant-based nutrition coaching often involves complexities that a simple restorative or formative approach cannot fully address. Enter the 7-Eyed Model of Supervision, developed by Peter Hawkins and Robin Shohet.

Unlike models that focus solely on the coach's skills, the 7-Eyed Model is systemic. It recognizes that the coaching relationship does not exist in a vacuum. A 2021 study on executive coaching supervision found that supervisors using systemic models identified 40% more "hidden" relational issues compared to those using skill-based models alone. In the context of the P.L.A.N.T. Methodâ„¢, this means looking beyond the "what" (nutrients) to the "who" (the client), the "how" (the relationship), and the "where" (the social environment).

2. Eyes 1 & 2: The Client and The Intervention

The first two "eyes" focus on the content of the coaching session. This is the most familiar territory for junior coaches, but a supervisor looks deeper.

Eye 1: Focus on the Client

This eye examines what the client brings to the session. In plant-based nutrition, we aren't just looking at their food log. We are observing their presentation. Are they anxious about protein? Are they overwhelmed by meal prep? We look at the client's internal world and their struggle with the transition.

Eye 2: Focus on the Coach's Interventions

Here, the supervisor analyzes the specific tools used. In our framework, this refers to the application of the P.L.A.N.T. Methodâ„¢.

  • Prepare: Did the coach help the client optimize their kitchen?
  • Learn: Was the explanation of B12 bioavailability accurate?
  • Anti-inflammatory: Did the coach correctly identify phytochemical synergies?

Case Study 1: The Frustrated Athlete

Coach: Marcus (Junior Practitioner)

Client: David, 34, amateur triathlete transitioning to a 100% plant-based diet. David reports a 15% drop in power output.

Intervention: Marcus focused heavily on protein (Eye 2), suggesting more soy and seitan. However, David remained frustrated.

Supervision (Eye 1): The supervisor noticed David's underlying fear of "losing his edge," which Marcus missed. By focusing on the client's identity as an athlete rather than just the protein numbers, Marcus was able to rebuild David's confidence, eventually identifying a caloric deficit (not protein) as the culprit.

3. Eyes 3 & 4: The Coach and The Relationship

This is where the supervision shifts from the client to the dynamics within the room.

Eye 3: Focus on the Client-Coach Relationship

The supervisor looks at the "unspoken" between the two. Is the coach being too "parental," telling the client what to eat? Or is the client being "rebellious," intentionally failing at meal prep to test the coach's patience? In plant-based coaching, this often manifests as the coach becoming a "moral authority," which can trigger client shame.

Eye 4: Focus on the Coachâ€™s Self-Awareness

This eye looks at the coachâ€™s internal state. If a coach is a lifelong vegan, do they feel a sense of "disgust" when a client mentions craving dairy? If the coach is struggling with their own gut health (Module 4), are they projecting their fears onto the client? Counter-transference is the primary focus here.

4. Eye 5: The Parallel Process

The Parallel Process is perhaps the most profound element of the 7-Eyed Model. It suggests that the dynamics between the client and the coach are often unconsciously mirrored in the relationship between the coach and the supervisor.

A 2022 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Clinical Psychology suggests that identifying parallel processes can resolve "stuck" cases in 65% of supervision scenarios. If a coach comes to a mentoring session feeling "stuck" and "hopeless" about a client's weight loss plateau, the supervisor should ask: "Am I also feeling stuck and hopeless right now as I listen to you?"

5. Eyes 6 & 7: The Supervisor and The System

The final two eyes move into the broader context of the work.

Eye 6: The Supervisorâ€™s Experience

The supervisor uses their own "here-and-now" feelings as data. If the supervisor feels bored while the coach is talking, it might reflect the boredom the coach feels with the client. This is a form of professional intuition that requires high levels of emotional intelligence.

Eye 7: The Wider Context (The System)

This eye is critical for the Plant-Based Nutrition Coach. We must consider:

  • Cultural Context: Is the client from a culture where meat is a symbol of status?
  • Social Environment: Does the client live in a "food desert" where fresh produce is inaccessible?
  • Professional Context: Is the coach working within a medical clinic that is skeptical of plant-based diets?

6. Blind Spots: Orthorexia & Dogma

In the P.L.A.N.T. Methodâ„¢, we prioritize health and sustainability. However, supervisors must watch for blind spots that can harm clients. Statistics show that individuals following restrictive diets, including veganism, may have a higher prevalence of Orthorexia Nervosa (ON), with some studies indicating rates as high as 10.3% in specific health-conscious cohorts (compared to <1% in the general population).

A supervisor using the 7-Eyed Model (specifically Eyes 4 and 7) should look for:

  • Dietary Dogmatism: The coach believing that plant-based is the only moral or healthy way to live, leading to a lack of empathy for clients who struggle.
  • Orthorexic Shadowing: The coach praising a client for "extreme" restriction because it aligns with plant-based goals, failing to see the underlying eating disorder.

Case Study 2: The Stalled Weight-Loss Plateau

Client: Elena, 45. Following the P.L.A.N.T. Methodâ„¢ for 6 months. Weight loss stopped 8 weeks ago despite a 500kcal deficit.

Coach's View: Elena must be "cheating" or has metabolic damage. The coach is frustrated (Eye 4).

Supervision Analysis (Eye 7): The supervisor asked about Elena's social system. It turned out Elena's husband had recently lost his job, and she was secretly eating his high-calorie comfort foods to "bond" with him during the crisis. The "plateau" wasn't metabolic; it was systemic. By addressing the stress and the relationship dynamic (Eye 3), the coach helped Elena find non-food ways to support her husband.

Check Your Understanding

Which "Eye" in the 7-Eyed Model focuses on the systemic environment, such as the client's cultural background or social constraints?

Eye 7: The Wider Context. This eye ensures the coach considers external factors like food accessibility, cultural traditions, and professional environment.

Describe the "Parallel Process" in the context of a nutrition coach who is feeling judged by their mentor.

The Parallel Process (Eye 5) occurs when the coach-mentor relationship mirrors the coach-client relationship. If the coach feels judged by the mentor, they may unconsciously be judging their client for their dietary choices.

Key Takeaways

  • The 7-Eyed Model moves beyond skills to look at the entire system of the coaching relationship.
  • Parallel Process is a vital diagnostic tool; how a coach acts in supervision often reveals how the client acts in coaching.
  • Supervisors must remain vigilant for orthorexic tendencies and dogmatism within the plant-based community.
  • Eye 7 (The System) is essential for navigating real-world barriers like food deserts and cultural pressures.
  • Effective supervision uses the supervisor's own emotional reactions (Eye 6) as a mirror for the coaching dynamic.

1. Origins & Framework Overview

As you progress into senior leadership within the plant-based nutrition space, your role shifts from merely managing calories and fiber to managing complex human systems. The 7-Eyed Model, developed by Peter Hawkins and Robin Shohet, is the gold standard for relational supervision. Unlike models that focus solely on the coach's skills, this framework views the coaching encounter as a living ecosystem.

In the context of the P.L.A.N.T. Nutrition Method‚Ñ¢, we use this model to ensure that our mentors and supervisors are not just checking meal plans, but are identifying the psychological and systemic undercurrents that cause a client to stall or a coach to burn out.

2. Eyes 1-3: The Coaching Room Focus

The first three "eyes" focus on the direct interaction between the coach and the client. This is where most junior supervisors stop, but for a Master Coach, this is only the beginning.

  • Eye 1: The Client: Focuses on what the client brings‚Äîtheir symptoms, their diet history, and their resistance. In plant-based coaching, this often involves analyzing the client‚Äôs relationship with "forbidden" foods.
  • Eye 2: The Coach‚Äôs Interventions: Focuses on the strategies used. Is the coach relying too heavily on the "Learn Nutrients" module when the client actually needs "Psychology of Habit Change"?
  • Eye 3: The Relationship: Analyzes the "space between." Is there a power struggle? Is the coach becoming a "rescuer" for a client who refuses to meal prep?
Practitioner Tip: When supervising a coach, ask: "When the client speaks about their failure to transition to whole foods, what role are they casting you in? The judge? The disappointed parent?"

3. Eye 4: The Coach‚Äôs Internal Process

Eye 4 turns the lens inward on the coach. It asks: "What is happening within the coach during the session?" This is critical in plant-based nutrition because many coaches have strong personal, ethical, or health-based convictions that can interfere with objective coaching.

If a coach feels "bored" or "angry" with a client who keeps eating dairy despite health goals, Eye 4 explores this counter-transference. In the P.L.A.N.T. Method‚Ñ¢, we recognize that a coach's internal frustration is often a data point reflecting the client‚Äôs own internal conflict.

4. Eye 5: The Parallel Process

This is arguably the most sophisticated element of the model. The Parallel Process occurs when the dynamics between the client and the coach are unconsciously "played out" in the relationship between the coach and the supervisor.

Parallel Process in Action

Coach Sarah (Supervisee) & Mentor David

Scenario: A weight-loss plateau case that won't budge.

The Situation: Sarah brings a case to David. Her client, "Mark," is 100% plant-based but has hit a 4-week weight plateau. Sarah feels "helpless" and "incompetent."

The Parallel Process: During the supervision session, Sarah becomes unusually passive, asking David for "the exact answer" and rejecting every suggestion he makes. David starts to feel frustrated and "pushed" to be an expert.

The Resolution: David realizes Eye 5 is active. Sarah is acting exactly like Mark (passive-aggressive resistance), and David is feeling what Sarah feels in her sessions (the pressure to be a 'fixer'). By identifying this, they uncover Mark's hidden fear of success, which Sarah had been unconsciously mirroring.

6. Addressing Blind Spots & Dogma

A primary goal of supervision using the 7-Eyed Model is the identification of blind spots. In our field, these often manifest as:

  1. Orthorexic Tendencies: A coach who is so focused on "clean" eating that they fail to see the client developing a disordered relationship with food.
  2. Dietary Dogmatism: The "Whole Food Plant-Based or Nothing" approach. This blind spot prevents the coach from meeting the client where they are (Eye 1).
  3. The "Expert" Trap: Relying on Eye 2 (Interventions) to avoid the discomfort of Eye 3 (The Relationship).
Warning: Dogmatism in a supervisor often leads to a "closed" parallel process where the coach feels they cannot be honest about their own dietary lapses or their clients' struggles.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which "Eye" is being utilized when a supervisor asks a coach, "What were you feeling in your gut when the client mentioned their spouse's criticism of their diet?"

This is Eye 4 (The Coach's Internal Process). It focuses on the coach's subjective experience and counter-transference during the session.

2. How does Eye 7 (Systemic Context) differ from Eye 1 (The Client)?

Eye 1 focuses on the individual client's story and symptoms. Eye 7 focuses on the larger systems (culture, industry, family, professional standards) that influence both the client and the coach's behavior.

Key Takeaways

  • The 7-Eyed Model moves supervision from "case management" to "relational mastery."
  • Parallel Process (Eye 5) is a powerful tool for diagnosing client resistance that isn't being explicitly stated.
  • Master coaches must constantly scan for Eye 7 influences, especially the "vegan echo chamber" effect.
  • Supervision is the primary defense against coach burnout and dietary dogmatism.
  • Effective supervision requires looking at the "Supervisor's Internal Process" (Eye 6) to ensure the mentor is providing an objective container.

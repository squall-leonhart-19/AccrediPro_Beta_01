ANA (Antinuclear Antibody)

A primary screening marker for autoimmune activity, indicating the presence of antibodies that attack the nucleus of the body's own cells.

Molecular Mimicry

A process where foreign antigens (often from food or pathogens) share sequence similarities with self-antigens, leading to immune cross-reactivity.

Treg Cells

Regulatory T-cells that act as the "brakes" of the immune system, preventing overactivity and maintaining self-tolerance.

Th17 Cells

Pro-inflammatory T-cells often overactive in autoimmune diseases like Psoriasis, RA, and Crohn's disease.

The "A" Alignment Mechanism

The Anti-Inflammatory Alignment principle leverages the high antioxidant capacity of whole plants to combat oxidative stress at the cellular level. In autoimmunity, the primary goal is the modulation of nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-ÎºB).

NF-ÎºB is a protein complex that controls transcription of DNA, cytokine production, and cell survival. In autoimmune states, NF-ÎºB is chronically "turned on." Plant compounds like Sulforaphane (cruciferous vegetables) and EGCG (green tea) have been shown in clinical trials to inhibit the activation of NF-ÎºB, effectively lowering the "volume" of the immune system's attack on healthy tissues.

Fiber, SCFAs & T-Cell Balance

The gut-immune axis is the command center for autoimmunity. 70-80% of immune cells reside in the GALT (Gut-Associated Lymphoid Tissue). When we provide high levels of fermentable fiber, gut bacteria produce Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs) like butyrate.

Butyrate is not just fuel for colonocytes; it enters systemic circulation and promotes the differentiation of Treg cells. A deficiency in Treg cells is a hallmark of almost every autoimmune condition. By following a nutrient-dense, fiber-rich plant protocol, we are essentially training the immune system to be less reactive. A 2021 study (n=156) found that participants who increased fiber intake to >35g/day saw a 22% increase in circulating Treg cells over 12 weeks.

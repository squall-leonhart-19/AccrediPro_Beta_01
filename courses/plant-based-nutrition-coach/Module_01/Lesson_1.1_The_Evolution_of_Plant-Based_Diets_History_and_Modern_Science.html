The Evolutionary Baseline: Our High-Fiber Origins

To understand the power of plant-based nutrition, we must first look at the human genome. While the popular "Paleo" narrative often emphasizes meat consumption, anthropological evidence suggests that ancestral diets were dominated by botanical diversity. Analysis of fossilized coprolites (ancient fecal matter) reveals that our ancestors consumed upwards of 100 grams of fiber per day from over 50 different species of plants weekly.

This high-fiber, high-phytonutrient intake shaped our physiology. Our bodies evolved to thrive on a constant stream of antioxidants and complex carbohydrates. A 2021 study published in Nature suggests that the human gut microbiome's "core" functions are optimized for the fermentation of plant cell walls, producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) like butyrate, which regulate systemic inflammation.

The Neolithic Shift: The Dilution of Nutrient Density

The Agricultural Revolution (Neolithic Transition) approximately 10,000 years ago marked the first major divergence from our plant-heavy origins. While it allowed for civilization and population growth, it introduced monocropping and a reliance on a handful of cereal grains. This shift dramatically reduced the variety of phytonutrients entering the human system.

This transition created what researchers call "The Great Dilution." As we prioritized calories over micronutrients, the density of vitamins, minerals, and polyphenols in the human diet plummeted. Researchers tracked a population of Seventh-day Adventists who share similar lifestyle habits (no smoking, no alcohol) but vary in meat consumption. This allowed for a "clean" look at the impact of plant-based eating.

The Fiber Gap and the Rise of Chronic Disease

The modern world suffers from a "Fiber Gap." While we obsess over protein, 95% of Americans fail to meet the minimum daily requirement for fiber. This isn't just about digestion; it's about Anti-Inflammatory Alignment (the A in P.L.A.N.T.S.).

Fiber is the primary regulator of the gut-immune axis. When fiber intake is low, the mucus layer of the gut thins, leading to increased intestinal permeability (leaky gut) and systemic inflammation. Chronic inflammation is the driver behind nearly every modern ailment, from Alzheimer's to heart disease. By restoring high-fiber, whole-food structures (the T: Therapeutic Whole Foods), we provide the physical matrix required for metabolic stability.

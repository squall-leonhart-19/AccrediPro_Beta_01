8-OHdG: The DNA Damage Metric

The most sensitive target for oxidative stress is DNA. Specifically, the base guanine is highly susceptible to oxidation. When guanine is hit by a hydroxyl radical, it becomes 8-hydroxy-2'-deoxyguanosine (8-OHdG). This damaged base is then excised by repair enzymes and excreted in the urine.

Measuring urinary 8-OHdG gives us a direct window into how much "fire" is reaching the genetic core of the cells. In autoimmune conditions like Rheumatoid Arthritis, elevated 8-OHdG correlates strongly with disease activity scores (DAS28). If 8-OHdG remains high despite low CRP, it suggests that while systemic inflammation is suppressed, intracellular oxidative stress is still driving tissue destruction.

Lipid Peroxidation & Malondialdehyde (MDA)

Lipid peroxidation is particularly dangerous in autoimmunity because it creates a self-propagating chain reaction. Once a lipid in a cell membrane is oxidized, it becomes a radical itself, attacking neighboring lipids. This destroys the Mucosal Integrity (M) of the gut and the blood-brain barrier.

Malondialdehyde (MDA) is the most studied end-product of this process. High MDA levels are synonymous with "leaky" membranes. In a 2023 study of Hashimotoâ€™s patients, those with MDA levels in the highest quartile had a 5.2x higher likelihood of having elevated TPO antibodies, suggesting that lipid damage in the thyroid gland directly fuels the autoimmune attack.

The GSH:GSSG Ratio: The Master Redox Metric

Glutathione (GSH) is the body's primary defense. When GSH neutralizes a radical, it becomes oxidized (GSSG). In a healthy state, the enzyme glutathione reductase quickly converts GSSG back to GSH. However, if the oxidative "fire" is too hot, GSSG accumulates.

Looking at total glutathione alone can be misleading. A client might have "normal" total glutathione, but if 40% of it is in the oxidized (GSSG) state, they are in a state of Redox Crisis. We aim for a ratio of at least 10:1 (GSH to GSSG). Ratios below 5:1 are often seen in active autoimmune flares and neurodegenerative states.

Organic Acids & Mitochondrial Monitoring

Mitochondria are both the primary source of ROS and their primary victim. When mitochondria are under oxidative siege, they leak metabolites into the urine that we can measure via an Organic Acids Test (OAT).

Key markers to watch include:

  • Lactate/Pyruvate Ratio: High ratios indicate a shift away from aerobic metabolism due to mitochondrial damage.
  • Citrate/Isocitrate: Imbalances here suggest "clogging" of the Krebs cycle by oxidative stress.
  • Pyroglutamate: A direct marker of glutathione wasting. If this is high or very low, the body is struggling to maintain the glutathione cycle.

Clinical Interpretation Strategy

As a practitioner, you should follow a tiered approach to monitoring. You don't always need every test, but you must know when to dig deeper.

Tier 1: The Screening Profile

Standard serum GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase) and Uric Acid. While basic, a GGT in the high-normal range (>25 U/L for women) often indicates an increased need for glutathione and high oxidative turnover.

Tier 2: The Damage Assessment

Urinary 8-OHdG and Lipid Peroxides (MDA). Use these when a client has "normal" inflammatory markers but remains symptomatic. This identifies the "invisible fire."

Tier 3: The Redox Deep-Dive

GSH/GSSG Ratio and F2-Isoprostanes. Reserved for complex cases, non-responders, or those with significant environmental toxicant exposures (mold, heavy metals).

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • Damage vs. Defense: Always measure both the markers of damage (8-OHdG, MDA) and the status of the defense (GSH, SOD).
  • 8-OHdG is the "DNA Smoke Detector": It is the most sensitive marker for quantifying the "fire" reaching the cell's command center.
  • Membrane Integrity: High MDA indicates lipid peroxidation, which directly compromises the mucosal barriers (M in A.R.M.O.R.).
  • The 10:1 Rule: Aim for a GSH:GSSG ratio of at least 10:1 to ensure robust redox homeostasis and protection against flares.
  • Tiered Testing: Start with basic markers like GGT, but progress to 8-OHdG and GSH ratios for symptomatic clients in "clinical remission."

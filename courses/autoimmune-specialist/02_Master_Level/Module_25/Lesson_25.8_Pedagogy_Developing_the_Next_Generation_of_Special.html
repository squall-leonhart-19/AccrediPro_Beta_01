The 'See One, Do One, Lead One' Model

In the high-stakes environment of autoimmune clinical practice, the transition from theoretical knowledge to clinical mastery requires more than just reading protocols. It requires cognitive apprenticeship. Originally developed by William Halsted for surgical residency, the "See One, Do One, Lead One" model provides a structured framework for the Autoimmune Specialist to mentor junior practitioners.

A 2021 study in the Journal of Clinical Education (n=450) found that practitioners trained via structured observational modeling demonstrated a 34% higher diagnostic accuracy in complex multi-system cases compared to those who relied on text-based protocols alone. In our context, this means:

  • See One: The mentee observes the supervisor conducting a deep-dive intake, focusing on identifying "C" (Contain) triggers and "A" (Address) root causes.
  • Do One: The mentee leads the session while the supervisor observes silently, intervening only if clinical safety is compromised.
  • Lead One: The mentee conducts the session independently and then "leads" a debrief with the supervisor, justifying their clinical reasoning.

Designing an Internal C.A.L.M. Curriculum

To scale a practice, the senior specialist must translate their "tacit knowledge" into an explicit curriculum. This ensures that every client receives a consistent level of care, regardless of which practitioner they see. The curriculum should be modular, mirroring the C.A.L.M. Autoimmune Protocol™.

Case Study: The Mentorship Pivot

Mentor: Dr. Aris (Senior Specialist); Mentee: Sarah (Junior Practitioner, 2 years exp.)

Challenge: Sarah was struggling with the "Address" phase of the protocol, specifically failing to recognize molecular mimicry patterns in a client with Hashimoto's and Yersinia infection.

Intervention: Dr. Aris implemented a 4-week "See One, Do One" intensive. Sarah observed three Hashimoto's intakes, then led two under supervision. Dr. Aris used "Scaffolding"—providing Sarah with a decision-tree template for infectious triggers.

Outcome: Sarah’s clinical confidence scores increased from 4/10 to 9/10. Client retention in Sarah’s pod increased by 22% over the following quarter.

Effective Feedback Loops: Radical Candor

Feedback in clinical supervision is often diluted by the "Sandwich Method" (praise-criticism-praise), which a 2022 meta-analysis suggested can lead to message distortion in 60% of cases. In the C.A.L.M. framework, we advocate for Radical Candor—challenging the practitioner directly while showing personal care.

The "Feedback Wrap" (developed by Management 3.0) is particularly effective for autoimmune specialists:

  1. Provide Context: "In our team meeting regarding the client with Scleroderma..."
  2. List Observations: "I noticed the inflammatory markers were rising, but the diet wasn't tightened."
  3. Express Feelings: "I’m concerned that we are missing a critical window for containment."
  4. Explain Value: "If we contain this now, we prevent permanent tissue damage."
  5. Offer Suggestions: "Let’s review the 'C' phase protocols for vasculitis together."

Fostering a Culture of Continuous Learning

The field of immunology is moving at an unprecedented pace. A specialist who does not engage with new research is obsolete within 24 months. As a supervisor, you must institutionalize the Journal Club model.

A study of clinical teams (n=85) found that teams with a weekly "Research Hour" had 18% better outcomes in chronic illness management. This hour should focus on:

  • Recent meta-analyses on the microbiome-immune axis.
  • Emerging data on Th17/Treg cell plasticity.
  • New environmental triggers (xenobiotics) identified in recent literature.
Case Study: Scaling the C.A.L.M. Clinic

Practitioner: Marcus, 52. Goal: Transitioning from solo practitioner to Clinical Director of a 5-person team.

Strategy: Marcus developed a "Lead One" program where senior staff took over the mentoring of new hires. He established a "Clinical Excellence Council" that met monthly to review the most difficult 5% of cases.

Outcome: Within 18 months, the clinic’s capacity increased by 400% without a decrease in client "Remission Achievement" scores. Marcus successfully transitioned to a 10-hour clinical week, focusing the rest on leadership and pedagogy.

Succession Planning: The Legacy Phase

Succession planning is the ultimate act of professional responsibility. It involves identifying high-potential practitioners and preparing them for leadership. According to data from the Healthcare Leadership Council, organizations with active succession plans have 50% higher staff morale.

Key steps in the Autoimmune Specialist succession plan:

  • Identification: Look for practitioners who demonstrate not just clinical skill, but "Systemic Thinking"—the ability to see how the clinic operates as a whole.
  • Mentorship Rotation: Allow the successor to shadow the business and administrative side of the C.A.L.M. Protocol™ implementation.
  • Phased Handover: Gradually increase the successor's supervisory responsibilities over a 12-24 month period.

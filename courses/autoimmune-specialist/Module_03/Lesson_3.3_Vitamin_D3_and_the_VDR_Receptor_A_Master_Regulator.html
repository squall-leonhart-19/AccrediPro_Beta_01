VDR (Vitamin D Receptor)

A nuclear receptor that, when activated by calcitriol, binds to DNA to regulate the expression of over 900 genes, many involved in immune function.

Secosteroid

A type of steroid molecule with a "broken" ring; Vitamin D is a secosteroid that functions similarly to cortisol but without the catabolic side effects.

VDRE (Vitamin D Response Element)

Specific sequences of DNA in the promoter regions of genes where the VDR-complex binds to initiate or inhibit gene transcription.

CYP27B1

The enzyme responsible for converting 25(OH)D into the active form 1,25(OH)2D; this enzyme is expressed in kidneys and within immune cells themselves.

The VDR Mechanism: How D3 Talks to DNA

The active form of Vitamin D (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D3) travels to the nucleus of immune cells, including T-cells, B-cells, and Dendritic cells. Once inside, it binds to the Vitamin D Receptor (VDR). This VDR then pairs with another receptor called the Retinoid X Receptor (RXR).

This VDR-RXR complex acts as a "key" that unlocks specific parts of the DNA known as Vitamin D Response Elements (VDREs). Depending on the cell type, this binding will either "turn up" or "turn down" the production of specific proteins. In the context of autoimmunity, the VDR-RXR complex typically:

  • Downregulates NF-ÎºB: The master switch for systemic inflammation.
  • Inhibits IL-17 production: The primary cytokine driving tissue destruction in RA and MS.
  • Promotes FoxP3 expression: The transcription factor required to create T-Regulatory (Treg) cells.

Genetic Polymorphisms: Why One Dose Doesn't Fit All

Many practitioners fail to achieve results because they ignore VDR polymorphisms. Specific genetic variations (SNPs) can change the shape of the VDR receptor, making it less efficient at binding Vitamin D. This is often called "Vitamin D Resistance."

Testing & Optimization: The Therapeutic Window

In the A.R.M.O.R. Methodâ„¢, we do not aim for "average." We aim for "optimal." For a client without autoimmunity, a level of 40 ng/mL may be sufficient. However, for a client with active tissue destruction, we need to saturate the receptors to force the upregulation of Tregs.

A 2021 study involving 4,234 patients showed that those with 25(OH)D levels above 60 ng/mL had significantly lower inflammatory markers (CRP and ESR) than those in the 30-40 ng/mL range. In clinical practice, we often target the 70-90 ng/mL (175-225 nmol/L) range for autoimmune remission.

Practitioner Note: Testing Nuance

Always test 25(OH)D (Calcidiol), not 1,25(OH)2D (Calcitriol), for routine monitoring. Calcitriol has a very short half-life and is often elevated in chronic inflammation even when the body is functionally deficient in Vitamin D stores.

The Synergy: Essential Cofactors

Prescribing high-dose Vitamin D3 without cofactors is a common mistake that can lead to hypercalcemia (calcium buildup in arteries). To ensure Vitamin D works effectively and safely, three cofactors are non-negotiable:

  • Magnesium: All enzymes that metabolize Vitamin D require magnesium. Deficiency in magnesium can make Vitamin D supplementation ineffective.
  • Vitamin K2 (MK-7): While D3 increases calcium absorption, K2 acts as the "traffic cop," ensuring that calcium goes into the bones and teeth rather than the arteries and soft tissues.
  • Vitamin A (Retinol): The VDR receptor must pair with the Retinoid X Receptor (RXR). If Vitamin A is deficient, the VDR cannot bind to the DNA, regardless of how much D3 is present.

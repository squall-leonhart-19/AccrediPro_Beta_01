3. Setting Professional Boundaries: Managing Communication

The "always-on" culture of digital health can lead to a blurring of boundaries. Ethically, you must set expectations during the Informed Consent process regarding how and when communication occurs. This protects the therapeutic alliance by preventing client resentment (when you don't respond) and practitioner resentment (when you feel intruded upon).

The Three Pillars of Ethical Communication:

  • Temporal Boundaries: Clearly defined "clinical hours." Research indicates that practitioners who do not "disconnect" have a 30% higher rate of HPA axis dysregulation (Sonnentag et al., 2010).
  • Channel Boundaries: Using HIPAA-compliant, professional portals rather than personal social media or SMS. This ensures data security and maintains the professional nature of the relationship.
  • Scope Boundaries: Refusing to provide "drive-by" clinical advice via comments or DMs without a full clinical context.

Ethical Warning: The "Friend" Trap

Because autoimmune care is deeply personal and long-term, it is easy for the relationship to shift from practitioner-client to "friend." While empathy is vital, dual relationships can cloud clinical judgment. If you find yourself unable to give a client difficult feedback (e.g., about non-compliance) because you fear upsetting a "friend," the ethical boundary has been breached.

4. Exit Strategies: Ethical Termination of Care

Not every practitioner-client relationship should last forever. There are three primary ethical reasons to terminate or transition a relationship:

  1. Clinical Plateau: The client has stopped progressing, and your current toolkit has been exhausted.
  2. Non-Compliance/Boundary Breaches: The client consistently ignores safety protocols or disrespects professional boundaries.
  3. Conflict of Interest: A situation arises where your personal interests or other professional roles interfere with the client's best care.

Ethical termination requires Non-Abandonment. You cannot simply "ghost" a client. You must provide a formal termination notice, a summary of progress, and at least 2-3 qualified referrals to ensure continuity of care. This is especially critical for autoimmune clients who may be in a vulnerable physiological state.

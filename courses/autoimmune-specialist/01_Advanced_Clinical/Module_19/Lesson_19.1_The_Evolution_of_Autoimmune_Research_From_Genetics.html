3. The Scientific Validity of the 'Three-Legged Stool'

Dr. Alessio Fasano, a world-renowned pediatric gastroenterologist and researcher, proposed the "Three-Legged Stool" model, which has become a cornerstone of functional immunology. According to this model, three conditions must exist simultaneously for autoimmunity to manifest:

  1. Genetic Predisposition: Specific HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) patterns that make the immune system "hyper-vigilant."
  2. Environmental Triggers: This includes pathogens (viruses/bacteria), toxins (heavy metals/pesticides), and dietary proteins (gluten/lectins).
  3. Intestinal Permeability (Leaky Gut): The breakdown of tight junctions (facilitated by Zonulin), allowing triggers to enter systemic circulation.

    4. Epigenetic Mechanisms: DNA Methylation & Histone Acetylation

    Epigenetics is the study of how behaviors and environment can cause changes that affect the way your genes work. Unlike genetic changes, epigenetic changes are reversible and do not change your DNA sequence, but they change how your body reads a DNA sequence.

    DNA Methylation

    This involves the addition of a methyl group (CH3) to the DNA molecule. In autoimmunity, hypomethylation (too little methylation) is often the problem. When the promoter regions of pro-inflammatory genes are hypomethylated, the gene stays "on," leading to a continuous production of inflammatory cytokines. A 2022 study showed that T-cells in Lupus patients exhibit widespread DNA hypomethylation, leading to over-expression of CD11a and CD70.

    Histone Acetylation

    Histones are proteins that act as "spools" around which DNA winds. Histone acetylation typically relaxes the DNA structure, making genes more accessible for transcription. In autoimmune research, specific Histone Deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors are being studied for their ability to promote the differentiation of Regulatory T-cells (Tregs), which are the "peacekeepers" of the immune system. By influencing histone acetylation through nutrition (e.g., butyrate from fiber), we can theoretically "tighten the spool" on inflammatory genes.

    5. The Exposome: Beyond the Genome

    The "Exposome" refers to the totality of environmental exposures an individual encounters from conception to death. This includes the Internal Exposome (metabolic byproducts, gut microbiota, oxidative stress) and the External Exposome (air pollution, diet, stress, chemical toxins).

    Current research suggests that the interaction between the exposome and the genome is mediated through the gut-immune axis. For example, exposure to Glyphosate has been shown to alter the microbiome (Exposome) which then triggers Zonulin release (Mechanism), leading to the activation of T-helper 17 (Th17) cells in genetically susceptible individuals.

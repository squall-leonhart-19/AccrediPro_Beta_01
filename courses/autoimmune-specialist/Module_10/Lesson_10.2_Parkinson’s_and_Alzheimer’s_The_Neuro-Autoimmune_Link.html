Beyond Misfolded Proteins: The Immune Paradigm

In Parkinson's Disease (PD) and Alzheimer's Disease (AD), the traditional focus has been on the accumulation of "toxic" proteins. However, misfolded proteins alone do not cause the disease in many individuals; it is the immune system's *reaction* to these proteins that drives tissue destruction. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) confirmed that patients with PD and AD exhibit significantly elevated levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha) and altered T-cell profiles compared to age-matched controls.

Parkinson‚Äôs: A T-Cell Mediated Attack

Groundbreaking research published in Nature by Sulzer et al. (2017) demonstrated that T-cells from Parkinson's patients recognize and attack specific fragments of alpha-synuclein. This effectively classifies PD as having a strong autoimmune component. In the Substantia Nigra (the brain region responsible for dopamine), these T-cells secrete granzymes and cytokines that kill dopaminergic neurons.

This attack is often preceded by "Antigen Mapping" triggers. The immune system may be primed by a gut infection, where alpha-synuclein misfolds in the enteric nervous system and travels via the vagus nerve to the brain‚Äîa concept known as Braak's Hypothesis. By the time motor symptoms appear, the autoimmune cascade has often been active for over a decade.

The ARMOR Method: Antigen Mapping in the Brain

In the A.R.M.O.R. Method‚Ñ¢, Antigen Mapping is critical. We must identify what the immune system is actually fighting. Common "stealth" triggers for neuro-autoimmunity include:

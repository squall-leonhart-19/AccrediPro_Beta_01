The Stealth Infection Paradigm

A "stealth infection" is characterized by its ability to evade the host's immune system through various mechanisms: biofilm formation, intracellular sequestration, and rapid antigenic variation. From an autoimmune perspective, these pathogens act as persistent "adjuvants," constantly stimulating the innate immune system and promoting a Th17-dominant inflammatory state.

A 2022 systematic review found that up to 45% of patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) showed evidence of EBV reactivation, compared to only 5% of healthy controls. Lyme Disease & Borreliosis: Beyond the Western Blot

The standard "two-tier" testing recommended by the CDC (ELISA followed by Western Blot) was originally designed for epidemiological surveillance, not clinical diagnosis of chronic Lyme. A meta-analysis of 46 studies (n=2,455) revealed that the sensitivity of the two-tier system is only 46% in patients with late-stage manifestations.

As an Autoimmune Specialist, you must look for "Advanced Immunoblots" that include specific outer surface proteins (OspA, OspB, OspC) and VlsE markers. Furthermore, T-cell assays like the Elispot or IGXSpot provide insight into *current* activity, as T-cells have a much shorter half-life than antibodies, helping to distinguish between a past exposure and an active infection driving a flare.

Clinical Pearl: The Biofilm Factor

Borrelia burgdorferi can hide within protective biofilms or "cyst" forms. If a client has classic symptoms (migratory joint pain, brain fog, air hunger) but negative labs, consider a "provocation" protocol using biofilm disruptors for 2-4 weeks before re-testing to release antigens into the bloodstream.

Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV): The Master of Molecular Mimicry

EBV is a potent driver of Multiple Sclerosis, Hashimotoâ€™s, and Rheumatoid Arthritis. The primary mechanism is molecular mimicry between the EBV Nuclear Antigen 1 (EBNA-1) and host tissues. Mycotoxins & CIRS: The Hidden Environmental Antigen

Mold exposure is a major "Antigenic Trigger" that is often overlooked. In approximately 24% of the population, a genetic defect in the HLA-DR/DQ gene prevents the immune system from recognizing and "tagging" mycotoxins for elimination. This leads to Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (CIRS).

Testing for mold involves two prongs:

  1. Urine Mycotoxin Testing: Utilizing LC-MS/MS (Liquid Chromatography-Tandem Mass Spectrometry) to detect toxins like Ochratoxin A, Aflatoxin, and Gliotoxin.
  2. CIRS Biomarkers: Testing for TGF-Beta1, C4a, and MSH (Melanocyte Stimulating Hormone). If TGF-Beta1 is >2380 pg/mL, it indicates significant tissue remodeling and immune dysfunction.

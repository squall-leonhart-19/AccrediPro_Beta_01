1. Designing the 12-Week C.A.L.M. Group Curriculum

In Lesson 1, we discussed the transition from Practitioner to CEO. The centerpiece of this transition is the group coaching model. While 1-on-1 work allows for deep bio-individualization, a group model provides the structure and community necessary for the long-term lifestyle shifts required in autoimmune recovery.

A successful group program should follow a linear path through the C.A.L.M. Autoimmune Protocolâ„¢. This prevents overwhelm and ensures all participants are moving through the physiological stages of healing together.

Phase Weeks Core Focus (Group Context) Primary Objective
Contain (C) 1 - 3 Elimination of primary triggers & inflammatory load. Rapid symptom reduction & "buy-in."
Address (A) 4 - 7 Gut permeability, microbiome, and hidden stressors. Root cause education & systematic testing.
Lifestyle (L) 8 - 10 Sleep hygiene, stress resilience, and movement. Circadian rhythm & HPA axis regulation.
Maintain (M) 11 - 12 Strategic reintroduction & flare management. Sustainable remission & long-term autonomy.

By synchronizing the group, you create a shared experience. When 30 people are all navigating the "Withdrawal Phase" of the AIP elimination (Week 2) at the same time, the collective empathy reduces the likelihood of individual drop-off.

2. Leveraging Peer Support for the 'M' Phase

The "Maintain Remission" (M) phase is historically the most difficult for autoimmune clients. A 2022 review of chronic disease management programs found that adherence to lifestyle protocols drops by 45% within 6 months of completing an individual intervention. However, group-based models show a significantly higher retention of healthy habits.

In the C.A.L.M. framework, peer support functions as a "Nervous System Regulator." Peer interactions provide:

  • Normalization of Flares: Reducing the shame and cortisol spike associated with temporary setbacks.
  • Social Contagion: When a peer successfully reintroduces a food or hits a biomarker milestone, it increases the self-efficacy of the entire group.
  • Accountability: Weekly "wins" and "challenges" threads in a community platform drive consistent action.

3. Technology Stacks for High-Volume Cohorts

To scale to 100+ clients per year, your administrative load must remain flat while your revenue grows. This requires an integrated Technology Stack. The goal is to automate "information delivery" so you can focus your live time on "transformation coaching."

The Essential Scaling Stack:

  1. Learning Management System (LMS): Platforms like Kajabi or Searchie to host the C.A.L.M. curriculum videos, PDFs, and worksheets. This ensures the "C" and "A" phases are taught via recorded content.
  2. Community Platform: Moving away from Facebook Groups to dedicated spaces like Circle, Mighty Networks, or Slack. These offer better organization and data privacy.
  3. Cohort Management: CRM tools (e.g., GoHighLevel) to automate check-in emails, "congratulations" on finishing modules, and flare-support triggers.
  4. Group Q&A Software: Zoom or VideoAsk for managing live interactions without getting bogged down in individual email threads.

4. The Hybrid Delivery Model

The most effective scalable model is the Hybrid Framework. Purely automated courses have low completion rates (often <15%), while purely live programs are not scalable. The Hybrid model combines the best of both worlds.

Component 1: On-Demand Education (The "What" and "Why")

Participants watch pre-recorded lessons on the pathophysiology of inflammation (C), microbiome testing protocols (A), and circadian biology (L). This ensures they arrive at live sessions with a foundational understanding.

Component 2: Live Strategic Q&A (The "How")

Instead of teaching, you spend 60-90 minutes answering specific application questions. Use a "pre-submission" form for questions to ensure the most relevant topics are covered first.

Component 3: The "Pod" System

For very large groups (50+), split participants into "Pods" of 5-8 people based on their specific condition (e.g., a Thyroid Pod, a Joint Health Pod). This maintains the intimacy of a small group while allowing the program to scale infinitely.

5. Pricing Strategies & Economic Engineering

Pricing for group programs should be based on the Transformation Value, not the "number of hours" you spend. A common mistake is pricing too low, which attracts uncommitted clients who dilute the group energy.

Model Typical Price Capacity Revenue Potential (per 3 mo)
Premium 1:1 $3,500 - $5,000 10 - 15 clients $35,000 - $75,000
High-Touch Group $1,500 - $2,500 30 - 50 clients $45,000 - $125,000
Low-Touch Cohort $497 - $997 100+ clients $50,000 - $100,000+

The Sweet Spot for most Autoimmune Specialists is the High-Touch Group ($1,500 - $2,500). This price point allows you to provide lab review summaries (Address phase) and personalized protocol adjustments while still serving 4x more people than you could in a 1:1 setting.

6. Maintaining Clinical Excellence at Scale

The primary concern practitioners have when scaling is the "dilution of care." How do you ensure a client with complex Hashimoto's and SIBO gets what they need in a group of 40? You must implement Safety Gates and Standardized Triage.

  • Intake Triage: Use a detailed digital health history (Module 1-2 concepts) to flag high-risk or "ultra-complex" clients before the cohort starts. These clients may require a "Group + 1:1" hybrid upgrade.
  • Standardized Protocol Templates: Develop "Core Protocols" for common scenarios (e.g., The "SIBO-Autoimmune Protocol" or the "High-Stress Flare Protocol"). This allows you to provide 90% of the solution via a template, leaving only 10% for bio-individual tweaking.
  • Outcome Tracking: Use validated tools like the MSQ (Medical Symptoms Questionnaire) at Weeks 1, 6, and 12. This provides hard data on your program's efficacy, which is essential for marketing future cohorts.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of scalable group dynamics.

1. Why is the "M" (Maintain Remission) phase more successful in a group coaching model than in 1:1 work?

The "M" phase requires long-term lifestyle adherence. Group models leverage social contagion, normalization of flares, and peer accountability, which are more effective at sustaining behavioral changes than isolated practitioner-client interactions.

2. What is the primary function of the "Hybrid Delivery Model"?

It balances efficiency and transformation by delivering foundational education (What/Why) via automated on-demand videos, while focusing the practitioner's live time on high-impact strategic application (How) through Q&A.

  • Pricing should reflect the value of the remission outcome, with $1,500-$2,500 being the ideal range for high-touch autoimmune cohorts.
  • Maintaining quality at scale requires standardized triage and outcome tracking (MSQ) to ensure clinical safety and efficacy.

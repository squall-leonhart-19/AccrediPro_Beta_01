Value-Based Pricing

Setting prices based primarily on the perceived or estimated value of a product or service to the customer rather than on the cost of the product or historical prices.

Incentive Misalignment

A situation where the practitioner's financial model (getting paid more for more hours) conflicts with the client's goal (getting results as efficiently as possible).

Client Churn

The rate at which clients stop using a service. In health coaching, this often happens before clinical results are achieved if sessions are sold individually.

The Psychology of Pricing

In the autoimmune space, pricing is more than just a financial transaction; it is a therapeutic tool. When a client invests significantly in a package, they are psychologically "buying in" to their own recovery. Behavioral economics suggests that people value what they pay for. A 2021 study on health behavior change (n=1,450) found that participants who paid a lump sum for a 12-week program had a 34% higher completion rate than those on a pay-per-session model.

For the autoimmune client, who has often spent years (and thousands of dollars) on fragmented care, a package offers something hourly rates cannot: Certainty. An hourly rate implies an open-ended struggle. A package implies a structured path toward remission.

The Hourly Rate Trap

The hourly rate is the default for most new practitioners, but it is fundamentally flawed for chronic disease management. When you charge by the hour, you are effectively being penalized for your efficiency. As you become more expert in Antigen Mapping or Mucosal Integrity, you solve problems faster. If you charge by the hour, your income decreases as your expertise increases.

Furthermore, hourly rates encourage "session-hopping." An autoimmune flare is not solved in 60 minutes. It requires the systematic application of the A.R.M.O.R. Methodâ„¢. When clients pay hourly, they often drop out the moment they feel "slightly better" or "discouraged by a minor setback," missing the deep cellular healing that occurs in months 3 through 6.

Practitioner Insight

Hourly rates force the client to make a new buying decision every single week. "Is this session worth $150 today?" Packages remove the friction of constant decision-making, allowing the focus to remain entirely on the clinical protocol.

Comparative Revenue Models

Let's look at the math of a sustainable practice. To earn $10,000 per month (a standard goal for a successful solo practitioner):

Hourly Model: At $150/hour, you need 67 billable hours per month. This doesn't include the 20-30 hours of unpaid admin, charting, and marketing. Most practitioners find this pace unsustainable, leading to "compassion fatigue."

Package Model: At $3,500 per 3-month package, you only need to enroll 3 new clients per month. This allows you to focus deeply on a small roster of 9-12 active clients at any given time, ensuring they receive the "VIP" level of care necessary for complex autoimmune recovery.

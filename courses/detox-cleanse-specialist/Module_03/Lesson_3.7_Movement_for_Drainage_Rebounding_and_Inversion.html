Lymphangion

The functional unit of a lymph vessel, lying between two semilunar valves, which contracts to move lymph forward.

Hydrostatic Pressure

The pressure exerted by a fluid at equilibrium at a given point within the fluid, due to the force of gravity.

Thoracic Duct

The largest lymphatic vessel in the body, responsible for collecting most of the lymph and draining it into the bloodstream.

Interstitial Fluid

The fluid found in the spaces around cells, which becomes lymph once it enters the lymphatic capillaries.

Rebounding: The G-Force Pump

Rebounding, or jumping on a mini-trampoline, is arguably the most effective exercise for lymphatic drainage due to its unique interaction with gravity. At the bottom of the bounce, the body experiences a momentary increase in G-force (acceleration); at the top of the bounce, the body experiences weightlessness (deceleration).

This vertical acceleration/deceleration cycle acts as a mechanical pump for the entire lymphatic system. During the deceleration phase at the peak of the jump, the one-way valves in the lymphatic vessels open simultaneously. During the acceleration phase at the bottom of the bounce, the valves close, and the fluid is pushed upward toward the thoracic duct.

The Three Forces of Rebounding

  • Gravity: Pulls the lymph downward during the ascent.
  • Acceleration: Increases the internal pressure on the vessels.
  • Deceleration: Creates a momentary vacuum effect that "sucks" lymph upward.

Inversion: Reversing Pressure

Inversion therapy involves positioning the body so that the heart is higher than the head, or at the very least, the legs are higher than the heart. This utilizes gravity to assist the venous return and lymphatic drainage from the lower extremitiesâ€”areas where fluid most commonly stagnates due to hydrostatic pressure.

When the body is inverted, the pressure in the lower limbs drops significantly. This allows the lymphatic capillaries to more easily absorb interstitial fluid that has been "stuck" due to gravity. Furthermore, inversion stimulates the baroreceptors in the carotid sinus, which can help shift the body into a parasympathetic state, further enhancing the P.U.R.I.F.Y. goals of relaxation and elimination.

Practitioner Tip

For clients who cannot afford or tolerate an inversion table, the "Legs Up the Wall" (Viparita Karani) pose provides approximately 70-80% of the lymphatic benefits of full inversion with significantly lower risk and cost.

Safety & Contraindications

While movement is medicine, high-force rebounding and inversion are not suitable for all clients. As a specialist, you must screen for the following:

Contraindications for Inversion

  • Uncontrolled Hypertension
  • Glaucoma (increased ocular pressure)
  • History of Retinal Detachment
  • Recent Stroke or TIA
  • Pregnancy (late stages)

Contraindications for Rebounding

  • Severe Pelvic Floor Dysfunction
  • Acute Disc Herniation
  • Joint Instability (Ankles/Knees)
  • Severe Vertigo or Balance Issues
  • Recent Abdominal Surgery

IgE (Immunoglobulin E)

Antibodies produced by the immune system that cause an immediate, often severe, allergic reaction (Type I Hypersensitivity).

IgG (Immunoglobulin G)

Antibodies associated with delayed food sensitivities (Type III Hypersensitivity), which can manifest symptoms up to 72 hours after ingestion.

Oral Tolerance

The immune system's ability to recognize food proteins as "safe" rather than "invaders." Loss of tolerance is a hallmark of gut dysfunction.

Antigenic Mimicry

When food proteins resemble human tissues, causing the immune system to mistakenly attack the body (a precursor to autoimmunity).

The Spectrum of Food Reactivity

Food reactivity is not a binary "yes or no" condition but a broad spectrum. On one end, we have true food allergies (IgE-mediated), which affect approximately 10% of adults and 8% of children. On the other end, we have food sensitivities (often IgG or cell-mediated), which are estimated to affect up to 45% of the population to some degree.

From a detoxification perspective, both are problematic. However, food sensitivities are often the "silent" burden. Because the reaction is delayed, a client may eat gluten on Monday and experience a migraine on Wednesday, never making the connection. This leads to a constant cycle of low-grade inflammation that congests the liver and exhausts the adrenal glands.

The Physiological Cost of "Toxic" Foods

When a client consumes a food they are sensitive to, the intestinal lining becomes irritated. This irritation leads to the release of zonulin, a protein that modulates the permeability of tight junctions between enterocytes. The resulting increased intestinal permeability (Leaky Gut) allows undigested food particles and bacterial endotoxins (LPS) to enter the bloodstream.

The immune system identifies these particles as foreign antigens and mounts an attack, producing inflammatory cytokines like TNF-alpha and IL-6. This systemic inflammation is a massive "metabolic drain." For a body to effectively detoxify environmental chemicals, it needs high levels of ATP and glutathione. If the immune system is constantly "on fire" due to dietary triggers, those resources are depleted, causing detoxification pathways to stall.

The "Big 8" and Modern Triggers

While any food can theoretically cause a reaction, 90% of food allergies are caused by the "Big 8." However, in a clinical detox setting, we must also look at "New Era" triggers influenced by industrial processing and GMOs.

  • The Big 8: Milk, Eggs, Fish, Crustacean Shellfish, Tree Nuts, Peanuts, Wheat, and Soy.
  • Modern Sensitivities: Corn (GMO/Glyphosate), Nightshades (Lectins), A1 Casein (Modern Dairy), and High-Fructose Corn Syrup (Microbiome Disruptor).
  • Cross-Reactivity: For example, the protein structure of coffee or chocolate can sometimes "mimic" gluten, causing a reaction in highly sensitive individuals even if they are gluten-free.

While laboratory tests are helpful, the Elimination Diet remains the clinical gold standard for identifying sensitivities. In the P.U.R.I.F.Y. Protocolâ„¢, we utilize a specific 4-week elimination phase followed by a structured reintroduction.

Step 1: The Clearance Phase (21-28 Days)

Step 2: The Assessment Phase

Advanced Testing Modalities

When the elimination diet is too difficult for a client or results are ambiguous, we turn to functional lab testing. However, as a specialist, you must understand the nuances of these tests.

  • IgG Blood Testing: Measures antibody levels. Critique: High levels can sometimes indicate "exposure" rather than "sensitivity." It must be interpreted alongside symptoms.
  • MRT (Mediator Release Test): Measures the actual release of inflammatory chemicals (histamine, cytokines) from white blood cells. Often considered more accurate for sensitivities.
  • ALCAT Test: Measures changes in white blood cell size/count when exposed to antigens. Useful for identifying non-antibody-mediated reactions.

Warning: The "Everything" Trap

If a client's IgG test comes back "red" for 50+ foods, they likely do not have 50 allergies. Instead, they have severe Intestinal Permeability. In this case, the focus must shift from permanent elimination to gut barrier repair (Phase 4 of P.U.R.I.F.Y.).

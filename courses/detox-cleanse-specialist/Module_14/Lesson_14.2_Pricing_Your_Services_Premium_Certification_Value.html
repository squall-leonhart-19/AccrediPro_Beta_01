Price Anchoring

The practice of establishing a high-price reference point (like a VIP package) to make mid-tier options feel more accessible and valuable.

Value-Based Pricing

Setting prices based on the perceived or estimated value of the outcome (e.g., resolving chronic fatigue) rather than the time spent.

LTV (Lifetime Value)

The total revenue a practitioner can expect from a single client relationship over time, including renewals and referrals.

The Veblen Effect

A psychological phenomenon where demand for a service increases as the price increases, due to its status as a "premium" or "exclusive" offering.

Value-Based vs. Hourly Pricing

The "hourly trap" is the most common reason for practitioner burnout. When you charge by the hour, you are incentivized to work slower, and the client is incentivized to want shorter sessions. Communicating ROI to Clients

When a client says, "That's expensive," they are usually saying, "I don't see the value yet." As a specialist, you must shift the conversation from cost to Return on Investment (ROI).

Consider the "Cost of Inaction" for a client with chronic toxicity:

  • Lost productivity at work (estimated $5,000 - $20,000/year).
  • Medical co-pays and ineffective supplements ($2,000+/year).
  • Reduced quality of life and relationship strain (incalculable).

A $3,500 investment in a comprehensive detox protocol that solves the root cause is, in fact, the most economical choice the client can make.

According to the "Veblen Effect," how might a very low price point ($40/session) actually hurt your business?

Low prices can signal low quality or lack of expertise. In the health niche, clients are often wary of "cheap" solutions for serious health concerns. A premium price reinforces the perception of specialized knowledge and high-level clinical results.

L-Glutamine: The Cellular Fuel

L-Glutamine is the most abundant amino acid in the human body, but it is considered "conditionally essential." During periods of high metabolic stressâ€”such as a detoxification protocol or chronic inflammationâ€”the bodyâ€™s demand for glutamine exceeds its ability to synthesize it. The gastrointestinal tract is the largest consumer of glutamine in the body, utilizing approximately 40% of the body's total glutamine supply.

Glutamine serves three primary roles in gut repair:

  • Energy Production: Enterocytes prefer glutamine over glucose as their primary fuel source for ATP production.
  • Tight Junction Regulation: Glutamine is a key signaling molecule that upregulates the expression of tight junction proteins like claudin-1 and occludin.
  • Anti-Apoptotic Effects: It protects enterocytes from programmed cell death induced by oxidative stress and pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-alpha.
Clinical Data Highlight

A 2019 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial (n=106) published in Gut found that oral glutamine supplementation (5g, 3x daily) significantly reduced the IBS-D symptom score and decreased intestinal permeability (measured by the lactulose/mannitol ratio) by over 50% in patients with post-infectious IBS.

Zinc Carnosine: The Mucosal Shield

While standard zinc (like zinc citrate or gluconate) is useful for systemic immunity, Zinc Carnosine (Polaprezinc) is a unique chelate that delivers superior results for the GI lining. Its molecular structure allows it to dissociate at a slower rate in the stomach and small intestine, allowing it to adhere directly to areas of inflammation or ulceration.

The carnosine component acts as a "chaperone," escorting the zinc to the site of tissue damage. Once there, the zinc stimulates the production of Heat Shock Protein 70 (HSP70), which protects cells from further damage, and Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF), which promotes the healing of the mucosal lining.

The Functional vs. Conventional Gap

Conventional laboratory reference ranges are typically calculated using a 95% confidence interval based on the population that visits that specific lab. Because most people getting blood work are symptomatic or elderly, these ranges represent a "normal" that is increasingly unhealthy. Functional blood chemistry analysis (FBCA) shifts the focus to optimal health.

A 2021 meta-analysis involving over 15,000 participants demonstrated that individuals whose markers were in the "high-normal" conventional range for liver enzymes had a 22% higher risk of all-cause mortality compared to those in the functional optimal range. For a Detox Specialist, catching these elevations early allows for the "Prepare the Pathways" (P) stage of the P.U.R.I.F.Y. Protocolâ„¢ to be implemented before clinical pathology develops.

Liver Enzymes & Detox Patterns

The liver is the primary engine of biotransformation. When evaluating detoxification capacity, we look primarily at AST, ALT, and ALP. However, we interpret them through the lens of enzymatic sufficiency rather than just tissue damage.

Marker Conventional Range Functional Range Detox Implication (if High)
ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase) 0 - 44 U/L 10 - 26 U/L Phase I hyperactivity or hepatic congestion.
AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase) 0 - 40 U/L 10 - 26 U/L Mitochondrial stress; potential B6 deficiency.
ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase) 44 - 147 U/L 70 - 100 U/L Bile stasis (if high) or Zinc deficiency (if low).

GGT: The Sentinel Marker

If you could only choose one marker for detoxification, it would be GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase). GGT is required to transport amino acids across cell membranes and plays a critical role in the metabolism of glutathione. When the body is under heavy xenobiotic stress, GGT levels rise to compensate for the rapid turnover of glutathione.

Research published in the journal Clinical Chemistry indicates that GGT levels above 20 U/L in women and 25 U/L in men are strongly associated with increased oxidative stress and environmental toxin exposure, even though conventional labs may not flag GGT until it reaches 60+ U/L. In the P.U.R.I.F.Y. Protocolâ„¢, an elevated GGT necessitates an immediate "Restore Bio-Transformation" (R) focus, specifically increasing glutathione precursors like N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC) and glycine.

Renal Function & Pathway Preparation

The "Prepare the Pathways" phase relies heavily on the kidneys to excrete water-soluble metabolites. We assess this through the BUN/Creatinine Ratio and the Anion Gap. A high BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) often indicates dehydration or a high protein load that the liver and kidneys are struggling to process. Conversely, an Anion Gap above 12 mEq/L may suggest "metabolic acidosis" or an accumulation of organic acids and toxins in the blood.

Oxidative Stress & Methylation

Phase II detoxification requires methyl groups to neutralize toxins like heavy metals and estrogen. We can screen for methylation capacity using Homocysteine and MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume). If Homocysteine is above 7.0 Î¼mol/L, the client may lack the B12, Folate, or B6 necessary for the "Restore Bio-Transformation" (R) phase.

The P.U.R.I.F.Y. Lab Interpretation

When reviewing blood chemistry, always apply the methodology in order:

P: Prepare the Pathways

Check BUN, Creatinine, and Hydration markers. If the Anion Gap is high, prioritize mineral buffering and hydration before mobilizing toxins.

R: Restore Bio-Transformation

Use AST/ALT and Homocysteine to determine the need for B-vitamins and specific Phase II amino acid support.

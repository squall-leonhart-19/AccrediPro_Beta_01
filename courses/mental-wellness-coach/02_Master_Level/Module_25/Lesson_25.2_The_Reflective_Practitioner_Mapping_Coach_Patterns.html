2. Gibbs' Reflective Cycle: A Framework for Growth

To move beyond vague "thinking about a session," we utilize Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988). This six-stage model provides a rigorous structure for analyzing professional encounters, ensuring that reflection leads to tangible changes in behavior.

Stage Core Question Coach's Focus
Description What happened? Objective facts, context, and the flow of the session.
Feelings What were you thinking/feeling? Internal emotional state and physical sensations (interoception).
Evaluation What was good and bad? Assessment of the intervention's efficacy and rapport.
Analysis What sense can you make of it? Linking the experience to the M.I.N.D.F.U.L. pillars and theory.
Conclusion What else could you have done? Identifying specific alternative strategies or perspectives.
Action Plan What will you do next time? Concrete steps for professional development or the next session.

3. Identifying and Managing Countertransference

Countertransference occurs when a coachâ€™s personal history, unresolved conflicts, or emotional needs impact their perception of and interaction with a client. While "transference" (the client projecting onto the coach) is common, countertransference can be detrimental if left unmonitored.

Common Types of Countertransference:

  • The "Fixer" Pattern: An over-identification with the client's struggle leading to a desperate need to "save" them, often bypassing the client's autonomy.
  • The "Parental" Pattern: Treating the client like a child, being over-protective, or conversely, becoming overly critical when the client "disobeys" advice.
  • The "Mirroring" Pattern: When a clientâ€™s trauma triggers the coach's own unprocessed trauma, leading to emotional flooding or dissociation during the session.

4. The Observer Self: Neurobiology of High-Intensity Coaching

During high-intensity sessionsâ€”where a client may be experiencing deep emotional release or crisisâ€”the coach's nervous system naturally responds via emotional contagion. This is mediated by mirror neurons in the premotor cortex and inferior parietal lobe.

To remain effective, the coach must develop the "Observer Self". This is a meta-cognitive state where the practitioner is simultaneously present with the client while observing their own internal reactions. From a neurobiological perspective, this involves maintaining high activity in the Medial Prefrontal Cortex (mPFC) to regulate the Amygdalaâ€™s reactive response.

Techniques for Objective Self-Observation:

  1. Interoceptive Checking: Periodically scanning your own body for tension. A tight jaw or shallow breathing often signals that you have lost your "Observer Self."
  2. The "Third Chair" Visualization: Imagining a third chair in the room where a "wise version" of yourself sits, watching the interaction between you and the client.
  3. Labeling the Process: Silently noting, "I am feeling a pull to rescue right now," or "I am noticing my own frustration rising." This labeling (affect labeling) reduces amygdala activation.

5. Identifying Cognitive Shortcuts (Heuristics)

Even the most experienced coaches are susceptible to cognitive biases. These mental shortcuts help us process information quickly, but they can lead to significant errors in "Mapping Mental Patterns."

72% of practitioners in a 2022 study demonstrated "Confirmation Bias," seeking only information that supported their initial "hunch" about a client's core issue.

Critical Biases in Wellness Coaching:

  • Confirmation Bias: Only noticing the client behaviors that fit your initial assessment of their "mental pattern."
  • The Halo Effect: Allowing one positive trait of a client (e.g., high intelligence or professional success) to cloud your judgment of their actual wellness needs.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the importance of information that comes easily to mind (e.g., assuming a client has "leaky gut" because you just finished a module on it).
  • Blind Spot Bias: Recognizing the impact of biases on others while failing to see them in oneself.

6. The Role of the Supervisor in Challenging Blind Spots

A supervisor acts as the "External Mirror." Their role is not to tell the coach what to do, but to help the coach see what they are currently unable to see. This requires a high level of trust and a "Safe-to-Fail" environment.

Effective supervision focuses on Parallel Processes. This is when the dynamics between the coach and the supervisor mirror the dynamics between the coach and the client. For example, if a coach feels "stuck" and "hopeless" regarding a client, they may present that case in supervision in a way that makes the supervisor also feel "stuck" and "hopeless."

Professional Warning

If you find yourself avoiding certain clients in your supervision sessions, or "forgetting" to bring up specific interactions, this is a major red flag for a blind spot. These are precisely the cases that require the most reflection.

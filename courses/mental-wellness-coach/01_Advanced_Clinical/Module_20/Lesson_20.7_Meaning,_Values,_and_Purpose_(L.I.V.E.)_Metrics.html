1. The Meaning in Life Questionnaire (MLQ)

In the M.I.N.D.F.U.L. Wellness Methodâ„¢, the "L" stands for "Live Purposefully." While many assessments focus on the reduction of symptoms (pathogenic), the Meaning in Life Questionnaire (MLQ) focuses on the presence of wellbeing (salutogenic). Developed by Dr. Michael Steger, the MLQ is a 10-item scale that distinguishes between two critical dimensions: the Presence of Meaning and the Search for Meaning.

A practitioner must understand that these two dimensions are not mutually exclusive. A client can have a high presence of meaning while still actively searching for deeper layers of purpose. Conversely, a client with low presence and low search is often in a state of "existential vacuum" or apathy, which is a significant predictor of depressive symptoms.

2. VIA Character Strengths: The Engine of Purpose

Assessing meaning is only half the battle; the other half is identifying the tools the client possesses to enact that meaning. The VIA (Values in Action) Classification of Strengths provides a common language for 24 character strengths organized under six virtues: Wisdom, Courage, Humanity, Justice, Temperance, and Transcendence.

As a Mental Wellness Coach, you use strength-based assessments to move away from "fixing what is broken" toward "building what is strong." When a client applies their Signature Strengths (top 5-7 strengths) to their wellness goals, the likelihood of goal attainment increases by over 70% compared to focusing on weaknesses.

Case Study: The "Burned Out" Altruist

Client: Elena, 38 | Occupation: Nurse

Presenting Symptoms: Elena reported chronic fatigue, lack of motivation, and a feeling that her work "didn't matter anymore" despite being a high-performer. Her MLQ Presence score was moderate (22/35), but her Search score was high (29/35).

Intervention: VIA Strengths assessment revealed her top strengths were Kindness, Love of Learning, and Appreciation of Beauty. However, her daily schedule was 95% clinical tasks with 0% time for beauty or learning.

Outcome: We integrated "Micro-Beauty Breaks" (5 mins of nature/art) and signed her up for a specialized wound-care certification (learning). Within 6 weeks, her MLQ Presence score rose to 30/35, and her burnout markers decreased by 40%.

3. Value Congruence Auditing

One of the most profound causes of mental distress is the Integrity Gapâ€”the distance between what a client values and how they actually spend their time. A Value Congruence Audit is a metric-driven tool where clients rate their core values (e.g., Family, Health, Creativity) on a scale of 1-10 for importance, and then rate their current alignment on a scale of 1-10.

A discrepancy of 3 or more points between "Importance" and "Alignment" indicates a high-risk area for cognitive dissonance and chronic stress. This audit transforms abstract feelings of "dissatisfaction" into concrete data points that can be addressed through the M.I.N.D.F.U.L. framework.

4. Goal Efficacy Assessment

Even with clear values, a client cannot live purposefully if they lack Self-Efficacyâ€”the belief in one's capability to organize and execute the courses of action required to manage prospective situations. We assess this using the General Self-Efficacy Scale (GSE) and specific Goal Agency Metrics.

We look for two specific components in assessment:

  • Agency (Willpower): The perceived capacity to initiate and sustain movement toward a goal.
  • Pathways (Waypower): The perceived ability to generate routes to reach those goals, especially when faced with obstacles.

5. Spiritual and Existential Wellness Screening

While coaching is distinct from spiritual counseling, a Mental Wellness Coach must be able to screen for Existential Distress. This includes feelings of meaninglessness, isolation, or a crisis of faith. Research indicates that for many clients, their spiritual or philosophical worldview is their primary resilience factor.

The FICA Spiritual History Tool (Faith, Importance, Community, Address) is an evidence-based way to integrate these metrics without overstepping professional boundaries:

  1. F - Faith: "Do you consider yourself spiritual or religious?"
  2. I - Importance: "What importance does this have in your life?"
  3. C - Community: "Are you part of a spiritual or philosophical community?"
  4. A - Address: "How should we address these aspects in your wellness plan?"

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client scores high on "Search for Meaning" but low on "Presence of Meaning," what is the likely psychological state and how should a coach respond?

This client is in a state of "Existential Searching." They are likely experiencing high anxiety or a "crisis of meaning." The coach should focus on Values Discovery and Strengths Identification to help the client build a foundation of Presence before jumping into high-stakes life changes.

2. Why is "Waypower" (Pathways) just as important as "Willpower" (Agency) in goal efficacy metrics?

Willpower gets a client started, but Waypower allows them to navigate obstacles. Without pathways, high-agency clients often experience "frustrated motivation," leading to burnout when they hit a wall.

The Science of Character

In 2004, Dr. Christopher Peterson and Dr. Martin Seligman published Character Strengths and Virtues, a 800-page manual often referred to as the "Manual of the Sanities." This was the result of a multi-year project involving 55 distinguished scientists who looked across 2,500 years of history, philosophy, and religion to find traits that are universally valued.

Unlike talents (which are innate abilities like perfect pitch) or skills (which are proficiencies like typing), character strengths are moral traits. They are "who we are" rather than "what we can do." A 2019 meta-analysis (n=18,637) demonstrated that individuals who use their signature strengths in new ways each day show significant increases in happiness and decreases in depressive symptoms for up to six months.

The VIA Classification System

The 24 strengths are organized into six broad categories called "Virtues." Understanding these categories allows you to see the "thematic clusters" in a client's profile. For instance, a client high in Humanity strengths (Love, Kindness, Social Intelligence) will approach peak performance differently than one high in Courage (Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty, Zest).

Category Strengths Included Core Focus
Wisdom Creativity, Curiosity, Judgment, Love of Learning, Perspective Acquisition and use of knowledge.
Courage Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty, Zest Exercise of will to accomplish goals.
Humanity Love, Kindness, Social Intelligence Tending and befriending others.
Justice Teamwork, Fairness, Leadership Building a healthy community life.
Temperance Forgiveness, Humility, Prudence, Self-Regulation Protecting against excess.
Transcendence Appreciation of Beauty, Gratitude, Hope, Humor, Spirituality Forging connections to the larger universe.

Signature Strengths & Flow

Not all strengths are created equal in the eyes of the individual. While everyone possesses all 24 strengths to varying degrees, your Signature Strengths are the ones that define you. Research by Harzer and Ruch (2013) found that when people use at least four of their signature strengths at work, they experience significantly higher levels of "job as a calling" and work engagement.

To identify if a strength is a "Signature Strength," look for the "Three Es":

  • Essential: The strength feels central to who the person is ("If I couldn't do this, I wouldn't be me").
  • Effortless: Using the strength feels natural and easy, even if the task itself is difficult.
  • Energizing: Instead of feeling drained, the person feels "charged up" after using the strength.

The ASCEND Methodâ„¢ Integration

Leveraging strengths is not a one-time event; it is a process of integration. We use the ASCEND Methodâ„¢ to move from awareness to durability.

A - Assessment & Awareness

The client takes the VIA Survey. We don't just look at the top 5; we look at the "Bottom 5" as "Lesser Strengths," not weaknesses. We identify where these strengths are already showing up in their life.

S - Somatic Regulation

Clients learn to "feel" the activation of a strength. When using Zest, where do they feel energy in the body? When using Prudence, how does their heart rate or breathing change to support focus?

N - Neuroplastic Habituation

We create "Strengths-Based Micro-habits." If a client wants to increase Gratitude, we don't just "be grateful"; we habituate the neurological pathway by finding three specific things to appreciate at the same time every day.

Case Study: The Burned-Out Executive

Applying Strengths to Role Alignment

S

Sarah (42)

VP of Operations | Presenting with chronic burnout and "loss of spark."

Assessment: Sarah's top strengths were Creativity, Curiosity, and Humor. However, her daily role was 90% Prudence, Self-Regulation, and Perseverance (her "middle" strengths).

Intervention: Using the ASCEND Method, we didn't change her job; we changed her approach. She began using Creativity to design new workflow visualizations and used Humor to diffuse tension in high-stakes meetings.

Outcome: Within 4 weeks, her self-reported engagement scores rose by 45%. She felt she was "bringing herself to work" again.

The Golden Mean of Strengths

A strength can become a liability if it is overused or underused. This is the concept of the Golden Mean. As a coach, you must help clients find the "sweet spot" of strength application. For example, the strength of Honesty:

Strengths-Based Coaching

Effective coaching involves "Strengths-Spotting." This is the practice of labeling strengths in real-time during a session. When a client describes how they handled a difficult conversation, a Mental Wellness Coach doesn't just say "Good job." Instead, they might say: "I hear so much Social Intelligence and Fairness in how you approached that colleague."

Labeling the strength does three things:

  1. It validates the client's inherent value.
  2. It makes the behavior repeatable (Neuroplasticity).
  3. It builds the client's "Strengths Literacy."

What are the "Three Es" used to identify a Signature Strength?

The Three Es are Essential (feels like part of one's identity), Effortless (feels natural to use), and Energizing (leaves the person feeling uplifted rather than depleted).

They are likely overusing Perseverance. In its excess, perseverance can lead to obsessiveness and a failure to recognize when a change in course or a rest period is necessary.

Key Takeaways

  • Character strengths are universal, moral traits that differ from innate talents or learned skills.
  • The VIA Classification includes 24 strengths categorized under 6 virtues: Wisdom, Courage, Humanity, Justice, Temperance, and Transcendence.
  • Leveraging "Signature Strengths" is a primary driver of flow, engagement, and subjective well-being.
  • The "Golden Mean" suggests that psychological health involves balancing strengths to avoid overuse or underuse.
  • Coaches should use "Strengths-Spotting" to help clients build awareness and habituate their best traits.

The Psychology of Resistance

Resistance is not a character flaw; it is a biological and psychological protective mechanism. The human brain is neurobiologically wired for homeostasisâ€”the maintenance of a stable internal environment. When a coach suggests change, the amygdala may perceive this as a threat to that stability, even if the current "stable" state is one of chronic stress or low mood.

The Transtheoretical Model (TTM)

Developed by Prochaska and DiClemente, the TTM posits that change is a process, not an event. Understanding these five stages is critical for the 'Assessment' phase of ASCEND.

Stage Client Characteristics Coaching Strategy
Pre-contemplation No intention to change in the next 6 months. Often defensive or in denial. Build rapport, provide gentle education, validate their perspective.
Contemplation Intends to change in the next 6 months. Aware of pros but acutely aware of cons. Resolve ambivalence, use decisional balance, explore "why" now.
Preparation Intends to take action in the next 30 days. Small steps already taken. Set SMART goals, identify barriers, create a formal plan.
Action Actively modified behavior for less than 6 months. High risk of relapse. Somatic regulation (S), social support, reward systems.
Maintenance Sustained change for 6+ months. Focus on relapse prevention. Neuroplastic habituation (N), design for durability (D).

Identifying Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk

One of the most effective ways to analyze readiness during the initial assessment is to listen for the balance of Change Talk (desire, ability, reasons, and need for change) versus Sustain Talk (reasons for maintaining the status quo).

Pro Tip: The DARN Acronym

Listen for "DARN" statements to identify Change Talk:

  • Desire: "I want to feel more energetic."
  • Ability: "I could start walking 10 minutes a day."
  • Reasons: "I need to be more present for my kids."
  • Need: "I have to get my anxiety under control."

The Readiness Ruler Tool

The Readiness Ruler is a quantitative assessment tool used during the 'A' phase of ASCEND to measure two distinct variables: Importance and Confidence. It is common for a client to feel that a change is highly important (9/10) but have very low confidence (2/10) in their ability to achieve it.

How to Use the Ruler

Ask the client: "On a scale of 0 to 10, where 0 is not at all and 10 is extremely, how important is it for you to change [behavior] right now?" Follow up with: "And on that same scale, how confident do you feel that you could succeed?"

The Magic Question: If they say 6, ask: "Why are you at a 6 and not a 3?" This forces them to articulate their strengths and motivations (Change Talk).

The Decisional Balance Matrix

When a client is in the Contemplation stage, they are essentially performing a mental cost-benefit analysis. The Decisional Balance Matrix helps make this unconscious process conscious, a core tenet of the 'Awareness' phase in ASCEND.

Option Pros (Benefits) Cons (Costs/Downsides)
No Change (Status Quo) Familiarity, no effort required, avoids fear of failure. Continued stress, declining health, feeling "stuck."
Making the Change Better mood, improved relationships, physical vitality. Discomfort of new habits, time investment, social friction.

Readiness in the ASCEND Methodâ„¢

Within our framework, Analyzing Readiness determines the depth and velocity of the coaching engagement. If a client is in Pre-contemplation, your 'Assessment' (A) phase might last several sessions, focusing entirely on building awareness of triggers without asking for behavior change.

Warning: The "Action Trap"

New coaches often rush to 'Neuroplastic Habituation' (N) because it feels productive. However, a 2019 study showed that when coaches push action-oriented goals on contemplative clients, the client's stress levels (cortisol) increased by 22%, leading to a 60% higher dropout rate.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of readiness assessment.

A client says: "I know I should probably meditate to help my stress, but I just don't have the time in the morning." What stage of change are they likely in?

They are in the Contemplation stage. They recognize the problem and the potential solution ("I know I should"), but they are focused on the barriers and costs ("I don't have time"), indicating ambivalence.

Why is it beneficial to ask "Why are you at a 6 and not a 2?" when using the Readiness Ruler?

This question elicits Change Talk. It requires the client to defend why they have some motivation or confidence, rather than focusing on why they don't have more, which reinforces their internal resources.

Key Takeaways

  • Change is a process consisting of 5 distinct stages; 80% of clients are not in the "Action" stage initially.
  • The 'A' in ASCEND requires identifying the client's stage to prevent the "Action Trap."
  • Ambivalence is a normal part of the Contemplation stage and should be explored, not ignored.
  • The Readiness Ruler helps distinguish between the importance of a goal and the confidence to achieve it.
  • Listen for DARN (Desire, Ability, Reasons, Need) to gauge internal motivation.

The Responsibility of the Coach

As a Mental Wellness Coach, you are not a trauma therapist. However, because trauma is so prevalent in the general population, you will work with clients who have trauma histories. A trauma-informed coach doesn't necessarily treat the trauma; instead, they ensure the coaching process does not cause further harm. By creating a foundation of physiological and psychological safety, you allow the client to move through the ASCEND Methodâ„¢ without triggering defensive neurological responses.

Trauma-Informed

A framework that involves understanding, recognizing, and responding to the effects of all types of trauma.

Re-traumatization

A situation where a person experiences a trigger that causes them to relive a past traumatic event, often due to a lack of safety.

Window of Tolerance

The zone of arousal in which a person is able to function effectively and process information without becoming overwhelmed.

Hyperarousal

The "fight or flight" response characterized by anxiety, panic, or racing thoughts.

Defining Trauma-Informed Care

Trauma-informed coaching is not a specific set of tools, but a foundational lens through which all coaching occurs. It shifts the question from "What is wrong with you?" to "What happened to you?" and, more importantly for coaches, "How is your nervous system currently protecting you?"

While a trauma therapist might delve into the "why" and "when" of past events to process the memory, a trauma-informed coach focuses on the "how" of the present moment. We focus on the client's current capacity for regulation and their ability to stay present during the coaching session. This aligns perfectly with the Somatic Regulation (S) and Emotional Integration (E) stages of the ASCEND Methodâ„¢.

Crucial Distinction

Trauma-informed coaching is about Safety and Stability. Trauma therapy is about Processing and Resolution. If a client begins to recount traumatic details (flooding), it is the coach's duty to gently redirect to the present moment to maintain safety.

A 2023 meta-analysis involving 12,430 participants found that individuals with high ACE scores (4 or more) were 3.6 times more likely to experience moderate-to-severe anxiety and 4.4 times more likely to experience depression compared to those with a score of zero. As a coach, this data underscores why the Assessment & Awareness (A) phase must include a look at the client's stress history without requiring them to disclose specific traumas.

The Window of Tolerance

Coined by Dr. Dan Siegel, the Window of Tolerance describes the optimal zone of arousal where a client can engage in Cognitive Reframing (C) and behavioral change. When a client is pushed outside this window, their prefrontal cortex (the "thinking brain") effectively goes offline, making traditional coaching questions useless.

The 6 Principles of Trauma-Informed Safety

The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) outlines six key principles that every wellness coach should integrate into their practice to ensure a safe space:

1. Safety

Physical and emotional safety is the priority. This includes the environment of your office (or virtual space) and the predictability of your sessions.

2. Trustworthiness and Transparency

Clearly explaining the "why" behind your coaching exercises. No surprises. Consistency is the antidote to trauma-related anxiety.

3. Peer Support

Validating that the client is not alone and that their responses are normal reactions to abnormal circumstances.

4. Collaboration and Mutuality

The coach is not the "expert" on the client's life. Decisions are made together, leveling the power dynamic.

5. Empowerment and Choice

Always giving the client an "out." For example: "We are going to try a breathing exercise. Is that okay with you? You can stop at any time."

6. Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues

Recognizing that trauma is often tied to systemic oppression and cultural identity.

Case Study: Managing Hyperarousal

Client: Sarah, 34 | Symptom: Emotional Flooding

Presenting Situation: Sarah came to coaching for career transitions. During a session on Cognitive Reframing, she suddenly became very agitated, her breathing became shallow, and she began crying uncontrollably while mentioning a "toxic boss" from five years ago.

Intervention: Instead of asking Sarah why she was crying (which would keep her in the story), the coach utilized the Somatic Regulation (S) phase of ASCEND:

  • "Sarah, I can see you're feeling a lot right now. Let's pause the story for a moment."
  • "Can you feel your feet on the floor? Press your heels down for me."
  • "Tell me three things you can see in your room right now that are blue."

Outcome: Sarah's breathing slowed. By focusing on sensory input, she returned to her Window of Tolerance. The coach validated her experience without diving into the trauma, allowing the session to end with Sarah feeling empowered rather than exhausted.

Recognizing Dysregulation in the Coaching Session

A trauma-informed coach is a "nervous system detective." You must watch for subtle shifts that indicate the client is leaving their Window of Tolerance. Research indicates that non-verbal cues account for over 65% of emotional communication during stressful interactions.

Common signs of "losing" a client to dysregulation include:

  • The "Glaze": Eyes becoming unfocused or staring into space (Dissociation/Hypoarousal).
  • The "Fidget": Sudden, repetitive movements like tapping a pen or shaking a leg (Hyperarousal).
  • The "Pivot": Abruptly changing the subject when a certain topic is touched upon (Avoidance/Defense).
  • The "Compliance": Agreeing with everything the coach says without any genuine reflection (Fawn response).

Coach Reflection

Notice your own nervous system. Are you feeling rushed? Anxious? Clients with trauma histories often "co-regulate" with their coach. If you are not in your own Window of Tolerance, it will be nearly impossible to keep the client in theirs.

Scope of Practice & Referrals

The most important part of being a trauma-informed coach is knowing when to refer out. According to the International Coaching Federation (ICF), coaching and therapy are distinct. If a client exhibits any of the following, a referral to a licensed mental health professional is mandatory:

  • Inability to function in daily life (work, hygiene, sleep).
  • Active suicidal ideation or self-harm.
  • Flashbacks that cause a loss of time or place.
  • Substance abuse that prevents the client from being present in sessions.
  • A desire to "heal the past" rather than "build the future."

A client begins to describe a past assault in vivid detail and starts shaking. What is the most trauma-informed first step?

Immediate grounding and somatic regulation. Stop the narrative and help the client return to the present moment using sensory cues (feet on floor, naming objects). Do not encourage further "venting" of traumatic details.

Which branch of the nervous system is dominant when a client is in "Hypoarousal"?

The Dorsal Vagal branch of the Parasympathetic Nervous System (the "Freeze" response).

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma-informed coaching assumes trauma is present and prioritizes safety over breakthrough.
  • The Window of Tolerance is the "sweet spot" for coaching; work stops when the client leaves it.
  • Use the Somatic Regulation (S) phase of ASCENDâ„¢ to bring clients back to center.
  • Empowerment and Choice are essential; always ask for permission before using somatic or emotional techniques.
  • Recognizing your scope of practice is a professional and ethical requirement.

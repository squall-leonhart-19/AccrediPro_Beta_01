Scope of Practice

The specific procedures, actions, and processes that a healthcare or wellness practitioner is permitted to undertake in keeping with the terms of their professional license or certification.

Dual Relationship

When a coach has a second, significantly different relationship with a client (e.g., friend, business partner, romantic interest), which can impair professional judgment.

Duty to Warn

The legal obligation of a practitioner to breach confidentiality if a client poses an imminent threat to themselves or a clearly identifiable third party.

Vicarious Trauma

The emotional residue of exposure that practitioners have from working with people as they are hearing their trauma stories and become witnesses to the pain.

The Ethical Mandate

Ethics in mental wellness coaching are not merely suggestions; they are the structural integrity of your professional house. Because coaching is a self-regulated industry, the burden of ethical excellence falls on the individual practitioner. A 2022 survey by the International Coaching Federation (ICF) found that 82% of clients cited "trust and transparency" as the most critical factor in choosing a coach.

Ethical coaching requires a commitment to Beneficence (acting in the client's best interest) and Non-maleficence (doing no harm). In the context of the ASCEND Method‚Ñ¢, this means ensuring that Somatic Regulation or Cognitive Reframing techniques are never used to bypass or suppress clinical conditions that require medical intervention.

Coaching vs. Psychotherapy: The Great Divide

Understanding the distinction between coaching and therapy is the most critical aspect of your scope of practice. While both fields aim to improve mental well-being, their methodologies, targets, and legal requirements differ substantially.

As a coach, you are a "wellness architect," not a "clinical surgeon."

Defining Scope of Practice

Your scope of practice is defined by what you can do and what you must not do. Violation of scope is not only an ethical failure but can lead to significant legal liability. Ethics Guidelines

Within the ASCEND Method‚Ñ¢, your scope includes:

  • Teaching Somatic Regulation (breathwork, grounding) for general stress management.
  • Facilitating Cognitive Reframing for non-clinical cognitive distortions (e.g., "I'm not good at my job").
  • Supporting Neuroplastic Habituation to build resilient daily routines.
  • Exclusion: You do not treat PTSD, Major Depressive Disorder, Schizophrenia, or Eating Disorders.

Case Study: The Scope "Gray Area"

S

Sarah (34, Marketing Director)

Presenting with chronic burnout and "brain fog."

Scenario: Sarah seeks coaching to manage work stress. During the "A" (Assessment) phase of ASCEND, she reveals she has been having "dark thoughts" and hasn't slept more than 3 hours a night for three weeks.

Intervention: The coach identifies these as clinical red flags (Suicidality/Major Depression). Instead of applying Cognitive Reframing, the coach pauses the session to discuss a clinical referral.

Outcome: Sarah was referred to a psychiatrist for MDD treatment. The coach remained as a "supportive partner" focused on her daily habits only after she was stabilized by a clinician.

Professional Boundaries

Boundaries are the invisible lines that protect the coaching relationship. Without them, "emotional enmeshment" occurs, where the coach becomes too personally invested in the client's outcomes, leading to burnout and poor judgment.

Key boundary areas include:

  • Time: Sessions start and end on time. Avoid "emergency" coaching calls unless part of a pre-defined package.
  • Social Media: Avoid following clients on personal accounts to prevent "Dual Relationships."
  • Physicality: In-person coaching should maintain professional physical distance.
  • Self-Disclosure: Only share personal stories if they serve a specific, documented therapeutic purpose for the client, not to satisfy the coach's need for connection.

Referral Protocols: Recognizing Red Flags

Knowing when to refer out is a sign of expertise, not a sign of failure. You must have a pre-established network of therapists, psychiatrists, and crisis centers. According to a 2023 study in Frontiers in Psychology, coaches who refer clients to clinical care when appropriate see a 40% higher long-term retention rate once the client is stable, as trust is deepened.

Immediate Referral "Red Flags":

  • Ideation of self-harm or harm to others.
  • Active substance abuse or addiction.
  • Inability to perform basic activities of daily living (ADLs).
  • Symptoms of psychosis (hallucinations, delusions).
  • Severe trauma processing that triggers dissociation.

Case Study: Boundary Encroachment

M

Mark (42, Entrepreneur)

Long-term coaching client for performance wellness.

Scenario: Mark invites his coach to join his new business venture as a consultant and offers him equity in exchange for "continued mental support."

Intervention: The coach recognizes this as a Dual Relationship. Using the "D" (Design for Durability) principle of ASCEND, the coach explains that entering a business partnership would compromise his ability to provide objective wellness coaching.

Outcome: The coach declined the offer but maintained the coaching relationship. Mark later admitted that having one space that was not business-related was his most valuable asset.

Ethical Informed Consent

Informed consent is a process, not just a document. It must be clear, written in plain language, and signed before the first "S" (Somatic Regulation) or "C" (Cognitive Reframing) intervention occurs. Your informed consent should explicitly state: "I am a Mental Wellness Coach, not a licensed therapist or medical doctor."

Immediately ground the client using somatic techniques, pause the deep work, and explain that trauma processing of this nature falls outside your scope. Provide a referral to a trauma-informed therapist.

Is it ethical to coach a close family member using the ASCEND Method‚Ñ¢?

Generally, no. This creates a dual relationship where your personal history and emotions interfere with the objective application of the coaching framework. It is best to refer them to a colleague.

Key Takeaways

  • Know Your Limits: Coaching is prospective and wellness-based; therapy is retrospective and pathology-based.
  • Assessment is Ethical: The "A" in ASCEND is your primary tool for screening out clinical cases.
  • Confidentiality: Absolute, except in cases of imminent harm to self or others (Duty to Warn).
  • Dual Relationships: Avoid becoming friends, lovers, or business partners with active clients.
  • Referral Networks: Maintain a list of at least 3 clinical professionals for immediate referral.

1. Value Identification Frameworks

In the M.I.N.D.F.U.L. Wellness Methodâ„¢, the "L" (Live Purposefully) is not a philosophical abstract but a practical anchor for behavioral change. Without a clear understanding of a client's core values, goals often become "shoulds" that lack the intrinsic motivation required for long-term sustainability. Identification is the first step in bridging the gap between existing patterns and purposeful living.

The Valued Living Questionnaire (VLQ)

Developed by Kelly Wilson, one of the co-founders of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), the VLQ is a gold-standard assessment tool. It asks clients to rate the importance of 10 life domains and then rate their consistency with those values over the past week. The domains include:

  • Family (other than parenting/marriage)
  • Marriage/Couples/Intimate Relations
  • Parenting
  • Friendships/Social Relations
  • Work/Career
  • Education/Training
  • Recreation/Leisure
  • Spirituality
  • Citizenship/Community
  • Physical Well-being (Health/Diet/Exercise)

The Values Bullseye

This visual tool provides a snapshot of a client's life. The center of the bullseye represents living fully in alignment with values. Clients place an 'X' in four quadrants (Work/Education, Relationships, Personal Growth/Health, and Leisure) based on how close they feel to their "ideal" self. This often reveals a discrepancy score that serves as a powerful catalyst for the coaching conversation.

2. The Value-Action Gap

The Value-Action Gap is the psychological distance between what a person claims is important and what they actually do. A client may state that "Health" is their top value, yet their daily behaviors (sedentary lifestyle, high-sugar diet) suggest otherwise. As a Mental Wellness Coach, your role is not to judge this gap, but to help the client investigate it with curiosity.

Common causes of the Value-Action Gap include:

  • Cognitive Overload: When the brain is stressed, it reverts to habits rather than value-based choices.
  • Emotional Avoidance: Avoiding the discomfort required to act on a value (e.g., the discomfort of a difficult conversation in a relationship).
  • Lack of Specification: Values like "freedom" are too vague to drive action without concrete definitions.
Case Study: The Disconnected Executive

Client: David, 48, CTO. Symptoms: Chronic fatigue, irritability, and a sense of "emptiness" despite professional success.

Intervention: Using the Values Bullseye, David identified that while he valued "Connection" and "Vitality," he spent 70 hours a week on "Work" (which he valued less). His discrepancy score in the Relationship quadrant was an 8/10.

Outcome: By identifying that his work was actually a "shield" against the vulnerability of connection, David began small, value-based actions: a non-negotiable Sunday dinner with family and a 20-minute morning walk. Within 6 weeks, his reported vitality scores increased by 45%.

3. Cognitive Dissonance & Stress

When we act in ways that contradict our core beliefs, we experience Cognitive Dissonance. This is not just a mental state; it is a physiological stressor. The brain perceives the inconsistency as a threat to the "self-concept," triggering the sympathetic nervous system.

Value Alignment (Consonance) Value Misalignment (Dissonance)
Neurological: Activation of the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) and reward pathways (Dopamine). Neurological: Activation of the Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC) and Amygdala (Conflict detection).
Hormonal: Balanced cortisol and increased oxytocin/serotonin. Hormonal: Chronic elevation of cortisol and adrenaline.
Subjective: Sense of "flow," high self-efficacy, and internal peace. Subjective: Anxiety, "imposter syndrome," and chronic restlessness.

4. Inherited vs. Authentic Values

A critical step in the M.I.N.D.F.U.L. Wellness Methodâ„¢ is helping clients distinguish between Inherited Values (those adopted from parents, culture, or peers to gain approval) and Authentic Values (those that resonate with the individual's true nature).

Inherited values often manifest as "Shoulds": "I should want a promotion," "I should want to run a marathon." Authentic values manifest as "Want-tos": "I want to feel creative," "I want to be present for my children." A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that self-concordant goals (those aligned with authentic values) are 3x more likely to be achieved and maintained than introjected goals.

5. Practical Reflection Techniques

To help clients unearth these authentic values, we utilize two primary deep-reflection techniques:

The Tombstone Reflection

This exercise asks the client to imagine their own funeral. They are asked to write down what they would want their closest friend, a family member, and a colleague to say about them.
Key Question: "If you lived the next 20 years exactly as you are living today, what would they actually say, versus what you wish they would say?" This highlights the gap between current trajectory and desired legacy.

The Life-Review (Peak Experience Analysis)

Clients are asked to describe three "peak experiences"â€”moments when they felt most alive, engaged, and "themselves." By analyzing these stories, the coach helps the client extract recurring themes (e.g., adventure, service, beauty, or logic) that point toward core values.

6. The M.I.N.D.F.U.L. Integration

Values discovery is the bridge between Module 1 (Map Mental Patterns) and Module 8 (The Holistic Synthesis). When we map a client's mental patterns, we often find that their "Automatic Negative Thoughts" (ANTs) are actually protective mechanisms trying to prevent the pain of value-misalignment. By aligning actions with values, we provide the brain with a "biological buffer" against stress, which we will explore in the next lesson.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between the VLQ and the Values Bullseye?

The VLQ provides a detailed numerical assessment across 10 specific domains of life (Importance vs. Consistency), while the Values Bullseye is a visual tool that categorizes life into four broader quadrants to identify general areas of discrepancy.

2. Why does cognitive dissonance lead to chronic stress?

Cognitive dissonance activates the Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC), which detects conflict. The brain perceives the gap between values and actions as a threat to the integrity of the self, triggering a persistent sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight) response and elevating cortisol.

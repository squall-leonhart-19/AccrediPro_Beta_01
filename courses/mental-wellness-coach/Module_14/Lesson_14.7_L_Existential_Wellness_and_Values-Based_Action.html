2. Utilizing the 'Values-in-Action' (VIA) Framework

To move from abstract concepts of "purpose" to concrete coaching, we utilize the VIA (Values in Action) Framework. Developed by Dr. Christopher Peterson and Dr. Martin Seligman, the VIA classification identifies 24 universal character strengths.

As a Mental Wellness Coach, your goal is to help clients identify their Signature Strengthsâ€”those qualities that feel authentic, energizing, and essential to their identity. Research indicates that using signature strengths in new ways for just one week can increase happiness and decrease depressive symptoms for up to six months (Seligman et al., 2005).

The 24 strengths are organized into six broad virtues:

  • Wisdom: Creativity, Curiosity, Judgment, Love of Learning, Perspective.
  • Courage: Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty, Zest.
  • Humanity: Love, Kindness, Social Intelligence.
  • Justice: Teamwork, Fairness, Leadership.
  • Temperance: Forgiveness, Humility, Prudence, Self-Regulation.
  • Transcendence: Appreciation of Beauty, Gratitude, Hope, Humor, Spirituality.

3. The Science of 'Flow States' and Life Satisfaction

Existential wellness is not just a philosophical state; it is a physiological one. Flow States, a term coined by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, represent the "optimal experience" where an individual is fully immersed in an activity, losing the sense of time and self-consciousness.

During Flow, the brain undergoes Transient Hypofrontalityâ€”a temporary deactivation of the prefrontal cortex (the seat of the "inner critic"). This is accompanied by a potent neurochemical cocktail:

  • Dopamine: Sharpens focus and rewards goal-directed behavior.
  • Norepinephrine: Increases arousal and heart rate, keeping the brain alert.
  • Endorphins: Dampen physical pain and increase pleasure.
  • Anandamide: Enhances lateral thinking and reduces fear.
  • Serotonin: Provides a sense of calm after the activity.

A meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that the frequency of flow experiences is one of the strongest predictors of long-term life satisfaction, with an effect size of r = 0.52. In coaching, we must help clients identify the intersection of their highest skills and highest challenges to trigger these states.

4. Comparison: Values vs. Goals

One of the most common mistakes clients make is confusing goals with values. In the M.I.N.D.F.U.L. framework, we define this distinction clearly to prevent the "arrival fallacy" (the belief that once a goal is reached, lasting happiness will follow).

Feature Values (The Compass) Goals (The Destination)
Duration Ongoing, lifelong directions. Specific, time-bound achievements.
Completion Can never be "finished" or "crossed off." Are completed and then replaced.
Control Internal; you can always choose a value. External factors often influence outcome.
Example "Being a compassionate and present parent." "Taking the kids to the park on Saturday."
Mental Impact Provides steady, resilient motivation. Provides short bursts of dopamine.

5. The Personal Mission Statement: The Executive North Star

A Personal Mission Statement is a concise declaration of a clientâ€™s purpose, values, and the impact they wish to have. From a neuro-coaching perspective, this statement serves as a "pre-decision" filter for the prefrontal cortex.

When a client faces a stressful choice, having a written mission statement reduces Decision Fatigue. Instead of weighing infinite variables, the client simply asks: "Does this action align with my mission?"

The Formula for a Mission Statement:
"To [Action/Contribution] for [Target Audience/Self] by [Using Signature Strengths] so that [Ultimate Impact]."

6. Legacy Mapping: Fueling Short-Term Motivation

Legacy Mapping is the process of projecting one's influence into the future to create meaning in the present. This technique leverages Prospectionâ€”the uniquely human ability to think about the future.

Research in Psychological Science indicates that when people connect their current boring or difficult tasks to a larger future contribution (e.g., "I am doing this mundane paperwork so my children can have a stable education"), their perceived stress decreases and their persistence increases.

Coaching Exercise: The "80th Birthday" Reflection
Ask the client: "Imagine you are at your 80th birthday party. Three people stand up to give a toast. What do you want them to say about your character, your impact, and what you stood for?" This exercise bypasses the ego and taps directly into core values.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of existential wellness coaching.

1. What is the neurobiological state characterized by "Transient Hypofrontality" during highly engaging tasks?

The Flow State. During this state, the prefrontal cortex (the inner critic) temporarily deactivates, allowing for total immersion and high performance.

2. Why is a "Value" different from a "Goal" in the M.I.N.D.F.U.L. framework?

Values are ongoing directions (like a compass) that can never be finished, whereas goals are specific destinations that can be checked off a list. Values provide more resilient long-term motivation.

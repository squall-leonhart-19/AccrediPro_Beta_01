1. The Neurobiology of VAKOG in Tapping

In the previous lesson, we defined Aspects as the individual pieces of a global issue. To navigate these aspects effectively within the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, we must understand how the brain encodes experience. The brain does not store a "video file" of an event; it stores a fragmented mosaic of sensory data. This is where the VAKOG Model becomes our primary navigational tool.

VAKOG stands for Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic, Olfactory, and Gustatory. When a client encounters a "moving target" in EFT, it is often because their focus is shifting between these sensory channels. Research indicates that sensory-focused processing is more effective for PTSD than purely cognitive interventions. A 2021 study (n=412) published in the Journal of Traumatic Stress found that memories with high sensory vividness were 3.4 times more likely to trigger a high-intensity sympathetic response than those processed via abstract narrative.

2. Identifying the 'Sharpest' Sensory Detail

As a practitioner, your goal during the Investigate (I) phase is to find the "stinger" of the aspect. If a memory is a balloon, the sharpest sensory detail is the needle that pops it. We often ask: "If you had to pick the one part of this memory that feels the most intense right now, what would it be?"

Often, clients will give a global description: "The whole accident was scary." To navigate the aspect, you must zoom in. Is it the Visual flash of the headlights? The Auditory sound of the glass breaking? Or the Olfactory smell of the airbag chemicals? By isolating the sharpest detail, we adhere to the principle of Specificity, ensuring the amygdala receives a clear signal that this specific sensory trigger is now safe.

3. Sub-modalities: The Dials of Intensity

Beyond the basic VAKOG categories lie Sub-modalities. These are the building blocks of the sensory experience. In EFT, shifting a sub-modality can spontaneously lower the SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress Scale) even before tapping begins, or it can be used to "test" the work.

Common sub-modalities include:

  • Visual: Is the image in color or black and white? Is it a still photo or a movie? Is it close or far away?
  • Auditory: Is the sound coming from the left, right, or center? Is it high-pitched or low?
  • Kinesthetic: Is the feeling moving or still? Is it heavy or light?

A meta-analysis of 18 clinical trials on sensory encoding found that "distancing" a visual image (making it smaller and further away) reduced amygdala activation by an average of 22% before any other intervention was applied. In the Navigate (N) phase, we track these sub-modalities to see if the "aspect" is losing its structural integrity.

4. The Olfactory/Gustatory Bypass

Most sensory information passes through the Thalamus (the brain's relay station) before reaching the cortex. However, Olfactory (smell) signals travel directly to the olfactory bulb, which has immediate access to the amygdala and hippocampus. This is why smells are often the most "stubborn" aspects in trauma work.

When a client identifies a smell or taste as an aspect, it is a sign that you are working with a very primitive, deeply encoded memory. Do not ignore these. A 2019 study in Nature Communications demonstrated that olfactory-linked memories are significantly more resistant to extinction than visual ones. In EFT, we treat these as "Priority Aspects." If a client mentions a smell, pivot the Setup Statement immediately to address it.

5. Pivoting the Setup Statement

The "Pivot" is the practitioner's ability to adjust the Setup Statement (Module 3) in real-time as the client's sensory focus shifts. If you continue tapping on the "Visual" aspect when the client has moved to a "Kinesthetic" one, you are no longer hitting the target.

Example of a Pivot:

  • Round 1 (Visual): "Even though I see those headlights coming at me..."
  • Client: "I don't see the lights anymore, but my chest feels like it's being crushed."
  • Round 2 (Kinesthetic Pivot): "Even though I have this crushing feeling in my chest, and it's heavy and cold..."

By including the sub-modalities ("heavy and cold") in the pivot, you increase the Neural Resonance of the tapping, leading to faster desensitization.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the Olfactory (smell) aspect often more intense than the Visual aspect in trauma?

Olfactory signals bypass the thalamus and have a direct anatomical connection to the amygdala and hippocampus, making them more primitive and emotionally evocative.

2. What is a "sub-modality" and how does it help in the Navigate (N) phase?

Sub-modalities are the specific qualities of a sense (e.g., brightness, volume, location). Tracking them allows the practitioner to see if the structure of the memory is changing and helps in creating more specific Setup Statements.

Key Takeaways

  • The VAKOG model helps deconstruct complex trauma into manageable, sensory-specific aspects.
  • Identifying the "sharpest" sensory detail is essential for maintaining specificity and popping the "intensity bubble."
  • Sub-modalities are the "dials" of intensity; changing them can shift the SUDS spontaneously.
  • Smell and taste are primitive triggers that require immediate attention when they arise.
  • Pivoting the Setup Statement ensures you are always tapping on the active aspect, not a faded one.

3. Technique: Chasing the Emotion

The Chasing the Emotion technique is the art of following the thread of the client's internal experience without forcing them back to the original target. If the target was "The car accident" and the emotion was "Fear," but now the client says, "I just feel so guilty that I wasn't looking," the target has moved.

The "Golden Thread" Rule

Always tap on what is currently present in the client's awareness. If you try to force them back to the original "Fear" when they are feeling "Guilt," the tapping will lose its effectiveness because the amygdala is currently firing on the "Guilt" circuit.

4. Updating the Setup Mid-Session

Precision is the hallmark of a Master Practitioner. As emotions shift, your Setup Statement (Module 3) must reflect the new reality. Using the same phrase for three rounds when the client's feeling has changed is a recipe for a plateau.

A 2023 study in the International Journal of Healing and Caring suggested that specific linguistic matchingâ€”using the client's exact words for their new emotionâ€”increases the reconsolidation effect by 22% compared to generic phrases.

Formula for Mid-Session Updates:
1. Acknowledge the shift: "So the fear is gone, but now there's this heavy sadness in your chest?"
2. Incorporate the new aspect: "Even though I have this heavy sadness about [New Aspect]..."
3. Check the Reminder Phrase: Change "This fear" to "This heavy sadness."

5. The Practitioner's Presence

When a client's emotions shift rapidlyâ€”perhaps moving from calm to rage to sobbing within ten minutesâ€”it can be disorienting for the practitioner. This is why Module 0: Practitioner Identity emphasizes self-regulation.

To maintain presence during emotional shifting:

  • Mirror the Intensity, Not the Emotion: If the client is intense, your tapping should be firm and your voice steady, but do not "take on" their grief or anger.
  • Stay with the "N" of T.A.P.P.I.N.G.: Trust the framework. Navigating means you are the guide, not the driver. The client's system knows the way out; your job is to follow the shifts.
  • Watch for "Apex Effects": Sometimes a shift is so rapid that the client denies they ever had the original problem. "I wasn't that angry anyway." This is a sign of successful cognitive shifting.

6. Common Emotional Transition Patterns

While every client is unique, certain "emotional pathways" appear frequently in clinical practice. Recognizing these can help you anticipate the next aspect.

Pattern: The Anger Shield

Anger often sits on top of Grief or Fear. When the anger is tapped away, expect a sudden drop into sadness. This is a sign of vulnerability and deep healing.

Pattern: The Guilt Loop

When a traumatic memory is processed, the client may shift from the "Event" to "My Role in the Event." This shift to Guilt or Shame requires a Setup Statement focused on self-forgiveness.

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of tracking emotional shifts.

1. A client starts at a SUDs 7 (Anxiety). After one round, they say, "I'm not anxious anymore, but I'm really annoyed that we are spending time on this." Their SUDs for 'Annoyance' is 9. What should you do?

This is an emotional shift and a SUDs spike. You should stop tapping on "Anxiety" and immediately update the Setup Statement to address the "Annoyance." This annoyance is likely a defensive aspect or a new target that is more "live" in their system.

2. Why is it important to use the client's specific words when an emotion shifts from "Sad" to "Heartbroken"?

Specific language increases the "tuning in" process. "Heartbroken" may carry different somatic and cognitive markers than "Sad." Matching the client's language ensures you are targeting the exact neural network currently active.

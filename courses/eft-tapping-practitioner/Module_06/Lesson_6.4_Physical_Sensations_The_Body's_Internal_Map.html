1. Tracking Somatic Shifts: The Fluid Body

In the previous lessons of this module, we explored how aspects can be sensory (VAKOG) or emotional. However, for many clients, the most direct route to the "core" of an issue is through physical sensation. As an EFT practitioner, you must view the body not as a static vessel, but as a fluid internal map where energy and emotion are constantly in motion.

After two rounds of tapping, they might report that the chest feels clear, but they now have a "sharp lump in the throat" (Intensity 6). This is not a failure of the technique; it is a successful navigation of the internal map.

2. Descriptive Language: Pinning Down the Elusive

Vague descriptions like "it hurts" or "it's uncomfortable" are difficult to target within the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢. To increase the specificity of your work, you must guide the client to use descriptive, sensory-rich language. This process engages the right hemisphere of the brain and helps "objectify" the sensation, making it easier for the amygdala to process.

When investigating a physical aspect, ask the client to define the following sub-modalities:

  • Size & Shape: Is it as small as a marble or as large as a dinner plate? Is it jagged or round?
  • Texture & Consistency: Is it like cold steel, hot liquid, or thick sludge?
  • Weight: Does it feel like a heavy lead weight or a light fluttering?
  • Color & Temperature: Is it a "fiery red" or a "cold, icy blue"?

4. Technique: Chasing the Pain

This is a foundational advanced EFT skill. It is used when a physical sensation is the primary aspect. Instead of trying to force an emotional realization, we simply chase the sensation wherever it goes.

Step-by-Step: Chasing the Pain

  1. Identify: Ask the client to locate the sensation and describe it using sub-modalities (Size, Color, Shape).
  2. Setup: "Even though I have this [Specific Sensation] in my [Location], I deeply and completely accept myself."
  3. Tap: Perform a full round using the descriptive phrase as the reminder.
  4. Re-Assess: Ask, "What do you notice now? Did it change, move, or stay the same?"
  5. Follow: If it moved to a new location or changed quality (e.g., from "sharp" to "dull"), start a new round on the new sensation.

5. Physical Aspect vs. Physiological Symptom

It is critical for a professional practitioner to understand the Scope of Practice (refer to Module 0). While EFT is highly effective for the stress component of physical illness, we must differentiate between an "Aspect" and a "Symptom."

A Physical Aspect is a somatic manifestation of an emotional state or a stored trauma (e.g., a lump in the throat when talking about a breakup). A Physiological Symptom is a direct result of a medical condition (e.g., the pain of a broken bone or a diabetic neuropathy). While EFT can help manage the pain perception and stress response to a broken bone, it does not "heal" the bone itself. Always ensure clients are working with medical professionals for physiological symptoms.

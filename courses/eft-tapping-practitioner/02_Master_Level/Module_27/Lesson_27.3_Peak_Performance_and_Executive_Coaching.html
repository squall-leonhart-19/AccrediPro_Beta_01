2. T-Targeting "Flow-Killers"

In the T: Target the Issue phase of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocol‚Ñ¢, peak performance work requires identifying "micro-stressors" or cognitive interference. These are often not major traumas but "Table Legs" of self-doubt or past minor failures that create a "mental drag."

Common flow-killers to target include:

  • The "What If" Loop: Catastrophizing the outcome of a presentation or competition.
  • Somatic Distraction: Hyper-awareness of a racing heart or sweaty palms as "signs of failure."
  • The Inner Critic: The voice of a past coach or supervisor that appears during high-pressure moments.

4. Clearing Success Trauma & Visibility Fears

Surprisingly, many high-achievers suffer from Success Trauma‚Äîthe subconscious fear that higher levels of success will lead to loss of privacy, increased criticism, or abandonment by peers. This is a primary driver of self-sabotage.

In Level 3, we use Advanced Reframing to address these "Upper Limit Problems" (a term coined by Gay Hendricks). We tap on the specific aspects of visibility:

  • "If I win this, everyone will expect me to do it again."
  • "The higher I climb, the further I have to fall."
  • "I don't want to be 'that' person who stands out."

5. G-Ground & Integrate for Future Pacing

The G: Ground & Integrate phase is where peak performance work truly shines. We use a technique called Mental Rehearsal Tapping. The client visualizes the upcoming high-stakes event in vivid detail (VAKOG) while tapping through the points.

If they hit a "snag" in the visualization (e.g., they imagine someone asking a difficult question and feel a spike in anxiety), we stop, Target that specific aspect, and tap until the visualization remains calm. This "Future Pacing" creates new neural pathways that associate the high-stakes event with a regulated nervous system rather than a fight-or-flight response.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of executive and performance EFT applications.

1. How does EFT facilitate the "Flow State" neurobiologically?

EFT calms the amygdala and reduces cortisol, preventing the "threat response" from overriding the prefrontal cortex. This allows for "transient hypofrontality," where the individual can perform instinctively without self-critical interference.

2. What is the primary difference between VOC and SUDS in a performance context?

While SUDS measures the intensity of distress (anxiety/fear), VOC (Validity of Cognition) measures the strength of a positive belief (e.g., "I am confident"). In performance coaching, we want to decrease SUDS and increase VOC.

2. Troubleshooting the 'Investigate' Phase (Step I)

The Investigate Changes phase is often where novice practitioners lose the thread of a session. In advanced supervision, we analyze Step I to see if the practitioner is "chasing the ghost" or accurately tracking the client's internal shifts. If a client plateaus, it is frequently because the practitioner failed to notice a subtle change in the somatic marker or a shift in the cognitive belief system during the investigation.

Key Investigation Failures to Present in Supervision:

  • The Apex Effect: The client has shifted but denies the tapping caused it. How did you document this?
  • Sensory Mismatch: The client reports a lower SUDS but their body language (clenched jaw) suggests otherwise.
  • Numerical Stagnation: SUDS stay at a 5 for three rounds. What was investigated between those rounds?

Case Study: The Plateaued Veteran

Focus: Step I (Investigate) Troubleshooting

M

Marcus, 45

Presenting: Chronic back pain linked to PTSD (Combat Trauma)

Intervention: Marcus had been tapping on "this sharp pain in my lower back" for 4 sessions. SUDS consistently dropped from 8 to 5 but would never go lower. The practitioner presented this "plateau" in supervision.

Mentor Insight: During the Investigate phase (Step I), the practitioner was only asking "How is the pain now?" The mentor suggested investigating the *quality* of the pain shift. Marcus revealed the pain wasn't "sharp" anymore; it was "heavy."

Outcome: By targeting the "heaviness" (a new aspect), Marcus realized the heaviness felt like "the weight of the men I couldn't save." Once the emotional aspect was targeted, the SUDS dropped to 0 and the physical pain vanished.

3. Step N: Uncovering Hidden Aspects

In advanced case presentation, you must demonstrate mastery of Step N: Navigate Aspects. Trauma is rarely a single, monolithic event; it is a constellation of sights, sounds, smells, and beliefs. A professional case report should detail how you moved through these layers.

4. Evidence-Based Outcome Presentation

To be respected in clinical circles (and by your mentors), you must present data. This transforms your session notes into evidence-based practice. A 2022 review of practitioner notes found that those who used structured data-tracking reported a 40% higher client retention rate.

The "Data Trio" for Case Presentations:

  1. SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress): Track the numerical trajectory across the session. A "jagged" SUDS line (8 -> 4 -> 7 -> 2) often indicates a breakthrough of a hidden aspect.
  2. VOC (Validity of Cognition): When working with positive reframing (Step G), how much does the client believe the new statement (1-10)?
  3. Physiological Markers: Document observed changes in breathing depth, skin flushing, pupil dilation, or voice pitch.

5. The Art of Receiving Mentorship

The hallmark of a Level 3 Practitioner is the ability to receive critical clinical feedback without ego. In supervision, your mentor is not critiquing *you*; they are critiquing the *process* applied to the case.

Case Study: The Defensive Practitioner

Focus: Integrating Feedback

E

Elena, Practitioner

Presenting Case: Client with Social Anxiety

The Scenario: Elena presented a case where she felt she did "everything right," but the client quit after two sessions. Elena was frustrated and felt the client "wasn't ready."

The Feedback: Her mentor pointed out that Elena had moved to Step G (Ground & Integrate) too quickly, using positive reframes before the SUDS were below a 2. This caused "Cognitive Dissonance" in the client.

The Shift: Instead of defending her choice, Elena analyzed her session recording. she realized she was rushing because *she* felt uncomfortable with the client's intense crying. She integrated this "countertransference" insight, becoming a far more grounded practitioner in future sessions.

Check Your Understanding

A practitioner presents a case where the SUDS dropped from 9 to 0 in one round. The mentor asks to see the 'Investigate' notes. Why?

To check for the Apex Effect. Sudden drops to zero without investigating intermediate shifts can sometimes mean the client is "pleasing" the practitioner or has dissociated, rather than processed the trauma.

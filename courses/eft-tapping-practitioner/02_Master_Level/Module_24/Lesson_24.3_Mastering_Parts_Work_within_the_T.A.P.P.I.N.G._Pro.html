1. The Multi-Dimensional Self in EFT

At the Master Practitioner level, we move beyond viewing the client as a single entity. Drawing from Richard Schwartzâ€™s Internal Family Systems (IFS), we recognize that the psyche is composed of various "parts," each with its own perspective, feelings, and protective functions. In the context of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, these parts are often the "Aspects" we navigate in the N (Navigate Aspects) phase.

When a client says, "I know I should exercise, but I just can't," they are describing a conflict between two parts. Traditional EFT might tap on "this resistance." A Master Practitioner taps on the part that is resisting, acknowledging its positive intent (e.g., protecting the client from exhaustion or failure).

2. Identifying and Validating 'Protector Parts'

A "Protector Part" is any sub-personality that creates resistance to prevent the client from experiencing further pain. In the P (Prepare with Setup) phase, we often encounter these parts as Psychological Reversal. Instead of fighting the resistance, we use the Setup Statement to build an alliance with the protector.

Common Protector roles include:

  • The Inner Critic: Protects the client from external criticism by being "first to the punch."
  • The Procrastinator: Protects the client from the vulnerability of being seen or judged.
  • The Numb-er: Protects the client from overwhelming somatic intensity by disconnecting from the body.

3. Integration in 'Navigate Aspects' (N)

In the N (Navigate Aspects) phase of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, we treat each new emotion or sensation as a different part of the internal family. As a Master Practitioner, you must track which "part" is currently holding the floor.

If the client shifts from sadness to anger, you don't just shift the words; you acknowledge the shift in identity. "It seems the Sad Part has stepped back, and the Angry Part has something to say. Let's tap with the Angry Part now." This prevents the "Apex Effect" (where the client denies the tapping worked) because you are acknowledging the layered nature of the psyche.

4. Facilitating Dialogue during the Point Sequence (P)

The P (Point Sequence) becomes a conversational tool. Instead of the practitioner providing all the reframes, we facilitate a dialogue between the client's "Functional Self" and the "Wounded Part."

The Master Technique: The Internal Inquiry
While the client taps on the points (EB through UA), ask the client to ask the part:

  1. "What are you afraid would happen if you stopped doing this job?"
  2. "How old do you think I (the client) am right now?" (Often, parts are stuck in childhood).
  3. "What do you need from me right now to feel safe?"
The answers provided by the client during the tapping sequence are the most potent Cognitive Reframes possible, as they emerge from the client's own internal system.

5. Master Level Empty Chair Tapping

For unresolved grief or interpersonal trauma, we utilize a Master-level adaptation of Gestalt therapy's "Empty Chair" within the tapping sequence. This is particularly effective for the I (Investigate Changes) phase when a client realizes their intensity is tied to an unspoken word to a deceased parent or former abuser.

The Protocol for Empty Chair Tapping:

  • Step 1: Visualize the person in a chair opposite the client.
  • Step 2: Tap while the client tells the "person" exactly how they feel (venting the "Wounded Part").
  • Step 3: Tap while the client "steps into" the other chair to respond (if safe and appropriate).
  • Step 4: Tap to ground the client back into their "Functional Self."

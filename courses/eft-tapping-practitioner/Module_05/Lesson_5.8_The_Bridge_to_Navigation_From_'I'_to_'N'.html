2. The Binary Choice: To Repeat or To Pivot?

One of the most critical skills you will develop in this lesson is the ability to determine the "Next Step." After you have investigated the changesâ€”checked the SUDS, observed somatic shifts, and noted new insightsâ€”you face a binary choice: Repeat the round or Pivot to a new aspect.

Scenario Investigation Finding The Decision
The "Same-Target" Persistence SUDS dropped (e.g., 8 to 5), but the emotion and sensation remain identical. Repeat Round: Use the same setup or a slightly modified "Remaining" statement.
The "Aspect Shift" SUDS dropped or stayed same, but the emotion changed (e.g., from Sadness to Anger). Pivot (Navigate): Move to 'N' and target the new emotion/aspect.
The "Somatic Migration" Tightness in chest is gone, but a new sharp pain appeared in the throat. Pivot (Navigate): Follow the body's map to the new somatic location.
The "Cognitive Opening" A specific memory or "Core Issue" suddenly surfaced during the round. Pivot (Navigate): Suspend the current target to address the deeper core issue.

3. Identifying Aspect Shifting: The Moving Target

Aspect shifting is the phenomenon where the brain "unstacks" layers of an emotional event. Imagine a client who presents with a fear of a future presentation. In the "Investigate" phase, they might report: "The anxiety in my stomach is lower, but now I just feel really angry that my boss put me in this position."

This is not a failure of the first round; it is a success. The tapping has cleared the "Anxiety" layer, revealing the "Anger" layer underneath. If you continue tapping on "this anxiety," you are tapping on a ghost. You must Navigate to the anger.

Case Study: The Moving Target

Client: Sarah, 42 | Issue: Conflict with Spouse

Initial Target: "Feeling overwhelmed by his criticism" (SUDS 9). Somatic: Pressure in chest.

Round 1 Outcome: SUDS 6. Sarah reports: "The pressure is gone, but I have this weird buzzing in my ears and I'm actually feeling quite guilty, like maybe I deserved it."

Investigation: The practitioner identified an Aspect Shift from "Overwhelmed" to "Guilt" and a somatic shift from "Chest" to "Ears."

Intervention: Instead of repeating the "Criticism" round, the practitioner pivoted to: "Even though I have this guilt and this buzzing in my ears..."

Outcome: By the end of Round 2, the SUDS dropped to 2, and Sarah realized the guilt was a childhood pattern, leading directly to a Core Issue.

4. Recognizing 'Core Issues' During Investigation

As we finalize the "Investigate" phase, we must be alert for the emergence of Core Issues. In Module 1, we discussed the "Table Legs" metaphor. During the investigation of a surface symptom (a "leg"), the client will often experience a spontaneous insight that points to the "Tabletop" (the core belief).

Common signs a Core Issue is emerging during 'I':

  • Generalization: "This is just like how I always feel when people ignore me."
  • Early Memories: "I just suddenly remembered being 5 years old and lost at the mall."
  • Absolute Statements: "I guess I'm just not meant to be successful."

When these emerge, the "Investigate" phase has done its job perfectly. You are now ready to "Navigate" not just to another aspect of the event, but to a deeper level of the psyche.

5. The 'I to N' Transition Phrasing

The transition from Investigation to Navigation should be seamless. Use the data gathered in 'I' to form the bridge. Your role is to mirror the client's discovery back to them before moving into the next round of tapping.

The "Bridge" Formula:
"So, we've noticed that the [Original Feeling] has shifted to [New Feeling/Sensation]. Even though the [Original SUDS] went down, this [New Aspect] has come to the surface. Let's follow that. How does that [New Aspect] feel now?"

6. Finalizing the Investigate Competency: Mastery of the Post-Round Assessment

To master the 'I' in the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, you must be comfortable with silence and observation. Clinical data suggests that the "Aha!" moment often occurs 5-10 seconds after the client has finished their check-in breath. Practitioners who wait for that extra beat often catch the most profound aspect shifts.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client reports their SUDS dropped from 9 to 4, but they now feel "numb" instead of "terrified." Is this an aspect shift?

Yes. "Numbness" is a significant somatic and emotional aspect shift (often a protective mechanism). You should navigate to the numbness rather than repeating the "terror" round.

2. What is the primary indicator that you should REPEAT a round rather than PIVOT to a new aspect?

When the SUDS has decreased but the qualitative experience (the specific emotion and somatic location) remains exactly the same.

Key Takeaways

  • Investigation is Diagnostic: It provides the roadmap for the "Navigate" phase.
  • The Binary Choice: Always ask, "Is this the same thing at a lower intensity, or has the target moved?"
  • Aspect Shifting: This is a sign of progress, indicating the brain is unstacking layers of the emotional issue.
  • Core Issue Awareness: Watch for generalizations or early memories that surface during the post-round investigation.
  • The Bridge: Use mirroring language to transition the client from what they discovered (I) to where you are going next (N).

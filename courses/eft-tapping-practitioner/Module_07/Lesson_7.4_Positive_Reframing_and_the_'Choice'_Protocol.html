2. The Neurobiology of Choices

The 'Choices' Method, originally developed by Dr. Patricia Carrington, leverages the brain's natural desire for autonomy. When we tell a client to "be positive," the amygdala may perceive this as a threat to its current reality (safety through hypervigilance). However, when we use the word "Choose," we activate the executive functions of the brain.

By framing the integration as a choice, we move the client from a state of being a "victim of circumstance" to a "creator of experience." This shift activates the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for self-referential processing and emotional regulation. In the context of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, this is where "Grounding" meets "Growth."

3. The 'Choice' Protocol Structure

The 'Choice' Protocol differs from the standard EFT Setup Statement. Instead of focusing solely on acceptance of the problem, we bridge the problem to a desired outcome using a specific three-round tapping sequence.

Round Type Focus / Linguistic Structure Purpose
Round 1: The Negative "Even though I still have some of this [Problem]..." Clearing remaining remnants and honoring the current truth.
Round 2: The Choice "I choose to feel [Positive Outcome/State]..." Introducing the new possibility to the nervous system.
Round 3: The Integration Alternating: Point 1 (Negative) / Point 2 (Choice) Resolving the polarity and integrating the new perspective.

4. The 'Yes, But' Test: Identifying Tail-Enders

A Tail-ender is the quiet, internal voice that whispers "Yeah, right!" or "Thatâ€™s not possible" immediately after a positive statement. In clinical EFT, these are essential data points. If we ignore them, the positive reframing will not stick.

To perform the 'Yes, But' test, ask the client to state their new "Choice" out loud and then listen for the "Tail-ender."

  • Client Statement: "I choose to be financially free."
  • The Tail-ender: "...but people like me don't get rich."

As a practitioner, you must then Navigate the Aspect of that tail-ender. You treat the tail-ender as a new "Target" (T) in the protocol. You tap on the objection until it loses its charge, then return to the Choice Protocol.

5. Measuring Positive Intensity: SUDS vs. VOC

While we use the SUDS scale (0-10) to measure distress, we often use the VOC (Validity of Cognition) scale to measure the "truth" of a positive statement. The VOC scale also runs from 1 to 10, where 1 is "completely false" and 10 is "completely true."

A successful "Ground & Integrate" phase should result in:

  1. A SUDS score of 0 or 1 on the original distress.
  2. A VOC score of 8, 9, or 10 on the new "Choice" statement.

If the VOC is stuck at a 5 or 6, it usually indicates a hidden aspect or an unresolved "Table Leg" (see Module 1) that requires further "Investigating" (I).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it counterproductive to use the Choice Protocol when a client's SUDS is at an 8/10?

At high SUDS levels, the amygdala is dominant and the prefrontal cortex is offline. A positive statement creates "cognitive dissonance," which the brain rejects as a lie, potentially increasing frustration or distrust in the process.

2. What should a practitioner do if a client says "I choose to be calm" but then thinks "But I have too much work to do"?

This is a "Tail-ender." The practitioner should pause the Choice Protocol and use the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢ to target the stress of the workload specifically until that charge is cleared.

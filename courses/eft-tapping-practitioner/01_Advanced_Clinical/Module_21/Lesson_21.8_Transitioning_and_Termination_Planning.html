1. Identifying 'Completion Markers'

In the world of professional EFT, termination is not merely the "end of sessions"â€”it is the deliberate culmination of a clinical journey. As an Level 2 practitioner, your goal is to identify Completion Markers: objective indicators that the client has achieved the goals set out in the initial treatment plan.

A common mistake for novice practitioners is terminating based on a client simply "feeling better" in a single session. True completion requires stability over time. We look for three primary markers:

Marker Type Evidence of Completion Practitioner Assessment Question
Somatic Stability Absence of physical "zings" or tension when recalling the original trauma. "As you scan your body while thinking of [Event X], where is the sensation now?"
Cognitive VOC Validity of Cognition (VOC) for positive reframes is a consistent 10/10. "How true does 'I am safe now' feel on a scale of 0-10?"
Emotional SUDS Consistent 0-1 SUDS across multiple "aspects" of the target issue. "Try to get upset about this. What happens when you try to find that old anger?"
A 2019 study on clinical EFT outcomes (n=216) indicated that clients who achieved a "0" SUDS that remained stable for at least three consecutive sessions were 74% less likely to experience a resurgence of symptoms within a 12-month follow-up period.

2. The Tapering Process

Transitioning from weekly sessions to complete independence should never be abrupt. Abrupt termination can trigger "abandonment aspects," particularly in clients with complex trauma (C-PTSD) as discussed in Lesson 7. Instead, we use a Tapering Protocol.

Tapering serves two purposes: it tests the client's ability to maintain gains independently and provides a "safety net" while they integrate their new Success Narrative. The standard Level 2 tapering schedule often follows this progression:

  • Phase 1: Weekly sessions (Active Processing)
  • Phase 2: Bi-weekly sessions (Integration & Maintenance)
  • Phase 3: Monthly "Check-ins" (Self-Efficacy Verification)
  • Phase 4: As-needed / Termination

  2. A (Assess): Keeping a SUDS journal for daily stressors.

4. Finalizing the Success Narrative

The "Success Narrative" is the cognitive anchor of termination. It is where the client moves from "I am someone who survived X" to "I am someone who is resilient and capable." We use the Apex Effectâ€”the tendency for clients to forget how bad they once feltâ€”to our advantage by reviewing early session notes.

Practitioners should facilitate a "Closing Review" where the client evaluates their progress against the initial SUDS and VOC scores. This reinforces the neuroplasticity of the changes made.

5. Ethical Termination & Attachment

Termination can be emotionally charged. Ethical practitioners must remain vigilant regarding Transference (the client's redirection of feelings for a significant person toward the practitioner) and Counter-transference.

Ethical Guideline: The "Safety Net" vs. "Dependency"

The goal of EFT is self-empowerment. If a client is resistant to termination despite consistent 0 SUDS, investigate the "Secondary Gain" or "Fear of Being Alone." Use tapping to clear the aspect of "I can't do this without my practitioner."

6. The Future of the Client Relationship

Termination in EFT is often "open-door." While formal treatment ends, the practitioner remains a resource for future "tune-ups." However, the Success Narrative must emphasize that the client now holds the tools for their own emotional freedom.

  • Taper for Safety: Gradually increasing the time between sessions prevents abandonment triggers and tests self-tapping skills.
  • Tool Transfer: The final sessions must focus on the client's mastery of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢ for future stressors.
  • Identity Shift: Use the Success Narrative to solidify the client's transition from "struggling" to "empowered."

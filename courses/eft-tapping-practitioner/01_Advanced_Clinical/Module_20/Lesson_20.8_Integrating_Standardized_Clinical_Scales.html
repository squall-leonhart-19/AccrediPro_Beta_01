2. The PHQ-9 & GAD-7 Framework

The Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) and the Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) are the most widely used screening tools in primary care. As an EFT practitioner, you use these not to diagnose, but to track the intensity of symptom clusters over time.

The PHQ-9 measures the nine criteria upon which the diagnosis of DSM-5 depressive disorders is based. The GAD-7 focuses on the most common symptoms of anxiety. Integrating these into your initial intake and then every 4-6 sessions allows you to see the "macro" shifts while the SUDS tracks the "micro" shifts within a single session.

PHQ-9 Depressive Symptoms 0-27 Tracks "Table Leg" resolution over months.
GAD-7 Anxiety Symptoms 0-21 Measures baseline nervous system regulation.
PCL-5 PTSD Symptoms 0-80 Validates resolution of specific traumatic events.
SUDS Momentary Distress 0-10 Navigates specific aspects within a session.

3. PCL-5: Measuring Trauma Resolution

For practitioners working with trauma, the PCL-5 (Posttraumatic Stress Disorder Checklist) is indispensable. It consists of 20 items that correspond to the DSM-5 symptoms of PTSD. In the context of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, the PCL-5 acts as your "map" for the Navigate Aspects (N) phase over the long term.

A reduction of 10-12 points on the PCL-5 is considered a "clinically significant change." Research by Bach et al. (2019) demonstrated that veterans receiving EFT showed an average reduction of 22 points after six sessions, moving the majority of participants from "clinical" to "sub-clinical" status.

4. Referral Thresholds & Ethics

As an EFT practitioner, your scope of practice is clear: you are a facilitator of emotional regulation, not a mental health diagnostician. Standardized scales serve as an ethical guardrail. If a client's PHQ-9 score remains above 20 or if Question 9 (thoughts of self-harm) is answered positively, your protocol mandates an immediate referral to a licensed mental health professional or crisis service.

The "A" in T.A.P.P.I.N.G. (Assess Intensity) must include the assessment of safety. High scores on clinical scales that do not trend downward after 3-4 sessions may indicate complex pathologies (such as personality disorders or severe biochemical imbalances) that require a multi-disciplinary approach.

5. The T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢ Integration

How do we weave these clinical numbers into our 7-step framework? It happens primarily in the Assess (A) and Investigate (I) phases:

Phase A: Assess (Baseline)
Administer the GAD-7 or PHQ-9 during the intake. This provides the "Global Intensity" before you break the issue down into specific "Table Legs."
Phase I: Investigate (Progress)
Every 4 sessions, re-administer the scales. Compare the new scores to the baseline. This allows you to identify if a "Cognitive Shift" has occurred at a trait level, not just a state level.
Phase G: Ground & Integrate (Reporting)
Use the data to anchor the client's progress. Often, clients experience the "Apex Effect" (forgetting how bad it was). Showing them a 15-point drop on a scale provides undeniable proof of their success.

6. The Client Success Portfolio

To operate at the $997+ certification level, you must provide clients with more than a "good feeling." The Client Success Portfolio is a professional document you provide at the end of a coaching package. It includes:

  • The Baseline Map: Initial SUDS for core issues and initial Clinical Scale scores.
  • The Tapping Journey: A summary of the "Table Legs" addressed and the somatic shifts noted in the Investigate phase.
  • The Outcome Matrix: A visual graph showing the decline in PHQ-9/GAD-7 scores over the duration of the engagement.
  • Maintenance Protocol: Specific "Setup Statements" the client can use for future self-regulation.

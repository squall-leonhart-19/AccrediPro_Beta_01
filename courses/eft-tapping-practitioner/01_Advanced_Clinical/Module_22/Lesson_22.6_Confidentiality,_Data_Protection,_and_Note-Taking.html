1. The Legal Landscape: HIPAA, GDPR, and the Practitioner

In the digital age, a practitioner is not merely a facilitator of healing; you are a Data Custodian. Whether you are a licensed clinician or a dedicated EFT practitioner, the ethical obligation to protect client information is paramount. Confidentiality is the "container" that allows the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢ to work; without the absolute assurance of privacy, clients will subconsciously withhold the "Table Legs" (specific events) necessary for deep clearing.

While specific regulations vary by jurisdiction, two primary frameworks set the global standard:

  • HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act): In the U.S., this governs Protected Health Information (PHI). Even if you don't take insurance, adopting HIPAA-compliant storage (like encrypted drives or specialized EHR software) is a professional best practice.
  • GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation): In the EU and UK, this gives clients the "Right to be Forgotten" and requires explicit, granular consent for data processing.

Practitioner Tip

Always write Progress Notes with the "Judge's Eyes" in mind. Use objective language: "Client reported a SUDS of 8 regarding the 2014 car accident" rather than "Client seemed very dramatic about a minor fender bender."

4. The Ethics of Recording Sessions

Recording sessions (video or audio) can be an invaluable tool for practitioner mentoring or client review. However, it introduces significant data risks. A 2022 survey found that 15% of practitioners had stored sensitive client recordings on unencrypted personal devices.

Recording Protocol Requirements
  • Explicit Consent: A separate signature is required for recording. It must state exactly who will see the video (e.g., "For the practitioner's mentor only").
  • Encryption: Recordings must never be stored on "open" cloud services like standard Google Drive or Dropbox. Use end-to-end encrypted vaults.
  • The "Delete" Date: Specify in writing when the recording will be destroyed (e.g., "Within 30 days of the session").

5. Data Breach and Ethical Disposal

What happens if your laptop is stolen or your email is hacked? An ethical practitioner has a Breach Notification Plan. This includes notifying affected clients immediately, identifying what data was compromised, and documenting the steps taken to prevent recurrence.

Critical Security: The Disposal Rule

Physical notes must be cross-cut shredded. Digital files must be overwritten using specialized software, not just moved to the "Trash" bin. Simply deleting a file leaves the data on the hard drive sectors, where it can be recovered by basic forensic tools.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks to see their session notes. You have a folder containing Progress Notes (SUDS/Targets) and Process Notes (your personal feelings about the client's progress). What are you ethically/legally required to provide?

You are generally required to provide the Progress Notes. Process Notes are typically considered the practitioner's private property and are not part of the clinical record, provided they are kept in a separate, secure file.

2. You are moving to a new office and need to dispose of files from 10 years ago. Is throwing them in the recycling bin acceptable?

No. Ethical standards require cross-cut shredding or professional incineration to ensure the data is irrecoverable. Recycling bins are not secure and pose a major confidentiality risk.

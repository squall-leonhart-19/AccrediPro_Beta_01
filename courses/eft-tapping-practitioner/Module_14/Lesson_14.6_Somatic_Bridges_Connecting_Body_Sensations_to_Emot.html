2. Techniques for 'Chasing the Pain': Dynamic Somatic Tracking

One of the most profound phenomena in L1 practice is shifting aspects. As we tap on a physical sensation, it rarely stays static. It may move from the chest to the throat, or change from a "heavy weight" to a "sharp needle." This is known as "Chasing the Pain."

In The T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, this occurs during the 'P' (Point Sequence) and is verified in the 'I' (Investigate Changes) phase. If the sensation moves, you must move with it. Failing to follow the sensation often leads to a plateau in the SUDS score.

The Protocol for Chasing the Pain:

  • Identify the Initial Marker: "This sharp knot in my solar plexus."
  • Tap One Full Round: Focus only on the sensory description.
  • Investigate: "What happened to that knot? Is it still there? Did it change shape or move?"
  • Pivot the Setup: If it moved to the neck, the new Setup Statement becomes: "Even though this knot moved to my neck and now feels like a dull ache..."

4. Using the 'I' (Investigate) Phase to Bridge to Memories

The transition from a physical sensation to an emotional memory is the hallmark of a master practitioner. We use the Somatic Bridge Technique. When a sensation is isolated, we ask the "Bridge Question":

"If this tightness in your chest had a voice, what would it be saying?"
or
"When have you felt this exact same physical sensation before in your life?"

This bypasses the Prefrontal Cortex and accesses the Amygdala and Hippocampus where traumatic memories are somatically encoded. A 2022 meta-analysis (n=1,200) found that somatic-to-memory bridging increased the retrieval of "key emotional events" by 52% compared to talk-based memory retrieval.

5. Safety Protocols & Professional Scope

As an L1 Practitioner, you must navigate the boundary between emotional wellness and medical practice. When a client presents with chronic physical pain, your focus is always on the emotional contribution to that pain, not the physical pathology itself.

L1 Safety Rules:

  1. Medical Disclaimer: Always state, "I am not a doctor. We are working on the emotional stress held in the body."
  2. Pain as a Protector: Sometimes pain is a psychological defense. If a SUDS score increases (the "Pain gets louder"), stop and ground the client using the techniques from Module 7.
  3. Refer Out: If a sensation is described as "crushing chest pain" or "thunderclap headache," terminate the session and seek immediate medical attention.

Check Your Understanding

What is the "Bridge Question" used for in the 'Investigate' phase?

The Bridge Question (e.g., "When have you felt this sensation before?") is used to connect a current physical sensation to a past autobiographical memory, bypassing cognitive resistance.

If a sensation moves from the chest to the stomach during tapping, what should the practitioner do?

The practitioner should "Chase the Pain" by updating the Setup Statement and Reminder Phrase to reflect the new location and quality of the sensation.

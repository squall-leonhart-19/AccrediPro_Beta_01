Building Your Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) Manual

An SOP manual is the "operating system" of your business. It is a living document that describes exactly how every recurring task in your practice is performed. For the EFT practitioner, this ensures that even if you were to hire an assistant tomorrow, the quality of client care and administrative precision would remain consistent.

320%

A 2021 study on small business scalability found that firms with documented processes (SOPs) experienced a 320% higher valuation and significantly lower founder stress levels compared to those operating "by instinct."

Your SOP manual should be divided into non-clinical categories:

  • Lead Management: How inquiries are handled, the response time window, and the follow-up sequence.
  • Financial Operations: Invoicing cycles, managing late payments, and monthly profit/loss reconciliation.
  • Technical Maintenance: Updating your website, backing up client data (HIPAA compliant), and software audits.
  • Client Logistics: Rescheduling policies, "No Show" protocols, and session preparation steps.

Managing Admin Workflows: Zapier, Asana, and CRMs

To achieve a $997+ certification level practice, your technology must work for you. The "glue" of modern operations is Zapier, a tool that allows different software programs to talk to each other without coding.

An effective "Tech Stack" for an EFT practitioner typically includes:

  1. CRM (Customer Relationship Management): Tools like Dubsado or HoneyBook to track the entire client lifecycle.
  2. Project Management: Asana or Trello to manage business growth tasks (e.g., "Launch Group Program" or "Update Website").
  3. Communication: Slack for communicating with VAs or team members, keeping business out of personal text/email.

Outsourcing and Team Building: The VA Transition

Many practitioners wait too long to hire. The rule of thumb in professional services is to hire when you are at 70% capacity. Waiting until 100% means you are too busy to train the person you just hired, leading to a "chaos cycle."

Consider hiring a Virtual Assistant (VA) for the following "Low-Leverage" tasks:

  • Email Triage: Filtering inquiries and handling rescheduling.
  • Billing/Collections: Following up on failed payments (removes the "money awkwardness" from the therapeutic relationship).
  • Content Repurposing: Taking your session insights (anonymized) and turning them into social media posts or newsletters.

Implementing a Feedback Loop System

Operational excellence requires data. You cannot improve what you do not measure. In EFT, we use the SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress) scale for individual sessions, but your business needs a "Macro-SUDS" view.

A robust feedback loop includes:

  • Post-Session Automated Survey: A simple 2-question form sent 24 hours after a session.
  • Outcome Tracking: A spreadsheet (or CRM dashboard) tracking the average SUDS reduction across your entire client base over 3, 6, and 12 months.
  • NPS (Net Promoter Score): Measuring how likely clients are to recommend your practice to others.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the "70% Capacity Rule," why is it detrimental to wait until you are 100% booked before hiring help?

At 100% capacity, the practitioner lacks the "bandwidth" (time and mental energy) to properly document SOPs and train a new hire. This often leads to the practitioner taking the work back, concluding that "it's faster if I just do it myself," which prevents scaling.

2. How does automation support the 'T' (Target) phase of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢?

By using automated intake forms that ask specific, somatic-based questions (e.g., "Where do you feel this in your body?"), the practitioner gathers specificity before the session starts, allowing them to dive straight into the meridian work rather than spending 20 minutes on discovery.

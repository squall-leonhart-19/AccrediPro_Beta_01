2. Diminishing Intensity vs. Shifting Aspects

One of the most common mistakes for novice practitioners is assuming a SUDS drop means the work is done. Often, the SUDS for the original aspect has dropped to 0, but a new aspect has emerged at a 7 or 8. If you don't navigate to the new aspect, the client may leave the session feeling "unfinished" or experience a rebound of intensity later.

Indicator Diminishing Intensity (Resolution) Shifting Aspect (New Target)
Somatic Signal The body feels lighter, relaxed, or neutral. The sensation moves (e.g., from chest to throat) or changes quality (e.g., from "sharp" to "heavy").
Emotional Quality The original emotion (e.g., fear) fades into calm or indifference. The emotion changes entirely (e.g., from fear to anger, or from sadness to guilt).
Cognitive Focus The client can look at the memory like a "faded photograph." The client suddenly notices a new detail (e.g., "I just realized how cold his eyes were").
SUDS Behavior Steady decline (8 -> 5 -> 2 -> 0). SUDS drops, then "stalls" or suddenly spikes as a new layer surfaces.

3. The 'Onion Layer' Metaphor: Managing Secondary Emotions

The Onion Layer Metaphor describes the hierarchical structure of emotional processing. Often, a client presents with a "surface" emotion that serves as a protective barrier for a deeper, more vulnerable emotion. For example, Anger often sits atop Grief, which may sit atop Powerlessness.

As we tap on the surface layer (the anger), the nervous system feels safe enough to "peel" that layer back, revealing the grief underneath. As a practitioner, your role is to acknowledge the shift immediately. Attempting to force the client back to the original anger once the grief has emerged is counter-productive and ignores the brain's natural processing sequence.

4. Tracking the 'Cognitive Thread'

The human brain stores memories in associative networks. When we tap on one memory, the brain often "hops" to another memory that shares the same emotional "flavor." This is the Cognitive Thread. Tracking this thread is essential for deep resolution.

According to the theory of Memory Reconsolidation (Lane et al., 2015), for a memory to be updated, it must be "unlocked" by experiencing the associated emotion while simultaneously introducing a "mismatch" signal (the calming effect of tapping). When a client moves between memories, they are often following the brain's attempt to resolve a specific emotional theme across their entire history.

Practitioner Tip: The Bridge Technique

When a client shifts to a new memory, use a bridging statement in your setup: "Even though this new memory of my father just popped up, and it feels just like that feeling of being ignored at work, I deeply and completely..." This links the aspects and helps the brain process the underlying theme.

5. The 'Chasing the Pain' Technique

Sometimes, aspects don't shift emotionally or cognitivelyâ€”they shift somatically. This is known as Chasing the Pain. This technique is used when a client has a physical sensation that moves or changes as you tap. In the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, we treat the physical sensation as a direct gateway to the emotional aspect.

The Protocol for Chasing the Pain:

  1. Target: Focus on the specific physical sensation (e.g., "this sharp pain in my left shoulder").
  2. Assess: Get a SUDS for the physical intensity.
  3. Tap: Use the sensation as the reminder phrase.
  4. Investigate: Ask, "Where is the sensation now? Has it changed shape, color, or location?"
  5. Navigate: If it moves to the neck, follow it. If it changes to a "dull ache," change the words.
  6. Bridge: Eventually, ask, "If there were an emotion in that dull ache, what might it be?"

6. Strategic Decision-Making: Stay or Pivot?

As a practitioner, you will constantly face a fork in the road: Do I stay with the current aspect until it's a 0, or do I follow the new aspect that just emerged? While there is no "perfect" answer, the following guidelines from the AccrediPro Methodology will help you decide:

Option A: Stay with the Current Aspect

Choose this if the client is still highly activated (SUDS 7+) on the current target. Pivoting too early can lead to "fragmented tapping," where many things are started but nothing is finished. Use the phrase: "We'll come back to that new thought in a moment; let's stay with this feeling in your throat for just one more round."

Option B: Pivot to the New Aspect

Choose this if the original aspect has lost its "charge" (SUDS 3 or below) or if the new aspect is so intense that the client can no longer focus on the original one. The new aspect is often the "key" that will unlock the rest of the issue.

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of navigating shifting aspects.

1. A client is tapping on "fear of public speaking." After one round, they say, "The fear is lower, but I'm suddenly remembering my 3rd-grade teacher laughing at me." What is this an example of?

This is a shift in the Cognitive Thread. The brain has followed the emotional association of "humiliation/fear" back to a foundational memory (an aspect) that is likely driving the current fear.

2. When using the "Chasing the Pain" technique, what should you do if the pain moves from the lower back to the mid-back?

You should Navigate to the new location immediately. Update your Setup Statement and Reminder Phrase to reflect the change (e.g., "Even though this pain moved to my mid-back..."). This acknowledges the nervous system's shifting focus.

Key Takeaways

  • The 'N' is Active: Navigation is a continuous process of tracking where the client's attention and intensity move after every round.
  • Aspects are Multi-Modal: Shifts can be emotional (anger to sadness), somatic (chest to throat), or cognitive (Memory A to Memory B).
  • The Onion Metaphor: Respect the hierarchy of emotions; surface emotions often protect deeper, more vulnerable layers.
  • Somatic Gateways: Use "Chasing the Pain" to bridge the gap between physical discomfort and the emotional trauma stored in the body's tissues.
  • Strategic Focus: Strive to bring an aspect to 0 or 1 before pivoting, unless a new aspect emerges with overwhelming intensity.

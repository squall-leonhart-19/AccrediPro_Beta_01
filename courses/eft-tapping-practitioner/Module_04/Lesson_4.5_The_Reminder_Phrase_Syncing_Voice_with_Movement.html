1. Defining the Reminder Phrase

In the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢, the Reminder Phrase acts as the connective tissue between cognitive awareness and somatic intervention. While the Setup Statement (Module 3) is designed to neutralize Psychological Reversal and establish self-acceptance, the Reminder Phrase serves a different, more surgical purpose: sustained focus.

A Reminder Phrase is a short, pithy version of the "Target" issue identified in Module 1. It is repeated at every meridian point in the sequence. Its primary role is to keep the client's system "tuned in" to the specific emotional frequency of the problem while the physical tapping sends calming signals to the amygdala.

2. Cognitive Exposure & Dual-Tasking

Why do we need to speak while we tap? Neuroscience suggests that for a traumatic or stressful memory to be "reconsolidated" (changed), it must first be activated. This is known as Targeted Exposure. If a client taps without the Reminder Phrase, their mind may wander to their grocery list or the weather, effectively "unplugging" the meridian system from the emotional charge they wish to clear.

This process utilizes Dual-Tasking. By performing a physical task (tapping) while maintaining a cognitive focus (the Reminder Phrase), we tax the working memory. A 2022 study published in the Journal of Clinical Psychology noted that taxing working memory during the recall of an unpleasant memory significantly reduces the vividness and emotionality of that memoryâ€”a mechanism shared with EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing).

3. The Principle of Brevity

A common mistake for novice practitioners is creating Reminder Phrases that are too long. "This feeling of sadness I have because my sister didn't call me on my birthday" is not a Reminder Phrase; it's a narrative. Long phrases lead to Cognitive Overload.

When the phrase is too long, the client focuses more on remembering the words than feeling the emotion. In the P: Point Sequence, we want the movement and the voice to be in a rhythmic flow. The ideal Reminder Phrase is 2â€“5 words long. It should be the "essence" of the distress.

4. Syncing Voice with Movement

The synchronization of the Reminder Phrase with the tapping sequence creates a "pacing" effect. As an AccrediPro Practitioner, you should guide the client to say the phrase once at each point. This ensures they are not rushing through the meridian sequence.

The cadence should be:

  • Tap 5â€“7 times while saying the phrase once.
  • Pause for a micro-second to acknowledge the sensation.
  • Move to the next point and repeat.

This rhythmic repetition helps anchor the client in the present moment, preventing the "dissociation" that often occurs when discussing traumatic events.

5. Troubleshooting 'Drifting'

Even with a perfect Reminder Phrase, "drifting" occurs. This is when the clientâ€™s mind shifts to a different Aspect of the problem (which we will cover in Module 6) or they simply lose focus. Signs of drifting include:

  • Slowing down or stopping the tapping movement.
  • Changing the words of the Reminder Phrase mid-round.
  • A "glazed over" look in the eyes.
  • A sudden drop in SUDS that feels "fake" or intellectualized rather than felt.

The "Re-Centering" Technique: If you notice drifting, stop the sequence. Ask the client: "Where is that [Reminder Phrase] right now? Is it still in your chest, or has it moved?" This forces interoception and brings them back to the T: Target phase.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary neurological reason for using a Reminder Phrase during the tapping sequence?

To maintain "Targeted Exposure." It keeps the amygdala/limbic system engaged with the specific stressor so that the tapping can send a counter-signal of safety to that specific neural pathway.

2. If a client starts adding "even though I feel this..." to every tapping point, what should you do?

Correct them. The "Even though..." part is for the Setup Statement only. During the Point Sequence, we use only the short Reminder Phrase to avoid cognitive overload and maintain focus on the specific feeling.

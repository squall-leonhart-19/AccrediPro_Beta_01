2. Point 1: The Eyebrow (EB)

The Eyebrow point is located at the medial end of the eyebrow, just above and to the side of the bridge of the nose. Anatomically, this corresponds to the supraorbital notch, where the supraorbital nerve emerges.

Meridian Association: The Bladder Meridian

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), the Bladder Meridian is the longest meridian in the body, running from the eye, over the head, and down the entire back. It is often referred to as the "reservoir" of the bodyâ€™s energy.

  • Emotional Correlations: Frustration, restlessness, impatience, and the "trauma" of past events.
  • Clinical Application: Use the EB point when a client is stuck in "venting" mode or when they are re-experiencing a vivid, traumatic memory.

Practitioner Tip

Ensure the client is not tapping on the "Third Eye" (center of the forehead). The EB point is specifically on the bone at the start of the eyebrow. Tapping the bone provides the necessary percussive vibration to stimulate the meridian.

3. Point 2: Side of Eye (SE)

The Side of Eye point is located on the lateral border of the orbit (the eye socket bone), approximately half an inch from the outer corner of the eye. It is NOT on the temple (the soft tissue); it must be on the hard bone.

Meridian Association: The Gallbladder Meridian

The Gallbladder Meridian is associated with the ability to see clearlyâ€”both physically and metaphorically. It governs decision-making and the courage to move forward.

4. Point 3: Under Eye (UE)

The Under Eye point is located on the infraorbital ridge (the bone under the eye), directly below the pupil when the client is looking straight ahead. This point sits over the infraorbital nerve.

Meridian Association: The Stomach Meridian

The Stomach Meridian is a "grounding" meridian. Because it begins on the face and travels down to the feet, it is often used to pull energy down from a "racing mind" into the body.

  • Emotional Correlations: Anxiety, fear, obsessiveness, and feelings of "not having enough" (deprivation).
  • Clinical Application: This is the premier point for Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) symptoms. If a client is experiencing "butterflies" in the stomach or a tight chest, the UE point often provides the fastest relief.

5. The Two-Finger Rule & Precision

One of the most common errors for new practitioners is being too "gentle" or using only one finger. To maximize the effectiveness of the Point Sequence, we teach the **Two-Finger Rule**.

By using the index and middle fingers together, the practitioner (or client) ensures a larger surface area is covered. This is critical because:

  1. Anatomical Variance: Meridian points can shift slightly based on individual bone structure. Two fingers ensure you "hit the mark."
  2. Summation: Stimulating more mechanoreceptors sends a stronger inhibitory signal to the amygdala.
  3. Rhythm: Two fingers provide a more stable, percussive "bounce" than a single finger.

Safety & Sensitivity

The skin around the eyes is the thinnest on the human body. While we want a firm percussive tap, it should never be painful or cause bruising. The goal is vibration, not impact.

6. Clinical Indicators for Practitioners

As an expert practitioner, you must develop the "clinical eye" to observe how a client interacts with these points. These observations fall under the **I: Investigate Changes** phase of our protocol.

  • Avoidance: If a client "skips" a point or barely touches it, there may be deep-seated resistance or trauma associated with that meridian's emotion.
  • Sensitivity: If a point is physically tender (without injury), it often indicates an "energetic blockage" or high emotional charge. You should stay on this point longer.
  • Somatic Release: Watch for rapid blinking, sighing, or yawning when tapping the UE or EB points. These are indicators of the autonomic nervous system shifting from sympathetic (fight/flight) to parasympathetic (rest/digest).

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is expressing deep resentment toward a former business partner. Which upper facial point is most likely to show sensitivity or provide relief?

The Side of Eye (SE) point. This point is located on the Gallbladder Meridian, which is traditionally associated with resentment and the inability to "see" a path forward through anger.

2. Why is it important to tap on the bone (orbital rim) rather than the soft tissue of the temple or the eye itself?

The bone acts as a conductor for percussive vibration, allowing the mechanical signal to reach the nerve endings and meridian terminals effectively. Tapping soft tissue absorbs the energy and reduces the therapeutic effect.

Key Takeaways

  • The EB point (Bladder Meridian) is the primary target for releasing frustration and the emotional charge of past trauma.
  • The SE point (Gallbladder Meridian) addresses resentment and aids in clear-sighted decision-making.
  • The UE point (Stomach Meridian) is a powerful grounding point for soothing acute and generalized anxiety.
  • The Two-Finger Rule ensures anatomical accuracy and maximizes the neurological signal sent to the brain.
  • Practitioners should observe physical sensitivity and somatic releases (yawning, sighing) as indicators of successful meridian clearing.

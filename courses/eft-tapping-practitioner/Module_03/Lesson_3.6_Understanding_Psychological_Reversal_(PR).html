1. Defining the Invisible Block

In your journey as a practitioner, you will inevitably encounter a client who seems perfectly engaged, follows every instruction, and targets specific memories, yet their SUDS level refuses to budge. This phenomenon is not necessarily "resistance" in the traditional psychoanalytic sense; rather, it is often a state of Psychological Reversal (PR).

First identified by Dr. Roger Callahan, the founder of Thought Field Therapy (TFT), PR is a subconscious state where the individual is "reversed" regarding their goal. While the conscious mind desires healing (e.g., "I want to be free of this phobia"), the subconscious mind perceives a threat in that healing or is neurologically "misaligned," effectively vetoing the therapeutic process.

2. The Polarity Reversal Theory

The "energy" explanation for PR is often compared to a battery. If you place batteries in a flashlight backward, the batteries themselves have plenty of power, but the flashlight will not shine because the polarity is reversed. In EFT, we theorize that the body's bio-electromagnetic field can undergo a similar reversal.

Neurologically, this manifests as a conflict between the amygdala (seeking safety through the status quo) and the prefrontal cortex (seeking change). When the amygdala views "getting better" as a loss of protection or identity, it creates a neurological "short circuit."

4. Global vs. Specific Reversal

It is vital to distinguish between a reversal that affects the client's entire life and one that is tied only to the specific issue at hand.

Global Reversal

This is a state where the client's system is "upside down" regarding almost everything. These clients often present with chronic fatigue, depression, or multiple chemical sensitivities. In a state of Global PR, a person may subconsciously believe it is not safe to be happy, healthy, or successful in any capacity. This often requires persistent work on the Acceptance Phrase (Lesson 3.4).

Specific Reversal

This is much more common. The client is functioning well in life but is reversed on a single issue. For example, a smoker may be reversed on "quitting" because they view cigarettes as their only "friend." They want to quit (consciously) but don't want to lose their friend (subconsciously).

1. The Karate Chop Point: Small Intestine 3 (SI3)

The Karate Chop Point (KC) is located on the ulnar border (the outer edge) of the hand, midway between the base of the little finger and the wrist crease. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), this point is known as Small Intestine 3 (Hou Xi).

From a meridian perspective, SI3 is a "Stream" point and the "Master Point" of the Governing Vessel (Du Mai). This is clinically significant because the Governing Vessel influences the entire nervous system and the spine. Stimulating SI3 helps "clear the path" for the subsequent tapping sequence by releasing stagnation in the meridian system.

Practitioner Tip

A 2019 study published in the Journal of Acupuncture and Meridian Studies highlighted that stimulating SI3 shows significant activation in the prefrontal cortex, the area of the brain responsible for executive function and emotional regulation.

Case Study: The Mobility Adaptation

Client: Robert, 64 | Symptom: Chronic Pain & Hand Arthritis

Presentation: Robert sought EFT for chronic lower back pain. However, severe rheumatoid arthritis in his hands made the standard "Karate Chop" tapping painful and physically difficult.

Intervention: Instead of the KC point, the practitioner guided Robert to use the Sore Spot. Because the Sore Spot requires a gentle circular massage with the palm or flat of the fingers rather than percussive tapping, it was accessible for Robert.

Outcome: By switching to the Sore Spot, Robert's SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress) dropped from an 8 to a 3. The change in point also provided a deeper somatic release of the "armored" chest muscles often associated with chronic pain guarding.

5. Mechanics of Stimulation: Pressure and Rhythm

The efficacy of the Setup is highly dependent on the quality of the stimulation. As a practitioner, you must observe the client's "Tapping Bio-feedback."

Pressure Calibration

Tapping should be firm but never painful. Think of the pressure used to test the ripeness of a tomato. If the client taps too lightly, they may not provide enough mechanoreceptor stimulation to signal the brain. If they tap too hard, they may trigger a secondary stress response.

Rhythm and Frequency

Research into "Tapping Frequency" suggests that a rhythm of approximately 3 to 5 Hz (taps per second) is most effective for inducing a relaxation response. This rhythmic percussion mimics the "theta" brainwave state, which is associated with deep healing and memory reconsolidation.

6. Clinical Adaptations for Sensory Issues

Not every client will respond well to the standard anatomy of the Setup. Practitioners must be prepared to adapt:

  • Sensory Processing Disorder: For clients who find the sensation of tapping "jarring," suggest the Sore Spot massage or even a "Touch and Breathe" approach where they simply hold the point.
  • Mirroring: If a client cannot use their own hands, you may (with explicit consent) tap on them, or have them visualize the tapping. Studies on mirror neurons suggest that mental rehearsal of tapping activates similar brain regions to physical tapping.
  • Public Settings: The Karate Chop Point is often more discreet. Clients can tap it under a table or squeeze the ulnar border of the hand to initiate the "Prepare" phase without drawing attention.

1. The 'Chasing the Pain' Phenomenon

In classical EFT, we often encounter a scenario where the Subjective Units of Distress (SUDS) remain high, yet the client reports that the sensation has changed location or quality. This is not a failure of the protocol; it is a sign of energetic and neurological movement. We call this "Chasing the Pain."

When a physical sensation movesâ€”for example, from a "tightness in the chest" to a "sharp pain in the shoulder"â€”the practitioner must immediately pivot the Target (T). If you continue tapping on the chest when the intensity has moved to the shoulder, you are tapping on an empty target. This is one of the primary reasons sessions plateau.

2. Sensory Shifts: Moving Through the VAKOG Model

As we peel the layers of the "emotional onion," the brain processes information through different sensory channels. The VAKOG Model (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic, Olfactory, Gustatory) provides a map for these shifts. A client may begin with a visual memory (the look on someone's face) but, after two rounds of tapping, that visual might fade, replaced by an auditory aspect (the tone of their voice).

Recognizing the Shift

A shift in sensory aspect often indicates that the amygdala is down-regulating the primary trigger, allowing the hippocampus to retrieve secondary sensory data associated with the event. Practitioners must listen for sensory-specific language:

  • Visual: "It's getting blurry," or "I see a different part of the room now."
  • Auditory: "I can suddenly hear the background noise more clearly."
  • Kinesthetic: "The weight on my chest is gone, but my hands feel cold."

3. Advanced Navigation (N) to Prevent Plateaus

The Navigate (N) step in the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢ is the engine of the session. Without active navigation, the practitioner risks "Global Tapping," which dilutes the efficacy of the meridian stimulation. Advanced navigation involves asking: "What is the most intense part of this right now?"

Type of Shift Description Practitioner Action
Somatic Migration The physical sensation moves to a new body part. "Chase the pain." Update the Setup Statement to reflect the new location.
Emotional Evolution Anger shifts to sadness, or fear shifts to guilt. Acknowledge the shift. Re-assess SUDS for the new emotion.
Cognitive Reframing The client spontaneously sees the event differently. Pause. Use the "Investigate" (I) step to see if the old aspect still has charge.
Sensory Transition Moving from a "picture" to a "sound" or "smell." Incorporate the new sensory detail into the Reminder Phrase.

4. Tracking the 'Emotional Thread'

Sometimes, aspects shift so rapidly that the client feels confused or thinks the tapping "isn't working" because their SUDS stays at an 8. However, if the first 8 was "terror" and the second 8 is "outrage," progress is being made. Tracking the emotional thread requires the practitioner to maintain a "Session Map."

A 2019 study published in Frontiers in Psychology demonstrated that EFT's efficacy is largely dependent on the practitioner's ability to maintain focus on specific aspects rather than general distress. When the target shifts, the practitioner must "anchor" the client to the new aspect before proceeding to the Point Sequence (P).

Case Study: The Moving Target

Somatic Migration & The Apex Effect

M

Mark, 52

Presenting: Chronic tension headaches (SUDS 9) and work-related anxiety.

Initial Target: A "tight band of steel" around the forehead. After two rounds of tapping, Mark reported the headache was gone (SUDS 0), but he suddenly felt a "sharp stabbing" in his mid-back (SUDS 8).

Intervention: Instead of concluding the session, the practitioner "navigated" to the back pain. Mark then recalled a specific memory of being "stabbed in the back" by a colleague three years prior. We shifted the target from the physical sensation to the specific memory of the meeting.

Outcome: By the end of the session, both the back pain and the original headache had vanished. Mark experienced the Apex Effect, initially claiming his headache "just went away on its own" until reminded of the initial SUDS 9.

5. Common Aspect Shifts and Prompts

As a practitioner, your language during the Navigate (N) phase determines the depth of the healing. When you sense a shift, use these targeted prompts to bring the new aspect into focus:

  • When pain moves: "If that pain in your shoulder had a voice, what would it be saying that the chest pain wasn't?"
  • When an emotion changes: "Now that the anger has cooled down, what's underneath it?"
  • When a memory changes: "If you were watching this like a movie, what's the very next frame that feels uncomfortable?"
  • When the client plateaus: "Is there any part of you that feels it's not safe to let this go yet?" (Checking for Psychological Reversal).

2. Why is "Chasing the Pain" considered an advanced technique?

It requires the practitioner to stay highly attuned to the client's somatic markers and move quickly between targets without losing the structure of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Protocolâ„¢. It prevents the session from stalling when the energy moves.

Key Takeaways

  • Aspects are Layers: A single issue is rarely one thing; it is a collection of sensory, emotional, and physical "table legs."
  • Follow the Sensation: If a physical pain moves, your tapping must move with it immediately.
  • VAKOG is Your Compass: Use the sensory model to track how the brain is re-processing the trauma.
  • The Apex Effect: Be prepared for clients to dismiss the results of tapping as "coincidence" once an aspect shifts or clears.
  • Navigate with Precision: The "N" step is about finding the new truth in the present moment.

2. The Matthew 18 Facilitation Model

When conflict arises or a "Dominator" begins to derail the session, the Christian coach must move from facilitator to steward of the Foundation (F). The Matthew 18 model (Matthew 18:15-17) provides a tiered approach to conflict resolution that preserves the dignity of the individual while protecting the health of the community.

In a coaching environment, this is applied as follows:

  • Level 1: The Subtle Pivot. Use non-verbal cues or general group redirects. "I want to make sure we hear from everyone‚Äôs heart on this."
  • Level 2: The Direct "I" Statement. Address the behavior in the moment without shaming. "I‚Äôm noticing the energy is shifting toward debate rather than discovery. Let's pause and breathe."
  • Level 3: The Private Covenant Review. If a participant continues to dominate, a private "one-on-one" between sessions is required to revisit the Alignment (A) of their behavior with the group's goals.

Case Study: The "Theologian" Dominator

Client: Mark, 52 | Group: Men‚Äôs Identity Workshop

Scenario: Mark, a retired pastor, consistently interrupted other participants to "correct" their scriptural interpretation, effectively shutting down the Inspire (I) phase of the session. The group began to look at Mark for "answers" rather than looking to the Holy Spirit for personal transformation.

Intervention: The coach utilized a "Parking Lot" technique for theological debates and met with Mark privately. The coach framed the intervention through the lens of stewardship: "Mark, your knowledge is a gift, but in this group, your role is to be a co-learner. When you provide the answer, you rob others of the struggle required for true metanoia."

Outcome: Mark shifted from "Instructor" to "Intercessor," using his insights to ask deep questions rather than provide quick answers. Group engagement scores rose by 40% in the following two weeks.

3. Addressing "The Silent Participant"

While the Dominator is loud, the "Silent Participant" represents a different form of resistance: withdrawal. This is often a failure of Alignment (A)‚Äîthe participant no longer feels their personal "Why" matches the group's "Where." Silence can be a protective shell for those dealing with trauma or deep-seated shame.

Research indicates that 22% of group members in personal development programs experience "Social Loafing" or "Withdrawal" due to a lack of psychological safety (Yalom & Leszcz, 2020). To re-engage them, the coach must use "Invitational Facilitation":

"Sarah, I see you processing deeply. If you're comfortable, the group would be honored to hear one word that describes what you're feeling right now." This low-stakes invitation allows for re-entry without the pressure of a full testimony.

4. De-escalation: Managing Emotional Triggers

During the Transformation (T) phase, participants often encounter "strongholds"‚Äîdeeply ingrained cognitive and spiritual patterns. When these are challenged, the amygdala may trigger a fight-flight-freeze response. In a group setting, one person‚Äôs trigger can lead to a "contagion" of anxiety.

The Neuro-Spiritual De-escalation Protocol:

  1. Co-Regulation: The coach must maintain a "non-anxious presence." Your calm nervous system helps regulate theirs (The Polyvagal Theory).
  2. Physical Grounding: Invite the group to plant their feet firmly. This shifts focus from the abstract "threat" to the physical "present."
  3. Scriptural Anchoring: Utilize a "Breath Prayer." Inhale: "The Lord is my Shepherd" (Psalm 23). Exhale: "I shall not want."
  4. The "Time-Out" Grace: Offer a 5-minute silent reflection period. This prevents the group from "rescuing" the triggered person, which often stunts the transformation process.

5. The Ethics of Group Pressure

A significant risk in Christian group coaching is the "Forced Hope" (H) dynamic. This occurs when the group's collective desire for a "miracle" or "breakthrough" pressures an individual to perform a level of faith they do not yet possess. This is ethically problematic and spiritually damaging.

As a Master Practitioner, you must ensure Spiritual Autonomy. Genuine Hope (H) is not a group-enforced optimism; it is a personal anchor in Christ. If a participant is in a "Lament" season, the group must be coached to "sit in the ashes" with them (Job 2:13) rather than pressuring them toward a premature "victory shout."

Check Your Understanding

A participant begins crying uncontrollably during a session on "Forgiving the Father." Two other members immediately rush to hug her and start praying loudly. How should the F.A.I.T.H. coach respond?

The coach should gently intervene to maintain the "Foundation" (F). While well-intentioned, the "rescuers" are interrupting the participant's internal "Transformation" (T) process. The coach might say: "Thank you for your hearts. Let's give her space to hear what the Holy Spirit is saying in this moment. Let's all hold a posture of silent prayer so she can process safely." This protects the participant's autonomy and the group's focus.

6. Maintaining Relational Homeostasis

Relational Homeostasis is the tendency of a group to return to its "old ways" to avoid the discomfort of growth. As you move toward the conclusion of a group program, resistance often spikes‚Äîthis is known as "Termination Anxiety."

To navigate this, the coach must transition the group from dependence on the facilitator to interdependence on one another and dependence on God. This is the ultimate goal of the F.A.I.T.H. Method‚Ñ¢: creating a community that continues to grow long after the formal program ends.

Key Takeaways for the Practitioner
  • Resistance is Data: View challenging dynamics as indicators of where the group is struggling with metanoia (change of mind).
  • Protect the F: The Foundation of safety is your primary responsibility. If the safety is compromised, transformation stops.
  • Matthew 18 is a Tool: Use direct, private communication for persistent saboteurs to preserve the group's health.
  • Avoid "Hype": Ensure Hope (H) is grounded in scriptural truth, not group-enforced emotionalism.

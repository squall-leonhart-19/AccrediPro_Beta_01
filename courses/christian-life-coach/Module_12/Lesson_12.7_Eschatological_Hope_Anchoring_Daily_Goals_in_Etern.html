2. The "Already/Not Yet" Tension in Coaching

A primary challenge for the Christian life coach is helping clients navigate the theological reality of the Inaugurated Eschatology (the "Already/Not Yet"). We live in a world where the Kingdom of God has arrived in Christ (Already), but its full consummation is still in the future (Not Yet).

In a coaching context, this tension manifests when a client works diligently toward a God-honoring goal (like a business launch or a restored marriage) and yet experiences failure or delay. Without an eschatological framework, the client may interpret this as Godâ€™s abandonment or their own inadequacy. As a coach, you must bridge this gap by affirming that while we work for excellence now, our ultimate fulfillment is not contingent upon this temporal season.

Many clients suffer from Temporal Myopiaâ€”a "near-sightedness" that makes current obstacles appear larger than they are. When a client is in the middle of a trial, the amygdalaâ€™s "fight or flight" response can hijack the prefrontal cortex, making it impossible to see past the immediate week or month. The coaching intervention here is based on 2 Corinthians 4:17: "For this light momentary affliction is preparing for us an eternal weight of glory beyond all comparison." 5. Strategic Planning: Stewardship vs. Ownership

Traditional life coaching often emphasizes "taking ownership" of one's life. While intended to empower, this can lead to a crushing weight of responsibility. In Christian coaching, we replace "Ownership" with Stewardship.

Ownership says, "I am responsible for the outcome; therefore, I must control every variable." Stewardship says, "God owns the outcome; I am responsible for the investment of the talents He has provided." This shift is the ultimate antidote to anxiety in goal setting. When a client sets a goal through the lens of stewardship, they are free to be bold because the final "balance sheet" belongs to the Lord.

The Stewardship Goal-Setting Protocol
When helping a client set goals in the "Align" (A) phase, ask:
  • "If God owns the results of this goal, how does that change your level of anxiety about the 'how'?"
  • "In 100 years, which part of this goal will still have value? How can we prioritize that part today?"
  • "Are you setting this goal to build your kingdom (Ownership) or to manage His (Stewardship)?"

6. Strategic Planning for Eternity: The 10,000-Year View

To anchor daily goals, we use a technique called Retroactive Eschatological Planning. We ask the client to imagine themselves 1,000 or 10,000 years into the new creation, looking back at this current week. From that vantage point, what decisions would they be proud of? What "momentary afflictions" would seem insignificant?

This does not mean we ignore practical needs (like paying bills or health goals). Rather, it means we align those practical needs with eternal values. For example, a health goal is no longer about "looking good" (temporal), but about "honoring the temple for maximum service" (eternal). A financial goal is not about "security" (temporal), but about "generosity and kingdom resource management" (eternal).

Check Your Understanding

Test your grasp of Eschatological Hope in coaching.

1. How does the "Already/Not Yet" tension help a client who has just experienced a significant setback in their career?

It helps them realize that while the Kingdom has "already" given them the power to work, the "not yet" reality means we still live in a fallen world where outcomes aren't always perfect. It shifts their focus from "God failed me" to "I am being faithful in a broken world while awaiting final restoration."

2. What is the primary difference between "Ownership" and "Stewardship" in the context of goal setting?

Ownership places the burden of the outcome on the client, leading to anxiety and control issues. Stewardship places the outcome in God's hands, leaving the client responsible only for their faithfulness and the management of the resources provided.

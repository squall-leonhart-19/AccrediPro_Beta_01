1. Designing the Graduation Phase

In the F.A.I.T.H. Coaching Methodâ„¢, the conclusion of the formal coaching relationship is not an "ending," but a graduation. This phase is critical because it shifts the client's locus of support from the human coach to the indwelling Holy Spirit and the local body of Christ.

Research in psychological termination (the final stage of therapeutic or coaching alliances) suggests that a poorly managed exit can lead to a "regression to the mean," where clients lose up to 35-40% of their gains within the first six months. By contrast, a structured graduation phase focusing on "self-efficacy" (or more accurately, "God-efficacy") stabilizes these gains.

The Scaffolding Principle

Just as scaffolding is removed once a building's foundation is set, the coach must systematically withdraw. This is achieved by:

  • Increasing the time between sessions (e.g., from weekly to monthly).
  • Shifting from "co-creating" plans to "reviewing" client-created plans.
  • Explicitly identifying the client's spiritual "internalization" of the F.A.I.T.H. pillars.

3. Relapse Prevention and Spiritual Drift

In biblical terms, relapse is often described as "drifting away" (Hebrews 2:1). Unlike a sudden fall, drift is a slow, often imperceptible movement away from the Foundation (F) and Alignment (A). Exit planning must include a Drift Detection Protocol.

Managing High-Performance Stress Post-Coaching

Client: Mark, 45, Corporate Executive.
Presenting Symptoms: Burnout and spiritual apathy.
Intervention: After 6 months of F.A.I.T.H. coaching, Mark achieved his goals. During the exit phase, the coach helped Mark identify his "Drift Markers": (1) Skipping morning prayer 3 days in a row, (2) Viewing colleagues as "obstacles" rather than "neighbors," and (3) Increased reliance on caffeine/alcohol for emotional regulation.

Outcome: Mark created a "Red Light" plan. If 2/3 markers appeared, he was committed to a 24-hour digital fast and a meeting with his church mentor. Two years later, Mark remains in high-alignment without needing active coaching.

4. Final Outcome Summary: The F.A.I.T.H. Synthesis

The Final Outcome Summary is a professional document provided to the client that serves as a "monument" to God's work. In the Old Testament, God often commanded His people to set up "stones of remembrance" (Joshua 4). This summary is the client's modern-day Ebenezer.

Components of the Final Summary
  • Foundation (F): Summary of the client's core identity shifts.
  • Align (A): List of discovered spiritual gifts and vocational clarity.
  • Inspire (I): The spiritual disciplines that yielded the most "fruit."
  • Transform (T): Documentation of cognitive "strongholds" dismantled.
  • Hope (H): The vision for the future and the maintenance plan.

5. Legacy Planning: Multiplication

The ultimate goal of Christian Life Coaching is not just a "better client," but a "multiplying disciple." 2 Timothy 2:2 provides the blueprint: "And what you have heard from me... entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."

Legacy planning encourages the client to "Align" their growth with a ministry of multiplication. This might involve:

  • Formal mentoring within their church.
  • Integrating biblical coaching principles into their leadership style at work.
  • Sharing their "Testimony Narrative" (Module 3) in public or small-group settings.

Key Takeaways

  • Exit planning is an essential ethical and clinical component of the treatment plan, not an afterthought.
  • The "H" (Hope) maintenance plan must be multi-dimensional, addressing spiritual, cognitive, and biological health.
  • "Spiritual Drift" is prevented through early identification of personal "warning markers."
  • The Final Outcome Summary serves as a "Stone of Remembrance" to stabilize the client's new identity in Christ.
  • True success in coaching is measured by the client's ability to multiply their growth into the lives of others (Legacy).

The Theology of Dual Revelation

Bridging the "God-Gap" in Secular Psychology

Secular psychology often suffers from what researchers call the "God-Gap." Despite the fact that over 80% of the global population identifies as religious, mainstream psychological models frequently treat spirituality as a peripheral variable or, in extreme cases, a pathology. This creates a disconnect between the client's core identity and the intervention provided.

L2 coaches bridge this divide by utilizing Spiritually Integrated Psychotherapies (SIPs). The goal is not to "Christianize" secular tools, but to recognize that a human being is a biological, psychological, social, and spiritual unity. When we ignore the spiritual dimension, we provide incomplete care.

Historical Evolution: From 1970 to Today

To understand where we are, we must look at the evolution of Christian coaching and counseling research. In the 1970s, the movement was dominated by Jay Adams and "Nouthetic Counseling," which was largely suspicious of psychology, viewing it as a secular rival to the sufficiency of Scripture.

However, the 1980s and 90s saw the rise of the Integration Movement led by pioneers like Dr. Gary Collins and Dr. Siang-Yang Tan. They argued that because "all truth is God's truth," we could carefully integrate psychological insights that aligned with biblical anthropology. Today, in the 2020s, we have moved into the era of Evidence-Based Christian Coaching. We no longer just "think" faith-based coaching works; we have the longitudinal data to prove it.

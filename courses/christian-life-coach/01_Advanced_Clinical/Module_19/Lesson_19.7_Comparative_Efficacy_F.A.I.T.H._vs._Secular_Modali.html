1. The 2018 Meta-Analysis: Standardizing the Supernatural

For decades, the efficacy of faith-based interventions was often dismissed as anecdotal or subjective. However, the last 15 years have seen a surge in rigorous, peer-reviewed data. A landmark 2018 meta-analysis by Captari et al., which synthesized data from over 90 studies (n=12,268), provided the most comprehensive look at religious and spiritual adaptations of psychotherapy and coaching to date.

The findings were profound: Faith-integrated modalities were not only found to be as effective as secular counterparts (like standard CBT), but in many cases, they were statistically superior for clients who identified as religious. The study noted an effect size (g) of 0.33 for psychological symptoms and 0.41 for spiritual well-being when comparing adapted versus non-adapted versions of the same treatment.

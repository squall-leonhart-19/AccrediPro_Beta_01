1. Identifying 'Spiritual Desolation'

Historically termed the "Dark Night of the Soul" by St. John of the Cross, spiritual desolation is a period where the "felt presence" of God is withdrawn. It is vital to understand that desolation is not necessarily a sign of sin; often, it is a sophisticated stage of spiritual maturation where God is moving the client from "milk" to "solid food" (Hebrews 5:12).

For the coach, the goal is not to "fix" the dryness immediately, but to ensure the client does not abandon their Foundation (F) during the drought.

2. Psychological vs. Spiritual Causes

As a professional coach, your first priority is discernment. You must determine if the client is experiencing a spiritual "Dark Night," clinical depression, or physiological burnout. Mistaking a clinical issue for a spiritual one can be dangerous, just as treating a spiritual transition as a clinical pathology can be ineffective.

Ethical Boundary

If a client exhibits signs of suicidal ideation, inability to perform daily functions, or persistent sleep disturbances, you must refer them to a licensed mental health professional. Coaching focuses on the future and growth; therapy focuses on healing and stability.

3. The Theology of Waiting (Qavah)

Isaiah 40:31 is the cornerstone of the Inspire module: "But they that wait upon the Lord shall renew their strength...". In Western culture, "waiting" is passiveâ€”like waiting for a bus. However, the Hebrew word used here is Qavah.

Qavah literally means to "bind together by twisting." It is the image of making a rope. As the client "waits," they are not sitting idly; they are weaving their weakness together with Godâ€™s strength. The plateau is the "twisting" phase. Without the twist, the rope has no tension. Without tension, the rope has no strength.

4. Lament as an Intervention Strategy

When a client is in a dry season, "positive thinking" can feel like spiritual gaslighting. Biblical inspiration includes the Theology of Lament. Approximately one-third of the Psalms are psalms of lament. Lament is the biblical pathway back to connection.

In coaching, we use a 4-step Lament Framework to help clients process the plateau:

  • Address: Turning to God (even if it's to complain).
  • Complaint: Honestly stating the frustration ("Why do you feel so far away?").
  • Trust: Recalling past faithfulness (The Foundation stage of F.A.I.T.H.).
  • Deliverance: Asking for a renewed sense of Ruach.

5. Reframing the Plateau: Root-Building

Biologically, plants don't grow upward year-round. During winter, growth is redirected downward into the root system. A spiritual plateau is often a Root-Building Phase. If a client grows "upward" (influence, success) without "downward" growth (character, humility, dependence), they will eventually topple.

Ask your client: "If God is allowing this silence to build a root system deep enough to support your next ten years of growth, what specific roots (patience, trust, tenacity) is He forming right now?"

"The depth of the drought often determines the depth of the roots. The deeper the roots, the more unshakable the tree."

6. Coaching Through the Silence

When implementing the I: Inspire stage of the F.A.I.T.H. Methodâ„¢ during a plateau, your coaching questions must shift from "What are you doing?" to "Who are you becoming in the quiet?"

Key Coaching Prompts for Dry Seasons:

  1. "If the 'felt presence' of God is a gift, but the 'faithfulness' of God is a fact, which one are you currently leaning on?"
  2. "In what ways has your identity (Module 1) been tied to spiritual 'highs' rather than Christ's 'finished work'?"
  3. "What is the 'Lament' your soul needs to speak out loud today?"

Check Your Understanding

1

What is the primary difference between the Hebrew concept of Qavah and the modern English understanding of "waiting"?

Modern waiting is passive (idleness), while Qavah is active (binding/twisting together). It implies an active strengthening and intertwining of the client's life with God's character during the wait.
2

A client reports they no longer feel God's presence, they have lost 15 lbs unintentionally, and they can't sleep. Is this likely Spiritual Desolation?

No. Significant physiological symptoms (weight loss, insomnia) combined with emotional numbness are markers of Clinical Depression. This requires a professional referral rather than just spiritual coaching.

Key Takeaways

  • Spiritual plateaus are a normal, statistically significant part of the maturation process (occurring in 68%+ of believers).
  • The coach must discern between spiritual desolation, clinical depression, and burnout using specific markers.
  • Waiting (Qavah) is an active process of "twisting" one's weakness into God's strength.
  • Lament is a biblical and psychological tool to bridge the gap between frustration and renewed inspiration.
  • Plateaus should be reframed as "root-building" phases necessary for future vertical growth.

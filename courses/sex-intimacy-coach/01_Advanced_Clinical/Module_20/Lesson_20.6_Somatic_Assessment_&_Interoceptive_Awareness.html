1. The MAIA-2: Quantifying the 'Mindful Presence' Phase

The Multidimensional Interoceptive Awareness (MAIA-2) scale is the gold standard for assessing how a client perceives and reacts to bodily signals. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Sexual Medicine (n=452) demonstrated that individuals with higher interoceptive awareness scores reported a 34% higher level of sexual satisfaction and significantly lower rates of "spectatoring" (cognitive distraction during sex).

The 8 Dimensions of the MAIA-2

  • Noticing: Awareness of uncomfortable, comfortable, and neutral body sensations.
  • Not-Distracting: The tendency not to ignore or distract oneself from sensations of pain or discomfort.
  • Not-Worrying: The tendency not to worry or experience emotional distress with sensations of pain or discomfort.
  • Attention Regulation: Ability to sustain and control attention to body sensations.
  • Emotional Awareness: Awareness of the connection between body sensations and emotional states.
  • Self-Regulation: Ability to regulate distress by attention to body sensations.
  • Body Listening: Active listening to the body for insight.

3. Pelvic Floor Tension & Diaphragmatic Breathing

The diaphragm and the pelvic floor function as a functional unit, moving in tandem during the respiratory cycle. In the context of sexual arousal, hypertonicity (over-activity) of the pelvic floor often correlates with shallow, thoracic breathing. This triggers the sympathetic nervous system (the "Brake" in the Dual Control Model), effectively shutting down the pleasure response.

Assessment Marker Sympathetic Dominance (Stress) Parasympathetic Dominance (Safety)
Breathing Pattern Upper chest, rapid, shallow Diaphragmatic, rhythmic, deep
Pelvic Floor State Guarded, hypertonic (clenched) Responsive, eccentric (relaxed)
Vagal Tone Low (Reduced HRV) High (Increased HRV)
Arousal Quality Anxious, performance-based Expansive, sensation-based

A 2023 meta-analysis (n=1,120) found that 72% of women presenting with dyspareunia (painful intercourse) exhibited significant pelvic floor hypertonicity combined with a "breath-holding" pattern during arousal. As coaches, we assess this by observing the client's breathing during somatic check-ins.

4. Assessing the 'Attune' Phase: Self vs. Other

In the Attune phase of the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Method, we must distinguish between Self-Attunement (listening to one's own body) and Partner-Pleasing Behaviors (the "Fawn" response). Many clients mistake "performance" for "connection."

The "Fawn Response" Assessment Protocol

Use these inquiry markers to gauge if the client is operating from a place of somatic integrity or performative pleasing:

  • Internal Monitoring: "During touch, is your focus on what you are feeling, or on what your partner is thinking about your body?"
  • The "No" Capacity: "How does your body feel when you think about saying 'stop' or 'change' during a sexual encounter?" (Look for throat constriction or stomach dropping).
  • Sensory Filtering: Can the client describe three distinct textures or temperatures they felt during their last intimate encounter? (Lack of detail often indicates dissociation).

5. Biofeedback Principles & Physiological Stress Responses

While most coaches do not use clinical biofeedback machines, we can use subjective biofeedback to assess physiological stress. This involves teaching the client to monitor their own Heart Rate Variability (HRV) and skin temperature as proxies for sexual readiness.

Research indicates that Skin Conductance Level (SCL) increases significantly during sexual arousal, but only when the client feels safe. If SCL increases without a corresponding increase in subjective pleasure, the client is likely experiencing "Non-Concordance"â€”a state where the body is aroused (physiologically) but the mind is in distress (psychologically).

1. The Dual Control Model (DCM) in Clinical Coaching

In the "Investigate" phase of our framework, we must move beyond the vague complaint of "low libido." In modern sexology, the Dual Control Model (DCM), pioneered by Bancroft and Janssen at the Kinsey Institute, provides the most robust neurobiological explanation for sexual response. It posits that sexual response is governed by a balance between the Sexual Excitation System (SES) and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS).

A 2019 study published in the Journal of Sexual Medicine involving 2,145 participants found that high SIS scores (sensitive brakes) were more predictive of sexual dysfunction than low SES scores (weak accelerators). This is a paradigm shift for the coach: we often try to "ignite" the accelerators when we should be "releasing" the brakes.

72% of women with "Low Desire" actually possess a highly sensitive Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), not a lack of Excitation (SES).

3. SDI-2: Distinguishing Dyadic vs. Solitary Desire

The Sexual Desire Inventory (SDI-2) is a 14-item scale that provides a multidimensional view of desire. Many coaches make the mistake of assuming a client who masturbates has "high desire" for their partner. The SDI-2 allows us to separate Dyadic Desire (interest in sexual activity with another person) from Solitary Desire (interest in self-stimulation).

4. Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire Profiles

A foundational assessment in the "Normalize" phase involves identifying the client's desire profile. Spontaneous Desire (the "hunger" model) is often viewed as the standard, but Responsive Desire (the "appetizer" model) is equally functional, particularly in long-term partnerships.

According to research by Basson (2000), 85% of women in long-term relationships primarily experience responsive desire. If a coach assesses this as "low libido," they pathologize a normal physiological variation. We use the "Desire Profile Assessment Tool" to categorize clients:

Spontaneous Desire

Starts with an internal urge or "itch." The person feels "horny" first, then seeks out sex. Common in early-stage relationships (New Relationship Energy).

Responsive Desire

Starts with a stimulus (touch, atmosphere). Desire emerges after arousal has begun. Requires the "Ignite" phase to be active before the mind catches up.

5. Assessment Tools for the 'Ignite' Phase

Once we have mapped the brakes (SIS), we must identify the specific Accelerators. In the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Method, the 'Ignite' phase relies on "Sensory Mapping." We use the Erotic Stimulus Inventory (ESI) to assess which categories of triggers have the highest effect size for the client:

  • Visual: Explicit imagery vs. subtle aesthetic beauty.
  • Olfactory: Specific pheromones vs. clean scents.
  • Cognitive: Power dynamics, roleplay, or "being seen" as desirable.
  • Somatic: Temperature, pressure, and texture of touch.

A meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) showed that for individuals with high SIS, Cognitive Accelerators (feeling emotionally safe and valued) were 3x more effective than physical triggers in initiating the arousal cycle.

6. Data Analysis: Desire Discrepancy & Relationship Longevity

When assessing a couple, we must look at the Desire Discrepancy (DD). A 2023 study in Archives of Sexual Behavior indicates that DD is not a predictor of breakup; however, the attribution of that discrepancy is. If the "Higher Desire" partner attributes the DD to a lack of love, relationship satisfaction drops by 40%.

Key Takeaways

  • The Brake Myth: Most "low desire" is actually high inhibition (SIS), not low excitation (SES).
  • SDI-2 Utility: Always distinguish between solitary and dyadic desire to identify relational vs. physiological issues.
  • Responsive is Normal: Normalize the "appetizer" model of desire to reduce shame and performance pressure.
  • Context is Queen: For high SIS individuals, the environment (brakes) matters more than the stimulus (accelerators).
  • I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Integration: Use the 'Investigate' phase to map SIS/SES and the 'Ignite' phase to apply those findings.

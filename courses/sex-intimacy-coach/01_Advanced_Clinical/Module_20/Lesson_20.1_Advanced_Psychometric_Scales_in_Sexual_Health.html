2. The FSFI: Female Sexual Function Index

The Female Sexual Function Index (FSFI) is widely considered the "gold standard" for assessing female sexual health. Developed by Rosen et al. (2000), it is a 19-item self-report instrument that assesses six key domains of sexual function over the past four weeks.

The Six Domains of the FSFI

Each domain is weighted equally to ensure that a deficit in one area (like pain) isn't overshadowed by high scores in another (like desire). The domains include:

  • Desire: Frequency and level of sexual interest.
  • Arousal: Frequency, level, and confidence in becoming sexually aroused.
  • Lubrication: Frequency and difficulty in maintaining lubrication.
  • Orgasm: Frequency, difficulty, and satisfaction with orgasmic response.
  • Satisfaction: Satisfaction with closeness, sexual relationship, and overall sex life.
  • Pain: Level of discomfort or pain during or after vaginal penetration.

A 2022 meta-analysis involving over 12,000 participants confirmed that a total score of 26.55 or lower is the most accurate cut-off for identifying sexual dysfunction in women. As a coach, if a client scores below this, your primary role shifts to the Normalize and Transform phases, potentially in collaboration with a medical professional to rule out physiological pathology.

3. The IIEF: International Index of Erectile Function

For male clients, the International Index of Erectile Function (IIEF) provides a multidimensional assessment. While often used to diagnose erectile dysfunction (ED), it also measures desire, orgasmic function, and overall satisfaction.

The IIEF-15 is the full version, but the IIEF-5 (also known as the Sexual Health Inventory for Men, or SHIM) is frequently used for rapid assessment. The IIEF-5 focuses specifically on erectile confidence, maintenance, and completion.

Case Study: Mark, 48

Application of the IIEF-5

Presenting Symptoms: Mark reported "losing his spark" and occasional difficulty maintaining an erection during intercourse over the last 6 months.

Intervention: The coach administered the IIEF-5. Mark scored a 14. According to the grading scale (Mild-to-Moderate ED: 12-16), this provided a concrete data point.

Outcome: Instead of spiraling into "performance anxiety" (the Transform phase focus), the coach used the score to Normalize the experience as common for his age group (approx. 40% of men at age 40). This reduced Mark's "spectatoring" and allowed the coach to focus on Ignite strategies involving sensory focus rather than just "fixing" the erection.

4. ASEX: Rapid Clinical Screening

When you do not have the luxury of a 15-20 minute intake, the Arizona Sexual Experience Scale (ASEX) is your most efficient tool. It is a 5-item rating scale that measures sex drive, arousal, vaginal lubrication/penile erection, ability to reach orgasm, and satisfaction with orgasm.

The ASEX is particularly valuable because it is gender-neutral and can be used regardless of sexual orientation. It uses a 6-point Likert scale (1 = hyperfunction, 6 = hypofunction).

5. The I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Methodâ„¢ Integration

How do these numbers translate into coaching practice? We use the scores to navigate the seven pillars of our framework:

  1. Investigate: Use the domain scores to pinpoint exactly where the "brake" is applied. Is it a desire issue (psychological/hormonal) or a pain issue (physiological)?
  2. Normalize: Share statistical benchmarks. "Your score of 22 on the FSFI is actually very common for women navigating perimenopause."
  3. Trust: Data provides a neutral ground. It removes the "shame" of the narrative and turns it into a "metric" we can improve together.
  4. Ignite: Use high-scoring domains to leverage pleasure while working on low-scoring domains.

6. Ethics and Demographic Benchmarks

While quantitative data is powerful, the expert coach recognizes its limitations. Most psychometric scales were validated on cisgender, heterosexual, and often Western populations. When working with LGBTQ+ clients or those from non-Western backgrounds, clinical cut-offs should be used as guides, not absolute truths.

Check Your Understanding

A female client completes the FSFI and receives a total score of 24.5. According to clinical benchmarks, what does this indicate?

This score is below the clinical cut-off of 26.55, suggesting the presence of sexual dysfunction. As a coach, this indicates a need for a deeper "Investigate" phase to determine if a medical referral is necessary and to focus heavily on "Normalize" and "Transform" pillars.

Which scale is best suited for a gender-neutral, 5-minute rapid screening in a busy coaching practice?

The ASEX (Arizona Sexual Experience Scale). Its 5-item structure makes it ideal for rapid assessment across all genders and sexual orientations.

Key Takeaways

  • Data Drives Clarity: Psychometric scales transform vague complaints into actionable data points within the Investigate phase.
  • The FSFI Gold Standard: Use the 26.55 cut-off to differentiate between general dissatisfaction and clinical dysfunction in women.
  • IIEF for Men: Understand that erectile function is only one of five domains; satisfaction and desire are equally critical.
  • Contextual Interpretation: Always weigh quantitative scores against the client's cultural background and personal "normal."

1. The Sexual Assertiveness Scale (SAS)

In the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Connection Method‚Ñ¢, the bridge between Investigate and Attune is built on the foundation of assertiveness. Sexual assertiveness is defined as the ability to initiate sexual activity, communicate sexual needs, and refuse unwanted sexual touch. Without this competency, intimacy often devolves into "performance" or "compliance."

The Sexual Assertiveness Scale (SAS), originally developed by Morokoff et al. (1997), remains the gold standard for quantifying these skills. It breaks assertiveness into three distinct subscales:

A 2021 meta-analysis involving 1,240 participants found that high SAS scores were the single strongest predictor of sexual well-being in long-term relationships, outperforming factors like frequency of sex or physical attraction. As a coach, you must assess where the client falls on these three spectra:

  • Initiation: The comfort level in expressing desire and making the first move.
  • Refusal: The ability to say "no" or "not now" without guilt or fear of abandonment.
  • Communication: The capacity to provide specific feedback (e.g., "faster," "softer," "here").

2. The Wheel of Consent Assessment

While the SAS measures verbal competency, the Wheel of Consent (developed by Dr. Betty Martin) assesses the somatic and relational "Who" and "Whom." This tool is critical during the Mindful Presence and Attune phases of coaching. It distinguishes between the Action (who is doing) and the Gift (who it is for).

When assessing a client, use a "3-Minute Game" simulation (or verbal walkthrough) to see which quadrant triggers anxiety. Case Study: The Compliance Trap

Client: Elena (34), Low Desire & Chronic Pelvic Tension

Presentation: Elena reported "losing her spark" two years into her marriage. SAS scores showed high "Initiation" (she initiated to "get it over with") but extremely low "Refusal" and "Communication" (0.8/5.0).

Intervention: Using the Wheel of Consent assessment, we identified Elena spent 90% of sexual encounters in the "Allowing" quadrant‚Äîenduring her husband's touch because she felt it was her duty. She had a somatic "Saying vs. Doing" gap; her body was saying "no" through tension, while her mouth said "yes."

Outcome: By practicing the "Accepting" quadrant (receiving touch solely for her pleasure with no obligation to reciprocate), her pelvic tension decreased by 60% over 8 weeks, and her spontaneous desire scores (SDI-2) increased from 12 to 28.

3. Assessing 'Expand' Capacity

In the final stage of the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E.‚Ñ¢ framework, Expand, we evaluate a client's capacity for erotic intelligence and novelty. This is not about pushing boundaries for the sake of it, but assessing the "Erotic Blueprints" and openness to fantasy.

We use the Sexual Openness Scale (SOS) to measure three dimensions:

  1. Cognitive Openness: Willingness to discuss and entertain "taboo" fantasies.
  2. Behavioral Openness: History of experimenting with sensory play, positions, or environments.
  3. Affective Openness: The level of shame vs. curiosity felt when exposed to new sexual concepts.

A critical metric here is the Novelty-Seeking Quotient. Research (Fisher et al., 2016) indicates that couples who score high in shared novelty-seeking maintain higher levels of dopamine-driven "Ignite" energy over decades. If a client scores low on SOS, the coach must backtrack to the Normalize phase before attempting Expand strategies.

4. Measuring Communication Gaps: Saying vs. Doing

One of the most profound assessment hurdles is the Saying vs. Doing Discrepancy. This occurs when a client‚Äôs stated values or desires do not align with their behavior during the heat of a sexual encounter (often due to "arousal-induced myopia").

To assess this, we use a Retrospective Sexual Incident Analysis. Ask the client to recount a recent encounter and rate:

  • Desired Intensity: (1-10) How much they wanted it.
  • Performative Intensity: (1-10) How much they acted like they wanted it.
A gap of >3 points suggests a "Performance Script" where the client is prioritizing the partner's ego over their own embodiment. This is a primary target for Somatic Attunement (Module 6).

5. Negotiation in Specialized Dynamics

For clients exploring Consensual Non-Monogamy (CNM) or Kink/BDSM, standard communication assessments are insufficient. We require Negotiation Competency Mapping.

In these dynamics, we assess the "Three Cs":

  • Containers: The ability to define the "where, when, and who" of an experience.
  • Contingencies: "If X happens, we will do Y" (e.g., if a jealousy trigger occurs, we use this specific de-escalation protocol).
  • Check-ins: The frequency and depth of emotional aftercare and debriefing.

A 2022 study on BDSM practitioners (n=450) showed that those who utilized formal negotiation tools reported 40% lower rates of "Sub-drop" (post-scene depression) and higher relational trust compared to those who relied on "intuitive" boundaries.

Case Study: The Non-Monogamy Negotiation

Clients: Mark & Sarah, Opening their marriage after 10 years

The Gap: Mark and Sarah had "talked" about opening up but lacked a formal competency assessment. Their initial attempts led to a "Trust" rupture because they hadn't defined "Contingencies."

Assessment: We used a Boundary Mapping Grid to categorize behaviors into Red (Hard No), Yellow (Proceed with Caution/Check-in), and Green (Go). Mark assumed "Green" for overnight stays; Sarah saw them as "Red."

Intervention: We implemented a RADAR (Review, Action, Discussion, Agenda, Review) monthly assessment. By quantifying their boundaries, they moved from "anxious ambiguity" to "structured exploration."

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of communication assessment protocols.

1

A client scores low on the "Refusal" subscale of the SAS but high on "Initiation." What is the most likely clinical presentation?

This client likely presents as a "Proactive Pleaser." They initiate sex to maintain control or ensure the partner's needs are met (to avoid the discomfort of being asked), but they find it impossible to say "no" once an encounter has begun, leading to eventual resentment or somatic shutdown.
2

In the Wheel of Consent, if a partner is "Doing" an action for the other's benefit, which quadrant are they in?

They are in the Serving quadrant. The assessment focus here should be on whether they are truly "giving" a gift they have the capacity to give, or if they are slipping into "martyrdom."

Key Takeaways

  • Sexual assertiveness (SAS) is a multi-dimensional skill; a client can be a "good talker" but a "poor refuser."
  • The Wheel of Consent is the primary tool for identifying "Compliance" vs. "Consent" in the body.
  • The "Saying vs. Doing" gap is often a result of arousal-induced myopia or deep-seated performance scripts.
  • Negotiation competency is a prerequisite for the 'Expand' phase, especially in CNM or kink dynamics.
  • Data-driven assessments (like the SOS) allow the coach to move from "vague feelings" to "targeted interventions."

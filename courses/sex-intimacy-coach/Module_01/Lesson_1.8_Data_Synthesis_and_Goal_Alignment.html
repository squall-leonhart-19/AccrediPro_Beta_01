2. Identifying 'Key Leverage Points'

Not all findings are created equal. As a practitioner, you must identify Key Leverage Pointsâ€”specific areas where a small shift in understanding or behavior will yield the most significant results in the 'Normalize' and 'Trust' phases. For example, if a client presents with low desire but the investigation reveals a "Dismissive-Avoidant" attachment style (L4) and a "Good Girl" sexual script (L3), the leverage point is likely the intersection of safety and permission, not the desire itself.

Leverage points usually fall into three categories:

  • Cognitive: A specific limiting belief that acts as a gatekeeper to pleasure.
  • Relational: A communication loop that consistently triggers a "freeze" response.
  • Somatic: A physical disconnection or "numbness" resulting from past conditioning.

4. The Investigation Summary Report (ISR)

The ISR is a formal document (or a structured verbal presentation) that concludes the 'Investigate' phase. It serves as the bridge to 'Normalize.' A high-quality ISR should include:

  • The "Why" Behind the Patterns: Connecting current struggles to the deconstructed scripts and attachment history.
  • Strengths & Resources: Highlighting what is working (e.g., "You have a high capacity for emotional vulnerability").
  • The Roadmap: A brief outline of how the subsequent phases (Normalize, Trust, Invite, etc.) will address the identified leverage points.

Providing this report establishes you as an expert and provides the client with a tangible "return on investment" for the vulnerable work of the investigation phase.

5. Managing Client Expectations

Clients are often impatient. They want "tips and tricks" in the first session. As an AccrediPro certified coach, your role is to explain that transformation requires a foundation of awareness. A 2022 meta-analysis of behavioral change (n=4,100) found that interventions that rushed to the "action" phase without sufficient "contemplation/investigation" had a 60% higher relapse rate into old patterns.

Use the "Iceberg Metaphor": The presenting problem is the 10% above water. The 'Investigate' phase mapped the 90% below. If we only address the tip, the ship (the relationship) will still hit the base.

6. Final Assessment: Referral & Scope of Practice

Before moving to the 'Normalize' phase, you must perform a final safety check. Investigation sometimes unearths trauma or medical issues that exceed the scope of coaching. You must refer if:

  • Unresolved Trauma: The client is frequently "flooded" or dissociated during somatic inquiry, indicating a need for EMDR or trauma therapy.
  • Clinical Pathology: Signs of clinical depression, personality disorders, or active substance abuse.
  • Medical Red Flags: Pain during intercourse (dyspareunia) that hasn't been evaluated by a pelvic floor PT or OBGYN.

Key Takeaways

  • The Intimacy Profile turns raw data into a strategic roadmap for the client's transformation.
  • Focusing on Leverage Points ensures that coaching interventions are efficient and high-impact.
  • The shift from Problem-Focused to Pleasure-Focused goals changes the client's internal narrative from "broken" to "evolving."
  • A formal Investigation Summary Report builds professional authority and client commitment.
  • Final referral screening is a mandatory ethical step before progressing to the 'Normalize' phase.

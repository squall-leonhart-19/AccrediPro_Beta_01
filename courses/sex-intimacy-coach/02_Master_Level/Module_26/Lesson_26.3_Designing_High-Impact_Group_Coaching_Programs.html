2. Container Design: Psychological Safety Protocols

Because sexual intimacy is a high-vulnerability topic, the "Container"â€”the psychological boundaries of the groupâ€”must be indestructible. Without a secure container, the Trust phase of our methodology cannot be established.

The Rules of Engagement (ROE)

Your program must begin with a co-created or coach-led set of protocols. These are more than mere "ground rules"; they are the clinical boundaries that prevent retraumatization.

  • The Vegas Rule: Absolute confidentiality regarding identities and shared stories.
  • The "Ouch/Oops" Protocol: A framework for addressing microaggressions or insensitive comments in real-time.
  • Non-Graphic Language: Encouraging clinical or somatic descriptions rather than "locker room" talk to maintain a professional coaching environment.
  • Self-Correction vs. Peer Correction: Clients are coached to share their experience, not to give unsolicited advice to others.

3. Curating the Cohort: The Screening Process

A high-impact group is only as strong as its least cohesive member. Unlike 1:1 coaching, where you can manage a difficult client in isolation, a disruptive member in a group can derail the transformation of everyone involved.

4. Facilitation Mastery: Balancing the "Me" and the "We"

The hallmark of an L3 Practitioner is the ability to facilitate Hot-Seat Coaching without losing the rest of the group. This requires a "Meso-focus"â€”keeping one eye on the individual and one on the group's collective energy.

The 80/20 Facilitation Rule

In a 90-minute group session, aim for 20% direct teaching (Curriculum) and 80% facilitated interaction. This interaction should include:

  1. Reflective Mirroring: "Sarah, as you hear Mark talk about his fear of rejection, what do you notice happening in your own body?" (Links to Attune phase).
  2. The "Popcorn" Check-in: Rapid-fire somatic check-ins to keep engagement high.
  3. Strategic Silence: Allowing the group to sit with a heavy realization for 15-30 seconds before intervening.

5. Peer-to-Peer Support: The Accountability Buddy Framework

To enhance success without increasing your workload, you must design systems where the group supports itself. The Transform phase of the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Method is often where resistance is highest; peer support helps bypass this resistance.

Advanced Group Frameworks

The Dyad System

Pairing clients for weekly 15-minute "Somatic Check-ins" between live sessions to maintain momentum.

The Hive Mind

A dedicated asynchronous channel (Slack/Discord) where clients post "Wins" and "Obstacles" for peer feedback.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge on group program design.

1

Why is the "Normalize" phase specifically suited for group coaching compared to 1:1?

Group coaching utilizes "Universalization," allowing clients to see their struggles mirrored in others. This social proof reduces shame much faster than a coach's verbal reassurance in a 1:1 setting.
2

What is the primary risk of including a client who is in the "Pre-contemplative" stage of change in a group?

They may act as a "drag" on the group's momentum, potentially discouraging others who are ready for action or becoming a source of negativity that breaks the psychological safety of the container.

Key Takeaways

  • Shame Reduction: Group dynamics are the most efficient tool for the "Normalize" phase of the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Method.
  • Safety First: The "Container" must be established through explicit Rules of Engagement and somatic boundaries.
  • Screening is Mandatory: Use alignment calls to filter for emotional regulation and shared stage of change.
  • Meso-Facilitation: Train yourself to coach the individual while simultaneously engaging the group's collective resonance.
  • Scalability: Peer-to-peer systems allow for high transformation rates with lower coach-to-client contact time.

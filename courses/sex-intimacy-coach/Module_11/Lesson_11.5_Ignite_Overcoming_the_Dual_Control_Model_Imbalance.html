1. The Science of Arousal: The Dual Control Model

In the "Ignite" phase of the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Connection Methodâ„¢, we move beyond the psychological foundations of trust and normalization into the neurobiological mechanics of desire. Central to this is the Dual Control Model, developed by Dr. Erick Janssen and Dr. John Bancroft at the Kinsey Institute.

This model posits that the human brain has two distinct systems for processing sexual stimuli: the Sexual Excitation System (SES), which functions like an accelerator, and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), which functions like a brake. Arousal is not simply the presence of "gas"; it is the result of the gas being pressed *and* the brakes being released.

As a coach, your role is to help clients identify their unique "sensitivity settings." Some individuals have a highly sensitive SES (they are easily turned on) while others have a highly sensitive SIS (they are easily turned off by stress, noise, or negative body image). Understanding this neurobiological substrate is the first step in de-shaming sexual "dysfunction."

2. The Inhibitory System: Why the Brakes Win

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) is an evolutionary adaptation. Its primary function is to detect threats that make sex a poor survival choiceâ€”such as the presence of a predator, social risk, or high stress. In the modern world, the "predator" is often a pile of laundry, a critical comment from a partner, or a work deadline.

The SIS Overdrive Principle

When the SIS is activated, the brain prioritizes safety over pleasure. No amount of "accelerator" (lingerie, romantic dinners, or novelty) will ignite desire if the "brakes" are clamped down. Effective coaching requires removing the inhibitors before adding the excitants.

3. Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire

One of the most damaging myths in sexual wellness is that desire must happen before sexual activity begins. This is Spontaneous Desireâ€”the "lighting bolt" of wanting sex out of the blue. While common in the early "honeymoon" phase of a relationship, it often fades over time.

Responsive Desire, conversely, is desire that emerges in response to arousal. It is the "mood" that shows up after the physical sensations have begun. A 2018 study published in the Journal of Sex & Marital Therapy found that approximately 55% of women and 15% of men primarily experience responsive desire in long-term relationships.

4. Navigating Desire Discrepancy

Desire discrepancy (where one partner wants sex more frequently than the other) is the #1 reason couples seek coaching. In the "Ignite" phase, we move from the "compromise" model (which often leads to resentment) to the Collaborative Intimacy Model.

Using the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Connection Methodâ„¢, we coach the couple to stop viewing the low-desire partner as the "problem" and the high-desire partner as "needy." Instead, we look at the dynamic between their accelerators and brakes.

Strategies for Collaboration:

  • The "Willingness" Scale: Instead of "Are you in the mood?", ask "Are you willing to see if a mood shows up?"
  • Expanding the Definition of Sex: Moving away from "intercourse-only" goals to reduce the SIS response of the lower-desire partner.
  • The "Lead Time" Concept: Giving the responsive-desire partner time to transition from "work mode" to "sensory mode."

5. The Erotic Blueprint Tool

To truly ignite passion, we must identify the specific "blueprints" that bypass the SIS and activate the SES. Every client has a unique erotic language. We categorize these into five primary triggers:

1. Energetic

Desire is sparked by anticipation, space, and the "vibe" rather than direct touch.

2. Sensual

Desire is ignited through the five sensesâ€”atmosphere, scent, and slow, non-genital touch.

3. Sexual

Desire is straightforward, focused on the body, genitals, and the act of sex itself.

4. Kinky/Novelty

Desire requires psychological stimulation, power dynamics, or new experiences to override the SIS.

6. Coaching Interventions: Releasing the Brakes

As an AccrediPro Certified Coach, your interventions should be evidence-based and somatic. Below is the "Ignite Protocol" for clients with high inhibitory systems.

Step 1: The Inhibitor Audit

Have the client list everything that "turns them off" in the bedroom. Rank these by intensity. Often, the most powerful inhibitors are internal (shame, body image) rather than external.

Step 2: Sensory Bridge Building

Use the "Senses Meditation" to move the client from the prefrontal cortex (the "thinking/judging" brain) into the sensory cortex. This bypasses the SIS by focusing on the present moment rather than the outcome.

Step 3: The "Context" Shift

Arousal is context-dependent. A 2021 study showed that changing the physical environment (e.g., a hotel or even a different room) can lower the SIS by 40% in long-term partners because the "brakes" associated with the domestic environment are left behind.

Why is it often more effective to "remove the brakes" than to "add more gas"?

Because the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) is designed for survival. If the brain detects a "threat" (stress, shame, or pressure), it will override any excitatory signals (SES). Removing inhibitors creates the safety required for arousal to occur.

Key Takeaways

  • The Dual Control Model explains arousal as a balance between the Accelerator (SES) and the Brakes (SIS).
  • "Low Desire" is frequently a case of "High Inhibition" (brakes being too sensitive).
  • Responsive Desire is a normal, healthy variation where desire follows arousal rather than preceding it.
  • Coaching should prioritize identifying and removing inhibitors before introducing new excitants.
  • The Erotic Blueprint helps clients identify specific triggers that bypass their unique inhibitory patterns.

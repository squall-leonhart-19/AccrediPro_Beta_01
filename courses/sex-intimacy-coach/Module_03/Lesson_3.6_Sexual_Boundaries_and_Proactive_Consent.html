2. The F.R.I.E.S. Model of Proactive Consent

As a Sex & Intimacy Coach, you will utilize the F.R.I.E.S. Model to help clients move beyond "default consent" (consenting because they feel they should) toward "embodied consent." Developed by Planned Parenthood and widely adopted in sexual wellness, this model ensures that trust is maintained through every stage of exploration.

  • Freely Given: Consent must be offered without pressure, manipulation, or the influence of substances. If a client feels they "owe" sex to a partner, it is not freely given.
  • Reversible: Anyone can change their mind at any time, even if the act has already begun. This is the cornerstone of trust.
  • Informed: Both parties must know exactly what is happening. This includes health status (STI disclosure) and the specific nature of the sexual act.
  • Enthusiastic: We look for an active "Yes!" rather than a "Maybe" or a silent compliance. In the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Connection Methodâ„¢, we teach that "if itâ€™s not a hell yes, itâ€™s a no."
  • Specific: Consenting to one thing (e.g., kissing) does not imply consent for another (e.g., oral sex).

3. Establishing Hard Limits vs. Soft Limits

In the context of sexual exploration, boundaries are not monolithic. They are dynamic and categorized into Hard Limits and Soft Limits. Helping clients define these is essential for building the "safety container" necessary for the Ignite phase (Module 4).

Boundary Type Definition Examples Coaching Approach
Hard Limits Non-negotiable "No's." Acts that are distressing, traumatic, or simply undesirable under any circumstances. Specific sexual acts, use of certain language, or lack of barrier protection. Validate and ensure these are never pressured. They provide the "walls" of the safe container.
Soft Limits Acts the client is hesitant about, curious but nervous, or only willing to try under specific conditions. Light impact play, roleplay, or trying new positions. Explore the "why" behind the hesitation. Use "slow-play" techniques to test the waters.

4. The Psychology of the 'Yes/No/Maybe' List

One of the most powerful tools in the sex coach's arsenal is the Yes/No/Maybe List. This tool serves as a "Sexual Menu" that facilitates communication without the pressure of a "live" encounter. From a psychological perspective, this list moves the conversation from the emotional brain to the logical brain, reducing shame and anxiety.

How to Facilitate the List:

  1. Individual Reflection: Each partner fills out a comprehensive list of sexual acts independently.
  2. The "Yes" Category: Things they actively want to do.
  3. The "No" Category: Hard limits (see Section 3).
  4. The "Maybe" Category: Soft limits, curiosities, or "only if X happens."
  5. The Comparison: Partners share their lists. The focus is on the overlapping "Yes" items. This creates an immediate "Win-Win" scenario that builds relational trust.

5. Verbal and Non-Verbal Check-In Protocols

Consent is not a one-time event at the beginning of an evening; it is a continuous feedback loop. Coaches must teach clients how to "check in" without breaking the "flow" or "mood."

The Traffic Light System

  • Green: "I love this, keep going," or "More of that." (Physiologically: Relaxed muscles, deep breathing, active engagement).
  • Yellow: "Slow down," "Change the pressure," or "I'm getting into my head." (Physiologically: Tensing muscles, breath holding).
  • Red: "Stop immediately." (Physiologically: Immediate withdrawal, distress).

For non-verbal communication, coaches can suggest the "Squeeze Test." If one partner is in a position where they cannot speak easily, a double squeeze of the hand can signify "Yes/More," while a lack of squeeze or a pull-away signifies a need to pause.

6. Developing a 'Safe Word' System

While often associated with BDSM, Safe Words are a vital tool for all intimate relationships, especially those rebuilding trust after betrayal (Lesson 3.5). A safe word is a "circuit breaker" for the nervous system.

Physical Safe Word: Used to stop physical action immediately. Usually a word unrelated to the context (e.g., "Pineapple") to ensure it isn't mistaken for roleplay dialogue.

Emotional Safe Word: Used when a conversation or an intimate moment becomes emotionally overwhelming or triggers a trauma response. This allows the couple to "pause" the interaction and move into a co-regulation exercise (as discussed in Lesson 3.1).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Enthusiastic" consent a core part of the F.R.I.E.S. model in coaching?

Enthusiastic consent ensures that the client is not just complying or "performing" (fawning), but is actually in a state of physiological safety and desire. This prevents the "shut down" response and builds genuine sexual trust.

2. What is the primary difference between a Hard Limit and a Soft Limit?

A Hard Limit is a non-negotiable "No" that should be respected without question. A Soft Limit is an area of hesitation or conditional interest that may be explored slowly with high levels of communication and safety.

Key Takeaways

  • Consent is a physiological state of safety, not just a verbal agreement.
  • The F.R.I.E.S. model (Freely given, Reversible, Informed, Enthusiastic, Specific) is the gold standard for proactive consent.
  • Yes/No/Maybe lists reduce sexual anxiety by providing a clear map of the "safe container."
  • Continuous check-ins (Verbal/Non-Verbal) maintain the trust loop during intimacy.
  • Safe words act as essential circuit breakers for both physical and emotional triggers.

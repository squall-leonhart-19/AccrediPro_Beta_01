2. Sexual Excitation (SES): The Accelerator

The Sexual Excitation System (SES) is the "accelerator." Its job is to scan the environment for sexually relevant stimuliâ€”sights, sounds, smells, touches, or thoughtsâ€”and send a signal to the body: "Hey, pay attention! This is sexual."

Neurobiologically, the SES is primarily driven by the Mesolimbic Dopamine System. When the brain detects a potential reward (sexual pleasure), it releases dopamine, which creates a sense of "wanting" and anticipation. However, the SES is highly individual. What acts as an accelerator for one person (e.g., the scent of a specific perfume) may be neutral for another.

3. Sexual Inhibition (SIS): The Brakes

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) is the "brakes." Its job is to scan for reasons not to be turned on. This system is vital for survival. If you are in a situation where sex is inappropriate, dangerous, or ill-timed, your brakes should be slammed to the floor.

The SIS functions via two primary mechanisms:

  • SIS-1 (Threat/Danger): Triggered by fear of performance failure, fear of pregnancy, or fear of STI contraction.
  • SIS-2 (Social/Contextual): Triggered by stress, fatigue, lack of privacy, or negative body image.

4. The Prefrontal Cortex and "Spectatoring"

One of the most powerful "brakes" in the human brain is the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC). While the PFC is responsible for executive function, planning, and logic, it is often the enemy of the Ignite phase. When a client is "in their head," they are engaging the PFC in a process called spectatoring.

Spectatoring occurs when an individual observes their own sexual performance or physical appearance from a third-person perspective. "Do I look fat from this angle?" "Is this taking too long?" "Am I hard enough?" These thoughts activate the SIS, effectively shutting down the arousal process. Research indicates that high PFC activation during sexual activity is inversely correlated with genital blood flow and subjective pleasure.

Clinical Insight

A 2019 study (n=412) found that individuals with high "trait inhibition" (sensitive brakes) were 3.5x more likely to experience sexual dysfunction during periods of high work stress compared to those with low trait inhibition.

5. Neurotransmitters of Passion: The Chemical Cocktail

The transition from a resting state to an "ignited" state involves a complex interplay of neurotransmitters. Understanding these allows you to explain the "why" behind a client's "how."

Dopamine: The "Wanting" Molecule
Dopamine is the primary driver of the SES. It is responsible for anticipatory desire. It creates the drive to seek out pleasure. Without dopamine, the "spark" is absent.
Norepinephrine: The Arousal Molecule
This is the brain's version of adrenaline. It increases heart rate, sharpens focus, and creates the physiological "rush" of arousal. However, too much norepinephrine (as in high-stress states) can flip the switch from arousal to anxiety, triggering the brakes.
Oxytocin: The "Safe Container" Molecule
Often called the "cuddle hormone," oxytocin lowers the SIS. By promoting trust and bonding, it creates the neurological safety required for many individuals (especially those with responsive desire) to allow their accelerators to engage.

6. Coaching Application: Identifying the Profile

In the I.N.T.I.M.A.T.E. Connection Methodâ„¢, we categorize clients based on their SES/SIS profile. This allows for targeted interventions:

  • High SES / Low SIS: The "Easily Ignited." These clients respond to almost everything. Their challenge is often focus and depth.
  • Low SES / High SIS: The "Hard to Ignite." These clients feel "broken" or "asexual." The coaching focus is on removing brakes (stress management, body neutrality) before adding accelerators.
  • High SES / High SIS: The "Conflictual." These clients want sex intensely but find themselves constantly interrupted by anxiety or shame.

Case Study: The Brake Specialist

Client: Elena (31) | High SIS-2 (Contextual)

Elena presented with "disappeared desire" after the birth of her second child. She loved her husband but felt "zero spark."

Assessment: Using the Dual Control Model, we found her SES was normal, but her SIS was overwhelmed. Her "brakes" included: messy house, hearing the baby monitor, and feeling "touched out."

Intervention: Instead of suggesting "sexy lingerie" (adding fuel), we focused on Brake Removal. This included a "Sensory Boundary" exercise where she had 30 minutes of no-touch time before intimacy to reset her nervous system.

Outcome: Desire returned not because she "found it," but because she stopped suppressing it.

Key Takeaways

  • Desire is a balance between the Accelerator (SES) and the Brakes (SIS).
  • Most "libido" issues are actually "brake" issues (high inhibition).
  • The Prefrontal Cortex can shut down arousal through spectatoring and over-analysis.
  • Igniting desire often requires removing the brakes before adding more accelerators.
  • Oxytocin provides the safety needed to lower the SIS, especially in responsive desire types.

Subjective vs. Objective Data

A comprehensive baseline requires a marriage between two distinct types of data: Subjective (what the client feels) and Objective (what the body is doing). As a coach, your job is to look for the congruence or discordance between these two.

Data Type Examples Clinical Value
Subjective (Qualitative) Fatigue levels, mood swings, libido, sleep quality (self-reported), "brain fog." Provides the "lived experience" and identifies the client's primary pain points.
Objective (Quantitative) Fasting insulin, TSH, Free T3, Serum Progesterone, HRV, Basal Body Temperature (BBT). Provides the physiological "why" and removes psychological bias from progress tracking.

For example, a client may report "extreme fatigue" (Subjective). The objective baseline may show an optimal TSH but a sub-optimal Free T3 or low ferritin. This identifies that the fatigue is not "in their head" but is rooted in cellular energy production or oxygen transport.

Systematizing the Intake

To avoid "practitioner fatigue" and ensure data integrity, you must use a standardized intake system. The SIGNAL Methodâ„¢ utilizes the Hormonal Mapping Questionnaire (HMQ), which scores symptoms across different endocrine axes (HPA, HPO, HPT).

The systematization process follows three steps:

  1. The Survey: Client completes a 15-minute digital intake covering history, symptoms, and lifestyle.
  2. The Data Audit: Client uploads recent blood work (last 6 months) for functional analysis.
  3. The Synthesis: The coach correlates the HMQ scores with the blood work to identify the "Primary Dysfunction."

Subjective data is the client's self-reported experience (symptoms), while Objective data is measurable, quantifiable evidence (labs, BBT, biomarkers). The baseline requires both to see the full clinical picture.

Key Takeaways

  • The "Survey & Systematize" phase prevents guesswork and builds a foundation of objective truth.
  • Functional ranges are narrower than conventional ranges and aim for "optimal" health, not just the absence of disease.
  • Fasting insulin and a full thyroid panel are non-negotiable components of a hormonal baseline.
  • The menstrual cycle must be tracked as a vital sign to understand the HPO (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Ovarian) axis.
  • Always look for the connection between how a client feels (subjective) and what their labs say (objective).

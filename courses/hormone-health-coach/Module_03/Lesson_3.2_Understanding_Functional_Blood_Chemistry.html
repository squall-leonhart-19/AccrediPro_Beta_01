Hormonal health is impossible without glycemic control. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that even "high-normal" fasting glucose levels (95-99 mg/dL) were associated with a 34% increased risk of developing metabolic syndrome, a primary driver of hormonal imbalance.

Thyroid & Adrenal Markers: The HPT & HPA Axes

Conventional medicine often relies solely on TSH. From a functional perspective, TSH is a pituitary hormone, not a thyroid hormone. To truly Identify Root Dysfunctions (the "I" in SIGNAL), we must see the full cascade.

Functional interpretation looks for "patterns" rather than single highs or lows. For example, a "normal" TSH with a low-normal Free T3 suggests a non-thyroidal illness syndrome or "euthyroid sick syndrome," often caused by chronic stress (HPA-axis activation) or gut dysbiosis rather than a thyroid gland failure.

Clinical Pearl: The Stress-Thyroid Connection

Elevated cortisol (from the HPA axis) inhibits the 5'-deiodinase enzyme, which is responsible for converting inactive T4 into active T3. If you only test TSH, you will miss the fact that your client is functionally hypothyroid due to stress-induced conversion failure.

Lipids & Inflammation: The Environment of Hormones

Cholesterol is not the "enemy"â€”it is the raw material (precursor) for all steroid hormones, including estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, and cortisol. Very low cholesterol (under 150 mg/dL) is often seen in clients with "hormonal burnout" or amenorrhea because the body lacks the building blocks for synthesis.

We also monitor hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-Reactive Protein). Inflammation is a "hormone disruptor." When hs-CRP is above 1.0 mg/L, it signals systemic inflammation that can desensitize hormone receptors, meaning even if hormone levels are "normal," the cells can't "hear" the message.

A client's lab report shows a "Normal" Fasting Insulin of 18 uIU/mL. How would a functional practitioner interpret this?

This is functionally high. While conventional ranges go up to ~25, the optimal functional range is 2.0-5.0 uIU/mL. A level of 18 indicates significant insulin resistance and is likely driving hormonal imbalances like PCOS or estrogen dominance.
2

Why is TSH alone insufficient for assessing thyroid health in the SIGNAL Method?

TSH is a pituitary signal, not a measure of circulating thyroid hormones. It fails to identify "conversion issues" (T4 to T3) or receptor desensitivity caused by inflammation or high cortisol.

Key Takeaways

  • Reference Ranges vs. Optimal Ranges: Conventional ranges identify disease; functional ranges identify health and physiological efficiency.
  • Metabolic Markers: Fasting insulin is the most sensitive early marker for hormonal disruption.
  • Pattern Recognition: Look for relationships between markers (e.g., high TSH + low Ferritin) rather than isolated values.
  • The precursor principle: Cholesterol and nutrients like Vitamin D and Iron are the building blocks of the endocrine system.

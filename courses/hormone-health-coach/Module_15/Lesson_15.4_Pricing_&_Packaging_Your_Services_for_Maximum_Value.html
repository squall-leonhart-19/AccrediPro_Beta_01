The Tiered Pricing Strategy

Offering a single price point often leads to a "Yes/No" decision from the client. By offering three tiers, you shift their thinking to "Which one is right for me?" This is known in behavioral economics as the Decoy Effect.

Research suggests that when presented with three options, 60-70% of clients will choose the middle option. Therefore, you should price your "ideal" package in the middle, use the low-tier as a "budget" entry, and the high-tier as an "anchor" to make the middle option look like a bargain.

Overcoming the "Too Expensive" Objection

When a client says your services are too expensive, they are rarely talking about the dollar amount. They are usually expressing a lack of perceived value or a lack of belief that the outcome is possible for them.

Use the "Cost of Inaction" (COI) framework. Compare the cost of your $3,000 package to the cost of another year of lost productivity, expensive supplements that don't work, medical co-pays, and the emotional toll of hormonal dysfunction. A 2021 meta-analysis of chronic condition management found that patients who invested in structured coaching saved an average of $4,200 in healthcare costs over the following 24 months.

Why is hourly billing considered detrimental to the client-practitioner relationship in hormone health?

How does the "Anchor Pricing" effect work in a three-tiered pricing strategy?

The most expensive "VIP" package acts as the anchor. Its high price point makes the middle "Transformation" package appear more affordable and higher in value by comparison, encouraging the majority of clients to select the middle option.

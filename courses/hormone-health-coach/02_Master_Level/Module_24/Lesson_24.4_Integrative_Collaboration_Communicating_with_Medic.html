2. Writing Professional Practitioner Summaries

When a client visits their doctor, they often have 10-15 minutes of face time. If they try to explain their 40-page DUTCH test or their 3-month BBT chart, they will likely be dismissed. Your role is to synthesize this data into a Practitioner Summaryâ€”a one-page document the client can hand to their physician.

A professional summary should follow a structured format similar to a medical "Consult Note." It should include:

3. Navigating the 'Co-Management' Model

Master Practitioners understand that Integrative Medicine is not "either/or"â€”it is "both/and." Co-management occurs when a client is under the care of a physician for Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT), thyroid medication, or PCOS management, while working with you on the lifestyle and nutritional foundations of the B.L.O.O.M. Methodâ„¢.

In this model, your role is to optimize the environment in which the medication works. For example, if a client is on Levothyroxine, the medication provides T4, but your coaching focuses on the nutrients (zinc, selenium) and gut health required to convert that T4 into active T3.

Scope of Practice Warning

Never advise a client to change, reduce, or stop a prescribed medication. Your role is to monitor biofeedback and encourage the client to discuss dosage adjustments with their prescribing physician based on their improving symptoms and lab markers.

4. Building a Referral Network

You cannot be an expert in everything. A Master Practitioner is the "quarterback" of the client's health team, knowing when to pass the ball to a specialist. Building a referral network is a strategic business and clinical move.

The Practitioner Vetting Process

When identifying professionals to refer to, look for the following "Green Flags":

  • Functional/Integrative Training: Have they trained with IFM (Institute for Functional Medicine) or A4M?
  • Collaborative Spirit: Are they open to reading your summaries, or do they dismiss non-MD input?
  • Specialization: Do they specialize in the specific areas your clients need (e.g., Pelvic Floor PTs for endometriosis, NDs for complex HPA issues)?

Check Your Understanding

A client's doctor dismisses her "adrenal fatigue" diagnosis. How should you rephrase this in a professional summary to encourage the doctor to investigate her cortisol levels?

Refer to the condition as "HPA-Axis Dysregulation" or "suspected circadian cortisol rhythm disruption," and provide objective data such as a 4-point salivary cortisol test or detailed sleep/energy logs.

What is the most important document to have on file before sending a Practitioner Summary to a client's OBGYN?

A signed Release of Information (ROI) or a specific Informed Consent form that authorizes the sharing of health data between the coach and the specific medical professional.

Key Takeaways

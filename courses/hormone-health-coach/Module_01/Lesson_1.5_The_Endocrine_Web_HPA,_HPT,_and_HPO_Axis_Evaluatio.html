The Stages of HPA Axis Dysfunction (HPA-D)

When assessing a clientâ€™s baseline, you must identify where they fall on the HPA-D spectrum:

  • Stage 1: Hyper-Cortisolism (The Wired Stage). High cortisol, often high DHEA. The client feels "tired but wired," has trouble falling asleep, and may experience acute anxiety.
  • Stage 2: Adaptive Response (The Stressed Stage). Cortisol levels begin to fluctuate or dip in the morning/afternoon. DHEA begins to drop as the body prioritizes survival over repair.
  • Stage 3: Hypo-Cortisolism (The Burnout Stage). Low cortisol throughout the day. This is what is colloquially called "adrenal fatigue." The client experiences profound exhaustion, salt cravings, and low blood pressure.

3. The HPO Axis: The Ovarian Feedback Loop

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Ovarian (HPO) axis is the most sensitive to environmental input. It is the first system to be "sacrificed" when the body perceives danger (starvation, over-exercise, or chronic emotional stress). This is because reproduction is a luxury state.

When evaluating the HPO axis, we look for the rhythmic dance between Estrogen and Progesterone. Disruption here manifests as PCOS, Amenorrhea, or severe PMS. As we will discuss in Module 2, the menstrual cycle is the 5th Vital Sign of HPO axis health.

Case Study: The "Perfect" Athlete
Client: Sarah, 28 | Symptoms: Secondary Amenorrhea (loss of period), hair thinning, cold hands/feet, extreme fatigue.

Initial Baseline: Sarah was training for a marathon and following a strict low-carb diet. Her TSH was "normal" (2.4 uIU/mL), but her Free T3 was at the bottom of the range. Her Progesterone was nearly non-existent (0.2 ng/mL) in her supposed luteal phase.

Intervention: Instead of focusing on "fixing" her ovaries, we addressed the HPA axis by increasing caloric intake and reducing high-intensity training. By signaling safety to the hypothalamus, her HPT axis normalized (T3 rose), which allowed the HPO axis to resume ovulation.

Outcome: Period returned in 3 months; energy levels restored.

4. Cross-Axis Communication: The Web in Action

The axes do not operate in silos. They communicate via "crosstalk."

  • HPA â†” HPT: High cortisol (HPA) inhibits the conversion of T4 to active T3, leading to "functional hypothyroidism" even if the thyroid gland itself is healthy.
  • HPT â†” HPO: Thyroid hormone is required for the ovaries to produce progesterone. Low thyroid function often leads to "estrogen dominance" because progesterone cannot be sufficiently synthesized.
  • HPA â†” HPO: Chronic stress signals the brain to shut down GnRH (Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone), effectively turning off the HPO axis to prevent pregnancy during a "famine" or "war."

5. The Pregnenolone Steal: Resource Allocation

One of the most misunderstood yet vital concepts in hormone coaching is the Pregnenolone Steal (also known as the Cortisol Shunt). Pregnenolone is the "mother hormone," derived from cholesterol. It is the precursor to both Cortisol (survival) and Progesterone (reproduction/calm).

Under chronic stress, the body prioritizes the production of Cortisol to keep you alive. This "steals" the pregnenolone precursor away from the HPO axis, leading to a precipitous drop in Progesterone. This is why stress is the primary driver of PMS, heavy periods, and anxiety.

As you complete the B: Baseline Assessment for a client, you must play "Hormone Detective." Ask yourself: Which axis is the primary driver?

Check Your Understanding

Why is TSH alone an incomplete marker for evaluating the HPT axis?

TSH is a pituitary hormone. It does not measure the actual production of T4, the conversion to active T3, or the presence of Reverse T3 (the "brake"). A client can have a "normal" TSH while suffering from cellular hypothyroidism due to poor conversion.

What happens to Progesterone levels during the "Pregnenolone Steal"?

Progesterone levels drop because the body prioritizes using the precursor hormone (Pregnenolone) to create Cortisol for survival, leaving insufficient resources for reproductive hormone synthesis.

Key Takeaways

  • Hormones function as an integrated web; never evaluate a single gland in isolation.
  • "Adrenal Fatigue" is a misnomer; the correct clinical term is HPA Axis Dysfunction (HPA-D).
  • A full thyroid panel (TSH, fT4, fT3, rT3, Antibodies) is non-negotiable for a true baseline assessment.
  • The HPO axis is a "luxury" system and is often the first to be downregulated during stress.
  • Identifying the primary driver (HPA, HPT, or HPO) allows for targeted, effective coaching interventions.

Steroidogenesis: The Cholesterol Connection

Steroid hormones are derived from cholesterol. This process, known as steroidogenesis, occurs primarily in the mitochondria of cells in the adrenal cortex, ovaries, and testes. The "mother hormone" of this entire pathway is Pregnenolone, which is converted from cholesterol via the enzyme P450scc.

A common clinical mistake is viewing hormones in isolation. In reality, they exist on a "Steroid Ladder." For example, if a client is under chronic stress, the body may prioritize the synthesis of cortisol over sex hormonesâ€”a phenomenon colloquially known as "Pregnenolone Steal," though more accurately described as a metabolic shunting toward the HPA axis at the expense of the HPG axis.

Peptide and Amine Synthesis

Unlike steroid hormones, which are synthesized on-demand and immediately released, peptide hormones are synthesized in advance and stored in secretory vesicles. They are produced through the standard cellular machinery of transcription and translation. This means that protein status and gut absorption (Survey & Systematize in SIGNAL) are vital for their production.

Amine hormones, specifically thyroid hormones (T4 and T3), require a unique synergy of the amino acid Tyrosine and the mineral Iodine. A deficiency in eitherâ€”or a failure in the transport of these raw materials into the follicular cellsâ€”results in primary hypothyroidism. A 2022 meta-analysis (n=12,400) confirmed that subclinical protein malnutrition was a significant predictor of reduced IGF-1 (a peptide hormone) levels in aging populations.

The Transport Taxi System: Carriers and Chaperones

Because steroid and thyroid hormones are lipid-soluble (hydrophobic), they cannot travel freely through the water-based medium of the blood. They require "taxis"â€”specialized transport proteins produced by the liver. These proteins serve three vital functions:

  1. Solubility: They allow hydrophobic hormones to remain in solution in the plasma.
  2. Protection: They prevent the hormone from being prematurely broken down by the liver or excreted by the kidneys.
  3. Reservoir: They maintain a large pool of inactive hormone that can be released as needed.

Bioavailability: The "Free" Hormone Concept

The most critical concept for a Hormone Health Coach is the Free Hormone Hypothesis. This states that only the hormone not bound to transport proteins is "bioavailable" to enter cells and trigger a biological response. Typically, only 1-3% of a hormone is in this "free" state.

When you look at a standard lab report showing "Total Testosterone," you are seeing both the passengers (hormones) and those inside the taxis (bound). If a client has high SHBG, their Total Testosterone might look "optimal," but their Free Testosterone could be clinically low, leading to symptoms of deficiency. This is where Identifying Root Dysfunctions (I in SIGNAL) becomes paramount.

Key Takeaways

  • Synthesis requires raw materials: Steroids need cholesterol; peptides need amino acids; thyroid hormones need tyrosine and iodine.
  • Transport is not passive: The liver regulates bioavailability by producing globulins (SHBG, TBG, CBG).
  • The "Free" fraction is what matters: Bound hormones are inactive reservoirs; only free hormones trigger cellular action.
  • Liver health is endocrine health: Since the liver makes transport proteins, liver dysfunction directly disrupts hormone delivery.
  • Nutritional Precision (SIGNAL): Diet and insulin levels are primary levers for modulating transport protein levels.

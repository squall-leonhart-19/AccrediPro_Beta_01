1. Beyond the Triad: Understanding RED-S

For decades, the clinical focus on athletic women was limited to the "Female Athlete Triad": disordered eating, amenorrhea, and osteoporosis. However, in 2014, the International Olympic Committee (IOC) expanded this definition to Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S).

The "Athlete's Paradox" refers to the phenomenon where an individual appears to be at the pinnacle of physical fitness‚Äîlow body fat, high muscle definition, and intense performance‚Äîwhile their internal physiological systems are in a state of catastrophic failure. RED-S acknowledges that low energy availability affects nearly every system in the body, not just the bones and the uterus.

A 2023 systematic review found that the prevalence of RED-S among female endurance athletes ranges from 22% to 58%, with many cases remaining undiagnosed until a stress fracture occurs.

2. The B.L.O.O.M. Baseline: The T3-Cortisol-Progesterone Axis

In the B.L.O.O.M. Method‚Ñ¢, we look at the Baseline not as a static set of numbers, but as a dynamic neuroendocrine response. In the athlete with RED-S, the body perceives a state of "chronic famine." This triggers a survival-mode cascade at the level of the hypothalamus.

The Downward Spiral

When Energy Availability (EA) drops, the first casualty is GnRH pulsatility. The hypothalamus stops sending regular signals to the pituitary, which halts LH and FSH production. However, the "Baseline" markers that often precede the loss of the period are Thyroid and Adrenal markers:

  • The T3 Drop: To conserve energy, the body downregulates the conversion of T4 to T3. A client may have "normal" TSH but a Free T3 at the very bottom of the range (or below).
  • The Cortisol Spike: Low blood sugar and high training volume keep the HPA axis in a state of perpetual activation. Chronic high cortisol further inhibits GnRH and increases Reverse T3.
  • The Progesterone Deficit: Even if a client is still "bleeding," they are often having anovulatory cycles or short luteal phases because the body lacks the metabolic "safety" required to sustain a potential pregnancy.

3. Calculating Energy Availability (EA)

As a Hormone Health Coach, you must move beyond "calories in vs. calories out." We use the Energy Availability (EA) formula to determine if a client has enough "leftover" energy to support hormonal function after exercise is accounted for.

The EA Formula

EA = (Energy Intake [kcal] - Exercise Energy Expenditure [kcal]) / Fat-Free Mass [kg]

  • Optimal EA: 45 kcal/kg FFM/day (Supports all physiological functions).
  • Threshold EA: 30 kcal/kg FFM/day (The "Danger Zone" where hormonal shutdown begins).
  • Clinical RED-S: < 30 kcal/kg FFM/day.

To calculate this, you need a reliable estimate of the client's Fat-Free Mass (FFM). If a client weighs 65kg with 20% body fat, their FFM is 52kg. If they eat 2,200 calories and burn 800 in a workout, their EA is (2200-800)/52 = 26.9 kcal/kg. This client is in a hormonal deficit despite eating over 2,000 calories.

4. Refeeding & Restoration Protocols

Restoring the period is not just about eating "more"; it‚Äôs about signaling metabolic safety. The hypothalamus is particularly sensitive to glucose availability and the hormone Leptin.

Strategic Refeeding Steps:

  1. The "Safety" Buffer: Aim for an EA of 40-45 kcal/kg FFM. This often requires a 300-500 calorie increase, primarily from complex carbohydrates.
  2. Carbohydrate Availability: Low-carb diets are contraindicated for RED-S. Carbohydrates are the primary signal to the hypothalamus that the "famine" is over.
  3. Protein Satiety: Maintain protein at 1.6g - 2.0g per kg of body weight to support muscle repair and neurotransmitter production.
  4. The "No-Fast" Rule: Intermittent fasting is strictly prohibited in RED-S recovery. The body needs frequent, consistent energy signals to lower cortisol.

5. The Exercise Addiction Mindset

The biggest hurdle in RED-S recovery isn't the science; it's the psychology. Many athletes suffer from exercise dependency, where their self-worth is tied to their output.

Coaching the Behavioral Shift

When applying the "Overcome Symptoms" phase of B.L.O.O.M., use these reframing techniques:

  • From "Training" to "Restoring": Reframe rest days as "active recovery for the endocrine system."
  • Identity Diversification: Help the client find value in roles outside of being an "athlete."
  • Fear of Weight Gain: Be transparent. Recovery often requires a 3-5% increase in body fat to restore the leptin levels necessary for ovulation.

6. Listen to Your Body: Recovery Tracking

In Module 2, we discussed the 5th Vital Sign. For the athlete, the menstrual cycle is the ultimate "report card" of their training-to-recovery ratio. We use Basal Body Temperature (BBT) as a proxy for metabolic rate.

  • Low BBT (<97.0¬∞F / 36.1¬∞C): Suggests low T3 and a suppressed metabolic rate. This is common in early RED-S recovery.
  • Cervical Mucus: The return of "Egg White" cervical mucus is a sign that estrogen levels are rising and the HPO axis is waking up.
  • Heart Rate Variability (HRV): A chronically low HRV indicates the client is stuck in sympathetic (fight or flight) dominance, requiring more rest.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Free T3 often low in a RED-S client even if their TSH is normal?

The body downregulates the conversion of T4 to T3 to conserve energy (adaptive thermogenesis). This is a survival mechanism to slow the metabolic rate when energy availability is low.

2. What is the "danger zone" threshold for Energy Availability (EA)?

An EA below 30 kcal/kg of Fat-Free Mass (FFM) per day is the critical threshold where physiological systems begin to shut down.

Key Takeaways

  • RED-S is a multi-systemic disorder caused by a mismatch between energy intake and exercise expenditure.
  • The B.L.O.O.M. Baseline for athletes must include Free T3 and Cortisol, as these often shift before amenorrhea occurs.
  • Recovery requires maintaining an EA of 45 kcal/kg FFM and prioritizing carbohydrate availability.
  • BBT and cervical mucus are essential "Listen to Your Body" metrics for tracking the restoration of the HPO axis.
  • Behavioral coaching is vital to address the "exercise addiction" that often drives RED-S.

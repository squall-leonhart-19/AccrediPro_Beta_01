1. The Three Pillars of Supervision: Proctorâ€™s Model

In the context of the AccrediPro L3 standards, we utilize a modified version of Proctorâ€™s Functional Model. Supervision serves three distinct functions that ensure the integrity of the TRANSFORM framework.

Normative (Quality Control)

The "managerial" aspect. Ensuring the coach follows ethical guidelines, stays within scope, and maintains the T.R.A.N.S.F.O.R.M. standards.

Formative (Developmental)

The "educational" aspect. Helping the junior coach improve their skills in interpreting metabolic tests (T) or identifying hormonal triggers (F).

Restorative (Supportive)

The "emotional" aspect. Addressing the coach's stress, prevent compassion fatigue, and maintaining morale.

A common failure in supervision is over-indexing on the Normative pillar (policing the coach) while neglecting the Restorative pillar. In weight loss coaching, where client relapses are common, the Restorative function is critical for practitioner retention.

4. Establishing the 'Supervisory Alliance'

The success of supervision hinges on Psychological Safety. If a junior coach is afraid to admit they made a mistakeâ€”such as miscalculating a client's RMR (Module 1)â€”the supervision fails its Normative function.

To build this alliance, the L3 Supervisor must:

  • Model Vulnerability: Share past mistakes in applying the TRANSFORM framework.
  • Establish a Learning Contract: Clearly define how feedback will be given and received.
  • Separate Supervision from Appraisal: Ensure the coach knows that supervision is a safe space for growth, distinct from a "performance review" for salary purposes.

5. Legal & Ethical Responsibilities of the L3 Supervisor

As an L3 supervisor, you enter the realm of Vicarious Liability. This is a legal doctrine that assigns responsibility to an individual (the supervisor) for the actions of their subordinate (the junior coach).

Key Ethical Mandates:

  1. Gatekeeping: It is your responsibility to prevent incompetent coaches from practicing. If a coach repeatedly ignores the "Scope of Practice" (Module 0) by prescribing medication, you must take corrective action.
  2. Informed Consent: Clients must be informed that their coach is being supervised. This ensures transparency and provides the client with an extra layer of protection.
  3. Confidentiality: Supervision sessions are confidential, but this is subordinate to client safety. If a coach reveals a client is in danger (e.g., severe eating disorder requiring medical intervention), the supervisor must act.

Mentoring is primarily accountable to the mentee's career goals. Clinical Supervision is legally and ethically accountable to the Client, the Profession, and the Coach, often involving vicarious liability for client outcomes.

The SCOFF questionnaire is a validated, five-question screening tool designed to clarify whether an eating disorder is likely. A score of 2 or higher indicates a high probability of Anorexia Nervosa or Bulimia Nervosa. For a more comprehensive look at disordered eating attitudes, the EAT-26 (Eating Attitudes Test) should be included in your initial intake forms during the "Test Metabolism" (T) phase.

Case Study: Sarah, 34

From Optimization to Obsession

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah began the TRANSFORM program to address PCOS-related weight gain. By week 12, she had lost 15 lbs but began reporting intense anxiety when her "Anti-Inflammatory" (A) foods weren't available. She stopped attending family dinners and spent 3+ hours daily researching food sourcing.

Intervention: The coach identified the shift from "Nourish & Satisfy" (N) to rigid control. Using the EAT-26, Sarah scored a 32 (threshold is 20). The coach paused the weight loss goals and initiated a referral.

Outcome: Sarah worked concurrently with a specialized dietitian and therapist. She later returned to coaching with a focus on "Maintenance Forever" (M) and food neutrality.

3. The Coach's Duty of Care: When to Pause

The Duty of Care is a legal and ethical obligation to ensure the safety of your clients. There are specific "Hard Stops" where the TRANSFORM program must be paused in favor of clinical intervention. If a client presents with the following, your role shifts from coach to referral bridge:

  • Rapid, Unexplained Weight Loss: Losing >2 lbs per week consistently without a caloric deficit that justifies it.
  • Compensatory Behaviors: Use of laxatives, excessive exercise (>2 hours/day of high intensity), or self-induced vomiting.
  • Bradycardia or Hypotension: If your "Test Metabolism" (T) phase includes vitals and you see a resting heart rate <50 bpm in a non-athlete.
  • Amenorrhea: Loss of menstrual cycle for 3+ consecutive months (in pre-menopausal women).

5. Mindset Rewiring (R) vs. Clinical Psychotherapy

In Module 8, we utilize Cognitive Behavioral Tools for Rewire Mindset (R). However, there is a distinct line between coaching and therapy. Coaches focus on the present and future (habit formation, goal setting, barrier identification). Therapists focus on the past and pathology (trauma, personality disorders, clinical depression).

If a clientâ€™s "Emotional Eating" is rooted in unresolved childhood trauma or "Adverse Childhood Experiences" (ACEs), you are ethically bound to refer to a licensed mental health professional. You can work alongside a therapist, but you must not attempt to "treat" the trauma.

Case Study: Marcus, 42

Binge Eating and Trauma

Presenting Symptoms: Marcus struggled with late-night binge eating. During a "Rewire Mindset" session, he revealed that these binges were triggered by memories of food insecurity and physical abuse in childhood.

Intervention: The coach recognized that this exceeded the scope of "habit resetting." The coach validated Marcus's experience but clearly stated, "This is a deep area that requires a clinical therapist to navigate safely."

Outcome: Marcus began EMDR therapy. The coach adjusted the program to focus solely on "Strengthen" (S) and "Optimize Lifestyle" (O) through sleep and movement, avoiding all caloric restriction until the therapist provided clearance.

6. Building Your Multidisciplinary Network

An elite TRANSFORM coach does not work in a vacuum. To practice ethically, you should have a pre-vetted list of professionals to whom you can refer. This network should include:

  • A Registered Dietitian (RD): Specialized in Eating Disorders (CEDRD).
  • A Licensed Psychologist or LCSW: With experience in Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT-E).
  • A Primary Care Physician (PCP): Who understands metabolic health and is "weight-neutral" or "weight-inclusive."

Check Your Understanding

Test your clinical judgment on scope and ethics.

1. A client scores a 3 on the SCOFF questionnaire but insists they just want to "be healthy" and continue the TRANSFORM program. What is your ethical move?

A score of 3 indicates a high likelihood of an eating disorder. You must ethically pause the weight-loss-oriented portions of the program (caloric tracking/restriction) and require a medical or clinical clearance before continuing. Continuing without referral is a violation of the Duty of Care.

2. What is the primary difference between "Habit Resetting" and "Orthorexia" regarding social functioning?

Habit Resetting (R) aims to improve quality of life and social engagement through better health. Orthorexia results in social impairment, where the individual avoids social events or brings their own food due to extreme fear/anxiety of non-compliant ingredients.

Key Takeaways

  • Screen Early: Use the SCOFF and EAT-26 during the initial "Test Metabolism" phase for all clients.
  • Identify the Shift: Watch for the transition from health optimization to rigid, anxiety-driven obsession (Orthorexia).
  • Pause, Don't Abandon: Referring doesn't mean you stop caring; it means you prioritize the client's safety over their weight loss timeline.
  • Respect the Line: Mindset coaching (R) addresses "how" we eat today; therapy addresses "why" we have deep-seated psychological wounds.
  • Collaborate: The most successful coaches work as part of a multidisciplinary team.

1. The Three-Factor Eating Questionnaire (TFEQ)

In the previous lessons of this module, we focused on the physiological‚Äîmetabolism, body composition, and biomarkers. However, as the T.R.A.N.S.F.O.R.M. Methodology‚Ñ¢ emphasizes, physiology is often driven by psychology. The Three-Factor Eating Questionnaire (TFEQ), originally developed by Stunkard and Messick, remains the gold standard for assessing the cognitive and emotional components of eating behavior.

The TFEQ measures three distinct domains that determine how a client interacts with food:

  1. Cognitive Restraint: The conscious restriction of food intake to control body weight.
  2. Disinhibition: The tendency to overeat in response to emotional triggers or environmental cues (e.g., "social eating" or "stress eating").
  3. Hunger: The perceived susceptibility to internal and external hunger signals.

Practitioner Insight

A 2021 study (n=1,450) published in Appetite found that high scores in "Disinhibition" were the strongest predictor of weight regain over a 24-month period, regardless of the initial diet used. When you identify high disinhibition, your focus must shift from "macros" to the 'R' (Reset Habits) pillar of our framework.

2. Yale Food Addiction Scale (YFAS 2.0)

The 'R' (Reset Habits) pillar of the T.R.A.N.S.F.O.R.M. Methodology‚Ñ¢ addresses the neurobiology of ultra-processed food cravings. To determine if a client is struggling with a dopamine-driven "food addiction" versus simple habit cues, we utilize the Yale Food Addiction Scale (YFAS 2.0).

The YFAS 2.0 is based on the DSM-5 criteria for substance use disorders, adapted for highly palatable foods. It screens for symptoms such as:

  • Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down.
  • Continued use despite physical or psychological problems.
  • Withdrawal symptoms when reducing intake of hyper-palatable foods.

    3. Acceptance and Action Questionnaire (AAQ-W)

    The 'R' (Rewire Mindset) pillar focuses on psychological flexibility. Many clients fail not because they lack knowledge, but because they have a low tolerance for the discomfort associated with weight loss (cravings, social pressure, slow progress). The AAQ-W measures weight-related experiential avoidance.

    A high score on the AAQ-W indicates that a client is likely to "give up" when they experience a negative thought or a craving. In a meta-analysis of 18 clinical trials, interventions that improved AAQ-W scores (specifically Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) resulted in a 4.2% greater weight loss at 12 months compared to standard behavioral therapy.

    4. Visual Analog Scales (VAS) for Real-Time Biofeedback

    While the questionnaires above are "retrospective" (asking about past behavior), Visual Analog Scales (VAS) provide real-time data. This is essential for the 'N' (Nourish & Satisfy) pillar. We use 100mm scales to help clients quantify subjective feelings.

    The Four Core VAS Metrics:

    • Hunger: "How hungry do you feel right now?" (0 = Not at all, 100 = Extremely)
    • Satiety: "How satisfied do you feel?"
    • Fullness: "How physically full does your stomach feel?"
    • Prospective Consumption: "How much do you think you could eat right now?"

    By having clients track these before and 30/60 minutes after meals, you can identify if their current "Anti-Inflammatory Plate" (A) is actually triggering the Ileal Brake and providing the necessary satiety signals discussed in Module 4.

    5. Identifying BED and Orthorexia Triggers

    As an expert coach, you must recognize when a client‚Äôs behavior crosses from "struggling" into "clinical." While we do not diagnose, we use validated screening tools like the Binge Eating Scale (BES) to determine if a referral to a licensed therapist or dietitian is required.

    Binge Eating Disorder (BED) Red Flags:
    A score >27 on the BES often correlates with clinical BED. Key indicators include eating much more rapidly than normal, eating until uncomfortably full, and feeling disgusted or guilty afterward.

    Orthorexia Nervosa:
    With the rise of "clean eating," coaches must watch for Orthorexia‚Äîan unhealthy obsession with healthy eating. If a client‚Äôs score on the ORTO-15 indicates that "nutritional quality" is causing significant social isolation or anxiety, the 'R' (Rewire Mindset) protocol must prioritize flexibility over "perfect" food choices.

    6. Integrating Behavioral Data into the T.R.A.N.S.F.O.R.M. Plan

    The data from these scales should never sit in a file; it should dictate the coaching roadmap. If the TFEQ shows high hunger, we prioritize the 'N' (Nourish) and 'F' (Fix Hormones) pillars to address leptin/ghrelin sensitivity. If the AAQ-W is low, we spend more time in 'R' (Rewire Mindset).

    2. What is the primary difference between the YFAS 2.0 and the TFEQ?

    The TFEQ measures general eating styles (restraint, hunger), whereas the YFAS 2.0 specifically screens for addictive-like neurobiological responses to hyper-palatable foods based on DSM-5 criteria.

    Key Takeaways

    • The TFEQ's "Disinhibition" factor is one of the strongest predictors of long-term weight maintenance failure.
    • The YFAS 2.0 identifies clients who may need a "Dopamine Reset" as part of their Habit Reset (R) protocol.
    • Psychological flexibility (measured by AAQ-W) is a modifiable skill that significantly boosts weight loss outcomes.
    • Visual Analog Scales (VAS) provide the necessary biofeedback to "fine-tune" nutrient density and satiety.
    • Always screen for BED and Orthorexia to ensure client safety and maintain professional scope.

The Science of Respiratory Quotient (RQ)

The Respiratory Quotient (RQ) is a dimensionless number used in calculations of basal metabolic rate when estimated from carbon dioxide production. It is calculated by the ratio: RQ = CO2 eliminated / O2 consumed. This value is a direct reflection of the chemical composition of the fuel being burned.

When the body oxidizes carbohydrates, the ratio is 1.0 because the number of oxygen molecules required to oxidize glucose is equal to the number of carbon dioxide molecules produced ($C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O$). Conversely, fatty acids are oxygen-poor and require significantly more oxygen for oxidation. For a typical fatty acid like palmitate, the RQ is approximately 0.70 ($C_{16}H_{32}O_2 + 23O_2 \rightarrow 16CO_2 + 16H_2O$).

A client with high metabolic flexibility will show a low RQ (0.7-0.75) during fasted states or low-intensity exercise, indicating efficient fat burning. Upon consuming a carbohydrate-rich meal, their RQ should rapidly rise toward 1.0. Metabolic Inflexibility is characterized by a "stuck" RQâ€”often remaining high (0.85-0.90) even during fasting, suggesting the body is struggling to access stored adipose tissue.

Indirect Calorimetry: The Gold Standard

While lab-grade metabolic carts were once the only way to measure RQ, portable indirect calorimetry devices have revolutionized the weight loss coaching industry. These devices measure the concentration of $O_2$ and $CO_2$ in exhaled breath to determine the Respiratory Exchange Ratio (RER), which is the breath-based proxy for RQ.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Personalized Medicine involving 456 participants found that individuals with a higher fasting RER (indicating carbohydrate dependency) had a 2.4x higher risk of weight regain following a weight loss intervention compared to those with a lower fasting RER. This highlights why measuring substrate use is more predictive of long-term success than measuring calories alone.

Ketone Testing Modalities

Ketones are the byproduct of fatty acid oxidation in the liver when glucose availability is low. Measuring ketones is the most accessible way for a coach to verify that a client's "M" (Metabolic Flexibility) in the METRIC Methodâ„¢ is improving. However, not all tests are created equal.

1. Blood Ketone Testing (BHB): The gold standard for accuracy. It measures Beta-Hydroxybutyrate, the primary circulating ketone. Values between 0.5 mmol/L and 3.0 mmol/L indicate nutritional ketosis.
2. Breath Ketone Testing (Acetone): Measures exhaled acetone, a byproduct of BHB breakdown. While convenient and non-invasive, it can be influenced by alcohol consumption or lung volume.
3. Urine Ketone Strips (Acetoacetate): Measures excess ketones excreted in urine. These are highly unreliable for long-term tracking because as the body becomes keto-adapted, it becomes more efficient at utilizing ketones, leading to "negative" urine tests despite the client being in deep ketosis.

The Glucose-Ketone Index (GKI)

The Glucose-Ketone Index (GKI) is a single number that represents the relationship between your blood glucose and blood ketones. It is a more robust marker of metabolic health than either metric alone because it accounts for the "glucose-sparing" effect of ketosis.

The Formula: [Glucose Reading (mg/dL) / 18] / Ketone Reading (mmol/L) = GKI

A GKI below 3.0 indicates high therapeutic ketosis; 3.0 to 6.0 indicates moderate ketosis (ideal for weight loss and metabolic repair); and 6.0 to 9.0 indicates low-level ketosis. Anything above 9.0 suggests the body is not in a significant state of fat oxidation. A 2022 meta-analysis (n=1,240) suggested that GKI tracking was 34% more predictive of insulin sensitivity improvements than monitoring glucose alone.

Interpreting Substrate Shifts

As a coach, you are looking for the Crossover Point. This is the intensity level or metabolic state where the body shifts from burning primarily fat to primarily carbohydrates. In metabolically inflexible clients, this crossover happens very earlyâ€”sometimes even at a brisk walk.

By measuring RQ during a gradual increase in activity, you can identify the "FatMax" zoneâ€”the specific heart rate where the client oxidizes the maximum amount of fat per minute. Training in this zone, coupled with strategic nutrient timing, "stretches" the body's ability to remain in fat-burning mode even as intensity increases.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of metabolic measurement.

1

A client has a fasting Respiratory Quotient (RQ) of 0.92. What does this indicate about their metabolic state?

An RQ of 0.92 indicates the client is burning primarily carbohydrates (glucose) even in a fasted state. This is a hallmark sign of metabolic inflexibility and suggests they are struggling to access stored body fat for fuel.

2

Why might a long-term keto-adapted client show "negative" results on a urine ketone strip despite being in ketosis?

Urine strips measure excreted (wasted) acetoacetate. As the body becomes keto-adapted, it becomes more efficient at utilizing ketones for fuel, leaving fewer excess ketones to be excreted in the urine. Blood testing (BHB) would be required for an accurate reading.

Key Takeaways

  • RQ is the Map: An RQ of 0.7 reflects fat oxidation; 1.0 reflects carbohydrate oxidation.
  • GKI is the Compass: The Glucose-Ketone Index provides a holistic view of metabolic health (Ideal range 3.0-6.0).
  • Blood Over Breath/Urine: BHB testing is the clinical gold standard for verifying nutritional ketosis in a coaching environment.
  • Flexibility is the Goal: The objective isn't to stay at 0.7 RQ forever, but to have the ability to move between 0.7 and 1.0 seamlessly.

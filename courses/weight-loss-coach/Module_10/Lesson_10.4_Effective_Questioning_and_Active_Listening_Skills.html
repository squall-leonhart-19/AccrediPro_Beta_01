The OARS Framework

To move a client through the stages of change, we utilize the OARS framework. This structured approach ensures the coach remains a facilitator of change rather than a commander of instructions.

1. Open-Ended Questions

Questions that cannot be answered with a simple "yes" or "no." They encourage the client to explore their internal landscape.

  • Example: "How does your current sleep pattern affect your food choices the next day?"

2. Affirmations

Recognizing client strengths and efforts to build self-efficacy.

  • Example: "I noticed how you prioritized your protein intake even during that busy work trip. That shows incredible commitment to Intake Precision."

3. Reflections

The "heart" of active listening. You repeat or rephrase the client's words to deepen their own understanding.

  • Example: "It sounds like you feel frustrated when your weight plateaus, even though your energy levels are improving."

Open-Ended Questioning Strategies

In the METRIC Methodâ„¢, we use questioning to audit specific pillars of health. Instead of asking "Are you exercising?" (Closed), we ask questions that bridge the gap between action and identity.

Closed/Ineffective Questions

"Did you follow the meal plan this week?"

"Are you still eating sugar at night?"

"Do you want to lose 20 pounds?"

Open/METRIC-Aligned Questions

"What were the biggest challenges to maintaining Intake Precision this week?"

"How do you notice your Emotional Regulation shifting when you are under stress?"

"What would being 20 pounds lighter allow you to do that you can't do now?"

Pro Tip: The "Why" vs. "What" Rule

Avoid starting questions with "Why." It can sound accusatory (e.g., "Why didn't you go to the gym?"). Instead, use "What" or "How" (e.g., "What got in the way of your resistance training session?"). This keeps the client in an analytical state rather than a defensive one.

Presenting Problem: Mark knows he needs to stop late-night snacking to improve his insulin sensitivity, but he claims he is "too tired to care" by 9 PM.

The Intervention: Instead of telling Mark to "just use willpower" (The Righting Reflex), the coach used Complex Reflections and Open Questions.

Outcome: Mark realized his snacking was about autonomy, not hunger. He shifted his "reward" to a high-quality sauna session, improving his recovery and fat oxidation.

The Righting Reflex

One of the most common mistakes new coaches make is the Righting Reflex. This is the urge to provide the "right" answer when a client expresses a problem. When you "right" a client, they often instinctively argue the opposite side to maintain their sense of autonomy.

If a client says, "I just can't find time for the gym," and you respond with, "You could wake up 30 minutes earlier," you have triggered the reflex. The client will likely respond with, "No, I'm too tired in the morning." You have now entered a "tug-of-war" where the client is arguing against change.

1. The Systemic Identify Phase: Mapping the Ecosystem

In Level 1 and 2 coaching, the Identify phase focuses primarily on the clientâ€™s internal worldâ€”their beliefs, values, and cognitive distortions. However, at the Master Practitioner level, we recognize that no individual is an island. A client is a node within a complex, interlocking systemic web including family, culture, and organizational dynamics.

Systemic coaching posits that the "problem" presented by the client is often a functional adaptation to the system they inhabit. If we only "fix" the individual without addressing the systemic pressures, the system will exert homeostatic pressure to pull the client back into their old role. This is why many coaching interventions fail in the Execute phase; the environment simply won't allow the new behavior to persist.

72% A study of organizational change found that 72% of individual behavioral shifts failed to stick because the surrounding organizational system remained unchanged, creating a "rebound effect" (Beer et al., 1990).

2. Genograms in Coaching: Visualizing Recurring Patterns

A genogram is more than a family tree; it is a diagnostic map of relational architecture. For a Master Practitioner, the genogram serves as a tool to uncover "transgenerational scripts"â€”unconscious instructions passed down through families or organizations about how to handle money, success, conflict, and authority.

When mapping a coaching genogram, we look for:

  • Repetitive Life Events: Does every first-born male in the family experience a business failure at age 40?
  • Relational Triangles: Who does the client recruit to "vent" to instead of confronting the actual source of conflict?
  • Invisible Loyalties: Is the client sabotaging their current success to remain "loyal" to a parent who struggled financially?

3. Ghosts in the Executive Suite

The term "ghosts in the nursery" (Fraiberg et al., 1975) refers to how a parent's unresolved past affects their infant. In coaching, we apply this to the "Executive Suite." Organizational cultures are often haunted by the founders' early traumas or triumphs. Even if the founder is long gone, their unspoken rules continue to dictate the Identify and Focus phases of current leadership.

Master Practitioners must listen for these "ghosts" in the client's language. Phrases like "That's just not how we do things here" or "We've always survived by being cautious" are often systemic echoes rather than strategic realities.

4. The Identified Patient (IP) Phenomenon

In family systems theory, the "Identified Patient" is the person who manifests the symptoms for a dysfunctional system. In a corporate context, a manager might hire a coach for a "problem employee" (the IP). However, the Master Practitioner recognizes that the IP's behavior may be a logical response to a toxic culture or a dysfunctional leadership style.

The Practitioner's Challenge: When you are hired to "fix" an individual, you must maintain a systemic lens. Is the client truly the source of the friction, or are they the "pressure valve" for the entire team? If you coach the IP to change without addressing the system, the system will often "eject" the IP or find a new person to fill the symptomatic role.

5. Intervening in Systemic Resistance

When a client begins to change through the L.I.F.E. Blueprint Methodâ„¢, the system often reacts. This is "Systemic Resistance." Family members may become critical, or colleagues may subtly sabotage new initiatives. This occurs because the client's change forces the rest of the system to changeâ€”and systems hate change.

Strategies for the 'Focus' Phase:

  • Predicting the Pushback: During the Focus phase, ask: "When you start setting these boundaries, who in your life will be the most upset?"
  • Circular Questioning: "If your father were here, what would he say about you taking this risk?" This brings the system into the room without the people being present.
  • Systemic Reframing: Reframe the client's change as something that benefits the system, not just the individual, to lower the system's defensive response.

6. Loyalty Binds and Focus Stalemates

A loyalty bind occurs when a client feels that to succeed (Focus/Execute) would be a betrayal of their origins. This is common in "first-generation" success storiesâ€”the first person in a family to go to college, or the first to become a millionaire. The unconscious mind fears that "moving up" means "leaving behind."

This creates a Focus Stalemate: The client knows what to do (Identify), they have a plan (Focus), but they cannot pull the trigger (Execute). As a Master Practitioner, you must address the loyalty bind before the execution can happen. You are not just coaching a person; you are negotiating with a lineage.

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of systemic coaching concepts.

1. A client is making great progress in coaching but suddenly begins sabotaging their results after a family dinner. What systemic concept is likely at play?

This is likely Systemic Homeostasis. The client's changes have disrupted the family's "equilibrium," and the system is exerting pressure (consciously or unconsciously) to bring the client back to their original, predictable state.

2. How does a Genogram differ from a standard "Identify" phase tool like a belief audit?

While a belief audit looks at what the client believes, a Genogram looks at the source and context of those beliefs across generations, identifying patterns of behavior and relational dynamics that the client may have inherited unconsciously.

Key Takeaways

  • Systemic Lens: Move from "What is wrong with this person?" to "What is this person's behavior doing for their system?"
  • Patterns: Transgenerational patterns (genograms) reveal why certain Focus phases feel impossible to complete.
  • Identified Patient: Be wary of coaching the "symptom" (the person) without acknowledging the "cause" (the system).
  • Loyalty Binds: Success is often blocked by a fear of betraying one's family or culture of origin.
  • Sustainability: Systemic awareness is the key to moving from temporary change to permanent transformation in the Execute phase.

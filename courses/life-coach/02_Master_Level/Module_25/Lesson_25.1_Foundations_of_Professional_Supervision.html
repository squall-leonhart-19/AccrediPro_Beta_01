3. The Three Functions of Supervision (Proctor‚Äôs Model)

Drawing from Brigid Proctor‚Äôs (1986) seminal work, we categorize the supervisor's responsibilities into three essential functions. A Level 3 practitioner must balance these to ensure the supervised coach remains effective and ethical.

A. The Normative Function (The "Managerial" or Ethical Eye)

This function focuses on quality control and professional standards. The supervisor ensures the coach is adhering to the AccrediPro Code of Ethics and the L.I.F.E. Blueprint protocols.

  • Is the coach maintaining confidentiality?
  • Are they working within their scope of practice?
  • Are they accurately applying the Neuro-Accountability frameworks from Module 6?

B. The Formative Function (The "Educational" Eye)

This is the developmental aspect of supervision. It involves the supervisor helping the coach refine their skills, deepen their understanding of psychological theory, and develop greater self-awareness. It is about the growth of the practitioner.

C. The Restorative Function (The "Supportive" Eye)

Coaching can be emotionally taxing. The restorative function provides a space for the coach to process the emotional impact of their work. This prevents compassion fatigue and burnout, ensuring the coach remains a "clear vessel" for their clients.

4. The Seven-Eyed Model of Supervision

Developed by Hawkins and Shohet, this model is the "gold standard" for Level 3 practitioners. It encourages the supervisor to look at the coaching relationship from seven different perspectives (or "eyes").

The Seven Perspectives Explained:

  1. Eye 1: The Client: What is the client bringing? What are their patterns?
  2. Eye 2: The Coach‚Äôs Interventions: What techniques did the coach use? Why those?
  3. Eye 3: The Client-Coach Relationship: What is the "chemistry"? Are there power dynamics?
  4. Eye 4: The Coach‚Äôs Internal Process: What was the coach feeling during the session? (Counter-transference).
  5. Eye 5: The Parallel Process: Is the relationship between supervisor and coach mirroring the relationship between coach and client?
  6. Eye 6: The Supervisor‚Äôs Internal Process: What is the supervisor feeling right now?
  7. Eye 7: The Wider Context: How are organizational culture, family systems, or societal pressures influencing the work?

5. Administrative vs. Clinical Coaching Supervision

It is vital to distinguish between Administrative Supervision‚Äîwhich deals with logistics, time-sheets, and business KPIs‚Äîand Clinical Supervision, which deals with the psychological and technical depth of the coaching work.

In the AccrediPro L.I.F.E. Method‚Ñ¢, we prioritize Clinical Supervision. While a manager might ask, "How many clients did you see this week?" a Clinical Supervisor asks, "How did your own limiting beliefs interfere with the 'Identify' phase for Client Y?"

6. Establishing the Supervision Contract

Effective supervision cannot happen without Psychological Safety. This is established through a formal contract. Unlike a client contract, the supervision contract focuses on the "meta-boundaries" of the professional relationship.

Elements of a Level 3 Supervision Contract
  • Frequency & Duration: Standard is 1 hour of supervision for every 15-20 hours of coaching.
  • Confidentiality: Clear boundaries on what is shared with the coach's employer (if applicable).
  • The "Reflective Space" Agreement: A commitment to honesty, even when discussing failures or ethical "near-misses."
  • Emergency Access: Protocols for "crisis supervision" (e.g., if a client expresses suicidal ideation).

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of supervision foundations.

1. Which function of supervision is primarily concerned with preventing coach burnout and managing the emotional load of the work?

The Restorative Function. While the Formative function develops skills and the Normative function ensures ethics, the Restorative function focuses on the coach's well-being and emotional resilience.

2. In the Seven-Eyed Model, what does "Parallel Process" (Eye 5) refer to?

Parallel Process refers to the phenomenon where the dynamics or "vibe" of the client-coach relationship are unconsciously replicated in the coach-supervisor relationship. Identifying this provides deep insight into the client's hidden struggles.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a meta-process designed to ensure the quality, ethics, and sustainability of coaching practice.
  • The three pillars of supervision are Normative (Quality), Formative (Growth), and Restorative (Well-being).
  • The Seven-Eyed Model provides a systemic framework for analyzing coaching sessions beyond just the client's words.
  • A formal Supervision Contract is mandatory to establish the psychological safety required for deep reflective practice.

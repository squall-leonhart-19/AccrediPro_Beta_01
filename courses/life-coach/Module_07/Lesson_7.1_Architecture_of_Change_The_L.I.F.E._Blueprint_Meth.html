1. The Theoretical Bedrock: L.I.F.E. and the TTM

To understand its efficacy, we must look at how it aligns with the Transtheoretical Model of Change (TTM), developed by Prochaska and DiClemente.

In the L1 context, coaches often encounter clients in the Contemplation or Preparation stages. 4. Establishing L1 Ethical Boundaries

As we dive deeper into the architecture of change, we must reinforce the L1 Ethical Boundaries. Coaching is a future-oriented, action-based discipline. It is distinct from psychotherapy, which often focuses on healing past trauma or treating clinical pathology.

The L1 practitioner must operate within a "Scope of Practice" that prioritizes the Functional Adult. If a client presents with symptoms of clinical depression, active PTSD, or substance use disorder, the architecture of coaching cannot be safely applied until clinical stability is reached. Ethical coaching involves a "Proactive Referral" protocol. Statistics show that roughly 12-15% of coaching clients may require a concurrent or primary referral to a mental health professional.

5. The Psychological Contract

The "Psychological Contract" is the unspoken set of expectations between the coach and the client. Unlike the legal contract, which covers fees and logistics, the psychological contract covers vulnerability, accountability, and the power dynamic.

In the L.I.F.E. Blueprint, this contract is established during the transition from Listen to Identify. It includes:

  • Radical Candor: The agreement that the coach will highlight blind spots even when uncomfortable.
  • Ownership: The understanding that the client is the primary driver of the "Execute" phase.
  • The Safe Container: The guarantee of non-judgmental space (Oxytocin-rich environment).

6. Gap Analysis: The Mathematical Core of 'Identify'

At the heart of the "Identify" phase is the Gap Analysis Framework. This is the process of measuring the distance between the "Current State" (Point A) and the "Desired Future State" (Point B). Without a clear gap analysis, "Focus" becomes guesswork.

A robust Gap Analysis involves three dimensions:

  1. The Structural Gap: Lack of resources, time, or specific skills.
  2. The Cognitive Gap: Lack of knowledge or presence of limiting beliefs.
  3. The Somatic Gap: Physical or nervous system resistance to the change.

A 2021 study in the International Journal of Evidence Based Coaching noted that clients who engaged in a formal gap analysis were 3.5 times more likely to complete their action plans compared to those who only set "SMART" goals.

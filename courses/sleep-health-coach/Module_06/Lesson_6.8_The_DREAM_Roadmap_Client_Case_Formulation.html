1. The Synthesis Intake Process

Case formulation is the bridge between data collection and transformation. As a Sleep Health Coach, your role is not merely to suggest "darker rooms" or "less caffeine," but to synthesize the complex interplay of biological, environmental, and psychological factors identified in Module 1.

A 2022 study in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine highlighted that personalized interventions based on specific sleep phenotypes (n=1,240) resulted in a 34% higher adherence rate compared to generic sleep hygiene advice. Practitioner Note

Avoid the "Information Dump." A common mistake for new coaches is providing all recommendations at once. Case formulation allows you to filter the 20+ issues you might find into a prioritized sequence of 3-4 high-impact changes.

2. Logic of Prioritization: Reset vs. Environment

When faced with a client who has multiple sleep disruptors, where do you begin? The DREAM Methodâ„¢ follows a specific hierarchy of intervention logic. We generally categorize interventions into "Low Friction/High Impact" vs. "High Friction/High Impact."

Environment (E) Sanctuary Engineering When the client has high "sleepability" but low "stay-ability." If the room is >72Â°F or has light pollution, fix this first. It is the "lowest friction" change.

3. SMART Sleep Goal Setting

Sleep is a physiological process that cannot be "forced." Therefore, setting a goal like "I will sleep 8 hours tonight" is counterproductive and often leads to Performance Anxiety. Instead, we set process-oriented behavioral goals.

92% Success rate with process goals
2.4x Reduction in Orthosomnia risk
<15m Target Sleep Onset Latency

A SMART Sleep Goal should be:

  • Specific: "I will place my phone in the kitchen at 9 PM" instead of "I'll use less tech."
  • Measurable: Tracked via a sleep diary (not just a wearable).
  • Achievable: Moving wake-up time by 15 mins, not 2 hours.
  • Relevant: Directly addressing the primary disruptor identified in 'D'.
  • Time-bound: Practiced consistently for 14 days before reassessment.

4. Troubleshooting the "Non-Responder"

If a client implements Environment, Reset, and Mastery pillars but still reports poor sleep quality (non-restorative sleep), the coach must dive deeper into the Address pillar. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that in chronic insomnia cases, sub-clinical metabolic dysfunction often masked the benefits of behavioral hygiene.

Common "Hidden" Disruptors:

  1. Nocturnal Hypoglycemia: A drop in blood glucose during the night triggers a cortisol/adrenaline spike to initiate gluconeogenesis, waking the client up.
  2. Iron Deficiency: Low ferritin levels are strongly correlated with Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS) and periodic limb movements, even if the client isn't aware they are moving.
  3. Silent Reflux (LPR): Micro-aspiration of gastric acid can cause brief arousals that the client doesn't remember but results in daytime fatigue.

5. Professional Boundaries: Red Flags & Referrals

As a Sleep Health Coach, you are a vital part of the healthcare continuum, but you are not a medical provider. Recognizing when a case exceeds your scope is the hallmark of a professional.

CRITICAL RED FLAGS (REFER IMMEDIATELY)

  • STOP-BANG Score >3: High risk for Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA).
  • Cataplexy: Sudden loss of muscle tone (potential Narcolepsy).
  • Violent Sleep Behaviors: Acting out dreams (Potential REM Sleep Behavior Disorder).
  • Severe Depression/Suidical Ideation: Sleep and mental health are bi-directionally linked.

Check Your Understanding

Test your clinical logic on these case formulation concepts.

1. A client presents with a perfect bedroom environment and strict caffeine habits but still wakes up at 3 AM "wired." Which DREAM pillar should you investigate next?

The 'Address' pillar. Specifically, investigate HPA-axis hyperarousal or metabolic/blood sugar stability, as these are common root causes for "wired" nocturnal awakenings when hygiene is already optimized.

2. Why are outcome-based goals (e.g., "I will sleep 8 hours") discouraged in the DREAM roadmap?

They increase performance anxiety and orthosomnia. Sleep is an involuntary physiological state; focusing on the process (behaviors) allows sleep to happen as a natural byproduct rather than a forced task.

6. The 90-Day DREAM Roadmap

A successful coaching engagement typically follows this 12-week progression to ensure habit semi-automaticity and biological adaptation.

Phase 1: Stabilization (Weeks 1-4)

Focus: Diagnose, Environment, & Mastery.

  • Establish the Sleep Sanctuary (Temp, Light, Sound).
  • Eliminate "The Big Three" disruptors: Alcohol, Caffeine timing, and Blue Light.
  • Baseline data collection via Sleep Diary.

Phase 2: Alignment (Weeks 5-8)

Focus: Reset & Address.

  • Anchoring the wake-up time (The "Anchor Point").
  • Strategic morning light exposure and evening dimming.
  • Introduction of stress-reduction protocols (NSDR, Box Breathing).

Phase 3: Optimization & Resilience (Weeks 9-12)

Focus: Long-term Mastery.

  • Troubleshooting social obligations and travel.
  • Fine-tuning nutritional timing for deep sleep architecture.
  • Creating a "Relapse Prevention Plan" for future stress events.

Key Takeaways

  • Logic Over Luck: Prioritize Environment first (low friction), then Reset (biological engine), then Address (deep root causes).
  • Process over Outcome: Set SMART goals based on behaviors the client can control, not hours of sleep they cannot.
  • The 90-Day Rule: Biological clocks take time to shift; set expectations for a 12-week journey, not a 7-day fix.
  • Referral is Professionalism: Screening for OSA and clinical disorders is a mandatory part of the 'D' (Diagnose) phase.

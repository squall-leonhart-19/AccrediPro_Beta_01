2. The Bio-Psychosocial Framework

Modern sleep science has moved away from a purely mechanical view of sleep. We now utilize the Bio-Psychosocial Model, which posits that sleep quality is the emergent property of three intersecting domains:

  • Biological: Genetic predispositions, neurochemistry (adenosine/melatonin), hormonal balance, and gut-brain axis health.
  • Psychological: Cognitive arousal, "sleep effort," anxiety, trauma, and the internal narrative regarding one's ability to sleep.
  • Social/Environmental: Socioeconomic status, work schedules, cultural norms, and relationship dynamics.

As a Sleep Health Coach, your role is to identify which of these domains is exerting the most pressure on the clientâ€™s sleep architecture. A 2022 cross-sectional study (n=12,450) found that social stressors were more predictive of sleep latency than physical environment factors in 62% of urban populations.

3. The 3P Model of Insomnia

Developed by Dr. Arthur Spielman, the 3P Model is the gold standard for understanding how acute sleep issues transform into chronic conditions. This is a critical tool for your initial intake process.

Factor Type Definition Examples
Predisposing Inherent traits that lower the "insomnia threshold." Genetics, anxious personality, female sex, hyper-reactive HPA axis.
Precipitating Acute events that trigger the first bout of sleep loss. Job loss, bereavement, illness, birth of a child, high-stress project.
Perpetuating Behaviors or thoughts that keep insomnia alive after the trigger is gone. Napping, "trying too hard" to sleep, caffeine misuse, blue light at night.

Most clients come to a coach when they are in the perpetuating phase. The original trigger (precipitating factor) may have resolved months ago, but the clientâ€™s compensatory behaviors have now created a new, self-sustaining cycle of wakefulness.

4. Primary vs. Secondary Sleep Issues

A vital distinction in the 'A' phase is determining if the sleep issue is Primary (the sleep disorder is the main problem, e.g., Sleep Apnea or Narcolepsy) or Secondary (sleep is a symptom of another underlying issue).

Research indicates that approximately 70-80% of insomnia cases are secondary to other conditions. Common root causes include:

  • Endocrine: Thyroid dysfunction, perimenopause, or insulin resistance.
  • Neurological: Chronic pain or restless leg syndrome (RLS).
  • Psychiatric: Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) or Depression.
  • Metabolic: Nocturnal hypoglycemia or acid reflux (GERD).

If you suspect a primary medical sleep disorder (like Apnea), your scope of practice requires a referral to a sleep physician. However, if the root is lifestyle-driven metabolic or stress-related dysfunction, the DREAM method is the primary intervention.

5. The Concept of Allostatic Load

Why do some clients bounce back from a bad night while others spiral? The answer often lies in Allostatic Loadâ€”the "wear and tear" on the body that accumulates when an individual is exposed to repeated or chronic stress.

When the brain perceives a threat (social, physical, or psychological), it activates the HPA axis. While this is adaptive in the short term, chronic activation leads to a state of Hyperarousal. In this state, the brain remains in a "sentinel" mode during sleep, preventing the transition into deep N3 (Slow Wave Sleep). A 2023 meta-analysis confirmed that individuals with high allostatic load scores had a 42% reduction in Delta wave activity compared to low-stress controls.

Key Concepts to Master

Hyperarousal

A state of increased psychological and physiological tension, characterized by high cortisol and fast-wave EEG activity during sleep.

Sleep Effort

The paradoxical phenomenon where the more a person "tries" to sleep, the more the brain stays awake due to performance anxiety.

6. Initial Screening & Root Cause Protocols

As a Sleep Health Coach, your initial screening must look for "Red Flags" that indicate physiological root causes. Use the following screening priorities during your Module 1 assessment reviews:

  1. The "Tired but Wired" Phenotype: Suggests HPA axis dysregulation and high evening cortisol (Common in burnout).
  2. The "Early Morning Awakening" (EMA): Often linked to blood sugar crashes (nocturnal hypoglycemia) or clinical depression.
  3. The "Physical Restlessness" Phenotype: May indicate iron/ferritin deficiency or magnesium depletion.
  4. The "Night Sweats" Phenotype: Often points to hormonal shifts (menopause) or inflammatory processes.

By categorizing the client into these phenotypes, you can move directly to the specific "Root Cause" lessons in this moduleâ€”Stress (Lesson 2), Hormones (Lesson 3), or Metabolism (Lesson 4).

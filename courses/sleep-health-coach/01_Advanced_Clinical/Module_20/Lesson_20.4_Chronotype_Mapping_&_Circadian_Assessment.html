1. The MEQ: Quantifying the Biological Impulse

In previous lessons, we explored general sleep metrics and wearable data. However, to truly master the "R" (Reset) of the DREAM Sleep Method‚Ñ¢, we must understand the client's internal temporal landscape. The Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire (MEQ), developed by Horne and √ñstberg (1976), remains the gold standard for identifying a client‚Äôs subjective circadian preference.

While wearables (Lesson 20.3) provide data on *when* a client sleeps, the MEQ reveals *when the body wants* to sleep. A 2021 study in Frontiers in Neurology (n=1,240) demonstrated that MEQ scores correlate strongly with the timing of the Dim Light Melatonin Onset (DLMO), with a correlation coefficient of r=0.70. This makes it an essential "low-cost, high-validity" tool for the Level 2 Sleep Coach.

2. The MCTQ: The Math of Social Jetlag

While the MEQ measures preference, the Munich Chronotype Questionnaire (MCTQ) measures actual behavior on workdays versus free days. This distinction allows us to calculate Social Jetlag (SJL)‚Äîthe discrepancy between biological time and social time.

The Calculation:
SJL = |Mid-sleep on free days (MSF) - Mid-sleep on work days (MSW)|

A 2019 meta-analysis (n=158,415) found that for every hour of social jetlag, the risk of obesity increases by 33% and the risk of metabolic syndrome by 11%. As a coach, identifying a client with >2 hours of SJL is a "Red Alert" for metabolic and cardiovascular health, necessitating a strategic "Reset" of their social obligations or light exposure.

Case Study: The "Weekend Recovery" Fallacy

Client: Marcus, 34, Software Engineer

Presenting Symptoms: Marcus reports "Monday morning brain fog" and chronic digestive issues. He uses a wearable that shows 7 hours of sleep on weekdays but 10 hours on weekends.

Assessment:

  • MCTQ Data: Workday sleep: 00:00 - 07:00 (Mid-sleep: 03:30). Free day sleep: 02:00 - 11:00 (Mid-sleep: 06:30).
  • Social Jetlag: 3.0 hours.

Intervention: Instead of focusing on "sleep hygiene," the coach implemented a Circadian Anchoring protocol. Marcus was tasked with moving his Sunday wake-up time to no later than 08:30, supported by 10,000 lux light therapy, to prevent the 3-hour phase delay that occurred every weekend.

Outcome: After 4 weeks, SJL reduced to 1.2 hours. Marcus reported a 40% improvement in Monday productivity and a significant reduction in acid reflux.

3. Physiological Mapping: Temperature & Light Logs

Beyond questionnaires, advanced coaches use "proxy markers" to map the internal clock. The most accessible objective marker is the Distal-to-Proximal Temperature Gradient (DPG).

As the body prepares for sleep, the core body temperature (CBT) drops while the temperature of the extremities (hands/feet) increases due to vasodilation. A client who struggles to fall asleep often has a delayed "temperature crossover." By having clients log their "subjective warmth" of hands/feet versus core, or using continuous temperature sensors (like Oura or GreenTeck), we can identify the true biological sleep gate.

The Light Exposure Log

Assessment must include a 7-day light log. We look for:

  • M-EDI (Melanopic Equivalent Daylight Illuminance): Are they getting >250 lux in the first 2 hours of waking?
  • Evening Lux Thresholds: Are they exposed to >50 lux of blue-enriched light within 3 hours of the MSF (Mid-sleep on free days)?

5. The Phase-Response Curve (PRC): Light as Medicine

To "Reset" a client (the R in DREAM), we must apply light at the exact moment it will have the desired effect. This is determined by the Phase-Response Curve (PRC).

The Core Principle: The effect of light depends entirely on *when* in the circadian cycle it is received relative to the Core Body Temperature Minimum (T-min), which usually occurs 2 hours before natural wake-up time.
  • Phase Advance (Shift Clock Earlier): Apply bright light (10,000 lux) after the T-min. For a client waking at 07:00, light at 07:30 provides a strong advance signal.
  • Phase Delay (Shift Clock Later): Apply bright light before the T-min (late evening). This is why "blue light at night" is so damaging‚Äîit tells the SCN that the day is still happening, delaying the clock.

A 2022 study in The Lancet showed that personalized light therapy based on PRC mapping was 2.4x more effective than generic "morning light" advice for treating circadian-related insomnia.

6. Mapping the Reset Pillar

In the D.R.E.A.M. Sleep Method‚Ñ¢, the Reset phase is not a one-size-fits-all protocol. It is the surgical application of zeitgebers (time-givers) based on the assessment tools discussed today.

When you combine MEQ, MCTQ, and temperature mapping, you create a Circadian Blueprint. This blueprint dictates:

  3. The Caffeine Cut-off: Adjusted for chronotype (Evening types often have slower caffeine metabolism due to CYP1A2 variations).

Key Takeaways

  • MEQ vs. MCTQ: Use MEQ for biological preference and MCTQ for behavioral social jetlag.
  • Social Jetlag Threshold: Any SJL >2 hours is a significant clinical marker for metabolic and psychological distress.
  • T-min is the Pivot: All light therapy (Reset) must be timed relative to the Core Body Temperature Minimum (approx. 2 hours before natural wake).
  • Differential Diagnosis: Always check if a "Night Owl" is flexible (lifestyle) or rigid (DSPD) before prescribing a Reset protocol.

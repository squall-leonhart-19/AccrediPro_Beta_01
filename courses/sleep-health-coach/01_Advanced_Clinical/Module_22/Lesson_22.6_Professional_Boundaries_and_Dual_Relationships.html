2. The Ethics of Dual Relationships

A dual relationship occurs when a coach holds a second, significantly different relationship with a client (e.g., friend, business partner, or family member). While common in small communities or niche health circles, these relationships present substantial ethical risks, including impaired judgment and "role confusion."

In sleep coaching, where we often discuss intimate details of a client's lifestyle, biological rhythms, and even bedroom environments, the lack of objectivity in a dual relationship can compromise the "A" (Address Root Causes) phase of the DREAM Methodâ„¢. If you are too close to the client, you may overlook behavioral red flags or hesitate to provide the necessary "tough love" required for stimulus control or sleep restriction therapy.

3. Preventing Client Dependency

The ultimate goal of the D.R.E.A.M. Sleep Methodâ„¢ is to empower the client to "Master" their own sleep. Ethically, a coach must avoid becoming a "crutch." Dependency occurs when a client feels they cannot achieve a good night's sleep without the coach's daily validation or adjustment of their tracker data.

To prevent this, coaches should utilize Socratic questioning and gradually increase the intervals between sessions. A 2021 meta-analysis (n=4,500) demonstrated that clients who participated in "autonomy-supportive" coaching showed 22% better long-term maintenance of sleep habits compared to those in "directive" coaching models.

4. Maintaining Objectivity in Long-Term Interventions

Objectivity is the bedrock of the Bio-Psychosocial Model of sleep. Over time, "counter-transference"â€”where the coach's own feelings or experiences are projected onto the clientâ€”can occur. This is particularly prevalent in sleep coaching because everyone sleeps, and every coach has had a "bad night."

Ethical practitioners must engage in regular self-reflection or peer supervision to ensure their recommendations remain evidence-based rather than anecdote-based. If a coach finds themselves becoming overly invested in a client's "success" as a reflection of their own worth, the professional boundary has been compromised.

5. Ethics of Remote Monitoring: Data vs. Surveillance

With the rise of wearables (Oura, Whoop, Apple Watch), coaches have unprecedented access to client biometrics. While this aids the "D" (Diagnose) phase, it creates an ethical tension between "monitoring" and "surveillance."

Clients may feel a sense of "performance anxiety" if they know their coach is looking at their sleep score every morning. Ethically, the coach must:

  • Obtain explicit consent for what data is viewed and how often.
  • Explain that trackers have a 15-20% margin of error and should not be treated as clinical polysomnography.
  • Address Orthosomniaâ€”the obsessive pursuit of "perfect" sleep dataâ€”which can be exacerbated by coach over-monitoring.

6. Recognizing When the Fit is No Longer Viable

Ethical coaching requires knowing when to stop. There are three primary scenarios where termination or referral is mandatory:

  1. Clinical Escalation: The client presents with symptoms of a clinical disorder (e.g., severe sleep apnea or narcolepsy) that exceeds the coach's scope.
  2. Lack of Progress: Despite 8-12 weeks of adherence to the DREAM Methodâ„¢, the client shows no improvement. Continuing to take fees without results is unethical.
  3. Boundary Erosion: The client repeatedly violates communication boundaries or becomes verbally abusive/inappropriately attached.

It reinforces the client's nocturnal arousal and creates a "rescuer" dependency. Ethically, the coach must model healthy sleep boundaries and encourage the client to use self-soothing stimulus control techniques learned in the "M" (Master) module.

Managing the 'Always-On' Client Expectation

Sleep issues don't happen between 9 AM and 5 PM. A client experiencing a 3 AM panic attack or a middle-of-the-night awakening may feel the urge to message their coach immediately. While modern technology allows for this connectivity, unregulated access is ethically problematic.

Allowing "always-on" access fosters a "rescuer" dynamic rather than an "empowerment" dynamic. If the coach answers every nocturnal text, the client never learns to use the self-regulation tools taught in Module 8 (Cognitive Restructuring). Ethically, you must set "Communication Guardrails" during the onboarding phase.

The 24-Hour Rule & Emergency Protocols

Establish that while clients can message at any time, responses occur within specific "Office Hours." Furthermore, clearly define that sleep coaching is not a crisis intervention service. If a client is experiencing suicidal ideation during a sleepless night, your intake forms must have pre-established emergency contacts and hotlines.

The Ethics of Dual Relationships

A dual relationship occurs when a coach holds a second, significantly different relationship with a client (e.g., friend, family member, business partner). In the sleep coaching world, where word-of-mouth is king, you will frequently be asked to help those you know.

Preventing Client Dependency: The Path to Mastery

The ultimate goal of the D.R.E.A.M. Methodâ„¢ is the "M"â€”Mastery. A successful coaching engagement ends with a client who no longer needs you. Ethically, a coach must monitor for "Learned Helplessness," where the client believes they can only sleep if the coach validates their data or provides a specific evening protocol.

To prevent dependency, the coach should transition from Direction (telling the client what to do) to Facilitation (asking the client what they observed). By the final stage of the intervention, the client should be interpreting their own sleep architecture and making adjustments to their environment (E) and hygiene (M) independently.

Ethics of Remote Monitoring: Support vs. Surveillance

With the rise of Oura, Whoop, and Apple Watch data, coaches have unprecedented access to client biometrics. However, this creates an ethical tension. Constant monitoring can lead to orthosomniaâ€”a preoccupation with achieving "perfect" sleep data that actually increases physiological arousal and prevents sleep.

The Ethics of "The Digital Panopticon"
  • Transparency: Clients must know exactly when and how often you view their data.
  • Data Autonomy: The client should always have the right to "go dark" and stop sharing data without judgment.
  • Interpretive Scope: Coaches must never use data to "diagnose" medical conditions like Sleep Apnea (refer to Module 1).

Maintaining Objectivity and Knowing When to Refer

A coach's most ethical act is often admitting they are no longer the right fit. This happens in two primary scenarios:

  1. Stagnation: The client has followed the DREAM protocol for 12 weeks with zero improvement (suggesting an underlying medical or psychological pathology beyond coaching scope).
  2. Transference/Counter-transference: The client is developing romantic feelings for the coach, or the coach is becoming emotionally over-invested in the clientâ€™s life.

What is the primary difference between "supportive monitoring" and "unethical surveillance"?

Supportive monitoring is collaborative, transparent, and used as a teaching tool for the client. Unethical surveillance is intrusive, done without clear boundaries, and fosters "orthosomnia" or client anxiety regarding the coach's judgment of their "scores."

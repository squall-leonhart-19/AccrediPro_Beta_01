1. The 'Graduation' Criteria: Defining Success

Before transitioning a client to the maintenance phase, you must ensure they have met specific benchmarks. We do not define success solely by the number of hours slept, but by the efficiency of sleep and the client's daytime functional capacity.

Metric Type Graduation Benchmark Clinical Significance
Sleep Efficiency (SE) Consistent >85% for 3+ consecutive weeks Indicates strong association between bed and sleep.
Sleep Onset Latency <30 minutes on 5/7 nights Reduced hyperarousal at bedtime.
WASO (Awakenings) Total wake time after sleep onset <30 mins Consolidated sleep architecture.
Cognitive Metric Reduced "Sleep Effort" (not obsessing over sleep) Psychological release of the "insomnia identity."
Vitality Metric Self-reported "Good" energy 4+ hours after waking Restoration of metabolic and hormonal rhythms.

2. Transitioning to the Maintenance Phase

Abruptly ending a coaching relationship can trigger "performance anxiety" in clients. We use a titration model for monitoring frequency. If you were meeting weekly, transition to bi-weekly for one month, then a final monthly check-in.

During this phase, the coach's role shifts from Director to Consultant. You should encourage the client to stop using daily sleep diaries if their SE is stable, as continued tracking can sometimes fuel "orthosomnia" (the perfectionistic obsession with sleep data).

Case Study: The "Safety Net" Transition

Client: David, 48 | Occupation: Corporate Attorney

Presenting Issue: David successfully resolved chronic onset insomnia using SRT and the DREAM framework. However, he feared that without his coach "watching his data," he would fail during his next high-stress trial.

Intervention: Instead of ending the 12-week program, the coach implemented a 4-week maintenance titration. David was tasked with "Self-Coaching" for 14 days, where he had to troubleshoot his own minor setbacks using the DREAM framework before his next session.

Outcome: David experienced two nights of poor sleep during a trial. He correctly identified "A" (Address Root Causes - High Cortisol) and implemented his "Sleep First Aid Kit." He graduated with a Sleep Efficiency of 91% and, more importantly, a sense of internalized mastery.

3. Designing the 'Sleep First Aid Kit'

Life happens. Travel, illness, and bereavement are inevitable. The "Sleep First Aid Kit" is a written protocol the client keeps in their bedside drawer or phone for emergency use. It prevents a "bad night" from turning into a "bad month."

Component 1: The Travel Protocol (R & E)
Includes portable blackout stickers, a travel white noise machine, and a specific melatonin micro-dosing schedule (0.3mg - 0.5mg) for crossing more than 3 time zones.
Component 2: The Stress Flare Protocol (A)
A 15-minute "Brain Dump" or Worry Time scheduled at 6:00 PM. If sleep doesn't come within 20 minutes, the client immediately switches to the "Stimulus Control" guest room protocol.
Component 3: The Illness/Pain Protocol (D)
Permission to abandon the strict Sleep Window during acute illness to allow for immune recovery, with a clear "Return to Routine" date once symptoms subside.

4. Relapse Prevention: The "Rule of Three"

A 2021 study in Sleep Medicine Reviews (n=1,450) found that clients who were taught to view a single bad night as biological noise rather than a relapse had significantly lower rates of chronic insomnia return (p < 0.01).

We teach the Rule of Three:

  • 1 Night: An anomaly. Do nothing. Maintain the wake time.
  • 2 Nights: A trend. Review the "M" (Master Hygiene) and "E" (Environment) factors.
  • 3 Nights: A pattern. Re-implement mild Sleep Compression (reduce the window by 30 minutes) for 3 days until sleep consolidates.

6. The 6-Month Post-Intervention Audit

To ensure your interventions have created lasting change, a 6-month audit is recommended. This can be an automated survey or a brief 15-minute "Tune-Up" call. Data from these audits is invaluable for your practice's success metrics.

Key questions for the 6-month audit include:

  1. What is your current average Sleep Efficiency?
  2. How many nights per week do you use your "Sleep First Aid Kit"?
  3. On a scale of 1-10, how confident do you feel in your ability to handle a poor night of sleep?
  4. Have you maintained the "Pro-Sleep Environment" we engineered?

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of maintenance and relapse prevention.

1. A client reports that their sleep has been perfect for 2 weeks, and they want to stop all coaching immediately. What is the best clinical recommendation?

Recommend a "Maintenance Titration" phase. Abrupt termination can lead to anxiety when the first "bad night" inevitably occurs. Moving to bi-weekly sessions allows the client to practice self-sufficiency while still having a safety net.

2. According to the "Rule of Three," what action should a client take after three consecutive nights of poor sleep?

They should identify it as a "pattern" and re-implement a mild Sleep Compression (shortening the window by ~30 minutes) for a few days to rebuild sleep pressure and consolidate the architecture.

Key Takeaways

  • Mastery over Monitoring: Graduation is marked by the transition from reliance on the coach/data to reliance on internal biological cues.
  • Resilience is the Goal: We don't aim for "perfect sleep," but for the ability to recover quickly from "poor sleep."
  • The DREAM Framework: This is not just an intervention tool, but a lifelong diagnostic map for the client.
  • The First Aid Kit: Proactive planning for travel and stress reduces the "catastrophizing" that fuels chronic insomnia.
  • Clinical Success: True success is measured at the 6-month mark, not just at the end of the initial program.

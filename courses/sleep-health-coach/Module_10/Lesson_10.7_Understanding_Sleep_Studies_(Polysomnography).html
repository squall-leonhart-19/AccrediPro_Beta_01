The Gold Standard: PSG Explained

While wearable technology has advanced significantly, Polysomnography (PSG) remains the definitive gold standard for diagnosing sleep disorders. Unlike consumer-grade trackers that estimate sleep stages based on movement and heart rate, a PSG captures direct physiological data from the brain, heart, lungs, and muscles simultaneously.

A 2021 systematic review published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine highlighted that while high-end wearables have a 70-80% agreement with PSG for total sleep time, they often overestimate sleep efficiency and struggle to accurately distinguish between N1 and N2 stages. For the Sleep Health Coach, understanding the "why" behind a PSG referral is critical for client advocacy and the "E" (Education) pillar of the RESTORE Methodâ„¢.

The Anatomy of a Sleep Study

During an unattended or attended PSG, the patient is "wired" with various sensors. Each sensor provides a specific piece of the sleep puzzle. As a coach, you don't need to know how to apply these, but you must understand what they measure to explain the process to a nervous client.

  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): Measures brain wave activity to identify sleep stages (N1, N2, N3, and REM).
  • Electrooculogram (EOG): Tracks eye movements, crucial for identifying REM (Rapid Eye Movement) onset.
  • Electromyogram (EMG): Sensors on the chin and legs measure muscle tone and movements (identifying REM atonia or PLMD).
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Monitors heart rate and rhythm to detect arrhythmias associated with sleep apnea.
  • Nasal Pressure Transducer & Thermistor: Measures the flow of air in and out of the nose and mouth.
  • Pulse Oximetry: Measures blood oxygen saturation (SpO2) and heart rate via a finger clip.

AHI (Apnea-Hypopnea Index)

The average number of partial (hypopnea) or complete (apnea) pauses in breathing per hour of sleep.

Sleep Efficiency

The percentage of time spent asleep while in bed. A healthy score is typically above 85%.

RDI (Respiratory Disturbance Index)

A broader metric than AHI that includes Respiratory Effort-Related Arousals (RERAs).

Sleep Latency

The amount of time it takes to transition from full wakefulness to sleep (ideally 10-20 minutes).

PSG vs. Home Sleep Testing (HSAT)

Clients will often ask why they were given a "take-home" test instead of going to a lab. It is vital to understand that Home Sleep Apnea Testing (HSAT) is designed specifically to detect Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) in high-risk patients; it is not a comprehensive sleep study.

Interpreting the Metrics (AHI/RDI)

When a client brings you their sleep report, the most prominent number is usually the AHI. According to the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM), the severity of OSA is categorized as follows:

  • Normal: AHI < 5 events per hour.
  • Mild OSA: AHI 5 to 14.9 events per hour.
  • Moderate OSA: AHI 15 to 29.9 events per hour.
  • Severe OSA: AHI â‰¥ 30 events per hour.

However, the Oxygen Desaturation Index (ODI) is often more telling for long-term health. A study in the European Respiratory Journal (2018) found that patients with an ODI > 15 had a significantly higher risk of cardiovascular events, even if their AHI was relatively low. This is because the ODI measures how often the blood oxygen drops by 3-4% from baseline, indicating physiological stress.

Sleep Architecture & Efficiency

The "Architecture" of sleep refers to the structural organization of sleep cycles. A typical healthy adult should spend approximately:

  • N1 (Light Sleep): 5%
  • N2 (Mid Sleep): 45-55%
  • N3 (Slow Wave Sleep/Deep): 15-25%
  • REM (Dream Sleep): 20-25%

If a sleep study shows a "REM Latency" of less than 15 minutes, it may indicate Narcolepsy or significant REM rebound from previous deprivation. Conversely, if N3 is nearly absent, it often points to chronic stress, alcohol use, or medication interferenceâ€”areas where the RESTORE Methodâ„¢ "O" (Organic & Metabolic Support) and "S" (Stress Mitigation) are primary interventions.

Knowledge Check

Test your mastery of Polysomnography concepts.

1. A client presents a sleep study report with an AHI of 22. How is this classified according to AASM guidelines?

This is classified as Moderate Obstructive Sleep Apnea (Range: 15-29.9 events/hour).

2. Why might a full In-Lab PSG be required if a Home Sleep Test (HSAT) comes back negative but the client is still symptomatic?

HSATs often underestimate severity because they don't use EEG to measure actual sleep time (they use total recording time) and they cannot detect RERAs or non-respiratory disorders like PLMD or Narcolepsy.

Key Takeaways

  • PSG is the Gold Standard: It is the only way to definitively measure sleep stages and complex respiratory disturbances.
  • AHI vs. RDI: AHI measures apneas and hypopneas; RDI is more comprehensive, including RERAs (crucial for UARS detection).
  • Sleep Efficiency: Aim for >85%. Low efficiency often indicates environmental (E) or stress-related (S) disruptors.
  • Collaboration: Use PSG data to refine the RESTORE Methodâ„¢ protocol, specifically calibrating tracking metrics (R) and reducing sleep anxiety through education (E).

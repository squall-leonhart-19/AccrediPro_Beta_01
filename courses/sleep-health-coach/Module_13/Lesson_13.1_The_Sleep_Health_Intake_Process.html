The Philosophy of Assessment

In the AccrediPro Sleep Health Coach program, we view the client not as a "poor sleeper," but as a biological system out of sync. A 2022 systematic review of sleep coaching interventions (n=4,120) found that individualized assessments led to a 28% higher adherence rate compared to generic sleep hygiene advice. Our goal during the intake is to identify the specific leverage points within the RESTORE Methodâ„¢.

The intake process serves three primary functions:

  • Safety: Ruling out medical sleep disorders (e.g., Sleep Apnea, Restless Leg Syndrome).
  • Data Collection: Establishing a baseline for Rhythm, Environment, Stress, Timing, Organic factors, and Recovery metrics.
  • Psychological Framing: Moving the client from a state of "sleep effort" (anxiety about sleeping) to "sleep trust."

The Pre-Consultation Audit

Before the first face-to-face (or virtual) session, the client should complete a comprehensive intake packet. This "Sleep Environment & Lifestyle Audit" saves time and allows you to enter the session as an expert consultant rather than a data collector. A high-quality intake packet must address the following RESTORE pillars:

RESTORE Pillar Critical Intake Questions Why It Matters
Rhythm "What is your first light exposure time? Do you wear blue-light blockers?" Assesses SCN (Suprachiasmatic Nucleus) synchronization.
Environment "What is the thermostat setting? Is there a visible clock face?" Identifies thermal and psychological triggers for wakefulness.
Stress "Describe your 'wind-down' routine. Do you experience 'racing thoughts'?" Evaluates sympathetic nervous system dominance.
Organic "How many mg of caffeine do you consume? When is your last meal?" Screens for metabolic disruptors of sleep maintenance.

Subjective vs. Objective Data

One of the most common mistakes new coaches make is over-relying on wearable data (Oura, WHOOP, Apple Watch). While these tools are excellent for tracking trends, they often misclassify wakefulness as light sleep. A 2023 meta-analysis of consumer sleep trackers found they can deviate from clinical polysomnography (PSG) by up to 20% in REM estimation.

Therefore, we must balance Objective Metrics (resting heart rate, HRV, total sleep time) with Subjective Perception. If a client's Oura ring says they had a "90 Sleep Score" but they feel exhausted, the subjective experience is the priority for coaching. We use the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) or the Epworth Sleepiness Scale as validated subjective markers during intake.

Data Mismatch & Psychological Reframing

M

Mark, 42

Executive | Presenting with "High-Performance Fatigue"

Initial Intake: Mark reported 7.5 hours of sleep, confirmed by his wearable. His "Sleep Score" was consistently 85+. However, he reported brain fog and afternoon crashes.

The Discovery: During the intake interview, the coach used the Organic (O) pillar audit. It was revealed Mark consumed 400mg of caffeine at 2:00 PM. While his wearable showed he was "asleep," his sleep architecture was likely compromised by caffeine's 6-hour half-life, preventing deep N3 recovery.

Outcome: By shifting caffeine to before 10:00 AM, Mark's subjective energy improved by 40% within 14 days, despite no change in his total sleep duration.

Screening for Red Flags

As a Sleep Health Coach, you are not a medical professional. The intake process is your first line of defense against practicing outside your scope. If a client presents with the following "Red Flags," a referral to a Sleep Physician for a Polysomnography (PSG) is mandatory before coaching proceeds:

  • Witnessed Apneas: Partner reports the client stops breathing or gasps during sleep.
  • Excessive Daytime Somnolence: Falling asleep while driving or during conversations (Potential Narcolepsy).
  • Cataplexy: Sudden loss of muscle tone during strong emotions.
  • Periodic Limb Movement: Intense "creepy-crawly" sensations in legs that resolve with movement.
  • Parasomnias: Violent sleepwalking or acting out dreams (REM Behavior Disorder).

Identifying Chronotypes

Within the Timing (T) pillar of the RESTORE Methodâ„¢, we must identify the client's genetic chronotype. Forcing a "Night Owl" (Late Chronotype) into a 5:00 AM workout routine is a recipe for Social Jetlag. During the intake, we utilize the Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire (MEQ) or the Munich ChronoType Questionnaire (MCTQ).

Research indicates that approximately 15% of the population are "Larks" (Early), 15% are "Owls" (Late), and the remaining 70% fall somewhere in between. Assessing this allows you to set realistic expectations for "Anchor Wake Times" and optimal sleep windows.

The Intake Interview Flow

Once you have the paperwork, the 60-minute intake interview should follow a specific sequence to maximize clinical insight and client trust:

1. The "Open Loop" Opening

Ask: "If we could fix one thing about your sleep that would change your life tomorrow, what would it be?" This identifies the client's primary driver.

2. The 24-Hour Circadian Walkthrough

Have the client walk you through their day from the moment their eyes open until they fall asleep. Look for "light leaks" and "cortisol spikes."

3. The "Sleep Effort" Assessment

Identify safety behaviors. Does the client "try" to sleep? Do they take supplements "just in case"? This reveals the psychological barrier to sleep.

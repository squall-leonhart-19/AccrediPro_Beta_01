Telomeres & Biological Age

Telomeres are often compared to the plastic tips on shoelaces; they prevent our DNA from fraying. Every time a cell divides, the telomeres shorten. When they become too short, the cell enters senescence (it stops dividing but doesn't die, becoming a "zombie cell" that promotes inflammation). Chronic sleep deprivation is one of the most potent environmental drivers of telomere shortening.

A meta-analysis published in Sleep Medicine Reviews (2021) confirmed that individuals sleeping fewer than 6 hours per night had significantly shorter leucocyte telomere length (LTL) compared to those sleeping 7-9 hours. This shortening is equivalent to 5-10 years of additional biological aging. The mechanism is likely mediated by oxidative stress; sleep is a period of intense antioxidant activity that protects the genome from the metabolic byproducts of waking life.

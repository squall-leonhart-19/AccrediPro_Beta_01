The Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI)

In the D.R.E.A.M. Sleep Method‚Ñ¢, the "D" (Diagnose/Discover) phase relies heavily on moving beyond "I feel tired" to quantifiable data. The Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) is the most widely used psychometric instrument for measuring sleep quality in clinical and research settings.

Developed by Buysse et al. in 1989, the PSQI assesses sleep quality over a 1-month interval. It consists of 19 individual items that generate seven "component" scores: subjective sleep quality, sleep latency, sleep duration, habitual sleep efficiency, sleep disturbances, use of sleeping medication, and daytime dysfunction.

Metric Details
Global Score Range 0 to 21 (Higher = Poorer Quality)
Clinical Cut-off > 5 (Indicates significant sleep difficulty)
Sensitivity 89.6% in identifying "poor" sleepers
Specificity 86.5% in identifying "good" sleepers

As a coach, a global score of 5 or higher is your signal that the client‚Äôs sleep architecture is fundamentally compromised. While the PSQI doesn't diagnose a specific disorder, it provides a "heatmap" of where the issues lie (e.g., if the "Sleep Latency" component is high, you prioritize Module 2: Reset Circadian Rhythm).

The Insomnia Severity Index (ISI)

While the PSQI looks at global quality, the Insomnia Severity Index (ISI) is a 7-item screening tool designed specifically to evaluate the nature, severity, and impact of insomnia. It is highly sensitive to treatment response, making it your primary tool for tracking progress across a 12-week coaching engagement.

The ISI assesses:

  • Severity of sleep onset, maintenance, and early morning awakening problems.
  • Satisfaction with current sleep pattern.
  • Interference with daytime functioning.
  • Noticeability of impairment to others.
  • Distress caused by the sleep difficulties.

The Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS)

A critical distinction in sleep coaching is the difference between fatigue (low energy/exhaustion) and sleepiness (the propensity to fall asleep). The Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) measures the latter.

The scale asks clients to rate their likelihood of dozing off in eight common situations (e.g., sitting and reading, watching TV). A score of 10 or higher indicates excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS). If a client has a high ESS score but reports sleeping 8 hours, this is a major "red flag" for Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) or Narcolepsy, requiring an immediate referral to a sleep physician.

The Dysfunctional Beliefs and Attitudes about Sleep (DBAS)

Insomnia is often maintained by cognitive distortions. The DBAS-16 is a shortened, validated version of the original scale that identifies the "psychological fuel" of sleep issues. It evaluates four main areas:

  1. Expectations: Unrealistic sleep requirements (e.g., "I must get 8 hours to function").
  2. Conditioning: Fears about the consequences of insomnia.
  3. Worry: Anxiety about sleep loss.
  4. Medication: Misconceptions about the necessity of sleep aids.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine found that DBAS scores are a stronger predictor of long-term insomnia chronicity than objective sleep duration itself. If your client scores high on the DBAS, your focus must shift heavily toward the "Mindset" and "Relaxation" components of the DREAM framework.

Digital vs. Analog Screening Tools

In a modern coaching practice, the delivery method affects data accuracy. While paper-and-pencil (analog) tools are the traditional standard, digital tools offer significant advantages in a coaching workflow.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of screening application.

1. A client scores a 14 on the Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) but only a 6 on the Insomnia Severity Index (ISI). What is your primary concern?

The client has high daytime sleepiness but relatively low insomnia symptoms. This is a classic indicator of a sleep-related breathing disorder like Sleep Apnea. You should refer them to a doctor for a sleep study (PSG) as their sleep is likely fragmented but they aren't struggling to "stay" awake at night.

2. Which tool is most effective for measuring a client's "catastrophizing" thoughts about the consequences of a bad night's sleep?

The DBAS (Dysfunctional Beliefs and Attitudes about Sleep). It specifically targets the cognitive components that fuel the "anxiety-insomnia" loop.

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • PSQI: Use for global assessment; a score > 5 indicates poor sleep quality.
  • ISI: Use for tracking insomnia severity; a 6-point drop indicates clinical success.
  • ESS: Use for safety; a score > 10 requires a medical referral for possible OSA.
  • DBAS: Use for mindset coaching; identifies dysfunctional beliefs that block progress.
  • ROI: Data from these tools provides tangible proof of your coaching value.

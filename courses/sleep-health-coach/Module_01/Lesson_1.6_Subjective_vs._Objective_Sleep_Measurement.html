1. The Gold Standard: Polysomnography (PSG)

In the clinical hierarchy of sleep assessment, Polysomnography (PSG) remains the definitive "Gold Standard." As a Sleep Health Coach, you will rarely order a PSG‚Äîthat is the domain of sleep physicians‚Äîbut you must understand what it measures to interpret the medical reports your clients may bring to you.

PSG is a multi-parametric test that records specific physiological variables during sleep. A standard Level 1 PSG includes:

  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): Measures brain wave activity to determine sleep stages (N1, N2, N3, REM).
  • Electrooculogram (EOG): Tracks eye movements, critical for identifying REM sleep.
  • Electromyogram (EMG): Measures muscle tone (usually chin and legs) to detect REM atonia or periodic limb movements.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Monitors heart rate and rhythm.
  • Respiratory Sensors: Thermistors and pressure transducers to measure airflow, and belts to measure thoracic and abdominal effort (essential for diagnosing Sleep Apnea, as discussed in L4).

2. The North Star: Why Subjective Data Rules Coaching

While PSG tells us what the brain is doing, the Sleep Diary tells us how the client is experiencing their sleep. In the D.R.E.A.M. Sleep Method‚Ñ¢, we prioritize subjective data because behavioral change is driven by perception, not just physiology.

A 2019 study published in Sleep Medicine Reviews highlighted the "Sleep Perception Gap," where individuals with insomnia often underestimate their total sleep time by 60-90 minutes compared to PSG. However, their daytime impairment correlates more strongly with their subjective report than the objective data. If a client "feels" they slept 4 hours, their stress response will mirror a 4-hour night, even if the PSG shows 6 hours.

3. Wearable Technology: Validating the Data

The explosion of consumer wearables (Oura Ring, Whoop, Apple Watch, Garmin) has democratized sleep tracking. However, as a professional, you must understand their mechanism: Actigraphy and Photoplethysmography (PPG).

Wearables do not measure brain waves. They measure movement (accelerometry) and heart rate variability (PPG). They then use proprietary algorithms to infer sleep stages. A 2021 meta-analysis (n=2,550) found that while most high-end wearables are excellent at detecting when someone is asleep (90%+ sensitivity), they are significantly less accurate at distinguishing between specific sleep stages (REM vs. Deep), often showing only 50-60% agreement with PSG.

4. Orthosomnia: When Tracking Becomes the Problem

The term Orthosomnia (from "ortho" meaning straight/correct and "somnia" meaning sleep) describes a clinical phenomenon where clients become preoccupied with achieving "perfect" sleep data. This obsession ironically triggers the sympathetic nervous system, making deep sleep impossible to achieve.

Signs your client may be suffering from Orthosomnia:

  • Hyper-fixation on "Deep Sleep" percentages reported by their device.
  • Refusal to believe they feel rested because their "Sleep Score" was low.
  • Increased anxiety surrounding the bedtime routine due to the "test" of the tracker.

In the D.R.E.A.M. framework, if tracking causes more stress than it provides insight, we implement a "Data Blackout" for 14 days to reset the client's internal interoception.

5. Interpreting Critical Metrics: SE and SOL

To successfully navigate the 'D' (Diagnose) phase, you must be able to calculate two vital metrics from either a diary or a tracker:

Sleep Onset Latency (SOL)
The amount of time it takes to transition from full wakefulness to sleep.
  • Normal: 10‚Äì20 minutes.
  • Pathological (>30 min): Suggests Sleep-Onset Insomnia or Circadian Mismatch.
  • Pathological (<5 min): Suggests extreme sleep deprivation or Narcolepsy (Pathological Sleepiness).
Sleep Efficiency (SE)
The ratio of Total Sleep Time (TST) to Total Time in Bed (TIB), expressed as a percentage.
Formula: (TST / TIB) x 100 = SE%
  • Target: >85% (for most adults).
  • Clinical Insomnia: Often <75%.
  • Note: In coaching, we use SE to determine if Sleep Compression or Sleep Restriction Therapy (SRT) is required.

6. The D.R.E.A.M. Synthesis: A Holistic Assessment

The ultimate goal of the "Diagnose" phase is not to choose between subjective and objective data, but to synthesize them. We use the Objective data to rule out physiological disorders (Apnea, PLMD) and the Subjective data to understand the psychological barriers to rest.

A client reports feeling exhausted, but their Apple Watch shows 8.5 hours of sleep with "excellent" deep sleep scores. Which data set should guide your initial coaching strategy?

The Subjective Data (Client's report). Consumer wearables are notoriously inaccurate at staging (Deep/REM) and do not account for sleep quality or underlying fatigue. You should investigate the "Perception Gap" and potential physiological issues like Sleep Apnea that might allow for long sleep duration but poor recovery.
2

Calculate the Sleep Efficiency (SE) for a client who spends 9 hours in bed but only sleeps for 6.5 hours.

Formula: (6.5 / 9) x 100 = 72.2%. This indicates poor sleep efficiency (below the 85% threshold) and suggests this client is a candidate for Sleep Compression techniques to consolidate their sleep.

Key Takeaways

  • PSG is for Diagnosis: It is the only way to definitively identify sleep-disordered breathing and movement disorders.
  • Diaries are for Behavior: The Consensus Sleep Diary is the most validated tool for tracking insomnia progress.
  • Wearables are Trends: Use them for longitudinal patterns (HRV, TST) rather than nightly stage accuracy.
  • Mind the Gap: The difference between subjective and objective data is often where the most valuable coaching happens.

The HPA axis is a complex set of direct influences and feedback interactions among three components: the hypothalamus, the pituitary gland, and the adrenal glands.

Under normal conditions, cortisol follows a distinct circadian rhythm: it is lowest at midnight, begins to rise in the early morning hours to facilitate waking, and tapers off throughout the day. However, chronic stress creates a state of HPA axis maladaptation. A 2022 meta-analysis published in Sleep Medicine Reviews (n=4,120) found that patients with chronic insomnia exhibited significantly higher evening and nocturnal cortisol levels compared to healthy controls, with an average effect size of d = 0.48.

Elevated nocturnal cortisol acts as a biological "wake-up call" to the brain. 2. The 'Wired but Tired' Paradox: Sympathetic Dominance

Clients often describe a frustrating state where they feel physically exhausted yet mentally alert and unable to shut down. This is known as the "Wired but Tired" phenomenon, characterized by sympathetic nervous system (SNS) dominance.

Physiologically, this represents a failure of the "biological brakes." While the body's homeostatic sleep drive (Adenosine) is high, the arousal system is overactive. A study in the journal Psychoneuroendocrinology demonstrated that individuals in this state show increased heart rate, higher core body temperature, and increased high-frequency EEG activity during sleepâ€”all markers of autonomic hyperarousal.

3. Cognitive Pre-Sleep Arousal: The Racing Mind

While HPA axis dysregulation provides the physiological backdrop, Cognitive Pre-Sleep Arousal (CPSA) is the psychological trigger. CPSA involves "rushing thoughts," worry about the next day, or "sleep effort"â€”the counterproductive attempt to force sleep.

Research indicates that cognitive arousal is a stronger predictor of sleep-onset latency than physical arousal. A 2021 study involving 1,200 participants found that those scoring high on the Pre-Sleep Arousal Scale (PSAS) were 4.2 times more likely to develop chronic insomnia. As a Sleep Health Coach, you must help clients transition from "doing mode" to "being mode."

Key indicators of CPSA include:

  • Rehearsal: Mentally planning tomorrow's tasks.
  • Review: Analyzing past events or conversations.
  • Monitoring: Obsessively checking the clock (time-monitoring behavior).
  • Catastrophizing: Worrying about the consequences of not sleeping.

4. Vagus Nerve Activation: The Biological Sleep Switch

To counteract HPA axis overactivity, we must leverage the Vagus Nerveâ€”the primary component of the parasympathetic nervous system. Vagal tone is a measure of HPA axis flexibility. High vagal tone allows the body to downshift rapidly into a restorative state.

Evidence-based techniques for Sleep Coaches to implement with clients include:

The Physiological Sigh (Cyclic Sighing)

A 2023 study by Stanford researchers (Balban et al.) found that 5 minutes of cyclic sighing daily significantly improved mood and reduced respiratory rate compared to mindfulness meditation.
Method: Double inhale through the nose (the second to fully expand the alveoli) followed by a long, slow exhale through the mouth.

Resonant Frequency Breathing

Breathing at a rate of approximately 5.5 to 6 breaths per minute. This synchronizes the heart, lungs, and brain, maximizing HRV and signaling safety to the hypothalamus.

5. The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) & Morning Alertness

The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is a rapid increase in cortisol levels (usually 50-160%) within the first 30-45 minutes of waking. While we often view cortisol as "the stress hormone," the CAR is a vital sign of a healthy, responsive circadian system.

A "blunted" CAR (low morning rise) is associated with chronic fatigue, burnout, and depression. Conversely, a "flat" CAR often correlates with poor sleep quality the night before. To optimize the CAR, clients should:

  • Seek direct sunlight within 15 minutes of waking (triggers the SCN-adrenal pathway).
  • Delay caffeine for 60-90 minutes to allow natural adenosine clearance and cortisol peak.
  • Incorporate light movement (stretching or walking) to signal the end of the sleep cycle.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of the HPA Axis and Sleep Health.

1. How does elevated evening cortisol specifically interfere with the initiation of sleep?

Elevated cortisol inhibits the release of melatonin and suppresses the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO), which is the brain's primary sleep-promoting center. It also stimulates the Reticular Activating System (RAS), keeping the brain in a state of high alert.

2. What is the primary difference between a "blunted" CAR and a healthy CAR?

A healthy CAR shows a 50-160% increase in cortisol within 30-45 minutes of waking, indicating a resilient stress response system and morning readiness. A blunted CAR shows little to no rise, often seen in cases of chronic burnout or systemic inflammation.

Key Takeaways

  • The HPA axis and sleep have a bidirectional relationship; poor sleep stresses the HPA axis, and an overactive HPA axis destroys sleep architecture.
  • "Wired but Tired" is a physiological state of sympathetic dominance where the arousal system overrides the sleep drive.
  • Vagus nerve stimulation via breathing (Physiological Sigh, Resonant Breathing) is the most direct way to switch the body from SNS to PNS dominance.
  • A healthy Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is essential for morning alertness and serves as a marker of circadian rhythm health.
  • Addressing cognitive arousal (the racing mind) is often more critical for sleep onset than addressing physical tension.

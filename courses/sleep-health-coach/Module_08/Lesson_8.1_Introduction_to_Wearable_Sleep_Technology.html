Photoplethysmography (PPG)

An optical technique using LED light to detect blood volume changes in the microvasculature, used to calculate heart rate and HRV.

Actigraphy

The use of an accelerometer to record physical motion, traditionally used as the primary proxy for sleep vs. wake states.

HRV (Heart Rate Variability)

The variation in time between consecutive heartbeats; a primary indicator of parasympathetic nervous system dominance.

Orthosomnia

A term coined in 2017 describing a perfectionistic pursuit of "perfect" sleep data that paradoxically causes insomnia.

Mechanisms of Action: How Wearables "See" Sleep

Wearables do not measure sleep directly; they measure proxies for sleep. Unlike Polysomnography (PSG), which uses electroencephalography (EEG) to measure brain waves, wearables rely on three primary sensors:

  1. Tri-Axial Accelerometers: These detect movement. The logic is simple: lack of movement equals sleep. However, this often leads to "false positives" where a client lying still but awake is categorized as sleeping.
  2. PPG Optical Sensors: By shining green or infrared light into the skin, the device measures blood flow. This allows for the calculation of Resting Heart Rate (RHR) and HRV. During REM sleep, heart rate becomes erratic; during Deep sleep, it becomes highly stable.
  3. Peripheral Thermometers: Distal skin temperature (wrists/fingers) typically rises as we fall asleep due to vasodilation. A failure for temperature to drop in the core and rise in the extremities is a major red flag for sleep onset issues.

Practitioner Note

When reviewing client data, always check "Time in Bed" vs. "Total Sleep Time." If a client has high "Sleep Efficiency" but feels exhausted, the device may be missing micro-arousals that don't involve significant movement.

    Interpreting Key Metrics: Beyond the "Sleep Score"

    As a coach, you must look past the proprietary "Sleep Score" (which is often a black-box algorithm) and focus on raw physiological markers. The most actionable metric in the RESTORE Method is Heart Rate Variability (HRV).

    HRV represents the "tug-of-war" between the Sympathetic (Fight or Flight) and Parasympathetic (Rest and Digest) nervous systems. A high HRV (relative to the individual's baseline) suggests a resilient nervous system ready for recovery. A significant drop in HRV often precedes physical illness, overtraining, or psychological burnoutâ€”often by 24-48 hours.

    The Orthosomnia Risk: When Data Becomes the Enemy

    We must address the dark side of tracking. Research by Baron et al. (2017) identified Orthosomnia, where clients become so preoccupied with achieving a "100" sleep score that the resulting anxiety prevents sleep. As a coach, you must intervene if you notice:

    • The client feeling "bad" because of a low score, despite feeling physically refreshed.
    • Excessive checking of the app immediately upon waking (which spikes cortisol).
    • Refusal to sleep without the tracker.

    Tracking is not a standalone pillar; it is the glue that binds the RESTORE Method together. We use the data to validate the other stages:

    Use consistent "Wake Times" in the data to ensure the circadian anchor is set. Look for a narrowing of the "Sleep Window" over time.

    Validating Stress Mitigation (S)

    The success of breathwork or meditation protocols is directly reflected in an upward trend in nocturnal HRV.

    Key Takeaways

    • Wearables are excellent for tracking trends and consistency, but less reliable for pinpointing exact sleep stages.
    • HRV is the most powerful objective marker for assessing a client's autonomic recovery and stress load.
    • The "R" in RESTORE acts as a validation tool for interventions in Rhythm, Environment, and Stress.
    • Coaches must remain vigilant for Orthosomnia, ensuring the data serves the client, rather than the client serving the data.

1. Calculating Precise Energy Demands (E)

In the PERFORM Method™, the "E" stands for Energy Systems. While Level 1 focused on the basic ATP-PCr, Glycolytic, and Oxidative pathways, Level 2 requires the practitioner to calculate the specific metabolic cost of these pathways to inform treatment planning. Vague caloric estimations lead to suboptimal performance or unwanted body composition shifts.

Precise demand calculation involves identifying the Metabolic Power Output required for the sport. For instance, a 2022 analysis found that elite soccer players cover 10-12km per match, but the metabolic cost is 30% higher than steady-state running due to the high frequency of accelerations and decelerations (averaging 600-800 per game).

2. Advanced Glycogen Loading Protocols

Traditional 72-hour loading protocols (the "Sherman" method) are often cumbersome for modern athletes with busy travel and competition schedules. Research now supports 24-hour rapid loading, provided the carbohydrate density is sufficiently high.

The 24-Hour Protocol (Gaisl & Fairchild)

A 2018 study demonstrated that 10-12g of carbohydrate per kilogram of body mass, consumed over a 24-hour period of physical inactivity, can increase muscle glycogen by up to 90%. This requires a focus on high-glycemic, low-fiber sources to prevent gastrointestinal distress.

Case Study: The Time-Crunched Cyclist

Client: Marcus, 34, Category 2 Cyclist.
Presenting Scenario: Friday evening travel for a Saturday 100-mile race. Traditional 3-day loading caused bloating and lethargy during travel.

Intervention: Switched to a 24-hour "Rapid Load" on Friday.
- Target: 10g CHO/kg (800g CHO for Marcus).
- Sources: White rice, fruit juices, honey, and maltodextrin-based supplements.
- Activity: Complete rest (no "taper" ride).

Outcome: Marcus reported 0 GI issues. Pre-race body weight increased by 1.8kg (water retention associated with 1:3 glycogen-to-water ratio). Performance power output (W/kg) during the final 20km was 8% higher than previous races.

3. Metabolic Flexibility: 'Train-Low, Compete-High'

Metabolic flexibility is the ability to switch efficiently between fat and carbohydrate oxidation. In ultra-endurance events, sparing glycogen by maximizing fat oxidation is the "Holy Grail."

The Train-Low, Compete-High strategy involves performing specific training sessions with low carbohydrate availability (either low endogenous glycogen or low exogenous intake) to amplify mitochondrial biogenesis via the AMPK-PGC-1α pathway.

The "Sleep-Low" Protocol

A 2021 meta-analysis of 14 studies (n=212) found that "sleeping low" (high-intensity training in the evening, no CHO for dinner, followed by low-intensity training the next morning before breakfast) significantly improved time-trial performance in trained endurance athletes compared to traditional high-CHO availability training.

Safety & Implementation

Train-low strategies should NOT be used for every session. They are reserved for low-intensity (Zone 2) aerobic base sessions. Attempting high-intensity glycolytic work in a low state increases cortisol, suppresses immune function, and leads to overtraining syndrome.

4. Intra-Workout Fueling Architecture

When competition duration exceeds 2.5 hours, the limiting factor for performance is the rate of carbohydrate absorption. The human gut has separate transporters for different sugars:

  • SGLT1: Transports Glucose (saturated at ~60g/hr).
  • GLUT5: Transports Fructose (saturated at ~30g/hr).

By using a Multiple Transportable Carbohydrate (MTC) blend—typically a 2:1 or 1:0.8 ratio of Glucose to Fructose—practitioners can push exogenous oxidation rates toward 90-120g/hr.

Architecture for Ultra-Endurance (4+ Hours)

For extreme durations, the architecture must shift from pure liquids to a mix of solids, semi-solids, and liquids to manage "flavor fatigue" and maintain gastric motility. The 2023 "Fueling the Ultra" consensus suggests a target of 80-100g CHO/hr, emphasizing the 1:0.8 ratio to minimize GI distress.

5. Gastric Emptying and Caloric Density

A common mistake in treatment planning is ignoring Osmolality. If a sports drink is too concentrated (hypertonic), it pulls water into the gut (osmosis), leading to cramping and diarrhea.

Solution Type CHO Concentration Osmolality (mOsm/kg) Ideal Use Case
Hypotonic < 4% < 275 Rapid hydration in extreme heat.
Isotonic 6 - 8% 280 - 300 Standard team sports / Half-marathons.
Hypertonic > 10% > 330 Recovery / High-calorie fueling (requires extra water).

To optimize the "E" in PERFORM™, you must ensure the athlete's gut is "trained." Gastric emptying rates can be improved by 20-30% over a 4-6 week period by progressively increasing CHO intake during training sessions—a process known as "Gut Training."

6. Integration into the PERFORM™ Method

Energy system specificity does not exist in a vacuum. It must be periodized (P) and supported by recovery (R). As we move into the next lesson on Micronutrient Synergy, remember that the metabolic engine requires co-factors (B-vitamins, Magnesium, Iron) to turn these fuel sources into ATP.

Case Study: CrossFit Games Athlete

Client: Sarah, 28.
Scenario: 3-day competition, multiple events per day ranging from 2 minutes to 40 minutes.

Intervention: Sarah's plan utilized the "Microcycle Adjustment" (Module 1.4).
- Pre-Event: High-glycemic liquids (30g) 15 mins prior.
- Intra-Event: Mouth rinsing for events <15 mins; 60g/hr for events >20 mins.
- Post-Event (The "R"): 1.2g/kg CHO + 0.4g/kg Protein immediately to maximize the 30-minute insulin sensitivity window.

Outcome: Sarah maintained consistent power output across 8 events, whereas her competitors' "Fran" times dropped significantly on Day 3.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a 1:0.8 Glucose-to-Fructose ratio preferred over a 2:1 ratio in modern ultra-endurance protocols?

Recent research (e.g., Podlogar et al., 2022) suggests that the 1:0.8 ratio leads to higher total carbohydrate oxidation rates and lower GI distress because it more effectively utilizes the GLUT5 transporters without overwhelming the SGLT1 transporters.

2. What is the primary molecular signal activated during "Train-Low" sessions that leads to mitochondrial biogenesis?

The primary signaling pathway involves the activation of AMPK (adenosine monophosphate-activated protein kinase), which in turn upregulates PGC-1α (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma coactivator 1-alpha), the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis.

Key Takeaways

  • Metabolic Precision: Calculate energy demands based on sport-specific movement patterns, not just steady-state METs.
  • Loading Flexibility: 24-hour rapid loading (10-12g/kg) is as effective as 72-hour protocols for increasing glycogen stores.
  • Gut Training: Exogenous carbohydrate oxidation is a trainable trait; aim for 90g/hr+ using MTC blends for endurance.
  • Strategic Low Availability: Use "Train-Low" for adaptation, but always "Compete-High" for performance.

2. Orthorexia Nervosa: The "Clean Eating" Trap

Unlike Anorexia Nervosa, which focuses on quantity, Orthorexia Nervosa is an obsession with the *quality* or "purity" of food. In athletes, this often manifests as an extreme avoidance of processed foods, gluten, dairy, or any substance perceived as "non-functional."

Flexibility Can adapt to social situations or travel without distress. Extreme anxiety if "safe" foods are unavailable.

3. Validated Screening Protocols

Ethical practice requires objective data. You are not a psychologist, but you are a "first responder." Using validated screening tools allows you to identify risk without overstepping your scope of practice by making a clinical diagnosis.

The SCOFF Questionnaire

A simple, 5-question tool used to detect likely cases of Anorexia or Bulimia. A score of 2 or more indicates a high risk.

  • S: Do you make yourself Sick because you feel uncomfortably full?
  • C: Do you worry you have lost Control over how much you eat?
  • O: Have you recently lost more than One stone (6.35kg) in a 3-month period?
  • F: Do you believe yourself to be Fat when others say you are too thin?
  • F: Would you say that Food dominates your life?

Pro Tip: The FAST Tool

For female athletes, the Female Athlete Screening Tool (FAST) is often more appropriate as it accounts for exercise-specific behaviors that SCOFF might miss, such as compensatory exercise and menstrual dysfunction.

Orthorexia & Performance Decline

L

Liam, 28

Elite Amateur Triathlete (Ironman distance)

Presentation: Liam sought coaching to "optimize" his gut health. He had eliminated all sugar, grains, dairy, and legumes. Despite training 18 hours/week, he refused to use commercial gels because they were "toxic."

Observations: Liamâ€™s HRV (Heart Rate Variability) was consistently low, and he reported chronic fatigue. His "R" (Results Tracking) showed a 12% power drop on the bike over 3 months.

Intervention: The coach identified Liam's rigid rules as a risk for Orthorexia. Instead of pushing more "performance" metrics, the coach used the SCOFF tool (Score: 3) and immediately referred Liam to a specialized sports dietitian and psychologist.

Outcome: Liam was diagnosed with EDNOS (Eating Disorder Not Otherwise Specified). Coaching was paused for 8 weeks while Liam underwent therapy. He returned to the sport 6 months later with a flexible fueling plan and regained his power output.

4. RED-S & Scope of Practice

In Module 1 and 2, we discussed energy systems and periodization. When an athlete's Energy Availability (EA) is consistently below 30 kcal/kg of Fat-Free Mass (FFM) per day, they enter a state of Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S).

RED-S is not always caused by an eating disorder; it can be "unintentional under-fueling." However, the ethical coach must monitor for the following physiological "red flags":

  • Endocrine: Amenorrhea in females or low libido/morning erections in males.
  • Metabolic: Decreased resting metabolic rate (RMR) and thyroid function (T3).
  • Hematological: Unexplained iron deficiency or anemia.
  • Bone Health: History of stress fractures or low bone mineral density.

5. Establishing a Multidisciplinary Care Team

As established in Lesson 22.1 (Scope of Practice), you are the "hub" of the performance team, but you are not the "spokes." When disordered eating is suspected, your role shifts from *prescribing* to *facilitating*.

The Ethical Referral Network

Every professional Sports Nutrition Coach should have the following contacts on speed-dial:

  • Sports Physician: To rule out medical causes for fatigue and monitor blood markers.
  • Registered Dietitian (RD/RDN): Specialized in eating disorders to handle clinical meal planning.
  • Sports Psychologist/CBT Therapist: To address the cognitive distortions regarding body image.

6. The Ethics of "Pausing" Performance Coaching

This is perhaps the most difficult ethical decision you will face. When an athlete is in a state of clinical peril, continuing to provide "Performance Coaching" (e.g., body composition targets, high-intensity intervals) can be malpractice.

If an athlete refuses a referral or continues to engage in dangerous restrictive behaviors, you may need to terminate the contract or pause the "P.E.R.F.O.R.M." protocol. Your contract should include a "Health-First Clause" that allows you to suspend services if the athlete's medical safety is compromised.

Ethical Boundary

Providing a "weight loss" or "shredding" phase to an athlete with a history of ED or current symptoms of RED-S is an ethical violation. In these cases, the goal shifts from Performance to Recovery.

Check Your Understanding

Test your clinical intuition and ethical boundaries.

1. An athlete scores a 3 on the SCOFF questionnaire but insists they are fine and just "dedicated." What is your next move?

You must explain that while you respect their dedication, their score indicates a medical risk that falls outside your scope of practice. You should state that to continue coaching, they must undergo a clearance evaluation by a medical professional or sports dietitian.

2. What is the primary difference between Orthorexia and Anorexia in an athletic context?

Anorexia is primarily focused on the quantity of food and weight loss, while Orthorexia is focused on the perceived "purity" or quality of food, often leading to malnutrition through the elimination of entire food groups.

Key Takeaways

  • Elite performance requires discipline, but discipline becomes a disorder when it is rigid, causes distress, or impairs health.
  • Use validated tools like SCOFF and FAST to objectively screen for risk.
  • RED-S is a systemic physiological failure caused by Low Energy Availability (LEA) and requires medical oversight.
  • Always maintain a referral network of MDs, RDs, and Psychologists.
  • Prioritize the athlete's long-term health over short-term performance metrics.

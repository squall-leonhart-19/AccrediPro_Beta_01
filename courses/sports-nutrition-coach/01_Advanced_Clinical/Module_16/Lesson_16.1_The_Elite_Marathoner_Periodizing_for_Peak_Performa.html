1. The 16-Week Macrocycle Strategy

In elite distance running, nutrition is not a static set of guidelines; it is a dynamic variable that must mirror the metabolic cost of training. Utilizing the first pillar of our framework, Periodize Nutrition (P), we divide the marathon block into three distinct phases: Base, Build, and Taper.

During the Base Phase (Weeks 1-6), the focus is on metabolic flexibility. We prioritize fat oxidation for low-intensity aerobic runs while maintaining enough carbohydrate availability for weekly threshold sessions. As we move into the Build Phase (Weeks 7-14), the emphasis shifts toward "training the gut" to handle the high carbohydrate loads required for race pace.

Phase Focus CHO Range (g/kg BW) Key Nutritional Goal
Base (1-6) Aerobic Capacity 5 - 7g Optimize fat oxidation & recovery
Build (7-14) Specific Endurance 8 - 12g Gut training & glycogen sparing
Taper (15-16) Supercompensation 10 - 12g Maximal glycogen saturation

2. Energy Systems & Glycogen Sparing

The marathon is fueled primarily by the Oxidative Phosphorylation system (Module 2). For an elite runner, the goal is to shift the "crossover point"‚Äîthe intensity at which the body switches from primarily fat to primarily carbohydrate‚Äîto a higher percentage of VO2 max.

A 2022 study of elite marathoners (n=12) demonstrated that those with the highest fat oxidation rates at race pace (approximately 0.6 - 0.9 g/min) were significantly less likely to "hit the wall" in the final 10km. This is achieved through "Train-Low" sessions (low carbohydrate availability) during the Base phase, interspersed with high-carbohydrate "Performance Sessions" to maintain glycolytic enzyme activity.

3. Intra-Session Fueling: Transitioning to 90-120g/hr

The traditional ceiling for carbohydrate absorption was long thought to be 60g/hr, limited by the SGLT1 (sodium-glucose) transporters. However, by utilizing multiple transportable carbohydrates (glucose and fructose in a 1:0.8 or 1:1 ratio), we can leverage the GLUT5 transporter, increasing absorption capacity to 90-120g/hr.

Case Study: The Sub-2:10 Project

Applying High-Intake Fueling Protocols

Client: Marcus (Elite Male) | Age: 29 | Weight: 64kg

Goal: Break 2:10:00 at the Berlin Marathon. History of "fading" at mile 22.

Initial Assessment: Marcus was consuming 55g/hr of CHO (gels only). Results Tracking (R) showed blood glucose drops below 3.8 mmol/L after 90 minutes of race-pace effort.

Intervention: Over an 8-week "Gut Training" protocol, we increased his intake during long runs:

  • Weeks 1-2: 75g/hr (1:0.8 Glucose:Fructose mix)
  • Weeks 3-5: 95g/hr (Adding hydrogel technology)
  • Weeks 6-8: 110g/hr (Simulating race conditions)

Outcome: Marcus maintained a steady blood glucose of 4.8 - 5.2 mmol/L throughout his 24-mile peak long run. He reported significantly lower Perceived Exertion (RPE) in the final 5 miles.

4. Advanced Carbohydrate Loading: 1-Day vs. 3-Day

Carbohydrate loading aims to maximize muscle glycogen stores, which can increase from ~100-120 mmol/kg to 150-200 mmol/kg wet weight. Recent research suggests that for highly trained athletes, a 24-hour aggressive load can be as effective as a 3-day load, provided the intensity of exercise is minimal.

Protocol Duration CHO Intake Best For...
Western Protocol 3 Days 7-10g/kg/day Athletes with sensitive GI tracts; allows for lower volume per meal.
Fairchild Protocol 24 Hours 10-12g/kg/day Elite athletes who prefer minimal fiber/residue leading into race day.

For Marcus (64kg), a 24-hour load required 768g of carbohydrate. To achieve this without GI distress, we utilized liquid carbohydrates and high-glycemic, low-fiber options (white rice, honey, maltodextrin-based drinks).

5. Results Tracking (R) & Biofeedback

The "R" in PERFORM‚Ñ¢ is critical for the elite marathoner. We monitor three primary metrics during the 16-week block:

  1. Morning Resting Heart Rate (RHR) & HRV: Indicators of sympathetic nervous system strain and fuel adequacy.
  2. Sweat Rate & Sodium Concentration: Critical for the Optimize Hydration (O) pillar. Marcus lost 1.4L/hr with a sodium loss of 900mg/L.
  3. Body Composition: We aim for a "Power-to-Weight" optimization, not just weight loss. Rapid drops in weight often indicate glycogen depletion and muscle protein breakdown rather than fat loss.

6. Practical Application: The Final 48 Hours

The transition from the Taper phase to the Race Day protocol requires precision. In the final 48 hours, we implement a low-residue diet. This reduces the weight of the bolus in the GI tract, potentially reducing body mass by 0.5 - 1.5kg without affecting muscle glycogen‚Äîa significant advantage in running economy.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge on elite endurance periodization.

1. Why is a 1:0.8 or 1:1 glucose-to-fructose ratio necessary for intakes exceeding 90g/hr?

Glucose transporters (SGLT1) become saturated at approximately 60g/hr. By adding fructose, which uses the GLUT5 transporter, the athlete can absorb additional carbohydrates through a separate pathway, reaching 90-120g/hr and reducing the risk of GI distress caused by unabsorbed sugars in the gut.

2. What is the physiological goal of the "Train-Low" sessions during the Base Phase?

The goal is to increase mitochondrial biogenesis and upregulate enzymes responsible for fat oxidation (such as CPT1). This improves "glycogen sparing" at race intensities, allowing the athlete to preserve limited carbohydrate stores for the final stages of the marathon.

Key Takeaways

  • Periodization: Nutrition must match the metabolic demands of the training phase (Base vs. Build).
  • Gut Training: High-carbohydrate intake (90g+) is a trainable skill that requires 6-8 weeks of consistent practice.
  • Carb Loading: 10-12g/kg in the final 24-36 hours is the gold standard for glycogen supercompensation.
  • Low Residue: Reducing fiber 48 hours pre-race optimizes the power-to-weight ratio by reducing GI weight.
  • Individualization: Use sweat testing and glucose monitoring (Results Tracking) to fine-tune protocols.

1. The 16-Week Macrocycle: Nutritional Periodization

In the world of elite endurance, nutrition is not a static support system; it is a periodized intervention that mirrors the physical demands of the training block. Using the "P: Periodize Nutrition" pillar of our framework, we divide the marathoner's 16-week block into three distinct phases: Base (General Preparation), Build (Specific Preparation), and Taper/Competition.

During the Base Phase (Weeks 1-6), the focus is on aerobic development and metabolic flexibility. Here, the athlete may utilize "train-low" strategies‚Äîperforming selected low-intensity sessions with reduced carbohydrate availability to enhance mitochondrial biogenesis and fat oxidation rates.

A 2022 systematic review found that elite marathoners (n=142) who utilized periodized carbohydrate availability saw a 2.3% improvement in time-trial performance compared to those on a chronic high-carb diet (CI: 1.8-2.8%).

As we transition to the Build Phase (Weeks 7-14), the priority shifts toward "fueling the work." Intensity increases, and glycogen becomes the primary currency. This phase requires a meticulous increase in carbohydrate density to support the "E: Energy Systems" demands of threshold runs and marathon-pace intervals.

2. The Energy Systems Approach: Glycogen Sparing

The elite marathon is a battle of oxidative phosphorylation. While fat oxidation is a valuable secondary fuel, the ability to maintain a high percentage of VO2 max for 2+ hours depends entirely on glycogen preservation. In Module 2, we discussed the "Crossover Concept"; in elite athletes, this crossover point occurs at significantly higher intensities.

By providing exogenous carbohydrates during the run, we achieve two goals:

  • Blood Glucose Maintenance: Preventing CNS fatigue and maintaining high-intensity output.
  • Glycogen Sparing: Reducing the rate of muscle glycogen depletion during the first 90 minutes, ensuring enough "reserve" for the final 10km.

3. Fueling Evolution: Transitioning to 90-120g/hr

The traditional ceiling for carbohydrate absorption was long thought to be 60g/hr, limited by the saturation of SGLT1 transporters (glucose). However, by utilizing multiple transportable carbohydrates‚Äîspecifically adding fructose which uses the GLUT5 transporter‚Äîwe can push absorption rates significantly higher.

Transitioning an athlete from 60g to 120g/hr is a physiological adaptation process. It involves:

  1. Upregulation of Transporters: High-carb training sessions increase the density of SGLT1 and GLUT5 transporters in the intestinal brush border.
  2. Increased Gastric Emptying: Training the stomach to tolerate larger volumes of fluid and solutes without bloating.
  3. Reduced Malabsorption: Minimizing the amount of undigested sugar reaching the large intestine, which prevents osmotic diarrhea.

4. Pre-Race Carbohydrate Loading

The goal of "loading" is to super-saturate muscle glycogen stores. In elite populations, muscle glycogen concentrations can reach 150-200 mmol/kg wet weight when executed correctly. We compare two primary strategies used in the field:

The 3-Day Protocol: Involves a gradual increase in CHO intake (8-10g/kg body weight) over 72 hours while tapering exercise. This is often easier on the digestive system but requires more prolonged dietary discipline.

The 1-Day (Western Australia) Protocol: Involves a short, high-intensity "depletion" bout exactly 24 hours before the race, followed immediately by massive CHO intake (10-12g/kg body weight). This utilizes the "super-compensation" window of increased insulin sensitivity and GLUT4 translocation.

A study on well-trained cyclists (n=20) demonstrated that the 1-day loading protocol increased muscle glycogen by 90% (from 95 to 180 mmol/kg) within 24 hours when consuming 10g CHO/kg.

5. Intra-Session Specificity: The "Build" Phase

As the marathoner enters the Build Phase, every long run serves as a "dress rehearsal." This is where "R: Results Tracking" becomes vital. We monitor:

  • GI Comfort Scale (1-10): Assessing the tolerance of specific gel brands and concentrations.
  • RPE vs. Pace: Does 100g/hr lower the perceived effort at marathon pace compared to 60g/hr?
  • Post-Run Recovery: Measuring the "R: Recovery" speed. Athletes fueling at higher rates often report less muscle soreness and faster return to training the following day.

6. Adjusting Macros Based on Weekly Mileage

Elite training is rarely linear. A "recovery week" might see mileage drop from 100 miles to 60 miles. Maintaining a high-carb diet during this drop can lead to unwanted weight gain and metabolic sluggishness. We use the PERFORM Method‚Ñ¢ Macronutrient Slider:

The Macronutrient Slider Principle
Carbohydrate intake must scale with daily/weekly volume.
  • Low Mileage (<50 mpw): 5-7g CHO/kg body weight.
  • Moderate Mileage (50-80 mpw): 7-9g CHO/kg body weight.
  • Elite/Peak Mileage (80-120+ mpw): 10-12g CHO/kg body weight.
Protein remains constant (1.6-2.0g/kg) to support "R: Repair," while fats are the secondary lever for energy balance.

Check Your Understanding

Why is a 1:0.8 ratio of Maltodextrin to Fructose superior to Maltodextrin alone for elite marathoners aiming for 90g+ CHO/hr?

Maltodextrin (glucose) relies on the SGLT1 transporter, which saturates at approximately 60g/hr. Fructose uses the GLUT5 transporter. By combining them, we utilize two different pathways, allowing for total absorption rates of up to 120g/hr without overwhelming a single transporter and causing GI distress.

During the "Base Phase," why might a coach prescribe a "Train-Low" session despite the marathon being a high-carb event?

Train-low sessions (low CHO availability) are used to enhance metabolic flexibility and upregulate fat oxidation enzymes and mitochondrial biogenesis. This improves the athlete's ability to spare glycogen at sub-maximal intensities, providing a broader aerobic base before the high-intensity specificity of the Build Phase begins.

Key Takeaways

  • Periodization is Mandatory: Nutritional needs shift from metabolic flexibility in the Base phase to maximum fueling in the Build phase.
  • Train the Gut: Absorption of 90-120g CHO/hr is a trainable physiological adaptation that requires 6-10 weeks of consistent practice.
  • Dual-Source Carb: Use a blend of glucose/fructose to bypass the 60g/hr SGLT1 saturation limit.
  • Scale with Volume: Use "Results Tracking" to adjust carbohydrate intake (5g/kg to 12g/kg) based on weekly mileage fluctuations.
  • Super-Compensation: 1-day and 3-day carb loading protocols are both effective, but the choice should depend on the athlete's GI tolerance and logistical preferences.

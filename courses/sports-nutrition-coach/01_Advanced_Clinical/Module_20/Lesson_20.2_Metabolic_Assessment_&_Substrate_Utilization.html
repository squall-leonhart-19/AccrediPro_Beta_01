1. The Science of Indirect Calorimetry

In Lesson 1, we explored anthropometry and the structural side of the athlete. Now, we transition to the functional side of performance: how the body actually utilizes energy. While predictive equations (like Harris-Benedict or Cunningham) are useful, they can carry an error margin of 10-20% in elite athletic populations.

Indirect Calorimetry (IC) is the gold standard for measuring Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR) in a clinical or high-performance setting. It functions on the principle that all energy metabolism in the body ultimately depends on oxygen (O2) consumption and carbon dioxide (CO2) production. By measuring the volume of these gases, we can calculate the heat produced by the body using the Weir Equation:

2. Interpreting RMR and RER

When you receive an IC report, two primary metrics dictate your nutritional strategy: the Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR) and the Respiratory Exchange Ratio (RER).

The Respiratory Exchange Ratio (RER)

RER is the ratio of VCO2 produced to VO2 consumed. Because different macronutrients require different amounts of oxygen for complete oxidation, the RER tells us exactly what "fuel" the athlete is burning at rest or during exercise.

RER Value % Fat Oxidation % CHO Oxidation Physiological State
0.70 100% 0% Pure Lipid Metabolism (Fasting/Keto-adapted)
0.85 ~50% ~50% Mixed Substrate Utilization (Typical)
1.00 0% 100% Pure Carbohydrate Oxidation (High Intensity)
>1.10 0% 100%+ Maximal Effort / Anaerobic Compensation

An athlete with an abnormally high resting RER (e.g., 0.92) while in a fasted state may exhibit metabolic inflexibility, suggesting they are over-reliant on glucose even when fat should be the primary fuel. This has massive implications for glycogen sparing in endurance sports.

3. The Crossover Point: The 'E' in PERFORM‚Ñ¢

The Crossover Concept, first popularized by Brooks and Mercier (1994), describes the exercise intensity at which the energy derived from carbohydrates exceeds that derived from lipids. As intensity increases, the recruitment of fast-twitch fibers and increased sympathetic nervous system activity drive a shift toward glycolysis.

In assessment, we look for the FatMax‚Äîthe point of maximal fat oxidation. In elite endurance athletes, this often occurs at 60-75% of VO2 max. In untrained or metabolically compromised individuals, it may occur as low as 35-45%.

Case Study 1: The "Bonking" Marathoner

Client: Marcus, 34 | Sport: Marathon (PB 3:15)

Symptoms: Severe fatigue ("hitting the wall") at mile 18 despite 60g CHO/hr intake.

Assessment: Metabolic testing revealed a crossover point at 52% of VO2 max. His race pace required 78% of VO2 max. At race intensity, his RER was 0.96, meaning he was burning almost exclusively glucose.

Intervention: Periodized nutrition (Module 1) to improve fat oxidation via "Train Low" sessions, coupled with a shift to 90g CHO/hr during the race to match his measured burn rate.

Outcome: Marcus reduced his RER at race pace to 0.89, sparing glycogen and finishing his next marathon in 3:02 without bonking.

4. Assessing Metabolic Flexibility

Metabolic Flexibility is the capacity to switch between fuel sources based on availability and demand. A metabolically flexible athlete can efficiently oxidize fats during low-intensity training (sparing glycogen) and rapidly switch to carbohydrate oxidation during high-intensity bursts.

How to Measure:

  • Resting RER: Should be low (0.7-0.8) in a fasted state.
  • Insulin Sensitivity: High insulin sensitivity correlates with better fuel switching.
  • Post-Prandial RER: The ability for RER to rise significantly after a high-carb meal (showing the body can use the fuel provided).

A study by San-Mill√°n et al. (2018) found that professional cyclists had a fat oxidation rate nearly 3x higher than individuals with metabolic syndrome, illustrating that metabolic flexibility is both a marker of health and a prerequisite for elite performance.

5. Lactate Threshold and CHO Requirements

While IC measures gas exchange, Lactate Threshold (LT) testing measures the metabolic byproduct of glycolysis. There is a strong correlation between the Lactate Threshold 1 (LT1) and the Crossover Point.

By using lactate data, we can calculate Exogenous Carbohydrate Needs. If an athlete is training above their LT1, their glycogen depletion rate increases exponentially. A 2022 study involving ultra-endurance runners (n=124) showed that those who matched their CHO intake to their measured oxidation rates (via LT and RER data) had 22% lower markers of muscle damage post-race.

The Formula for Intra-Workout Precision:

If measured CHO oxidation at intensity "X" is 120g/hour, and the athlete's maximum absorption rate is 90g/hour, the "net deficit" is 30g/hour. This allows the coach to predict exactly when glycogen depletion will occur and adjust the P (Periodization) of the pre-workout meal accordingly.

6. Correcting for TEF and NEAT

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is more than just RMR + Exercise. For high-volume athletes, Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT) and the Thermic Effect of Food (TEF) can fluctuate wildly.

  • TEF: Usually 10% of TDEE, but in high-protein athletic diets (e.g., 2.5g/kg), this can rise to 15%.
  • NEAT: In "overreaching" phases, athletes often subconsciously reduce NEAT (fidgeting less, sitting more) to conserve energy. This can lead to a "metabolic gap" where TDEE is lower than calculated.

RMR 60-70% 50-60% (due to high EEE) Measure via IC

Check Your Understanding

An athlete performs an IC test and shows a resting RER of 0.88 while fasted. What does this suggest about their metabolic state?

This suggests metabolic inflexibility or poor fat oxidation. The athlete is relying heavily on carbohydrates even at rest, which may lead to premature glycogen depletion during endurance exercise.

At what RER value does an athlete reach the theoretical 'Crossover Point' where they are burning 50% fat and 50% carbohydrate?

An RER of approximately 0.85 indicates a roughly 50/50 split of fat and carbohydrate oxidation.

Key Takeaways

  • Indirect Calorimetry is the gold standard for determining RMR and substrate utilization, eliminating the 10-20% error of predictive equations.
  • RER provides a real-time snapshot of fuel use; values closer to 0.7 indicate fat use, while 1.0 indicates pure carbohydrate use.
  • The Crossover Point is a trainable metric; moving it to a higher intensity allows for better glycogen sparing and performance.
  • Metabolic Flexibility is essential for the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ Method, ensuring the athlete can utilize the 'E' (Energy Systems) most appropriate for the task.
  • Always adjust for TEF and NEAT, especially in athletes with high protein intakes or those in heavy training cycles where "lazy" NEAT may occur.

Key Assessment Terminology

Urine Specific Gravity (USG)

The ratio of the density of urine to the density of water, indicating the concentration of solutes and overall hydration status.

Sweat Sodium Concentration ([Na+])

The amount of sodium (mg) lost per liter of sweat; highly variable based on genetics and heat acclimatization.

Digital Refractometry

A diagnostic tool that uses light refraction to measure the concentration of a liquid, providing more accuracy than visual urine color charts.

Hyponatremia

A condition where blood sodium levels fall dangerously low (<135 mmol/L), often due to over-drinking plain water during prolonged effort.

2. Field-Based Sweat Rate Testing

The gold standard for the field practitioner is the Body Mass Change Protocol. This assessment determines the volume of fluid an athlete requires to maintain performance and avoid a body mass loss of >2%, which is associated with increased cardiovascular strain and perceived exertion.

The Protocol: Pre/Post-Exercise Weighing

To conduct a valid test, the practitioner must follow these steps precisely:

  1. Pre-Exercise: Have the athlete void their bladder, then weigh them in minimal clothing (nude is most accurate, but dry underwear is standard). Record weight to the nearest 0.1kg.
  2. The Session: Record the exact duration of exercise (minutes) and the volume of all fluids consumed during the session (mL).
  3. Environmental Data: Record temperature and humidity (Heat Index).
  4. Post-Exercise: Towel off all sweat immediately. Weigh again in the same dry clothing/underwear.
  5. Urine Output: If the athlete voids during the session, this volume must be subtracted from the calculation (often estimated if not measured).
The Calculation Formula

Sweat Loss (L) = [Pre-weight (kg) - Post-weight (kg)] + Fluid Intake (L) - Urine Output (L)
Sweat Rate (L/hr) = Sweat Loss (L) / Duration (hrs)

3. Electrolyte Profiling & Sodium Patches

Knowing how much an athlete sweats is only half the battle. We must also know what is in that sweat. Sodium is the primary electrolyte lost in sweat, with concentrations ranging from 300mg/L to over 2,000mg/L.

Practitioners utilize Sweat Chloride Patches (e.g., Gatorade Gx, hDrop, or clinical-grade patches) to capture regional sweat. While regional sweat (usually from the forearm or scapula) is more concentrated than whole-body sweat, correction equations (Baker et al., 2017) allow us to estimate total sodium loss.

4. Digital Refractometry & USG

While sweat rate tells us about the *activity*, Urine Specific Gravity (USG) tells us about the *state* of the athlete upon arrival. In the PERFORM Method‚Ñ¢, we use USG to determine "Hydration Readiness."

Visual urine color charts are notoriously unreliable due to the influence of riboflavin (B2) supplements, certain foods (beets), and lighting. Digital refractometry measures the refractive index of urine, providing a numerical value that correlates highly with plasma osmolality.

USG Thresholds for Athletes
  • < 1.010: Well Hydrated (Potentially over-hydrated if < 1.005)
  • 1.010 - 1.020: Euhydrated (Optimal range for start of session)
  • 1.021 - 1.030: Hypohydrated (Action required: 500-750mL fluid bolus)
  • > 1.030: Significantly Dehydrated (High risk of heat illness; consider modifying intensity)

5. The 'Salty Sweater' & Cramping

For decades, Exercise-Associated Muscle Cramps (EAMC) were attributed solely to electrolyte depletion. Contemporary research (Schwellnus et al.) suggests a "Neuromuscular Theory" (muscle fatigue). However, for the Salty Sweater, the electrolyte theory remains clinically relevant.

A "Salty Sweater" is identified by:

  1. Visible white salt streaks on skin or dark clothing after exercise.
  2. Stinging eyes from sweat.
  3. Sweat that tastes notably "briny."
  4. High sodium concentration (>1,200 mg/L) confirmed via patch testing.

In these individuals, large sodium deficits can lead to a contraction of the interstitial fluid compartment, potentially sensitizing the muscle spindle and triggering cramps. For these athletes, pre-loading with high-sodium solutions (1,500mg/L) 2 hours before exercise is a critical Level 2 intervention.

Check Your Understanding

1. An athlete weighs 80.0kg before a 60-minute run and 78.5kg after. They drank 500mL of water during the run. What is their hourly sweat rate?

Sweat Rate = (80.0 - 78.5) + 0.5 = 2.0 Liters/hour. This is a very high sweat rate, requiring aggressive replacement.

2. Why is a digital refractometer preferred over a urine color chart for elite athletes?

Refractometry provides an objective measure of solute concentration (density) that isn't skewed by vitamins (B-complex), diet, or subjective visual interpretation.

Key Takeaways

  • Individual Variability: Sweat rates and sodium concentrations can vary by 5-10x between athletes; generic plans are obsolete for L2 practitioners.
  • The 2% Rule: Aim to limit body mass loss to <2% to preserve cognitive and physical performance.
  • Sodium Concentration: High-sodium sweaters require specific electrolyte replacement (up to 1,500mg/L) to prevent hyponatremia and cramping.
  • USG Readiness: Use digital refractometry to ensure athletes are euhydrated (USG 1.010-1.020) before high-intensity sessions.
  • Dynamic Planning: Always adjust hydration protocols based on the Heat Index and the specific Energy System (E) being utilized.

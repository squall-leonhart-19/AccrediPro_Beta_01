1. Measuring and Manipulating Maximal Fat Oxidation (MFO)

In elite sport, particularly ultra-endurance events (6+ hours), the rate-limiting factor is rarely aerobic capacity (VO2max), but rather glycogen preservation. Substrate utilization refers to the relative contribution of fats and carbohydrates to total energy expenditure. Metabolic flexibility is the capacity of the mitochondria to switch seamlessly between these fuels based on intensity and availability.

A 2022 study of elite cyclists found that MFO rates varied from 0.45g/min to 1.62g/min, demonstrating that even at the same relative intensity, one athlete may deplete glycogen three times faster than another. We define MFO as the highest rate of fat oxidation achieved during a graded exercise test, typically occurring at 45% to 65% of VO2max.

The FatMax Zone: This is the exercise intensity (expressed as % VO2max or Heart Rate) where MFO occurs. In the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ Method, we prioritize identifying this zone to prescribe "Low-Glycemic Training" (LGT) sessions that upregulate mitochondrial enzymes like 3-hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase (HAD).

2. Exogenous Ketones: The 'Fourth Fuel'

While endogenous ketosis requires days of carbohydrate restriction, exogenous ketone esters (KE) can elevate blood Beta-hydroxybutyrate (Œ≤HB) to 3.0‚Äì5.0 mmol/L within 30 minutes, even in the presence of high glycogen stores. This creates a unique metabolic state: high carbohydrate availability co-existing with high ketone availability.

In professional cycling (e.g., the Pro Peloton), ketones are utilized not just for energy, but as a signaling molecule. Research indicates that KE ingestion may:

  • Decrease blood lactate accumulation by ~20% during submaximal work.
  • Spare muscle glycogen by reducing the glycolytic rate during steady-state efforts.
  • Improve post-exercise resynthesis of glycogen when co-ingested with carbohydrates (an increase of ~50% in some trials).

3. Advanced Carbohydrate Loading Protocols

The traditional "Astrand" model involving a 3-day depletion phase is largely obsolete in elite circles due to the psychological stress and immune suppression it causes. Modern practitioners now choose between the 3-Day Non-Depletion and the 24-Hour Rapid Load.

Protocol Duration Carbohydrate Intake Glycogen Outcome Best For
Traditional (Sherman) 3-6 Days 7-10g/kg BW ~150-180 mmol/kg Marathon, Stage Racing
Rapid (Fairchild) 24 Hours 10-12g/kg BW ~190-210 mmol/kg Single-day events, Weight-class
Depletion-Based 7 Days Low then High ~200+ mmol/kg Bodybuilding (Peak Week)

The Rapid Loading Model (Goforth et al.) utilizes a short bout of high-intensity exercise (e.g., 150 seconds at 130% VO2max) followed immediately by massive carbohydrate ingestion. This stimulates GLUT-4 translocation and non-insulin-dependent glucose uptake, saturating muscle glycogen in a fraction of the time.

Case Study: The "Wall" Breaker

Metabolic Efficiency in Ironman Triathlon

JD

James D., Age 34

Elite Amateur Triathlete | Frequent "bonking" at mile 18 of the run.

Assessment: Metabolic testing revealed James had an MFO of only 0.38g/min at race pace, meaning he was burning ~2.8g of glucose per minute. At this rate, even with 90g/hr exogenous fuel, he would deplete his 500g glycogen store in less than 3 hours.

Intervention: 12 weeks of Metabolic Efficiency Training (MET). We removed high-sugar processed snacks and implemented twice-weekly "Fast-Low" sessions (90 min at 60% VO2max, fasted). We introduced Ketone Esters during the bike-to-run transition.

Outcome: James' MFO increased to 0.82g/min. He reduced his carbohydrate dependence by 35% at race intensity. He finished his next Ironman with a 12-minute PB and no gastrointestinal distress or "bonking."

4. Metabolic Efficiency Training (MET)

Metabolic Efficiency Training, a concept popularized by Bob Seebohar, focuses on the "Crossover Point"‚Äîthe intensity where the body shifts from using primarily fat to primarily carbohydrates. For many athletes, this crossover occurs too early (low intensity), leading to early fatigue.

To improve MET within the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ Method, we manipulate the 'E' (Energy Systems) through:

  1. Periodized Carbohydrate Availability: "Sleep Low, Train High" models.
  2. Mitochondrial Biogenesis: High-volume, low-intensity base training to increase mitochondrial density.
  3. Nutritional Periodization: Reducing insulin spikes during the base phase to keep hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL) active.

5. Energy Systems ('E') in High-Intensity Transitions

In intermittent sports like soccer or rugby, substrate utilization is chaotic. An athlete may sprint (ATP-PCr), jog (Oxidative), and then engage in a high-intensity bout (Glycolytic) within 60 seconds. Metabolic flexibility here is defined by how quickly an athlete can clear lactate and return to fat oxidation during low-intensity "micro-recoveries."

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=1,102) showed that athletes with higher aerobic efficiency (higher MFO) recovered their phosphocreatine (PCr) stores 15-20% faster than those with poor metabolic efficiency, despite equal anaerobic power.

Check Your Understanding

1. At what approximate intensity (% of VO2max) does Maximal Fat Oxidation (MFO) typically occur in well-trained athletes?

MFO typically occurs between 45% and 65% of VO2max. In elite endurance athletes, this point (FatMax) can be shifted to higher intensities (up to 75% VO2max) through Metabolic Efficiency Training.

2. Why are exogenous ketone esters considered a "fourth fuel" and how do they impact glycogen?

They are a "fourth fuel" because they provide energy (ketones) without requiring carbohydrate restriction. They spare glycogen by providing an alternative substrate for the mitochondria and inhibiting the glycolytic enzyme phosphofructokinase (PFK).

Key Takeaways

  • MFO is Trainable: High fat oxidation rates (1.0g/min+) are a hallmark of elite endurance performance and can be developed through MET.
  • Ketones are Strategic: Use Ketone Esters (KE) for glycogen sparing during long efforts or to enhance recovery/cognition in stage races.
  • Loading has Evolved: The 24-hour rapid loading protocol is highly effective and minimizes the side effects of traditional multi-day depletion.
  • The Crossover Point: Shifting the crossover point to the right (higher intensity) is the primary goal of the 'E' (Energy Systems) pillar in the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ Method.

In 2014, and updated in 2018 and 2023, the International Olympic Committee (IOC) introduced the term Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). This framework acknowledges that low energy availability affects nearly every system in the bodyâ€”including immunity, protein synthesis, and cardiovascular healthâ€”and that it affects both male and female athletes.

2. The Pathophysiology of Low Energy Availability (LEA)

At the core of RED-S is Low Energy Availability (LEA). This is not necessarily synonymous with an eating disorder; it is often the result of "unintentional under-fueling" where an athlete's energy intake (EI) fails to match their exercise energy expenditure (EEE), leaving insufficient energy for basic physiological functions.

The calculation for Energy Availability (EA) is:

EA = (Energy Intake - Exercise Energy Expenditure) / Fat-Free Mass (FFM)
EA Status Value (kcal/kg FFM/day) Physiological Impact
Optimal ≥ 45 Normal hormonal function; optimal recovery and growth.
Sub-Clinical LEA 30 - 45 Weight maintenance possible, but potential subtle hormonal shifts.
Clinical LEA < 30 Suppression of HPG axis, reduced T3, impaired bone turnover.

3. Hormonal Consequences and Bone Health

When EA drops below the 30 kcal/kg FFM threshold, the body enters a state of "metabolic conservation." The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) axis is suppressed to save energy. In women, this leads to a decrease in GnRH pulsatility, resulting in low Luteinizing Hormone (LH) and subsequently low estrogen.

The Bone Mineral Density (BMD) Trap

Bone is a metabolically active tissue. Estrogen acts as a brake on bone resorption (breakdown). Without it, bone resorption outpaces bone formation. Furthermore, LEA leads to low levels of IGF-1, a critical hormone for bone building. This dual hit results in rapidly declining BMD and a significantly increased risk of stress reactions and fractures.

Client: Sarah, 22 | Sport: NCAA Cross Country | Symptom: Recurrent tibial stress fractures, secondary amenorrhea (8 months).

Sarah transitioned to a "plant-based, whole-food" diet to improve performance. While her volume increased to 60 miles/week, her caloric density decreased. Assessment showed an EA of 24 kcal/kg FFM.

Intervention: Using the PERFORM Methodâ„¢, we focused on "P" (Periodize) to increase energy density during high-volume blocks. We added liquid calories (smoothies) and increased dietary fats to 30% of total intake.

4. Clinical Intervention Protocols: The Nutritional Road to Recovery

The primary goal of nutritional intervention in RED-S is to restore energy balance. This requires a multidisciplinary approach, but the Sports Nutrition Coach leads the fueling strategy. Restoration of the menstrual cycle (eumenorrhea) is the "Gold Standard" metric for recovery in females.

The Refuel Protocol (PERFORMâ„¢ Integration)
  • Energy Surplus: Increase daily intake by 300â€“600 kcal above maintenance.
  • Weight Targets: Aim for a 0.5kg (1.1lb) gain per week until a healthy "set point" is reached (often 2-3kg above the weight at which symptoms started).
  • Macro Distribution: Ensure Carbohydrate availability is >5g/kg FFM to support the HPG axis and thyroid function (T3).
  • Micronutrient Support: Calcium (1300-1500mg) and Vitamin D (2000-4000 IU) are non-negotiable for bone repair.

5. The Male Athlete Perspective

It is a common misconception that RED-S only affects women. In men, LEA manifests as Hypogonadotropic Hypogonadismâ€”a clinical drop in testosterone levels. This leads to reduced libido, poor morning erections, increased body fat despite training, and decreased power output.

A 2023 study of elite male cyclists found that those in a chronic energy deficit had 25% lower testosterone levels and significantly lower power-to-weight ratios compared to fueled peers, despite identical training volumes.

Case Study 2: The Low-Carb Cyclist
Client: Mark, 34 | Sport: Competitive Cycling | Symptom: Chronic fatigue, low power, loss of morning erections.

Mark followed a strict ketogenic diet while training 15 hours/week. Bloodwork showed Total Testosterone at 210 ng/dL (Clinical Low). EA was calculated at 28 kcal/kg FFM.

Intervention: Reintroduced carbohydrates using the E (Energy Systems) framework. Targeted 6g/kg CHO on training days. Reduced fasted training sessions.

Outcome: Testosterone returned to 480 ng/dL within 12 weeks. Power at FTP increased by 18 watts as glycogen availability improved.

6. Screening and Collaborative Care

As a Sports Nutrition Coach, you are often the first line of defense. You must use validated screening tools rather than relying on intuition. The LEAF-Q (Low Energy Availability in Females Questionnaire) and the RED-S CAT (Clinical Assessment Tool) are the industry standards.

The Collaborative Care Team

RED-S is a complex clinical condition. Your role within the Performance Team (established in Module 0) is critical. You must collaborate with:

  • Physician: To order bloodwork (T3, Ferritin, Estradiol/Testosterone) and DXA scans.
  • Psychologist: To address potential disordered eating patterns or exercise addiction.
  • Coach/Trainer: To adjust training load (the "E" in PERFORM) during the recovery phase.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the specific mathematical threshold of Energy Availability (EA) below which physiological systems begin to shut down?

The threshold is 30 kcal/kg of Fat-Free Mass (FFM) per day. Below this level, the body prioritizes survival over non-essential functions like reproduction and bone building.

2. Why is "clean eating" often a risk factor for RED-S in endurance athletes?

"Clean" foods (vegetables, lean proteins, whole grains) are often high in fiber and volume but low in caloric density. Athletes may feel full before they have met the high energy demands of their sport, leading to unintentional LEA.

Key Takeaways

  • RED-S is Gender-Neutral: While the Triad is female-specific, RED-S affects all athletes and impacts metabolic rate, immunity, and mental health.
  • EA vs. Calories: Energy Availability must be calculated relative to Fat-Free Mass (FFM), not just total body weight.
  • Hormonal Markers: Low T3 (Thyroid) is often the earliest blood marker of LEA, even before sex hormones drop.
  • Refueling is the Cure: Most physiological impairments from RED-S are reversible with 6-12 months of consistent energy surplus and weight restoration.
  • Bone Health is Urgent: Loss of BMD in young athletes can be permanent if not addressed quickly, leading to early-onset osteopenia.

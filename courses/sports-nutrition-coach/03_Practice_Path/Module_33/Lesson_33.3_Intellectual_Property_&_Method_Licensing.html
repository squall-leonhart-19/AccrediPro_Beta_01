Key Legal Terms

Intellectual Property (IP)

Intangible creations of the mind, such as coaching protocols, brand names, and proprietary data algorithms.

Work for Hire

A legal doctrine where IP created by an employee or contractor is owned by the employer/hiring entity.

License Agreement

A contract allowing another party to use your IP in exchange for a fee or royalty while you retain ownership.

2. Protecting the Brand: Trademarks & Copyright

A common mistake among sports nutritionists is confusing Trademarks with Copyrights. A Trademark protects your "identity" in the marketplace, while Copyright protects the "expression" of your ideas.

    3. Licensing Your Methodology

    Licensing is the primary mechanism for scaling. Instead of coaching 100 athletes yourself, you license your PERFORM‚Ñ¢ protocols to a CrossFit box or a collegiate athletic department. They pay you for the right to use your systems, but you remain the owner.

    Key Elements of a Coaching License:

    1. Scope of Use: Can the licensee use it for 10 athletes or 1,000?
    2. Territory: Is this license exclusive to a specific city or state?
    3. Quality Control: As the licensor, you must have the right to audit their coaching to ensure they aren't diluting the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ brand.
    4. Term & Termination: What happens if they stop paying or misuse the protocols?

    4. NDAs & Protecting the 'Mental Edge'

    In Module 7 and 8, we discussed the Mental Edge‚Äîthe neurobiology of performance. These protocols often involve specific supplement ratios or cognitive timing strategies that are easily "stolen" if not protected by a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA).

    When working with elite athletes or junior practitioners, your NDA should cover:

    • Definition of Confidential Information: Be specific. Include "all nutritional formulas, timing protocols, and biofeedback data."
    • Non-Compete vs. Non-Solicitation: Ensure that junior coaches you train cannot leave and immediately open a competing practice using your PERFORM‚Ñ¢ templates within a 10-mile radius.
    • The "Survival" Clause: The obligation to keep secrets must survive even after the coaching relationship ends (typically for 2-5 years).

    5. Navigating 'Work for Hire' in Professional Sports

    If you are hired by a professional team (NFL, EPL, NBA), pay close attention to the IP Ownership Clause. Most professional teams use "Work for Hire" language, meaning any protocol you develop while on their payroll belongs to the team, not you.

    Negotiation Strategy

    If you bring the PERFORM Athletic Method‚Ñ¢ into a team environment, ensure your contract includes a "Pre-Existing IP" carve-out. This states that the core framework belongs to YOU, and only the specific athlete data generated belongs to the TEAM.

    6. Digital Enforcement & DMCA

    In the digital age, your PDFs and videos can be shared globally in seconds. The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) is your primary tool for enforcement. If you find your PERFORM‚Ñ¢ manuals on a pirated site or a competitor's blog, you can issue a "Takedown Notice" to the hosting provider.

    Steps for International Protection:

    1. Watermarking: Every PDF should have a dynamic watermark with the client's name or email to discourage sharing.
    2. Metadata Tagging: Embed copyright information in the metadata of your images and videos.
    3. WIPO Treaties: Most countries recognize IP rights through the World Intellectual Property Organization. If your IP is stolen in Europe, you have legal standing under the Berne Convention.

    Check Your Understanding

    Test your knowledge of IP and licensing.

    1. You develop a unique "3 Rs" recovery spreadsheet for a client. Which legal protection automatically applies the moment you save the file?

    Copyright. In most jurisdictions, copyright protection is automatic upon the creation of an original work in a "tangible medium" (like a digital file). Registration is only required for filing a lawsuit.

    2. What is the main difference between "Work for Hire" and an "Independent Contractor" agreement regarding IP ownership?

    Under Work for Hire, the employer is the legal author and owner of the IP. Under an Independent Contractor agreement, the creator usually retains ownership UNLESS the contract explicitly transfers those rights to the client.

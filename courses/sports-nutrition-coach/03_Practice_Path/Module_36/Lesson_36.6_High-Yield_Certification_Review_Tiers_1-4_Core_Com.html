1. Bioenergetics & Macronutrient Review (E)

The foundation of the PERFORM™ Method begins with Energy Systems (E). As a certified coach, you must transcend basic calorie counting and master metabolic flux—the rate at which substrates (carbohydrates, fats, and phosphagens) flow through metabolic pathways to regenerate ATP.

Recall the Crossover Concept: At low intensities (approx. < 45% VO2 max), lipids are the primary fuel source. As intensity increases, the "crossover" occurs where carbohydrate oxidation becomes dominant. A key competency is identifying the Glycolytic Flux required for intermittent sports (e.g., soccer, MMA) versus the steady-state oxidative requirements of ultra-endurance.

2. Periodization Strategies (P)

Periodization (P) is the strategic manipulation of nutrition to match the training load. You must be able to design a Macrocycle (annual plan) that accounts for the General Preparatory Phase (GPP), Specific Preparatory Phase (SPP), and the Competition Phase.

Key Periodization Models:

  • Linear Periodization: Gradual increase in carbohydrate availability as training volume/intensity increases.
  • Undulating (Daily) Periodization: Matching high-carb days with high-intensity training (HIT) and low-carb days with recovery or aerobic base building ("Train Low, Compete High").
  • Block Periodization: Concentrating on a specific metabolic adaptation (e.g., a 4-week "fat-adaptation" block or a "hypertrophy" block).
Case Study: Elite Marathoner (Male, 32)

Presenting Scenario: Client "David" is entering a 12-week marathon prep. His mileage is increasing from 40km/week to 90km/week.

Intervention: Implemented Undulating Nutritional Periodization. On "Threshold Run" days, carb intake was set at 8-10g/kg. On "Recovery" days, intake dropped to 3-4g/kg to promote mitochondrial biogenesis via the AMPK pathway.

Outcome: David achieved a 4-minute PR, maintaining a steady blood glucose of >4.5 mmol/L throughout the race without GI distress.

3. Functional Foods (F) & Ergogenic Aids

Functional Foods (F) represent the "fine-tuning" of the PERFORM™ method. Mastery involves understanding the pharmacokinetics of whole-food bioactives. For certification, focus on the "Big Three" evidence-based aids:

  1. Nitrates (Beetroot Juice): Enhances nitric oxide (NO) bioavailability, reducing the oxygen cost of exercise. Dosage: 400-800mg nitrates 2-3 hours pre-event.
  2. Tart Cherry Juice (Anthocyanins): Accelerates recovery of muscle function and reduces systemic inflammation. Protocol: 30ml concentrate twice daily for 7 days post-heavy load.
  3. Caffeine: Antagonizes adenosine receptors to reduce RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion). Dosage: 3-6mg/kg body weight.

4. Hydration & Electrolyte (O) Mastery

Optimizing Hydration (O) requires more than telling a client to "drink when thirsty." In elite performance, thirst is a lagging indicator. You must be able to calculate Sweat Rate and determine the Sodium Replacement Ratio.

Certification Calculation Challenge

An athlete weighs 80kg before a 60-minute training session and 78.5kg after. They consumed 500ml of water during the session and did not void. What is their hourly sweat rate?

Answer: 2.0 Liters per hour.
Calculation: (Pre-weight 80kg - Post-weight 78.5kg) = 1.5kg loss.
Add fluid consumed: 1.5kg + 0.5kg (500ml) = 2.0kg total loss.
Since it was a 1-hour session, the sweat rate is 2.0 L/hr.

5. Results Tracking (R) & Mental Edge (M)

Tier 3 competencies involve the synthesis of biofeedback and cognitive performance. Results Tracking (R) focuses on body composition (DXA, skinfolds) but must also include subjective metrics like the Recovery-Stress Questionnaire for Athletes (RESTQ-Sport).

The Mental Edge (M) focuses on the neurobiology of performance. Ensure you understand the role of Tyrosine as a precursor to dopamine and Alpha-GPC for acetylcholine synthesis, especially in sports requiring high cognitive precision (e.g., motor racing, gymnastics).

6. L4: Scaling, Marketing & Ethics

In the final tier of the Sports Nutrition Coach certification, we transition from the science of the athlete to the business of the practitioner. Professionalism and ethics are paramount.

The "Triple Threat" of Business Scaling
  • Retention (LTV): It is 5-7x cheaper to keep a current athlete than to acquire a new one. Focus on "Outcome-Based Coaching" to prove value.
  • Authority Marketing: Use evidence-based content (white papers, webinars) to establish yourself as an expert, not just an influencer.
  • Ethical Boundaries: Always refer out for clinical issues (e.g., eating disorders, renal failure) as per the AccrediPro Standard.
Case Study: Scaling a Nutrition Practice

Practitioner: Sarah, L4 Coach.

Problem: Sarah was trading time for money, capped at 15 1-on-1 clients.

Intervention: Sarah launched a Hybrid Group Program focused on "Fueling for CrossFit." She used a tiered pricing model: Tier 1 (Self-paced), Tier 2 (Group Coaching), and Tier 3 (VIP 1-on-1).

Outcome: Sarah increased her monthly recurring revenue (MRR) by 240% while reducing her direct coaching hours by 10 hours per week.

Final Ethics Check

An athlete asks you to design a cycle of SARMs (Selective Androgen Receptor Modulators) to help them recover from a meniscus tear. What is the correct response according to the PERFORM™ Code of Ethics?

Correct Response: Refuse the request and explain that prescribing or recommending banned substances (WADA prohibited list) is outside your scope of practice and a violation of the AccrediPro ethics standard. Refer the athlete to a sports medicine physician for legitimate rehabilitation protocols.

Key Takeaways for Certification

  • Metabolic Flux: Fuel selection is determined by the intersection of intensity (VO2 max) and substrate availability.
  • Periodization: Nutrition must mirror the micro, meso, and macrocycles of training to prevent RED-S and overtraining.
  • Hydration Math: Sweat rate = [Weight Change + Fluid Intake - Urine] / Time.
  • Scope of Practice: Coaches optimize performance; physicians and RDs treat clinical pathology.
  • Business Growth: Scaling requires moving from "Time-for-Money" to "Value-Based" hybrid models.

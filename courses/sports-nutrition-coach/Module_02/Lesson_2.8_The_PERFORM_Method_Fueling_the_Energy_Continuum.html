1. Integrating the 'E' in PERFORM‚Ñ¢

Throughout Module 2, we have dissected the ATP-PCr, Glycolytic, and Oxidative systems. However, in elite sports nutrition, these systems never operate in isolation. The PERFORM Method‚Ñ¢ views energy systems as a "continuum"‚Äîa seamless handoff of metabolic responsibility that requires specific substrate availability to prevent performance decay.

The "E" (Energy Systems) in our framework focuses on Substrate Matching. If an athlete's training session is dominated by glycolytic flux (e.g., 400m repeats or high-intensity interval training), but their nutritional intake is high-fat/low-carb, we create a "metabolic mismatch." This mismatch leads to the "bioenergetic failure" we discussed in Lesson 7, manifesting as increased RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) and decreased power output.

2. Practical Math: Calculating Carbohydrate Requirements

As an AccrediPro Sports Nutrition Coach, your recommendations must move beyond "eat more carbs." We use a volumetric approach to carbohydrate periodization. A 2022 consensus statement (Thomas et al.) emphasizes that carbohydrate needs are a moving target based on the daily "E" demand.

The PERFORM Daily Calculation

To calculate the daily target, we categorize the training microcycle (from Module 1) into intensity tiers:

Activity Level Dominant Energy System Target CHO (g/kg BW)
Low Intensity (Skill-based) Oxidative (Fat dominant) 3 - 5 g/kg
Moderate (~1 hr/day) Mixed (Glycolytic/Oxidative) 5 - 7 g/kg
High (1-3 hrs/day) Glycolytic (High flux) 6 - 10 g/kg
Extreme (>4 hrs/day) Oxidative / Glycolytic 8 - 12+ g/kg

For example, a 70kg soccer player on a high-intensity match day would require 490g to 700g of carbohydrates to maintain the glycolytic flux required for repeated sprinting.

3. Intra-Workout Fueling: Transporter Optimization

When exercise exceeds 60‚Äì90 minutes, endogenous glycogen stores begin to deplete. To sustain the energy continuum, we must provide exogenous carbohydrates. However, the bottleneck is not the muscle's ability to burn glucose, but the gut's ability to absorb it.

SGLT1 and GLUT5 Dynamics

Glucose is absorbed via the SGLT1 (Sodium-Glucose Linked Transporter 1). This transporter saturates at approximately 60 grams per hour. If you provide 100g of pure maltodextrin (glucose) per hour, 40g will sit in the gut, causing osmotic shifts and significant GI distress.

To bypass this "ceiling," we utilize the GLUT5 transporter, which is specific to fructose. By using a "Multiple Transportable Carbohydrate" (MTC) blend‚Äîtypically a 2:1 or 1:0.8 ratio of glucose to fructose‚Äîwe can increase total exogenous oxidation to 90‚Äì120g per hour.

4. Pre-Competition Priming: The Ultimate Pre-Game Meal

The goal of the pre-competition meal is to top off liver glycogen and ensure blood glucose stability as the athlete transitions from the Oxidative system at rest to the Glycolytic system at whistle-blow. Within the PERFORM Method‚Ñ¢, we use the "3-2-1 Priming Rule":

  • 3-4 Hours Before: High carbohydrate (1-4g/kg), moderate protein, low fiber, low fat. This allows for gastric emptying.
  • 2 Hours Before: Fluid-based topping (e.g., sports drink) to ensure hydration and top-tier glucose availability.
  • 1 Hour Before: Minimal "slow" carbs; focus on avoiding reactive hypoglycemia (the insulin spike that can cause a blood sugar crash right as the race starts).

5. Monitoring and Adjusting: Refining the Season

Bioenergetics are not static. As an athlete becomes more "metabolically flexible" (see Lesson 4), their crossover point shifts. You must monitor three key markers to refine the energy system fueling:

  1. Morning Body Mass: Acute drops (>2%) often indicate glycogen depletion (as glycogen holds 3-4g of water per gram).
  2. Heart Rate vs. Power/Pace: If heart rate is higher than usual for a standard power output, the athlete may be "running on fumes" (increased sympathetic drive to compensate for low substrate).
  3. The "Third Quarter" Slump: Subjective feedback on focus and explosive power during the latter half of sessions.

Check Your Understanding

Why is a 2:1 Glucose-to-Fructose ratio superior to pure glucose for an ironman triathlete?

Pure glucose is limited by the SGLT1 transporter saturation (approx. 60g/hr). Fructose uses the GLUT5 transporter, allowing the athlete to absorb up to 90-120g/hr total, providing more fuel for the oxidative and glycolytic systems simultaneously.

An athlete weighs 80kg and is entering a 4-hour "Extreme" training day. What is their minimum recommended daily carbohydrate intake?

Based on the 8-12g/kg recommendation for extreme volume, the athlete should consume at least 640g of carbohydrates (80kg x 8g/kg).

Key Takeaways

  • The energy continuum requires substrate matching‚Äîhigh-intensity glycolytic work demands high carbohydrate availability.
  • Exogenous carbohydrate oxidation is limited by gut transporters (SGLT1 and GLUT5), not muscle metabolic capacity.
  • Carbohydrate periodization should range from 3g/kg to 12g/kg depending on the daily training volume.
  • Pre-competition priming (the 3-2-1 rule) ensures the athlete enters the event with maximal glycogen and optimal blood glucose.
  • Continuous monitoring of body mass and RPE is essential to adjust fueling as the athlete's metabolic efficiency improves.

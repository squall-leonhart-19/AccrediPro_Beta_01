1. Defining the Threshold for Low Energy Availability (LEA)

In previous modules, we discussed the "E" in the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ Method‚ÄîEnergy Systems. However, the most critical factor for long-term health is not just how we use energy, but how much is available after the demands of exercise are met. Energy Availability (EA) is defined as the amount of dietary energy remaining for other physiological functions after accounting for exercise energy expenditure (EEE).

30 kcal/kg FFM/day The critical threshold below which physiological disruption occurs

Research consistently demonstrates that an EA of 45 kcal/kg of Fat-Free Mass (FFM) per day is optimal for energy balance in most athletes. When EA drops below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day, the body enters a state of Low Energy Availability (LEA). At this point, the body begins to prioritize immediate survival over "luxury" functions like reproduction, bone building, and protein synthesis.

2. Hormonal Cascades: The HPG Axis and Metabolic Gatekeepers

The body monitors energy status through a complex hormonal signaling network. The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) axis is the primary victim of chronic LEA. When energy is scarce, the hypothalamus reduces the pulsatile release of Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone (GnRH), which in turn lowers Luteinizing Hormone (LH) and Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH).

Leptin and T3: The Metabolic Thermostats

Two key hormones act as the primary sensors for energy availability:

  • Leptin: Produced by adipose tissue, leptin signals the brain about fat stores and current energy intake. In LEA, leptin levels plummet, signaling the hypothalamus to downregulate metabolic rate.
  • Triiodothyronine (T3): The active thyroid hormone. During caloric restriction, the body reduces the conversion of T4 to T3, effectively lowering the Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) to conserve energy. This is often referred to as "euthyroid sick syndrome" in extreme cases.

3. RED-S: Identifying Markers in Male and Female Athletes

While the "Female Athlete Triad" focused on disordered eating, amenorrhea, and osteoporosis, the 2023 IOC Consensus Statement emphasizes Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). This framework recognizes that LEA affects nearly every system in the body and occurs in both men and women.

4. Adaptive Thermogenesis: The "Starvation Response"

Weight loss plateaus are rarely a result of "broken" metabolisms; they are the result of Adaptive Thermogenesis. This is a disproportionate reduction in energy expenditure beyond what can be explained by the loss of body mass (FFM and Fat Mass).

A 2021 meta-analysis of 12 longitudinal studies found that BMR can drop by as much as 15-20% beyond predicted values during extreme dieting. This occurs through three primary mechanisms:

  1. Mitochondrial Efficiency: The mitochondria become "stingier," producing more ATP per unit of fuel, reducing heat dissipation.
  2. NEAT Suppression: The athlete subconsciously moves less (fidgeting, posture, general daily movement).
    3. TEF Reduction: The Thermic Effect of Food drops simply because less food is being processed.

5. Restoring Metabolic Rate: Structured Reverse Dieting

When an athlete has spent months in LEA, a sudden return to "maintenance" calories can lead to rapid, disproportionate fat gain because the metabolic machinery is still suppressed. This is where Reverse Dieting‚Äîthe systematic, incremental increase in caloric intake‚Äîbecomes essential.

The Reverse Dieting Protocol (The R-Phase)

  • The Initial Bump: Increase calories by 10-15% immediately to move out of the "danger zone" (< 30 kcal/kg FFM).
  • Weekly Titration: Add 2-5% to total daily calories (primarily from carbohydrates) based on biofeedback (sleep quality, temperature, libido).
  • The "Hold" Pattern: If weight increases by >1% of body mass in a week, hold calories steady for 7-14 days to allow metabolic adaptation to catch up.

Check Your Understanding

1. An athlete has 60kg of FFM and consumes 2,500 kcal while burning 1,000 kcal during training. What is their Energy Availability (EA)? Is it considered LEA?

EA = (Energy Intake - Exercise Energy Expenditure) / FFM. EA = (2,500 - 1,000) / 60 = 25 kcal/kg FFM. Yes, this is considered Low Energy Availability (LEA) as it is below the 30 kcal/kg FFM threshold.

2. Why does T3 drop during a weight loss plateau?

The body senses low energy (via low leptin) and downregulates the conversion of T4 to the active T3 hormone to reduce BMR and conserve energy for vital survival functions.

Key Takeaways

  • The 30 kcal Threshold: Physiological health is compromised when EA drops below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day.
  • RED-S is Universal: It affects both males (low testosterone, poor bone health) and females (amenorrhea).
  • Adaptation is Survival: Metabolic slowing is a protective mechanism, not a "broken" system.
  • Reverse Dieting: Incremental increases in calories allow for the restoration of T3, leptin, and sex hormones while minimizing fat regain.
  • Periodize Recovery: The "R" in PERFORM‚Ñ¢ requires intentional phases of high energy availability to maintain long-term athletic longevity.

The Pathophysiology of Exercise-Associated Hyponatremia (EAH)

Exercise-Associated Hyponatremia (EAH) is defined as a serum, plasma, or whole-blood sodium concentration below the normal reference range (usually <135 mmol/L) occurring during or within 24 hours of physical activity. It is primarily a condition of dilutional hyponatremia.

While sweat contains sodium, EAH is rarely caused by "salty sweating" alone. Instead, it is driven by two converging factors:

  1. Excessive Fluid Intake: Consumption of water or hypotonic sports drinks in volumes exceeding sweat and urinary losses.
  2. Inappropriate ADH Secretion: During exercise, the body often fails to suppress Antidiuretic Hormone (arginine vasopressin) despite falling osmolality. Factors like pain, nausea, and intense exertion trigger ADH, which prevents the kidneys from excreting excess water.

Identifying High-Risk Athletes

As a Sports Nutrition Coach, you must recognize that EAH does not strike all athletes equally. Historically, it was viewed as a rare occurrence, but data from major marathons and triathlons suggest it is a significant clinical threat for specific profiles.

The "Slow and Steady" Participant

Elite runners rarely develop EAH because their high intensity limits their ability to ingest large fluid volumes. The highest risk is found in back-of-the-pack participants who are on the course for 5â€“18 hours. These individuals have more "opportunity time" to over-drink and lower sweat rates compared to elites.

Low Body Weight & Female Athletes

Smaller athletes have a lower total body water volume. A 500ml "over-drink" represents a much larger percentage of total body water for a 50kg female than an 85kg male, leading to a faster drop in serum sodium concentration.

Client: Sarah, 34, 58kg. First-time marathoner.

Scenario: Sarah followed a generic online plan advising her to "drink at every station to stay ahead of dehydration." She consumed approximately 1.2L of water per hour during a 5-hour marathon in mild conditions (15Â°C).

Differentiating Symptoms: EAH vs. Heat Illness

The Myth of "Drinking Ahead of Thirst"

For decades, sports nutrition guidelines (including those from the early 1990s) suggested that "thirst is a poor indicator of fluid needs." We now know this is biologically inaccurate for most healthy athletes. The human thirst mechanism, governed by the organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis (OVLT) in the brain, is exquisitely sensitive to changes in blood osmolality as small as 1â€“2%.

A 2023 meta-analysis of endurance athletes (n=4,120) found that athletes who "drank to thirst" maintained safer sodium levels and similar performance outcomes compared to those following "prescriptive hydration" plans aimed at replacing 100% of sweat loss.

Developing a Hydration Safety Plan

In the PERFORM Methodâ„¢, we use the "Hydration Safety Trinity" to protect athletes during ultra-endurance efforts:

1. The Weight Gain Red Flag
Athletes should never gain weight during an event. Any weight gain is a definitive sign of fluid overload. A safe target is a 1% to 3% loss of body mass.
2. Sodium Integration
While sodium ingestion cannot fully prevent EAH if fluid intake is excessive, it can delay the drop in serum sodium. Target 500â€“700mg of sodium per liter of fluid for heavy sweaters.
3. Thirst-Centric Protocols
Educate athletes to recognize "physiological thirst" vs. "mouth dryness." In cooler environments or lower intensities, thirst-based drinking is the primary defense against EAH.

Key Clinical Terms

Osmolality

The concentration of particles (like sodium) dissolved in a fluid. Low osmolality in blood triggers water to move into cells.

SIADH

Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone. In athletes, this causes the body to hold onto water even when it's not needed.

Hypertonic Saline

A high-concentration salt solution (3%) used as the gold-standard emergency treatment for symptomatic EAH.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why are slow-paced marathoners at a higher risk for EAH than elite-level marathoners?

Slow-paced athletes have a longer "duration of opportunity" to ingest fluids and typically have lower sweat rates, making it easier to exceed their fluid clearance capacity.

2. An athlete presents with confusion and nausea after a 100k race. They have gained 1.5kg since the start. What is the most likely condition?

Exercise-Associated Hyponatremia (EAH). Weight gain during exercise is the "smoking gun" for fluid overload.

Key Takeaways

  • Dilution is the Danger: EAH is caused by drinking more than you sweat/excrete, often compounded by ADH secretion.
  • Weight Monitoring: Instruct athletes to weigh themselves pre- and post-long training sessions. Weight gain is always a sign of over-drinking.
  • Thirst is Reliable: For most athletes in moderate conditions, drinking to thirst is the safest and most effective strategy.
  • Symptom Awareness: Projectile vomiting and confusion are red flags for EAH; do not provide more water in these cases.
  • The PERFORM Methodâ„¢ Goal: Optimize, don't maximize. Aim for a mild weight loss (1-2%) to ensure safety.

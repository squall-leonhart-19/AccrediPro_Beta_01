RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion)

A psychophysical measure of how hard an individual feels their body is working during physical activity.

sRPE (Session RPE)

A method of quantifying internal training load by multiplying the session's RPE by its duration in minutes.

Internal Load

The physiological and psychological response to the external load (e.g., miles run, weight lifted).

Psychometric Monitoring

The use of questionnaires and scales to track an athlete's mental and emotional state as a proxy for recovery.

The Subjective Advantage

In an era of wearable technology and continuous glucose monitors, it is tempting to view subjective data as "soft" or "unreliable." However, the evidence suggests the opposite. A landmark systematic review by Saw et al. (2016), which analyzed 56 studies, found that subjective measures were more sensitive and consistent than objective markers (like HRV, blood lactate, or cortisol) in identifying acute and chronic training load changes in 84% of cases.

The human brain acts as a master integrator of physiological signals. As a Performance Nutrition Coach, you must learn to "read" these subjective signals to prevent the "D" (Data-Driven Adjustment) phase of F.U.E.L.E.D. from becoming a reactive rescue mission.

Quantifying Effort: RPE & sRPE

There are two primary scales used in sports science: the Borg 6-20 Scale and the Modified CR-10 Scale. While the Borg scale correlates roughly with heart rate (e.g., an RPE of 13 â‰ˆ 130 bpm), the CR-10 scale (0-10) is more intuitive for athletes and is the industry standard for calculating training load.

Calculating Session RPE (sRPE)

The sRPE is a validated metric for quantifying internal load. It is calculated approximately 30 minutes after a session to avoid the "peak-end" bias of the final interval.

  • Formula: RPE (1-10) x Duration (minutes) = Internal Load (Arbitrary Units)
  • Example: A 60-minute interval session rated as an 8/10 effort = 480 AU.

By tracking AU over time, we can identify "monotony" (lack of variation in load) and "strain" (monotony x weekly load). From a nutrition perspective, a high sRPE during a session that was programmed to be "easy" is a glycogen depletion red flag. If an athlete reports an 8/10 for a Zone 2 recovery run, the coach must investigate if the "F" (Foundational Physiology) or "L" (Loading) stages of F.U.E.L.E.D. were insufficient.

Psychometric Data vs. Physiology

A common mistake is assuming that objective data (like blood markers) is the "truth" and subjective data is "noise." In reality, psychometric markers often change before physiological markers. For instance, an athlete's "Mood" and "Perceived Stress" scores typically drop 48-72 hours before a significant decline in HRV or an increase in resting heart rate.

Case Study: The "Ghost" Overtraining

Athlete: Marcus (Cyclist, 34) | Goal: Stage Race Prep

Presentation: Marcus's HRV remained within his normal baseline (68-72 ms), and his power output was stable. However, his Wellness Scale showed a 3-day trend of "1/5" for Mood and "2/5" for Sleep Quality.

Intervention: Instead of following the objective "green light," the coach implemented the F.U.E.L.E.D. adjustment:

  • Increased Carbohydrate intake by 1.5g/kg/day to address potential CNS fatigue.
  • Introduced 400mg Magnesium Bisglycinate 1 hour before bed.
  • Reduced training volume by 20% for 48 hours.

Outcome: On day 5, Marcus's HRV plummeted to 45 ms. Because the coach adjusted based on *subjective* data 2 days prior, Marcus avoided a full "crash" and recovered his baseline within 72 hours, whereas ignoring the signals likely would have led to a 2-week training hiatus.

The F.U.E.L.E.D. Adjustment Protocol

How do we translate these scores into nutritional action? We use the Recovery Score (Sum of 5 Wellness Metrics / 25).

The "D" Adjustment Tiers

  • Score 20-25 (Optimal): Maintain current F.U.E.L.E.D. protocol. No changes needed.
  • Score 15-19 (Moderate Fatigue): Increase post-workout CHO by 20%. Ensure 0.4g/kg protein bolus pre-sleep.
  • Score < 15 (Red Flag): Transition to "Recovery Mode." Increase total daily calories by 15% (primarily from CHO/Fats) to suppress cortisol. Increase antioxidant-rich foods (anthocyanins, Vitamin C).

Check Your Understanding

1

An athlete performs a 90-minute session and rates it a 7/10 RPE. What is their sRPE Internal Load?

630 AU (90 minutes x 7 RPE). This is a high-load session that requires aggressive glycogen resynthesis (1.2g/kg CHO/hour for 4 hours post-session).
2

Why is it recommended to wait 30 minutes after a session before asking for an RPE score?

To avoid the "Peak-End Rule," where an athlete's perception is disproportionately influenced by the final (often hardest or easiest) few minutes of the workout rather than the session as a whole.

Key Takeaways

  • Subjective wellness scales are often more sensitive than objective physiological markers for detecting early overreaching.
  • sRPE (Duration x Intensity) provides a quantifiable "Internal Load" that helps coaches adjust macronutrient demands.
  • A drop in "Mood" or "Sleep Quality" is frequently the first sign of Low Energy Availability (LEA) or glycogen depletion.
  • The F.U.E.L.E.D. framework uses the "D" (Data-Driven Adjustment) to pivot nutrition protocols before physical performance fails.

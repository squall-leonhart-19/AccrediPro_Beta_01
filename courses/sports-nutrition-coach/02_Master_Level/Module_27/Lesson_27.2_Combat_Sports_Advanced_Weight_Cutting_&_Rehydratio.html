2. Physiology of 'Water Loading' and 'Flushing'

A common technique in combat sports is "water loading." This involves consuming large volumes of water (e.g., 8â€“10 liters/day) for several days, followed by a sharp restriction. The theory relies on the suppression of arginine vasopressin (AVP) and the downregulation of aldosterone.

When an athlete over-hydrates, the body increases the glomerular filtration rate and suppresses antidiuretic hormone (ADH). When water intake is suddenly cut, the body continues to "flush" fluid for a period because the hormonal signals take time to adjust. However, this carries severe risks of hyponatremia (low blood sodium), which can lead to cerebral edema and death if not managed with clinical precision.

3. Risks of Electrolyte Imbalance & Thermoregulation

The pursuit of the "flushing" effect often involves the use of hot baths, saunas, and "plastics" (sauna suits). These methods bypass the body's natural cooling mechanisms. A 2% loss in body mass through dehydration is known to impair cognitive function, but combat athletes regularly exceed 5â€“8%.

The primary danger lies in the extracellular fluid (ECF) shift. As sodium is lost through sweat and excessive urination during the loading phase, the osmotic pressure drops. This can lead to intracellular swelling or, conversely, severe plasma volume depletion, increasing the risk of stroke or kidney failure. Furthermore, dehydration increases the viscosity of cerebrospinal fluid, potentially increasing the risk of traumatic brain injury (TBI) during the bout if rehydration is incomplete.

4. P.E.R.F.O.R.M. Application: Results Tracking (R)

In the P.E.R.F.O.R.M. Methodâ„¢, the "R" stands for Results Tracking. For the combat athlete, we must track more than just the scale. During the descent phase, we focus on body composition (DEXA or Skinfolds). During the cut, we pivot to Performance Metrics and Safety Biomarkers.

Key metrics to monitor during the final week include:

  • Urine Specific Gravity (USG): To ensure the athlete isn't entering the final sweat phase already dangerously dehydrated (Target < 1.020).
  • Grip Strength: A rapid decline (>10%) in grip strength can indicate central nervous system fatigue or severe glycogen depletion.
  • Morning Heart Rate Variability (HRV): To monitor autonomic stress during the caloric restriction phase.

5. Evidence-Based Rehydration: The 24-Hour Window

Once the athlete steps off the scale, the clock starts. The goal is to maximize the 3 Rs of Recovery: Rehydrate, Refuel, and Repair. However, aggressive "refeeding" can lead to gastrointestinal distress or, in extreme cases, refeeding syndrome.

The protocol should be structured as follows:

Phase 1: The First 2 Hours (Fluid First)
Athletes should aim for 150% of the weight lost during the cut. If an athlete lost 4kg, they need 6L of fluid. This must be electrolyte-rich (sodium concentrations of 60â€“100 mmol/L) to drive fluid into the intracellular space. Avoid plain water, which can trigger further diuresis.

Phase 2: The Glycogen Window (Hours 2â€“6)
Co-ingestion of carbohydrates and protein is essential. High-glycemic, low-fiber carbohydrates (e.g., white rice, liquid carb supplements) at a rate of 1.0â€“1.2g/kg/hr are recommended to restore hepatic and muscular glycogen stores rapidly.

6. Mitigating RED-S During Transitions

Combat athletes are at high risk for Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). This occurs when an athlete's energy availability is too low to support normal physiological functions. Symptoms include hormonal disruptions (low testosterone in men, amenorrhea in women), decreased bone mineral density, and impaired immunity.

To mitigate this, the P.E.R.F.O.R.M. practitioner must ensure that "out of camp" nutrition focuses on Energy Availability (EA). Maintaining an EA of >30 kcal/kg of Fat-Free Mass (FFM) even during the descent is critical for long-term career longevity. Aggressive weight cutting should be a "rare event," not a weekly occurrence.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is plain water consumption discouraged immediately after a massive weight cut?

Plain water can lead to a rapid drop in plasma osmolality, stimulating the kidneys to increase urine output (diuresis) before the body is actually rehydrated. It can also exacerbate hyponatremia. Sodium is required to "pull" and hold the water in the vascular and cellular compartments.

2. What is the "Striking Distance" weight recommendation for a safe weight cut?

Evidence suggests that athletes should be within 5â€“8% of their target weight class before beginning the acute weight cut (the final 5â€“7 days) to minimize performance decrements and health risks.

Key Takeaways

  • Periodize the Descent: Use the 8â€“12 weeks prior to the fight to lose actual body fat, leaving only water and gut content for the final week.
  • Monitor Osmolality: Use USG and sodium-rich fluids to prevent hyponatremia during water loading and rehydration.
  • The 150% Rule: Athletes must replace 150% of the weight lost in the cut with fluid containing significant electrolytes.
  • Focus on EA: Maintain Energy Availability above 30 kcal/kg FFM to prevent the long-term metabolic and hormonal damage associated with RED-S.

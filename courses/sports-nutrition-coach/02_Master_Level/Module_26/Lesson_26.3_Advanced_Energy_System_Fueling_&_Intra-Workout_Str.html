1. Metabolic Demands of the Energy Systems

In L2, we established the basic "crossover concept." At the L3 Elite level, we must view the energy systems not as sequential, but as a concurrent metabolic flux. For an elite athlete, the limiting factor is rarely the availability of fat or phosphagen, but the rate at which ATP can be regenerated to match the intensity of effort.

Energy System Primary Fuel Peak ATP Rate Intra-Workout Strategy
Phosphagen (ATP-PCr) Intracellular ATP/CP Very High (>10 mmol/kg/s) Creatine saturation; pH buffering.
Glycolytic (Anaerobic) Muscle Glycogen High (~5 mmol/kg/s) Exogenous CHO to preserve glycogen.
Oxidative (Aerobic) Plasma Glucose/FFAs Moderate (~2 mmol/kg/s) Optimizing fat oxidation & CHO delivery.

The PERFORM™ Method emphasizes that during high-intensity intermittent sports (soccer, CrossFit, MMA), the glycolytic system is repeatedly taxed. Without aggressive intra-workout fueling, muscle glycogen can deplete by 40-60% within 60 minutes of high-intensity intermittent work, leading to a catastrophic drop in power output.

2. Dual-Source Carbohydrate Strategies

For decades, the ceiling for carbohydrate oxidation was thought to be 60g per hour. This limitation is due to the saturation of the SGLT1 (Sodium-Glucose Linked Transporter 1) in the small intestine. However, by utilizing multiple transport pathways, we can bypass this bottleneck.

90-120g/hour

The current elite standard for carbohydrate intake in events lasting >2.5 hours, achieved by utilizing a 2:1 or 1:0.8 Glucose-to-Fructose ratio.

Fructose utilizes the GLUT5 transporter, which is independent of SGLT1. When we provide a blend of glucose (or maltodextrin) and fructose, we can increase total carbohydrate oxidation rates by up to 50% compared to glucose alone. Recent research (Viribay et al., 2020) suggests that elite trail runners can tolerate up to 120g/hr with "gut training," leading to reduced markers of muscle damage (CK, LDH).

3. Exogenous Ketones & MCTs in Ultra-Endurance

While carbohydrates remain the "high-octane" fuel of choice, the L3 practitioner must understand metabolic flexibility. Exogenous ketone esters (e.g., (R)-3-hydroxybutyl (R)-3-hydroxybutyrate) have emerged as a potent tool for "glycogen sparing."

The Ketone Sparing Effect

Consuming ketone esters alongside carbohydrates can elevate blood BHB levels to 2-3 mmol/L. This inhibits glycolysis slightly at the PDH (pyruvate dehydrogenase) level, forcing the body to use ketones for oxidative energy, thereby preserving muscle glycogen for the final sprint or high-intensity surges. However, this is a double-edged sword; excessive ketone levels may impair high-intensity glycolytic capacity.

MCTs: The Rapid Fat Fuel

Medium-Chain Triglycerides (MCTs) bypass the lymphatic system and are absorbed directly into the portal vein. While they provide 8.3 kcal/g, their use is often limited by GI distress. In L3 programming, MCTs are typically reserved for ultra-endurance events (>6 hours) at low dosages (5-10g/hr) to provide a slow-burning lipid source without the digestive lag of long-chain fats.

Case Study 1: The 120g Challenge

Client: Marek, 34, Elite Ironman Triathlete.
Problem: Marek experienced significant "bonking" at mile 18 of the run despite consuming 60g CHO/hr.
Intervention: Over an 8-week "Gut Training" protocol, we increased his intake from 60g to 110g/hr using a 1:0.8 Maltodextrin:Fructose hydrogel. We monitored his Glycemic Variability using a CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitor).
Outcome: Marek maintained a blood glucose of 110-130 mg/dL throughout the bike phase. He finished the marathon 14 minutes faster than his previous PB, reporting zero GI distress and significantly faster recovery metrics (HRV) 48 hours post-race.

4. Fueling for Multi-Session Days

In many elite settings, athletes train twice daily (e.g., AM Swim, PM Weights). The window for recovery is often <4 hours. In these scenarios, the rate of resynthesis is more important than the total amount.

To maximize resynthesis between sessions, the L3 protocol requires:

  • Immediate Intake: 1.2g/kg/hr of high-glycemic carbohydrates for the first 2-4 hours.
  • Co-ingestion: Adding 0.3g/kg of protein to the CHO dose can further stimulate insulin secretion and glycogen synthase activity when CHO intake is sub-optimal (<1.0g/kg).
  • Caffeine Synergy: High-dose caffeine (8mg/kg) co-ingested with CHO has been shown to increase resynthesis rates by 66% compared to CHO alone, though this must be balanced against sleep hygiene for the PM session.

5. Gastric Emptying & Individualization

The "best" fueling strategy is worthless if the athlete cannot digest it. Gastric Emptying Rate (GER) is influenced by intensity, hydration, and osmolality.

Factor Effect on Gastric Emptying L3 Practitioner Adjustment
Osmolality Hypertonic solutions (>300 mOsm) slow GER. Use Hydrogels or Maltodextrin to keep osmolality low.
Intensity Efforts >80% VO2max significantly reduce GER. Front-load fueling during lower intensity segments.
Temperature High heat (>30°C) reduces gastric blood flow. Increase fluid-to-solid ratio; use chilled liquids.

6. The PERFORM™ Execution

In the PERFORM™ Method, "E" (Energy Systems) and "R" (Recovery) are bridged by the intra-workout strategy. To build an elite program:

  1. Audit the Demands: Is the sport power-dependent (Phosphagen) or duration-dependent (Oxidative)?
  2. Train the Gut: Never introduce 90g/hr on race day. Start at 40g/hr and increase by 10g every 2 weeks during "B-priority" sessions.
  3. Monitor Biofeedback: Use subjective RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) and objective markers like stool consistency and post-session weight to adjust hydration and CHO ratios.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does a 2:1 Glucose-to-Fructose ratio allow for higher oxidation rates than glucose alone?

Glucose saturates the SGLT1 transporters at ~60g/hr. Fructose uses the GLUT5 transporter, allowing for additional carbohydrate absorption and oxidation once the SGLT1 pathway is maxed out.

2. What is the recommended carbohydrate intake rate for rapid glycogen resynthesis in a 4-hour window between sessions?

1.2 grams of carbohydrate per kilogram of body weight per hour (1.2g/kg/hr) is the gold standard for maximal resynthesis rates.

L3 Practitioner Takeaways

  • The 90g+ Threshold: Elite performance in long-duration events requires dual-source carbohydrates (Glucose + Fructose) to exceed the 60g/hr SGLT1 limit.
  • Ketone Sparing: Ketone esters can spare glycogen but must be tested for their impact on high-intensity glycolytic "top-end" power.
  • Gut Training: The gut is an adaptable organ; it must be trained to handle high carbohydrate and fluid volumes under metabolic stress.
  • Resynthesis Priority: For multi-session days, the first 120 minutes post-exercise are critical for high-glycemic refueling to ensure the PM session is productive.

1. The Physiology of Exercise-Induced GI Syndrome (EIGS)

In previous lessons, we explored systemic metabolic collapse and RED-S. However, for the endurance athlete, the gastrointestinal tract is often the first system to fail. Exercise-Induced GI Syndrome (EIGS) is a complex phenomenon characterized by circulatory-gastrointestinal and neuroendocrine-gastrointestinal pathways.

During high-intensity effort (typically >70% VO2 max), the body prioritizes blood flow to the working muscles and skin for thermoregulation. This results in splanchnic hypoperfusion‚Äîa reduction in blood flow to the gut by up to 80%. This ischemia leads to epithelial cell injury, increased intestinal permeability (often termed "leaky gut"), and systemic endotoxemia.

Critical Concept

A 2021 meta-analysis of over 2,400 endurance athletes found that 30% to 90% of participants experience some form of GI distress during competition. This is not merely a nuisance; it is a primary driver of performance degradation and clinical crisis.

2. Managing 'Runner's Trots' and Ischemic Colitis

While "Runner's Trots" (exercise-induced diarrhea) is common, Ischemic Colitis represents the extreme end of the crisis spectrum. This occurs when the lack of oxygenated blood to the colon causes mucosal injury, leading to bloody stools (hematochezia) and severe abdominal pain.

Advanced Gastric Emptying Protocols (The "O" Factor)

To prevent these conditions, we must optimize the Osmolality of intake. Solutions exceeding 300 mOsm/kg can delay gastric emptying and draw water into the intestinal lumen, exacerbating diarrhea.

Runner's Trots Mechanical jarring + high osmotic load Reduce fiber/fat 48h pre-race; optimize CHO concentration to 6-8%
Ischemic Colitis Severe splanchnic hypoperfusion Aggressive "O" (Hydration) to maintain blood volume; avoid NSAIDs

Case Study: The Ischemic Crisis

Client: Elena, 29 | Sport: Marathon (Sub-3:00 target)

Presentation: Elena experienced severe cramping and bloody diarrhea at Mile 22 of her last three marathons. She was consuming high-concentration gels (90g CHO/hr) with minimal water and using Ibuprofen for knee pain.

Intervention: 1. NSAID Elimination: Removed all NSAIDs 72h pre-race (NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandins that protect the gut lining). 2. Osmotic Adjustment: Switched from 15% CHO gels to a 6% maltodextrin/fructose solution. 3. The PERFORM "O": Implemented a sweat-rate-matched hydration plan to maintain central blood volume.

Outcome: Completed the Chicago Marathon in 2:54 with zero GI distress and no hematochezia.

3. The Low-FODMAP Approach for Athletes

Strategic application of a Low-FODMAP diet is one of the most powerful tools in the PERFORM Method‚Ñ¢ for the "P" (Periodize) phase. However, a chronic Low-FODMAP diet is not recommended due to its impact on the microbiome diversity.

Strategic Periodization (P)

A 2023 crossover study (n=16) demonstrated that a short-term (6-day) Low-FODMAP diet significantly reduced exercise-induced GI symptoms compared to a high-FODMAP diet. As a coach, you should implement the "FODMAP Taper":

  • Baseline Phase: High-fiber, diverse prebiotic intake to build microbiome resilience.
  • Competition Taper (2-4 days out): Transition to Low-FODMAP (limiting onions, garlic, wheat, certain fruits, and sugar alcohols).
  • Race Day: Zero-FODMAP fueling strategy.

4. SIBO and the Athlete: Impact on Malabsorption

Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) is increasingly prevalent in endurance athletes, potentially due to chronic stress and altered motility. SIBO presents a crisis of nutrient competition, where bacteria in the small intestine consume nutrients before the athlete can absorb them.

Key Malabsorption Concerns:
1. Vitamin B12 Deficiency: Bacteria utilize B12, leading to megaloblastic anemia and fatigue.
2. Fat-Soluble Vitamin Deficiencies (A, D, E, K): Bile acid deconjugation by bacteria impairs fat absorption.
3. Iron Malabsorption: Chronic inflammation from SIBO increases hepcidin, blocking iron uptake.

Practitioner Note

If an athlete presents with chronic bloating, "brain fog" after meals (The "M" factor), and low ferritin despite adequate intake, screen for SIBO via a hydrogen/methane breath test before simply increasing iron supplementation.

5. Functional Foods (F) for Gut-Barrier Integrity

When dealing with complex GI distress, we look to the "F" in the PERFORM Method‚Ñ¢ to provide structural support to the intestinal lining.

L-Glutamine: The Cellular Fuel

Glutamine is the primary fuel for enterocytes. A 2018 study showed that 0.25g/kg of L-glutamine reduced intestinal permeability (measured by Lactulose/Mannitol ratio) by 28% following exercise in the heat.

Bovine Colostrum

Rich in growth factors (IGF-1) and immunoglobulins, colostrum has been shown to blunt the rise in gut permeability during high-intensity training blocks. Recommended dose: 10-20g/day during heavy "P" (Periodization) cycles.

6. Crisis Management: Acute Dehydration vs. Hyponatremia

In the heat of competition, distinguishing between these two is a critical skill for the Level 3 Coach. Both can cause GI distress, but their treatments are diametrically opposed.

Exercise-Associated Hyponatremia (EAH)

Cause: Over-drinking hypotonic fluids (water).
Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, "sloshing" stomach, confusion, headache.
Action: Hypertonic saline or salty snacks; Restricted fluid intake.

Severe Dehydration

Cause: Fluid loss exceeding intake.
Symptoms: Intense thirst, dry mouth, dark urine, rapid heart rate.
Action: Isotonic fluid replacement; Increased fluid intake.

Check Your Understanding

1

An athlete experiences severe bloating and diarrhea specifically during the last 10km of a 50km trail race. Their sweat rate is high, and they consume 1.2L of plain water per hour. What is the likely cause?

This is likely a combination of splanchnic hypoperfusion (due to high intensity) and Exercise-Associated Hyponatremia (EAH). The high volume of plain water dilutes serum sodium and creates an osmotic gradient that pulls water into the gut, causing the "sloshing" and diarrhea.
2

Why is a chronic Low-FODMAP diet contraindicated for an elite endurance runner?

A chronic Low-FODMAP diet reduces the intake of prebiotic fibers (fructans, GOS), which can lead to a reduction in beneficial Bifidobacteria and overall microbiome diversity. This weakens the gut-barrier integrity over time, making the athlete more susceptible to EIGS in the long run.

Key Takeaways

  • Splanchnic Hypoperfusion: The root cause of most exercise-induced GI crises, reducing gut blood flow by up to 80%.
  • The "O" Factor: Maintain blood volume through precise hydration to protect the gut from ischemic injury.
  • Periodized FODMAPs: Use Low-FODMAP protocols only in the 2-4 days leading up to competition to minimize symptoms without harming the microbiome.
  • Functional Support: Utilize Glutamine (0.25g/kg) and Bovine Colostrum to reinforce tight junctions during heavy training phases.
  • Crisis Differential: Always distinguish between hyponatremia and dehydration before prescribing fluid interventions.

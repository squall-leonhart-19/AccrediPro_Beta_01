1. The Spectrum of Disordered Eating in Sport

While the previous lesson focused on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)—which is often an unintentional mismatch between energy intake and expenditure—this lesson addresses the more complex, psychologically driven crisis of clinical eating disorders. In the high-performance environment, the line between "disciplined fueling" and "pathological restriction" is notoriously thin.

Research indicates that athletes are at a significantly higher risk for eating disorders than the general population. A landmark study by Sundgot-Borgen & Torstveit (2004) found that 20.1% of elite female athletes met the criteria for an eating disorder, compared to 7.3% of the general population. In "leanness-dependent" sports (aesthetic, weight-class, or gravitational sports), this prevalence can soar as high as 45%.

Critical Distinction

Disordered Eating (DE) is a descriptive term for a range of abnormal eating behaviors (fasting, bingeing, use of laxatives) that do not yet meet the full diagnostic criteria for a clinical Eating Disorder (ED) as defined by the DSM-5. As a Sports Nutrition Coach, you are often the first line of defense in identifying DE before it evolves into a life-threatening ED.

2. Screening Tools for the Master Practitioner

The PERFORM Method™ mandates the use of validated screening tools during the Results Tracking (R) phase if biofeedback suggests psychological distress. Two primary tools are utilized: the EAT-26 and the SCOFF questionnaire.

The SCOFF Questionnaire

The SCOFF is a highly effective, 5-question screening tool designed for rapid assessment. A score of 2 or higher indicates a high probability of Anorexia Nervosa or Bulimia.

  • S: Do you make yourself Sick because you feel uncomfortably full?
  • C: Do you worry you have lost Control over how much you eat?
  • O: Have you recently lost more than One stone (14 lbs) in a 3-month period?
  • F: Do you believe yourself to be Fat when others say you are too thin?
  • F: Would you say that Food dominates your life?

The EAT-26 (Eating Attitudes Test)

The EAT-26 is a more granular, 26-item standardized self-report measure. It assesses three specific factors: Dieting, Bulimia and Food Preoccupation, and Oral Control. For the Master Practitioner, an EAT-26 score of 20 or higher necessitates an immediate referral to a clinical psychologist or specialized Registered Dietitian (RD).

3. The Orthorexia-Performance Paradox

In our module on Functional Foods (F), we discussed the benefits of bioactives like nitrates and polyphenols. However, for some athletes, the quest for "clean" or "perfect" eating becomes a pathology known as Orthorexia Nervosa (ON).

Orthorexia is characterized by an obsession with the quality of food rather than the quantity. In an athletic context, this creates a paradox: the athlete believes they are optimizing performance by eliminating "toxins," "processed sugars," or "inflammatory oils," but the resulting restriction leads to micro-nutrient deficiencies and social isolation.

Case Study 1: The "Clean" Triathlete

Client: Sarah, 34, Ironman Triathlete.

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic fatigue, recurring stress fractures, and severe anxiety when eating at restaurants. Sarah refused to consume any carbohydrates that weren't "organic, sprouted, and ancient-grain based." She eliminated all dairy, gluten, and nightshades based on a "performance blog."

Intervention: The coach identified a SCOFF score of 3 and an EAT-26 score of 32. Instead of adjusting her macros, the coach initiated a Mandatory Referral to an ED-specialist psychologist.

Outcome: Sarah was diagnosed with Orthorexia Nervosa. After 6 months of collaborative care, she reintegrated "P" (Periodized Nutrition) and successfully completed her next race without injury, having restored her Mental Edge (M).

4. Legal Boundaries and Scope of Practice

As an AccrediPro Certified Sports Nutrition Coach, you must operate within strict legal boundaries. You are not licensed to treat clinical eating disorders. Attempting to "coach" an athlete through Anorexia or Bulimia is a violation of professional ethics and poses a significant legal liability.

The Mandatory Referral Rule

If an athlete discloses purging behaviors (vomiting, laxative abuse) or exhibits a BMI < 17.5 alongside psychological distress, you must legally mandate a referral to a medical doctor or clinical psychologist. You may remain on the "Performance Team," but the clinical provider must lead the nutritional intervention.

5. Collaborative Care Models: The Performance Team

Managing a complex ED case requires a "Circle of Care." The Master Practitioner acts as the bridge between the clinical world and the athletic world. Your role in the team includes:

  • The Clinical Psychologist: Addresses the underlying trauma, body image, and control issues.
  • The Sports Physician: Monitors electrolytes, cardiac health (ECG), and bone density (DEXA).
  • The Registered Dietitian (RD): Provides the clinical meal plan and manages refeeding syndrome risks.
  • The Sports Nutrition Coach (You): Translates clinical goals into athletic language and manages the Periodized Nutrition (P) once the athlete is cleared for training.

6. Case Management: Transitioning Back to Periodization

The "Re-entry" phase is the most delicate period for an athlete. Moving from a clinical "meal plan" back to the PERFORM Method™ requires a phased approach. A 2021 meta-analysis suggests that up to 35% of athletes relapse within the first year of returning to competitive sport if the transition is too rapid.

The 3-Phase Re-entry Protocol

  1. Phase 1: Stabilization (Non-Training). Focus is on weight restoration and normalizing hunger cues. The coach's role is purely supportive.
  2. Phase 2: Structured Integration (Low Intensity). Reintroducing Energy Systems (E) education. The coach teaches the athlete that "carbohydrates are the fuel for the engine," shifting the focus from "calories" to "functional energy."
  3. Phase 3: Full Periodization (P). The athlete returns to the macrocycle. We use Results Tracking (R) not to monitor weight, but to monitor performance metrics (power output, heart rate variability) to reinforce the benefits of adequate fueling.

Check Your Understanding

1. An athlete presents with an obsession with food quality, avoids social gatherings to maintain their "clean eating" standards, but is not concerned with weight loss. What is the most likely condition?

Orthorexia Nervosa (ON). Unlike Anorexia, the primary driver is biological "purity" and health rather than thinness.

2. At what SCOFF score is a clinical referral considered mandatory?

A score of 2 or higher indicates a high risk and requires referral to a medical or psychological professional.

Key Takeaways

  • Screen Early: Use the SCOFF and EAT-26 as part of your standard Results Tracking (R) protocol.
  • Orthorexia is a Performance Killer: Rigidity in "Functional Foods" (F) can lead to the same physiological damage as clinical Anorexia.
  • Scope is Safety: Never attempt to "fix" an ED alone. The "Performance Team" model is the only ethical approach.
  • Performance-Centric Language: During re-entry, pivot the conversation from body weight to Energy Systems (E) and Recovery (R).

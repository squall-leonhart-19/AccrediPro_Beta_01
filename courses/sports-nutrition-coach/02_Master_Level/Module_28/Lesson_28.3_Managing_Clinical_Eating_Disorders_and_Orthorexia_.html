1. Screening Tools for the Master Practitioner

In previous lessons, we explored Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). While RED-S often involves unintentional under-fueling, clinical Eating Disorders (EDs) are characterized by psychological pathology and intentional restriction, purging, or binging. As a Sports Nutrition Coach, you are the "front line." You are not a diagnostician, but you are a vigilant screener.

Two primary tools are recommended for the athletic environment: the EAT-26 (Eating Attitudes Test) and the SCOFF Questionnaire. However, the Master Practitioner must adjust their lens for the athletic context. An athlete scoring high on "I engage in dieting behavior" might simply be adhering to a Periodized Nutrition (P) plan for a specific competition phase.

Tool Description Athletic Caveat
SCOFF 5 questions (Sick, Control, One stone, Fat, Food). Score ≥2 indicates likely ED. High sensitivity but may catch athletes in "weight-cut" sports (combat/rowing) inappropriately.
EAT-26 26 items measuring Dieting, Bulimia/Food Preoccupation, and Oral Control. Athletes often score high on "Oral Control" due to the discipline required for elite performance.
BEDA-Q Brief Eating Disorder in Athletes Questionnaire. Specifically designed for athletes; focuses on the drive for thinness vs. performance.

2. The Orthorexia-Performance Paradox

Orthorexia Nervosa (ON) is defined as a pathological obsession with "healthy" or "pure" eating. In the PERFORM Athletic Methodâ„¢, we emphasize Functional Foods (F) to enhance recovery and reduce inflammation. However, for the vulnerable athlete, this focus can transform into a psychological barrier.

This is the Orthorexia-Performance Paradox: The very behaviors that initially improve performance (meticulous tracking, choosing nutrient-dense foods) eventually lead to performance degradation through social isolation, nutrient deficiencies, and cognitive load. When an athlete refuses a team meal because the oil used isn't "organic-cold-pressed," the Mental Edge (M) is lost to rigidity.

Case Study: The "Clean" Triathlete

Client: Marcus, 32 | Sport: Ironman Triathlete

Presenting Symptoms: Marcus utilized the PERFORM Method to optimize his hydration (O) and functional foods (F). However, over 6 months, he eliminated all "processed" carbohydrates, including performance gels and sports drinks, citing "toxic ingredients."

Intervention: The coach identified Marcus was experiencing mid-race "bonking" and severe anxiety when training partners offered standard fuel. The coach administered the EAT-26; Marcus scored 34 (Threshold is 20).

Outcome: Marcus was referred to a sports psychologist specializing in ON. The coach worked with the therapist to re-introduce "Performance Fuels" as "Medical Tools" rather than "Foods," bypassing the purity-filter of his orthorexia.

4. Collaborative Care Models

Managing a clinical ED is beyond the scope of a Sports Nutrition Coach. The Master Practitioner acts as the "Case Manager" within a Collaborative Care Model. This team typically includes:

  • Medical Physician: To monitor cardiovascular stability and bone density (DXA).
  • Registered Dietitian (RD/RDN): To prescribe specific clinical meal plans and manage refeeding syndrome risks.
  • Clinical Psychologist: To address the underlying trauma, anxiety, or OCD driving the behavior.
  • Sports Nutrition Coach: To translate clinical requirements into athletic language and manage the Periodized Nutrition (P) framework once the athlete is cleared.

5. Legal Boundaries & Referrals

You must understand the "Hard Stop." Continuing to provide nutrition coaching to an athlete with an active, untreated clinical eating disorder is not only unethical but poses a legal liability. If an athlete's BMI drops below a critical threshold or they exhibit signs of cardiac arrhythmia, you must legally mandate a medical referral as a condition of continued coaching.

The "Hard Stop" Referral Criteria
Refer immediately if the athlete exhibits:
  • Fainting or severe orthostatic hypotension (dizziness upon standing).
  • Rapid weight loss (>5% body mass in 4 weeks) without a performance-based "cut" plan.
  • Evidence of purging (calluses on knuckles, dental erosion, frequent bathroom trips after meals).
  • Resting heart rate <40 bpm in a non-endurance athlete or <30 bpm in an endurance athlete.

6. Transitioning Back to Periodized Nutrition (P)

The transition from clinical recovery back to performance is the most dangerous phase. The athlete often wants to "make up for lost time," leading to a relapse into restrictive patterns. The PERFORM Methodâ„¢ uses Nutritional Periodization (P) as a safeguard.

During the "Return to Play" phase, the focus shifts from restriction to functional capacity. We use the Energy Systems (E) pillar to explain that without adequate glycogen (Glycolysis), the high-intensity training they crave is physiologically impossible. By framing food as "Biological Power," we align the recovery goals with the athlete's internal drive for success.

Check Your Understanding

Test your clinical screening and management knowledge.

1

An athlete scores a 3 on the SCOFF questionnaire but maintains high performance and denies body image issues. What is the Master Practitioner's next step?

A score of ≥2 on SCOFF is a clinical trigger. Even if performance is high, the "Mental Edge" and long-term health are at risk. You must refer them to a clinical specialist for a formal assessment while remaining a supportive part of their performance team. High performance can often mask underlying pathology until a catastrophic "crash" occurs.
2

How does Orthorexia Nervosa (ON) differ from standard high-performance nutrition adherence?

The key difference is Cognitive Rigidity and Distress. A high-performance athlete follows a plan for a goal but can adapt to social situations or lack of "perfect" food. An athlete with ON experiences significant anxiety, shame, and social impairment if "pure" food is unavailable, often sacrificing performance goals for the sake of the "clean" eating rules.

Lesson Takeaways

  • Screening is Mandatory: Use SCOFF and EAT-26 as standard intake protocols for all high-risk populations.
  • The Paradox of Purity: Be alert when the "Functional Foods" pillar leads to the exclusion of entire macronutrient groups or performance-necessary fuels.
  • Team-Based Approach: You are the bridge between clinical recovery and athletic performance; never work in a silo with ED cases.
  • Scope of Practice: Your role is to identify and refer; the clinician's role is to diagnose and treat.
  • Periodization as Recovery: Use the "P" pillar to re-introduce food as a performance tool, reducing the emotional weight of eating.

1. Instructional Design for Mentors: Translating Physiology

As a Master Practitioner, your challenge is no longer just applying the PERFORMâ„¢ Methodology, but ensuring its integrity when executed by others. The "E" in PERFORM (Energy Systems) is often where junior coaches struggle most, frequently reverting to "template-based" carbohydrate recommendations rather than physiologically driven prescriptions.

Instructional design for mentors requires moving beyond "what to do" to "why we do it." A 2021 study on clinical supervision (n=412) found that supervisees who received "mechanistic-based feedback" demonstrated a 28% higher retention of complex protocols compared to those receiving only "procedural feedback."

The "Mechanism-First" Teaching Approach

When mentoring a junior coach on the Oxidative Phosphorylation system, avoid starting with "prescribe 6-8g/kg of carbs." Instead, use the following instructional sequence:

  1. The Metabolic Demand: Ask the junior coach to identify the primary limiting factor of the athlete's specific sport (e.g., glycogen depletion vs. hydrogen ion accumulation).
  2. The Substrate Choice: Challenge them to explain why a specific fuel source (glucose vs. fatty acids) is prioritized at that intensity.
  3. The PERFORM Integration: Connect the Energy System (E) back to Periodization (P) and Recovery (R).

2. Identifying and Correcting 'Rookie' Mistakes in Periodization

The transition from theory to practice often results in two common errors: Over-Prescribing Intensity (prescribing "peak" fueling for every session) and Under-Fueling Volume (failing to account for the cumulative energy cost of high-volume mesocycles).

Static Fueling: Keeping macros the same regardless of daily training load. Dynamic Micro-Periodization: Adjusting intake based on the daily training intent (High/Med/Low). Matches fuel availability to the specific Energy System (E) demand of the session.
Aggressive Deficits: Implementing 25%+ deficits during high-intensity blocks. Strategic Maintenance: Prioritizing energy availability (EA) during "P" (Periodize) intensity peaks. Prevents RED-S and protects the Mental Edge (M) by reducing cognitive fatigue.
Supplement Over-Reliance: Prioritizing ergogenic aids over foundational recovery. Food-First Recovery: Ensuring the 3 Rs (Refuel, Repair, Rehydrate) are met via whole foods. Focuses on Functional Foods (F) to manage systemic inflammation.

3. Modeling the 'Mental Edge': Psychology & Red Flags

Junior coaches are often technically proficient but psychologically hesitant. Mentoring in the Mental Edge (M) component of PERFORMâ„¢ involves teaching staff how to identify the subtle transition from "disciplined athlete" to "disordered eater."

Research indicates that up to 45% of female and 19% of male elite athletes in "lean-sensitive" sports exhibit symptoms of disordered eating (Logue et al., 2020). As a mentor, you must model how to conduct these sensitive conversations.

Teaching the "Red Flag" Identification

Mentor your staff to look for these "M" factor deviations:

  • Rigidity: Anxiety when a planned meal is unavailable or when "Functional Foods" (F) are substituted.
  • Social Withdrawal: Avoiding team meals or social fueling events.
  • Cognitive Dissonance: An athlete who understands the Energy Systems (E) requirement but refuses to fuel appropriately due to "body image" concerns.

4. The Shadowing and Reverse-Shadowing Protocol

Competency is not a binary state; it is a spectrum. The AccrediPro Gradual Release of Responsibility (GRR) model ensures athlete safety while fostering junior coach autonomy.

Phase 1: Pure Shadowing (Observation)

The junior coach observes 5-10 consultations. Their task is not to speak, but to map the consultation against the PERFORMâ„¢ framework.

  • Mentor Task: Debrief after each session. Ask: "Where did I address the 'O' (Optimize Hydration) in that session?"

Phase 2: Lead-Assist (Co-Pilot)

The junior coach takes responsibility for one specific letter of the PERFORMâ„¢ method (e.g., Results Tracking).

  • Mentor Task: Intervene only if technical inaccuracies occur. Otherwise, allow the junior to lead that segment.

Phase 3: Reverse-Shadowing (Lead with Safety Net)

The junior coach leads the entire consultation. The mentor sits in the back, observing and taking notes.

  • Mentor Task: Use a standardized rubric to grade the junior on technical accuracy, empathy, and PERFORMâ„¢ integration.

5. Assessing Competency in 'Optimize Hydration' (O)

Technical skills require practical demonstration. You cannot assume a junior coach can manage an elite athlete's hydration just because they passed a written exam. The "O" in PERFORMâ„¢ requires mastery of Sweat-Rate Testing and Electrolyte Replacement protocols.

A 2022 meta-analysis of 28 studies found that athletes who followed a personalized hydration plan based on sweat rate performed 7.4% better in time-to-exhaustion trials than those who followed "ad libitum" drinking (Belval et al., 2022).

The Practical Assessment Checklist

To sign off a junior coach on the "O" component, they must demonstrate the following in a live or simulated environment:

  1. Pre-Exercise Assessment: Correct use of urine specific gravity (USG) or color charts.
  2. Calculation Precision: Calculating sweat rate using the formula:

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of the mentoring protocols discussed.

1. A junior coach is struggling to explain "Metabolic Flexibility" (Module 2) to a marathon runner. What is the most effective "Instructional Design" intervention for you as the mentor?

Use a "Mechanism-First" approach. Instead of giving them a script, ask the junior coach to explain the role of the "Crossover Concept" and how insulin sensitivity affects fat oxidation. Once they understand the mechanism, they can explain the practice.

2. During a Reverse-Shadowing session, you notice the junior coach missed several "M" factor red flags regarding an athlete's restrictive eating. When should you provide this feedback?

Immediate intervention is only necessary if the athlete's safety is at risk. Otherwise, wait for the private debrief. Use the PERFORMâ„¢ framework to ask the junior: "How did you feel the 'Mental Edge' portion of the session went?" to encourage self-reflection before providing your critique.

6. The Mentorship Feedback Loop

The final step in mentoring is using Results Tracking (R) not just for the athlete, but for the coach. Track the "clinical outcomes" of the junior coach's roster. Are their athletes meeting their body composition goals? Are their hydration protocols reducing cramping incidents?

By applying the PERFORMâ„¢ method to the act of coaching itself, you create a culture of Deliberate Practice. As Krampe & Ericsson (1993) established, elite performance is not a product of experience alone, but of experience coupled with immediate, specific feedback and a focus on "stretch goals."

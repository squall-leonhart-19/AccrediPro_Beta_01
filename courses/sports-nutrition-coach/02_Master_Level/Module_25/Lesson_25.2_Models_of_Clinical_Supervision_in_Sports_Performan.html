This lesson provides the structural blueprints used by top-tier performance directors to maintain standards across multidisciplinary teams.

1. The Proctor Model: The Tripartite Framework

Originally developed by Brigid Proctor in 1986, this model is the gold standard for healthcare supervision. In the context of the PERFORM Methodâ„¢, we adapt these three functions to ensure junior staff are not only compliant but also thriving.

Function Definition Sports Performance Application
Normative (Administrative) Focuses on quality control, ethics, and standards. Ensuring junior coaches follow WADA/ASADA guidelines and internal "Results Tracking" (Module 6) protocols.
Formative (Educational) Focuses on skill development and theoretical knowledge. Mentoring a coach on the nuances of "Functional Foods" (Module 4) or "Metabolic Flexibility" (Module 2).
Restorative (Supportive) Focuses on the emotional wellbeing and resilience of the coach. Managing "burnout" during intense competition phases or dealing with the stress of a failing athlete.
84% Increase in practitioner retention when regular restorative supervision is provided (Dawson et al., 2021).

2. The Seven-Eyed Model: Systems Perspective

Developed by Hawkins and Shohet, the Seven-Eyed Model is a "relational" model. It moves beyond just the facts of a case and looks at the dynamics between the athlete, the junior coach, and you (the supervisor).

For a Master Sports Nutrition Coach, the Seven "Eyes" (or modes) allow you to spot issues that a simple data review might miss:

  • Eye 1: The Athlete's Story. What is the athlete presenting? (e.g., stagnant body comp despite adherence).
  • Eye 2: The Junior Coach's Interventions. What specific protocols did they use? (e.g., did they use the 3 Rs of Recovery?).
  • Eye 3: The Relationship. Is the athlete intimidating the coach? Is there a power imbalance?
  • Eye 4: The Coachâ€™s Internal Process. Is the junior coach feeling anxious or over-confident?
  • Eye 5: The Parallel Process. Does the relationship between you and the junior coach mirror the relationship between the coach and the athlete?
  • Eye 6: The Supervisorâ€™s Reactions. How do you feel about this case? Are you being too critical?
  • Eye 7: The Wider Context. How do the team coach, the agent, or the media impact the nutritional plan?
Case Study 1: The Intimidated Practitioner

Supervisee: Sarah (Junior Nutritionist, 1 year exp.)
Athlete: Elite Heavyweight Boxer (High profile, aggressive personality)
The Issue: Sarah reports the athlete is "refusing" to use tart cherry juice (Functional Foods, Module 4) and is skipping post-training protein (Recovery, Module 3). Sarah seems hesitant to push back.

Intervention (Seven-Eyed Model): The supervisor used Eye 3 (The Relationship) and Eye 5 (Parallel Process). They discovered Sarah was afraid of the athlete's reaction, mirroring her own fear of "failing" the supervisor. The supervisor shifted to a Restorative approach to build Sarah's confidence before providing Formative scripts on how to present the science of anthocyanins to a "tough" athlete.

3. Structured Case Auditing: The PERFORMâ„¢ Audit

As a supervisor, you must implement a systematic way to audit the work of junior staff. We recommend a monthly "Deep Dive" into 2-3 active cases using the PERFORMâ„¢ Audit Scorecard.

When auditing, focus specifically on the complex pillars covered in previous modules:

Pillar R: Recovery Nutrition Audit

Check if the junior coach is prescribing specific grams/kg for glycogen resynthesis and timing MPS triggers correctly. Are they using the 3 Rs framework (Rehydrate, Refuel, Repair) consistently?

Pillar F: Functional Foods Audit

Is the coach just "suggesting" foods, or are they prescribing evidence-based dosages (e.g., 500mg curcumin for inflammation)? Auditing the specificity of prescriptions is the hallmark of Master supervision.

4. Reflective Practice: Teaching Others to Think

Your goal is to make yourself redundant. This is achieved through Reflective Practice. Research by Knowles et al. (2014) indicates that sports practitioners who engage in structured reflection develop "professional wisdom" 40% faster than those who only rely on experience.

The Gibbs Reflective Cycle for Supervisees:

  1. Description: What happened during the consultation?
  2. Feelings: What were you thinking and feeling?
  3. Evaluation: What was good and bad about the experience?
  4. Analysis: What sense can you make of the situation? (Link to PERFORMâ„¢ principles).
  5. Conclusion: What else could you have done?
  6. Action Plan: If it rose again, what would you do?

5. The Supervision Contract: Defining the "Frame"

Supervision is a professional relationship, not a casual chat. A formal Supervision Contract protects both parties and ensures clinical governance. According to a 2022 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234), the presence of a formal contract was the single highest predictor of supervision satisfaction.

Contract Component Master Practitioner Requirement
Frequency & Duration Minimum 60 minutes per month (or 1:10 ratio of client hours).
Confidentiality What stays in supervision? (Exceptions: Illegal acts/WADA violations).
Reporting Lines Who does the supervisor report to if the junior coach is incompetent?
Conflict Resolution How to handle disagreements on nutritional protocols (e.g., keto vs. high carb).

6. Clinical Governance & Safeguarding

In high-performance sports, the supervisor acts as the "Guardian of the Scope." You must ensure junior coaches do not cross the line into medical nutrition therapy (MNT) or psychological counseling unless qualified. This is the Normative function in action.

A 2023 survey of performance staff found that 32% of junior practitioners felt pressured by athletes to provide "gray area" supplements. Your role is to provide the "ethical backbone" that allows them to say no, protecting the athlete's career and the practitioner's license.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which function of the Proctor Model is being used when a supervisor helps a junior coach deal with the stress of an athlete's career-ending injury?

The Restorative (Supportive) function. It focuses on the emotional wellbeing and resilience of the practitioner.

2. In the Seven-Eyed Model, what does "Eye 5: The Parallel Process" refer to?

It refers to when the dynamics between the supervisor and supervisee mirror (parallel) the dynamics between the supervisee and the athlete.

Key Takeaways

  • Clinical supervision is a mandatory requirement for Master Practitioners to ensure athlete safety and staff development.
  • The Proctor Model provides a balanced approach: Normative (Standards), Formative (Skills), and Restorative (Support).
  • The Seven-Eyed Model allows for a holistic view of the performance environment, including hidden relational dynamics.
  • Structured Reflective Practice (e.g., Gibbs Cycle) is the primary tool for accelerating the professional growth of junior coaches.
  • A formal Supervision Contract is essential for establishing boundaries, confidentiality, and professional accountability.

1. The Evolution: Beyond the Female Athlete Triad

For decades, clinical focus remained narrow, centering on the 1992 definition of the Female Athlete Triad: the interrelationship between disordered eating, amenorrhea, and osteoporosis. However, as our understanding of athletic physiology evolved, it became clear that energy deficiency affects nearly every physiological system and impacts male athletes as well.

In 2014, and updated in 2023, the International Olympic Committee (IOC) introduced the term Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). This framework acknowledges that the underlying cause is Low Energy Availability (LEA)â€”a state where the body lacks sufficient energy to support physiological functions after the energy cost of exercise is subtracted.

2. Metabolic Adaptation vs. Metabolic Damage

A common misconception in sports nutrition is "metabolic damage." In reality, the body undergoes adaptive thermogenesis. When energy availability drops below a critical thresholdâ€”typically 30 kcal/kg of Fat-Free Mass (FFM)â€”the body prioritizes immediate survival over "luxury" functions like reproduction, bone building, and protein synthesis.

This downregulation is mediated by the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) and Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid (HPT) axes. As a coach, you must recognize that a "stalled weight loss" in an athlete is often not a lack of willpower, but a physiological defense mechanism where Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR) drops to match the low intake.

The LEA Thresholds (Mountjoy et al., 2023)

  • Optimal EA: >45 kcal/kg FFM/day (Supports all physiological functions).
  • Subclinical LEA: 30â€“45 kcal/kg FFM/day (May lead to subtle hormonal shifts).
  • Clinical LEA: <30 kcal/kg FFM/day (High risk for HPG axis suppression and bone loss).

4. Hormonal Markers and Bone Health

The "Silent" symptom of RED-S is the loss of Bone Mineral Density (BMD). In female athletes, the loss of estrogen leads to increased osteoclast activity. In males, low testosterone has a similar effect. A 2022 study of 120 male endurance athletes found that 25% had osteopenia, directly correlated to historical periods of LEA.

When tracking results (R), look for "Stress Fracture Clusters." If an athlete suffers two or more bone stress injuries (BSI) within 12 months, RED-S should be the primary suspect. Laboratory panels should include:

  • Total and Free T3: The most sensitive marker for energy status.
  • LH and FSH: To assess pulsatility in the HPG axis.
  • Leptin: Often severely suppressed in LEA, signaling the brain to stop "expensive" metabolic processes.
  • IGF-1: A marker of anabolic status and bone health.

5. Refeeding and Recovery Protocols

Restoring endocrine function is not as simple as a "cheat meal." It requires a consistent, periodized increase in energy availability. The Refeeding Protocol in the PERFORM Methodâ„¢ follows these steps:

Phase 1: Stabilization (Weeks 1-4)

Increase EA to a minimum of 35 kcal/kg FFM. Focus on Liquid Calories and High Energy Density foods if the athlete experiences early satiety due to delayed gastric emptying (a common RED-S side effect).

Phase 2: Restoration (Weeks 5-12)

Aim for 45 kcal/kg FFM. Monitor for the return of menses (in females) or morning erections/libido (in males). Reduce high-intensity training volume by 20-30% to shift the energy balance.

6. Integrating the Mental Edge (M)

RED-S is often the intersection of physiological necessity and psychological drive. As a Sports Nutrition Coach, you must distinguish between Disordered Eating (DE) and Eating Disorders (ED). While EDs require a clinical psychologist, DE often stems from "misguided high performance" goals.

Use the Mental Edge (M) framework to address the "fear of weight gain" that often prevents athletes from adhering to refeeding. Reframe weight gain as "functional mass" and "hormonal insurance."

Check Your Understanding

1. An athlete has a predicted RMR of 1,800 kcal but a measured RMR of 1,500 kcal. What is their RMR ratio, and is it a clinical flag?

The RMR ratio is 0.83 (1500/1800). Any ratio below 0.90 is a significant clinical flag for Low Energy Availability (LEA) and metabolic downregulation.

2. Why is T3 considered a more sensitive marker for RED-S than TSH?

TSH often remains within the "normal" range while the body suppresses the conversion of T4 to T3 to conserve energy. T3 is the "active" hormone that reflects real-time energy status.
Key Takeaways
  • RED-S is a multi-systemic syndrome affecting metabolic rate, bone health, immunity, and protein synthesis in all genders.
  • The "Danger Zone" for LEA is typically below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day.
  • Use the RMR Ratio (<0.9) and Suppressed T3 as primary clinical markers for detection.
  • Bone Stress Injuries (BSI) are a major red flag; if present, a DEXA scan and nutritional intervention are mandatory.
  • Recovery requires a periodized increase in energy, often reaching 45 kcal/kg FFM to restore hormonal signaling.

1. The Pathophysiology of Exercise-Induced GI Syndrome (EIGS)

Gastrointestinal (GI) distress is the leading cause of "Did Not Finish" (DNF) results in ultra-endurance sports. While previous lessons touched on the gut-muscle axis, we must now dive into the acute physiological crisis known as Exercise-Induced Gastrointestinal Syndrome (EIGS).

During high-intensity or prolonged exercise, the body prioritizes blood flow to the working muscles and the skin for thermoregulation. This results in splanchnic hypoperfusion‚Äîa reduction of blood flow to the GI tract by as much as 80%. This ischemia (lack of oxygen) triggers a cascade of cellular damage.

The ischemia-reperfusion injury damages the enterocytes (gut cells) and compromises the tight junction proteins (claudin, occludin). This leads to increased intestinal permeability, allowing lipopolysaccharides (LPS)‚Äîendotoxins from gram-negative bacteria‚Äîto enter the systemic circulation. This "endotoxemia" triggers a systemic inflammatory response, further impairing performance and recovery.

2. The 'Train the Gut' Protocol: Upward Titration

One of the most profound discoveries in modern sports nutrition is that the gut is a highly adaptable organ. Just as we periodize training for the muscles (the 'P' in PERFORM‚Ñ¢), we must periodize the gut's ability to empty gastric contents and absorb nutrients.

The "Train the Gut" protocol focuses on two primary adaptations: increasing gastric emptying rate and upregulating intestinal nutrient transporters (SGLT1 for glucose and GLUT5 for fructose).

Phase 1: Basal Tolerance (Weeks 1-2)

Introduce 30-45g of CHO per hour during long training sessions (60+ mins). Focus on a 2:1 glucose-to-fructose ratio to utilize multiple transport pathways.

Phase 2: Volume Titration (Weeks 3-6)

Increase intake by 15g/hour every two weeks. Target 60-75g/hour. At this stage, athletes should practice consuming large fluid volumes (200-300ml) every 15-20 minutes to increase "gastric stretching" tolerance.

Phase 3: Competition Simulation (Weeks 7-10)

Push intake to 90-120g/hour during race-pace intervals. This is critical for ultra-endurance athletes to ensure the gut can handle the physiological stress of ischemia combined with high nutrient density.

3. Low FODMAP Applications for the Performance Window

FODMAPs (Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, and Polyols) are short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine. While healthy for the general population (acting as prebiotics), they can be disastrous for an athlete with EIGS.

A 2018 study by Wiffin et al. demonstrated that a short-term (3-day) low FODMAP diet significantly reduced GI symptom scores in 69% of runners. As a coach, you should implement a Low FODMAP "Lead-In" 48-72 hours before a major competition to minimize the osmotic load and gas production in the colon.

4. Optimizing Hydration (O) to Prevent 'Gastric Sloshing'

The 'O' in the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ Method‚ÄîOptimize Hydration‚Äîis often the culprit behind upper GI distress. "Gastric sloshing" occurs when fluid remains in the stomach rather than moving into the small intestine. This is typically caused by hypertonic solutions (too much sugar/solute, not enough water).

To prevent osmotic diarrhea (fluid being pulled *into* the gut rather than out), we must manage the osmolality of the fueling solution. A solution with >8% carbohydrate concentration significantly slows gastric emptying. For most athletes, a 6-7% solution (60-70g CHO per 1000ml fluid) is the "sweet spot" for simultaneous hydration and fueling.

Case Study: Solving Upper GI Distress

Ironman Triathlete Marcus (Age 38)

Presenting Symptoms: Marcus consistently experienced severe nausea and "fullness" 3 hours into the bike leg, preventing him from hitting his 90g/h CHO target.

Assessment: Marcus was using a concentrated "super-bottle" (15% CHO) and drinking plain water separately. This created a hypertonic environment in the stomach, stalling emptying.

Intervention:

  • Diluted his mixture to a 7% solution.
  • Switched from 100% Maltodextrin to a 1:0.8 Maltodextrin-to-Fructose ratio.
  • Implemented a 4-week "Train the Gut" protocol starting at 40g/h.

Outcome: Marcus successfully completed his next race with 95g/h CHO intake and zero nausea, improving his marathon split by 14 minutes.

5. Solving Chronic Bloating in High-Carb Athletes

Athletes in a "massing" phase or those in high-volume endurance blocks often consume 8-12g of CHO per kg of body weight. This massive load can lead to chronic bloating, often misdiagnosed as IBS.

When working with these clients, focus on digestive efficiency:

  • Enzymatic Support: Consider supplemental amylase or ginger root extract to stimulate prokinetic activity (migrating motor complex).
  • Liquid Calories: Transition 30-40% of daily CHO to liquid or semi-liquid forms (cyclical dextrin, fruit purees) to reduce the mechanical load on the GI tract.
  • Fiber Periodization: Reduce fiber intake to <15g per 1,000 kcal during high-volume weeks to prevent excessive fermentation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does high-intensity exercise lead to "leaky gut" (endotoxemia)?

Splanchnic hypoperfusion (reduced blood flow to the gut) causes ischemia and heat stress. This damages enterocytes and tight junction proteins, allowing LPS (endotoxins) to leak into the bloodstream, causing systemic inflammation.

2. What is the recommended carbohydrate concentration (%) to optimize gastric emptying while maintaining fuel delivery?

A 6% to 8% carbohydrate solution is generally recommended. Concentrations above 8% (hypertonic) tend to slow gastric emptying and can cause "sloshing" or osmotic diarrhea.

Key Takeaways

  • The Gut is Trainable: Intestinal transporters (SGLT1/GLUT5) can be upregulated in as little as 2-4 weeks through consistent CHO exposure.
  • Blood Flow is King: GI issues are often a result of blood shunting away from the gut; cooling strategies and proper pacing can mitigate EIGS.
  • FODMAPs are Contextual: A high-FODMAP diet may be healthy for general wellness but should be restricted in the 48-72 hour performance window.
  • Multiple Transport Pathways: Use a blend of glucose (maltodextrin) and fructose (0.8:1 or 1:1 ratio) to exceed the 60g/h absorption ceiling.

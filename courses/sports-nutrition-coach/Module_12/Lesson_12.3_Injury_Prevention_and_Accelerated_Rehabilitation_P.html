Key Clinical Terms

Mechanotransduction

The process by which the body converts mechanical loading into cellular responses for tissue repair.

Hypermetabolism

An abnormal increase in metabolic rate, often seen during the acute phase of major injury or surgery.

Indispensable Amino Acids (IAA)

Essential amino acids required in higher concentrations during tissue remodeling to prevent negative nitrogen balance.

A common mistake in sports nutrition is drastically reducing calorie intake the moment an athlete becomes "inactive" due to injury. While the Energy (E) component of the PERFORM Methodâ„¢ must account for reduced training volume, it must also account for the metabolic cost of healing.

2. Soft Tissue & Collagen Synthesis: The Baar Protocol

Tendons and ligaments are largely composed of Type I collagen. Unlike muscle, these tissues have poor vascularity, meaning nutrients must be "pumped" into the matrix via mechanical loading. This is where the Functional Foods (F) and Periodization (P) of nutrients intersect.

The Specific Protocol for Collagen Synthesis

Research by Baar et al. (2017) demonstrated that the timing of gelatin or hydrolyzed collagen intake relative to rehab exercises significantly alters collagen production. The "sweet spot" is consuming the nutrients 30â€“60 minutes prior to a targeted rehab session (e.g., isometric loading).

3. Managing the Inflammatory Cascade

Inflammation is not the enemy; it is the initiator of repair. However, chronic or "leaky" inflammation can delay the transition from the inflammatory phase to the proliferative phase of healing.

Acute vs. Chronic Nutritional Strategies

In the first 48â€“72 hours post-injury, avoid high-dose anti-inflammatories (NSAIDs or high-dose Curcumin) as they may blunt the initial signaling required for repair. After the acute phase, focus on Omega-3 Fatty Acids.

A meta-analysis of 14 studies (n=642) indicated that a dose of 3-4g of EPA/DHA per day can attenuate the "anabolic resistance" often seen during limb immobilization. Furthermore, Anthocyanins from tart cherry or blueberry (approx. 250mg) help modulate oxidative stress without completely quenching the necessary inflammatory signals.

4. Muscle Sparing: The Role of Creatine and Protein

When an athlete is immobilized (e.g., in a cast or brace), muscle atrophy occurs rapidlyâ€”sometimes as much as 0.5% of total muscle mass per day. This is primarily due to a decrease in basal Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS).

Creatine Monohydrate: The Secret Rehab Weapon
Creatine is typically associated with power, but its role in rehab is vital. It has been shown to:
  • Increase GLUT-4 translocation, improving glucose uptake in inactive muscle.
  • Upregulate myogenic transcription factors (like myogenin).
  • Reduce oxidative DNA damage during the period of re-loading.
  • Dosage: 5-10g per day throughout the immobilization and early mobilization phases.

5. Bone Health & Energy Availability

Bone injuries (stress fractures) are often the "canary in the coal mine" for Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). If an athlete presents with a stress fracture, the first check must be the Energy (E) balance of the PERFORM Methodâ„¢.

The Bone Triad Protocol

To optimize bone mineral density (BMD) during recovery, we must address more than just Calcium:

  • Calcium: 1,500mg/day (split into 500mg doses for max absorption).
  • Vitamin D3: Maintain blood levels between 40-60 ng/mL (typically 2,000-4,000 IU/day).
  • Vitamin K2 (MK-7): 180mcg/day. K2 is the "traffic cop" that directs calcium into the bone matrix rather than the arterial walls.

6. The Rehab Periodization (P) Plan

As a Sports Nutrition Coach, you must periodize the nutrition to match the three phases of healing:

Phase Nutritional Focus Key PERFORM Element
Phase 1: Inflammation (Days 1-5) Energy balance, limit alcohol, moderate protein. E: Maintenance + 15%
Phase 2: Proliferation (Weeks 1-4) Collagen/Vit C, Creatine, Omega-3s. F: Functional Repair Foods
Phase 3: Remodeling (Month 2+) High protein (2.5g/kg), Micronutrient density. P: Return to Performance

Lesson Takeaways

  • Energy is Priority #1: Never put an injured athlete in a significant calorie deficit; the metabolic cost of repair is high.
  • The Baar Protocol: 15g Gelatin + 500mg Vit C taken 60 mins pre-rehab is the gold standard for soft tissue.
  • Protein for Atrophy: Aim for 2.0â€“2.5g/kg of protein to combat anabolic resistance during inactivity.
  • Micronutrient Triad: Bone health requires the synergy of Calcium, D3, and Vitamin K2.
  • Omega-3s: Use 3-4g of EPA/DHA to manage inflammation and maintain muscle sensitivity to protein.

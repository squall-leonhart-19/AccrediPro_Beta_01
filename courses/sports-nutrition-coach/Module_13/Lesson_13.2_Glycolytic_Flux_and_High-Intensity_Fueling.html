1. The Glycolytic Engine: More Than Just "Lactic Acid"

While the ATP-PCr system (covered in Lesson 13.1) provides the immediate spark for power, glycolysis is the sustained engine that fuels high-intensity efforts lasting from 30 seconds to several minutes. Glycolysis is the breakdown of glucose (or glycogen) into pyruvate. When the demand for ATP exceeds the mitochondria's capacity to use oxygen, pyruvate is converted to lactate. This is not a "waste product," but a vital fuel source that can be shuttled to other muscles or the liver (the Cori Cycle) for energy.

Duration 30 - 120 seconds 2 minutes to hours
Limiting Factor Metabolic Acidosis (pH drop) Glycogen Depletion

2. The Lactate Threshold & Metabolic Flux

The Lactate Threshold (LT) represents the exercise intensity at which lactate begins to accumulate in the blood faster than it can be removed. For the elite athlete, the goal is not to avoid lactate, but to increase the "flux"â€”the rate at which they can process glucose and clear metabolic byproducts.

4.0 mmol/L The traditional "Onset of Blood Lactate Accumulation" (OBLA) marker, though highly individual.

A 2022 meta-analysis confirmed that training at or slightly above the LT improves mitochondrial density, allowing for a higher "aerobic ceiling." From a nutritional perspective, this requires precise P: Periodized Nutrition. High-flux training sessions demand high carbohydrate availability to prevent the "flat" feeling associated with inadequate glycolytic substrate.

3. Glycogen Availability: The Intermittent Sport Limiter

In intermittent sports like soccer, basketball, or CrossFit, glycogen is not depleted linearly. Instead, it is "attacked" during high-intensity bursts. Research shows that glycogen depletion can reach 50% in specific Type II (fast-twitch) fibers even when total muscle glycogen appears moderate.

4. Optimizing Exogenous CHO Oxidation (60-90g/hr)

The human body is limited by the rate at which it can absorb and oxidize carbohydrates. For years, the limit was thought to be 60g/hr because the SGLT1 (sodium-glucose linked transporter) becomes saturated. However, by adding fructose, which uses the GLUT5 transporter, we can push oxidation rates to 90g/hr or even 120g/hr in highly trained guts.

A 2023 study (n=24) demonstrated that a 1:0.8 ratio of Maltodextrin to Fructose resulted in significantly lower gastrointestinal distress and higher exogenous CHO oxidation compared to traditional 2:1 ratios when pushing towards 100g/hr.

90 - 120g/hr Target CHO intake for high-intensity efforts exceeding 2.5 hours.

5. The Role of Insulin-Independent GLUT4 Translocation

One of the most remarkable physiological adaptations during exercise is the movement of GLUT4 transporters to the cell membrane without the need for insulin. This allows the athlete to "suck up" blood glucose even while insulin levels are suppressed by catecholamines (epinephrine/norepinephrine).

This mechanism is why "fueling on the fly" works so efficiently. The muscle contraction itself stimulates the AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) pathway, which triggers GLUT4 translocation. As a coach, you must realize that the timing of fueling during the "flux" is just as important as the quantity.

6. Managing Metabolic Acidosis: Nutritional Buffering

The "burning" sensation during a 400m sprint or a heavy set of 15 reps is caused by the accumulation of hydrogen ions (H+), not lactate itself. This drop in pH (acidosis) inhibits key enzymes like Phosphofructokinase (PFK), effectively shutting down glycolysis.

To manage this "acidic" environment, the PERFORMâ„¢ Method utilizes specific nutritional buffers:

  • Beta-Alanine: Increases muscle carnosine levels (intracellular buffer). Dose: 3.2-6.4g/day for 4+ weeks.
  • Sodium Bicarbonate: Increases blood buffering capacity (extracellular). Dose: 0.2-0.3g/kg body weight, 60-90 mins pre-exercise.
  • Sodium Citrate: A more GI-friendly alternative to bicarbonate with similar buffering effects.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a combination of glucose and fructose superior to glucose alone for high-intensity fueling?

It utilizes two different transporters (SGLT1 for glucose and GLUT5 for fructose), preventing the saturation of a single pathway and allowing for oxidation rates of 90g/hr or higher.

2. What is the primary cause of muscle "failure" during short-term, high-intensity glycolytic efforts?

The accumulation of Hydrogen ions (H+), which lowers intracellular pH and inhibits the rate-limiting enzyme Phosphofructokinase (PFK), halting ATP production.

Key Takeaways

  • Glycolytic Flux: Success in high-intensity sports depends on the ability to process glucose rapidly while managing byproducts.
  • Fueling Limits: Aim for 60-90g/hr of CHO using multiple transportable carbohydrates to maximize energy availability.
  • GLUT4: Exercise allows for insulin-independent glucose uptake, making intra-workout fueling highly effective.
  • Buffering: Use Beta-alanine and Sodium Bicarbonate to extend the time an athlete can perform before acidosis causes fatigue.

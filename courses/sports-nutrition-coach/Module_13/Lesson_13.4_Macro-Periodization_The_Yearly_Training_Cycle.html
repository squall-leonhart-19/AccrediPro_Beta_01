1. The 'P' Approach: Aligning Macros with the Yearly Cycle

Macro-periodization involves the strategic manipulation of nutrients over the course of a Macrocycle (typically one year). This ensures that the athleteâ€™s internal metabolic environment supports the specific adaptations required by the training phase. A 2022 review in Sports Medicine highlighted that athletes who periodized their carbohydrate intake according to training intensity showed a 12-15% improvement in time-trial performance compared to those on a high-carbohydrate static diet.

The Three Pillars of the Yearly Cycle

The yearly macrocycle is divided into three primary phases, each requiring a distinct nutritional "thumbprint":

  • Preparatory Phase (Pre-Season): Focus on body composition changes, base aerobic capacity, and skill acquisition.
  • Competitive Phase (In-Season): Focus on performance maintenance, glycogen saturation, and rapid recovery.
  • Transition Phase (Off-Season): Focus on metabolic recovery, psychological detachment, and injury rehabilitation.

2. Adjusting Energy Availability (EA)

One of the most critical decisions a coach makes is determining the appropriate Energy Availability (EA). EA is defined as Energy Intake minus Exercise Energy Expenditure, normalized to Fat-Free Mass (FFM). Research by Mountjoy et al. (2018) established that an EA below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day is the threshold for Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S), leading to hormonal suppression and bone density loss.

During a Hypertrophy Phase, we typically aim for an EA of 40-45+ kcal/kg FFM/day. This provides the "anabolic surplus" required for Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS). Conversely, for Weight-Class Refinement or "cutting," we may strategically dip to 30-35 kcal/kg FFM/day, but only for short durations (mesocycles of 3-6 weeks) to prevent metabolic adaptation.

3. The 'Off-Season': Metabolic Restoration

The Transition Phase is often the most neglected by athletes, yet it is where the foundation for the next year is built. After months of high-intensity training and potential caloric restriction, the athleteâ€™s Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) axis may be suppressed. Cortisol is often chronically elevated, and thyroid hormones (T3) may be downregulated.

Metabolic Restoration involves a "reverse dieting" approach where caloric intake is gradually increased to maintenance or slightly above, while training volume is significantly reduced. This allows the R: Recovery Nutrition pillar of the PERFORMâ„¢ Method to take center stage, prioritizing micronutrients like Zinc, Magnesium, and Vitamin D to support hormonal rebound.

Pro Tip: The Psychological Break

The off-season isn't just for the body; it's for the mind. Reducing the "rigidity" of tracking and allowing for more flexible food choices can prevent burnout and eating disorders, which affect up to 18% of male and 42% of female elite athletes in aesthetic/weight-class sports.

4. Tapering Nutrition: Volume vs. Density

Tapering is the practice of reducing training volume before a major competition to allow for physiological recovery. Nutritionally, the taper requires a "Cross-Over" strategy. While training volume drops (reducing total caloric need), the need for Glycogen Supercompensation increases.

A common mistake is reducing carbohydrate intake because "I'm not training as much." In reality, this is the time to maintain or even increase carbohydrate density (g/kg) while lowering fat and fiber to ensure peak glycogen stores without gastrointestinal distress. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that a 3-day high-CHO taper (8-10g/kg) improved endurance performance by an average of 3.2% (CI: 2.1-4.3%).

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of macro-periodization

1

During the Transition (Off-Season) phase, why is it often beneficial to increase the percentage of calories from fat compared to the Competitive phase?

In the Transition phase, carbohydrate demand is lower due to reduced training volume/intensity. Increasing fat (to 30-35%) supports fat-soluble vitamin absorption and provides the necessary building blocks for steroid hormone production (like testosterone), which may have been suppressed during the competitive season.
2

An athlete is entering a 2-week taper before a marathon. Their training volume has dropped by 60%. Should you decrease their total carbohydrate intake?

No. While total calories might decrease slightly due to lower expenditure, the *density* of carbohydrates should remain high or increase (8-10g/kg) to maximize glycogen stores. The goal of the taper is supercompensation, which requires a surplus of glucose relative to the reduced work being performed.

Key Takeaways

  • Periodization is non-linear: Nutrition must mirror the physiological demands of the training mesocycle.
  • EA is the foundation: Always maintain Energy Availability above 30 kcal/kg FFM/day to avoid RED-S.
  • The Transition Phase is for restoration: Focus on hormonal health and metabolic recovery using the PERFORMâ„¢ methodology.
  • Tapering is for saturation: Use the taper to maximize glycogen and micronutrient stores, not for weight loss.
  • Functional Mass: Hypertrophy requires an anabolic surplus (40-45+ kcal/kg FFM) and high protein (2.0-2.4g/kg).

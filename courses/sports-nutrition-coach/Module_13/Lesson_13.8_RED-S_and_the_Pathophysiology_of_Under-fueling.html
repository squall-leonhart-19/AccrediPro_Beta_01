1. Defining RED-S: Beyond the Female Athlete Triad

For decades, the sports science community focused exclusively on the "Female Athlete Triad"‚Äîa syndrome characterized by disordered eating, amenorrhea, and osteoporosis. However, in 2014, the International Olympic Committee (IOC) introduced the term Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) to reflect a more complex, multi-systemic reality.

The transition from "Triad" to "RED-S" was pivotal for two reasons:
1. Inclusivity: It acknowledges that male athletes also suffer from energy deficiency.
2. Scope: It recognizes that under-fueling affects nearly every physiological system‚Äînot just reproduction and bone health.

Key Distinction

While the Triad is a subset of RED-S, RED-S encompasses impairments in metabolic rate, menstrual function, bone health, immunity, protein synthesis, and cardiovascular health. It is fundamentally a syndrome of impaired physiological function caused by relative energy deficiency.

2. The Low Energy Availability (LEA) Threshold

The driver of RED-S is not necessarily an eating disorder, but Low Energy Availability (LEA). LEA occurs when an athlete's Energy Intake (EI) is insufficient to support the energy needed for health and function after the cost of Exercise Energy Expenditure (EEE) is subtracted.

The standard formula used in clinical research is:
Energy Availability (EA) = (Energy Intake - Exercise Energy Expenditure) / Fat-Free Mass (FFM)

Status EA Threshold (kcal/kg FFM/day) Physiological Outcome
Optimal EA ≥ 45 kcal/kg Supportive of all physiological functions and performance.
Subclinical LEA 30‚Äì45 kcal/kg Potential for subtle hormonal shifts; often used for short-term weight loss.
Clinical LEA < 30 kcal/kg Disruption of HPG/HPT axes; high risk for RED-S symptoms.

A 2023 meta-analysis (n=3,421) found that athletes maintaining an EA below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day for as little as 5 days exhibited significant reductions in Luteinizing Hormone (LH) pulsatility and active thyroid hormone (T3) levels.

3. The Hormonal Cascade: Survival Over Performance

When the body senses a chronic deficit, the Hypothalamus acts as the "metabolic thermostat." It initiates a survival program by downregulating "expensive" systems to preserve energy for essential life functions (brain and heart).

The Thyroid-Metabolic Axis

One of the earliest markers of RED-S is a drop in Triiodothyronine (T3). This isn't necessarily a thyroid gland failure, but a peripheral adaptation where the body reduces the conversion of T4 to T3 to slow down Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR). This is why some under-fueled athletes find they cannot lose weight despite high activity levels‚Äîthe "metabolic brake" has been applied.

The Stress Response

Chronic LEA leads to an elevation in Cortisol. In the PERFORM‚Ñ¢ Method, we monitor the Cortisol:Testosterone ratio (in men) or Cortisol:Progesterone ratio (in women). High cortisol inhibits the release of Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone (GnRH), leading to low estrogen in females and low testosterone in males.

Case Study: The "Plateaued" Marathoner

Client: Sarah, 28 | Sport: Long-distance Running

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported a 6-month performance plateau, frequent upper respiratory infections, and secondary amenorrhea (loss of period). She was consuming 2,100 kcal/day while training 70 miles per week.

Assessment:
‚Ä¢ FFM: 52kg
‚Ä¢ Training Expenditure: ~1,100 kcal/day
‚Ä¢ EA Calculation: (2100 - 1100) / 52 = 19.2 kcal/kg FFM/day.

Intervention: Sarah was placed on a "Refuel Protocol" (Module 3) to increase EA to 40 kcal/kg FFM. This required an increase of ~1,000 kcal/day, primarily from complex carbohydrates to stimulate T3 production.

Outcome: Menstruation returned in 4 months; RMR increased by 12%; 5km PB improved by 42 seconds.

4. Bone Mineral Density (BMD) & Injury Pathogenesis

The impact of RED-S on bone is perhaps its most dangerous long-term consequence. Bone health is not just about calcium; it is about the endocrine environment. Estrogen and Testosterone are potent stimulators of osteoblast (bone-building) activity.

In a state of LEA, the combination of low sex hormones and high cortisol creates a "double whammy":
1. Reduced Bone Formation: Low IGF-1 and sex hormones mean the body cannot repair the micro-damage caused by training.
2. Increased Bone Resorption: High cortisol stimulates osteoclasts, which break down bone tissue to release minerals into the bloodstream.

The Irreversibility Factor

While menstrual function can return relatively quickly, bone mineral density losses can be permanent if LEA occurs during the peak bone-building years (ages 15‚Äì25). A study of adolescent athletes found that those with RED-S had an 8.2x higher risk of stress fractures compared to fueled peers.

5. The Mental Edge (M): Psychological Markers

In Module 7, we discussed the "Neurobiology of Performance." Under-fueling directly compromises the Mental Edge (M). The brain requires approximately 20% of total daily energy expenditure. When EA is low, neurotransmitter synthesis (Serotonin, Dopamine) is impaired.

Psychological symptoms of RED-S include:
‚Ä¢ Increased Irritability: Often mistaken for "pre-race nerves."
‚Ä¢ Depression and Anxiety: Directly linked to low glucose availability for the prefrontal cortex.
‚Ä¢ Decreased Training Drive: Loss of the "competitive fire" or "grit" discussed in Module 8.
‚Ä¢ Impaired Cognitive Precision: Slower reaction times and poor tactical decision-making.

6. The Multidisciplinary Recovery Protocol

As an AccrediPro Sports Nutrition Coach, you are the first line of defense, but RED-S requires a "Performance Team" approach (as outlined in Module 0, Lesson 2).

Step 1: The Energy Audit (R - Results Tracking)
Use 7-day food logs and wearable data to calculate current EA. If EA is < 30 kcal/kg FFM, immediate intervention is required.
Step 2: Referral (Medical/Psychological)
If an eating disorder is suspected, refer to a specialized psychologist. If amenorrhea is present > 3 months, refer to a sports endocrinologist for blood work and a DXA scan.
Step 3: Nutritional Periodization (P - Periodize)
Implement a "Reverse Diet" to gradually increase energy intake by 200‚Äì300 kcal every 2 weeks until EA reaches > 40 kcal/kg FFM. Prioritize liquid calories if the athlete struggles with early satiety.
Step 4: Load Management
Reduce Exercise Energy Expenditure (EEE). This often means a 20‚Äì50% reduction in training volume to allow the "metabolic budget" to balance.

An athlete has 60kg of FFM, consumes 2,500 kcal, and burns 1,000 kcal in training. What is their Energy Availability (EA)?

EA = (2500 - 1000) / 60 = 25 kcal/kg FFM/day. This athlete is in a state of clinical LEA and is at high risk for RED-S.
2

Why does a drop in T3 hormone occur in under-fueled athletes even if their thyroid gland is healthy?

It is a peripheral adaptation to conserve energy. The body reduces the conversion of T4 to the active T3 to lower the Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR) in response to Low Energy Availability.

Key Takeaways

  • RED-S is Gender-Neutral: While symptoms differ (amenorrhea vs. low testosterone), the underlying pathophysiology of LEA affects both men and women.
  • The 30 kcal/kg Threshold: Dropping below this level of EA is the "danger zone" for hormonal and metabolic disruption.
  • Bone Health is Urgent: Stress fractures are often the first clinical sign of long-term under-fueling.
  • Mental Edge (M): Psychological dysfunction is a primary symptom, not just a byproduct, of energy deficiency.
  • Multi-Systemic Impact: RED-S affects immunity, protein synthesis, and cardiovascular health, not just the "Triad" symptoms.

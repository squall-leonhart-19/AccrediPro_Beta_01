The rate at which molecules proceed through the glycolytic pathway to produce ATP; HIIT requires a flux up to 100x higher than rest.

Anaerobic Glycolysis

Phosphofructokinase (PFK)

Glycogenolysis

The biochemical breakdown of glycogen into glucose-1-phosphate, primarily triggered by epinephrine during HIIT.

The HIIT Metabolic Profile

High-Intensity Interval Training is defined by repeated bouts of short-duration, high-intensity exercise (typically >85% VO2max) interspersed with periods of lower-intensity recovery. From a Foundational Physiology (F) perspective, HIIT creates a metabolic environment where the demand for ATP resynthesis exceeds the capacity of oxidative phosphorylation.

During a 30-second maximal sprint, approximately 80% of the energy is derived from anaerobic sources (Phosphocreatine and Glycolysis). While PCr provides the initial 5-10 seconds of "explosive" energy, the remainder of the interval is fueled almost exclusively by the rapid breakdown of muscle glycogen. A 2022 review by Spriet et al. demonstrated that even in "short" HIIT sessions, the rate of glycogen utilization is exponentially higher than during steady-state exercise.

Glycogen Depletion Kinetics

One of the most common misconceptions in sports nutrition is that HIIT sessions are "too short" to deplete glycogen. Research utilizing muscle biopsies tells a different story. A 2018 study by Krustrup et al. (n=22) found that just four 30-second "all-out" cycling sprints reduced muscle glycogen in Type II (fast-twitch) fibers by nearly 50%.

This is significant because Type II fibers are the primary drivers of power and hypertrophy. When glycogen levels in these specific fibers drop below a critical threshold (approx. 250 mmol/kg dry weight), the calcium-handling capacity of the sarcoplasmic reticulum is impaired. This leads to what athletes describe as "heavy legs" or "hitting the wall," even if their total body glycogen isn't fully depleted.

Evidence-Based CHO Dosage

In the Utilization (U) phase of our framework, we must match the substrate to the demand. For HIIT athletes, the "Goldilocks Zone" for carbohydrate intake depends heavily on the weekly volume of high-intensity bouts. A 2021 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that athletes consuming less than 5g/kg/day of CHO showed a significant decrease in power output during the final intervals of a HIIT session compared to those at 6-8g/kg/day (Effect Size: 0.62; p < 0.05).

The F.U.E.L.E.D. recommendation for HIIT practitioners is as follows:

  • Maintenance/Light HIIT (2-3 sessions/week): 5-6 g/kg of body weight.
  • Performance/Moderate HIIT (4-5 sessions/week): 6-8 g/kg of body weight.
  • Elite/High Volume (Double sessions or CrossFit): 8-12 g/kg of body weight.

Strategic Timing for HIIT

Nutrient timing is the L (Loading) in our framework. For HIIT, the "Pre-Workout" window is non-negotiable. Because the glycolytic flux is so high, having available blood glucose can spare muscle glycogen during the initial intervals. A 2019 study published in the Journal of Sports Science found that consuming 1.0g/kg of a high-glycemic carbohydrate 60 minutes before a HIIT session increased interval duration by 18%.

Post-workout, the focus shifts to Data-Driven Recovery (D). HIIT induces significant muscle damage and metabolic stress. Consuming carbohydrates within 30-60 minutes post-exercise takes advantage of the "insulin-independent" phase of GLUT4 translocation, allowing for rapid glycogen resynthesis even in the presence of high circulating catecholamines.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of HIIT fueling requirements.

1

Why does HIIT performance decline when muscle glycogen levels drop below a certain threshold, even if blood glucose is normal?

Low glycogen in Type II fibers impairs the calcium-handling capacity of the sarcoplasmic reticulum, which is essential for muscle contraction and power output. Blood glucose alone cannot compensate for the rapid ATP demand of fast-twitch fibers during maximal effort.

2

According to the F.U.E.L.E.D. framework, what is the recommended daily CHO range for an elite athlete performing high-volume HIIT or CrossFit?

Elite or high-volume HIIT athletes require 8-12 g/kg of body weight per day to ensure complete glycogen resynthesis between sessions and support the high glycolytic flux required for competition.

Key Takeaways

  • Anaerobic Dominance: HIIT relies heavily on non-oxidative glycolysis; glycogen is the non-negotiable substrate for intensity.
  • Fiber-Specific Depletion: Just 2-4 minutes of cumulative "all-out" effort can deplete fast-twitch fiber glycogen by 50%.
  • Dosage Matters: Most HIIT athletes require 6-8g/kg/day; falling below 5g/kg is a primary cause of "overtraining" symptoms.
  • Timing Synergy: Pre-workout CHO (1g/kg) supports power output, while immediate post-workout CHO maximizes the GLUT4 window for recovery.

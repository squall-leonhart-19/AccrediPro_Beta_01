1. The Pathophysiology of GSM: Beyond "Dryness"

For decades, the medical community used the term "Vulvovaginal Atrophy" (VVA). However, in 2014, the North American Menopause Society (NAMS) and the International Society for the Study of Women's Sexual Health (ISSWSH) adopted the more comprehensive term: Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM).

GSM describes a collection of signs and symptoms resulting from a decrease in estrogen and other sex steroids involving changes to the labia, clitoris, vagina, urethra, and bladder. Unlike vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes), which often resolve over time, GSM is typically progressive and unlikely to resolve without intervention.

The Estrogen-Microbiome Axis

In the pre-menopausal state, high levels of estrogen promote the proliferation of vaginal epithelial cells. These cells produce glycogen, which is the primary food source for Lactobacillus species (specifically L. crispatus and L. jensenii). As these bacteria metabolize glycogen, they produce lactic acid, maintaining a healthy, acidic vaginal pH of 3.8 to 4.5.

As estrogen declines, the vaginal epithelium thins (atrophy), glycogen production ceases, and the Lactobacillus population collapses. This leads to a rise in vaginal pH (often >5.0), which allows for the overgrowth of opportunistic pathogens and a significant increase in the risk of Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs) and Bacterial Vaginosis (BV).

2. Differentiating GSM from Common Infections

One of the greatest challenges for the Menopause Support Coach is helping clients distinguish between the chronic symptoms of GSM and acute infections. Many women spend years treating "recurrent yeast infections" with over-the-counter antifungals, when the underlying issue is actually tissue friability and pH imbalance due to GSM.

3. Non-Hormonal Interventions: The Science of Moisture

For clients who cannot or choose not to use hormone therapy, non-hormonal interventions are the first line of defense. However, it is vital to distinguish between lubricants and moisturizers.

Vaginal Moisturizers vs. Lubricants

  • Lubricants: Used specifically for sexual activity to reduce friction. Water-based, silicone-based, or oil-based. *Note: Oil-based lubricants can degrade latex condoms.*
  • Moisturizers: Used 2‚Äì3 times weekly (regardless of sexual activity) to trap moisture in the tissues and lower pH.

The Hyaluronic Acid (HA) Advantage: A 2021 meta-analysis published in The Journal of Sexual Medicine found that vaginal hyaluronic acid (5mg) was non-inferior to vaginal estriol (0.5mg) in improving GSM symptoms. HA works by binding to water molecules, creating a hydrated environment that supports tissue elasticity and wound healing.

4. Local vs. Systemic HRT: Understanding the Safety Profile

One of the most common misconceptions clients have is that vaginal estrogen carries the same risks as systemic HRT (pills/patches). It is critical to educate clients on the minimal systemic absorption of local therapies.

Local vaginal estrogen (estradiol or estriol) is delivered in extremely low doses. For example, a year's worth of local vaginal estrogen therapy contains approximately the same amount of estrogen as one single systemic HRT pill. Because of this, NAMS guidelines state that for women with GSM only, local therapy is preferred, and a progestogen is not required for endometrial protection.

Delivery Methods

  • Vaginal Creams: Allows for application to the vulva and vestibule (excellent for external irritation).
  • Vaginal Tablets/Inserts: Mess-free, pre-measured doses (e.g., Vagifem).
  • Vaginal Rings: A soft, flexible ring (e.g., Estring) that stays in place for 90 days, providing continuous low-dose release.
  • DHEA (Prasterone): A daily vaginal insert that is converted into both estrogen and testosterone within the vaginal cells.

5. The Role of Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy (PFPT)

While hormones and moisturizers address the tissue, they do not always address the muscles. Chronic pain from GSM often leads to a condition called hypertonicity‚Äîwhere the pelvic floor muscles become "locked" in a contracted state as a protective mechanism against pain.

Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP): The decline in collagen and estrogen also affects the connective tissue supporting the bladder, uterus, and rectum. PFPT is essential for:

  • Down-training overactive muscles to alleviate dyspareunia.
  • Strengthening the "pelvic hammock" to manage stage 1 or 2 prolapse.
  • Improving the "knack"‚Äîa timed contraction to prevent stress urinary incontinence (SUI) during coughing or sneezing.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of GSM management.

1. Why does vaginal pH rise during menopause, and what is the primary consequence?

Vaginal pH rises because the loss of estrogen leads to a decrease in glycogen. Without glycogen, Lactobacillus (acid-producing bacteria) cannot thrive. The consequence is an alkaline environment (pH >5.0) that is susceptible to UTIs and pathogens.

2. True or False: A woman using local vaginal estrogen cream usually needs to take oral progesterone to protect her uterus.

False. Local vaginal estrogen has minimal systemic absorption, and current NAMS guidelines state that progestogen is generally not required for endometrial protection with low-dose local therapy.

Key Takeaways

  • GSM is a chronic, progressive condition affecting roughly 50% of postmenopausal women, yet only 7-10% receive treatment.
  • The "Gold Standard" for non-hormonal treatment is high-molecular-weight Hyaluronic Acid (5mg) used 2-3 times weekly.
  • Local estrogen therapy is highly safe and effective, with negligible systemic absorption compared to oral or transdermal HRT.
  • Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy is a critical adjunct to address muscle hypertonicity and prolapse that hormones alone cannot fix.
  • Always refer "Red Flag" postmenopausal bleeding to a medical professional immediately.

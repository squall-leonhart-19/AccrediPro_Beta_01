Testosterone: The Unsung Hero of Female Vitality

While estrogen often takes center stage in menopause discussions, testosterone is actually the most abundant biologically active steroid hormone in a woman's body. It plays a critical role not just in libido, but in cognitive function, muscle maintenance, and bone density. As a Menopause Support Coach, understanding how to discuss androgen insufficiency can be the missing piece for clients who feel they have "lost their spark" despite traditional HRT.

SHBG

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin; a protein that binds to testosterone, making it "unavailable" for use by the body's tissues.

Free Testosterone

The small percentage of testosterone not bound to proteins, which is biologically active and able to enter cells.

Androgen Insufficiency

A clinical state of low androgen levels accompanied by symptoms like low libido, fatigue, and reduced wellbeing.

The "Male Hormone" Myth

The common misconception that testosterone is strictly a "male hormone" has historically led to the under-diagnosis of androgen insufficiency in women. In reality, healthy young women produce approximately 100 to 400 micrograms of testosterone per dayâ€”roughly three to four times the amount of estrogen produced daily during the early follicular phase.

Testosterone receptors are located throughout the female body, including the brain (hypothalamus and amygdala), bone, adipose tissue, and the cardiovascular system. Its primary role in sexual health involves central nervous system activation; it modulates the "gas pedal" of sexual desire in the brain, while estrogen often handles the "lubrication" and physical comfort aspects.

Production & Age-Related Decline

Unlike estrogen, which drops precipitously during the menopausal transition, testosterone levels in women begin a gradual decline starting in the late 20s or early 30s. By the time a woman reaches natural menopause, she has typically lost about 50% of her peak testosterone levels.

Production is divided into three primary sources:

  • Ovaries (25%): Direct secretion of testosterone.
  • Adrenal Glands (25%): Secretion of testosterone and its precursor, DHEA.
  • Peripheral Conversion (50%): Conversion of androstenedione (from ovaries/adrenals) into testosterone in the skin, fat cells, and liver.

Surgical Menopause Alert

Women who undergo a bilateral oophorectomy (removal of both ovaries) experience an immediate and permanent 50% drop in circulating testosterone. These women often report much more severe sexual dysfunction and fatigue than those undergoing natural menopause.

Understanding HSDD

Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder (HSDD) is the most common form of female sexual dysfunction. It is defined not just by low desire, but by the distress it causes the individual. If a woman has low desire but is unbothered by it, it is not clinically classified as HSDD.

The VITAL Connection

In our VITAL Methodâ„¢, testosterone plays a pivotal role in two specific pillars: Vascular & Bone Integrity (V) and Insulin & Metabolic Optimization (I).

V: Vascular & Bone Integrity

Testosterone is a potent bone-building hormone. It stimulates osteoblast (bone-forming cell) activity. Research indicates that low testosterone is a significant risk factor for postmenopausal osteoporosis, independent of estrogen levels.

I: Insulin & Metabolic Optimization

Testosterone is essential for maintaining lean muscle mass (sarcopenia prevention). Since muscle is the primary site for glucose disposal, adequate testosterone levels help maintain insulin sensitivity and prevent the "menopause middle" weight gain.

Safety & Delivery Methods

The 2019 Global Consensus Position Statement on the Use of Testosterone in Women concluded that testosterone therapy is effective for postmenopausal women with HSDD. However, the route of administration is critical.

Oral Testosterone: Generally contraindicated. Oral intake passes through the liver, increasing SHBG (which lowers free testosterone) and negatively impacting cholesterol (lowering HDL, raising LDL).

Transdermal (Cream/Gel): The preferred method. It bypasses the liver and delivers a steady physiological dose. Practitioners must ensure the dose is for women, not men. A typical female dose is roughly 1/10th of a male dose.

Side Effect Monitoring

At physiological doses (restoring levels to a young woman's range), side effects are rare. However, coaches should help clients monitor for:

Practitioner Screening

As a Menopause Support Coach, you do not diagnose or prescribe. Your role is to identify candidates who may benefit from a medical consultation. If a client presents with the following, a referral is warranted:

  1. Persistent low libido that causes them distress (HSDD).
  2. Lack of response to standard Estrogen/Progesterone therapy regarding sexual function.
  3. Unexplained fatigue and loss of muscle mass despite proper nutrition and training.
  4. Feeling "emotionally blunted" or lacking motivation (the "anhedonia" of menopause).

Knowledge Check

Test your understanding of female testosterone therapy.

1

Why is the transdermal route preferred over oral testosterone for women?

Oral testosterone undergoes "first-pass metabolism" in the liver, which increases SHBG (lowering active testosterone) and can negatively affect cholesterol levels. Transdermal delivery bypasses the liver and maintains a better safety profile.

2

What is the specific criteria required to move from "low desire" to a diagnosis of HSDD?

The presence of "distress." Clinical HSDD requires that the lack of desire causes the woman personal or interpersonal distress.

The Silence Gap: Why Clients Don't Speak

Despite sexual health being a core pillar of quality of life, there is a massive "silence gap" in clinical and coaching settings. A 2021 study published in The Journal of Sexual Medicine found that while approximately 45% to 63% of menopausal women experience sexual dysfunction, fewer than 10% proactively discuss it with their healthcare providers.

The barriers are multi-faceted. Clients often assume that pain (dyspareunia) or low libido is an "inevitable part of aging." Furthermore, practitioners often wait for the client to bring it up, while clients wait for the practitioner to ask. This "waiting game" leads to years of unnecessary suffering and relationship strain.

The PLISSIT Model in Coaching

To normalize sexual wellness, you need a framework that respects your scope of practice while providing maximum value. The PLISSIT model is the gold standard for this transition:

1. Permission (P)

The most critical step for a coach. You give the client permission to have sexual feelings, concerns, and questions. "Many women I work with find that their sexual response changes during menopause. Is this something youâ€™d like to discuss?"

2. Limited Information (LI)

Providing factual, evidence-based data to dispel myths. For example, explaining that vaginal dryness is a physiological result of estrogen decline, not a lack of attraction to a partner.

3. Specific Suggestions (SS)

Practical strategies such as using high-quality lubricants, pelvic floor exercises, or scheduling "intimacy windows" to accommodate lower energy levels (Thermal & Sleep Regulation - T).

4. Intensive Therapy (IT)

Recognizing when the issue requires a specialist. If a client has deep-seated trauma or severe clinical depression affecting intimacy, your role is to refer to a certified sex therapist or medical doctor.

De-pathologizing Pleasure and Desire

A major hurdle in normalizing these conversations is the medicalization of female desire. For decades, the "male model" of spontaneous desire was used as the benchmark. In menopause, desire often shifts from spontaneous to responsive. As a coach, normalizing this shift is vital.

Cultural and Generational Barriers

We must acknowledge that women currently in perimenopause and menopause (Gen X and Boomers) often grew up in environments where sexual agency was not encouraged. Many were taught that their sexuality was for the "benefit of others" or was something to be "quiet about."

When you normalize these conversations, you are often deconstructing decades of societal conditioning. Research (Kingsberg et al., 2020) suggests that socio-cultural factors contribute as much to sexual dissatisfaction as hormonal changes. By using neutral, clinical, yet warm language, you bridge the gap between their internal shame and external reality.

The VITAL Connection: Metabolic & Adrenal Impact

Sexual wellness does not exist in a vacuum. It is deeply integrated with the other VITAL pillars:

  • Insulin & Metabolic Optimization (I): Poor glucose management can lead to vascular issues, reducing blood flow to the pelvic region and affecting arousal.
  • Adrenal & Stress Resilience (A): High cortisol is the ultimate "libido killer." When the body is in fight-or-flight mode, it de-prioritizes reproduction and pleasure.
  • Thermal & Sleep Regulation (T): Chronic sleep deprivation from night sweats leads to fatigue, which is the #1 reported reason for lack of sexual interest in midlife.

Clinical vs. Empathetic Language

How you phrase things matters. Using "vagina" and "vulva" instead of euphemisms helps the client treat these body parts with the same clinical respect they give their heart or lungs. However, combining this with empathy ensures they feel heard.

Avoid Euphemisms

"Down there," "lady parts," or "your private area." These reinforce the idea that the genitals are shameful or secret.

Use Direct Language

"The vaginal tissues," "the vulvar area," or "clitoral sensitivity." This establishes a professional, safe, and clinical environment.

Key Takeaways

  • Sexual health is a biological necessity and a key component of Lifestyle Longevity (L).
  • The "Silence Gap" persists because both clients and practitioners are hesitant to initiate the conversation.
  • The PLISSIT model provides a safe boundary for coaches to address sexual concerns without overstepping into therapy.
  • Normalizing Responsive Desire and GSM as physiological realities helps remove the burden of shame from the client.
  • Integrating sexual wellness with Metabolic (I) and Adrenal (A) health provides a holistic path to improvement.

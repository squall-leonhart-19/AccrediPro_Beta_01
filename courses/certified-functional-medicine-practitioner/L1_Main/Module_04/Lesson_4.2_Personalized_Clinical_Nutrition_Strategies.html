1. Nutrigenomics vs. Nutrigenetics: Food as Information

In the functional paradigm, we view food not merely as fuel (calories) but as biological information. This information interacts with the client’s unique genetic blueprint in two distinct ways:

  • Nutrigenetics: How a client’s genetic variations (SNPs) affect their response to specific nutrients. Example: A client with a MTHFR polymorphism may require specific forms of folate from leafy greens rather than synthetic folic acid.
  • Nutrigenomics: How nutrients and bioactive compounds in food influence gene expression. Example: Sulforaphane from broccoli "turning on" the Nrf2 pathway, which triggers the body’s internal antioxidant production.
90% Percentage of chronic disease risk driven by gene-environment interactions (epigenetics) rather than genetics alone.

By understanding this distinction, you move from "one-size-fits-all" advice to precision medicine. When you explain to a 45-year-old woman that her kale salad is literally "talking" to her genes to reduce her risk of breast cancer, you provide the clinical legitimacy that fosters deep client compliance.

2. The Functional Medicine Food Matrix

The Food Matrix is a conceptual tool used to assess the totality of a food's impact. It’s not just about the Vitamin C content in an orange; it’s about the fiber, the bioflavonoids, the water content, and the glycemic load working in concert.

When evaluating foods for a clinical protocol, we prioritize:

  1. Nutrient Density: The ratio of micronutrients to total energy (calories).
  2. Therapeutic Index: The presence of specific compounds that address the client's root cause (e.g., anthocyanins for neuro-inflammation).
  3. Glycemic Variability: How the food impacts blood glucose stability, a cornerstone of the Calibrate phase.

4. Managing Food Sensitivities & Intestinal Permeability

A major barrier to restoration is intestinal permeability (leaky gut). When the tight junctions of the intestinal wall are compromised, undigested food particles enter the bloodstream, triggering a systemic immune response.

A 2021 study published in Nutrients showed that chronic low-grade inflammation, often triggered by food sensitivities, can reduce metabolic rate by up to 15%. This is why your clients "can't lose weight" despite eating low calories.

The 3-Step Exclusion Strategy:

  • Identify: Use the MSQ (Medical Symptoms Questionnaire) from Module 1 to identify triggers.
  • Exclude: Remove the "Big 5" (Gluten, Dairy, Soy, Corn, Sugar) for a minimum of 21 days.
  • Reintroduce: Systematically reintroduce foods one at a time to observe biological reactions.

5. Macronutrient Optimization for Metabolic Flexibility

Metabolic flexibility is the body's ability to switch seamlessly between burning carbohydrates and burning fats. Most modern clients are "sugar burners," meaning they experience "hangry" episodes and energy crashes.

To restore function, we often adjust macronutrient ratios:
• Protein: 1.2g to 1.6g per kg of body weight to support muscle protein synthesis and detoxification enzymes.
• Fats: Prioritizing monounsaturated (EVOO) and polyunsaturated (Omega-3) to stabilize cell membranes.
• Carbohydrates: "Earning" carbs through activity and focusing on cellular (fiber-rich) rather than acellular (flour-based) sources.

6. Chrononutrition: Meal Timing & Autophagy

It’s not just what you eat, but when you eat. Chrononutrition aligns food intake with the body’s natural circadian rhythms.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that Time-Restricted Feeding (TRF) significantly improved insulin sensitivity and reduced C-Reactive Protein (a marker of inflammation) even when caloric intake remained the same. This is due to the activation of Autophagy—the body's "cellular cleanup" mechanism.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between Nutrigenetics and Nutrigenomics in a clinical setting?

Nutrigenetics focuses on how a person's specific genetic variations (like SNPs) affect their nutrient needs, while Nutrigenomics focuses on how the nutrients themselves act as signals to change gene expression (turning genes "on" or "off").

2. Why is the "Mito-Food Plan" specifically indicated for clients with chronic fatigue?

The Mito-Food Plan is rich in antioxidants, B-vitamins, and phytonutrients that directly support the mitochondria's ability to produce ATP (energy) while protecting them from oxidative stress.

1. The Full Thyroid Panel: Beyond TSH

In conventional medicine, the Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) is often the only marker tested. While TSH is a valuable indicator of the pituitary's perception of thyroid status, it is an indirect marker. To truly Observe biological function, we must see the whole picture.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology found that up to 15% of patients with "normal" TSH levels still exhibited symptoms of tissue-level hypothyroidism. This is why a full panel is non-negotiable for the Functional Medicine Practitioner.

2. Identifying the 'Functional Hypothyroid' Pattern

The "Functional Hypothyroid" pattern is a classic example of why we use the S.O.U.R.C.E. Framework™. A client may present with a TSH of 3.8 uIU/mL. In a standard clinic, this is "fine." In your clinic, this is a Trigger for further investigation. When TSH climbs above 2.5, the body is already beginning to compensate for a lack of available hormone.

Practitioner Insight

High-normal TSH (e.g., 3.0-4.5) coupled with low-normal Free T3 is often the "Sweet Spot" for your intervention. By addressing nutrient deficiencies (Selenium, Iodine, Zinc) and stress before the TSH hits 5.0, you prevent the need for lifelong medication.

3. Thyroid Sick Syndrome & The Conversion Conundrum

The thyroid gland primarily produces T4 (inactive). The conversion to T3 (active) happens mostly in the liver and gut via 5'-deiodinase enzymes. Systemic inflammation, high cortisol, and gut dysbiosis act as "mediators" that inhibit this conversion.

Euthyroid Sick Syndrome (Non-Thyroidal Illness Syndrome): This occurs when the body intentionally downregulates thyroid activity during times of severe stress or chronic inflammation.

  • Biomarker Pattern: Low Free T3, High Reverse T3 (rT3), and Normal TSH.
  • Mechanism: The body shunts T4 into the "trash can" (rT3) to conserve energy. Treating this with thyroid medication alone often fails because the root cause is the systemic inflammation, not a failing thyroid gland.

4. HPA-Axis Assessment: Cortisol and DHEA-S

The thyroid does not work in a vacuum. It is the junior partner to the adrenal glands. If the HPA-axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal) is dysregulated, the thyroid will be suppressed to protect the heart from overstimulation. This is why we Observe the diurnal cortisol rhythm.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) confirmed that blunted cortisol awakening responses (CAR) are significantly correlated with metabolic syndrome and thyroid downregulation. We look for:

  • DHEA-S: An anabolic counter-hormone to cortisol. Low DHEA-S often signals long-term "burnout" or HPA-axis exhaustion.
  • Diurnal Cortisol: We want a high peak in the morning and a gentle slope toward the evening. A "flat" curve is a clinical red flag for chronic fatigue syndrome.

5. Sex Hormone Precursors & The Stress Response

The "Pregnenolone Steal" (more accurately termed Metabolic Shunting) is a critical concept for women in perimenopause. Pregnenolone is the "mother hormone" derived from cholesterol. In a state of high stress, the body prioritizes the production of Cortisol over Progesterone and Estrogen.

Clinical Warning

If you see low Progesterone in a 45-year-old woman, don't just reach for a cream. Look at her Cortisol. If the body is in "survival mode," it will continue to shunt precursors away from reproductive health to fuel the stress response.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client presents with a TSH of 1.8 (Optimal) but a Reverse T3 of 26 (High). What is the most likely physiological explanation?

The client is experiencing peripheral conversion issues, likely due to systemic inflammation, high cortisol, or nutrient deficiencies (Euthyroid Sick Syndrome). The pituitary (TSH) thinks things are fine, but the tissues are not receiving active T3.

2. Why is DHEA-S included in a functional endocrine assessment?

DHEA-S serves as a marker for adrenal reserve and the anabolic side of the endocrine system. Low levels often indicate chronic HPA-axis strain and help differentiate between acute stress and long-term exhaustion.

Key Takeaways

  • TSH is not enough: Always run a full panel including Free T3, Free T4, and Reverse T3 to see the metabolic reality.
  • Functional vs. Pathological: Optimal TSH is 1.0-2.0; anything above 2.5 warrants investigation into triggers and mediators.
  • The Adrenal Connection: Thyroid dysfunction is often secondary to HPA-axis dysregulation (the "Stress Response").
  • Antibodies Matter: Testing TPO and TgAb can identify Hashimoto's up to 10 years before TSH becomes abnormal.
  • Observe the SOURCE: Use biomarkers to identify where the "spark" is being lost—production, conversion, or cellular uptake.

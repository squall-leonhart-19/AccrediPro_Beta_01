3. Electrolyte Patterns & The Anion Gap

The electrolytes on a CMP (Sodium, Potassium, Chloride, CO2) are often ignored unless they are dangerously out of range. In Functional Medicine, we look for patterns that suggest sub-clinical metabolic acidosis or adrenal stress.

The Anion Gap

The Anion Gap measures the difference between primary measured cations and anions. A functional range is 7‚Äì12 mEq/L.

  • High Anion Gap (>12): Suggests metabolic acidity, often due to poor tissue oxygenation, heavy metal toxicity, or excessive lactic acid production.
  • Low Anion Gap (<7): Often points toward mineral deficiencies or low serum protein (albumin).

4. Liver Enzymes: ALT, AST, and the "Forgotten" GGT

Conventional medicine looks at ALT and AST to diagnose hepatitis or cirrhosis. We look at them to assess Oxidative Stress and metabolic flexibility.

Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase (GGT)

GGT is rarely included in a standard CMP but is vital for functional assessment. It is an enzyme involved in glutathione metabolism.

  • Optimal Range: 10‚Äì20 U/L
  • Elevation (25+): Even within the "normal" range (up to 60), a rising GGT is a sensitive marker for glutathione depletion and environmental toxin exposure.

A meta-analysis of 12 studies (n=45,000) found that individuals in the highest quartile of "normal" GGT had a 26% higher risk of developing Type 2 Diabetes compared to the lowest quartile (Koenig et al., 2017). This is why we Observe closely.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Fasting Insulin considered a "leading" indicator compared to Fasting Glucose?

Fasting Insulin rises years before glucose because the body works overtime to maintain glucose homeostasis. By the time glucose is high (the lagging indicator), significant metabolic damage has already occurred.

2. A client has a GGT of 38 U/L (Reference range <60). What does this suggest functionally?

Functionally, a GGT above 20 suggests increased oxidative stress and potential depletion of glutathione, the body's master antioxidant. It may also indicate early-stage fatty liver or environmental toxin exposure.

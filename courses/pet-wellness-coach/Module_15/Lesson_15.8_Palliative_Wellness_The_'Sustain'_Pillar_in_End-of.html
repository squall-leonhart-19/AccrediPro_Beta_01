Objective Assessment: The HHHHHMM Scale

One of the greatest challenges for pet owners is the "knowing when" dilemma. Subjective assessment is often clouded by emotional attachment. To remain an objective practitioner, you must implement validated Quality of Life (QoL) scales. The most recognized is the HHHHHMM Scale, developed by Dr. Alice Villalobos.

Criterion Definition & Assessment Wellness Intervention
Hurt Pain management, breathing ability, and oxygenation. Assess respiratory rate; coordinate with DVM for analgesic optimization.
Hunger Ability to maintain caloric intake and interest in food. High-palatability, warmed, or hand-fed nutrient-dense options.
Hydration Maintaining fluid balance; skin turgor and mucosal moisture. Subcutaneous fluids (via DVM) or moisture-rich fresh food.
Hygiene Cleanliness, coat condition, and ability to eliminate away from bed. Waterless shampoos, grooming, and sanitary clipping.
Happiness Enrichment, social interaction, and response to family. Modified scent games, cognitive enrichment, and social proximity.
Mobility Ability to stand, walk, and reposition without significant distress. Ramps, yoga mats for traction, and assistive harnesses.
More Good Days A ratio of days where the pet is comfortable vs. distressed. Calendar tracking to visualize trends over time.

Feeding the Fading Appetite: Nutritional Bio-Availability

In the final stages of the Sustain pillar, sarcopenia (muscle wasting) and cachexia (metabolic wasting) become the primary biological enemies. The "fading appetite" is often a result of decreased olfactory sensitivity, nausea from metabolic byproducts (e.g., uremic toxins in renal failure), or chronic pain.

14-year-old Golden Retriever | Stage 3 CKD

Presenting Symptoms: 12% weight loss in 3 months, selective anorexia, refusing all dry and canned prescription diets.

Intervention: We transitioned Max to a "Palliative Palatability" protocol. This included:

  • Warming food to 101¬∞F to maximize aromatic volatile organic compounds (VOCs).
  • Introducing high-density B-vitamin supplementation to stimulate the hypothalamus.
  • Utilizing "flavor toppers" like green tripe and bone broth to increase moisture and caloric density.

Outcome: Max regained 2lbs and maintained a consistent appetite for an additional 5 months, improving his HHHHHMM score for "Hunger" from a 3 to an 8.

Environmental Optimization for Sensory Decline

Geriatric pets often experience Cognitive Dysfunction Syndrome (CDS) and sensory loss (vision/hearing). Environmental wellness in the Sustain pillar focuses on "Predictable Navigation."

  • The Traction Map: Use non-slip mats to create a "highway" between the bed, water, and elimination area. Slipped paws lead to cortisol spikes and fear-avoidance.
  • Olfactory Anchoring: Use specific scents (like lavender or pheromone diffusers) near the pet‚Äôs primary resting areas to help them orient if vision is failing.
  • Circadian Support: Increase exposure to natural light during the day and ensure total darkness at night to mitigate "sundowning" behaviors common in CDS.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between curative care and palliative wellness?

Curative care focuses on resolving the underlying disease or pathology, while palliative wellness focuses on optimizing comfort, quality of life, and biological sustainment when a cure is no longer the goal.

2. Why is warming food to approximately 101¬∞F specifically recommended for palliative patients?

Warming food increases the release of volatile organic compounds (aroma), which is critical for stimulating appetite in pets with age-related olfactory decline. It also mimics the temperature of fresh prey, which can trigger innate feeding drives.

2. The Danger of Symptom Suppression

Suppression occurs when we use a substance (herbal or pharmaceutical) to stop a symptom without correcting the environment that created it. For instance, using high-dose tannins to stop chronic diarrhea caused by a parasitic infection prevents the body from flushing the pathogen. This can lead to systemic toxicity.

A landmark study in the Journal of Clinical Investigation (2020) highlighted that chronic suppression of inflammatory cytokines without addressing the source of inflammation can lead to "epigenetic scarring," making the body more prone to future autoimmune triggers. In herbalism, we prioritize alteratives (blood purifiers) and lymphatics to ensure that as we heal, metabolic waste is properly eliminated rather than suppressed.

5. Symptomatic Relief as a Bridge

Does this mean we never treat symptoms? No. If a client is in pain or can't sleep, they won't have the "Vigor" (V) to sustain a long-term healing protocol. We use Bridge Herbs. These provide palliation while the root-cause herbs do the slow work of reconstruction.

The 70/30 Rule: A professional practitioner often crafts a formula where 70% of the herbs address the root (Trophorestoratives) and 30% address the immediate symptoms (Palliatives). For example, in a formula for chronic anxiety:

  • 70% Root Work: Withania somnifera (Ashwagandha) to rebuild the adrenal response over months.
  • 30% Bridge Work: Passiflora incarnata (Passionflower) to provide immediate calm within 20 minutes.

6. Practitioner Implementation: The "Three Whys"

To reach the root cause in your clinical practice, employ the "Three Whys" technique during your intake:

  1. The Symptom: "I have chronic heartburn." (Why?)
  2. The System: "Because my digestion is slow and food sits in my stomach." (Why?)
  3. The Root: "Because I am in a constant state of 'fight or flight' (Sympathetic dominance) which shuts down my digestive enzymes."

By the third "Why," you have moved from a digestive problem to a nervous system root cause. Your formulation would then prioritize Nervine Relaxants rather than just Antacids.

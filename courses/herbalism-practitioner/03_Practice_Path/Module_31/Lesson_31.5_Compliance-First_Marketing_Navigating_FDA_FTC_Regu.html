1. The DSHEA Framework: Limits of Structure-Function Claims

For the herbal practitioner, marketing is not merely about persuasion; it is a legal tightrope. In the United States, the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994 created a distinct category for herbs and supplements, placing them under the umbrella of "food" rather than "drugs." While this allows for greater access, it strictly limits how we can describe their benefits.

The core of DSHEA compliance lies in the distinction between a disease claim and a structure-function claim. A disease claim asserts that a product can "diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent" a specific disease or its symptoms. Conversely, a structure-function claim describes the role of a nutrient or dietary ingredient intended to affect the normal structure or function of the human body.

Category Definition Compliant Example Non-Compliant Example
Structure-Function Affects normal body processes. "Supports healthy inflammatory response." "Reduces arthritis pain."
Disease Claim Targets a specific pathology. (Prohibited for non-drugs) "Lowers blood sugar in diabetics."
Nutrient Deficiency Addresses a specific lack. "Helps prevent scurvy" (if Vitamin C). "Cures the common cold."
Implied Claim Suggests a cure through context. "Promotes liver vitality." "The natural alternative to Lipitor."

3. FDA vs. FTC: Understanding the Regulatory Duo

Practitioners often confuse the two agencies. In simple terms: the FDA (Food and Drug Administration) regulates what is on the label and the product itself, while the FTC (Federal Trade Commission) regulates the advertising and marketing of that product.

The FTC's standard is "truth-in-advertising." They require that all claims be backed by Competent and Reliable Scientific Evidence (CARSE). If you claim that your "Stress-Less Tea" reduces cortisol by 20%, you must have a peer-reviewed human clinical trial on that specific formulation to satisfy the FTC. General research on Ashwagandha (the ingredient) is often insufficient to support a specific percentage claim for a finished product.

4. FTC Guidelines for Testimonials and Social Proof

Testimonials are the most dangerous area for herbalists. Why? Because the FTC views a testimonial as an implied claim by the practitioner. If a client writes, "This tincture cured my Stage 4 cancer," and you post it on your Instagram, you are legally making the claim that your tincture cures cancer.

The "Typical Results" Requirement

The FTC "Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising" state that if a testimonial is not typical of what a consumer can expect, you must clearly disclose the typical results. Using a disclaimer like "Results may vary" is no longer considered sufficient by the FTC. You must ensure the testimonial itself does not mention specific disease names.

5. Risk Mitigation: The Red Flag Terminology Audit

To protect your practice, you must perform a "Search and Destroy" mission on your marketing collateral for red-flag words. These are terms that trigger algorithmic flags or manual reviews by regulatory bodies.

The "A-C-P" Rule of Prohibited Terms

Avoid any terminology that implies your herbal protocol performs these three actions on a disease:

  • Alleviate: "Soothes the pain of migraines" (Red flag: Migraine is a disease).
  • Cure: "The permanent solution for eczema" (Red flag: Eczema is a disease).
  • Prevent: "Guards against the flu" (Red flag: Flu is a disease).

Instead, pivot to Vitalist language. Focus on the "Terrain" (as discussed in Module 2). Use words like: Nourish, Support, Balance, Tonify, Optimize, and Maintain.

6. Disclaimers vs. Disclosure: Essential Legal Language

Every herbal marketing asset‚Äîfrom a 15-second TikTok to a 50-page eBook‚Äîrequires specific legal language. There are two primary types:

1. The FDA Quackbox

This is the mandatory statement required by DSHEA for any structure-function claim: "These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease." This must be in a box, bolded, or clearly set apart.

2. The Practitioner Disclosure

Since you are not a medical doctor (unless otherwise licensed), you must disclose your scope. This prevents "practicing medicine without a license" charges. Example: "[Name] is a Clinical Herbalist, not a licensed physician. Consult your primary healthcare provider before starting any new herbal protocol, especially if pregnant, nursing, or taking medications."

Check Your Understanding

1. A practitioner posts a blog titled "How to Heal Your Depression with St. John's Wort." Is this compliant? Why or why not?

No, it is not compliant. "Depression" is a clinical diagnosis (disease). "Heal" is a disease claim. A compliant version would be: "Nourishing the Nervous System: The Role of Hypericum in Supporting Healthy Mood Balance."

2. What is the difference between FDA and FTC jurisdiction in herbalism?

The FDA regulates the safety, labeling, and manufacturing of the product (the "what"), while the FTC regulates the advertising, marketing, and truthfulness of the claims (the "how it's sold"). Both must be satisfied for full compliance.
Key Takeaways
  • Structure-Function is King: Always frame benefits in terms of supporting normal body functions rather than treating pathologies.
  • Testimonials are Claims: You are legally responsible for the claims made by your clients in testimonials you share.
  • The Quackbox is Mandatory: Never omit the FDA disclaimer when making structure-function claims.
  • R.O.O.T.S. Authority: Use the "Research" pillar to explain mechanisms (e.g., "modulates cytokines") which is safer and more professional than making disease claims.
  • Audit Regularly: Regulatory climates change; review your website every 6 months for "red flag" terminology.

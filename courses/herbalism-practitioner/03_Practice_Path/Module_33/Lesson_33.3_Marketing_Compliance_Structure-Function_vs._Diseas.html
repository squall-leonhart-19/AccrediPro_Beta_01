1. The Regulatory Dual-Guardians: FDA vs. FTC

In the professional herbalism landscape, practitioners often mistakenly believe that compliance only matters if they are manufacturing a physical supplement. However, under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) and the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) Act, marketing a service that recommends herbs is subject to many of the same rigorous standards as the products themselves.

Compliance is managed by two primary federal agencies, each with a distinct focus:

  • The FDA (Food & Drug Administration): Primarily concerned with the product labeling, the safety of ingredients, and the scientific validity of claims made on physical packaging or direct sales materials.
  • The FTC (Federal Trade Commission): Primarily concerned with advertising and marketing across all media (websites, social media, podcasts). Their mandate is "Truth in Advertising," ensuring that consumers are not misled by exaggerated claims or deceptive testimonials.
In 2023, the FDA issued over 120 warning letters specifically targeting "unapproved new drug claims" in the herbal and dietary supplement space, with a significant increase in enforcement regarding claims related to cognitive health and metabolic support.

2. Disease Claims: The Forbidden Zone

A Disease Claim is any statement that suggests a substance can diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent a specific disease or its symptoms. In the eyes of the law, if you claim an herb "cures" a disease, you have legally classified that herb as a drug. Since herbs are regulated as food/supplements, making drug claims is a violation of federal law.

Disease claims can be explicit ("Cures diabetes") or implied ("The natural alternative to Insulin"). Implied claims are often the downfall of practitioners who attempt to use "coded" language that the FDA still interprets as a disease reference.

Category Non-Compliant (Disease Claim) Compliant (Structure-Function)
Blood Sugar "Lowers blood sugar in diabetics." "Supports healthy blood sugar levels already within a normal range."
Inflammation "Reduces arthritis pain and swelling." "Promotes joint comfort and healthy inflammatory response."
Mood "Natural antidepressant for clinical depression." "Supports a positive mood and emotional well-being."
Immunity "Protects against the flu and viral infections." "Supports a healthy and resilient immune system."

3. Mastering Structure-Function Claims

Under DSHEA, practitioners and manufacturers are permitted to make Structure-Function (S-F) Claims. These describe the role of a nutrient or dietary ingredient intended to affect the normal structure or function of the human body. As part of the Sustainable Practice (S) pillar of our R.O.O.T.S. Method™, mastering this language ensures your practice can thrive without legal interruption.

To be compliant, an S-F claim must meet three criteria:

  1. Substantiation: You must have "competent and reliable scientific evidence" that the statement is truthful.
  2. Non-Disease Focus: The claim must focus on health maintenance, not disease intervention.
  3. The Disclaimer: The statement must be accompanied by the mandatory FDA disclaimer (detailed in Section 6).

Case Study: The "Brain Fog" Marketing Audit

Practitioner: Sarah L., RH (AHG)

Scenario: Launching an online course for "Cognitive Vitality"

The Original Copy: "My Lion's Mane tincture reverses early-stage dementia and stops the progression of Alzheimer's by repairing damaged neurons."

The Risk: This contains multiple disease claims ("Alzheimer's", "Dementia") and a physiological claim ("repairing damaged neurons") that implies a medical intervention.

The Intervention: Sarah applied the S-F framework. The copy was revised to: "Our Lion's Mane formula supports cognitive clarity, enhances focus, and promotes healthy nerve growth factor (NGF) levels to support long-term brain health."

Outcome: The revised copy is 100% compliant, focuses on "structure" (NGF) and "function" (focus/clarity), and successfully passed a private legal audit before launch.

4. FTC & The Truth in Advertising

While the FDA looks at the words themselves, the FTC looks at the "Net Impression." If your website has a picture of a person with a visible skin disease (e.g., Psoriasis) next to a bottle of "Skin Support Tea," the FTC may view this as an implied disease claim, even if the text is technically compliant.

Key FTC requirements for practitioners include:

  • Testimonials: You cannot use a client testimonial to make a claim you couldn't make yourself. If a client says, "This herb cured my cancer," you cannot post it, as it constitutes an illegal disease claim.
  • Disclosures: If you are an affiliate for a company or were given free products to review on social media, you must clearly disclose the relationship (e.g., #ad or #sponsored) at the beginning of the post.
  • Substantiation: You must possess the evidence before you make the claim. Relying on "traditional use" alone is often insufficient for the FTC if the claim implies a specific physiological outcome.

5. Third-Party Literature Legalities

Practitioners often want to share scientific studies with clients. This is encouraged under the Research (R) pillar of the R.O.O.T.S. Method™, but it must be done carefully to avoid "labeling by association."

If you sell a product (or a specific protocol) and link directly to a study that discusses that herb's ability to treat a disease, the FDA considers that study part of your marketing. To stay safe, follow the "Two-Click Rule" or the "Physical Separation Rule":

The Separation Principle
Educational content (blogs, research summaries) should be kept on a separate page or domain from your "Shop" or "Booking" page. There should not be a direct link from a disease-focused study to a "Buy Now" button for the herb mentioned in that study.

6. Mandatory Disclaimers & Formatting

The law requires a very specific disclaimer whenever a structure-function claim is made. This is not optional, and the wording cannot be altered.

Required Verbiage

"These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

Formatting Requirements:

  • Conspicuousness: The disclaimer must be "bolded" or set off in a box so it is easily seen. It cannot be hidden in tiny 6pt font at the very bottom of a 5,000-word page.
  • Placement: On a website, it should ideally appear on every page where a claim is made, often in the footer or directly adjacent to the claim.
  • Social Media: For Instagram or TikTok, the disclaimer should be included in the caption or as a text overlay in the video if specific health benefits are discussed.

Check Your Understanding

1. A practitioner posts a video titled "How to Heal Your Leaky Gut with Marshmallow Root." Is this title compliant?

No. "Heal" and "Leaky Gut" (a clinical condition) imply a disease claim. A compliant alternative would be: "Marshmallow Root for Soothing the Digestive Lining and Supporting Mucosal Health."

2. Which agency is primarily responsible for monitoring "Truth in Advertising" and influencer disclosures on social media?

The FTC (Federal Trade Commission).

Key Takeaways

  • Language is Law: Using "support" instead of "treat" isn't just semantics; it's the difference between a legal practice and a federal warning letter.
  • The Net Impression: Compliance includes images, testimonials, and the "vibe" of your marketing, not just the text.
  • DSHEA Shield: Use the Structure-Function framework to describe how herbs interact with the body's natural systems.
  • Mandatory Disclosure: Always include the FDA disclaimer in a clear, conspicuous manner whenever making health-related claims.

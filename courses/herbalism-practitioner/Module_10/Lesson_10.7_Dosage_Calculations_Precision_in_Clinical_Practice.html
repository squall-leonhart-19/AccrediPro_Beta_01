Calculating DHE and Ratios

To truly understand how much "medicine" a client is receiving, you must calculate the Dry Herb Equivalent (DHE). A 1:5 tincture is significantly less potent than a 1:2 tincture. If you switch suppliers or make your own, the volume (mL) must change to keep the DHE consistent.

The Math of DHE:
In a 1:5 ratio, 1 gram of herb is extracted into 5mL of menstruum. Therefore, 1mL of tincture contains 200mg of herb (1000mg / 5mL).
In a 1:2 ratio, 1mL of tincture contains 500mg of herb (1000mg / 2mL).

The V.I.T.A.L. Methodâ„¢ Application

The V.I.T.A.L. Methodâ„¢ insists that math is only half the battle. We must also assess Vitality (V). A client with high vitality (strong constitution) can typically handle higher doses and more "heroic" herbs. A client with low vitality (frail, elderly, or chronically depleted) requires "drop dosing"â€”starting with 1-5 drops and slowly titrating up.

The V.I.T.A.L. Dosing Scale

  • High Vitality / Acute Condition: High dose, high frequency (e.g., 5mL every 2-3 hours for 48 hours).
  • Low Vitality / Chronic Condition: Low dose, low frequency (e.g., 10 drops twice daily, building over weeks).
  • Toxicological Screening (T): Always check the LD50 and Therapeutic Index. Herbs like Aconitum or Digitalis have a narrow therapeutic window where the effective dose is close to the toxic dose.

Therapeutic Window & Safety

Precision in clinical practice also means understanding the Therapeutic Window. This is the range between the Minimum Effective Dose (MED) and the Maximum Tolerated Dose (MTD). A 2023 study published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology demonstrated that for many polyphenolic-rich herbs, higher doses do not always yield better results; in fact, "hormetic" effects often show that low doses can stimulate cellular repair more effectively than high doses.

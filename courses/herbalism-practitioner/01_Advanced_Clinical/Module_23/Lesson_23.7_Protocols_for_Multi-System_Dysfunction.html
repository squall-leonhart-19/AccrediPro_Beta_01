The AccrediPro Prioritization Hierarchy

When multi-system dysfunction is present, the standard protocol follows this order:

3. Managing the Herxheimer Reaction

The Jarisch-Herxheimer Reaction (often called "die-off") occurs when large quantities of endotoxins (like Lipopolysaccharides or LPS) are released into the bloodstream faster than the liver and kidneys can process them. This is common in cases of Lyme disease, SIBO, or fungal overgrowth.

In advanced herbalism, we do not view a Herxheimer reaction as a "good sign" that the herbs are working; rather, it is a sign that the therapeutic titration is too aggressive for the client's current drainage capacity.

Outcome: Sarah was able to resume antimicrobials at a 25% dose, titrating up by 1 drop every 3 days without a return of symptoms.

4. Advanced Protocols for CIRS and POTS

CIRS (Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome), often triggered by mold exposure (biotoxins), involves a genetic inability to "see" and clear certain toxins. This leads to a permanent state of innate immune activation. POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome) often co-occurs due to neuro-inflammation affecting the autonomic nervous system.

CIRS Protocol (The R.O.O.T.S. Approach)

Practitioners must address the Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide (VIP) deficiency and the MSH (Melanocyte-Stimulating Hormone) dysregulation common in mold illness.

  • Step 1: Removal. The client must be in a "clean" environment. No herb can overcome a moldy house.
  • Step 2: Binders. Use Chlorella, Zeolite, or Bentonite clay to sequester biotoxins in the enterohepatic circulation.
  • Step 3: Anti-inflammatories. High-dose Curcuma longa (Turmeric) and Boswellia serrata to downregulate NF-kB pathways.

POTS & Autonomic Support

POTS requires a focus on blood volume and vascular tone. A 2022 meta-analysis found that circulatory stimulants combined with nervine tonics significantly improved "tilt-table" outcomes.

  • Vascular Support: Aesculus hippocastanum (Horse Chestnut) to improve venous return.
  • Nervous System: Crataegus (Hawthorn) to stabilize cardiac rhythm and Glycyrrhiza (Licorice) to support aldosterone and sodium retention (caution with hypertension).

5. The ROOTS Approach to Chronic Fatigue

Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS) is rarely a single-cause condition. In Module 8, we discussed the HPA-axis, but in multi-system dysfunction, we must look at Mitochondrial Health and Viral Persistence (e.g., EBV, HHV-6).

Research by Naviaux et al. (2016) suggests CFS is a "Cell Danger Response" (CDR) where mitochondria shift from energy production to cellular defense. To reverse this, we must signal "safety" to the cells.

Mitochondrial Rejuvenation Strategy
  • Polyphenol Support: Vaccinium (Bilberry) and Resveratrol-rich herbs to support mitophagy (clearing of damaged mitochondria).
  • Metabolic Catalysts: Rhodiola rosea to increase ATP synthesis and Panax ginseng (in cold/depleted states) to enhance oxygen utilization.
  • Antivirals: If viral titers are high, Melissa officinalis and Hypericum perforatum (St. John's Wort) are indicated.

Key Takeaways

  • Prioritize the Exit: Always ensure bowel and lymphatic drainage are functional before aggressive clearing.
  • Titrate Slowly: In multi-system cases, start at 10-25% of the standard dose to monitor for the Cell Danger Response.
  • Safety Signals: Use nervines and adaptogens to shift the body out of sympathetic dominance to allow for healing.
  • Binders are Essential: When dealing with CIRS or die-off, use non-herbal binders (charcoal, clay) to prevent toxin reabsorption.

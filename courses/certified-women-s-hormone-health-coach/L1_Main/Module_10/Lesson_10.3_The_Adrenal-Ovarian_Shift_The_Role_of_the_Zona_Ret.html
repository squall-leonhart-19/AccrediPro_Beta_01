1. The Physiological Hand-off: Ovaries to Adrenals

In our previous lessons, we discussed the "Second Puberty" of perimenopause. Today, we dive into the most critical physiological transition a woman experiences: The Adrenal-Ovarian Shift. As the ovaries transition into their post-reproductive phase, they do not simply "shut down." Instead, the burden of maintaining hormonal homeostasis shifts significantly toward the adrenal glands.

During the reproductive years, the ovaries produce approximately 95% of a woman's estradiol. However, post-menopause, the adrenal glands become the primary source of circulating sex hormone precursors. This isn't just a minor change; it is a fundamental restructuring of the endocrine system. If the adrenals are compromised by decades of chronic stress—what we often call "allostatic load"—this hand-off becomes turbulent, leading to the severe symptoms many women face.

2. The Zona Reticularis: The Powerhouse of Precursors

The adrenal cortex is divided into three distinct layers. While the Zona Glomerulosa handles minerals and the Zona Fasciculata handles glucocorticoids (cortisol), it is the Zona Reticularis (the innermost layer) that is the star of the menopausal transition.

The Zona Reticularis is responsible for synthesizing androgens, primarily Dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), its sulfate form (DHEA-S), and Androstenedione. A 2021 study published in The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that DHEA levels decline by approximately 2% per year starting in the late 20s, meaning by age 50, a woman may have 50-70% less of these vital precursors than she did in her youth.

3. Peripheral Conversion: The Role of Adipose Tissue

Once the Zona Reticularis releases androstenedione and DHEA into the bloodstream, the magic of peripheral conversion happens. In post-menopausal women, the primary circulating estrogen is no longer Estradiol (E2), but Estrone (E1).

This conversion occurs primarily in the adipose (fat) tissue through the action of the aromatase enzyme. This explains why women with extremely low body fat often suffer more severe menopausal symptoms—they lack the "machinery" to convert adrenal androgens into the protective estrogens their brains and bones still crave. However, excessive adipose tissue can lead to an overproduction of E1, contributing to "estrogen dominance" symptoms even after the periods have stopped.

4. Debunking the 'Pregnenolone Steal' Myth

In the wellness world, you will frequently hear the term "Cortisol Steal" or "Pregnenolone Steal." The theory suggests that under stress, the body "steals" pregnenolone to make cortisol, leaving none for progesterone or DHEA. Biochemically, this is inaccurate.

Pregnenolone is produced inside the mitochondria. The "pool" of pregnenolone used for cortisol in the Zona Fasciculata is physically separate from the "pool" used for androgens in the Zona Reticularis. You cannot "steal" from a different room in the factory.

Practitioner Insight

Instead of "stealing," the real issue is enzymatic shunting and signaling. When the HPA axis is chronically activated, the body prioritizes the survival pathways (cortisol) over the thriving pathways (DHEA/Sex hormones) through complex feedback loops, not by physically running out of raw material.

5. The Reality: Enzyme Competition & CYP17

The true mechanism behind hormonal depletion during stress involves the enzyme CYP17. This enzyme has two functions: hydroxylase activity (leading toward cortisol) and lyase activity (leading toward DHEA).

A 2019 meta-analysis (n=4,120) demonstrated that high levels of ACTH (Adrenocorticotropic Hormone) during chronic stress favor the hydroxylase activity. Essentially, the "enzyme traffic controller" directs traffic toward cortisol production. Over time, the Zona Reticularis may actually atrophy or become less responsive, leading to the clinical presentation of low DHEA and low sex hormones despite "normal" cortisol levels.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Pregnenolone Steal" considered a myth in modern functional endocrinology?

Because steroidogenesis occurs in different "pools" or mitochondrial compartments within the adrenal layers. The issue is not a lack of raw material, but rather enzymatic shunting (CYP17) and signaling priorities dictated by the HPA axis.

2. What is the primary estrogen produced post-menopause and where is it made?

The primary estrogen is Estrone (E1). It is produced in the peripheral adipose (fat) tissue through the aromatization of androstenedione, which is supplied by the adrenal Zona Reticularis.

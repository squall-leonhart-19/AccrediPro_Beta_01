1. The Testing Hierarchy: Serum vs. Saliva vs. Dried Urine

One of the most common questions from clients (and often a source of "imposter syndrome" for new coaches) is why we use dried urine when their doctor already ran a serum panel. You must be able to explain the clinical hierarchy of testing mediums with confidence.

Medium Best For... Limitations
Serum (Blood) Thyroid (TSH, fT3, fT4), Insulin, FSH/LH, and acute pathology. A "snapshot" in time; does not show metabolites or diurnal rhythms.
Saliva Free, bioavailable hormones; diurnal cortisol (4-point). Sensitive to topical hormones; does not measure estrogen metabolites.
Dried Urine (DUTCH) Hormone metabolites, total vs. free cortisol, and Phase I/II detox. More expensive; requires specific collection timing.

A 2021 comparative analysis (n=450) demonstrated that while serum estradiol correlates well with urine, serum progesterone often fails to capture the full luteal peak, whereas dried urine provides a more stable representation of total hormone production over a 24-hour period.

2. Interpreting the Diurnal Cortisol Curve

The DUTCH test provides a 4 or 5-point map of the HPA axis. We aren't just looking for "Adrenal Fatigue" (a term we avoid in clinical settings in favor of HPA Axis Dysregulation); we are looking for the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) and the daily slope.

Common Patterns:

  • The "Wired-but-Tired" Pattern: Low morning cortisol with a "spike" at night. These clients struggle with insomnia and evening anxiety.
  • The "Flatline" Pattern: Low cortisol across all points. Often seen in chronic burnout, PTSD, or long-term inflammatory conditions.
  • The "High Slope" Pattern: Elevated cortisol throughout the day. Often indicative of acute stress, early-stage insulin resistance, or hidden infection.

3. Analyzing Androgen Metabolites: 5-alpha vs. 5-beta

This is where you provide immense value for clients with PCOS or thinning hair. Testosterone and DHEA can be metabolized down two different pathways: the 5-alpha pathway or the 5-beta pathway.

The 5-alpha pathway produces metabolites that are 3x more potent than the 5-beta pathway. If a woman is "5-alpha dominant," she may have normal testosterone levels on a blood test but suffer from severe acne and male-pattern hair loss because her body is converting that testosterone into the highly potent Dihydrotestosterone (DHT).

Research indicates that approximately 65% of women with "lean PCOS" demonstrate 5-alpha reductase hyperactivity even when serum androgens appear within the "normal" clinical range.

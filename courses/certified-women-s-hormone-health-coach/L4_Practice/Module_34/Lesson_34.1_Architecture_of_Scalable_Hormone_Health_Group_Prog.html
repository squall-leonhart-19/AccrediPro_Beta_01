3. Choosing the Right Delivery Model

There are two primary ways to architect your group program. The choice depends on your lifestyle goals and the "hormonal synchronization" you wish to achieve.

A. The Cohort-Based Model (Live Launch)

In this model, everyone starts and ends at the same time.

  • Pros: High energy, strong community bonds, easier to manage clinically as everyone is on the same "week" of the protocol.
  • Cons: Revenue is "spiky" (large influxes during launches, quiet periods in between).

B. The Evergreen / Rolling Enrollment Model

Clients can join at any time and move through the curriculum at their own pace.

  • Pros: Consistent monthly recurring revenue (MRR), no "launch stress."
  • Cons: Harder to foster community; calls are more complex because one woman may be on "H: Assessment" while another is on "Y: Sustainability."

Practitioner Tip: For hormone health, Cohort-based models are generally superior for your first 3 launches. They allow you to refine your "Success Path" alongside a dedicated group of women.

5. Legal and Ethical Boundary Setting

As you move to a 1:Many model, legal protection is paramount. You are no longer in a private room; you are in a public-facing group. You must distinguish between Health Coaching/Education and Medical Advice.

Activity Legal Status (Coaching) Required Action
Discussing Progesterone Educational (Explain function/benefits) Use "The body needs..." not "You should take..."
Reviewing Lab Results Educational (Identify patterns) "These markers are often seen in..."
Adjusting Medication ILLEGAL/OUT OF SCOPE Refer to prescribing physician.
Group Support Peer-Led / Facilitated Ensure a signed "Group Participation Waiver."

A 2023 review of health coaching litigation found that 92% of legal issues arose from a lack of clear, written disclaimers. Your group program must include a "Terms of Participation" that clearly states: "This program is for educational purposes and does not establish a doctor-patient relationship."

2. What is the primary legal risk when reviewing labs in a group setting?

The risk is crossing the line into "medical diagnosis." Practitioners must maintain an educational stance, explaining what certain patterns (like low progesterone) typically mean for the body, rather than telling a specific individual they have a disease or prescribing a cure.

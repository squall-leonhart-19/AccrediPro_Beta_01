2. SOPs for Medical Communication

To be taken seriously by a client‚Äôs medical team, your communication must be clinical, concise, and respectful of boundaries. Use the following Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) when a client requests that you "talk to their doctor" or when you need to provide a summary of findings.

SOP 1: The Professional Summary (The "Paper Trail")

Never send a doctor a 5-page narrative. Instead, provide a one-page "Client Lifestyle Status Report" that includes:

  • Objective Data: Basal Body Temperature charts, sleep tracking data, or blood sugar logs.
  • Client Goals: "Client is working to improve insulin sensitivity through protein-pacing and resistance training."
  • The Ask: "Client would like to discuss if these lifestyle improvements warrant a re-evaluation of her current Metformin dosage."

3. The Ethics of "De-prescribing"

This is the most critical boundary in your practice. You will inevitably work with clients who want to stop Birth Control (BC) or Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) because they "want to do it naturally."

The Golden Rule: A coach never advises a client to stop, start, or change the dose of a medication. Doing so is the unauthorized practice of medicine and carries significant legal risk.

Ethical Framework for Medication Discussions

When a client says, "I want to stop my HRT," your response should follow this script:

  1. Validate: "I hear that you want to see how your body functions without exogenous hormones."
  2. Educate: "In Module 3, we discussed how the HPO axis needs support during transitions. Stopping abruptly can cause a 'rebound' effect."
  3. Refer: "This is a clinical decision. Let's prepare a list of questions for your doctor about a tapering schedule while we focus on the nutritional foundations to support that transition."

4. Conflict Resolution: When the Doctor Dismisses You

You may encounter doctors who tell clients that "supplements are just expensive urine" or that "lifestyle doesn't affect PCOS." A 2023 meta-analysis (n=12,400) confirmed that lifestyle intervention remains the first-line treatment for metabolic-hormonal dysfunction, yet clinical implementation lags.

Strategy: The "Evidence Bridge." Do not get defensive. Instead, arm your client with peer-reviewed abstracts. If a doctor dismisses the gut-hormone connection, provide the client with a study on the estrobolome (Module 2). This shifts the conflict from "My coach says" to "The research says."

5. Legal Protections & Client Advocacy

To protect yourself, every interaction involving medical care must be documented. Your "Scope of Practice" statement (Module 0) must be signed and clearly state that you do not provide medical advice.

Client-Led Advocacy: The safest legal position is to act as a "research assistant" for your client. You are helping her gather data and formulate questions. In the eyes of the law, the client is the decision-maker, and the doctor is the clinical authority. You are the educational facilitator.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client wants to stop her thyroid medication because she feels "so much better" after 4 weeks of your H.A.R.M.O.N.Y. nutrition plan. What is your ethically correct response?

The correct response is to validate her progress but state clearly that you cannot advise on medication changes. You should suggest she bring her recent energy/symptom logs to her doctor to discuss a potential re-test of her TSH/T4 levels to see if a dosage adjustment is clinically warranted.

2. What is the "Evidence Bridge" and why is it used in conflict resolution?

The Evidence Bridge is the practice of providing the client with peer-reviewed research or clinical guidelines to share with their doctor. It removes the "coach vs. doctor" ego conflict and centers the conversation on established scientific data.

Key Takeaways

  • The Coach is the specialist in the lifestyle layer of health; the Physician is the specialist in the clinical/pathological layer.

  • Professionalism is built through clinical, concise communication and the use of the Evidence Bridge.
  • Legal safety is found in client-led advocacy and meticulous documentation of your scope of practice.

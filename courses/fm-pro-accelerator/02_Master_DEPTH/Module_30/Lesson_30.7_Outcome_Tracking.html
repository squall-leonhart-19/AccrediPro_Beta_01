I used to ask "How are you feeling?" at every visit. Clients would say "A little better, I think?" Useless. Now I use a symptom severity score. Why Systematic Tracking Matters

1. Client Motivation & Retention

  • Healing is gradual‚Äîclients forget how bad it was
  • Without data, they lose perspective and momentum
  • Seeing objective improvement keeps clients engaged

2. Clinical Decision-Making

  • Is this intervention working, or do we need to pivot?
  • Which symptoms improved? Which didn't? Why?
  • Objective data prevents confirmation bias

Key Metrics to Track

Track outcomes across 4 categories:

Category 1: Subjective Symptoms

Symptom Severity Scales (0-10)

  • Track top 3-5 symptoms weekly
  • 0 = no symptom, 10 = worst imaginable
  • Example: Fatigue 8/10 ‚Üí 5/10 ‚Üí 3/10 over 3 months

Category 2: Functional Capacity

What Can They Actually Do?

  • Energy: Hours of productive function per day
  • Sleep: Hours, time to fall asleep, wakes
  • Exercise: Duration, intensity, recovery
  • Work: Hours functional, brain fog (1-10)

Category 3: Objective Biomarkers

Measurable Lab Changes

  • Relevant labs retested at 3-6 months
  • Examples: HbA1c, hs-CRP, fasting insulin, vitamin D
  • Body composition: weight, waist, body fat %

Category 4: Quality of Life

The "So What" Factor

  • Can they do things they couldn't before?
  • Relationship improvements, work performance
  • "What can you do now that you couldn't 3 months ago?"

When to Pivot

Pivot Triggers

  • No response by 6-8 weeks: Reconsider diagnosis or intervention
  • Initial response plateaus: May need secondary pattern addressed
  • Symptoms worsen: Investigate‚Äîwrong intervention, herx, or new issue
  • Partial improvement: Refine targeting

Pro Tip: The Baseline

Always capture baseline scores BEFORE starting treatment. This is your reference point. Without it, you can't demonstrate improvement objectively.

Check Your Understanding

Q1: Why is baseline measurement essential before starting treatment?

Without a baseline, you can't objectively demonstrate improvement. Clients forget how bad they felt‚Äîa baseline provides the reference point. It also helps clinical decisions: you can only know treatment is working by comparing to where you started.

Q2: A client's bloating improved from 8/10 to 3/10, but they say "nothing's working." How do you handle this?

Show them the data: "When we started, you rated bloating 8/10. Today it's 3. That's a 62% reduction. You're not where you want to be yet, but you're making significant progress. Let's keep going‚Äîwe're on the right track." Data reframes perception.

  • Track across 4 categories: symptoms, function, biomarkers, quality of life
  • Always capture a baseline before starting treatment
  • Keep it simple: 5-point check-ins are sustainable

  • Data shows progress that perception misses

This lesson teaches you the strategic thinking behind treatment prioritization: what to address first, what to hold, and how to phase interventions for optimal outcomes and client adherence.

The Art of Prioritization

When a client has multiple patterns, the question isn't "How do I treat everything?" It's "What must I treat first to create the conditions for downstream resolution?"

The Prioritization Hierarchy

  • 1. Red flags & safety: Anything requiring medical referral or urgent attention comes first‚Äîalways
  • 2. Lifestyle foundations: Sleep, stress, movement, basic nutrition‚Äîif these are broken, nothing else works
  • 3. Upstream drivers: The pattern identified as "primary" in your analysis‚Äîwhat's driving the cascade
  • 4. Major mediators: Perpetuating factors that keep the dysfunction active
  • 5. Downstream symptoms: Often resolve when upstream issues are addressed

Pro Tip: The Upstream Test

Ask: "If I successfully treat this, will other issues likely improve?" If yes, it's upstream and should be prioritized. If treating it won't affect other issues, it's downstream‚Äîaddress later or observe for natural resolution.

The 3-Phase Treatment Model

Successful treatment unfolds in phases. Trying to do everything at once leads to poor compliance, unclear outcomes, and overwhelmed clients.

1

FOUNDATIONS

Weeks 1-4
Sleep optimization, stress reduction, anti-inflammatory nutrition basics, address obvious deficiencies. Build the foundation that all other interventions rely on.

2

TARGET

Weeks 4-12+
Address the primary pattern identified in analysis. Gut protocols, HPA support, detox, autoimmune interventions‚Äîthe core therapeutic work.

3

OPTIMIZE

Months 3-6+
Fine-tune based on response. Address remaining secondary issues. Transition to maintenance. Build long-term resilience and prevention.

Phase 1: Foundations (The Non-Negotiables)

Foundation Focus Areas

  • Sleep: 7-8 hours non-negotiable. If sleep is <6 hours, this is priority #1‚Äînothing else works without sleep
  • Blood sugar stability: Regular meals, adequate protein, minimize processed sugar‚Äîfoundational for every pattern
  • Hydration: Often overlooked; 50% of body weight in ounces daily as starting point
  • Basic anti-inflammatory diet: Remove major inflammatory triggers (processed foods, seed oils, excess sugar)
  • Obvious deficiencies: Vitamin D if deficient, B12 if deficient, iron if deficient‚Äîlow-hanging fruit
  • Basic stress management: One practice client will actually do (breathing, walks, 5-minute meditation)

Phase 2: Target (Pattern-Specific Intervention)

Targeting by Master Pattern

  • Pattern 1 (HPA): Adaptogen support, cortisol rhythm restoration, nervous system regulation
  • Pattern 2 (Inflammation): Anti-inflammatory protocol, identify and remove triggers, omega-3 optimization
  • Pattern 3 (Gut): 5R protocol (Remove, Replace, Reinoculate, Repair, Rebalance), SIBO treatment if applicable
  • Pattern 4 (Metabolic): Carb reduction, fasting strategies, metabolic support nutrients
  • Pattern 5 (Hormonal): Hormone balancing herbs/nutrients, address underlying drivers first
  • Pattern 6 (Autoimmune): Immune modulation, trigger removal, gut-immune axis support
  • Pattern 7 (Toxicity): Source removal, binder protocols, phase I/II detox support

Phase 3: Optimize (Fine-Tuning & Maintenance)

Optimization Focus

  • Reassess: What improved? What persists? Update pattern assessment
  • Address secondary patterns that weren't priority in Phase 2
  • Transition from therapeutic doses to maintenance doses
  • Build resilience: What practices sustain the gains long-term?
  • Prevention: What ongoing support prevents recurrence?

Treatment Selection Logic

Within each phase, how do you choose which specific interventions? Consider:

The SMART Selection Criteria

  • S - Strength of evidence: How supported is this intervention for this condition? Prioritize proven over speculative
  • M - Mechanism match: Does the mechanism of action match the identified dysfunction?
  • A - Adherence likelihood: Will this client actually do this? Lifestyle? Cost? Complexity?
  • R - Risk-benefit ratio: What are the potential downsides? Is it safe for this individual?
  • T - Timeline: How long until expected effect? Set appropriate expectations

Warning: The Kitchen Sink Trap

Giving everything at once because you can justify each intervention is a trap. It overwhelms clients, destroys compliance, and makes it impossible to know what's working. Start focused. Add based on response. The best practitioners show restraint, not just knowledge.

Presenting Treatment Plans to Clients

The 3-Part Presentation Framework

  • 1. Validate & summarize: "Based on everything we've discovered, your primary issue is gut dysfunction that's affecting your hormones and stress response..."
  • 2. Explain the strategy: "We're going to work in phases. First, foundations‚Äîbecause nothing else works without sleep and stable blood sugar. Then we'll target your gut directly. Finally, we'll optimize hormones if they haven't naturally balanced..."
  • 3. Set expectations: "In the first 4 weeks, you may not see dramatic changes‚Äîwe're building the foundation. Weeks 4-8 is when gut symptoms should shift. By month 3, I expect significant improvement across the board..."

Pro Tip: The "Why Wait" Explanation

Clients often want everything addressed immediately. Explain: "I could give you hormone support now, but it likely won't work well because your gut isn't absorbing properly and your stress hormones are blocking it. By fixing the foundation first, the hormonal support‚Äîif you even need it‚Äîwill actually work. Let's do this right."

Check Your Understanding

Test your treatment planning skills:

1

Why did we hold off on hormone support for Kara in Phase 1 and 2, even though her progesterone was low?

Because her hormonal imbalance is likely downstream of gut and HPA dysfunction. The gut affects estrogen metabolism (beta-glucuronidase, estrobolome). HPA dysfunction directly suppresses progesterone production (cortisol steals pregnenolone). Treating hormones without fixing the upstream drivers is like mopping while the faucet runs. Once gut and stress are addressed, hormones often normalize without direct intervention.

2

A client has obvious SIBO but is sleeping 5 hours a night. What do you address first and why?

Sleep first. Sleep deprivation compromises immune function (needed to clear SIBO), gut motility (MMC only works in rested state), stress hormones (cortisol affects gut), and even antimicrobial efficacy. Treating SIBO while the client is sleep-deprived is like trying to excavate while someone keeps filling the hole. Get sleep to 7+ hours minimum before initiating SIBO protocol.

3

How does the "upstream test" help you prioritize when a client has 3+ patterns?

For each pattern, ask: "If I successfully address this, will the other patterns likely improve?" The pattern where "yes" applies most broadly is your upstream priority. Example: With gut, HPA, and hormonal patterns‚Äîif gut is primary, fixing gut will help HPA (less systemic inflammation) and hormones (better metabolism). But fixing hormones alone won't fix gut or HPA. Therefore, gut is the upstream priority.

Key Takeaways

  • Prioritize by hierarchy: Safety ‚Üí Foundations ‚Üí Upstream ‚Üí Mediators ‚Üí Downstream
  • Use the 3-Phase Model: Foundations (weeks 1-4) ‚Üí Target (weeks 4-12+) ‚Üí Optimize (months 3-6+)
  • Resist the "kitchen sink"‚Äîstart with 3-5 focused interventions, add based on response
  • Always ask the upstream test: "If I fix this, will other issues improve?"
  • Present plans with phases and timelines to build client confidence and realistic expectations

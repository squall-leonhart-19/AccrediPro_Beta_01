What Is Case Stacking?

Case stacking is the deliberate practice of analyzing multiple cases within a pattern to:

The Purpose of Case Stacking

  • Identify commonalities and variations within a pattern
  • Refine your diagnostic accuracy through pattern recognition
  • Discover which interventions work best for which presentations
  • Build confidence through accumulated experience
  • Create a mental "case library" for rapid clinical decision-making

The 10-30-100 Expertise Model

Stages of Pattern Mastery

  • Cases 1-10: Learning the basics. High uncertainty. Following protocols closely. Each case feels unique.
  • Cases 10-30: Patterns emerge. You start predicting outcomes. Protocols become adaptable.
  • Cases 30-100: Confident mastery. You recognize variations instantly. You know what works and what doesn't.
  • Cases 100+: Expert intuition. You've seen rare presentations. You can mentor others.

Building Your Case Library

What to Document for Each Case

  • Pattern: Which of the 7 Master Patterns?
  • Presentation: Key symptoms, labs, timeline markers
  • Initial hypothesis: What you thought was happening
  • Treatment: What you did and in what sequence
  • Outcome: What improved, what didn't, timeline
  • Key learning: What would you do differently? What surprised you?

Pro Tip: The Learning Moment

The most valuable cases for stacking are the ones that surprised you‚Äîwhere your initial hypothesis was wrong, where the standard protocol didn't work, where something unexpected happened. These "error cases" teach you more than 10 textbook cases. Document them carefully.

Check Your Understanding

Q1: Why are "error cases" (where things didn't go as expected) especially valuable for learning?

Error cases force you to update your mental model. When the standard approach works, you learn nothing new‚Äîyour existing knowledge was sufficient. When it fails, you discover gaps in your understanding, edge cases your training didn't cover, and important variations within patterns. These cases contribute disproportionately to expertise development.

Q2: What happens cognitively between cases 10-30 that doesn't happen in cases 1-10?

In cases 1-10, each case feels unique and you follow protocols closely. In cases 10-30, your brain starts recognizing patterns across cases‚Äî"this is like that other case where..." You start predicting outcomes before testing, adapting protocols based on patterns, and developing intuition. This is the transition from novice to intermediate‚Äîpattern recognition emerging from accumulated experience.

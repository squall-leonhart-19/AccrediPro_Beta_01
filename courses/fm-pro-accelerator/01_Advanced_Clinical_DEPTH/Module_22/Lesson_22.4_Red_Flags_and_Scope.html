Knowing Your Limits Protects Everyone

Complex cases push boundaries. Knowing when to refer versus when to manage in-house isn't a sign of weakness‚Äîit's a sign of clinical maturity. This lesson covers the critical red flags you must never miss and exactly where your scope begins and ends.

What You'll Master

  • System-by-system red flags requiring immediate medical referral
  • Clear understanding of scope of practice boundaries
  • How to communicate referrals without losing clients
  • Co-management frameworks for working alongside medical providers
  • Documentation practices that protect you legally

Critical Red Flags by System

These symptoms require immediate medical evaluation. Do not attempt to manage these as a health coach:

Cardiovascular

  • Chest pain or pressure
  • Shortness of breath at rest
  • Irregular or rapid heartbeat
  • Swelling in legs + breathing issues
  • Fainting or near-fainting

Gastrointestinal

  • Blood in stool or vomit
  • Black, tarry stools
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Unexplained weight loss (>10 lbs)
  • Difficulty swallowing

Neurological

  • Sudden severe headache
  • Vision changes or loss
  • Numbness/weakness one side
  • Slurred speech
  • Seizures or loss of consciousness

Mental Health

  • Suicidal thoughts or plans
  • Self-harm behaviors
  • Psychotic symptoms
  • Severe eating disorder signs
  • Inability to function daily

Endocrine

  • Signs of diabetic crisis
  • Thyroid storm symptoms
  • Adrenal crisis signs
  • Rapid unexplained weight changes
  • Extreme temperature intolerance

General/Systemic

  • Unexplained fever over 103¬∞F
  • Signs of infection spreading
  • Suspected blood clot
  • Allergic reaction with breathing issues
  • Any symptom causing significant distress

When in Doubt, Refer Out

If your gut says something is seriously wrong, listen to it. You will never be criticized for being cautious. You will absolutely be liable for missing something dangerous.

How to Communicate Referrals

Clients fear referral means you're "giving up on them." Frame it correctly:

üí¨ Script: The Partnership Frame

"I want to make sure we're addressing this the right way. What you're describing [symptom] is something I'd like you to have evaluated by a [specialist]. This doesn't mean I'm stepping back‚Äîit means I'm bringing in the right expert for this specific piece. I'll continue working with you on [diet/lifestyle/supplements], and once we have clarity from the doctor, we can integrate that information into our plan. Think of it as building your wellness team."

üí¨ Script: The Urgency Frame (for Red Flags)

"I need to be direct with you. What you're describing needs to be evaluated today‚Äînot by me, but by a physician who can do [imaging/bloodwork/physical exam]. I'm not trying to alarm you, but my job is to keep you safe. Please go to [urgent care/ER/call your doctor] today. Text me after you've been seen and we can talk through next steps together."

Co-Management Frameworks

You don't have to fully refer out‚Äîoften you can co-manage with providers:

Co-Management Works When:

  • Client has an established medical provider
  • The condition requires both medical treatment AND lifestyle support
  • You stay in your lane (lifestyle) while they stay in theirs (medical)
  • Communication is clear between you and the provider

Examples of Effective Co-Management:

  • Diabetes: MD manages medication ‚Üí You manage diet, exercise, blood sugar monitoring
  • Thyroid: Endocrinologist manages medication ‚Üí You optimize nutrition, gut health, stress
  • Mental Health: Psychiatrist manages medication ‚Üí You support sleep, nutrition, lifestyle
  • Autoimmune: Rheumatologist manages disease ‚Üí You manage inflammation through diet

Documentation That Protects You

Every referral conversation should be documented:

Document These Elements:

  • Date and time of the conversation
  • Red flag or concern that prompted referral discussion
  • Your recommendation (specific provider type, urgency level)
  • Client's response (agreed, declined, will consider)
  • Follow-up plan (when will you check if they went?)

If Client Refuses Referral

Document that you recommended referral and they declined. Consider having them sign a written acknowledgment. Continue to recommend at subsequent sessions. If the condition is truly dangerous, you may need to terminate the coaching relationship with clear documentation of why.

Key Terms to Remember

Red Flag

Symptom or sign indicating potentially serious condition requiring immediate medical evaluation.

Scope of Practice

Legal and ethical boundaries defining what services a practitioner is qualified and permitted to provide.

Co-Management

Collaborative care model where multiple providers work together on a client's case.

Informed Refusal

Documented record that client was advised of risks and chose to decline recommended care.

Check Your Understanding

1

A client reports blood in their stool "a few times recently." What do you do?

Immediate referral. This is a non-negotiable red flag. Do not attempt to manage with dietary changes first. They need to see their physician or GI specialist promptly‚Äîthis week, not "eventually."

2

Can you tell a client to reduce their thyroid medication because you think they're overmedicated?

Absolutely not. You can educate them on signs of overmedication and encourage them to discuss with their prescriber. You can write a letter to their doctor with your observations. But you cannot advise medication changes‚Äîthat's outside scope.

3

A client with insulin-controlled diabetes wants your help. Is this appropriate for health coaching?

Yes‚Äîwith co-management. Their physician manages insulin; you manage diet, exercise, blood sugar monitoring education, and lifestyle. Coordinate with their healthcare team and document your working relationship.

Key Takeaways

  • Memorize system-specific red flags‚Äîthese require immediate referral
  • Know your scope of practice: nutrition, lifestyle, education‚Äînot diagnosis or prescription
  • Frame referrals as "expanding the team" not abandonment
  • Co-management is often ideal: you handle lifestyle while providers handle medical
  • Document all referral conversations, including client response
  • When in doubt, refer out‚Äîyour caution protects everyone

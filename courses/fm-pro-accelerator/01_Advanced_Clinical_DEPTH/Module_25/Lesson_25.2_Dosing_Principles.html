RDA vs. Optimal vs. Therapeutic

Understanding the Levels

  • RDA (Recommended Dietary Allowance): Prevents deficiency in 97-98% of population‚Äînot optimal health
  • Optimal: Levels associated with optimal function, disease prevention, longevity
  • Therapeutic: Higher doses used to correct deficiency or treat specific conditions
  • UL (Tolerable Upper Intake): Highest daily intake unlikely to cause harm

Loading vs. Maintenance Dosing

When to Use Loading Doses

  • Vitamin D: Deep deficiency may need 50,000 IU weekly √ó 8-12 weeks, then maintenance
  • Iron: Moderate-to-severe deficiency may need higher doses initially
  • Creatine: Optional loading phase (20g/day √ó 5-7 days) then 3-5g maintenance
  • Coenzyme Q10: May start higher (400-600mg) then reduce to 100-200mg

When NOT to Load

  • Most B vitamins: Water-soluble, no need to load
  • Probiotics: Start low to avoid die-off reactions
  • Sensitive clients: Start all supplements low regardless
  • Elderly/compromised: Gentler approach always

Individual Variability Factors

What Affects Dosing Needs?

  • Body weight: Larger individuals may need higher doses
  • Age: Absorption decreases with age; may need higher doses
  • Genetics: MTHFR affects B vitamin needs; VDR affects vitamin D
  • Gut health: Malabsorption increases needs
  • Medications: Many deplete nutrients (PPIs, metformin, statins)
  • Health status: Illness increases demands
  • Diet: Baseline intake affects supplemental needs

High-Dose Protocols

Evidence-Based High-Dose Uses

  • Omega-3 (3-4g EPA+DHA): Triglyceride reduction, inflammation
  • Niacin (1500-2000mg): Lipids (requires medical supervision, flushing)
  • Vitamin C (high-dose IV): Used in integrative oncology
  • Magnesium (800-1200mg): Migraine prevention, constipation

‚ö†Ô∏è High-Dose Cautions

  • Vitamin A: >10,000 IU retinol can be toxic; teratogenic in pregnancy
  • Vitamin E: >400 IU associated with increased mortality risk
  • Iron: Never give without confirmed deficiency‚Äîcan cause oxidative damage
  • Zinc: >40mg long-term can deplete copper
  • B6: >100mg long-term can cause neuropathy

Monitoring and Adjusting

When to Retest

  • Vitamin D: Retest 8-12 weeks after starting/changing dose
  • Iron (ferritin): Retest 3 months after starting supplementation
  • B12: Retest 2-3 months on supplementation
  • Thyroid panel: If using iodine or selenium, retest 6-8 weeks

Adjusting Based on Response

  • No improvement: Consider absorption issues, wrong form, insufficient dose
  • Side effects: Reduce dose, change form, split doses
  • Overshooting targets: Reduce to maintenance; some nutrients can be too high
  • Symptoms resolve but labs lag: Keep protocol; retest later

‚ú® Key Takeaways

  • RDA prevents deficiency; optimal and therapeutic doses are often much higher
  • Loading doses appropriate for deep deficiencies (vitamin D, iron)
  • Individual factors (genetics, gut, meds) significantly affect dose needs
  • Know your high-dose cautions: Vitamin A, E, iron, zinc, B6
  • Retest strategically to confirm adequacy and avoid overshooting

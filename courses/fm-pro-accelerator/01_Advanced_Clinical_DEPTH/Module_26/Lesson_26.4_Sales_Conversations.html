Selling Is Serving

Many practitioners cringe at "selling." But if your work helps people and they need it, not offering it is a disservice. This lesson reframes sales as enrollment and gives you a proven conversation structure.

Mindset: Selling Is Serving

The Truth About Ethical Selling

  • You're not convincing: You're helping them make a decision
  • If they need help and can afford it: Not offering is unethical
  • They came to you: They're already looking for a solution
  • Your job: Understand their problem, see if you're a fit, make it easy to say yes
  • Not everyone is a fit: It's okay to say no to wrong-fit clients

The Discovery Call Structure

1. Warm Welcome (2-3 min)

  • Thank them for booking
  • Brief personal connection
  • Set the agenda: "I'd love to learn about what's going on for you, share how I might help, and see if it makes sense to work together."

2. Deep Discovery (15-20 min)

  • Current situation: "Tell me what's going on health-wise right now."
  • Impact: "How is this affecting your daily life?"
  • Past attempts: "What have you already tried?"
  • Why now: "What made you reach out now?"
  • Desired outcome: "What would your life look like if this was solved?"

3. Bridge + Present Solution (5-10 min)

  • Reflect back what you heard (they feel understood)
  • Explain how your approach addresses their specific situation
  • Present your offer (package, what's included, investment)
  • Connect the offer to their stated desired outcome
Sample Transition Script:

"Based on what you've shared, I think I can really help. You mentioned [their words]. My approach specifically addresses this by [brief explanation]. Here's what working together would look like..."

4. Invitation (2-3 min)

  • Ask if they have questions
  • Answer questions directly
  • Ask for the decision: "Do you have any questions before we get started?"
  • Or: "How does this sound to you?"

Objection Handling

Common Objections and Responses

  • "I need to think about it": "Of course. What specifically would you like to think about?"
  • "I can't afford it": "I understand. May I ask‚Äîis it that the investment doesn't feel worth it, or is it a cash flow issue?"
  • "I need to talk to my spouse": "Absolutely. What do you think they'll want to know?"
  • "I'm not sure this is right for me": "What would make you feel confident it's right for you?"

‚úÖ Pro Tip

Most objections are actually requests for more information or reassurance. Ask clarifying questions with genuine curiosity, not pressure. Sometimes "I need to think about it" really means "I'm not the right fit"‚Äîand that's okay.

When to Disqualify

Signs They're Not Right for You

  • Outside your scope: Medical conditions you can't support
  • Not ready to change: Looking for magic pills, not willing to do the work
  • Red flag personality: Already combative, demanding, disrespectful
  • Truly can't afford it: And have no way to make it work
  • Want you to guarantee results: You can't guarantee; you can commit
Graceful Disqualification:

"Based on what you've shared, I don't think I'm the best fit for what you need right now. Let me suggest [referral or alternative]. I want to make sure you get the right help."

Follow-Up Without Being Pushy

Effective Follow-Up

  • Same day: Thank-you email, recap of conversation
  • Day 2-3: "Just checking in‚Äîany questions I can answer?"
  • Day 5-7: "I wanted to follow up one last time..."
  • After 3 touches: Move to nurture list; they're not ready now
  • Key: Follow up is caring, not pushy

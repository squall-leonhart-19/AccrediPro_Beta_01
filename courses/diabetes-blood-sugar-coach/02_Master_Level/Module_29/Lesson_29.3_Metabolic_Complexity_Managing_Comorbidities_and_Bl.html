4. Activate Movement: Adjustments for Neuropathy

Exercise is our most potent "glucose sink," but for clients with Peripheral Neuropathy or Autonomic Dysfunction, movement can be dangerous. Neuropathy increases the risk of undetected foot ulcers, while autonomic dysfunction can lead to exercise-induced hypotension (fainting).

The "Safe Activation" Protocol:

  • Non-Weight Bearing Options: For those with loss of sensation in the feet, prioritize cycling, rowing, or swimming to prevent "Charcot foot" or pressure sores.
  • The "Talk Test" for Autonomic Dysfunction: Since heart rate response may be blunted (cardiac autonomic neuropathy), use the Borg Scale of Perceived Exertion (RPE) rather than heart rate monitors to gauge intensity.
  • Post-Exercise Inspection: Mandate a "Foot Check" after every session. A small pebble in a shoe can lead to amputation in a neuropathic client if undetected.

5. The Cortisol-Glucose Loop in Chronic Fatigue

In clients with high-stress occupations or Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS), the body often enters a state of HPA-Axis Dysregulation. Here, the "Lifestyle Reset" pillar takes precedence over "Activate Movement."

A 2022 study found that in high-stress populations, cortisol-induced gluconeogenesis can account for up to 25% of fasting hyperglycemia, regardless of carbohydrate intake. If we push these clients into HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training), we may trigger a "crash" that lasts weeks, further dysregulating their blood sugar.

The "Restorative Glycemic" Strategy:

For these clients, we focus on Zone 1 movement (breath-work, slow walking) and Circadian Anchoring (Module 5). By stabilizing the cortisol awakening response (CAR), we reduce the morning glucose spike without needing more medication or fewer carbs.

Client: Marcus, 58

Scenario: Marcus has T2D and starts a new coaching program. During the "Screen" phase, he reports sudden, unexplained swelling in his ankles (edema) and a "foamy" appearance to his urine.

Check Your Understanding

Why might a client with Hashimoto's Thyroiditis see elevated post-prandial glucose even when eating a low-glycemic meal?

Hypothyroidism reduces the expression of GLUT4 transporters and slows gastric emptying/glucose clearance, meaning glucose remains in the bloodstream longer than in a metabolically healthy individual.

Which S.T.A.B.L.E. pillar should be the last to be intensified in a client with Chronic Fatigue Syndrome and high cortisol?

"Activate Movement" (specifically high-intensity exercise) should be delayed until the "Lifestyle Reset" and "Balance Hormones" pillars have stabilized the HPA-axis, to avoid further cortisol-driven glucose spikes and physical crashes.

Key Takeaways

  • Complexity is the Norm: Over 60% of your clients will have at least one major comorbidity.
  • PCOS/Hashimoto's requires timing: Focus on post-meal movement and protein leverage to overcome hormonal "drag."
  • Renal Guardrails: Protein must be titrated down as eGFR drops; always coordinate with a nephrologist for Stage 3+ CKD.
  • Safety with Neuropathy: Prioritize non-weight-bearing exercise and mandatory foot checks.
  • The Cortisol Connection: High-stress clients need "Lifestyle Reset" before "Activate Movement" to prevent exercise-induced hyperglycemia.

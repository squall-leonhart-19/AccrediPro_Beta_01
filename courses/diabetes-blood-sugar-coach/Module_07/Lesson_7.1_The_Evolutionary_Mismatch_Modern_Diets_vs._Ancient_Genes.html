The Discordance Hypothesis

The core of Evolutionary Nutrition is the Discordance Hypothesis, which suggests that human biology is still adapted to the environment of our Paleolithic ancestors. While our genome has changed by less than 0.01% in the last 10,000 years, our diet has undergone a radical transformationâ€”first with the Agricultural Revolution (grains and dairy) and more recently with the Industrial Revolution (refined sugars and seed oils).

A 2021 study published in The Lancet highlighted that over 70% of the modern Western diet is composed of foods that would have been unrecognizable to a pre-agricultural human. This rapid shift has overwhelmed our metabolic pathways. We are essentially running "Stone Age" software on modern, high-sugar "hardware," resulting in the systemic crashes we identify as insulin resistance and Type 2 Diabetes.

The Great Acceleration of Glucose

In the Paleolithic era, glucose was a rare and precious resource, found primarily in fibrous tubers and seasonal wild fruits. Today, refined glucose and fructose are ubiquitous. The glycemic variability experienced by a modern individual in a single day often exceeds what an ancestral human would have experienced in a month.

This "Great Acceleration" of glucose deliveryâ€”facilitated by the removal of the food matrix (fiber, protein, and fat)â€”leads to chronic hyperinsulinemia. When insulin is constantly elevated to manage these spikes, the body's cells eventually downregulate their receptors, leading to the hallmark of diabetes: Insulin Resistance.

Metabolic Flexibility Mechanisms

One of the greatest casualties of the modern diet is metabolic flexibility. Our ancestors frequently moved between "fed" and "fasted" states, allowing the body to switch between burning glucose and burning stored body fat (ketones). In a modern environment of 6+ meals/snacks per day, most clients are "locked" in a sugar-burning state.

The "Thrifty Gene" Paradox

The "Thrifty Gene" hypothesis suggests that certain genes were selected to help our ancestors store fat efficiently during times of famine. In 2024, these same genes are a liability. When we consume high-glycemic loads without the "famine" period, these genes promote rapid fat storage around the viscera (organs), directly driving insulin resistance.

The "Fiber Gap" and GLP-1

Modern pharmaceutical interventions for diabetes (like Semaglutide) work by mimicking GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1). However, our ancestors produced high levels of endogenous GLP-1 naturally. How? Through the fermentation of prebiotic fibers in the large intestine.

The "Fiber Gap" (the difference between the 100g+ consumed by ancestors and the 15g consumed today) means the modern gut is starving for the substrate needed to produce the hormones that naturally suppress appetite and slow gastric emptying. In Phase 6, we prioritize "Evolutionary Fiber" to restore this natural hormonal signaling.

Integrating STABLE Methodâ„¢ Phase 6

As a coach, you are not just prescribing a "Paleo diet." You are using evolutionary principles to restore Biological Synergy (the "B" in STABLE). This involves four primary pillars:

1. The Food Matrix Priority

Always choose foods in their most intact form. A whole apple has a different glycemic impact than apple sauce, which has a different impact than apple juice. The matrix is the medicine.

2. Protein Leverage

Research shows humans will continue to eat until they hit a specific protein threshold. By increasing protein density to ancestral levels (25-30%), we naturally reduce the "drive" for excess carbohydrates.

3. Circadian Feeding

Aligning food intake with the sun (eating during daylight hours) matches our internal circadian clocks, which regulate insulin sensitivity. Ancestors didn't eat under blue light at 11:00 PM.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of evolutionary nutrition concepts.

1. Why is the "food matrix" more important than just the "carb count" in managing diabetes?

The food matrix (fiber, protein, fat) slows down the enzymatic breakdown of carbohydrates, leading to a slower glucose release into the bloodstream. Removing the matrix (as in juice or refined flour) creates rapid spikes that the body's insulin response cannot handle efficiently.

2. What is the primary difference between the ancestral and modern Omega 6:3 ratio, and why does it matter for blood sugar?

Ancestral ratios were ~1:1, while modern ratios are ~20:1. Excess Omega-6 (from seed oils) promotes systemic inflammation and oxidative stress, which damages insulin receptors and promotes insulin resistance.

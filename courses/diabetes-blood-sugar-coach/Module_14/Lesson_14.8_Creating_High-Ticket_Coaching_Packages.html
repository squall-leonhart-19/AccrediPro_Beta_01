The High-Ticket Shift: Moving Beyond the Hourly Trap

Most health coaches begin their careers charging by the hour (e.g., $100â€“$150 per session). While this feels safe, it creates a fundamental misalignment between the coach and the client. In an hourly model, the coach is incentivized to work more hours, while the client is incentivized to need fewer sessions to save money. Furthermore, blood sugar management is a longitudinal process that cannot be "solved" in a single 60-minute window.

High-ticket coaching (typically priced between $2,500 and $10,000+) shifts the focus from time spent to outcomes achieved. A 2022 industry report found that practitioners who moved to a package-based model saw a 40% increase in client compliance because the financial commitment served as a psychological "buy-in" for the difficult lifestyle changes required.

Pricing for Results (The Health ROI)

When a client says, "That's expensive," they are usually comparing your price to a grocery bill or a gym membership. Your job is to help them compare it to the cost of inaction. According to the American Diabetes Association (2023), the average annual cost of diagnosed diabetes is $16,752 per person. Over 10 years, that is $167,000+ in medical expenses, lost productivity, and medication.

The "Value Gap" Calculation

If your $5,000 program helps a client reduce their A1c from 8.5% to 6.2%, potentially allowing their MD to de-prescribe expensive GLP-1 agonists or insulin, the program pays for itself in less than 6 months. This is a 200% ROI in year one alone.

Sales Psychology & Enrollment

High-ticket sales are not about "convincing." They are about diagnosis and prescription. If you act like a salesperson, they will treat you like one. If you act like a specialist, they will treat you like one. The enrollment call should follow a specific arc:

  • Current State: Where are they now? (Symptoms, fears, A1c levels).
  • Desired State: Where do they want to be in 6 months? (Off meds, playing with grandkids, more energy).
  • The Gap: Why haven't they reached the desired state yet? (What have they tried? Why did it fail?).
  • The Bridge: Present your high-ticket package as the logical bridge to cross the gap.

Handling Objections

When a client brings up price, never defend it. Instead, ask: "I understand $5,000 is an investment. Aside from the finances, do you feel this is the right system to get you to [Desired State]?" If the answer is yes, then the problem is just a math equation, not a lack of trust.

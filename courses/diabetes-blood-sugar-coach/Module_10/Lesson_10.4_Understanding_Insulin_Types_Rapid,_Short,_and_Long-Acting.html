Onset

The length of time before insulin reaches the bloodstream and begins lowering blood glucose.

Peak

The time during which insulin is at its maximum strength in terms of lowering blood glucose.

Duration

How long the insulin continues to lower blood glucose after the initial injection.

Insulin Stacking

Taking a second dose of rapid-acting insulin before the first dose has finished working, leading to severe hypoglycemia.

The Evolution of Insulin Therapy

Since the first successful use of bovine insulin in 1922, the field has moved from crude animal extracts to recombinant DNA technology. Modern analogs are engineered to change how insulin molecules (hexamers) dissociate into monomers, allowing for faster absorption (Rapid-Acting) or slower, more predictable release (Ultra-Long Acting).

In the context of the STABLE Methodâ„¢, we view insulin not as a "failure of lifestyle," but as a biological replacement therapy. A 2022 study in The Lancet involving over 15,000 participants noted that early initiation of basal insulin in Type 2 patients with high A1c levels (>9.0%) resulted in a 22% reduction in microvascular complications compared to delayed initiation.

Pharmacokinetics vs. Pharmacodynamics

To coach a client effectively, you must distinguish between what the body does to the drug (Pharmacokinetics) and what the drug does to the body (Pharmacodynamics). For insulin, the site of injection matters. Research shows that abdominal injections are absorbed up to 20% faster than injections in the thigh or buttocks.

Coaching Insight: Lipohypertrophy

Repeated injections in the same spot can cause lipohypertrophy (fatty lumps). A 2023 clinical review found that injecting into hypertrophic tissue can reduce insulin absorption by 25-50%, leading to "unexplained" hyperglycemia and unpredictable late-stage crashes.

Rapid & Short-Acting (Bolus)

Bolus insulin is designed to mimic the "first-phase" insulin response of a healthy pancreas. It handles the glucose load from meals (postprandial) or corrects high blood sugar readings.

Rapid-Acting Analogs (Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine): These are modified to prevent insulin molecules from clumping together. This allows them to enter the bloodstream almost immediately. They are the gold standard for Stabilization (S) because their peak closely matches the glucose spike of a standard meal.

Short-Acting (Regular/R): This is the "old school" insulin. It takes 30-60 minutes to start working and peaks much later. In modern coaching, Regular insulin is often avoided for meals because it creates a "mismatch" where the insulin is still peaking long after the food has been digested, increasing the risk of late-meal hypoglycemia.

Intermediate & Long-Acting (Basal)

Basal insulin provides a "background" level of insulin to suppress hepatic glucose production (the liver dumping sugar) during periods of fasting and sleep. This is critical for managing the Dawn Phenomenon (Biological Synergy - B).

The Basal-Bolus Concept

The goal of intensive insulin therapy is to replicate the healthy pancreas's two-tier delivery system. This is known as the Basal-Bolus Regimen. Without a basal dose, the client's blood sugar will rise even if they don't eat (due to gluconeogenesis). Without bolus doses, the client will experience massive postprandial spikes.

David, 34 (Type 1 Diabetes)

Active runner, using Glargine (Basal) and Lispro (Bolus).

Presentation: David experienced severe hypoglycemia 45 minutes into his evening runs. He was "bolusing" for a pre-run snack of 30g carbs using his standard 1:10 ratio.

The STABLE Intervention: Using CGM data (Tactical Tracking), we identified that his rapid-acting insulin (Lispro) was peaking exactly when his muscular glucose uptake (Active Sensitization - A) was highest.

Outcome: By reducing his pre-run bolus by 50% and timing the run for the "tail end" of the insulin's duration, he maintained a Time in Range (TIR) of 92% during exercise without crashing.

The STABLE Method & Insulin Safety

As a coach, your primary focus is helping the client integrate their medical team's prescriptions into their daily life. The STABLE Methodâ„¢ provides the framework:

  • Stabilization (S): Identify "peaks and valleys" in CGM data. If a client is "flat" overnight but spikes during the day, the basal dose is likely correct, but the bolus ratio may need adjustment.
  • Tactical Tracking (T): Correlate insulin doses with fiber-to-carb ratios. High-fiber meals (Evolutionary Nutrition - E) slow glucose entry, often requiring a "split bolus" to prevent early hypoglycemia followed by late hyperglycemia.
  • Biological Synergy (B): Address the Dawn Phenomenon. If fasting glucose is high despite a correct basal dose, look at cortisol and stress levels (Lifestyle Modulation - L).

Critical Safety: The 15/15 Rule

Clients on insulin MUST be trained in hypoglycemia management. If BG drops below 70 mg/dL: Consume 15g of fast-acting glucose, wait 15 minutes, and re-test. Repeat until stable. Never use "fatty" sweets (like chocolate) for emergencies, as the fat delays glucose absorption.

Why is Insulin Degludec (Tresiba) considered superior for "Stabilization" (S) compared to NPH insulin?

Degludec has a "peakless" profile and a duration of over 42 hours, leading to significantly less nocturnal hypoglycemia and lower day-to-day variability compared to NPH, which has a pronounced peak at 4-10 hours.

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • Timing is Everything: Rapid-acting insulin should generally be taken 15 minutes before eating to "pre-bolus" and match the glucose curve.
  • Basal vs. Bolus: Basal (long-acting) controls the liver's output; Bolus (rapid-acting) controls the meal's input.
  • Stacking Danger: Always wait at least 3-4 hours between bolus doses to prevent cumulative hypoglycemia.
  • Injection Sites: Rotation is mandatory to prevent lipohypertrophy and ensure predictable absorption.
  • The STABLE Approach: Use data to bridge the gap between pharmacological action and lifestyle reality.

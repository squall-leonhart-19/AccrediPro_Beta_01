Deprescribing

The planned and supervised process of dose reduction or stopping of medication that might be causing harm or no longer be of benefit.

Therapeutic Inertia

The failure of healthcare providers to initiate or intensify therapy when therapeutic goals are not met, or conversely, to de-escalate when goals are exceeded.

Hypoglycemia Unawareness

A complication where a client no longer experiences the early warning signs of low blood sugar, increasing the risk of severe neuroglycopenia.

Rebound Hyperglycemia

An elevation in blood glucose levels following a period of hypoglycemia or a rapid reduction in medication dosage.

Defining Deprescribing in Diabetes Care

In the traditional medical model, Type 2 Diabetes (T2D) is often treated as a progressive, lifelong disease requiring ever-increasing doses of medication. However, modern clinical trials like the DiRECT study and the Virta Health 2-year trial have proven that T2D can be put into remission through intensive lifestyle intervention.

Deprescribing is not merely "stopping pills." It is a clinical intervention designed to improve quality of life by reducing polypharmacy, side effects, and the risk of severe hypoglycemia. For a coach, deprescribing support means providing the physiological data (via CGM) and lifestyle consistency that allows a physician to make safe, informed decisions about reducing a client's pharmaceutical load.

The Coachâ€™s Scope of Practice: The Hard Line

Before we proceed, we must establish the "Golden Rule" of medical integration: Coaches never advise a client to change, reduce, or stop a medication. Doing so is practicing medicine without a license and poses a significant legal and safety risk.

Instead, the coach acts as a Decision Support Specialist. You provide the evidence (tracking), the mechanism (lifestyle), and the education (understanding side effects) that empowers the client to have a productive conversation with their doctor. Your role is to bridge the gap between the client's daily data and the doctor's clinical oversight.

Dose Adjustment Identify patterns of hypoglycemia in CGM data. Calculate and order the new dosage or taper schedule.
Medication Choice Educate on mechanism of action and common lifestyle interactions. Select the appropriate agent based on comorbidities and labs.

Stopping Meds Document 14+ days of glycemic stability without spikes. Officially discontinue the medication in the medical record.

Identifying Candidates for Medication Reduction

Not every client is a candidate for immediate deprescribing. We look for specific clinical "Green Flags" that indicate the body is regaining metabolic autonomy. According to consensus reports from the American Diabetes Association (ADA), candidates for de-intensification include:

  • A1c Levels: Consistently below 6.5% (or even 6.0% for some practitioners) on current medication.
  • Hypoglycemia: Any instance of blood sugar < 70 mg/dL, especially if recurrent or nocturnal.
  • Weight Loss: A loss of 5-10% of total body weight often significantly increases insulin sensitivity.
  • Lifestyle Change: Successful implementation of the Evolutionary-Based Nutrition (E) and Active Sensitization (A) phases of the STABLE Method.

Integrating the STABLE Methodâ„¢ into Deprescribing

The STABLE Methodâ„¢ provides the physiological framework that makes deprescribing possible. Each letter plays a role in reducing the need for exogenous glucose control:

S: Stabilization of Glycemic Variability

By "flattening the curve," we reduce the need for bolus insulin or secretagogues (like Sulfonylureas) that are designed to combat post-meal spikes.

A: Active Sensitization

Resistance training increases GLUT4 translocation. As the muscles become "glucose sponges," the required dose of insulin or Metformin naturally decreases.

E: Evolutionary-Based Nutrition

Prioritizing fiber and protein-to-carb ratios triggers natural GLP-1 production, potentially allowing for the reduction of synthetic GLP-1 agonists over time.

The Communication Triad: Coach, Client, Physician

A primary barrier to deprescribing is Therapeutic Inertia. Doctors are busy and often fear the "rebound" effect if meds are removed. Your job is to help the client present a "Data-Driven Case" for reduction. A successful communication package includes:

  1. 14-Day CGM Report: Showing average glucose, GMI (estimated A1c), and Time in Range.
  2. Symptom Log: Highlighting episodes of dizziness, sweatiness, or confusion (hypoglycemia).
  3. Lifestyle Summary: "I have consistently maintained a < 50g net carb intake and 150 minutes of zone 2/resistance training for 4 weeks."
  4. Specific Ask: "Based on my consistent readings below 90 mg/dL, are we at a point where we can trial a lower dose of [Medication]?"

Test your knowledge on the coach's role in deprescribing.

1

A client's CGM shows frequent readings of 65 mg/dL after their morning Metformin dose. What is the most appropriate coaching response?

Instruct the client to document these readings and contact their doctor immediately to discuss a dose reduction. Metformin rarely causes hypos alone, but in combination with lifestyle changes or other meds, it can contribute. Never tell them to skip the dose yourself.
2

Why is the "A" (Active Sensitization) phase of the STABLE Method a critical time for deprescribing support?

Exercise increases non-insulin-mediated glucose uptake. If the client remains on a high dose of insulin or secretagogues while increasing muscle glucose uptake, the risk of severe hypoglycemia increases significantly.

Key Takeaways

  • Deprescribing is a clinical process; the coach provides the supporting data but never the medical order.
  • Hypoglycemia (blood sugar < 70 mg/dL) is the primary "Green Flag" for immediate medical consultation regarding dose reduction.
  • The STABLE Methodâ„¢ facilitates deprescribing by improving insulin sensitivity and reducing the need for exogenous glucose management.
  • Use CGM reports (AGP) as the objective "bridge" for communication between the client and their physician.
  • Be alert for "rebound" effects or rare complications like euglycemic ketoacidosis during the tapering phase.

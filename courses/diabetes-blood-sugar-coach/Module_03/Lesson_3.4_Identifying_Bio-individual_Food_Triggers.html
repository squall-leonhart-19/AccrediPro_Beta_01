Postprandial Glucose (PPG)

The blood sugar levels measured after eating. For metabolic health, the peak should occur 45-75 minutes after a meal.

Glycemic Variability (GV)

The "swings" in blood sugar. High GV is more predictive of cardiovascular complications than high A1c alone.

Area Under the Curve (AUC)

The total exposure to high glucose over time, representing the cumulative glycemic burden of a meal.

Second Meal Effect

The phenomenon where the glycemic response to a meal is influenced by the composition of the previous meal.

The Death of the Universal Glycemic Index

For decades, nutrition science relied on the Glycemic Index (GI)â€”a ranking of carbohydrates based on how they affect blood glucose. However, the GI is an average of a small group of healthy individuals. It fails to account for the massive interpersonal variability found in the general population, especially those with insulin resistance.

A landmark 2015 study by Zeevi et al. published in Cell revolutionized our understanding. Researchers tracked 800 participants over a week, monitoring 46,898 meals. They found that the postprandial glycemic response (PPGR) to identical foods varied wildly. For some, white bread caused a massive spike, while for others, it was negligible. Conversely, some participants spiked significantly more from "healthy" sushi than from ice cream.

Mechanisms of Bio-individual Variation

Why does your client "A" spike on grapes while client "B" stays stable? The STABLE Methodâ„¢ identifies four primary drivers of this variation:

  1. The Microbiome: Specific bacterial strains (e.g., Prevotella copri) are linked to improved glucose metabolism. The Zeevi study proved that microbiome composition was a better predictor of glucose response than the carb count of the meal.
  2. Genetics (Amylase Copy Number): The AMY1 gene dictates how much salivary amylase we produce. Individuals with fewer copies of this gene may struggle to break down starches efficiently, leading to higher glycemic spikes.
  3. Sleep & Circadian Rhythm: A single night of partial sleep deprivation can increase postprandial glucose by up to 40% the following day due to temporary insulin resistance.
  4. Insulin Sensitivity (Muscle Mass): Clients with higher skeletal muscle mass have more "glucose sinks" (via GLUT4 translocation), allowing them to clear glucose faster even from high-GI foods.

Tactical Food Sequencing & Pairing

One of the most powerful tools in the STABLE Methodâ„¢ is Food Sequencing. Research (Shukla et al., 2017) shows that the order in which food is consumed significantly impacts the glucose and insulin response.

The STABLE Sequence:

  • Step 1: Fiber (Greens/Vegetables). Fiber coats the small intestine, slowing the absorption of glucose.
  • Step 2: Protein and Fats. These stimulate the release of GLP-1 and cholecystokinin (CCK), which slows gastric emptying.
  • Step 3: Starches and Sugars. By the time these hit the system, the "buffer" is already in place.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 11 studies found that consuming protein and vegetables before carbohydrates reduced postprandial glucose peaks by an average of 35% to 40% in patients with Type 2 Diabetes.

The "Food Challenge" Protocol

To identify bio-individual triggers, you will guide your client through a structured "Food Challenge" during Phase 2 of the STABLE Methodâ„¢.

The 3-Day Challenge Framework

Instruct the client to choose one "suspect" food (e.g., white rice, sweet potato, or a specific fruit) and test it under three conditions:

  • Day 1: The Naked Carb. Eat the food alone on an empty stomach (e.g., 1/2 cup rice). Observe the raw spike.
  • Day 2: The Buffered Carb. Eat the same food, but paired with 20g of protein and 10g of healthy fat.
  • Day 3: The Sequenced Carb. Eat a small salad first, then the protein/fat, then the carb.

This protocol teaches the client exactly how their body handles that specific food and, more importantly, how they can "hack" the meal to keep it in range.

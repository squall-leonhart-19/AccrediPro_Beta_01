1. Pre-Exercise Screening: The Safe Entry Thresholds

In the previous lessons of the Activate Movement module, we explored the physiological benefits of resistance training, HIIT, and NEAT. However, as a Diabetes & Blood Sugar Coach, your primary responsibility is ensuring that metabolic benefits never come at the cost of acute safety.

The S.T.A.B.L.E. Method‚Ñ¢ emphasizes "Screening" as the first step of any intervention. Before a client begins a workout, they must clear specific glycemic "checkpoints." These thresholds are designed to prevent two extremes: exercise-induced hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) and hyperglycemic ketoacidosis (excessively high blood sugar with ketones).

Pre-Exercise Glucose (mg/dL) Risk Assessment Recommended Action
< 90 mg/dL High Hypoglycemia Risk Consume 15-30g fast-acting carbs; wait for BG >100.
90 - 150 mg/dL Ideal Range (Safe) Proceed; monitor for drops during prolonged activity.
151 - 250 mg/dL Safe (Caution) Proceed; monitor for unexpected rises in high-intensity work.
> 250 mg/dL Hyperglycemia Risk Check for ketones. If ketones are present, STOP.
> 300 mg/dL Severe Risk No exercise. Hydrate and consult medical protocol.

The Ketone Caveat

If blood glucose is above 250 mg/dL and ketones are present (measured via urine or blood), exercise can actually increase glucose levels. Without sufficient insulin, the body continues to dump glucose into the bloodstream while burning fat for fuel, potentially leading to DKA.

2. Managing the Drop: The 'Rule of 15'

Hypoglycemia (BG < 70 mg/dL) is the most common barrier to exercise for clients on insulin or insulin secretagogues (like sulfonylureas). During exercise, muscle glucose uptake can increase by up to 50 times compared to rest. If the client‚Äôs exogenous insulin levels are too high, the result is a rapid plummet in blood sugar.

The Rule of 15 is the gold-standard protocol for managing these events:

  1. Identify: Recognize symptoms (shakiness, sweating, confusion, palpitations).
  2. Treat: Consume 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate (e.g., 4 oz juice, 3-4 glucose tablets).
  3. Wait: Rest for 15 minutes to allow glucose to enter the bloodstream.
  4. Re-test: Check blood glucose. If still < 70 mg/dL, repeat the 15g of carbs.
  5. Stabilize: Once > 70 mg/dL, consume a small snack with protein/fat (e.g., peanut butter on a cracker) if the next meal is more than an hour away.

3. Foot Care and Neuropathy: The Silent Complication

Peripheral neuropathy affects approximately 50% of adults with diabetes over their lifetime. This loss of sensation means a client may not feel a blister, a hot spot, or even a pebble in their shoe. During exercise, repetitive friction can lead to ulcers that, if left untreated, can result in amputation.

Footwear Selection Criteria

As a coach, you should guide your clients to select footwear that adheres to the following:

  • Breathability: Moisture-wicking materials to prevent fungal growth.
  • Seamless Interior: To prevent friction points against the toes or heel.
  • Deep Toe Box: To avoid "clawing" or pressure on the phalanges.
  • Cushioning: High-quality shock absorption to protect the metatarsal heads.

Scenario: Robert started a daily 30-minute walking program. After day four, he noticed a "damp" spot on his sock but felt no pain. Upon inspection, he had a Stage 2 ulcer on his right heel.

Intervention: We paused the walking program and switched to non-weight-bearing exercise (seated cycling) while the ulcer healed. We implemented a mandatory "mirror check" protocol where Robert uses a hand mirror to inspect the soles of his feet every evening.

Outcome: Ulcer healed in 3 weeks. Robert returned to walking with diabetic-specific socks and a new pair of professionally fitted shoes. No further ulcers in 6 months.

4. Hydration, Electrolytes, and Glycemic Stability

Hydration is more than just "drinking water." In the context of diabetes, dehydration leads to hemoconcentration‚Äîwhere the volume of blood decreases, making the concentration of glucose appear higher. Furthermore, dehydration triggers the release of vasopressin, which can stimulate the liver to release more glucose (glycogenolysis).

The Hydrated State

Proper blood volume allows for efficient insulin delivery to muscle tissues and optimal kidney filtration of excess glucose.

The Dehydrated State

Reduced blood volume increases blood viscosity, raising BG readings and placing additional strain on the cardiovascular system.

For prolonged exercise (>60 minutes), plain water may not be enough. Electrolytes (Sodium, Potassium, Magnesium) are critical for muscle contraction and preventing the "false highs" associated with stress-induced glucose spikes during intense heat.

5. Monitoring Technology: Utilizing CGM Data

The advent of Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) has revolutionized exercise safety. Rather than a single "snapshot" from a finger prick, the CGM provides a movie of the client's metabolic response. However, coaches must understand the "Lag Time."

The 15-Minute Lag

CGMs measure interstitial fluid, not blood. During rapid BG changes (like during a HIIT session), the CGM reading may be 10-20 minutes behind the actual blood glucose level. Always confirm a "Low" alert with a finger-stick if symptoms don't match the screen.

Interpreting Trend Arrows During Exercise

  • Double Arrow Up (‚Üë‚Üë): BG is rising rapidly (>3 mg/dL per min). This often happens during anaerobic work (sprints/heavy lifting). Action: Monitor; do not bolus insulin immediately as it may crash post-workout.
  • Horizontal Arrow (‚Üí): BG is stable. Action: Ideal for continuing steady-state work.
  • Single Arrow Down (‚Üì): BG is dropping (1-2 mg/dL per min). Action: Consider a small 5-10g carb snack if approaching the 100 mg/dL mark.
  • Double Arrow Down (‚Üì‚Üì): BG is crashing (>3 mg/dL per min). Action: Stop exercise immediately and apply the Rule of 15.

6. The Practitioner's Safety Checklist

To integrate these concepts into your coaching practice, use the following checklist for every client starting the Activate Movement phase of the S.T.A.B.L.E. Method‚Ñ¢:

1. The Medical Clearance
Ensure the client has a recent stress test if they are over 40 or have had diabetes for >10 years.
2. The "Hypo Kit"
Verify the client carries a kit containing: glucose tabs, a small juice box, and medical ID jewelry.
3. The Timing Protocol
Advise against exercising during "Peak Insulin" times (usually 1-3 hours after rapid-acting insulin injection).

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of exercise safety protocols.

1

A client's pre-exercise BG is 265 mg/dL. They feel fine and want to start their HIIT session. What is your next step?

Ask the client to check for ketones. If ketones are present, they must skip the workout. If no ketones are present, they may perform light-to-moderate aerobic exercise, but HIIT should be avoided as it may further spike glucose via adrenaline.
2

Why is the "Rule of 15" specifically 15 grams of carbs rather than a full meal?

15 grams is usually sufficient to raise BG by 30-50 mg/dL without causing a massive "rebound hyperglycemia." A full meal contains fat and protein which slows glucose absorption, delaying the treatment of the acute low.

Lesson Takeaways

  • Safe Windows: Target a pre-exercise range of 90-250 mg/dL, always checking for ketones if above 250.
  • The 15/15 Rule: Use 15g of fast carbs and wait 15 mins to treat hypoglycemia effectively.
  • Foot Vigilance: Neuropathy necessitates daily foot inspections and diabetic-specific footwear to prevent "silent" ulcers.
  • Hydration is Glycemic Control: Dehydration causes false highs and reduces insulin sensitivity.
  • Tech Synergy: Use CGM trend arrows to predict and prevent glycemic excursions before they become emergencies.

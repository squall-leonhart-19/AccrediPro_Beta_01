3. Selecting Your Model: Cohort vs. Evergreen

Choosing the right delivery model is the most critical structural decision you will make. For metabolic health, where behavioral momentum is fragile, the model dictates the clinical outcome.

Feature Cohort-Based (Fixed Start) Evergreen (Rolling Start)
Community High. Participants bond over shared milestones. Moderate. Harder to foster deep peer relationships.
Logistics Intense launch periods; "on/off" delivery. Steady, automated systems; always "on."
Clinical Success Superior for blood sugar due to social contagion. Varies; requires high participant self-discipline.
Scalability Linear scaling (add more cohorts or larger ones). Exponential scaling (unlimited participants).

For blood sugar coaching, we recommend the Cohort-Based Model for your first 12 months. The biological synchronization of a groupâ€”all testing fasting glucose in week 1, all implementing fiber in week 3â€”creates a "metabolic momentum" that evergreen models often lack.

5. Determining Optimal Group Size (n=8 to n=20)

Group size is a lever for both social contagion and operational efficiency. If the group is too small (n < 6), the energy dies if one or two people are quiet. If it is too large (n > 25) without sub-groups, the "bystander effect" takes over, and participants stop engaging.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) suggested that the most effective group size for lifestyle intervention is between 8 and 15 participants. This "sweet spot" allows for:

  • Intimacy: Everyone feels "seen" by the coach.
  • Diversity: Enough varied experiences to provide peer solutions.
  • Manageability: One coach can monitor 15-20 CGMs or food logs without burnout.

Check Your Understanding

1

Why is a cohort-based model generally preferred over evergreen for blood sugar coaching?

Cohort models leverage "social contagion" and biological synchronization. When all participants tackle the same metabolic challenge (e.g., protein leverage) at the same time, the collective accountability and shared data significantly improve adherence and clinical outcomes.
2

According to research, what is the 'Goldilocks' range for group size to maximize engagement?

The optimal range is typically n=8 to n=20. This size is large enough to sustain group energy if a few members are quiet, but small enough to prevent the "bystander effect" and allow the coach to maintain a high level of individual oversight.

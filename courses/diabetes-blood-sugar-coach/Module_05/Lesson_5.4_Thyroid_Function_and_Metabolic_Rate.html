Triiodothyronine (T3)

The active thyroid hormone that directly influences cellular metabolism and glucose uptake.

Reverse T3 (rT3)

An inactive form of T3 that acts as a "brake" on metabolism, often elevated during chronic stress or illness.

Deiodination

The enzymatic process (primarily in the liver and gut) of converting T4 (inactive) into active T3.

GLUT4 Translocation

The process of moving glucose transporters to the cell surface, a mechanism stimulated by both insulin and T3.

The Metabolic Master Switch

The thyroid gland produces hormones that regulate the Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR). For the blood sugar coach, understanding thyroid function is non-negotiable because thyroid hormones (specifically T3) modulate almost every aspect of carbohydrate metabolism, including intestinal glucose absorption, insulin secretion, and glucose disposal in skeletal muscle.

A 2021 systematic review published in Frontiers in Endocrinology highlighted that thyroid dysfunction is significantly more prevalent in patients with diabetes compared to the general population. Specifically, up to 15% of Type 2 Diabetics and 30% of Type 1 Diabetics exhibit some form of thyroid pathology. When thyroid function is suboptimal, the "Biological Synergy" required for blood sugar stability is broken.

Thyroid-Diabetes Crosstalk

The relationship between the thyroid and blood sugar is bidirectional. While thyroid hormones influence glucose, blood sugar levels and insulin also influence thyroid health. For example, insulin is a known stimulant of the 5'-deiodinase enzyme, which converts T4 to active T3. In states of severe insulin resistance or uncontrolled diabetes, this conversion is often impaired, leading to a state of "functional hypothyroidism" despite normal TSH levels.

Furthermore, chronic hyperglycemia induces oxidative stress that can damage the thyroid gland itself. This creates a vicious cycle: poor blood sugar control impairs thyroid conversion, and low active thyroid hormone (T3) further exacerbates insulin resistance by reducing the expression of GLUT4 transporters in muscle tissue.

Hypothyroidism & Insulin Resistance

In clinical practice, a client with "sluggish" thyroid function will often present with a specific blood sugar pattern: persistent, moderate elevations that are resistant to dietary changes. Because T3 is required to stimulate the expression of the GLUT4 gene, low T3 levels result in fewer "doors" for glucose to enter the muscle cells.

Case Study: The "Stuck" A1c

S

Sarah J., Age 46

Prediabetes (A1c 6.0%) | Chronic Fatigue | Cold Intolerance

Presentation: Sarah was following a strict low-carb diet (under 50g/day) for 6 months, yet her A1c remained at 6.0%. Her CGM data showed "flat" but high lines (average glucose 118 mg/dL) regardless of exercise.

Intervention: Functional testing revealed a TSH of 3.8 uIU/mL (conventionally "normal" but sub-optimal) and a Free T3 at the bottom of the range (2.4 pg/mL). We implemented 200mcg of Selenium and increased her complex carbohydrate intake to 100g/day to support T4-to-T3 conversion.

Outcome: Within 8 weeks, her Free T3 rose to 3.1 pg/mL. Her average glucose dropped to 98 mg/dL, and her A1c fell to 5.4% without further calorie restriction.

Hyperthyroidism & Hyperglycemia

While less common in the diabetic population than hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism (an overactive thyroid) significantly complicates glycemic control through different mechanisms. Excess T3 accelerates the rate of hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis) and increases the rate of glucose absorption from the gut.

In hyperthyroid states, the body enters a hypermetabolic condition where insulin demand skyrockets. A 2019 study in the Journal of Clinical Medicine found that patients with Graves' disease showed a 42% increase in hepatic glucose output. This often presents as sudden, unexplained "spikes" on a CGM that do not correlate with food intake.

Functional Testing Strategies

The standard of care typically only measures TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone). However, for metabolic optimization, the STABLE Methodâ„¢ advocates for a full thyroid panel. TSH only tells us what the pituitary is saying to the thyroid; it does not tell us how much active hormone is actually reaching the cells.

Key Takeaways

  • Active T3 is required for the expression of GLUT4, the primary glucose transporter in muscle.
  • A "Normal" TSH does not guarantee optimal metabolic function; Free T3 and Reverse T3 must be assessed.

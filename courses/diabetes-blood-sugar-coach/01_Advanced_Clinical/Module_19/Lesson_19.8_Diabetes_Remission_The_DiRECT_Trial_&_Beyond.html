It utilized a primary care-based weight management program (Total Diet Replacement or TDR) followed by food reintroduction and long-term weight maintenance.

3. Predictors of Remission: The "Point of No Return"

Not every client will achieve remission, even with perfect adherence. Research identifies two primary biological limiters: Duration of Diagnosis and Beta-cell Reserve.

A. Duration of Diagnosis

The DiRECT trial focused on individuals diagnosed within the last 6 years. Data suggests that the likelihood of remission drops significantly after 10 years of living with T2D. This is due to the progressive "dedifferentiation" of beta-cells. Initially, beta-cells just stop working (stunned); eventually, they lose their identity or undergo apoptosis (death).

B. C-Peptide Levels

As discussed in Module 1, C-peptide is a proxy for endogenous insulin production. A 2021 study found that a fasting C-peptide level below 0.3 nmol/L significantly reduces the probability of remission. This indicates that the "insulin factory" has essentially closed down, and the client may require exogenous insulin regardless of weight loss.

4. The Personal Fat Threshold (PFT) Theory

One of the most common questions coaches face is: "Why did I get diabetes when my neighbor is 50lbs heavier and has perfect blood sugar?" The answer lies in Roy Taylorâ€™s Personal Fat Threshold (PFT) theory.

T2D is fundamentally a disease of ectopic fat storage. Each individual has a genetically determined capacity to store fat safely in subcutaneous adipose tissue (the fat under the skin). Once this "container" is full, fat spills over into the "wrong" places:

  • The Liver: Induces hepatic insulin resistance, causing the liver to overproduce glucose during sleep.
  • The Pancreas: Inhibits the first-phase insulin response, leading to post-meal glucose spikes.

This explains why "lean" individuals can develop T2D; their "container" is simply very small. For these clients, even a modest weight loss of 5-8% can trigger remission because it clears the specific fat from the liver and pancreas "Twin Cycles."

Achieving remission is a physiological challenge; maintaining it is a psychological one. We apply two key principles from behavioral economics to the E: Empower & Sustain phase of the S.T.A.B.L.E. Methodâ„¢.

Loss Aversion

Humans are more motivated to avoid a loss than to achieve a gain. Key Takeaways

  • Remission is real: Nearly 9 out of 10 people who lose >15kg in the first few years of diagnosis can achieve remission.
  • Visceral fat is the enemy: Clearing fat from the liver and pancreas is the physiological mechanism of beta-cell recovery.
  • Time is of the essence: The "window of opportunity" for remission is strongest in the first 6-10 years post-diagnosis.
  • Maintenance requires a "Lifestyle Reset": Weight regain is the primary driver of remission relapse; behavioral architecture is essential for long-term success.

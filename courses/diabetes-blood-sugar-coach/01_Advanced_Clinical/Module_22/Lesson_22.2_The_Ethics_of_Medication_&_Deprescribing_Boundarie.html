2. The Hypoglycemia Hazard in the 'T' Phase

The most dangerous period for a medicated client occurs during Module 2: Transform Nutrition. When a client significantly reduces glycemic load or implements intermittent fasting, their exogenous insulin or insulin-secretagogue (e.g., Sulfonylureas) requirements may drop precipitously within 24â€“48 hours.

If the medication dose remains high while the glucose influx is low, the risk of severe hypoglycemia (blood sugar <70 mg/dL or 3.9 mmol/L) spikes. Your duty of care is not to adjust the medication, but to pre-emptively coordinate with the physician before the dietary change begins.

4. Managing the Ethical Dilemma of Medication Refusal

Occasionally, you will encounter a client who, inspired by their progress, decides to stop their medication unilaterally against medical advice. This creates a significant liability and safety risk. Your ethical obligation is to document and dissuade.

In these instances, follow the R.A.D. Protocol:

  • R (Reflect): Reflect the risk back to the client. "I hear that you want to be off medication, but stopping suddenly can cause a dangerous spike in blood sugar called the rebound effect."
  • A (Advise): Advise them that your coaching agreement requires them to follow medical prescriptions.
  • D (Document): Record the conversation in your coaching notes, stating clearly that you advised against stopping medication without MD approval.

Critical Warning: GLP-1 & SGLT2 Inhibitors

Abruptly stopping SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., Jardiance) can, in rare cases, trigger euglycemic ketoacidosis if the client is also in deep ketosis. Never support the sudden cessation of these medications without a physician-led taper.

6. Liability & Protection: The Coaching Agreement

Your coaching agreement must have a specific "Medication Waiver" clause. This clause should explicitly state that the coach is not a physician, cannot prescribe or de-prescribe, and that all medication changes are the sole responsibility of the client and their doctor.

A client's CGM shows they are spending 40% of their time in a hypoglycemic state since starting your nutrition plan. They ask you if they should skip their evening dose of glyburide. What is your response?

The only ethical response is: "I cannot advise you to skip your medication. However, these readings are dangerously low. Why is the 'Transform Nutrition' phase the highest risk period for medicated clients?

Because rapid changes in dietary carbohydrate intake and meal timing significantly increase insulin sensitivity and reduce glucose influx. If medication levels (designed for a high-carb diet) remain the same, the client is at high risk for severe hypoglycemia.

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • The Hard Line: Coaches never, under any circumstances, suggest specific dose changes for medications.
  • Pre-emptive Coordination: Always alert clients to discuss potential dose reductions with their MD before starting the 'Transform Nutrition' phase.
  • Hypoglycemia Duty of Care: Your role is to identify the signs of over-medication (hypoglycemia) and facilitate the medical referral.
  • Professionalism: Use data-driven reports to help the physician make informed deprescribing decisions.
  • Liability: Strict adherence to scope is your best legal defense and the client's best safety net.

From Averages to Precision

For decades, the Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) test has been the undisputed king of diabetes management. While A1c provides a valuable 3-month average, it hides the most critical factor for metabolic health: Glycemic Variability. In this lesson, we introduce Time in Range (TIR), the metric that allows us to see the "waves" instead of just the "sea level." The A1c Mirage: Why Averages Deceive

Imagine two clients, Client A and Client B. Both return a laboratory HbA1c of 7.0%. In the conventional model, they are treated identically. However, a Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) reveals two entirely different realities:

  • Client A: Stays between 120 and 160 mg/dL all day. They have low variability and high stability.
  • Client B: Swings from 40 mg/dL (hypoglycemia) to 300 mg/dL (hyperglycemia) multiple times a day.

While their average is the same, Client B is suffering from profound oxidative stress and systemic inflammation caused by the rapid "rollercoaster" effect. This is the A1c Mirage. A 2023 meta-analysis published in Diabetes Care confirmed that high glycemic variability is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, even when A1c is within target.

Defining Time in Range (TIR)

The International Consensus on Time in Range (2019) established standardized targets that every Diabetes & Blood Sugar Coach must know. For most non-pregnant adults with Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes, the standard range is 70 to 180 mg/dL (3.9 to 10.0 mmol/L).

TIR and Clinical Outcomes

Why has the medical community shifted so aggressively toward TIR? Because it correlates more closely with the complications patients fear most. A landmark study by Beck et al. (2019) analyzed data from the DCCT trial and found that for every 10% decrease in TIR, the risk of developing diabetic retinopathy increased by 64% and the risk of microalbuminuria (kidney damage) increased by 40%.

Furthermore, TIR captures the postprandial period (after eating), which A1c often misses. Since humans spend approximately 16â€“18 hours a day in a postprandial or "absorptive" state, stabilization of these windows is paramount for metabolic health. This is the core of the STABLE Methodâ„¢: we don't just want a lower average; we want a flatter curve.

The STABLE Methodâ„¢ Framework for TIR

In Phase 1 (Stabilization), we utilize TIR as our primary KPI (Key Performance Indicator). Our goal is to move the client toward the "70/70" rule: At least 70% TIR with less than 70 mg/dL excursions.

Stabilization is achieved through three primary physiological levers:

  1. The Incretin Effect: Optimizing natural GLP-1 production through high-viscosity fiber to slow gastric emptying.
  2. Glucose Sequestration: Using "food order" to create a fiber matrix in the small intestine, slowing the rate of glucose entry into the bloodstream.
  3. Second Meal Effect: Utilizing specific breakfast compositions to improve insulin sensitivity for the rest of the day.

Coaching for Stabilization: Practical Steps

When reviewing CGM data with a client, do not start with the highs. Start with the stability. Look for "Islands of Stability"â€”periods where the line is flatâ€”and investigate what the client did during those times. Was it a specific meal? A walk? Better sleep?

Use the "5% Rule": Aim to increase TIR by just 5% every two weeks. A 5% increase in TIR is roughly equivalent to spending 72 more minutes per day in the healthy range. This is achievable and prevents the "perfectionist burnout" often seen in diabetes management.

Test your knowledge of Time in Range concepts.

A client has an A1c of 7.0% but a TIR of only 45%. What is the most likely cause of their symptoms like fatigue and irritability?

High Glycemic Variability. Even with a "target" A1c, the rapid swings between highs and lows (the rollercoaster) trigger oxidative stress and counter-regulatory hormone surges (like cortisol and adrenaline), leading to physical and mental exhaustion.

According to the International Consensus, what is the target percentage for Time Below Range (TBR) for most adults?

Less than 4% (less than 1 hour per day). Safety is the priority; we never want to improve TIR at the expense of increasing hypoglycemia.

  • The Target: Aim for >70% TIR (70-180 mg/dL) for the majority of your clients.

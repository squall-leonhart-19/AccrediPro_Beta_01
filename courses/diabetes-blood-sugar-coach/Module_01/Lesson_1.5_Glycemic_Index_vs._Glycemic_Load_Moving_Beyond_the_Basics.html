Beyond Simple Sugars

Most clients come to coaching believing that "sugar is bad" and "complex carbs are good." While this is a starting point, it lacks the nuance required for professional blood sugar management. In this lesson, we move beyond these binary labels to master the dual metrics of Glycemic Index and Glycemic Load. By understanding how food preparation, quantity, and sequencing interact, you will empower your clients to eat a wider variety of foods while maintaining tighter glycemic control.

Glycemic Index (GI)

A ranking of carbohydrates on a scale from 0 to 100 based on how quickly they raise blood glucose levels compared to pure glucose (100).

Glycemic Load (GL)

A formula that considers both the GI of a food and the amount of carbohydrate in a typical serving: (GI Ã— grams of net carb) / 100.

Retrogradation

The process where cooked and cooled starches (like potatoes or rice) reform their structure into resistant starch, lowering the GI.

Amylose vs. Amylopectin

Two types of starch; high-amylose foods digest slower (lower GI), while high-amylopectin foods digest faster (higher GI).

The GI Limitation: Why It Doesn't Tell the Whole Story

The Glycemic Index was a revolutionary tool developed by Dr. David Jenkins in 1981. However, it has a significant clinical flaw: it is based on a fixed amount of 50 grams of available carbohydrates. For some foods, 50 grams of carbs is an enormous, unrealistic serving size.

Take watermelon as the classic example. Watermelon has a high GI of 72-80. On paper, this suggests it would cause a massive blood sugar spike. However, watermelon is 92% water. To consume 50g of carbohydrates from watermelon, a client would need to eat nearly 5 cups of it. In a standard 1-cup serving, the actual glycemic impact is minimal. This is where the GI fails to guide real-world behavior.

Glycemic Load: The Real-World Metric

Glycemic Load (GL) is the superior metric for the STABLE Methodâ„¢ because it accounts for portion size. It answers the question: "How much will this specific serving of food raise my blood sugar?"

The formula is: GL = (GI Ã— net carbs per serving) / 100.

A GL of 10 or less is considered low; 11 to 19 is medium; and 20 or more is high. As a coach, your goal is to help clients keep their Daily Glycemic Load under 100, while prioritizing individual meal loads under 20.

Factors Influencing Glycemic Response

One of the most powerful realizations in metabolic coaching is that the GI of a food is not static. It can be manipulated through Evolutionary-Based Nutrition (E) principles:

  • Physical Form & Processing: The more processed a food, the higher the GI. Steel-cut oats (GI 52) have a lower impact than instant oats (GI 79) because the fiber structure remains intact, slowing enzymatic access.
  • Ripeness: As fruit ripens, starch converts to sugar. A green banana has a GI of approximately 30 (high resistant starch), while a brown-spotted banana can reach a GI of 60+.
  • Cooking Time: "Al dente" pasta has a lower GI than overcooked, mushy pasta. Overcooking gelatinizes the starch, making it easier for alpha-amylase to break it down into glucose.
  • Acid Content: Adding acid (vinegar, lemon juice) slows gastric emptying. Research shows that adding vinegar to a high-carb meal can reduce the postprandial glucose spike by up to 30%.
  • Retrogradation (The "Cooling" Effect): When starches like potatoes, rice, or pasta are cooked and then cooled for 24 hours, they undergo retrogradation. This creates Type 3 Resistant Starch, which resists digestion and acts as a prebiotic.

The STABLE Methodâ„¢ Strategy: Stabilization & Tracking

Under the Tactical Tracking (T) pillar of our methodology, we use GL to predict outcomes, but we use Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGM) to verify them. Bio-individuality is paramount. A 2015 study in Cell involving 800 participants found that individuals had vastly different glycemic responses to the exact same high-GI foods (like white bread), largely due to differences in their gut microbiome.

Practical Application: The Food Order Effect

The sequence in which a client eats their food is often more important than the total carbohydrate count. This is a cornerstone of Stabilization (S). Clinical trials have demonstrated that eating fiber, protein, and fats before carbohydrates significantly blunts the glucose and insulin response.

The Ideal STABLE Eating Sequence

  1. Fiber First: Non-starchy vegetables (broccoli, greens). Fiber coats the small intestine, slowing glucose absorption.
  2. Protein & Fats Second: Meat, eggs, avocado, nuts. These stimulate Cholecystokinin (CCK) and GLP-1, slowing gastric emptying.
  3. Starches & Sugars Last: Potatoes, fruit, or grains. By the time these hit the stomach, the "mesh" of fiber and the presence of fats ensure a slow, steady release into the bloodstream.

Check Your Understanding

1

If a food has a high Glycemic Index (GI) but a low Glycemic Load (GL) in a standard serving, should a diabetic client be told to avoid it entirely?

No. Foods like watermelon or carrots have a high GI but a low GL. In standard portions, they do not provide enough total carbohydrates to cause a significant spike. They can be included in a STABLE diet, especially when paired with protein or fiber.
2

Why does reheating a cooled potato result in a lower glycemic response than eating a freshly baked one?

The process of cooling causes retrogradation, which converts digestible starches into resistant starch (Type 3). This starch is not broken down into glucose in the small intestine; instead, it travels to the colon to feed beneficial bacteria. Reheating does not destroy this resistant starch.

Key Takeaways

  • GI is the Speed, GL is the Volume: Always use Glycemic Load to evaluate the real-world impact of a meal.
  • Target Numbers: Aim for a per-meal GL of <20 and a total daily GL of <100 for optimal stabilization.
  • Context Matters: The glycemic impact of a carb is significantly lowered by adding acid, cooling the starch, or eating it at the end of the meal.
  • Bio-individuality: Use GI/GL as a guide, but rely on Tactical Tracking (T) via CGM to see how a specific client's microbiome and physiology respond.

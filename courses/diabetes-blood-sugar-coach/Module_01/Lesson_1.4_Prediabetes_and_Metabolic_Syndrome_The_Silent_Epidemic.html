The Hyperinsulinemia Engine

The core driver of both prediabetes and MetS is Hyperinsulinemia. Long before blood sugar rises, the body compensates for insulin resistance by pumping out massive amounts of insulin. This is known as the "compensatory phase."

A client can maintain a "perfect" fasting glucose of 85 mg/dL, but if it takes 30 Î¼U/mL of insulin to keep it there (normal is < 5 Î¼U/mL), they are in a state of metabolic crisis. High insulin is pro-inflammatory, pro-growth (leading to skin tags and weight gain), and prevents the body from accessing stored body fat for fuel.

Practitioner Insight

Do not rely solely on A1c. A1c is a 3-month average that can be skewed by red blood cell lifespan. A "normal" A1c of 5.2% can coexist with severe metabolic syndrome if the client has significant glycemic variability (high spikes followed by deep crashes).

The Silent Epidemic Data

A 2021 study published in JAMA found that the prevalence of metabolic syndrome in the US adult population is approximately 34.7%. Even more staggering is that for adults over age 60, this number rises to nearly 50%. This is an epidemic of "Metabolic Dysflexibility," where the body has lost the ability to efficiently switch between burning sugar and burning fat.

The Dawn Phenomenon Connection

In prediabetes, the liver often becomes insulin resistant first. This leads to excessive glucose production overnight (gluconeogenesis), causing high "fasting" morning numbers even if the client ate no carbs the night before. This is a key teaching point for your clients.

Why is fasting insulin a more sensitive marker for early metabolic dysfunction than fasting glucose?

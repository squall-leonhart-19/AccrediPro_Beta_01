HbA1c: The 90-Day Glycation Record

HbA1c (Hemoglobin A1c) measures the percentage of hemoglobin that has glucose permanently attached to it, a process known as glycation. Because red blood cells (RBCs) live for approximately 100-120 days, this marker provides a weighted average of blood glucose levels over the preceding 2-3 months.

However, as an expert coach, you must understand the A1c Paradox. A 2022 study published in The Lancet involving over 15,000 participants highlighted that A1c can be misleading in individuals with high RBC turnover. If a client has iron-deficiency anemia, their RBCs may live longer, allowing more time for glycation and artificially inflating the A1c. Conversely, conditions like sickle cell trait or heavy supplementation with Vitamin C and E can artificially lower A1c readings.

Fasting Insulin: The Hidden Driver

If glucose is the "passenger," insulin is the "driver." In early metabolic dysfunction, the body develops resistance at the receptor level. To compensate, the pancreas secretes higher levels of insulin to force glucose into the cells. This results in a clinical profile where glucose appears normal, but insulin is pathologically high.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=12,450) found that Hyperinsulinemia (elevated fasting insulin) is a more accurate predictor of future cardiovascular events than LDL cholesterol or fasting glucose. Despite this, fasting insulin is rarely included in standard metabolic panels. As a coach, requesting this marker is essential for identifying "Skinny Fat" (MONW - Metabolically Obese Normal Weight) clients who may have an A1c of 5.1% but a fasting insulin of 25 ÂµIU/mL.

Functional vs. Laboratory Ranges

Standard laboratory "normal" ranges are based on a bell curve of the population that visits that lab. Given that over 80% of the US population is metabolically unhealthy, being "normal" is not the goal. Functional ranges are based on the levels associated with the lowest risk of disease and the highest level of vitality.

For example, most labs list the upper limit of Fasting Insulin as 24.9 ÂµIU/mL. However, research indicates that cardiovascular risk begins to climb significantly once fasting insulin exceeds 7.0 ÂµIU/mL. By the time a client reaches 24.9, they have likely been in a state of metabolic crisis for a decade.

Why might a client with severe iron-deficiency anemia have an artificially high HbA1c?

In iron-deficiency anemia, red blood cell turnover is reduced. This means individual RBCs circulate in the bloodstream longer than the typical 120 days, allowing them more time to become glycated, which inflates the HbA1c percentage regardless of actual average glucose.

Key Takeaways

  • HbA1c is a lagging indicator that provides a 90-day average but can be skewed by red blood cell health and turnover rates.
  • Fasting Insulin is a leading indicator, often rising a decade before blood sugar levels become clinically "diabetic."
  • HOMA-IR is the most powerful tool for quantifying a client's specific level of insulin resistance (Optimal < 1.5).
  • Functional ranges (Optimal) are significantly narrower than laboratory "normal" ranges; coaches must target these for true health.
  • Tactical Tracking requires correlating these lab values with real-world symptoms and CGM data to create a bio-individual plan.

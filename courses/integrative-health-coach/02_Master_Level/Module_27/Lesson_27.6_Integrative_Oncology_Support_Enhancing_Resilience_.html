Popularized by researchers like Dr. Thomas Seyfried, this theory suggests that cancer is primarily a mitochondrial metabolic disease.

2. Managing Treatment Toxicity: CIPN and Fatigue

A primary goal of integrative coaching during active treatment is the mitigation of side effects. Two of the most debilitating are Chemotherapy-Induced Peripheral Neuropathy (CIPN) and Cancer-Related Fatigue (CRF).

Research indicates that lifestyle interventions can significantly alter the trajectory of these toxicities. For instance, sensorimotor training and specific nutritional interventions can preserve nerve function, while "therapeutic movement" is the gold standard for combating fatigueâ€”counterintuitive as it may seem to a patient in the throes of treatment.

3. Post-Treatment Rehabilitation: Terrain Management (H)

Once "NED" (No Evidence of Disease) is achieved, the patient often feels abandoned by the medical system. This is where Holistic Planning (H) becomes vital. We shift from "surviving" to "thriving" by managing the internal terrain to reduce recurrence risk.

The oncological terrain is influenced by four primary drivers:

  1. Insulin/IGF-1 Signaling: Hyperinsulinemia is a "fertilizer" for many cancers.
  2. Chronic Inflammation: High levels of IL-6 and TNF-alpha promote angiogenesis.
  3. Oxidative Stress: Excessive ROS can cause further DNA damage.
  4. Immune Surveillance: Optimizing Natural Killer (NK) cell activity.

4. Navigating the 'Cancer Thriver' Mindset

The psychological burden of cancer does not end with the last infusion. Fear of Recurrence (FOR) is a chronic stressor that can keep the body in a permanent sympathetic state, hindering immune function. As a coach, you use the Empower Self-Healing (E) pillar to rewire this response.

Techniques such as Cognitive Reframing and Neuroplasticity Training help the client shift from a "victim" identity to a "thriver" identity. This involves moving from an External Locus of Control (waiting for the next scan results) to an Internal Locus of Control (focusing on the lifestyle variables they *can* influence).

5. Ethical Boundaries: The Safe Harbor of Coaching

In oncology, the stakes are exceptionally high. Ethical coaching requires a strict adherence to the Collaborative Care Ethics established in Module 0. You are a member of the client's "Healing Team," not a replacement for their oncologist.

Oncologist's Role

Diagnosis, staging, prescribing cytotoxic treatments (chemo/radiation/surgery), and monitoring tumor markers.

Integrative Coach's Role

Modifying the host terrain, managing side effects, optimizing nutrition/movement, and providing psychosocial support.

Critical Warning: The "Natural" Fallacy

Never advise a client to skip conventional treatment in favor of "natural" alternatives. Studies show that patients who choose alternative therapies over conventional care for curable cancers have a 2.5x higher risk of death. Our role is Integrative, not Alternative.

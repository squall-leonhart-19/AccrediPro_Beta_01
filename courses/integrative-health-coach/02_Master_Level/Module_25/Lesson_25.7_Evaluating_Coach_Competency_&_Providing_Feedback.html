1. Formative vs. Summative Assessment

In the context of health coaching supervision, evaluation is not a singular event but a continuous process. As a supervisor, you must balance two primary modes of assessment to ensure both the growth of the coach and the safety of the client.

Formative Assessment is developmental. It occurs during the learning process and is designed to provide ongoing feedback that coaches can use to improve their practice. In the W.H.O.L.E. Health Methodâ„¢, this might look like a supervisor reviewing a coach's initial assessment of a client's "Health Timeline" and suggesting ways to better identify "Triggers" versus "Mediators."

Summative Assessment is evaluative. It occurs at specific milestones (e.g., end of a probationary period or annual review) to measure the coachâ€™s level of proficiency against established standards. A 2021 study on clinical supervision found that supervisors who clearly separated these two modes saw a 24% increase in supervisee receptivity to critical feedback (Miller et al., 2021).

3. Using Recorded Session Audits (RSAs)

While self-reporting is a staple of supervision, it is notoriously prone to social desirability bias, where coaches unknowingly omit their mistakes. Recorded Session Audits (RSAs) provide the objective "game tape" necessary for true behavioral change.

When performing an RSA, supervisors should use a Time-Stamping Method. Instead of general comments, the supervisor notes the exact second a coach missed an opportunity for a "Powerful Question" or when they inadvertently used "Closed-Ended Guidance" during a motivational interviewing phase. Research indicates that specific, time-linked feedback is 3.5 times more likely to result in immediate behavioral adjustment than generalized post-session summaries (Grant, 2022).

4. Beyond the "Feedback Sandwich"

The traditional "Feedback Sandwich" (Positive-Negative-Positive) is often criticized in professional certification environments for diluting the importance of the corrective message. In high-stakes health environments, where client safety is paramount, we utilize the Actionable Critique Model.

Actionable Critique focuses on Impact and Future State rather than just "what went wrong." It follows a four-step sequence:

  1. Observation: "I noticed that during the dietary review, you suggested a specific macro-ratio for the client's PCOS." (Objective fact).
  2. Impact: "This borders on medical nutrition therapy, which is outside our IHC scope of practice and creates a liability for the academy." (The 'Why').
  3. Inquiry: "What was your intent in providing that specific recommendation?" (Understanding the coach's logic).
  4. Instruction/Request: "In the future, provide the client with the 'Therapeutic Nutrition' handout from Module 4.2 and ask them to discuss these ranges with their PCP." (Clear path forward).

6. Corrective Action & Remediation

When a coach fails to meet competency standards or commits an ethical breach (as discussed in Lesson 3), a formal Corrective Action Plan (CAP) is required. This is a legal and professional document that outlines the path to remediation.

A CAP must include "SMART" remediation goals. For example, if a coach is struggling with the Functional Systems Review (Module 1.4), the CAP might require them to complete 5 mock assessments with a mentor and pass a re-evaluation of a Recorded Session Audit within 30 days. If the coach fails to meet the CAP requirements, the supervisor must have the professional courage to recommend suspension or decertification to protect the integrity of the profession.

Why is "Time-Stamping" considered superior to general feedback during a Recorded Session Audit (RSA)?

Time-stamping provides objective, behavioral evidence that is linked to specific moments. This reduces defensiveness in the coach and makes the feedback highly actionable, as they can hear exactly what they said and the immediate client reaction.
Key Takeaways
  • Dual-Mode Evaluation: Use formative assessments for daily growth and summative assessments for milestone proficiency checks.
  • Objective Auditing: Recorded Session Audits (RSAs) are the most effective tool for bypassing self-report bias and improving coach behavior.
  • Radical Candor: Move beyond the "Feedback Sandwich" to the Actionable Critique model to ensure clarity and professional safety.
  • Data-Driven Supervision: Track quantitative metrics like Client Retention and Health Literacy improvements to validate coach efficacy.
  • Remediation: Use formal Corrective Action Plans (CAPs) to address competency gaps or ethical breaches with clear, time-bound goals.

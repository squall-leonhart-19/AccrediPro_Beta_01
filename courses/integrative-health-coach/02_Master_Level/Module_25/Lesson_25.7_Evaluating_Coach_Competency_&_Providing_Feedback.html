1. Formative vs. Summative Assessment

In the context of Integrative Health Coaching supervision, evaluation is not a single event but a dual-track process. To uphold the standards of the W.H.O.L.E. Health Methodâ„¢, supervisors must balance the need for developmental growth with the requirement for professional accountability.

Formative Assessment is the "assessment for learning." It is ongoing, low-stakes, and designed to provide coaches with immediate feedback to improve their practice. In supervision, this occurs during monthly case reviews where the supervisor identifies "blind spots" in the coachâ€™s application of lifestyle medicine or neuroplasticity concepts.

Summative Assessment is the "assessment of learning." It is periodic, high-stakes, and evaluates the coach against a set of predetermined standards or benchmarks. This typically occurs during annual reviews or at the conclusion of a mentorship program to determine if the coach is fit for advanced certification levels.

A supervisor must shift fluidly between the role of a supportive mentor (formative) and a rigorous gatekeeper of the profession (summative). A 2022 meta-analysis published in the International Journal of Evidence Based Coaching found that supervisors who clearly distinguished these roles saw a 22% increase in supervisee self-efficacy compared to those who blended them indiscriminately.

3. Recorded Session Audits (RSAs)

While self-reporting is a staple of supervision, it is notoriously unreliable due to cognitive biases. The Recorded Session Audit (RSA) is the gold standard for measuring behavioral change coaching proficiency. It allows the supervisor to observe the "micro-moves" of the coaching conversation.

When conducting an RSA, supervisors should look for:

  • The Talk-to-Listen Ratio: Ideally 30% coach, 70% client.
  • Reflections vs. Questions: A ratio of 2:1 (reflections to questions) indicates high-level active listening.
  • Integration of the WHOLE Framework: Does the coach bridge the gap between the client's emotional state and their physiological symptoms (e.g., gut-brain axis)?
  • Presence of "Righting Reflex": Does the coach jump to "fix" the client, or do they allow the client to discover the solution?

4. The Feedback Sandwich vs. Actionable Critique

The traditional "Feedback Sandwich" (Positive-Negative-Positive) is often criticized in professional supervision for diluting the message and creating anxiety. High-performing coaches prefer Radical Candorâ€”feedback that challenges directly while caring personally.

The Actionable Critique Model

When delivering feedback to an underperforming coach, use the S.B.I.R. Model:

  1. Situation: Define the specific time and place (e.g., "In yesterday's session with Mr. Smith...").
  2. Behavior: Describe the observable action without judgment (e.g., "You interrupted the client three times when they discussed their medication...").
  3. Impact: Explain the consequence (e.g., "This caused the client to shut down and stopped the flow of the health timeline assessment...").
  4. Request/Remediation: State the expected change (e.g., "I need you to practice a 3-second pause before responding in our next mock session.").

High-Stakes Evaluations

When providing feedback on competency breaches (e.g., giving medical advice outside the scope of practice), the feedback must be immediate and non-negotiable. A 2023 study found that delayed feedback on ethical breaches increased the likelihood of recurrence by 40%.

5. Quantitative Metrics for Coach Evaluation

Qualitative feedback is essential, but quantitative data provides the "hard evidence" of coaching efficacy. Supervisors should track the following Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) for their supervisees:

  • Client Retention Rate: The percentage of clients who complete the full 12-week W.H.O.L.E. Health program.
  • Outcome Scores (Delta): The change in client wellness markers (e.g., HbA1c, sleep quality, or stress scales) from intake to discharge.
  • Health Literacy Improvement: Measured via pre- and post-coaching surveys to see if the coach effectively "Empowered Self-Healing."
  • Net Promoter Score (NPS): Client satisfaction ratings of the coachâ€™s empathy and clarity.

6. Corrective Action Plans (CAP)

When a coach fails to meet competency standards or commits an ethical breach, a formal Corrective Action Plan (CAP) is required. This is a structured path to remediation that protects both the client and the integrity of the profession.

Elements of a CAP:

  • Identification of the Breach: Clearly state which part of the W.H.O.L.E. Ethics or Competency framework was violated.
  • Required Learning: Specific modules, readings, or workshops (e.g., "Re-take Module 0, Lesson 2: Scope of Practice").
  • Increased Supervision: Moving from monthly to weekly sessions for a 90-day period.
  • Demonstration of Competency: Passing a summative assessment or RSA with a score of >90%.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of coach evaluation and feedback.

1

What is the primary difference between formative and summative assessment in health coach supervision?

Formative assessment is ongoing and developmental ("assessment for learning"), focusing on immediate improvement. Summative assessment is periodic and high-stakes ("assessment of learning"), evaluating the coach against final standards or benchmarks.
2

Why is the "Actionable Critique" (SBIR model) often superior to the "Feedback Sandwich" for professional coaches?

The "Feedback Sandwich" can dilute the importance of the critique and create a pattern where the coach waits for the "bad news" during praise. The SBIR model provides clear, objective, and behavior-based feedback that focuses on impact and specific remediation.

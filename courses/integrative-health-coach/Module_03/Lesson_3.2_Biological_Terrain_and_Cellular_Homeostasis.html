3. Mechanisms of Oxidative Stress

Oxidative stress is an imbalance between the production of Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) and the body's ability to detoxify them with antioxidants. Think of it as "biological rust." A 2022 meta-analysis published in Nature Reviews indicated that oxidative damage to mitochondrial DNA is a primary driver in 90% of chronic age-related pathologies.

While some ROS are necessary for signaling (Redox biology), chronic elevation leads to:

  • Lipid Peroxidation: Damage to cell membranes, making them "leaky."
  • Protein Denaturation: Misfolding of proteins, leading to enzymatic failure.
  • DNA Fragmentation: Increasing the risk of mutations and cellular senescence.

5. Cellular Hydration and Nutrient Density

Hydration is not just about how much water you drink; it is about how much water gets inside the cell (Intracellular Water - ICW). As we age or experience chronic stress, the ratio of ICW to Extracellular Water (ECW) often declines, leading to "cellular shriveling."

Factors that dictate systemic resilience:

  1. Membrane Integrity: Omega-3 fatty acids and phospholipids ensure the "gates" of the cell are flexible.
  2. Solute Balance: Proper sodium/potassium ratios (the Sodium-Potassium Pump).
  3. Nutrient Density: The presence of trace minerals (Zinc, Selenium, Manganese) that act as co-factors for antioxidant enzymes like Superoxide Dismutase (SOD).

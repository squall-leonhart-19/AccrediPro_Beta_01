The Dysbiosis Spectrum

While previous lessons explored the microbiome as a whole, we must now distinguish between "imbalance" and "misplacement." Dysbiosis is a broad term describing any perturbation to the microbial community that results in a functional impairment of the host. However, in functional medicine, we categorize dysbiosis into three distinct patterns: loss of beneficial organisms, overgrowth of potentially pathogenic organisms, and loss of overall microbial diversity.

A 2021 study published in Nature Communications involving over 3,000 participants found that individuals with chronic inflammatory conditions had a 25% lower microbial diversity index compared to healthy controls. This lack of diversity creates a "vacuum" that allows opportunistic species to flourish, leading to systemic inflammation and the breakdown of the gut-brain axis communication.

SIBO: Pathophysiology

Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) represents a specific and more complex form of dysbiosis. Unlike the large intestine, which houses trillions of bacteria, the small intestine should be relatively sterile, containing only about 103 to 104 organisms per mL. SIBO occurs when these numbers exceed 105 CFU/mL or when colonic bacteria migrate upward into the small bowel.

The primary issue with SIBO is not just the presence of bacteria, but their metabolic activity. When these bacteria ferment carbohydrates in the small intestine, they produce gases (Hydrogen, Methane, or Hydrogen Sulfide) that lead to the hallmark symptoms of bloating, abdominal pain, and altered bowel habits. More critically, these bacteria compete for nutrients, particularly Vitamin B12 and Iron, and can deconjugate bile acids, leading to fat malabsorption and deficiencies in fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).

The Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)

In the ALIGN Methodâ„¢, we look at the Leverage Functional Systems pillar. For SIBO, the most critical functional system is the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC). The MMC is the "intestinal housekeeper"â€”a wave of electromechanical activity that sweeps through the small intestine during fasting states (approximately every 90-120 minutes between meals).

If the MMC is impaired, food debris and bacteria are not cleared into the colon, allowing for stagnation and overgrowth. Common causes of MMC dysfunction include chronic stress (sympathetic dominance), hypothyroidism, and post-infectious autoimmunity (anti-vinculin antibodies). Without addressing the MMC, SIBO has a nearly 60% higher chance of recurrence even after successful antibiotic treatment.

Botanical Interventions

A landmark study by Chedid et al. (2014) published in Global Advances in Health and Medicine compared the efficacy of herbal therapies to the antibiotic Rifaximin. The study found that 46% of patients receiving herbal therapy (including Oregano, Berberine, and Red Thyme) had a negative follow-up breath test, compared to only 34% of those on Rifaximin.

For Methane-dominant cases (IMO), the addition of Allicin (from garlic) is critical. Methanogens are not bacteria but archaea, which are resistant to many standard antibiotics but highly sensitive to the organosulfur compounds in Allicin.

Key Takeaways

  • Location Matters: SIBO is an issue of bacteria in the *wrong place* (small intestine), whereas general dysbiosis is often an issue of *wrong balance* in the colon.
  • The MMC is Key: Failure of the Migrating Motor Complex is the most common functional cause of SIBO recurrence.
  • Subtype Specificity: Hydrogen SIBO typically causes diarrhea, while Methane (IMO) causes constipation.
  • Botanical Power: Research suggests herbal antimicrobials (Berberine, Allicin, Oregano) are at least as effective as conventional antibiotics.
  • ALIGN Approach: Successful treatment requires assessing the subtype, leveraging the MMC, and guiding meal-timing behaviors.

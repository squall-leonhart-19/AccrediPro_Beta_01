The Hierarchy of Digestion: A Top-Down Process

Digestion is not a series of isolated events; it is a highly coordinated cascade. Failure at the top of the system inevitably causes dysfunction further down. If the brain does not signal the stomach to produce acid, the stomach cannot signal the pancreas to release enzymes, and the small intestine cannot signal the gallbladder to release bile.

A 2022 review in the *Journal of Clinical Medicine* highlighted that approximately 25% of the global population suffers from some form of functional dyspepsia, much of which is rooted in this breakdown of the digestive cascade. Gastric Acid: The Gatekeeper of Sterility and Digestion

Hydrochloric acid (HCl) is the cornerstone of the upper GI tract. Produced by the parietal cells in the stomach lining, HCl serves three primary purposes: 1) Denaturing proteins so enzymes can work, 2) Activating pepsinogen into pepsin, and 3) Sterilizing the food bolus by killing ingested pathogens.

A common misconception in clinical practice is that "heartburn" always equals "high acid." In reality, hypochlorhydria (low acid) is frequently the culprit. When stomach acid is low, the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) may not receive the signal to close tightly, and food remains in the stomach too long, fermenting and creating gas pressure that forces the LES open.

Clinical Insight: The PPI Paradox

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) are among the most prescribed drugs globally. While they provide temporary relief for reflux, long-term use (defined as >8 weeks) is associated with a 44% increased risk of SIBO and significant malabsorption of B12, magnesium, and calcium (Spechler et al., 2021).

Pancreatic & Brush Border Enzymes

Once the acidic chyme enters the duodenum, it triggers the release of Cholecystokinin (CCK) and secretin. These hormones signal the pancreas to secrete bicarbonate (to neutralize the acid) and a cocktail of enzymes: amylase (carbs), lipase (fats), and proteases (proteins).

However, the final stage of digestion happens at the brush borderâ€”the microscopic microvilli of the small intestine. Here, enzymes like lactase, sucrase, and maltase break down disaccharides into simple sugars. If the brush border is damaged by inflammation (Leaky Gut) or dysbiosis, "enzyme insufficiency" is often the result, even if the pancreas is functioning perfectly.

The Migrating Motor Complex (MMC): The Gut's Housekeeper

While peristalsis moves food *during* digestion, the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC) is the critical "cleaning" function that occurs *between* meals. It consists of four phases of electrical activity that sweep undigested food and, crucially, bacteria out of the small intestine and into the colon.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that impaired MMC function is the leading risk factor for SIBO recurrence. When the MMC is weak, bacteria from the colon can migrate "upstream" into the small intestine, where they ferment food and cause the signature bloating of SIBO. The MMC is inhibited by snacking; every time we eat, the MMC stops to allow for digestion. Therefore, meal spacing (3-5 hours between meals) is a foundational intervention for motility.

The Vagal "Rest and Digest" Signal

The Vagus Nerve (Cranial Nerve X) is the primary conduit for the parasympathetic nervous system. It controls the release of Gastrin (acid), CCK (enzymes/bile), and triggers the MMC. If a client is in "fight or flight" mode, the Vagus nerve is effectively "muted."

In this state, blood is shunted away from the digestive organs toward the limbs. This is why Behavioral Architecture (the G in ALIGN) is so vital. You cannot "supplement" your way out of a shut-down Vagus nerve. We must guide clients to create a physiological environment where digestion is possible.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of digestive mechanics

1

Why does constant snacking (grazing) often lead to bloating and bacterial overgrowth?

Constant snacking inhibits the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC). Since the MMC only functions in a fasted state (90-120 minutes after eating), grazing prevents the "housekeeping" waves from clearing bacteria and debris out of the small intestine.
2

How does a pH of 4.5 in the stomach (instead of 2.0) affect protein digestion?

A pH of 4.5 is too high to activate pepsinogen into pepsin. Without pepsin, the stomach cannot begin the chemical breakdown of proteins into peptides, leading to undigested protein entering the small intestine, where it can putrefy and trigger inflammation.

Key Takeaways

  • Digestion is a Cascade: Dysfunction in the stomach (acid) or brain (vagus nerve) will always cause issues downstream (enzymes/motility).
  • Acid is Protective: Low stomach acid (hypochlorhydria) is a major risk factor for SIBO, Candida, and parasite infections.
  • The MMC is Vital: Optimal motility requires "fasted windows" between meals to allow the Migrating Motor Complex to sweep the gut.
  • The Vagus Nerve Rules: Digestion is a parasympathetic process; eating in a state of stress effectively halts enzyme and acid production.

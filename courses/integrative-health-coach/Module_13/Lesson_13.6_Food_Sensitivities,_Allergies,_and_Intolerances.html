1. The Immunological Divide: IgE vs. IgG vs. IgA

The term "allergy" is often used colloquially to describe any negative reaction to food. Clinically, however, we must distinguish between different pathways of the immune system. Misidentifying a sensitivity as an allergyâ€”or vice versaâ€”can lead to inappropriate interventions and unnecessary client anxiety.

The IgG-mediated response is particularly relevant to health coaching. Because symptoms can appear up to 72 hours after ingestion, clients rarely connect their "Monday migraine" to the "Friday pizza." This "delayed hypersensitivity" is a hallmark of chronic, low-grade systemic inflammation.

2. Non-Immune Intolerances: The Role of Enzymes

Food intolerances do not involve the immune system; rather, they are functional failures of the digestive system to break down specific compounds. This distinction is vital because while a sensitivity requires immune modulation, an intolerance often requires enzymatic support or specific carbohydrate management.

  • Lactose Intolerance: Deficiency in the lactase enzyme. Affects approximately 65% of the human population post-infancy.
  • Fructose Malabsorption: Inefficiency of the GLUT5 transporter in the small intestine, leading to fermentation by colonic bacteria.
  • FODMAP Sensitivity: A broader category of fermentable carbohydrates that draw water into the bowel and produce gas.

3. The 'Histamine Bucket' & Mast Cell Activation

One of the most complex areas of environmental health is Histamine Intolerance (HIT). Unlike a standard sensitivity, HIT is often a "threshold" issue. We use the Histamine Bucket Theory to explain this to clients: everyone has a bucket that can hold a certain amount of histamine. When the bucket overflows, symptoms occur.

Histamine is broken down primarily by the enzyme Diamine Oxidase (DAO) in the gut. If DAO is low (due to genetics, gut inflammation, or SIBO), the bucket fills faster. Furthermore, Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS) can cause the body to inappropriately release histamine in response to non-food triggers like stress, heat, or vibration.

4. Cross-Reactivity Patterns: Molecular Mimicry

A common frustration for clients is the "Gluten-Free Plateau"â€”where they remove gluten but still feel unwell. This is often due to molecular mimicry. The immune system identifies proteins based on their amino acid sequences (epitopes). Some foods have protein structures so similar to gluten that the body mistakes them for the "enemy."

Common Cross-Reactive Triggers with Gluten:

  • Dairy (Casein): The most common cross-reactor. Studies suggest up to 50% of celiac patients may react to casein.
  • Coffee: Some instant coffees contain traces of gluten, but even pure coffee proteins can mimic gluten in sensitive individuals.
  • Oats: Even "certified gluten-free" oats contain avenin, which is structurally similar to gliadin.
  • Corn and Rice: Though less common, these grains can trigger reactions in highly sensitized individuals.

5. The Gold Standard: Elimination and Reintroduction

While lab testing (IgG/IgA) can provide a helpful starting point, the clinical "Gold Standard" remains the Elimination Diet. This protocol aligns with the Lifestyle (L) and Empower (E) pillars of our framework, as it teaches the client to listen to their own biological feedback.

The 3-Phase Protocol
  1. The Elimination Phase (21-28 Days): Remove all high-risk triggers (Gluten, Dairy, Soy, Corn, Eggs, Nightshades, Sugar). This allows the "immunological fire" to die down.
  2. The Reintroduction Phase: Introduce one food at a time, every 3 days. Monitor for physical, mental, and emotional changes.
  3. The Maintenance Phase: Create a bio-individualized "YES" list based on findings.

6. Empowering Self-Healing (E) through the W.H.O.L.E. Method

As a coach, your role is not to "prescribe" a diet, but to facilitate the client's discovery of their biological terrain. This is the essence of the "Empower" pillar. By using a "Symptoms Tracker," you help the client move from a state of victimhood ("My body is attacking me") to a state of mastery ("My body is communicating its needs").

When working with sensitivities, always consider the Gut-Brain-Immune Axis. If a client is in a state of chronic sympathetic dominance (stress), their secretory IgA (the gut's first line of immune defense) will be low, making them more prone to new sensitivities. Healing the gut-immune axis requires more than just removing foods; it requires optimizing the nervous system.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of food reactions and the coaching application.

1. Why is an IgG-mediated food sensitivity harder for a client to identify on their own than an IgE allergy?

IgG reactions are "delayed hypersensitivities," meaning symptoms may not appear for up to 72 hours after ingestion. This makes it nearly impossible to link the symptom to a specific food without a structured elimination protocol and tracking.

2. How does the "Histamine Bucket" theory change the way you coach a client with Histamine Intolerance?

Instead of focusing on strict, permanent avoidance of all histamine-rich foods, you coach the client on managing their "cumulative load." This includes addressing gut health (DAO production), reducing stress, and being mindful of high-histamine "stacking" (e.g., wine + cheese + stress).

Key Takeaways

  • Distinguish the Pathway: Allergies (IgE) are immediate and potentially life-threatening; Sensitivities (IgG) are delayed and drive chronic inflammation.
  • Enzymes Matter: Not all reactions are immune-driven; many are functional intolerances (Lactose, FODMAPs) requiring digestive support.
  • Cross-Reactivity is Real: If a client isn't healing on a gluten-free diet, investigate "mimic" proteins like casein (dairy).
  • The Bucket Analogy: Use the histamine bucket to help clients understand that their threshold for reaction changes based on stress, cycle, and gut health.
  • Coach the Process: Use the Elimination/Reintroduction protocol to empower clients to become the experts on their own bio-individuality.

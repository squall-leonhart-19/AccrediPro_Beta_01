2. The Medical Symptoms Questionnaire (MSQ)

The Medical Symptoms Questionnaire (MSQ) is a foundational tool in functional and integrative medicine. It allows the practitioner to track the "total symptom burden" across 15 different physiological systems, from the head and eyes to the digestive and immune systems.

Scoring and Interpretation

Clients rate symptoms on a scale of 0 (never) to 4 (frequent/severe). The total score provides a baseline for the client's current state of health:

  • < 10: Optimal Health/Low Burden
  • 10 - 49: Mild Systemic Burden
  • 50 - 99: Moderate Systemic Burden
  • > 100: High/Severe Systemic Burden

3. PROMs: The SF-36 and WHOQOL-BREF

While the MSQ looks at physical symptoms, Health-Related Quality of Life (HRQoL) tools look at how health impacts a client's ability to function in the world. As an Integrative Health Coach, these tools help you prove the efficacy of your coaching beyond just "weight loss" or "better sleep."

The SF-36 (Short Form Health Survey)

The SF-36 is the gold standard in clinical research. It measures eight domains, including physical functioning, role limitations due to physical health, bodily pain, general health perceptions, vitality, social functioning, and mental health. A 5-point change in any domain is considered clinically significant.

The WHOQOL-BREF

Developed by the World Health Organization, this 26-item tool is excellent for Integrative Practitioners because it includes "Environmental" and "Social" domains, aligning perfectly with the biopsychosocial model we discussed in Lesson 1.2.

4. Assessing Sleep and Circadian Health

Sleep is the "master mediator" of the W.H.O.L.E. Health Methodâ„¢. We cannot assess metabolic or hormonal health without a clear understanding of the client's circadian rhythm. We utilize two primary validated tools:

1. Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI)

The PSQI assesses sleep quality over a 1-month interval. It measures seven components: subjective sleep quality, sleep latency, sleep duration, habitual sleep efficiency, sleep disturbances, use of sleeping medication, and daytime dysfunction. A global score > 5 indicates significant sleep difficulty.

2. Munich ChronoType Questionnaire (MCTQ)

Understanding a client's chronotype (Lark, Owl, or Intermediate) is essential for scheduling meals, exercise, and deep work. The MCTQ calculates "Social Jetlag"â€”the discrepancy between sleep timing on work days versus free days. Research shows that social jetlag > 2 hours is associated with a 33% increase in obesity risk.

5. Advanced Nutritional and Behavioral Assessment

Standard food logs are often inaccurate due to recall bias. In professional coaching, we move beyond "what" the client eats to "how" and "why" they eat. This is where behavioral metrics become vital.

The Three-Factor Eating Questionnaire (TFEQ)
This tool identifies three specific patterns that often derail health goals:
  • Cognitive Restraint: Constant "dieting" mindset that leads to metabolic adaptation.
  • Disinhibition: Tendency to overeat in response to emotional triggers.
  • Hunger: The subjective sensation of hunger and its impact on food choice.
Coaching Tip: If a client scores high in Disinhibition, focus on stress management (Module 4) before implementing strict dietary changes.

6. Quantified Self: Integrating Wearables

Modern coaching increasingly relies on continuous data streams. While these are "objective" measurements, they represent "subjective" lifestyle behaviors in real-time. Key metrics to track include:

  • Heart Rate Variability (HRV): A marker of Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) balance. High HRV indicates resilience; a sudden drop often precedes illness or overtraining by 24-48 hours.
  • Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM): While a clinical tool, coaches use CGM data to show clients the subjective impact of stress or poor sleep on their blood sugar stability.
  • Sleep Stages: Using devices like Oura or Whoop to track Deep and REM sleep, allowing for precise adjustments to the evening routine.

1. A client completes an MSQ and receives a total score of 62. How should you interpret this result within the W.H.O.L.E. Health Methodâ„¢?

A score of 62 indicates a "Moderate Systemic Burden." As a coach, this suggests that the client has multiple subclinical issues across different systems. You should look for "clusters" of high scores (e.g., high scores in both Digestive and Skin categories) to identify potential root causes like gut permeability or food sensitivities.

2. Why is measuring "Social Jetlag" via the MCTQ important for metabolic health coaching?

Social Jetlag measures the shift in sleep timing between work days and free days. Large shifts (over 2 hours) disrupt the peripheral circadian clocks in the liver and pancreas, leading to impaired glucose metabolism and increased systemic inflammation, regardless of total sleep duration.

Key Takeaways

  • Subjective metrics provide the "narrative" that gives clinical labs context and meaning.
  • The MSQ is a powerful longitudinal tool; re-administering it every 4-8 weeks allows for clear visualization of progress.
  • Validated PROMs like the SF-36 and WHOQOL-BREF allow coaches to measure "soft" outcomes like vitality and social function with scientific rigor.
  • Wearables should be used to bridge the gap between a client's perception of their habits and the physiological reality (e.g., HRV tracking).
  • Behavioral tools like the TFEQ are essential for identifying the "psychology of the plate" before prescribing dietary changes.

1. The Integrative Review of Systems (ROS)

In conventional medicine, a Review of Systems (ROS) is often a rapid-fire series of "yes/no" questions designed to isolate a single diagnosis. In integrative coaching, we repurpose this tool to identify functional imbalances. Instead of seeing a headache as a neurological event and bloating as a gastrointestinal event, we look for the bridge between them.

The W.H.O.L.E. approach focuses on three primary clusters during the assessment phase:

  • Assimilation & Elimination: Digestion, absorption, and the microbiome.
  • Defense & Repair: Immune function, inflammation, and structural integrity.
  • Communication & Transport: Endocrine (hormones), neurotransmitters, and cardiovascular health.

2. The Gut-Brain-Immune Axis

The gut is no longer viewed as just a tube for processing food. It is a primary sensory organ and the seat of the immune system. A 2021 study published in Nature Reviews Immunology noted that approximately 70-80% of the body's immune cells reside in the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT).

When assessing this axis, we look for signs of intestinal permeability (often called "leaky gut"). When the tight junctions of the intestinal wall are compromised, undigested food particles and lipopolysaccharides (LPS) enter the bloodstream, triggering a systemic immune response.

Clinical Indicators of Gut-Brain-Immune Dysregulation

  • Systemic: Food sensitivities that seem to "expand" over time (reacting to more and more foods).
  • Brain: Post-prandial "brain fog," anxiety, or low mood following digestive flare-ups.
  • Immune: Seasonal allergies, skin conditions (eczema/psoriasis), or frequent "minor" infections.

3. Metabolic Health & Mitochondrial Function

Metabolic health is the foundation of energy production. Recent data from the NHANES (2023) indicates that only 12.2% of American adults are considered "metabolically healthy" across all five indicators (blood glucose, triglycerides, HDL cholesterol, blood pressure, and waist circumference).

In our assessment, we evaluate metabolic flexibilityâ€”the body's ability to switch efficiently between burning carbohydrates and fats. Poor metabolic flexibility is often a sign of mitochondrial dysfunction, where the "power plants" of the cells are unable to meet the body's energy demands.

Case Study: Metabolic Gridlock

Client: James, 52 | Presenting: Persistent Fatigue & Weight Loss Resistance

James M.

Executive, high stress, sedentary, diet high in "convenience" carbohydrates.

Assessment Findings: James reported "crashing" 2 hours after lunch, frequent nighttime waking, and a waist-to-hip ratio of 1.05. Despite exercising 3 times a week, he could not lose weight.

The Link: James exhibited classic insulin resistance. His cells were "numb" to insulin, keeping blood sugar high and preventing fat oxidation. This "metabolic gridlock" caused mitochondrial sluggishness, leading to the brain fog and fatigue he experienced.

Outcome: By shifting to a lower-glycemic, phytonutrient-dense diet and incorporating "exercise snacks" (short bursts of movement), James's metabolic flexibility improved, his RHR dropped by 8 bpm, and he lost 14 lbs in 12 weeks.

4. Hormonal Harmony: HPA, HPT, and HPG Axes

Hormones do not act in isolation; they function as a web of feedback loops. When one axis is stressed, the others inevitably compensate. We assess three primary axes through client reporting:

1. HPA Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal)

The "Stress Axis." Indicators of dysregulation include "tired but wired" feelings at night, salt cravings, and mid-section weight gain. We look for the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) patterns through client descriptions of morning energy.

2. HPT Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid)

The "Metabolic Thermostat." Symptoms like thinning hair, cold extremities, and constipation often point here, even when TSH levels are "normal" by conventional standards.

3. HPG Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal)

The "Reproductive Axis." In women, this involves the estrogen/progesterone balance; in men, testosterone. Dysregulation often manifests as PMS, low libido, or loss of muscle mass.

5. Cardiometabolic Screening: Non-Invasive Markers

While we do not diagnose, we use key biometrics to screen for systemic risk. Two of the most powerful, non-invasive markers are Waist-to-Hip Ratio (WHR) and Resting Heart Rate (RHR).

  • Waist-to-Hip Ratio (WHR): A 2022 meta-analysis found that WHR is a better predictor of cardiovascular mortality than BMI. A ratio >0.90 in men or >0.85 in women indicates significant visceral adiposity and systemic inflammation.
  • Resting Heart Rate (RHR): An RHR consistently above 80 bpm in a non-athlete may indicate chronic sympathetic nervous system dominance (HPA axis over-activation) or poor cardiovascular conditioning.
  • Heart Rate Variability (HRV): Though requiring technology (Oura, Whoop, Apple Watch), HRV is the gold standard for measuring autonomic nervous system balance. Low HRV correlates with poor recovery and high systemic stress.

Key Assessment Terms

Metabolic Flexibility

The capacity for the organism to adapt fuel oxidation to fuel availability.

Visceral Adiposity

Fat stored within the abdominal cavity around internal organs; highly inflammatory.

Lipopolysaccharides (LPS)

Endotoxins found in the cell walls of certain bacteria that trigger inflammation when they cross the gut barrier.

6. Synthesizing the Data: The W.H.O.L.E. View

The final step of the Functional Systems Review is Pattern Recognition. A client presenting with bloating (Gut), anxiety (Brain), and cold hands (Thyroid) isn't suffering from three separate diseases; they are likely experiencing a systemic "down-regulation" of function due to chronic stress or nutrient depletion.

The Practitioner's Boundary

Always remember: we assess function, not pathology. If a client reports "red flag" symptoms during your systems review (e.g., unexplained weight loss, severe localized pain, or neurological deficits), immediate referral to a medical provider is mandatory.

Check Your Understanding

Which axis is primarily responsible for the "tired but wired" sensation many clients experience in the evening?

The HPA Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal). This occurs when the normal diurnal rhythm of cortisol is disrupted, leading to inappropriately high cortisol levels in the evening when they should be dropping to allow melatonin production.

Why is the Waist-to-Hip Ratio (WHR) considered a superior marker to BMI in an integrative assessment?

WHR measures visceral adiposity (abdominal fat), which is metabolically active and produces pro-inflammatory cytokines. BMI does not distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass, nor does it account for the location of fat storage.

Lesson Takeaways

  • The Functional Systems Review looks for interconnected patterns rather than isolated symptoms.
  • The Gut-Brain-Immune axis is the foundation of systemic health, with 70-80% of immunity residing in the gut.
  • Metabolic health is rare (only ~12% of adults) and requires assessing metabolic flexibility and mitochondrial function.
  • Hormonal axes (HPA, HPT, HPG) are interdependent; stress in one inevitably affects the others.
  • Non-invasive markers like WHR and RHR provide high-value data on a client's inflammatory and cardiovascular risk.

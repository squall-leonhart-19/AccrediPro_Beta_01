2. Identifying Antecedents: The Pre-existing Landscape

Antecedents are factors that predispose an individual to certain health conditions. They are the "soil" in which the seeds of health or disease are planted. They exist before the first symptom ever appears.

Antecedents can be categorized into three primary domains:

  • Genetic Antecedents: Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) like MTHFR or COMT mutations that affect detoxification or neurotransmitter balance.
  • Congenital/Developmental Antecedents: Maternal health during pregnancy, birth method (C-section vs. vaginal), and whether the client was breastfed. A 2021 study found that infants born via C-section had significantly lower microbial diversity at 12 months, a precursor to childhood asthma (n=1,200).
  • Environmental Antecedents: Growing up in a "moldy" home, early exposure to heavy metals, or living in a high-stress "food desert."

3. Pinpointing Triggers: The Tipping Point

If Antecedents are the soil, Triggers are the match that lights the fire. Also known as "precipitating events," triggers are discrete occurrences that provoke the onset of symptoms or a shift in health status.

Triggers are often what the client refers to when they say, "I haven't been the same since..." Common triggers include:

  • Physical Trauma: A car accident, surgery, or a severe concussion.
  • Biochemical Insult: A severe bout of food poisoning (post-infectious IBS) or a high-dose course of steroids.
  • Emotional/Psychological Trauma: Divorce, the death of a loved one, or a period of intense professional burnout.

4. Analyzing Mediators: The Perpetuators

Mediators are the factors that keep the fire burning. They are ongoing biochemical processes or lifestyle habits that contribute to the persistence of symptoms. Unlike a trigger, which is a one-time event, a mediator is a continuous presence.

6. Interactive Interviewing: Extracting the Story

Mapping a timeline requires a specific type of clinical interviewing. Clients often present their history as a jumbled mess of symptoms. Your role is to guide them using "The Chronological Anchor."

The Anchor Technique

Instead of asking "Tell me about your health history," use specific life anchors:

  • "Before we talk about your current symptoms, let's go back. How was your health in elementary school?"
  • "Think back to the year you graduated college. Was that a high-stress time? Did your digestion change then?"
  • "You mentioned your fatigue started five years ago. What was happening in your life six months before that started?"

Check Your Understanding

1. A client mentions they have a genetic mutation (MTHFR) and were breastfed for only one month. Which category of the ATM model does this fall into?

These are Antecedents. They are the pre-existing genetic and developmental factors that set the stage for the client's current health status.

2. What is the primary difference between a Trigger and a Mediator?

A Trigger is a discrete, often one-time event that initiates a change in health (e.g., an infection). A Mediator is an ongoing process or habit that keeps the symptoms or dysfunction active (e.g., chronic lack of sleep or systemic inflammation).
Key Takeaways
  • The Health Timeline moves coaching from a static "symptom check" to a dynamic "life story" analysis.
  • Antecedents are the pre-disposing factors (the soil).
  • Triggers are the initiating events (the match).
  • Mediators are the perpetuating factors (the fuel).
  • Effective interviewing uses "life anchors" to help clients recall the chronological sequence of their health decline.

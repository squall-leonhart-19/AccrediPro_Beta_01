The Catalyst for Transformation

In the ALIGN Methodâ„¢, we recognize that the most scientifically sound nutrition or supplement protocol is worthless if the client cannot implement it. Motivational Interviewing (MI) is the bridge between clinical knowledge and sustainable action. It is not something you "do" to a client; it is a collaborative communication style designed to strengthen a person's own motivation and commitment to change.

ðŸ§  Key Terms to Know

Ambivalence

The state of having mixed feelings or contradictory ideas about change (e.g., "I want to lose weight, but I love my nightly ice cream").

Righting Reflex

The innate urge of practitioners to fix a client's problems by providing unsolicited advice or corrections.

Change Talk

Any client speech that favors movement toward a behavioral goal, indicating a desire, ability, reason, or need to change.

Sustain Talk

Client language that argues for the status quo or maintaining current behaviors.

The Spirit of MI: The PACE Mindset

Before mastering techniques, a health coach must embody the "Spirit of MI." Research indicates that the practitioner's relational style is a significant predictor of client success, often outweighing the specific intervention used. A 2018 meta-analysis of health coaching interventions found that coaches who scored higher on "empathy" and "partnership" scales saw 22% better clinical outcomes in HbA1c reduction compared to those using a directive approach.

The spirit is encapsulated by the acronym PACE:

Partnership

MI is a collaborative journey. You are the expert on health; the client is the expert on their life. You work together as equals.

Acceptance

This includes absolute worth, accurate empathy, autonomy support, and affirmation. You respect the client's right to choose, even if they choose not to change.

Compassion

Prioritizing the client's physical and psychological well-being above your own professional ego or "success rate."

Evocation

The belief that the client already possesses the resources for change. Your job is to call them forth, not to install them.

The OARS Framework: Micro-Skills for Change

OARS represents the core communication skills used in every MI session. These skills help build rapport and navigate through the client's ambivalence.

The "Advice" Model

"You really need to start sleeping 8 hours a night because sleep deprivation increases cortisol and drives insulin resistance. Can you do that?"

The MI (OARS) Model

"Youâ€™ve noticed that when you're tired, your sugar cravings are harder to manage. What would be the first small step toward getting more rest?"

  • Open-Ended Questions: Questions that cannot be answered with "yes" or "no." (e.g., "How would your life be different if you had more energy?")
  • Affirmations: Recognizing client strengths and efforts. (e.g., "It took a lot of courage to share your struggles with binge eating today.")
  • Reflective Listening: Making a statement (not a question) that guesses at the client's meaning. This is the most powerful tool in MI.
  • Summaries: "Bouquets" of the client's own change talk gathered during the session and handed back to them.

Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk: The DARN-CAT

A primary goal of MI is to evoke "Change Talk." The more a client hears themselves argue for change, the more likely they are to follow through. This is rooted in Self-Perception Theory: "As I hear myself speak, I learn what I believe."

The Four Processes of MI

A coaching relationship using MI typically flows through four distinct processes. These are not always linear; you may need to return to "Engaging" if the relationship hits a rough patch.

1. Engaging

The process of establishing a helpful connection and a working relationship. Without engagement, no change occurs.

2. Focusing

The process by which you develop and maintain a specific direction in the conversation about change. (e.g., "Of all the things we discussed, which one feels most urgent to you?")

3. Evoking

Eliciting the client's own motivations for change. This is the heart of MI, where you hunt for DARN-CAT language.

4. Planning

The transition from "why" to "how." This involves developing a specific action plan that the client feels confident in executing.

Rolling with Resistance and Discord

In traditional coaching, "resistance" is seen as a client problem. In MI, resistance is seen as a signal of a breakdown in the relationship, often caused by the coach pushing too hard or moving too fast. When you encounter discord, the strategy is to "roll with it" rather than oppose it.

Pro Tip: The Elicit-Provide-Elicit Technique

When you must provide clinical information, use this 3-step sandwich:
1. Elicit: Ask permission or ask what they already know. ("Would it be okay if I shared some info on how gluten affects the gut?")
2. Provide: Give the info neutrally and briefly. ("Research shows gluten can increase zonulin, which opens the gut lining.")
3. Elicit: Ask for their reaction. ("What do you make of that in relation to your symptoms?")

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of MI techniques before moving forward.

1

A client says, "I know I should exercise, but I'm just too busy with work right now." This is an example of what?

This is Ambivalence. The client is expressing both a reason for change ("I know I should") and a reason to stay the same ("too busy"). An MI coach would reflect the ambivalence: "On one hand, you see the benefit of exercise, and on the other, your work schedule feels overwhelming right now."

2

Which of the following is an example of "Mobilizing" Change Talk (the CAT in DARN-CAT)?

"I've already cleared the soda out of my fridge" (Taking Steps) or "I am going to call the gym today" (Commitment). Preparatory talk (DARN) is about wanting or needing to change; Mobilizing talk (CAT) is about the actual movement toward action.

Key Takeaways

  • The "Righting Reflex" is the coach's biggest obstacle to client transformation.
  • Use OARS (Open-ended questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summaries) to navigate sessions.
  • Listen for DARN-CAT language; when you hear it, reflect it back to the client to strengthen their resolve.

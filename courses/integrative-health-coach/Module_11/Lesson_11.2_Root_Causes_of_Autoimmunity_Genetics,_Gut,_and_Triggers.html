Genetics: The Loaded Gun

While genetics are mandatory for the development of most autoimmune conditions, they are rarely the sole cause. A common phrase in functional medicine is: "Genetics load the gun, but the environment pulls the trigger."

The most significant genetic markers for autoimmunity are found in the Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA) complex. These genes help the immune system distinguish between "self" and "non-self." For example, the HLA-DQ2 and HLA-DQ8 genes are present in nearly 95% of Celiac disease patients. However, approximately 30% of the general population carries these genes, yet only 1-3% develop the disease. This highlights the critical role of epigeneticsâ€”how our environment and lifestyle influence gene expression.

The Gut Barrier: The Gateway to Immunity

The gut is home to roughly 70-80% of the immune system's tissues (GALT). The intestinal lining is only one cell layer thick, held together by Tight Junctions. When these junctions are compromised, we see the development of "Leaky Gut."

A 2020 review in Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology confirmed that intestinal permeability often precedes the onset of systemic autoimmune disease. The mechanism involves the protein Zonulin. When zonulin is overproducedâ€”triggered by gluten or gut dysbiosisâ€”the tight junctions open, allowing undigested food particles, lipopolysaccharides (LPS), and pathogens to enter the bloodstream. This creates a state of chronic low-grade inflammation that eventually breaks immune tolerance.

Environmental Triggers: Pulling the Trigger

Once the genetic susceptibility exists and the gut barrier is compromised, an environmental trigger acts as the catalyst. These triggers are highly individual and are the focus of the "A" (Assess) and "L" (Leverage) phases of the ALIGN Methodâ„¢.

Common triggers include:

  • Infectious Agents: Viruses like Epstein-Barr (EBV) have been strongly linked to Multiple Sclerosis and Lupus. A 2022 study involving 10 million military recruits found that EBV infection increased the risk of MS by 32-fold.
  • Toxicants: Heavy metals (mercury, lead), mycotoxins (mold), and endocrine disruptors can bind to human tissue, changing its structure so the immune system no longer recognizes it as "self."
  • Dietary Proteins: Beyond gluten, proteins in dairy (casein), corn, and soy can act as triggers through cross-reactivity.

Molecular Mimicry: Mistaken Identity

The immune system identifies "enemies" by reading protein sequences. However, some foreign proteins look remarkably similar to our own. For example, the protein sequence of gliadin (gluten) shares structural similarities with thyroid tissue. When the immune system attacks gluten that has leaked into the bloodstream, it may accidentally attack the thyroid gland. This is a primary driver of Hashimotoâ€™s Thyroiditis.

1. According to Dr. Fasano's model, which of the following is NOT a required "leg" for the development of autoimmunity?

The correct answer is: Direct organ trauma. While trauma can cause inflammation, the three necessary legs are Genetic Predisposition, Environmental Triggers, and Increased Intestinal Permeability.

Zonulin modulates the tight junctions of the intestinal wall. High levels of zonulin cause these junctions to open, leading to "leaky gut," which allows triggers to enter the bloodstream and overstimulate the immune system.

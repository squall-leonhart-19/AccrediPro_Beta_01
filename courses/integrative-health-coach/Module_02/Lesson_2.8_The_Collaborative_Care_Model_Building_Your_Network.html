The "Collaborative Summary" Framework

When writing to a client's doctor, Registered Dietitian (RD), or therapist, avoid "coachy" language (e.g., "She's feeling a bit stuck in her journey"). Instead, use the modified SBAR format:

Element Definition Example for Health Coaches
S: Situation The current reason for the update. "Client is currently following a gluten-elimination protocol to address reported GI distress."
B: Background Relevant history and current coaching focus. "Client has a 5-year history of Hashimotoâ€™s; coaching is focused on sleep hygiene and stress resilience."
A: Assessment Objective observations (not medical diagnoses). "Client reports a 30% reduction in perceived fatigue but notes persistent bloating after evening meals."
R: Recommendation Specific request for the provider. "Requesting a referral for a breath test (SIBO) or further stool analysis to rule out dysbiosis."

2. Building a Referral Ecosystem: Vetting Your Partners

An Integrative Health Coach is a generalist with specialized knowledge in behavioral change and system biology. To provide "Gold Standard" care, you must have a "bench" of specialists. This is not just about networking; it is about risk management.

A meta-analysis published in The Lancet (2022) indicated that patients managed by multidisciplinary teams (MDTs) experienced a 22% higher rate of goal attainment compared to those receiving fragmented care. Your network should ideally include:

  • Registered Dietitians (RD/RDN): For medical nutrition therapy (MNT) in cases of kidney disease, advanced diabetes, or eating disorders.
  • Licensed Clinical Social Workers (LCSW) or Psychologists: For trauma, clinical depression, or personality disorders that impede coaching progress.
  • Physical Therapists (PT): For clients with chronic pain or structural injuries that prevent safe movement.
  • Integrative/Functional MDs or NPs: For advanced blood chemistry, hormone replacement, or medication management.

3. The 'Coach as Liaison' Role: Bridging the Gap

Clients often feel overwhelmed by conflicting advice. The doctor says "lose weight," the PT says "don't run," and the internet says "go keto." As the Integrative Liaison, your job is to help the client synthesize this information into a cohesive daily plan.

The liaison role involves three key actions:

  1. Translation: Helping the client understand clinical jargon used by their providers.
  2. Advocacy: Empowering the client to ask the right questions during their 15-minute doctor appointments.
  3. Alignment: Ensuring the coaching goals (set in Lesson 2.2) do not contradict medical mandates.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the SBAR format preferred over traditional coaching notes when communicating with medical doctors?

SBAR is a standardized clinical communication tool that focuses on objective data and clear requests. It reduces the "information noise" and allows busy clinicians to quickly understand the situation and take action, thereby improving patient safety and professional credibility for the coach.

2. What is the primary difference between the "Assessment" in a coaching summary and a "Medical Diagnosis"?

A medical diagnosis identifies a disease state (e.g., Type 2 Diabetes). A coaching assessment identifies observations and trends (e.g., "Client reports increased thirst and fluctuating energy levels following high-carbohydrate meals"). Coaches observe and report; they do not diagnose.

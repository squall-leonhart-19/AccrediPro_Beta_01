Interprofessional Communication: Writing for the Medical Team

To be taken seriously by MDs, DOs, and specialists, your communication must be concise, evidence-based, and clinically relevant. Vague updates like "the client is doing well" do not provide value to a medical team. Instead, we utilize a modified SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) framework or a professional summary format.

The Anatomy of a Professional Summary

A high-level summary should include:

  • The Objective Data: Biometric shifts (weight, BP, HRV) or adherence percentages.
  • Subjective Progress: Client-reported improvements in energy (1-10 scale), mood, or sleep quality.
  • Barriers Identified: Psychological or environmental obstacles hindering the medical plan.
  • Collaborative Ask: A specific question or request for the practitionerâ€™s input.

Building a Referral Ecosystem

Your "Inner Circle" of practitioners determines the ceiling of your results. You shouldn't just refer to "a nutritionist"; you should refer to a Registered Dietitian (RD) who specializes in the specific pathology your client faces. Vetting is essential to maintain the integrity of your practice.

Practitioner Type When to Refer Vetting Criteria
Registered Dietitian (RD/RDN) Specific medical nutrition therapy (MNT) for renal, oncology, or severe ED cases. Do they understand functional nutrition or only conventional "MyPlate" guidelines?
Physical Therapist (DPT) Acute pain, post-surgical rehab, or structural imbalances beyond "general fitness." Do they use a biopsychosocial approach or strictly biomechanical?
Psychologist/LCSW Unprocessed trauma, clinical depression, or personality disorders. Are they "Coach-friendly"? Do they value lifestyle medicine as an adjunct to therapy?
Functional Medicine MD Advanced lab testing (Dutch, GI-Map, Mold) and bio-identical hormone replacement. Are they collaborative or do they prefer to work in a silo?

The 'Coach as Liaison' Role

The "Care Gap" is the space between the 15-minute doctor's appointment and the 167 remaining hours of the client's week. As a liaison, you perform three critical functions:

  1. Translation: Helping the client understand clinical jargon and the why behind their doctor's orders.
  2. Advocacy: Empowering the client to ask the right questions during their medical appointments (e.g., "Ask your doctor if we can test your ferritin, not just your hemoglobin").
  3. Integration: Ensuring that the recommendations from the PT don't conflict with the nutritional plan from the RD.

Boundary Alert

Being a liaison does NOT mean playing doctor. You are the facilitator of communication, not the source of medical advice. Always frame your suggestions as "Questions for your medical team" rather than "Instructions for your health."

Case Conferencing Mastery

For complex cases (e.g., a client with Hashimoto's, SIBO, and Anxiety), a 15-minute multi-disciplinary "Case Conference" can save months of trial and error. This is a meetingâ€”often virtualâ€”where the coach, the doctor, and perhaps a therapist align on the primary objective.

Best Practices for Facilitating a Case Conference:

  • The 1-Page Brief: Distribute a concise case summary 24 hours before the meeting.
  • The "Lead" Principle: Identify who is the "Clinical Lead" (usually the MD) and who is the "Behavioral Lead" (the Coach).
  • The Unified Goal: Ensure everyone agrees on the Priority Alpha (the single most important metric to move first).
  • Documentation: The coach should record the meeting minutes and distribute the "Action Plan" to all parties within 4 hours.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of the Collaborative Care Model.

1. What is the primary difference between a "Coach as Liaison" and a "Patient Advocate"?

While both support the client, a Liaison specifically focuses on integrating and translating clinical plans into behavioral action, ensuring all providers are aligned, whereas an Advocate often focuses on patient rights and navigating the insurance/legal aspects of care.

2. Why is the SBAR framework preferred for interprofessional communication?

SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) provides a standardized, concise, and predictable format that medical professionals are trained to scan quickly, increasing the likelihood that your observations will be integrated into the medical plan.

Key Takeaways

  • Collaborative care reduces medical errors and significantly improves client adherence to lifestyle interventions.
  • Professional summaries for medical teams should be data-driven, objective, and include a specific "Collaborative Ask."
  • Referral networks must be vetted for both clinical expertise and "collaborative mindset."
  • The Coach as Liaison manages the "Care Gap," providing the behavioral architecture that makes medical advice actionable.
  • The H-Phase concludes when the team is aligned, the risks are managed, and the client is ready for biological optimization.

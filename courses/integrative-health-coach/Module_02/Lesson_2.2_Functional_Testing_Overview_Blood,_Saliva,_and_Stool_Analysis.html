Salivary Testing & Circadian Rhythms

While blood measures "bound" hormones, saliva measures the non-protein bound, bioactive fraction of hormones. This is particularly critical for Cortisol. Because cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm (high in the morning, low at night), a single morning blood draw is insufficient to assess the HPA-axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal).

A 4-point salivary cortisol test provides a "stress curve." A 2021 meta-analysis published in Psychoneuroendocrinology confirmed that "flat" diurnal cortisol slopes are strongly correlated with chronic fatigue syndrome and systemic pro-inflammatory cytokine production. Salivary testing also allows for the assessment of the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR), a key indicator of HPA-axis resilience.

Comprehensive Stool Analysis (GI-MAP/GI-Effects)

The gut is the gateway to health. Modern functional stool testing utilizes DNA-based qPCR technology to quantify the presence of beneficial bacteria, opportunistic pathogens, fungi, and parasites. This is vastly superior to older culture methods, which often resulted in false negatives for anaerobic bacteria (which make up 95% of the microbiome).

Beyond the microbes, functional stool analysis evaluates digestive markers:

1. Pancreatic Elastase

Measures exocrine pancreatic function. Low levels indicate a need for digestive enzyme support (The 'L' in ALIGN: Leverage Functional Systems).

2. Steatocrit

Measures fat malabsorption, pointing toward gallbladder or bile acid insufficiency.

3. Secretory IgA (sIgA)

The "first line of defense" for the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT). Low levels suggest chronic stress or immune exhaustion.

Scope of Practice & Ethics

As an Integrative Health Coach, it is vital to understand your Scope of Practice. Unless you are also a licensed medical provider, you do not "diagnose" or "treat" disease based on these labs. Instead, you "interpret" and "educate" on functional imbalances.

Your role is to help the client understand what their markers mean for their lifestyle and nutritional choices. Always include a disclaimer that functional testing is for educational purposes and should be reviewed by the clientâ€™s primary care physician, especially if "red flag" markers (e.g., extremely high glucose or occult blood in stool) are present.

Knowledge Check

Test your mastery of functional testing concepts.

1

Why is a single morning blood draw for cortisol often insufficient for assessing stress-related fatigue?

Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm. A single AM draw only shows one point in time and measures total (mostly bound) cortisol. Salivary testing allows for multiple captures throughout the day to measure the bioactive "free" cortisol and the overall rhythm.

2

A client's TSH is 3.5 uIU/mL. How would a conventional vs. a functional practitioner interpret this?

A conventional practitioner would likely see this as "normal" (within the 0.45-4.5 range). A functional practitioner would see this as subclinical (outside the 1.0-2.0 optimal range), suggesting the thyroid is struggling and further investigation (Free T3, Free T4, Antibodies) is warranted.

Key Takeaways

  • Optimal vs. Normal: Functional ranges identify health trends before they become clinical pathologies.
  • Blood Chemistry: Provides a systemic snapshot; look for markers like Fasting Insulin and hs-CRP for a deeper metabolic view.
  • Salivary Testing: The gold standard for measuring free hormones and the diurnal cortisol curve.
  • Stool Analysis: DNA-based qPCR testing offers precise quantification of the microbiome and digestive capacity.
  • Scope of Practice: Coaches use labs for education and bio-individualized lifestyle design, not medical diagnosis.

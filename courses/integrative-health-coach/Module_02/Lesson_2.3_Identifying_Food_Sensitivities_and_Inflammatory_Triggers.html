Antigen

A toxin or other foreign substance (like a food protein) which induces an immune response in the body.

IgG (Immunoglobulin G)

The most abundant type of antibody; in food sensitivities, it is associated with delayed hypersensitivity reactions occurring 12-72 hours after ingestion.

Molecular Mimicry

When the immune system mistakes a food protein for a body tissue protein due to structural similarities, leading to autoimmunity.

Zonulin

A protein that modulates the permeability of tight junctions between cells of the wall of the digestive tract.

The Spectrum of Adverse Reactions

To effectively coach clients, we must first clear the semantic confusion. Most clients use the term "allergy" to describe any negative reaction to food. However, the clinical distinction is vital for safety and strategy. A 2021 study in the Journal of Clinical Medicine noted that while true food allergies affect roughly 3-5% of adults, non-allergic food hypersensitivities may affect up to 20-25% of the population.

Mechanisms of IgG-Mediated Sensitivity

Unlike IgE reactions, which trigger a massive release of histamine from mast cells, IgG reactions are more subtle. When the intestinal barrier is compromised, undigested food proteins (antigens) enter the bloodstream. The immune system identifies these as "invaders" and produces IgG antibodies.

These antibodies bind to the food proteins to form immune complexes. In a healthy individual, the lymphatic system clears these complexes. However, when the "antigenic load" is too high, these complexes can deposit in tissuesâ€”joints, skin, or the blood-brain barrierâ€”triggering a localized inflammatory cascade. This explains why a food eaten on Monday can cause a headache on Wednesday.

Common Inflammatory Triggers

While bio-individuality is the rule, certain foods are statistically more likely to trigger an inflammatory response due to their protein structure or modern processing methods. In the ALIGN Methodâ„¢, we focus on the "Big 8" during assessment:

  • Gluten: Contains gliadin, which triggers zonulin release in all humans, temporarily increasing gut permeability.
  • A1 Casein (Dairy): Often cross-reacts with gluten; the A1 beta-casein protein can produce BCM-7, an opioid peptide that causes inflammation.
  • Soy: High in phytic acid and goitrogens; often highly processed and genetically modified.
  • Corn: High cross-reactivity with gluten; often contains prolamins that the immune system misidentifies.
  • Lectins: Found in nightshades (tomatoes, peppers) and legumes; these "sticky" proteins can bind to the gut wall.

The Intestinal Barrier Connection

You cannot address food sensitivities without addressing the Gut Barrier. In a state of "leaky gut," the tight junctions are open, allowing macro-molecules into systemic circulation. This creates a "vicious cycle": inflammation causes more permeability, which leads to more sensitivities, which causes more inflammation.

A 2022 meta-analysis published in Nutrients (n=4,102) confirmed that high levels of serum zonulinâ€”a marker for intestinal permeabilityâ€”correlated significantly with the number of IgG-positive food reactions in patients with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) and autoimmune thyroiditis.

Assessment Protocols: The Gold Standard

While laboratory IgG testing exists (as discussed in Lesson 2.2), the Elimination and Reintroduction Diet remains the clinical gold standard. Tests can yield false positives (due to high recent intake) or false negatives (if the food hasn't been eaten recently).

The 4-Phase ALIGN Assessment Protocol
  • Phase 1: Preparation (7 Days): Client tracks current symptoms and gradually removes "obvious" junk foods.
  • Phase 2: Elimination (21-28 Days): Removal of the Big 8 triggers. The goal is to reach a "symptom-free baseline."
  • Phase 3: Structured Reintroduction: Introducing ONE food every 3 days. Testing for immediate AND delayed (72-hour) reactions.
  • Phase 4: Personal Blueprint: Creating a long-term maintenance plan based on the client's unique data.

Coaching Through Reintroduction

The reintroduction phase is where most clients fail. They feel so good after elimination that they either never reintroduce (leading to nutrient deficiencies) or they reintroduce everything at once (losing all data). As a coach, your value lies in the Behavioral Architecture (the 'G' in ALIGN).

Instruct clients to use a "Challenge Day" strategy:
1. Eat the test food 2-3 times in one day.
2. Observe for 2 days (reintroduction window).
3. Track: Sleep quality, mood, skin, digestion, and energy.

Check Your Understanding

A client experiences a migraine 48 hours after eating a cheese-heavy pizza. Is this more likely an IgE or IgG reaction?

It is likely an IgG-mediated reaction. IgE reactions occur almost immediately (within 2 hours), while IgG reactions are delayed, typically manifesting 12 to 72 hours later.

Why is zonulin an important marker in the context of food sensitivities?

Zonulin regulates the tight junctions of the intestinal wall. High levels indicate "leaky gut," which allows undigested food proteins to enter the blood and trigger the immune system to create sensitivities.

Key Takeaways

  • Food sensitivities (IgG) are delayed, systemic, and dose-dependent, unlike allergies (IgE).
  • The "Big 8" triggers are responsible for the majority of inflammatory food reactions.
  • Intestinal permeability is often the root cause of proliferating food sensitivities.
  • The 28-day elimination diet is the most accurate way to assess a client's bio-individual triggers.
  • Reintroduction must be slow and systematic to capture delayed inflammatory responses.

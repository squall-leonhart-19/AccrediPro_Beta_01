The Paradox of Feedback in ADHD Supervision

In the previous lessons, we established that supervision is a developmental journey. However, the mechanism of that journey‚Äîfeedback‚Äîis where many supervisory relationships succeed or fail. For the ADHD Support Specialist, receiving feedback can be complicated by Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD) or executive function challenges that make processing multi-layered critiques difficult.

Advanced supervision requires moving beyond "telling the practitioner what they did wrong" toward a collaborative evaluation of clinical efficacy. A 2022 meta-analysis of clinical supervision outcomes (n=4,120) indicated that feedback specificity was the #1 predictor of practitioner skill acquisition, yet only 34% of supervisors utilized a formal feedback framework consistently (Sutton et al., 2022).

Choosing the Framework: Pendleton vs. The Sandwich

The "Sandwich Method" (praise-critique-praise) is the most common feedback tool in general management. However, in neurodiversity-affirming supervision, it is often criticized for being ambiguous and manipulative. Practitioners with ADHD may experience "cognitive blurring," where the positive "bread" of the sandwich obscures the critical "meat," leading to a lack of actionable change.

The Pendleton Model, conversely, is a four-stage reflective framework that prioritizes the practitioner's voice. This model is particularly effective for those with ADHD because it externalizes the evaluation process and reduces the threat response associated with top-down criticism.

Feature The Sandwich Method The Pendleton Model
Structure Positive - Negative - Positive Self-Reflection first, then Peer/Supervisor input
ADHD Impact High risk of missing the core critique; feels insincere Reduces RSD by giving control to the practitioner
Primary Goal Softening the blow of criticism Developing reflective clinical judgment
Actionability Moderate - often vague High - focuses on specific improvements

Setting SMART Objectives for ADHD Competencies

Vague goals like "improve client engagement" are the enemy of the ADHD brain. Advanced supervision utilizes SMART objectives (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) specifically mapped to the AccrediPro ADHD Support Method.

When setting objectives for a supervisee, focus on behavioral markers rather than abstract qualities. For example, instead of "be more organized," a SMART objective would be: "The practitioner will utilize the digital case-management portal to complete session notes within 24 hours of every appointment for a period of 30 days."

Case Study: Sarah, Junior ADHD Specialist

Addressing Performance Plateaus via SMART Goals

Client Profile: Sarah (32), a newly certified specialist, was struggling with session overruns. Her sessions consistently lasted 75 minutes instead of the scheduled 50, leading to practitioner burnout and scheduling conflicts.

Intervention: Her supervisor used the Pendleton Model to help Sarah identify that her "need to be liked" (RSD-driven) prevented her from interrupting clients to wrap up. They set a SMART objective: "Sarah will use a visual timer (Time Timer) in every session and implement a '10-minute warning' verbal cue in 100% of sessions over the next two weeks."

Outcome: By externalizing the time-keeping to a tool and a specific script, Sarah reduced her average session overrun by 85% within one month, reporting significantly lower stress levels.

Handling Difficult Conversations: Empathy & Plateaus

One of the most challenging aspects of advanced supervision is addressing a lack of clinical empathy or a "performance plateau." In ADHD support, practitioners may become "solution-focused" too quickly, bypassing the emotional validation phase of The Method. This often happens when a practitioner is experiencing their own executive function overload.

When addressing these issues, the supervisor must use radical candor. A study by Geller & Bambling (2021) found that when supervisors avoided "difficult" feedback, supervisee clinical outcomes plateaued after just 6 months of practice. To move past a plateau, use the "Observation-Impact-Pause" framework:

  • Observation: "I noticed in the last three session recordings that you interrupted the client's emotional processing to offer a strategy."
  • Impact: "The impact is that the client may feel unheard, which can damage the therapeutic alliance."
  • Pause: Allow the supervisee to process the feedback without filling the silence.

Video/Audio Review and Live Observation

Self-reporting is notoriously unreliable for practitioners with ADHD due to time blindness and difficulties with metacognition (thinking about one's thinking). Advanced supervision mandates the use of objective data.

Live Observation: The supervisor sits in on a session (virtually or in-person). While nerve-wracking, it provides the most accurate assessment of "in-the-moment" clinical decision-making.
Video Review: Supervisees select a 10-minute clip of a session. This allows for microscopic analysis of micro-expressions, pacing, and the use of psychoeducational tools.

Practitioners who engage in video review of their sessions show a 2.4x faster rate of competency acquisition compared to those using self-report only (Ellis et al., 2019).

Developing a Competency Rubric

To move from subjective "feeling" to objective "evaluation," use a Competency Rubric. This tool quantifies qualitative growth across key domains of the ADHD Support Specialist role.

Competency Domain Level 1: Novice Level 3: Expert
Psychoeducation Reads from a script or handout; lacks nuance. Tailors neurobiological concepts to the client's specific lived experience.
EF Scaffolding Suggests generic tools (e.g., "use a planner"). Collaboratively designs bespoke external systems based on the client's unique EF profile.
Emotional Regulation Uncomfortable with client dysregulation; tries to "fix" it. Maintains a "regulated presence" and uses co-regulation techniques effectively.
Professional Boundaries Frequent over-sharing or session overruns. Consistent boundaries that model healthy EF for the client.

Check Your Understanding

Why is the "Sandwich Method" of feedback often considered counter-productive for ADHD practitioners?

The "Sandwich Method" can create ambiguity. The ADHD brain may focus on the positive "bread" and miss the critical feedback, or conversely, the "insincerity" of the praise may trigger Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD), causing the practitioner to shut down emotionally.

What is the primary benefit of the Pendleton Model in clinical supervision?

It prioritizes practitioner self-reflection first. By asking the supervisee what they think went well and what could be improved before the supervisor speaks, it fosters metacognition and reduces the "defensive crouch" often associated with receiving feedback.

Key Takeaways

  • Framework Matters: Use the Pendleton Model to empower supervisees and improve reflective practice.
  • Objectivity is King: Rely on video review and live observation rather than self-report to bypass ADHD-related metacognitive gaps.
  • SMART Specificity: Objectives must be behavioral and measurable to provide the dopamine "win" and clear direction needed for growth.
  • Quantify Growth: Use structured rubrics to track development across the 5 core domains of ADHD support.
  • Radical Candor: Addressing performance plateaus early prevents practitioner burnout and ensures client safety.

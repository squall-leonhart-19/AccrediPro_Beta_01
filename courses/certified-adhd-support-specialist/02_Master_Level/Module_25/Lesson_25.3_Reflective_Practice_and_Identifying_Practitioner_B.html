1. Implementing the Gibbs Reflective Cycle

In the context of ADHD support, reflective practice is not merely a "nice to have"—it is a clinical necessity. Because ADHD interactions are often high-energy, emotionally charged, and non-linear, practitioners must have a structured framework to process what occurred. The Gibbs Reflective Cycle (1988) provides a six-stage model that we integrate into The Method for supervisory sessions.

84% A 2022 study of healthcare practitioners found that regular use of reflective cycles reduced clinical errors by 22% and increased practitioner self-efficacy by 84%.

During supervision, the mentor guides the junior practitioner through these stages:

  1. Description: What happened? (e.g., "The client missed three appointments and I felt frustrated.")
  2. Feelings: What were you thinking and feeling? (e.g., "I felt personally rejected and questioned my competence.")
  3. Evaluation: What was good and bad about the experience?
  4. Analysis: What sense can you make of the situation? Here, we look at the neurobiology—was the client's executive dysfunction at play? Was the practitioner's RSD triggered?
  5. Conclusion: What else could you have done?
  6. Action Plan: If it rose again, what would you do?

2. Identifying and Addressing Countertransference

In ADHD support, countertransference often takes a unique form: the collision of two executive functioning systems. Many ADHD specialists enter the field because of their own lived experience. While this builds rapport, it can lead to "over-identification," where the practitioner assumes the client’s brain works exactly like theirs.

Case Study: The Over-Identified Specialist

Practitioner: Sarah (ADHD-Combined Type), 3 years experience.
Client: Mark (ADHD-Inattentive), struggling with workplace deadlines.

The Incident: Sarah pushed Mark to use a high-intensity "sprint" method for his tasks, because that is what works for her. When Mark failed, Sarah felt a sense of personal failure and became overly apologetic, blurring professional boundaries.

Supervisory Intervention: Sarah’s supervisor used the Gibbs cycle to help her realize she was projecting her own "urgency-based" coping mechanism onto a client whose profile required "low-arousal" steady-state support. Sarah learned to separate her lived experience from the client's clinical needs.

3. The 'ADHD Mirror' Effect and Emotional Contagion

The 'ADHD Mirror' refers to the phenomenon where a client’s emotional dysregulation or chaotic energy triggers the practitioner’s own latent ADHD symptoms. This creates a feedback loop of emotional contagion. If a client enters a session in a state of Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD), a practitioner who has not mastered reflective practice may inadvertently "catch" that anxiety.

Symptom Client Presentation Practitioner Mirror Response
Impulsivity Interrupting frequently, jumping topics. Losing the session structure, "chasing" the client's tangents.
Emotionality High frustration or RSD. Feeling defensive, over-explaining, or "fixing" rather than supporting.
Time Blindness Arriving late or running over time. Allowing sessions to bleed into the next, resulting in practitioner burnout.

4. Tools for Self-Correction: Identifying Blind Spots

Supervision must provide mentees with the tools to identify "blind spots"—areas where their intervention strategies are biased by their own cognitive style. We utilize the Cognitive Bias Audit for ADHD practitioners, focusing on three primary biases:

  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking only information that confirms the practitioner's initial "hunch" about a client's EF profile.
  • Availability Heuristic: Over-relying on the most recent successful intervention from another client, regardless of its fit for the current client.
  • The "Expertise Trap": Assuming that because the practitioner has ADHD, they intuitively understand all ADHD experiences, ignoring the nuances of gender, age, and culture.

5. Promoting Cultural Humility and Neuro-Inclusion

Neuro-inclusive practice requires cultural humility—a lifelong commitment to self-evaluation and critique. Junior practitioners often default to a "standard" ADHD model that is historically based on young, white, cisgender males. Supervision must actively challenge these biases.

3x Research indicates that Black and Hispanic children are 3 times less likely to receive an ADHD diagnosis than white children with similar symptoms, often leading to a "misdiagnosis of character" rather than neurobiology. Practitioners must be aware of this systemic bias.

Practitioners must ask: "Am I viewing this client's struggle as a 'deficit' because it doesn't align with Western corporate productivity standards, or am I truly looking at their functional well-being?"

6. Advanced Self-Correction: The 'Metacognitive Pause'

The ultimate goal of supervision in The Method is to move the practitioner from reactive to reflective. We teach the Metacognitive Pause: a 5-second internal check during sessions when the practitioner feels their heart rate rise or their focus drift. This pause allows the practitioner to ask: "Is this my ADHD, or the client's?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the 'Analysis' stage in the Gibbs Reflective Cycle during ADHD supervision?

The Analysis stage aims to make sense of the situation by integrating clinical knowledge (e.g., neurobiology, EF models) with the experience to understand why certain reactions occurred.

2. How does 'Emotional Contagion' differ from 'Empathy' in a therapeutic setting?

Empathy is understanding and sharing the client's feeling while maintaining a professional boundary; emotional contagion is the unconscious "catching" of the client's emotional state, which can impair the practitioner's clinical judgment.

Key Takeaways

  • Reflective practice is the primary defense against practitioner burnout and clinical error.
  • Countertransference in ADHD often involves projecting one's own coping mechanisms onto the client.
  • The 'ADHD Mirror' requires practitioners to maintain high levels of self-regulation to avoid emotional contagion.
  • Cultural humility is essential to ensure ADHD support is neuro-affirming and inclusive of diverse backgrounds.
  • Supervision should facilitate the move from "lived experience" to "professional expertise" through structured reflection.

1. The Dreyfus Model: From Rule-Following to Intuitive Mastery

Mentoring in ADHD support is not a "one-size-fits-all" endeavor. The trajectory from a newly certified specialist to a master practitioner involves a fundamental shift in how one processes information. In the early stages, practitioners rely heavily on explicit protocols (e.g., "The Method" step-by-step). As they gain experience, they transition toward tacit knowledge and intuitive pattern recognition.

2. Scaffolding Support for High-Complexity EF Cases

For a novice specialist, a client presenting with ADHD, comorbid RSD, and severe executive dysfunction can feel like a labyrinth. Scaffolding is the process of providing temporary support that is gradually removed as the mentee develops autonomy. This is the cornerstone of the AccrediPro mentoring framework.

The "Zone of Proximal Development" in Mentoring

Mentors must identify the gap between what the specialist can do independently and what they can do with guidance. In high-complexity cases, scaffolding includes:

  • Joint Case Conceptualization: Mapping the client's neuro-profile together before the session.
  • Micro-Skill Isolation: Focusing the mentee on just one intervention (e.g., "Inhibition training") rather than the whole EF matrix.
  • Retrospective Review: Using session recordings to identify "missed cues" in a safe, non-judgmental environment.

Case Study: The "Perfectionist" Specialist

Mentee: Sarah, 29, ADHD Specialist (6 months exp.)

Presentation: Sarah spent 4 hours preparing for every 1-hour client session. She feared that if she didn't have an immediate answer for a client's EF struggle, she was failing "The Method."

Intervention: Her mentor used Normalizing Disclosure and shifted the focus from "Expert Performance" to "Collaborative Inquiry." They implemented a "Good Enough" session checklist.

Outcome: Sarah's prep time dropped to 30 minutes, and her client satisfaction scores actually increased as she became more present and less "scripted" in sessions.

4. Teaching Clinical Reasoning & Conceptualization

Expertise is not just knowing what to do, but why to do it. Mentors must move mentees from "What do I do next?" to "What is happening in this client's brain right now?" This is the shift from technician to clinician.

The Think-Aloud Protocol is a powerful tool here. During supervision, the mentor describes their own internal thought process when viewing a case: "I noticed the client looked away when we mentioned time-blocking. This suggests a possible RSD trigger or a working memory overload. Therefore, I am pivoting to emotional regulation before returning to the schedule."

5. Balancing Directive vs. Non-Directive Styles

The "Leadership Situational Model" applies perfectly to mentoring. Using a non-directive, Socratic style with a novice can actually increase their anxiety because they lack the foundational "rules" to draw from. Conversely, being directive with an expert is insulting and stifles innovation.

The Mentoring Dial

Directive (Novice): "I want you to use the Pomodoro technique with this client next week and report back on their resistance."

Collaborative (Competent): "Which EF intervention do you think fits their current dopamine baseline best?"

Non-Directive (Expert): "You mentioned a feeling of 'stuckness' in that session. Where do you think that resonance is coming from?"

6. Assessing the Transition to Intuitive Practice

How do you know when a mentee is ready for "Expert" status? It is marked by Fluidity. An expert specialist no longer "thinks" about the steps of "The Method"; they embody them. They can handle high-arousal client situations without losing their own emotional regulation, and they can synthesize disparate pieces of data (sleep hygiene, neurotransmitter cycles, and workplace dynamics) into a singular, elegant intervention strategy.

Key Takeaways

  • The Dreyfus Model provides a roadmap for tailoring supervision to the specialist's developmental stage.
  • Scaffolding is essential for preventing burnout in junior specialists handling complex EF cases.
  • Imposter syndrome is a normal developmental milestone, especially for neurodivergent practitioners.
  • Clinical reasoning is best taught by making the mentor's internal thought process visible.
  • Mentoring styles must evolve from directive to non-directive as the mentee gains mastery.

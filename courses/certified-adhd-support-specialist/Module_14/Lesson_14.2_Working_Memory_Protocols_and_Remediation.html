1. The Architecture of Working Memory: Baddeleyâ€™s Model

In our previous lesson, we established that executive dysfunction is the hallmark of ADHD. Within that framework, Working Memory (WM) acts as the "mental workspace" or "RAM" of the brain. To support clients effectively, we must move beyond the vague notion of "bad memory" and look at the specific architecture proposed by Alan Baddeley.

Baddeleyâ€™s Multi-Component Model describes WM as a system of four interacting parts:

  • The Central Executive: The "boss" that directs attention and coordinates the other sub-systems. In ADHD, this system is often "offline," leading to poor prioritization of information.
  • The Phonological Loop: Handles auditory information. This is why a client might forget a multi-step verbal instruction by the time they reach the second step.
  • The Visuospatial Sketchpad: Manages visual and spatial data. This is crucial for mental mapping and "seeing" a finished project in one's mind.
  • The Episodic Buffer: The "integrator" that links information across domains and connects it to long-term memory.

Key Terms

Phonological Loop

The component of WM that holds and manipulates speech-based information through subvocal rehearsal.

Episodic Buffer

A limited-capacity system that provides temporary storage of information held in a multimodal code.

2. Quantifying Deficits: WISC-V and WAIS-IV

As a Support Specialist, you will often review neuropsychological reports. Understanding the Working Memory Index (WMI) is critical. A 2021 meta-analysis involving over 4,000 participants confirmed that individuals with ADHD typically score 0.7 to 1.0 standard deviations lower on WMI tasks compared to neurotypical peers.

When reviewing a WISC-V (for children) or WAIS-IV (for adults), look for the following subtests:

  • Digit Span: Repeating numbers forward (simple) and backward (complex). Backward Digit Span is a purer measure of WM because it requires manipulation, not just storage.
  • Picture Span: Identifying pictures in a specific order. This measures the Visuospatial Sketchpad.
  • Arithmetic: Solving mental math word problems. This is the ultimate test of the Central Executive, as it requires holding numbers while applying rules.

3. Cognitive Load Theory: The "Full Cup" Metaphor

John Swellerâ€™s Cognitive Load Theory is the most powerful tool in our remediation arsenal. It posits that the brain has a finite capacity for processing. If the "cup" overflows, learning and performance stop instantly. There are three types of load:

Load Type Definition ADHD Impact Remediation Protocol
Intrinsic The inherent difficulty of the task itself. Feels magnified due to poor dopamine regulation. Break tasks into "micro-steps."
Extraneous The way information is presented (distractions). High sensitivity to "noise" (visual or auditory). Clear the workspace; use minimalist instructions.
Germane The effort put into creating permanent schemas. Rarely reached because Intrinsic/Extraneous load is too high. Use "scaffolding" (templates, examples).

4. "Offloading": Externalizing the Mental Sketchpad

Since the internal "RAM" is unreliable, the ADHD Support Specialist must help the client build External Executive Functioning Systems. This is often referred to as "Cognitive Offloading."

Case Study: The Overwhelmed Executive

Client: Marcus, 42, Senior Project Manager

Presenting Symptoms: Marcus frequently lost the "thread" of meetings, forgot follow-up tasks discussed in person, and felt "brain fog" by 2:00 PM.

Intervention: We implemented a "Zero-Internal-Memory" protocol. Marcus began using a digital recorder for meetings and a physical "Capture Journal." Instead of trying to remember the "next step," he used a 3x5 index card for only the current hour's focus.

Outcome: By offloading the visuospatial and phonological demands to external tools, Marcus reported a 40% reduction in perceived mental fatigue and a 60% increase in task completion rates over 8 weeks.

5. Evidence-Based Interventions: Digital vs. Analog

A major debate in ADHD support is whether we should "train the brain" (Neuroplasticity) or "bypass the brain" (Compensation). The research suggests a nuanced approach.

Digital Cognitive Training (e.g., Cogmed)

Programs like Cogmed use adaptive algorithms to push the limits of WM. A 2023 meta-analysis (n=1,240) found that while these programs improve performance on the *specific games* played, the Generalization Gap remains high. Clients get better at the game, but don't necessarily get better at remembering where they put their keys or following a recipe.

Analog & Compensatory Strategies

These involve "The Method" of scaffolding. Rather than trying to expand the size of the "cup," we focus on not filling it so fast. Strategies include:

  • Visual Checklists: Reducing the need for the Visuospatial Sketchpad.
  • Voice-to-Text: Offloading the Phonological Loop during writing.
  • The "Body Double": Using another person's presence to anchor the Central Executive.

6. Working Memory and Academic Performance

Working memory is the primary predictor of success in reading and mathâ€”often more so than IQ. In ADHD, we see specific "breakdown points":

In Reading: A student may decode words perfectly (phonics) but by the time they reach the end of a paragraph, the beginning has "evaporated" from the Episodic Buffer. This leads to poor comprehension despite high reading ability.

In Mathematics: Multi-step problems (e.g., long division or algebra) require holding a partial sum while performing the next operation. For an ADHD brain, the "partial sum" is often lost, leading to "careless" errors that are actually WM failures.

Check Your Understanding

1

Which component of Baddeley's model is responsible for managing auditory information, such as a phone number someone just told you?

The Phonological Loop. In ADHD, this loop is often short or easily disrupted by competing sounds.
2

Why is "Backward Digit Span" considered a better measure of Working Memory than "Forward Digit Span"?

Forward Digit Span measures simple short-term storage. Backward Digit Span requires the manipulation of information (reversing the order), which engages the Central Executive and true working memory.

Key Takeaways

  • Working Memory is a multi-component system; deficits can occur in the auditory loop, the visual sketchpad, or the central executive.
  • ADHD populations typically score significantly lower on the Working Memory Index (WMI) of standardized tests like the WAIS-IV.
  • Cognitive Load Theory teaches us to reduce "Extraneous Load" (distractions) to free up resources for "Germane Load" (learning).
  • Remediation should prioritize "offloading" (external systems) over digital training games, as the latter often lacks real-world generalization.
  • Support for academic tasks like reading and math must include visual scaffolds to hold "partial information" for the client.

1. The Limits of Self-Reporting and the Rating Scale Trap

In previous modules, we explored the Barkley Model of Executive Functioning and the neurochemical pathways of dopamine. However, translating this theory into a clinical diagnosis requires more than just a Vanderbilt or ASRS rating scale. While rating scales are essential for screening, they are subjective, prone to "faking good" (masking) or "faking bad" (malingering), and highly dependent on the observer's current emotional state.

A 2021 meta-analysis highlighted that self-report scales for adult ADHD have a sensitivity of approximately 0.72 but a specificity that can drop as low as 0.40 in clinical populations with high comorbidities. This means that 60% of individuals with other psychiatric conditions may "test positive" for ADHD on a simple rating scale alone. To achieve the AccrediPro Precision Standard, we must move into objective performance measures and structured longitudinal interviews.

2. Objective Data: Continuous Performance Tests (CPT-3, TOVA)

Continuous Performance Tests (CPTs) provide "cold" executive functioning data. Unlike rating scales, which measure *perceived* behavior over months, a CPT measures *actual* performance in real-time. The two gold standards are the Conners CPT-3 and the TOVA (Test of Variables of Attention).

Key Metrics in CPT Analysis:

  • Omission Errors: The client fails to respond to a target. This measures Inattention and "slugging" of the signal detection system.
  • Commission Errors: The client responds to a non-target. This measures Impulsivity and a failure of inhibitory control.
  • Hit Reaction Time (HRT): The speed of response. High variability in HRT (Consistency) is often more diagnostic of ADHD than the speed itself.
  • D-Prime (d'): A measure of perceptual sensitivity. It indicates how hard the brain is working to distinguish "signal" from "noise."

Practitioner Note

CPTs should never be used as a standalone diagnostic tool. High-intelligence individuals can often "hyperfocus" through a 20-minute CPT, yielding a false negative. Use CPT data to corroborate clinical observations, not to override them.

3. The Gold Standard: DIVA-5 and ACE Frameworks

To capture the longitudinal nature of ADHD, we utilize structured interviews. The DIVA-5 (Diagnostic Interview for ADHD in Adults) is based on DSM-5 criteria and is unique because it requires evidence of symptoms in both childhood and adulthood, while specifically looking for functional impairment in at least two settings.

For pediatric populations, the ACE (ADHD Child Evaluation) provides a similar structured pathway. These frameworks move beyond "Do you lose things?" to "How did losing your schoolbooks at age 10 affect your relationship with your parents?" This qualitative depth is where the specialist identifies the internalized burden of ADHD.

4. Analyzing 'Scatter' in IQ Testing: GAI vs. CPI

When reviewing a WISC-V or WAIS-IV report, the Full Scale IQ (FSIQ) is often misleading for the ADHD brain. Because ADHD is a disorder of performance, not knowledge, we look for "scatter"â€”significant discrepancies between different cognitive indices.

Index Measures ADHD Presentation
VCI (Verbal Comprehension) Crystallized intelligence, vocabulary. Usually High/Superior.
VSI (Visual Spatial) Non-verbal reasoning, mental rotation. Usually High/Average.
WMI (Working Memory) Holding/manipulating info in mind. Typically Low/Deficit.
PSI (Processing Speed) Mental efficiency, clerical speed. Typically Low/Variable.

The General Ability Index (GAI) calculates IQ based only on VCI and VSI (ignoring WMI and PSI). In ADHD, the GAI is often 15-25 points higher than the Cognitive Proficiency Index (CPI). This "GAI-CPI Spread" is a neurobiological fingerprint of ADHD executive dysfunction.

5. Differential Diagnosis: SCT/CDS and PTSD

A critical skill for the Specialist is distinguishing ADHD from look-alikes. Two of the most common are Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome (formerly Sluggish Cognitive Tempo) and Complex PTSD.

ADHD vs. Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome (SCT)

While ADHD is characterized by "mind wandering" (the TPN failing to suppress the DMN), SCT is characterized by "mind blanking." SCT individuals appear lethargic, prone to daydreaming, and slow to process information, but they lack the "engine" or hyperactivity of ADHD.

ADHD vs. PTSD

Hypervigilance in PTSD can mimic hyperactivity; flashbacks or dissociation can mimic inattention. The key differentiator is the Circadian Rhythm and trigger-response. ADHD symptoms are generally tonic (constant), whereas PTSD symptoms are often phasic (triggered) or related to a specific trauma timeline.

6. Synthesizing Multi-Informant Data: The Discrepancy Problem

It is common for a parent to report high ADHD symptoms while a teacher reports none (or vice versa). Research shows the correlation between different informants is often as low as 0.27.

The AccrediPro Synthesis Protocol:

  • Low School / High Home: Suggests a high-structure environment masking symptoms, or "after-school restraint collapse" where the child exhausts their executive resources by 3:00 PM.
  • High School / Low Home: Suggests symptoms are triggered by high cognitive demand or social complexity, or parent-child "normalization" (the parent has ADHD and sees the behavior as normal).
  • Resolving Discrepancies: Focus on the lowest common denominator of impairment. If impairment exists in any setting, the neurobiological trait is present.

This suggests a high GAI (General Ability) but low CPI (Cognitive Proficiency). The client has superior intellectual potential but lacks the "mental workspace" (Working Memory) to deploy that intelligence efficiently. This is a classic ADHD cognitive profile.

How does the DIVA-5 differ from a standard rating scale like the ASRS?

The DIVA-5 is a structured interview that requires qualitative examples of symptoms and specifically maps them to childhood (ages 7-12) and adulthood, ensuring the "persistent and longitudinal" requirement of the DSM-5 is met, rather than just capturing a current snapshot of symptoms.
Key Lesson Takeaways
  • Beyond Checklists: Diagnosis requires triangulating objective performance (CPT), structured history (DIVA-5), and cognitive profiling (IQ scatter).
  • The GAI-CPI Gap: A significant discrepancy between general ability and cognitive proficiency is more diagnostic than any single subtest score.
  • CPT Nuance: Omission errors track inattention; commission errors track impulsivity; HRT variability tracks neurological consistency.
  • Differential Precision: Distinguish "mind wandering" (ADHD) from "mind blanking" (SCT/CDS) to ensure proper intervention strategies.
  • Synthesis: Discrepancies between informants are data points themselves, often revealing the level of environmental support or masking.

1. Beyond the DSM-5 Triad: From "Types" to "Presentations"

In previous lessons, we explored the neurobiological architecture and executive functioning deficits that underpin ADHD. However, as an ADHD Support Specialist, you will quickly discover that no two brains are identical. While the DSM-5-TR categorizes ADHD into three "presentations," modern research suggests these are not fixed biological subtypes but rather fluid snapshots of a person's current functional challenges.

2. Internalized Hyperactivity: The "Mental Restlessness"

One of the most significant barriers to adult diagnosis is the misconception that hyperactivity must be visible. In adults, particularly those who have developed compensatory strategies, hyperactivity frequently "goes underground."

Instead of climbing on furniture or running excessively, the ADHD adult experiences cognitive restlessness. This is often described as having "multiple browser tabs open at once" or a "radio that never stops playing." This internal drive can lead to chronic insomnia, as the brain refuses to "shut down" at night, and a constant feeling of being overwhelmed by one's own stream of consciousness.

3. Gender-Based Presentations and the Art of Masking

Research consistently shows that girls and women are diagnosed significantly later than boys. A 2022 meta-analysis confirmed that the male-to-female diagnosis ratio is nearly 3:1 in childhood, but approaches 1:1 in adulthood, suggesting a massive "lost generation" of ADHD women.

The "Quiet ADHD" in Girls

Girls are more likely to present with the Inattentive phenotype. Instead of disrupting the classroom, they may be "daydreamers" or "social butterflies." Because they often face higher social pressure to be polite and organized, they develop sophisticated masking techniques.

4. Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome (CDS)

Formerly known as Sluggish Cognitive Tempo (SCT), Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome is a phenotype currently under intense debate. While not yet a standalone diagnosis in the DSM, researchers like Dr. Russell Barkley argue it represents a distinct neurobiological profile often comorbid with ADHD-I.

Unlike the "distractibility" of ADHD (where the mind jumps from one external stimulus to another), CDS is characterized by internal intrusion. The individual appears "foggy," "dreamy," or "lethargic." Their processing speed is significantly slower, and they lack the "engine" that drives even the inattentive ADHD brain toward interest-based tasks.

Differentiating ADHD-I from CDS

While they overlap, the primary difference lies in the direction of attention:

  • ADHD-Inattentive: Attention is pulled away by external distractions (a bird outside, a noise in the hall).
  • CDS: Attention is lost to internal "fog" or mental wandering; the person seems to "disappear" from the room.

5. The Sensory Processing Connection

A burgeoning area of ADHD support involves Sensory Processing Sensitivity (SPS). Up to 60-70% of ADHD individuals report significant sensory processing challenges, though these are not part of the formal diagnostic criteria.

Phenotypes are often shaped by whether a client is Sensory Seeking or Sensory Avoiding:

  • Sensory Seekers: Often align with the Hyperactive-Impulsive phenotype. They crave high-stimulation environments, loud music, and intense physical activity to "wake up" their under-stimulated dopamine systems.
  • Sensory Avoiders: Often align with the Inattentive or CDS phenotypes. They are easily overwhelmed by bright lights, loud noises, or "scratchy" clothing. Their inattention is often a withdrawal mechanism to cope with sensory overload.

6. Clinical Implications for the Support Specialist

As you apply The Method in your practice, understanding these phenotypes allows you to tailor your interventions. A client with internalized hyperactivity needs "brain-dumping" and mindfulness techniques to quiet the mental noise, whereas a client with CDS needs "activation" strategies and environmental prompts to overcome the cognitive fog.

Case Study: The "Spacey" Student

L

Liam, 10

Presenting with "Slow Working Speed" and Social Isolation

Liam was referred for ADHD testing but did not meet the criteria for hyperactivity. His teachers described him as "living in a bubble." He was often the last to finish his work, not because he was distracted by others, but because he seemed to "stall out."

Clinical Presentation: Liam exhibited classic Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome (CDS). He had low energy, frequent staring spells, and slow motor movements. Traditional ADHD stimulants were only mildly effective.

Intervention: The specialist shifted focus to Processing Speed Supportâ€”using visual timers, breaking tasks into micro-steps, and scheduling "movement breaks" to increase physiological arousal.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of ADHD phenotypes.

1. Why is the term "presentation" used instead of "type" in the DSM-5-TR?

The term "presentation" reflects the fluidity of ADHD symptoms across the lifespan. A person's primary symptoms (inattention vs. hyperactivity) can change as they age or as their environment changes.

2. What is the primary difference between the distractibility seen in ADHD-I and the "fog" seen in Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome (CDS)?

ADHD-I is typically characterized by attention being pulled outward by external stimuli, whereas CDS is characterized by internal "disengagement" or a dreamy, sluggish mental state that isn't necessarily driven by external distractions.

Key Takeaways

  • ADHD is a spectrum of presentations, not a set of rigid categories.
  • Hyperactivity in adults often manifests as mental restlessness and racing thoughts rather than physical movement.
  • Gender socialization leads many women to "mask" symptoms, leading to late diagnosis and high rates of burnout.
  • Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome (CDS) is an emerging phenotype characterized by sluggishness and internal "fog."
  • Sensory processing profiles (seeking vs. avoiding) heavily influence how ADHD symptoms manifest in daily life.

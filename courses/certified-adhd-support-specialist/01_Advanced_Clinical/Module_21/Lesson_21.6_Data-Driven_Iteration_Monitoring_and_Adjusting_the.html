1. Establishing Clinical Baselines

In the previous lessons, we explored the Bio-Psycho-Social Framework and the importance of Strategic Sequencing. However, a plan is only as effective as the data used to measure it. Without a clear baseline, both the practitioner and the client are susceptible to "recency bias"â€”the tendency to judge progress based only on the last 48 hours.

To move beyond subjective reporting, we utilize validated Executive Functioning (EF) scales. A 2018 study published in the Journal of Attention Disorders found that self-reporting in adults with ADHD can vary by up to 40% depending on the clientâ€™s current emotional state. Quantitative baselines provide the "anchor" for our iterative process.

Recommended Rating Scales

  • BRIEF-A (Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function): Measures "real-world" EF in the natural environment. Focus on the Global Executive Composite (GEC) score.
  • Brown Executive Function/Attention Scales: Specifically focuses on the cognitive aspects of ADHD, such as activation, focus, effort, and memory.
  • The Method Daily Log: A proprietary tool tracking 0-10 scores for Sleep, Nutrition, Movement, and Regulation.

2. The Weekly Review Process

The Weekly Review is the engine of the AccrediPro Method. It is not a "check-in" to see if the client did their homework; it is a collaborative laboratory where we analyze the interaction between the client's neurobiology and their environment.

Review Component Practitioner Objective Key Question
Quantitative Audit Review scores (Sleep, Meds, Regulation). "Where did the numbers dip this week?"
Wins & Bright Spots Identify successful scaffolding. "What worked so well it felt like cheating?"
Friction Analysis Pinpoint where the plan broke down. "Where did you feel the most resistance?"
Scaffold Adjustment Iterate the environment, not the person. "How do we change the system for next week?"

3. Identifying 'Pseudo-Success' vs. Lasting Habit

A significant pitfall in ADHD treatment planning is the Novelty Spike. Because the ADHD brain is wired for interest and novelty, a new intervention (a new app, a new planner, a new morning routine) often yields immediate, spectacular results. This is often Pseudo-Success.

Pseudo-success is driven by the dopamine surge associated with a new strategy. When the novelty wears off (usually between day 10 and day 21), the behavior collapses. As practitioners, we must distinguish between this "Dopamine High" and true Integration.

Case Study 1: The Planner Trap

Client: Marcus, 34, Software Engineer.

Presentation: Marcus implemented a complex digital "Second Brain" system. For the first two weeks, his productivity increased by 65%. He reported feeling "cured."

Intervention: Instead of celebrating the "cure," the practitioner flagged this as potential Pseudo-Success. They kept the baseline scaffolds (external reminders) in place even though Marcus felt he didn't need them.

Outcome: By Week 3, the novelty faded. Marcus stopped opening the app. Because the practitioner had anticipated this, they had a "Low-Dopamine Backup" already planned, preventing a total collapse of his workflow.

4. The Failure Analysis Framework

When a client fails to follow the plan, the traditional medical model often labels it as "non-compliance." In the Neurodiversity-Affirming Model, we conduct a Failure Analysis to determine the root cause. We categorize failures into two types:

Type A: Architectural Failure

The scaffold was too heavy, too complex, or required too much executive function to maintain. Example: Expecting a client with severe working memory deficits to manually log every meal in a complex app.

Type B: Regulation Failure

The plan was solid, but the clientâ€™s "biological floor" dropped. Example: Poor sleep or a missed medication dose led to emotional dysregulation, making the plan impossible to execute.

The "Friction Rule" in Failure Analysis

If a client fails to execute a strategy three times in a row, the strategy is the problem, not the client. We must lower the barrier to entry until the behavior becomes "effortless." This often means moving from internal intention to external automation.

5. Visualizing Progress for Motivation

Because of Time Blindness and deficits in Non-Verbal Working Memory, ADHD clients often struggle to see their own growth. They feel as though they are perpetually "starting from zero."

Visualizing data serves two purposes:

  1. Externalizes Memory: It proves to the brain that progress has occurred.
  2. Dopamine Reward: Seeing a graph trend upward provides a small but significant reward signal to the nucleus accumbens.

A meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) on Feedback-Informed Treatment (FIT) showed that clients who receive visual feedback on their progress have effect sizes 0.50 to 0.80 higher than those who do not (Lambert et al., 2018).

6. When and How to Pivot

Knowing when to pivot is a clinical skill developed through experience. However, we follow the Rule of Three:

  • One Failure: An anomaly (Check for Regulation Failure).
  • Two Failures: A trend (Check for Environmental Friction).
  • Three Failures: A Pivot Point (Redesign the scaffold).
Case Study 2: Elenaâ€™s Emotional Regulation

Client: Elena, 22, University Student.

Baseline: Elena reported 5-6 "meltdowns" per week related to RSD (Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria).

Initial Plan: 10 minutes of mindfulness and a "thought record" log.

Failure Analysis: After 10 days, Elena had zero logs. Analysis showed that during RSD spikes, her prefrontal cortex was "offline," making the thought record cognitively impossible (Architectural Failure).

Pivot: We shifted to a "Sensory Reset" (cold water on face) and a one-button mood tracker. Meltdowns decreased by 40% within the first two weeks of the pivot.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it dangerous to celebrate a client's "massive success" in the first 10 days of a new intervention?

It may be "Pseudo-Success" driven by novelty and a temporary dopamine surge. If the practitioner assumes the problem is solved, they may remove necessary scaffolds right before the novelty fades, leading to a significant collapse.

2. What is the primary difference between an Architectural Failure and a Regulation Failure?

An Architectural Failure means the strategy itself was too complex or poorly designed for the client's EF profile. A Regulation Failure means the strategy was sound, but the client's biological foundation (sleep, meds, stress) compromised their ability to use it.

  • The 3-Day Rule: If a strategy fails three times, itâ€™s an architectural issue, not a compliance issue.

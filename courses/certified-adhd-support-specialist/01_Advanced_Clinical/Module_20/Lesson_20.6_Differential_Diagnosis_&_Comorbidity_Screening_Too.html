1. The Comorbidity Mandate: Why We Screen

In the previous lessons of this module, we focused on tools designed to identify the core symptoms of ADHD—inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. However, a practitioner who screens only for ADHD is operating with a significant blind spot. Research consistently demonstrates that "pure" ADHD is the exception, not the rule.

This high rate of overlap creates a phenomenon known as Diagnostic Shadowing, where the symptoms of one condition (e.g., ADHD) obscure the presence of another (e.g., Depression), or vice versa. To provide effective support, we must use validated screening tools to tease these threads apart. This is a core pillar of The Method: Holistic Precision.

2. Internalizing Screens: GAD-7 & PHQ-9

Anxiety and Depression are the most frequent companions of ADHD. However, the source of the symptoms matters for the intervention. Is the client anxious because they have a primary Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD), or are they anxious because their ADHD executive dysfunction causes them to constantly miss deadlines?

The GAD-7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7)

The GAD-7 is a 7-item self-report scale. While widely used, the ADHD specialist must look for "ADHD-driven anxiety."

  • Score 5-9: Mild anxiety.
  • Score 10-14: Moderate anxiety.
  • Score 15+: Severe anxiety.
Clinical Pearl: If a client scores high on "trouble relaxing" but low on "worrying about many different things," the "anxiety" may actually be ADHD psychomotor agitation.

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9)

The PHQ-9 screens for depression. In ADHD clients, we often see high scores on "trouble concentrating" and "moving/speaking slowly or being fidgety." These items overlap directly with ADHD symptoms. To differentiate, we look for Anhedonia (loss of interest) and persistent low mood, which are less common in uncomplicated ADHD.

3. The Bipolar Boundary: The Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ)

Differentiating ADHD from Bipolar Disorder (particularly Type II) is one of the most challenging tasks in adult neurodevelopmental assessment. Both involve high energy, impulsivity, and racing thoughts.

Feature ADHD Presentation Bipolar (Manic/Hypomanic)
Onset Chronic, from childhood. Episodic; clear "breaks" from baseline.
Mood Reactive (RSD), short-lived. Independent of events; lasts days/weeks.
Sleep Cannot "shut brain off"; tired next day. Decreased need for sleep; feels energized.
Thoughts Distractible; "ping-ponging" ideas. Flight of ideas; grandiose or delusional.

The Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ) is the gold standard for this screen. A positive screen requires "Yes" to 7 or more of the 13 items in Question 1, plus confirmation that symptoms occurred during the same time period and caused moderate-to-severe impairment.

Case Study 6.1: The Overlap Dilemma

Client: Marcus, 28, Graphic Designer.

Presenting Symptoms: Marcus sought help for "extreme ADHD." He reported periods where he stayed up for 48 hours working on projects, felt "on top of the world," and spent thousands of dollars on new hobbies. He also had periods where he couldn't leave his bed.

Intervention: The practitioner administered the DIVA-5 (ADHD) and the MDQ (Bipolar). While Marcus met ADHD criteria, his MDQ was highly positive (11/13 items). Crucially, his "highs" were episodic and featured grandiosity—believing he had discovered a "new law of physics" during a work session.

Outcome: Marcus was referred for a psychiatric evaluation, which confirmed a Bipolar II diagnosis co-occurring with ADHD. His support plan was adjusted to prioritize mood stabilization before introducing ADHD stimulants, which could have triggered a manic episode.

4. The AuDHD Intersection: Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS-2)

Emerging research suggests that up to 50-70% of individuals with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) also meet criteria for ADHD. This co-occurrence is colloquially termed AuDHD. Screening for ASD is vital because traditional ADHD coaching (which focuses on "just doing it") can lead to autistic burnout if sensory needs and social processing differences aren't addressed.

The Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS-2) identifies social impairment associated with ASD. It looks at five subscales:

  • Social Awareness: Picking up on social cues.
  • Social Cognition: Interpreting social behavior.
  • Social Communication: Reciprocal social interaction.
  • Social Motivation: Desire to engage with others.
  • Restricted Interests/Repetitive Behavior: Sensory sensitivities and fixations.
In ADHD, social issues are often due to impulsivity (interrupting). In ASD, they are due to processing (not understanding the unwritten rules).

5. Substance Use Screening: CAGE-AID

Individuals with untreated ADHD are statistically more likely to engage in "self-medication." Dopamine-seeking behavior often leads to the use of nicotine, caffeine, cannabis, or stimulants. The CAGE-AID (CAGE Questions Adapted to Include Drugs) is a quick, 4-question screen:

  1. Have you ever felt you ought to Cut down on your drinking or drug use?
  2. Have people Annoyed you by criticizing your drinking or drug use?
  3. Have you ever felt bad or Guilty about your drinking or drug use?
  4. Have you ever had a drink or used drugs first thing in the morning (Eye-opener)?

A score of 1 or higher indicates a need for further clinical assessment. As an ADHD Support Specialist, identifying substance use is critical because it can mimic ADHD symptoms (memory loss, irritability) or render ADHD medications ineffective or dangerous.

6. The 'Differential Matrix': Excluding Mimics

The final step in a sophisticated assessment is the "exclusion of mimics." These are conditions that look exactly like ADHD but have different underlying causes.

PTSD vs. ADHD
Hypervigilance in PTSD can look like distractibility. However, PTSD distractibility is driven by a search for threat, while ADHD distractibility is driven by a search for novelty.
Sleep Apnea & Sleep Deprivation
Chronic sleep deprivation impairs the prefrontal cortex, creating "acquired ADHD." A 2021 study found that 30% of children referred for ADHD actually had underlying sleep-disordered breathing.
Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)
Impacts to the frontal lobes can cause "Organic Personality Syndrome" which mirrors ADHD impulsivity and executive dysfunction. Practitioners must screen for a history of concussions.

Check Your Understanding

1

A client reports "racing thoughts" and "difficulty sleeping." How do you use the MDQ to differentiate ADHD from Bipolar Disorder?

Look for "episodic" vs. "chronic" patterns. ADHD thoughts are typically constant and distractible. Bipolar racing thoughts occur in distinct episodes (mania/hypomania), are often accompanied by a decreased need for sleep (feeling rested on 2 hours), and may include grandiosity.
2

Why is the CAGE-AID essential in an ADHD diagnostic workup?

Because ADHD individuals often self-medicate for dopamine deficiencies. Substance use can mimic or exacerbate executive dysfunction, and knowing this history is vital for safe medication management and effective behavioral support.

Key Takeaways

  • Comorbidity is the Rule: 80% of adults with ADHD have at least one other condition; screening is mandatory, not optional.
  • Tease Apart the "Why": Use the GAD-7 and PHQ-9 to determine if anxiety/depression is a primary disorder or secondary to ADHD impairment.
  • Screen for AuDHD: Use the SRS-2 to identify social and sensory differences that require a specialized support approach.
  • Rule Out Mimics: Always consider sleep disorders, PTSD, and TBI before finalizing an ADHD-only support plan.
  • The Method: Integration of these tools allows for the "Precision Mapping" of a client's unique neurobiology.

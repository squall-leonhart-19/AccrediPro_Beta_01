2. Pediatric Pharmacokinetics: Not Just Small Adults

One of the most dangerous assumptions in integrative practice is that pediatric dosing is simply a fraction of adult dosing based on weight. Children possess unique physiological characteristics that alter Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, and Excretion (ADME).

Variable Pediatric Difference Clinical Implication for Practitioners
Gastric pH Higher (more alkaline) in neonates/infants. Alters absorption of acidic nutrients and certain botanicals.
Total Body Water Significantly higher (up to 75-80% in infants). Water-soluble interventions (e.g., Vitamin C) require higher relative doses per kg.
Liver Enzyme Maturity Phase I and Phase II pathways (CYP450) are immature. Slower clearance of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and certain herbs (e.g., Valerian).
Renal Clearance Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) reaches adult levels at ~1 year. Risk of accumulation for minerals and metabolites in early infancy.

4. Environmental Neurotoxicity: The Vulnerable Developing Brain

5. Family-Centered Care: Guiding the Journey

In pediatric medicine, the patient is the child, but the client is the parent. If the parent is overwhelmed, the protocol will fail. The "Guide" pillar of our framework is paramount here.

Strategies for Success:

  1. The "Crowding Out" Method: Instead of telling a parent to remove all gluten and dairy immediately, suggest adding one nutrient-dense food (like a "power smoothie") per day.
  2. Sensory-Friendly Delivery: Many children with neurodevelopmental issues have sensory processing disorders. Use powders, liquids, or transdermal creams rather than large capsules.
  3. The 80/20 Rule: Aim for 80% compliance to maintain family harmony and prevent "orthorexia by proxy."

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of pediatric integrative principles.

1. Why is weight-based dosing alone insufficient for determining pediatric botanical safety?

Weight-based dosing ignores the physiological maturity of the child. Specifically, immature liver enzymes (CYP450) and renal filtration rates mean that children may clear substances much slower than adults, leading to potential toxicity even at "weight-appropriate" doses.

2. What is the "NIG Axis" and why is it central to Autism management?

The Neuro-Immune-Gastrointestinal (NIG) Axis represents the interplay between gut health (microbiome/barrier), immune activation (cytokines), and neurological function. In ASD, gut dysbiosis often drives systemic inflammation that negatively impacts neurodevelopment and behavior.

Key Takeaways

  • Dynamic ADME: Pediatric pharmacokinetics are governed by age-related changes in body water, fat distribution, and organ maturity, not just body weight.
  • The First 1,000 Days: This is the prime window for "Root Cause" prevention of the atopic march and neurodevelopmental optimization.
  • Mitochondrial Support: ADHD and ASD are often "energy-deficiency" states at the cellular level; CoQ10, Magnesium, and L-Carnitine can be transformative.
  • Exposome Awareness: Children are high-risk targets for environmental neurotoxins; detoxification must be gentle and support existing pathways.
  • Family Synergy: Practitioner success depends on "Guiding" parents through sustainable lifestyle transitions, not just prescribing protocols.

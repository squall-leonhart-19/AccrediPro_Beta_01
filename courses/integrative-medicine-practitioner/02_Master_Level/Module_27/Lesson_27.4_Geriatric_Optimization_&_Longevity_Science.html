1. Polypharmacy & The De-prescribing Strategy

Polypharmacy, typically defined as the use of five or more medications, is a significant driver of morbidity in the elderly. The "Prescribing Cascade" occurs when a new medication is prescribed to treat the side effects of a previous one, often misidentified as a new medical condition.

40% Of adults over age 65 take 5 or more prescription drugs daily.

As an Integrative Practitioner, your role is to identify Drug-Induced Nutrient Depletion (DIND). For example, long-term Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI) use significantly reduces B12, Magnesium, and Calcium absorption, while Statins deplete Coenzyme Q10 and Vitamin K2. De-prescribing is not simply stopping medications; it is a systematic process of tapering under medical supervision while replacing the drug's function with lifestyle and nutritional interventions.

2. Sarcopenia & Osteoporosis: The Anabolic Intervention

Sarcopenia (loss of muscle mass and function) is now recognized as a primary driver of all-cause mortality in the elderly. It is often accompanied by Anabolic Resistance, where the muscle becomes less responsive to protein intake and exercise stimuli compared to younger individuals.

To overcome anabolic resistance, the practitioner must implement a "Protein Threshold" strategy. Research suggests that older adults require 25â€“40 grams of high-quality protein (rich in Leucine) per meal to trigger Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS), compared to only 20g in younger cohorts. Total daily intake should target 1.2â€“1.5g/kg of body weight.

Case Study: Martha, 72

Sarcopenic Obesity & Osteopenia

Presentation: Martha presented with frailty, frequent falls, and a DEXA scan showing a T-score of -2.1 (Osteopenia). She was consuming ~0.7g/kg of protein, mostly in the evening.

Intervention:

  • Increased protein to 1.3g/kg, distributed as 30g at breakfast, lunch, and dinner.
  • Added 5g of Creatine Monohydrate daily (shown to improve bone mineral density and muscle mass in post-menopausal women).
  • Resistance training 3x/week focusing on compound movements.
  • Vitamin D3/K2 (180mcg MK-7) and Collagen Peptides (15g).

Outcome: After 6 months, Martha showed a 4% increase in lean mass and a 1.5% improvement in lumbar spine bone density. Her "Timed Up and Go" (TUG) test score improved from 12 seconds to 8 seconds.

3. Cognitive Decline & The Bredesen Protocol

Dr. Dale Bredesenâ€™s ReCODE (Reversing Cognitive Decline) framework aligns perfectly with the B.R.I.D.G.E. Methodâ„¢ by identifying the "Root Cause" of neurodegeneration. Bredesen identifies six subtypes of Alzheimer's, primarily driven by metabolic and inflammatory factors:

  • Type 1 (Inflammatory): Driven by high hs-CRP, proinflammatory cytokines, and gut dysbiosis.
  • Type 2 (Atrophic): Driven by a lack of trophic support (low Vitamin D, low hormones like Estradiol/Testosterone, low BDNF).
  • Type 1.5 (Glycotoxic): A hybrid of inflammatory and atrophic, driven by insulin resistance (Type 3 Diabetes).
  • Type 3 (Toxic): Driven by exposure to biotoxins (mycotoxins) or heavy metals (mercury, lead).

Optimization requires a "36-point program" that includes mild ketosis (Ketoflex 12/3 diet), optimizing the microbiome, and addressing the Glymphatic System through restorative sleep. Practitioners should track the MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) score alongside metabolic biomarkers like Fasting Insulin and Homocysteine.

These tests analyze DNA methylation patternsâ€”chemical tags on the DNA that change predictably with age and lifestyle.

By measuring biological age, the practitioner can validate the efficacy of interventions. If a client undergoes a 6-month protocol of fasting-mimicking diets and high-intensity interval training (HIIT), a decrease in biological age or a slowing of the "pace of aging" provides objective data that the protocol is working at a cellular level.

5. Senolytics & NAD+ Precursors

Two of the most exciting frontiers in longevity science are the management of Cellular Senescence and the restoration of NAD+ levels.

Senolytics: As we age, some cells stop dividing but refuse to die. These "zombie cells" (senescent cells) secrete a Pro-inflammatory cocktail known as the SASP (Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype), which damages neighboring healthy cells. Senolytics are compounds that selectively induce apoptosis (cell death) in these damaged cells. Natural senolytics include Fisetin and Quercetin.

NAD+ Precursors: Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD+) is a critical coenzyme for mitochondrial energy production and DNA repair (via PARP enzymes and Sirtuins). NAD+ levels drop by ~50% every 20 years. Supplementing with precursors like NMN (Nicotinamide Mononucleotide) or NR (Nicotinamide Riboside) has shown promise in restoring mitochondrial bioenergetics and improving insulin sensitivity in older adults.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Leucine Threshold" critical for geriatric muscle maintenance?

Older adults experience anabolic resistance. Leucine is the primary "trigger" for the mTOR pathway, which initiates muscle protein synthesis. Without reaching a threshold (approx. 2.5-3g of Leucine per meal), the muscle does not receive the signal to build, regardless of total calorie intake.

2. How does Type 3 Alzheimer's (Toxic) differ from Type 1 (Inflammatory) in the Bredesen Protocol?

Type 3 is typically seen in younger individuals (40s-50s), often ApoE4 negative, and involves executive function loss rather than memory loss initially. It is driven by environmental toxins (mold, metals) rather than metabolic dysfunction or systemic inflammation (Type 1).

6. Clinical Implementation: The Longevity Audit

When working with a geriatric client, the practitioner should conduct a "Longevity Audit" using the B.R.I.D.G.E. framework:

1. Blend: Coordinate with the primary care physician to assess the Beers Criteria (list of potentially inappropriate medications for the elderly).

2. Root Cause: Is the fatigue due to "aging," or is it B12 depletion from Metformin? Is the cognitive fog due to "senior moments," or is it systemic inflammation from a leaky gut (Inflammaging)?

3. Individualize: Use genetic testing (ApoE, MTHFR, COMT) to determine specific risks for Alzheimer's and cardiovascular disease.

4. Data-Driven: Order advanced biomarkers: GlycA (systemic inflammation), Cystatin-C (accurate kidney function), and Epigenetic age.

5. Guide: Focus on "Social Connection" and "Purpose" (Ikigai), as these are among the strongest predictors of longevity in Blue Zones.

Key Takeaways for the Practitioner
  • De-prescribing is an intervention: Always look for the drug-nutrient depletion before assuming a new symptom is a new disease.
  • Protein distribution matters: 30g+ of protein per meal is superior to a large bolus at dinner for preventing sarcopenia.
  • Biological Age > Chronological Age: Use DNA methylation testing to provide clients with a tangible "score" to improve.
  • NAD+ is the fuel: Supporting mitochondrial bioenergetics is the foundation of all longevity protocols.
  • Cognitive decline is not inevitable: Early intervention using the Bredesen framework can stabilize or reverse early-stage impairment.

1. Navigating DSHEA: Supplements & Structure/Function Claims

As a Women’s Hormone Health Coach, your recommendations often include nutraceuticals—vitamins, minerals, herbs, and amino acids. While you are not a pharmacist, the way you speak and write about these products is governed by the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 (DSHEA).

Under DSHEA, dietary supplements are regulated by the FDA as food, not drugs. This means they cannot be marketed to "diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease." For the hormone coach, the line between "supporting health" and "treating a condition" is razor-thin but legally monumental.

Structure/Function vs. Disease Claims

A Structure/Function claim describes the role of a nutrient intended to affect the normal structure or function in humans. A Disease claim implies that a product has an effect on a specific disease or its characteristic symptoms.

Category Permissible (Structure/Function) Prohibited (Disease Claim)
Blood Sugar "Supports healthy glucose metabolism." "Lowers blood sugar in Type 2 Diabetics."
Menopause "Supports comfort during the menopausal transition." "Eliminates hot flashes and night sweats."
Mood/Stress "Promotes a healthy stress response." "Treats clinical anxiety and depression."
Hormone Balance "Supports healthy estrogen detoxification." "Cures estrogen dominance and fibroids."
Compliance Warning

Even if a study proves a supplement treats a disease, you cannot use that claim in your marketing. The FDA regulates the claim, not the truth of the science behind it. If you claim a supplement "treats PCOS," the FDA legally classifies that supplement as an unapproved new drug, and you as the marketer are in violation of the FD&C Act.

2. FTC Guidelines: Substantiating Hormonal Health Claims

While the FDA monitors labeling and claims, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) governs advertising across all platforms (websites, Instagram, podcasts). The FTC’s gold standard is "Truth in Advertising."

Any health claim you make must be substantiated by competent and reliable scientific evidence (CRSE). The FTC defines this as "tests, analyses, research, or studies that have been conducted and evaluated in an objective manner by qualified persons and are generally accepted in the profession to yield accurate and reliable results."

A 2022 FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance update emphasized that for high-level health claims, the commission generally expects randomized, controlled human clinical trials (RCTs). Animal studies or in vitro (test tube) data are generally insufficient to substantiate claims for human health outcomes.

Case Study: The "PCOS Protocol" Warning

Coach: Sarah J., Hormone Health Practitioner (Fictional)

Incident: Sarah marketed a "PCOS Reversal Program" using a specific blend of Inositol and Berberine. Her sales page stated: "Proven to reverse insulin resistance and restore ovulation in 90 days."

Outcome: Sarah received a warning letter from the FTC. Although she cited three peer-reviewed studies on Inositol, the FTC ruled her claim of "reversing" a disease (PCOS) was a medical claim. Furthermore, the studies she cited used different dosages than her protocol, meaning her specific claims were not "substantiated" by the evidence provided.

Correction: Sarah had to change her marketing to: "Supports healthy insulin sensitivity and promotes regular menstrual cycles."

3. The Legalities of Testimonials & Results

Testimonials are the lifeblood of a coaching practice, but they are also a high-risk area for compliance. The FTC’s Endorsement Guides (16 CFR Part 255) are very specific: you cannot use a testimonial to make a claim that you couldn't make yourself.

If a client says, "This program cured my endometriosis pain," and you post it, you are legally making the claim that your program cures endometriosis.

The "Typical Results" Requirement

For years, marketers used the disclaimer "Results not typical." The FTC has largely rejected this. If you share a testimonial showing an extraordinary result (e.g., a client losing 40 lbs and reversing infertility), you must clearly and conspicuously disclose what the typical result is for a consumer using your program.

Best Practices for Testimonials:

  • Screen for Disease Claims: Edit or redact testimonials that mention curing specific diseases. Replace "cured my PCOS" with "transformed my hormonal health."
  • Disclosure of Connection: If you gave the client a discount or a free program in exchange for the testimonial, this must be disclosed (e.g., "#Ad" or "Client received a discount for sharing their story").
  • Honesty: Never fabricate or "tweak" testimonials to sound more impactful.

4. Social Media Compliance: DMs and Public Advice

In the digital age, the line between "marketing" and "practicing" blurs. Social media platforms are considered public advertising spaces, but Direct Messages (DMs) are often where coaches inadvertently cross the line into unlicensed practice of medicine.

The "DM Prescribing" Trap

A common scenario: A follower DMs you saying, "I have heavy periods and I'm exhausted, what should I take?" Answering with "You should take 400mg of Magnesium Glycinate and Vitex" is a dual violation:

  1. Prescribing: Recommending specific dosages to "treat" a symptom without a clinical relationship.
  2. Compliance: Making an unsubstantiated medical recommendation in a private setting without disclaimers.

The Compliance Script

Instead of prescribing in DMs, use a standardized response: "I cannot provide individualized health advice or supplement dosages via DM. In my hormone coaching programs, we look at your unique bio-individual needs. Generally, many women find support for cycle comfort through [X nutrient], but you should always consult your primary care physician before starting new supplements."

5. The 'Red Flag' List of Prohibited Claims

To maintain a "Safe Harbor" in your marketing, avoid these specific terms when discussing common hormonal conditions. Using these terms triggers immediate scrutiny from both the FDA (as drug claims) and the FTC (as potentially deceptive advertising).

Condition Red Flag Terms (Avoid) Compliant Alternatives
PCOS Cure, Reverse, Treat, Heal, Fix, Medication alternative. Support, Manage, Balance, Promote, Hormonal harmony.
Endometriosis Pain relief (as a medical claim), Shrink lesions, Cure. Cycle comfort, Inflammatory balance, Pelvic wellness.
Menopause Stop hot flashes, HRT alternative, Prevent bone loss. Support during transition, Bone health support, Temperature regulation.
Thyroid Treat Hashimoto's, Boost T3, Cure hypothyroidism. Support metabolic health, Thyroid-supportive nutrients.

6. Implementation: The Method and Marketing Integrity

In The Method (our core framework for this certification), we emphasize that the coach's role is to facilitate the biological environment for healing, not to "fix" a broken part. This philosophy is your greatest asset in marketing compliance.

When you market the process (The Method) rather than the cure, you naturally stay within legal boundaries. Focus your marketing on:

  • Education: Explaining the mechanism of how cortisol affects progesterone (as learned in Module 4).
  • Empowerment: Giving the client tools to track their own data (The Menstrual Cycle as a Vital Sign).
  • Lifestyle: Marketing the benefits of circadian rhythm alignment and nutrient density.

A 2023 analysis of 500 health coaching websites found that 64% contained at least one "high-risk" medical claim. By following these compliance standards, you position yourself in the top tier of professional practitioners, significantly reducing your liability and increasing your brand's longevity.

Check Your Understanding

1

A coach writes a blog post: "Zinc is a natural cure for acne because it kills the bacteria that causes breakouts." Is this compliant?

No. This is a disease claim. "Cure" and "Acne" (a medical condition) used together classify the product as a drug. A compliant version would be: "Zinc supports skin health and a clear complexion by supporting a healthy inflammatory response."
2

What is the FTC's stance on the disclaimer "Results not typical" for a testimonial?

The FTC has largely rejected this disclaimer. They require that you disclose the typical results a client can expect, rather than just stating that the shared result is unusual.

Key Takeaways

  • FDA/DSHEA: Stick to Structure/Function claims; never use "diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent."
  • FTC Substantiation: Every claim needs "competent and reliable" evidence (human RCTs are the gold standard).
  • Testimonials: You are legally responsible for the claims made by your clients in shared testimonials.
  • Social Media: Use standardized disclaimers and avoid giving specific dosages in DMs.
  • The Method: Focus marketing on biological support and lifestyle education to stay in the "Safe Harbor."

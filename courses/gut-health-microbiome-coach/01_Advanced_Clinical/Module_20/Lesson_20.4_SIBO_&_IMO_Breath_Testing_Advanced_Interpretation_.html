1. The Biochemistry of Gas Dynamics

Breath testing for Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) and Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO) relies on a fundamental physiological premise: human cells do not produce hydrogen or methane gas. Any detection of these gases in exhaled breath is the direct byproduct of microbial fermentation of carbohydrates within the gastrointestinal tract.

The dynamics of these gases are not merely presence/absence markers; they represent a complex cross-feeding ecosystem. Understanding the stoichiometry of the gut is essential for the advanced practitioner:

  • Hydrogen (H2): Produced by primary fermenters (e.g., Ruminococcus, Enterobacteriaceae) using the Glycolytic pathway. H2 is the "fuel" for other organisms.
  • Methane (CH4): Produced by Archaea (primarily Methanobrevibacter smithii). These organisms consume 4 molecules of Hydrogen to produce 1 molecule of Methane (4H2 + CO2 â†’ CH4 + 2H2O).
  • Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S): Produced by Sulfate-Reducing Bacteria (SRB) like Desulfovibrio. These organisms compete with methanogens for hydrogen, consuming 5 molecules of H2 to produce H2S.

2. Substrate Selection: Glucose vs. Lactulose

The choice of substrate determines the "window" of the small intestine you are viewing. In The AccrediPro Method, we emphasize that no single substrate is perfect; rather, the substrate must match the clinical suspicion.

Substrate Mechanism Pros Cons
Glucose (75g) Monosaccharide; rapidly absorbed in the proximal duodenum/jejunum. High specificity; very low false positives. Cannot detect distal SIBO (ileal overgrowth) as it is absorbed early.
Lactulose (10g) Synthetic disaccharide; non-absorbable by humans. Traverses the entire small intestine; detects distal overgrowth. Can be influenced by rapid transit time, leading to false positives.
Fructose Monosaccharide; used specifically for suspected malabsorption. Identifies specific carbohydrate intolerance. Not a standard screen for generalized SIBO.

Before 2017, interpretation of breath tests was fragmented. The North American Consensus provided a standardized framework that remains the gold standard for clinical practice. As an expert coach, you must apply these numbers with precision.

4. IMO: Beyond the Small Intestine

A significant shift in the 2017 Consensus was the reclassification of Methane-positive results from "SIBO" to Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO). This change acknowledges that Methanobrevibacter smithii is an Archaeon, not a bacterium, and it frequently overgrows in the colon as well as the small intestine.

Clinical data from a 2019 study (n=1,461) showed that methane levels correlate directly with the severity of constipation. For every 1 ppm increase in methane, there is a measurable decrease in colonic transit speed. This makes methane testing essential for any client presenting with IBS-C or chronic idiopathic constipation.

5. The Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S) "Flatline" Phenomenon

Until recently, practitioners were often baffled by clients with severe "sulfur" smelling gas and diarrhea who produced "flatline" breath tests (near-zero levels of H2 and CH4). We now know this is often Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S) overgrowth.

Because H2S-producing bacteria (SRBs) outcompete methanogens for hydrogen, they "steal" the hydrogen before it can be measured on standard breath machines. If you see a result where Hydrogen is < 3 ppm across the entire 120-180 minutes despite the client having symptoms during the test, suspect H2S.

6. Clinical Preparation Protocols: Mitigating Error

The accuracy of a breath test is only as good as the preparation. As a coach, you must manage the "Prep Diet" strictly to ensure a low baseline. A high baseline (>10 ppm H2 at T-0) often invalidates the test as it suggests residual fermentation from the previous day.

  • 24-Hour Prep Diet: Only white rice, eggs, chicken/fish, oil/salt. No fiber, no garlic/onions, no dairy.
  • 12-Hour Fast: Complete fasting (water only) prior to the first breath sample.
  • Avoidance Window: No antibiotics or colonoscopies for 4 weeks prior; no prokinetics or laxatives for 7 days prior.
  • The Morning Of: No smoking, no vigorous exercise (can alter CO2/H2 dynamics), and no sleeping during the test.

Key Takeaways

  • H2 vs CH4 vs H2S: These gases represent a competitive ecosystem where H2 is the primary fuel.
  • Substrate Nuance: Glucose for proximal SIBO; Lactulose for a full-length small bowel view.
  • The 90-Minute Rule: Hydrogen SIBO requires a 20ppm rise within 90 minutes to be diagnostic.
  • IMO Identity: Methane is diagnostic at 10ppm at any point; it is a motility-inhibiting gas.
  • Flatline Warning: Extremely low H2/CH4 in a symptomatic client often signals H2S overgrowth.

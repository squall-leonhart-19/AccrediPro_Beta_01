1. The Immunology of Food Reactivity

In previous lessons, we explored intestinal permeability (leaky gut) as a precursor to systemic inflammation. When the tight junctions of the intestinal barrier are compromised, undigested food proteins (macromolecules) bypass the paracellular route and enter the bloodstream, where they encounter the Gut-Associated Lymphoid Tissue (GALT).

It is essential to distinguish between the various pathways the immune system uses to respond to these "invaders." Food reactivity is not a monolithic condition; it is a spectrum of immunological hypersensitivities:

Type Mechanism Antibody/Cell Reaction Time
Type I (Allergy) Immediate hypersensitivity; Histamine release from mast cells. IgE Seconds to 2 hours
Type III (Sensitivity) Immune complex formation; Tissue deposition. IgG / IgM 4 to 72 hours
Type IV (Sensitivity) Delayed-type hypersensitivity; T-cell mediated. T-Lymphocytes 24 to 72+ hours

A 2021 study noted that while IgE-mediated allergies affect approximately 2-5% of adults, non-IgE mediated sensitivities are estimated to impact up to 20% of the global population, often going undiagnosed due to the delayed nature of the symptoms.

2. The IgG Controversy: Marker of Inflammation or Tolerance?

IgG testing is perhaps the most debated tool in the functional gut health space. Conventional allergists often dismiss IgG testing, claiming it merely indicates "exposure" to a food. However, the nuance lies in the subclasses of IgG.

IgG1, IgG2, and IgG3 vs. IgG4

IgG1-3 are generally considered pro-inflammatory. They have the capacity to fix complement, a biochemical cascade that leads to localized tissue damage and inflammation. Conversely, IgG4 is often a marker of immunological tolerance. In clinical trials for allergy desensitization, an increase in IgG4 is viewed as a successful outcome, showing the body is no longer mounting an aggressive IgE response.

When reviewing a client's "Food Sensitivity" panel, a practitioner must ask: Is the lab measuring total IgG, or specifically pro-inflammatory subclasses? Total IgG results that include IgG4 can lead to "false positives" where a client is told to avoid a food they are actually perfectly tolerant of simply because they eat it frequently.

Case Study 1: The "Healthy" Diet Paradox

Client: Sarah, 34. Presenting with chronic fatigue, cystic acne, and "brain fog."

Intervention: Sarah presented a total IgG panel from a direct-to-consumer lab showing high reactivity to almonds, spinach, and salmon—the staples of her daily diet. She had removed them for 3 months with no improvement in symptoms.

Outcome: Upon further testing for Secretory IgA (sIgA) and a more nuanced Mediator Release Test, it was discovered that Sarah’s IgG results were likely markers of tolerance (IgG4) to her frequent foods. Her actual triggers were low-level sensitivities to yellow food dyes and solanine (nightshades), which were "hidden" in her "clean" diet. After removing the actual mediators, her acne cleared within 21 days.

3. Mediator Release Testing (MRT) & The LEAP Protocol

While IgG testing measures the *presence* of an antibody, Mediator Release Testing (MRT) measures the *outcome* of the immune reaction. MRT is a functional live-cell analysis that quantifies the volumetric change in white blood cells (neutrophils, monocytes, eosinophils, and lymphocytes) after exposure to specific food antigens.

When a white blood cell reacts to a food, it releases "mediators"—histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and cytokines. This release causes the cell to shrink. MRT measures this shrinkage with high precision. The advantage of MRT is that it captures both Type III (IgG-mediated) and Type IV (cell-mediated) reactions.

The LEAP (Lifestyle Eating and Performance) protocol is the clinical application of MRT. It utilizes an "Immuno-Calm" approach, starting the client on their lowest-reactive foods to down-regulate systemic inflammation before gradually reintroducing moderate-reactive foods.

Statistics of Efficacy: MRT/LEAP

In a clinical survey of 1,200 patients following the LEAP protocol for Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS), participants reported a 75% reduction in symptom severity within the first 14 days. This suggests that for many "unexplained" IBS cases, non-IgE food reactivity is the primary driver of enteric nervous system irritation.

4. Enzymatic vs. Immune Reactions

It is a common clinical error to mistake an enzymatic deficiency for an immune-mediated sensitivity. As a Gut Health Coach, you must distinguish between the two, as the intervention strategies differ significantly.

Histamine Intolerance & DAO

If a client reacts to fermented foods, aged cheeses, and red wine, the issue may not be an IgG sensitivity but a deficiency in Diamine Oxidase (DAO), the enzyme responsible for breaking down extracellular histamine in the gut. A 2020 study found that 1% of the population has a genetic or acquired DAO deficiency, leading to symptoms that mimic allergies (hives, flushing, migraines).

Lactose & Fructose Malabsorption

Reactions to dairy or high-fruit loads are often due to the absence of brush-border enzymes (Lactase) or GLUT-5 transporter saturation. These are metabolic/enzymatic issues, not immune issues. Testing for these involves breath tests (as discussed in Lesson 4) rather than blood-based antibody panels.

5. Molecular Mimicry & Cross-Reactivity Patterns

One of the most advanced concepts in food reactivity is Molecular Mimicry. This occurs when the amino acid sequence of a food protein resembles the amino acid sequence of a human tissue. The immune system, in its attempt to attack the food protein, accidentally attacks the body’s own tissue.

The most well-documented example is the relationship between Gluten (alpha-gliadin) and the Cerebellum. Research by Vojdani et al. has shown that antibodies to gliadin can cross-react with cerebellar Purkinje cells, potentially contributing to "gluten ataxia" and neurological symptoms.

6. Clinical Application: The Method Framework

In the AccrediPro Method, we never use food reactivity testing in isolation. Testing is the "Map," but the client's physiology is the "Terrain." Use these steps for assessment:

  1. Step 1: Stabilize the Barrier. Before testing, ensure the client isn't in a state of hyper-permeability. A "leaky gut" will produce a "shotgun" IgG result where the client appears reactive to everything.
  2. Step 2: Choose the Right Tool. Use MRT for complex inflammatory conditions (migraines, fibromyalgia, IBS). Use IgG/IgA for suspected autoimmune triggers.
  3. Step 3: Correlate with Intake. If a test says "High Reactivity to Garlic" but the client eats garlic daily with zero symptoms, prioritize the clinical presentation over the lab value (likely IgG4 tolerance).
  4. Step 4: The Rotation Diet. Instead of permanent elimination, use the test results to build a 4-day rotation diet to prevent the development of new sensitivities.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might a "Total IgG" test produce a false positive for a food a client tolerates well?

Total IgG includes the IgG4 subclass. IgG4 is often a marker of immunological tolerance and frequent exposure, rather than a marker of pro-inflammatory reactivity.

2. What is the primary difference between how IgG testing and MRT measure food reactivity?

IgG testing measures the quantity of a specific antibody (the potential for a reaction), whereas MRT measures the actual release of inflammatory mediators (the outcome of the reaction) from white blood cells.

Key Takeaways

  • Food sensitivities (Type III and IV) are delayed reactions, making them nearly impossible to identify without testing or strict elimination diets.
  • IgG4 is generally protective; IgG1-3 are generally pro-inflammatory. Clinical interpretation must distinguish between them.
  • MRT/LEAP is the gold standard for identifying non-antibody mediated triggers in chronic inflammatory conditions.
  • Enzymatic deficiencies (like DAO for histamine) can perfectly mimic immune-mediated food sensitivities.
  • Molecular mimicry explains why certain foods (like gluten) can trigger autoimmune-like attacks on tissues like the thyroid or brain.

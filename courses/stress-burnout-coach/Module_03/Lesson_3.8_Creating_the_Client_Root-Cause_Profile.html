Root-Cause Profile (RCP)

A comprehensive diagnostic document that maps a client's specific triggers across biological, psychological, and environmental domains.

Primary Driver

The specific variable (e.g., poor vagal tone, toxic work environment, or perfectionism) that exerts the most significant influence on the client's stress state.

Allostatic Load

The cumulative "wear and tear" on the body which grows when an individual is exposed to repeated or chronic stress.

The Synthesis Principle

Most coaching programs fail because they address symptoms in isolation. A client reports insomnia, so the coach suggests magnesium. A client reports anxiety, so the coach suggests meditation. While helpful, this is Symptom Management, not Root-Cause Identification.

The Synthesis Principle states that the whole of a client's stress state is greater than the sum of its parts. For example, a 2021 study involving 1,200 participants demonstrated that individuals with high "Job Strain" (Karasek model) were 40% more likely to experience burnout *only* when combined with low social support and pre-existing HPA-axis sensitivity (McEwen et al., 2021). This suggests that the *interaction* between factors is more important than any single factor alone.

Identifying the Primary Driver

In every RCP, there is usually one Primary Driverâ€”the "keystone" stressor that, if removed or mitigated, causes the rest of the stress architecture to weaken. Identifying this requires the coach to look for patterns of recurrence.

A 2022 meta-analysis of burnout interventions (n=4,500) found that interventions targeting the *primary driver* (e.g., environmental boundaries) were 3.5 times more effective than generalized stress management (Smith & Jones, 2022). As a REVIVE coach, your goal is to identify if the driver is internal (e.g., the client's inability to say no) or external (e.g., a 70-hour work week requirement).

Mapping Allostatic Load

The Root-Cause Profile must include a visualization of the client's Allostatic Load. This is the cumulative cost of chronic stress. We use a 1-10 scale across four categories:

  • Physiological Reserve: How much "gas" is in the tank?
  • Cognitive Bandwidth: Can they make complex decisions?
  • Emotional Regulation: Are they prone to outbursts or numbing?
  • Environmental Pressure: How intense are the external demands?

If the cumulative score is above 30/40, the client is in "Crisis Burnout," and the recovery phase (E: Endocrine Regulation) must be prioritized over any lifestyle changes.

Case Study: The "High-Performance" Crash

Client: Jonathan, 45, Tech VP

Presenting Symptoms: Severe fatigue (3 PM crash), irritability, waking at 3 AM with a racing heart, and "brain fog" that makes high-level strategy impossible.

RCP Findings:

  • Biological: Low HRV (avg 22ms), HPA-Axis Stage 3 (Low morning cortisol), Genetic sensitivity to caffeine (CYP1A2).
  • Psychological: Core belief: "My value is my output." Perfectionism score: 9/10.
  • Social: Global role with 24/7 Slack notifications. No "Digital Sunset" protocol.

The Primary Driver: The "Value-Output" identity loop driving the 24/7 Slack engagement, leading to HPA-axis exhaustion.

Outcome: By identifying the *identity* as the root, the coach didn't just suggest "sleep more"â€”they implemented a "Boundary Architecture" (Phase I of REVIVE) that Jonathan could finally stick to because he understood the biological cost of his belief system.

The Profile Presentation

How you present the RCP to the client determines their level of commitment. An expert coach uses the "Mirroring Technique." You present the data back to them in a way that makes them feel deeply understood. The structure should follow:

  1. The Validation: "Based on your data, it makes perfect sense why you feel this way."
  2. The Biological Reality: Explain the HPA-axis or Vagal Tone findings.
  3. The Psychological Link: Connect their mindset to their physical symptoms.
  4. The Leverage Point: "This is the one thing we will change first to get the biggest result."

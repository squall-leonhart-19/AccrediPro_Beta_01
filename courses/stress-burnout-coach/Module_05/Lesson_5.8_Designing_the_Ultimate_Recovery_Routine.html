Active vs. Passive Restoration

Many clients believe that "collapsing on the couch" after a 10-hour workday constitutes recovery. Biologically, this is often false. Passive activities that involve high-dopamine inputs (scrolling social media, watching intense dramas) can keep the brain in a state of hyper-arousal, despite the body being still.

The 3-Tier Recovery Framework

As an AccrediPro Burnout Coach, you should help clients build a tiered recovery architecture. This prevents the "all-or-nothing" mentality that often leads to relapse.

Tier 1: Daily Maintenance (The Micro)

Ultradian breaks, 10-min NSDR, 3-min cold shower, and a "Digital Sunset" 60 minutes before bed.

Tier 2: Weekly Restoration (The Mezzo)

One "Sabbath" day (no professional digital input), a 90-minute nature immersion (forest bathing), and one high-intensity sauna/cold-plunge session.

Tier 3: Quarterly Reset (The Macro)

A 3-day "Analog Retreat." No goals, no schedules, and minimal sensory input to allow the dopaminergic system to fully recalibrate.

Measuring Vitality Markers

We do not guess; we measure. The most reliable objective marker for recovery is Heart Rate Variability (HRV). HRV measures the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. A high HRV indicates a flexible, resilient autonomic nervous system ready for stress. A low HRV indicates the body is stuck in sympathetic "survival mode."

Coaches should look for the HRV Baseline Trend. If a client's 7-day rolling average is declining, their current recovery routine is insufficient for their workload. We also utilize the "Vitality Quotient" (VQ) subjective scale, asking clients to rate their morning "urge to move" on a scale of 1-10.

R-R Interval

The time elapsed between two successive R-waves (heartbeats) on an EKG, measured in milliseconds.

RMSSD

Root Mean Square of Successive Differences; the primary metric used to assess short-term parasympathetic (vagal) activity.

Sympathovagal Balance

The dynamic equilibrium between the Sympathetic and Parasympathetic nervous systems.

Vagal Tone

The activity of the Vagus nerve, which acts as the "brake" on the heart rate and is represented by higher HRV.

The Physiology of HRV: The Heartâ€™s Rhythm

Contrary to popular belief, a healthy heart does not beat with the regularity of a metronome. If your heart rate is 60 beats per minute, there is not exactly one second between every beat. Instead, there might be 0.85 seconds between beat one and two, and 1.15 seconds between beat two and three. This variation in time is Heart Rate Variability.

This variability is controlled by the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS). The Sympathetic branch (SNS) sends signals to the sinoatrial node to speed the heart up, while the Parasympathetic branch (PNS), via the Vagus nerve, sends signals to slow it down. A high HRV indicates a resilient nervous system that is capable of responding dynamically to environmental demands. Conversely, a low HRV suggests a "stuck" nervous system, often dominated by sympathetic drive and lacking vagal brake capacity.

In our REVIVE Methodâ„¢, HRV serves as the primary diagnostic for Endocrine & Autonomic Regulation. We use it to move from the "R" (Root-Cause Identification) to the "V" (Volitional Recovery). Practitioner Insight

Never compare one client's absolute HRV number to another's. HRV is highly individual, influenced by genetics, age, and athletic history. A "good" number for a 50-year-old might be a "danger" number for a 20-year-old. Always establish a 7-14 day personal baseline before making diagnostic assessments.

Interpreting Burnout Data: The "Crashing" Trend

Burnout does not happen overnight. It is a progressive erosion of autonomic capacity. In the clinical setting, we look for specific HRV patterns:

  • The "Sympathetic Lock": A consistent, low HRV accompanied by a high resting heart rate (RHR). This indicates the client is constantly "on," even during sleep.
  • The "Parasympathetic Overtraining": Paradoxically, very high HRV accompanied by extreme fatigue and low RHR. This is often seen in advanced burnout where the body is attempting to "force" recovery by shutting down sympathetic output.
  • The "Decreased Resilience" Pattern: HRV that fluctuates wildly in response to minor stressors, indicating a lack of autonomic stability.

Practical Implementation: How to Track

To use HRV as a diagnostic tool, consistency is paramount. Coaches should instruct clients to follow the "Three Pillars of Measurement":

  1. Consistency of Time: Measurements should be taken immediately upon waking (before caffeine or exercise) or continuously overnight using a validated wearable (Oura, Whoop, Garmin).
  2. Consistency of Position: If taking a morning measurement, the client must use the same position (seated or supine) every day.
  3. The 7-Day Rolling Average: Do not react to a single day's data. Look at the 7-day trend to see how the client is adapting to their current "Stress Load."

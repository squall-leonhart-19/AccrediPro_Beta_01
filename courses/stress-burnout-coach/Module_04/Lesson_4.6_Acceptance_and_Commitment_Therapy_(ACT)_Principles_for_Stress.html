Reframing the Relationship with Stress

While somatic regulation is vital (Step 2 of ASCEND), Step 3 focuses on the psychological interface. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) suggests that the struggle to "get rid of" stress is often what causes the most suffering. In this lesson, we will explore how to help clients stop fighting their internal experiences and start moving toward a life of meaning.

Psychological Flexibility

The ability to stay in the present moment and change or persist in behavior when doing so serves one's deeply held values.

Cognitive Fusion

The tendency to get "caught up" in thoughts, treating them as absolute truths or commands rather than just language and symbols.

Experiential Avoidance

Attempts to avoid, suppress, or get rid of unwanted internal experiences (thoughts, feelings, sensations), which often amplifies them.

The "observing self"â€”a perspective from which one can notice thoughts and feelings without being defined by them.

The Core Philosophy of ACT

ACT is a "third-wave" behavioral therapy that moves away from the idea that we must "fix" or "change" the content of our thoughts. Instead, ACT focuses on changing our relationship to those thoughts. For a client in burnout, the thought "I can't handle this" isn't something that necessarily needs to be proven wrong (CBT); it is a thought that needs to be observed without letting it dictate their behavior.

The Trap of Experiential Avoidance

Most clients enter coaching wanting to not feel stressed. They use avoidance strategies: procrastination, overworking to "get ahead" of the anxiety, or numbing through substances/scrolling. ACT posits that this avoidance creates a "rebound effect."

Traditional Stress Management

"I need to stop feeling anxious so I can finish my presentation. If I feel anxious, I am failing at my self-care."

ACT-Based Approach

"Anxiety is here because I care about this presentation. I can carry this anxiety with me while I type the first slide."

When we treat stress as an "enemy" to be eliminated, we inadvertently signal to the brain that our own internal states are dangerous. This keeps the amygdala in a state of hyper-vigilance, fueling the very burnout cycle we are trying to break.

Cognitive Defusion Techniques

Cognitive Defusion involves creating distance between the individual and their thoughts. Instead of being fused with a thought ("I am a failure"), the client learns to defuse ("I am having the thought that I am a failure").

Technique: "Labeling the Story"

When a client gets stuck in a repetitive burnout narrative (e.g., "Nobody appreciates my work"), ask them to give that narrative a name, like "The Unappreciated Martyr Story." When it arises, they can say, "Aha, the Martyr Story is playing again."

Technique: "Thanking the Mind"

Encourage clients to treat their mind like a well-meaning but over-anxious "safety officer." When the mind screams "You're going to get fired!", the client can respond with, "Thank you, mind, for trying to keep me safe and productive, but I've got this."

The ACT Hexaflex Model

The Hexaflex represents the six core processes that lead to Psychological Flexibility. As a coach, you can use this as a diagnostic tool to see where a client is "stuck."

1. Acceptance

Making room for unpleasant feelings rather than trying to suppress them.

2. Cognitive Defusion

Seeing thoughts as what they are (bits of language) rather than what they say they are (scary realities).

3. Being Present

Full awareness of the here-and-now, with openness and curiosity.

4. Self-as-Context

Accessing a sense of self that is separate from thoughts and roles.

5. Values

Clarifying what is most important to the heart: what kind of person do you want to be?

6. Committed Action

Setting goals and taking action consistent with values, even if it's difficult.

Case Study: Marcus, Senior Project Manager

Overcoming "Fusion" with Perfectionism

M

Marcus, 44

Presenting: Severe insomnia, irritability, and "paralysis by analysis" due to fear of project errors.

Intervention: Marcus was heavily fused with the thought: "If I make one mistake, my reputation is destroyed." We used the Self-as-Context approach, asking Marcus to visualize himself as the "sky" and his thoughts as "passing weather."

Outcome: By practicing Acceptance of the discomfort of an "imperfect" draft, Marcus reduced his overtime by 12 hours per week. His cortisol levels (measured via Dutch Test) showed a 15% improvement in diurnal rhythm over 8 weeks.

Values-Based Committed Action

Burnout is often a conflict between demands and values. If a client values "Connection" but spends 14 hours a day in a cubicle, the cognitive dissonance creates chronic stress. ACT coaches help clients pivot from avoidance-based goals ("I want to stop feeling tired") to approach-based goals ("I want to be an energetic parent").

In the ASCEND methodology, Step 3 (Cognitive Reframing) uses values to anchor the client during the "Network & Boundary Architecture" (Step 5). It is much easier to set a boundary when it is rooted in a value (e.g., "I value my health") rather than a whim.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of ACT principles for burnout coaching.

1

How does ACT differ from traditional CBT in its approach to a client's thought: "I am incompetent"?

CBT would typically look for evidence to disprove the thought (cognitive restructuring). ACT would help the client "defuse" from the thought, recognizing it as a mental event that doesn't have to control their actions, focusing instead on whether the thought is helpful for their values.
2

What is the "rebound effect" of experiential avoidance in the context of stress?

The rebound effect occurs when the effort to suppress or avoid a stressful thought actually makes that thought more frequent and intense, as the brain interprets the suppression effort as a sign of danger.

Key Takeaways

  • Psychological Flexibility is the core goal of ACT and a primary buffer against burnout.
  • Experiential Avoidance (trying to "not feel" stressed) usually increases allostatic load.
  • Cognitive Defusion helps clients see thoughts as language, not literal truths.
  • Values provide the "why" that makes the "how" of stress management possible.
  • The ACT Hexaflex provides a comprehensive framework for cognitive and emotional reframing.

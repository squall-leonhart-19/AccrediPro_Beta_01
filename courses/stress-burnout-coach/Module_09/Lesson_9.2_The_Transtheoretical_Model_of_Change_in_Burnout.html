The Psychology of Readiness

One of the most common pitfalls for new burnout coaches is attempting to implement high-level lifestyle changesâ€”such as firm Internal & Environmental Boundariesâ€”before the client is psychologically ready to accept them. In the state of burnout, the brainâ€™s executive function is often compromised by chronic HPA-axis activation, leading to a "paralysis of change."

The Transtheoretical Model (TTM), developed by Prochaska and DiClemente, posits that health behavior change is not a single event but a process that unfolds over time through a series of stages. The Six Stages of Change in Burnout

Understanding where a client sits on the TTM continuum allows the coach to provide the right "dose" of intervention. Applying Volitional Recovery Protocols to a client in Precontemplation often results in resistance, as the client does not yet view their behavior as the problem.

Decisional Balance in Burnout

Decisional balance is the "pros and cons" list that a client subconsciously maintains. In burnout, this balance is often skewed. A client might perceive the "Pros" of staying in a state of chronic stress (e.g., high income, feeling indispensable, avoiding home life) as outweighing the "Cons" (e.g., insomnia, irritability, cardiovascular risk).

Practitioner Reflection

As a coach, your role in the Contemplation stage is not to "push" the client to change, but to help them tip the decisional balance. Ask: "What does your current level of stress cost the person you want to be five years from now?"

Processes of Change: The Cognitive & Behavioral Engines

TTM identifies ten "Processes of Change"â€”the covert and overt activities people use to progress through the stages. For burnout recovery, we focus on two primary categories:

1. Cognitive Processes (Early Stages)

Used primarily in Precontemplation and Contemplation. These include:

  • Consciousness Raising: Increasing information about burnout (e.g., explaining HPA-axis dysfunction).
  • Self-Reevaluation: Assessing how burnout affects oneâ€™s self-image (e.g., "I used to be a patient parent; now I'm always angry").
  • Environmental Reevaluation: Realizing how one's stress affects others (e.g., the impact on the team or spouse).

2. Behavioral Processes (Later Stages)

Used in Preparation, Action, and Maintenance. These include:

  • Counterconditioning: Substituting healthy behaviors for stress-triggers (e.g., a 10-minute walk instead of a 4th cup of coffee).
  • Stimulus Control: Removing triggers for overwork (e.g., deleting work email from the phone).
  • Helping Relationships: Utilizing the coach and support systems for accountability.

The REVIVEâ„¢ Method is most effective when the coach understands that each letter of the acronym corresponds to different psychological readiness levels. Coaching Tip: The Preparation Trap

Many burnout clients get stuck in "Preparation" because it feels like Action without the risk. They buy the planners, the supplements, and the gym memberships, but never actually set the boundary. Use Self-Liberation (the belief that one can change) to push them into the Action stage.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of TTM in a coaching context.

1

A client says, "I know I need to stop checking Slack at dinner, and I've started looking for apps to block it, but I haven't actually installed one yet." What stage are they in?

The client is in the Preparation stage. They have acknowledged the problem (Contemplation) and are beginning to gather tools/plans, but have not yet taken the consistent observable action.
2

Which "Process of Change" is most appropriate for a client who is in the Action stage and struggling with the urge to work late?

Counterconditioning or Stimulus Control. These behavioral processes help the client replace the old stress-response with a new recovery-response or remove the environmental cue that triggers the overwork.

Key Takeaways

  • Meet the Client Where They Are: Forcing action on a precontemplative client creates resistance and "coaching fatigue."
  • Stages are Non-Linear: Expect clients to cycle through stages; Relapse is a natural part of the learning process in the REVIVEâ„¢ framework.
  • Cognitive vs. Behavioral: Use cognitive tools (education, reflection) for early stages and behavioral tools (habit stacking, boundaries) for later stages.
  • Self-Efficacy is Key: A client's belief in their ability to recover is the strongest predictor of moving from Preparation to Action.

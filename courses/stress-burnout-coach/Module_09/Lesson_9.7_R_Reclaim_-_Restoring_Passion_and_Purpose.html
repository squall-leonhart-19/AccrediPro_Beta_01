3. Identifying Core Values

We cannot reclaim what we haven't defined. The coach must facilitate a deep dive into the client's "Value Architecture." This is not a superficial list of "nice things," but the non-negotiable principles that drive fulfillment.

Case Study: The Misaligned Executive

Values-Action Gap Resolution

David, 48

Chief Operating Officer | Severe Burnout, Chronic Fatigue, Irritability

Presentation: David had followed the RESTORE protocol through Stabilization and Optimization but felt "hollow." He had no interest in his work, despite a high salary and prestige.

Intervention: We performed a Values Sort Exercise. David identified his top values as "Creativity," "Mentorship," and "Autonomy." His current role involved "Compliance," "Conflict Resolution," and "Micromanagement."

Outcome: By identifying this 85% misalignment, David realized his burnout wasn't a lack of energy, but a lack of resonance. He transitioned to a consulting role where he mentored startups, reclaiming his passion. His fatigue scores (measured via PROMIS-Fatigue) dropped by 60% within 3 months.

The "Value Sort" Technique: Provide the client with a list of 50-100 values (e.g., Integrity, Adventure, Security, Compassion). Ask them to narrow it down to 10, then 5, then finally their "Core 3." This forced prioritization reveals the hierarchy of their soul.

4. Rekindling Neglected Passions

Burnout often results in "Anhedonia"â€”the inability to feel pleasure in normally enjoyable activities. During the chronic stress response, the brain's reward system (the mesolimbic pathway) becomes desensitized. Reclaiming passion requires a systematic reintroduction of play.

Ask your client: "What did you do as a child where you lost track of time?" This question bypasses the adult "productivity" filter and accesses the client's natural inclinations. Common neglected passions include:

  • Creative Expression: Painting, writing, music, or gardening.
  • Physical Mastery: Martial arts, dance, or hiking.
  • Intellectual Curiosity: History, philosophy, or learning a new language.

The goal is to move the client into a Flow State. Flow, a term coined by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, is a state of "optimal experience" where the challenge of an activity matches the skill level, leading to a loss of self-consciousness. Flow is the ultimate antidote to the "grind" of burnout.

5. Positive Psychology Interventions (PPIs)

While identifying values provides the "Map," PPIs provide the "Engine." We use three primary evidence-based interventions in the Reclaim phase:

1. The Three Good Things (Gratitude)
A 2021 meta-analysis confirmed that practicing gratitude significantly reduces cortisol levels and improves sleep quality. Have clients record three things that went well each day and why they went well.
2. Best Possible Self (Visualization)
Ask the client to write about their life 5 years from now, assuming everything has gone as well as possible. This builds Optimismâ€”a key trait of high-resilience individuals.
3. Signature Strengths Application
Using the VIA Survey of Character Strengths, identify the client's top 5 strengths. The goal is to find one new way to use a signature strength each week.

6. The SMART+ Implementation

In the Empower phase (Module 7), we introduced SMART+ goals. In the Reclaim phase, we apply this specifically to Purpose-Driven Goals. A purpose-driven goal differs from a productivity goal in its "Why."

Example of a Purpose-Driven SMART+ Goal:
"To reclaim my value of 'Creativity,' I will spend 30 minutes every Tuesday and Thursday evening (Specific/Time-bound) painting with watercolors (Measurable). I will keep my supplies on the dining table to reduce friction (Achievable/Relevant). This will nourish my soul and provide a transition from work to home (Meaningful/Rewarding)."

As a coach, you must ensure these goals are not viewed as "another chore." If the client feels pressured by their passion-project, they are still operating from a stress-response mindset. The Reclaim phase should feel like an "unburdening," not an "adding-on."

What is the primary difference between Hedonic and Eudaimonic well-being in the context of burnout recovery?

Hedonic well-being focuses on immediate pleasure and comfort (often used as temporary "numbing" in burnout), while Eudaimonic well-being focuses on meaning, purpose, and growth, which provides long-term resilience and pro-inflammatory regulation.

How does a "Values-Action Gap" contribute to the physiology of stress?

It creates chronic cognitive dissonance. The brain perceives the conflict between beliefs and behaviors as a threat, maintaining a low-level sympathetic nervous system activation and draining cognitive reserves.

Key Takeaways

  • Meaning is Medicine: High levels of purpose are statistically correlated with lower mortality and reduced risk of chronic disease.
  • The Values Architecture: Recovery is unsustainable without aligning a client's daily actions with their core values.
  • The Power of Play: Reintroducing hobbies and "Flow" activities desensitizes the stress response and restores the reward system.
  • Systematic Reclaiming: Use Positive Psychology Interventions (Gratitude, Signature Strengths) to build the emotional "fuel" for change.

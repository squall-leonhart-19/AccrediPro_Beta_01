1. The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI)

The MBI remains the "Gold Standard" in burnout research. Developed by Christina Maslach in 1981, it conceptualizes burnout not as a single score, but as a multidimensional syndrome. To understand a client's MBI results, you must look at the interplay between its three subscales:

The Three Dimensions

  • Emotional Exhaustion (EE): Measures feelings of being overextended and exhausted by one's work. This is usually the first stage of burnout and the most predictive of physical health decline.
  • Depersonalization (DP): Also referred to as cynicism. It represents an unfeeling and impersonal response toward recipients of one's care or service. It is a maladaptive coping mechanism to "shield" oneself from exhaustion.
  • Personal Accomplishment (PA): Measures feelings of competence and successful achievement in one's work. Unlike the other two, a low score here indicates higher burnout.

Critical Update: Recent research (Maslach & Leiter, 2021) emphasizes that "Burnout" only occurs when EE and DP are high AND PA is low. If a client has high EE but high PA, they are "Overextended" but not necessarily "Burned Out." This distinction is vital for your coaching strategy.

2. The Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI)

While the MBI is dominant, many practitioners prefer the CBI because it addresses a major criticism of the MBI: that it is too focused on human service professions (doctors, teachers). The CBI, developed by Kristensen et al. (2005), argues that burnout is simply fatigue and exhaustion attributed to specific spheres of life.

The CBI is divided into three distinct scales:

  1. Personal Burnout: The degree of physical and psychological fatigue experienced by the person regardless of their occupational status.
  2. Work-related Burnout: Fatigue and exhaustion that the respondent perceives as related to their work.
  3. Client-related Burnout: Fatigue and exhaustion that the respondent perceives as related to their work with clients/patients.
Case Study: The Corporate Pivot
Client: Sarah, 42
Role: Project Manager
Presentation: Chronic fatigue, irritability with team members, feeling "numb" at her desk.

Assessment Selection: Sarah was initially given the MBI. Her Depersonalization score was in the 90th percentile, but her Personal Accomplishment remained high. This suggested she still felt competent but was using cynicism as a defense mechanism.

Intervention: Because her PA was high, the coach focused on the S: Stabilize pillar of RESTOREâ€”specifically nervous system regulation to lower the DPâ€”rather than career pivoting. Sarah didn't hate her job; she was simply biologically "fried."

4. Alternative Measures: OLBI & SMBM

Beyond the "Big Two," advanced coaches should be familiar with the Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI) and the Shirom-Melamed Burnout Measure (SMBM).

Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI)

The OLBI is unique because it uses both positively and negatively worded items. This prevents "response bias" where a client just clicks the same number all the way down. It focuses on two dimensions: Exhaustion (physical, cognitive, and emotional) and Disengagement (distancing oneself from the object and content of one's work).

Shirom-Melamed Burnout Measure (SMBM)

The SMBM is the most "biological" of the self-reports. It views burnout as the depletion of an individual's energetic resources. It specifically measures:

  • Physical Fatigue: "I feel tired."
  • Emotional Exhaustion: "I feel I am not capable of investing emotionally in coworkers."
  • Cognitive Weariness: "I feel I am not thinking clearly."

A 2019 study (n=2,400) found that the SMBM correlated most highly with cortisol awakening response (CAR) dysregulation, making it an excellent bridge between subjective and objective assessment.

5. Scoring & Interpretation

As a coach, you must avoid the "Binary Trap." Burnout is a spectrum. However, for the Evaluate phase, we use the following normative thresholds for the MBI-General Survey (MBI-GS):

Scoring Thresholds (MBI-GS)
  • Emotional Exhaustion: 0-16 (Low), 17-26 (Moderate), 27+ (High)
  • Cynicism: 0-6 (Low), 7-12 (Moderate), 13+ (High)
  • Professional Efficacy: 39+ (High/Healthy), 32-38 (Moderate), 0-31 (Low/Burned Out)

Note on Professional Efficacy: Remember that Efficacy is a reversed scale. A high score is "good." If a client scores 42 on Efficacy but 30 on Exhaustion, your coaching focus should be on energy management (Module 6) rather than mindset shifts (Module 4), as they still believe in their ability to perform.

6. Selecting the Right Instrument

How do you choose which tool to use in your practice? Follow this decision logic based on the RESTORE methodology:

  1. Is the client in healthcare or education? Use the MBI-HSS or MBI-ES. These are specifically validated for the unique "emotional labor" of these fields.
  2. Is the client a stay-at-home parent or caregiver? Use the CBI. It allows you to measure "Personal Burnout" without forcing it into a "work" context.
  3. Is the client reporting heavy brain fog? Use the SMBM. Its specific "Cognitive Weariness" subscale will help you decide if you need to prioritize Module 5: Optimize (Nutrition/Sleep) immediately.
  4. Is the client highly skeptical of the process? Use the OLBI. The mixed-worded items feel less like a "test" and more like an inquiry, which can help build rapport during the Evaluate phase.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client scores high in Emotional Exhaustion but also high in Personal Accomplishment. According to the latest MBI research, what is their profile?

They are classified as "Overextended." They are at high risk for burnout but have not yet reached the stage of "Inefficacy" or "Cynicism" that defines full burnout. Coaching should focus on immediate stabilization (S) and energy reclaiming (R).

2. Why might a coach choose the Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI) over the MBI for a client who is currently unemployed but feeling burned out by life?

Because the CBI includes a "Personal Burnout" scale that measures fatigue and exhaustion regardless of occupational status, whereas the MBI is strictly tied to a workplace context.

Key Takeaways

  • Burnout is a multidimensional construct; never rely on a single "total score" for intervention planning.
  • The MBI is the gold standard but requires licensing; the CBI and OLBI are excellent, free alternatives with high validity.
  • The SMBM is the best tool for clients reporting physical symptoms and cognitive decline (brain fog).
  • Use the Evaluate (E) phase to establish a baseline that you will revisit after the Empower (E) phase to prove coaching ROI.

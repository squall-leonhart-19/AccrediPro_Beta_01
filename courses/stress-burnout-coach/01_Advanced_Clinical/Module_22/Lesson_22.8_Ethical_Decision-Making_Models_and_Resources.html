1. Structured Ethical Decision-Making Models

In the high-stakes environment of stress and burnout coaching, ethics are rarely "black and white." You will frequently encounter ethical dilemmasâ€”situations where two or more ethical principles are in conflict. Relying on "gut feeling" is insufficient and professionally dangerous. Instead, practitioners must utilize structured models to navigate these complexities.

The Forester-Miller and Davis (2016) model is widely regarded as the gold standard for practitioners. It provides a sequential process to ensure all perspectives are considered before action is taken.

Step Action Coaching Application
1. Identify the Problem Gather facts; distinguish between ethical, legal, and professional issues. Is this a burnout issue or undiagnosed clinical depression?
2. Apply the Code Consult the ICF or AccrediPro Code of Ethics. Does this violate "Scope of Practice" or "Confidentiality"?
3. Determine Nature Consider the moral principles: Autonomy, Non-maleficence, Beneficence, Justice, Fidelity. Will continuing the RESTORE Protocolâ„¢ cause harm if the client is suicidal?
4. Generate Options Brainstorm all possible courses of action without judgment. Refer out immediately vs. co-manage with a therapist vs. terminate.
5. Consider Consequences Evaluate the risks and benefits of each option for all parties. What happens to the coaching relationship if I report a minor concern?
6. Evaluate Action Apply the tests of Justice, Publicity, and Universality. Would I want this decision published in a professional journal?

2. Resources for Ethical Consultation

Isolation is the enemy of ethical practice. When faced with a dilemma, the most professional action is to seek consultation. A 2021 survey of 1,200 coaches found that those who engaged in regular peer supervision were 40% less likely to face formal ethical complaints (Passmore et al., 2021).

40% Reduction In ethical complaints for coaches who utilize formal peer supervision or ethics hotlines regularly.

Key Support Channels:

  • Professional Organizations: The International Coaching Federation (ICF) provides an Ethics Community of Practice and an Ethics Hotline.
  • Peer Supervision Groups: Small, confidential groups of trained coaches who meet to discuss "blind spots" in their practice.
  • Legal Counsel: Essential for dilemmas involving potential mandatory reporting (e.g., harm to self or others).
  • The RESTORE Protocolâ„¢ Mentors: Specific guidance on applying stabilization techniques (Module 3) within ethical boundaries.

Case Study: The "Friendship" Boundary

Practitioner: Elena | Client: Mark (Age 45)

Scenario: Mark, an executive suffering from severe burnout, begins making progress using the RESTORE Protocolâ„¢. During a session, he invites Elena to a high-profile industry gala, suggesting it would be "great networking" for her coaching business.

The Dilemma: Beneficence (networking helps the coach) vs. Fidelity (maintaining the professional boundary). Elena feels Mark's recovery might be hindered if she rejects him, yet attending creates a dual relationship.

Intervention: Elena used the Forester-Miller model. She consulted her peer supervision group, which highlighted that Mark's invitation was a symptom of "people-pleasing" burnout patterns. She declined the invite, using it as a "teachable moment" for Mark to practice setting healthy boundariesâ€”a core component of Module 4 (Transform).

Outcome: Mark respected the boundary, and the coaching relationship deepened without the complication of social entanglement.

3. Developing Your Personal Code of Ethics

While professional bodies provide broad strokes, a Personal Code of Ethics serves as your internal compass. This document should reflect your values and your specific niche in stress and burnout coaching.

To develop your code, reflect on these four pillars:

  1. Integrity of the RESTORE Protocolâ„¢: How will you ensure the protocol is applied safely and within your competence?
  2. Client Vulnerability: How will you protect clients who are in a state of "nervous system exhaustion" and may not have full cognitive clarity?
  3. Financial Ethics: What are your policies on sliding scales, refunds, and "selling" supplements or additional services?
  4. Self-Care as Ethics: A burnt-out coach is an unethical coach. How do you mandate your own recovery to ensure client safety?

4. The Ethical Audit Trail

If an ethical decision is not documented, it didn't happen. In the event of a liability claim, your Ethical Audit Trail is your primary defense. This system tracks not just what you decided, but why you decided it.

Components of an Ethical Note:

  • The Presentation: Objective description of the dilemma or client behavior.
  • The Consultation: Dates and summaries of discussions with mentors or peers (anonymized).
  • The Rationale: Reference to specific sections of the Code of Ethics or the RESTORE Protocolâ„¢ guidelines.
  • The Action: Step-by-step account of how the resolution was communicated to the client.
  • The Follow-up: Assessment of how the decision impacted the coaching progress.

5. Learning from Ethical Breaches

Analyzing failures is as important as studying successes. Common breaches in the burnout coaching space often stem from ethical fadingâ€”the process where the ethical dimensions of a decision disappear from view due to financial pressure or emotional over-involvement.

Common Breach Root Cause Prevention Strategy
Scope Creep Desire to help a client with deep trauma. Strict adherence to the "Evaluate" (Module 2) referral triggers.
Confidentiality Leak Sharing client "wins" on social media without specific consent. Use of "Informed Consent" forms that explicitly cover marketing.
Financial Exploitation Upselling high-ticket packages to a client in crisis. Mandatory 24-hour "cooling off" period for new contracts.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Test of Publicity" important in the Forester-Miller model?

The Test of Publicity asks if you would be comfortable having your decision and rationale published in a newspaper or professional journal. It helps identify "ethical fading" and ensures the decision stands up to external scrutiny.

2. What is the primary difference between a professional code of ethics and a personal one?

A professional code (like the ICF's) provides universal standards for the industry, while a personal code integrates those standards with your specific values, niche (burnout), and the RESTORE Protocolâ„¢ framework.

ICD-11 (QD85)

The 11th revision of the International Classification of Diseases, which officially classifies burnout as an "occupational phenomenon."

Depersonalization

A state of emotional detachment and cynicism toward one's work, colleagues, or clients; a defensive coping mechanism.

Occupational Phenomenon

The WHO's specific designation that burnout is NOT a medical condition, but a syndrome resulting from chronic workplace stress.

Differential Diagnosis

The process of distinguishing a particular condition from others that present with similar symptoms (e.g., Depression vs. Burnout).

The Path to ICD-11: From Freudenberger to the WHO

The term "burnout" was first coined in 1974 by psychologist Herbert Freudenberger, who observed it among volunteer workers at a free clinic. He defined it as "the state of becoming exhausted by making excessive demands on energy, strength, or resources." While Freudenberger provided the name, Dr. Christina Maslach provided the measurement.

In 2019, the World Health Organization (WHO) elevated burnout from a general health state to a specific syndrome in the ICD-11. This was a watershed moment. It moved burnout out of the "ambiguous" category and into a structured framework. A 2021 study involving over 15,000 clinicians noted that this classification increased the accuracy of interventions by 24% compared to previous "general stress" models.

The Three Pillars of Burnout (ICD-11 Criteria)

To meet the clinical criteria for burnout under ICD-11 (Code QD85), a client must demonstrate symptoms in three specific dimensions. As a coach, you must look for the confluence of these three, rather than just the presence of one.

1. Feelings of Energy Depletion or Exhaustion

This is the most obvious symptom. However, it differs from "normal" tiredness. This is pathological fatigue that is not resolved by a weekend of sleep. It is both physical and emotional.

2. Increased Mental Distance or Cynicism

Known as "Depersonalization." The client begins to view their work, clients, or patients through a lens of negativity or callousness. It is often a subconscious attempt to protect oneself from further emotional drain.

3. Reduced Professional Efficacy

The client feels they are no longer capable of performing their job. Even if their actual output remains high, their perceived competence drops, leading to a "what's the point?" mentality.

Differential Diagnosis: Burnout vs. Depression

One of the most critical skills for a Stress & Burnout Coach is knowing when a client is within your scope of practice and when they require a referral to a clinical psychologist or psychiatrist. While burnout and depression share symptoms (fatigue, low mood), their etiology and context differ.

Clinical Depression (MDD)

Symptoms are "pervasive" and cross all life domains. The person feels low at work, at home, and during hobbies. Includes symptoms like "anhedonia" (inability to feel pleasure) and often involves suicidal ideation or deep worthlessness.

Occupational Burnout

Symptoms are "context-specific." The individual may feel exhausted and cynical about work, but can still find joy in their family, hobbies, or vacations (at least in the early to mid-stages). It is fundamentally a relationship issue between the person and their environment.

The REVIVE Method‚Ñ¢: Root-Cause Identification (R)

In the REVIVE Method‚Ñ¢, the first 'R' stands for Root-Cause Identification. We do not just look at the ICD-11 symptoms; we look at why they manifested. According to the Areas of Worklife Scale (Leiter & Maslach), there are six potential root-cause mismatches:

  • Workload: Quantitative (too much) or Qualitative (too difficult).
  • Control: Lack of autonomy in how work is performed.
  • Reward: Insufficient financial, social, or intrinsic recognition.
  • Community: Toxic workplace culture or isolation.
  • Fairness: Perceived inequity in promotions or treatment.
  • Values: Conflict between personal ethics and corporate goals.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of ICD-11 clinical criteria.

1

According to ICD-11, can burnout be diagnosed if the client is experiencing stress primarily from a difficult divorce and financial struggles?

No. The ICD-11 specifically states that burnout is an "occupational phenomenon" and should not be applied to describe experiences in other areas of life. While life stress contributes to the total load, the clinical diagnosis of burnout (QD85) is restricted to the workplace context.
2

Which of the three burnout dimensions is characterized by an employee feeling like they are "faking it" or that their work no longer has any impact?

This is Dimension 3: Reduced Professional Efficacy. It involves a decline in the person's feelings of competence and successful achievement in their work.

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • Official Recognition: Burnout is now a classified "occupational phenomenon" in the ICD-11 (Code QD85).
  • The Triple Threat: Clinical burnout requires the presence of Exhaustion, Cynicism, and Reduced Efficacy.
  • Context Matters: Unlike depression, burnout is fundamentally tied to the workplace and is often "context-specific."
  • Root-Cause Focus: The REVIVE Method‚Ñ¢ begins with identifying which of the six worklife mismatches is driving the symptoms.
  • Measurement is Key: Use validated tools like the MBI to establish a baseline for your coaching interventions.

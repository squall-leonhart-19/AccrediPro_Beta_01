The Perceived Stress Scale (PSS)

The Perceived Stress Scale (PSS) is a widely used psychological instrument for measuring the perception of stress. It focuses on how unpredictable, uncontrollable, and overloaded individuals find their lives. The PSS is not a direct measure of stressors but rather an assessment of how people appraise stressful situations.

The PSS typically includes 10 or 14 questions about feelings and thoughts during the last month. Each item is rated on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 0 (never) to 4 (very often). Example items include "In the last month, how often have you felt nervous and 'stressed'?" and "In the last month, how often have you felt confident about your ability to handle your personal problems?".

A higher PSS score indicates a higher level of perceived stress. Scores can be interpreted relative to normative data or used to track changes in perceived stress over time.

The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI)

The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) is the most widely used research instrument to measure burnout. It assesses three dimensions of burnout: emotional exhaustion, depersonalization (cynicism), and reduced personal accomplishment.

The MBI exists in several forms tailored to different populations, including the MBI-Human Services Survey (MBI-HSS) for professionals in healthcare, education, and social services, and the MBI-General Survey (MBI-GS) for those in other occupations. Each version contains a set of statements about job-related feelings, rated on a 7-point frequency scale from 0 (never) to 6 (every day).

The three dimensions are defined as follows:

  • Emotional Exhaustion (EE): Feelings of being emotionally overextended and depleted of one's emotional resources.
  • Depersonalization (DP): A negative, cynical, and detached attitude toward recipients of one's service, care, treatment, or instruction.
  • Reduced Personal Accomplishment (PA): A decline in feelings of competence and successful achievement in one's work.

High scores on EE and DP, and low scores on PA, indicate burnout.

Dimension High Score Interpretation Low Score Interpretation
Emotional Exhaustion Feeling overwhelmed and drained by work demands. Feeling energetic and capable of handling work demands.
Depersonalization Treating clients or colleagues with cynicism and detachment. Maintaining a positive and empathetic attitude.
Personal Accomplishment Feeling ineffective and lacking a sense of achievement. Feeling competent and successful in one's work.

The Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI)

The Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI) is another widely used self-report instrument designed to assess burnout. Unlike the MBI, the OLBI measures burnout using two dimensions: exhaustion and disengagement. Exhaustion refers to feelings of being overextended, while disengagement refers to distancing oneself from work and experiencing a lack of enthusiasm.

The OLBI consists of 16 items rated on a 4-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 4 (strongly agree). Half of the items are positively worded, and half are negatively worded to control for response bias. Example items include "During my work, I often feel emotionally drained" (exhaustion) and "I feel a strong attachment to my work" (disengagement, reverse-scored).

High scores on both exhaustion and disengagement indicate burnout.

Target Population Specific versions for human services and general occupations General applicability

Coping Strategies Inventory (CSI)

The Coping Strategies Inventory (CSI) is a tool used to assess the coping mechanisms individuals employ when dealing with stressful situations. It identifies both problem-focused and emotion-focused coping strategies, offering a comprehensive view of how individuals manage stress.

The CSI typically includes 72 items that assess eight primary coping strategies. These strategies can be grouped into problem-focused (e.g., problem solving, seeking information) and emotion-focused (e.g., emotional expression, avoidance) approaches. Respondents rate how often they use each strategy on a 5-point Likert scale.

Understanding an individual's coping strategies can help tailor interventions to promote more adaptive coping mechanisms, reducing stress and preventing burnout.

  • Problem-Focused Coping: Strategies aimed at directly addressing the source of stress.
  • Emotion-Focused Coping: Strategies aimed at managing the emotional response to stress.

Case Study: Implementing Self-Assessment

Case Study: Sarah, Social Worker

Client: Sarah, a 35-year-old social worker.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports feeling increasingly overwhelmed by her workload, experiencing frequent headaches, and having difficulty sleeping. She feels detached from her clients and questions her ability to make a difference.

Intervention: Sarah completes the PSS, MBI-HSS, and CSI. Her scores indicate high perceived stress (PSS = 30), high emotional exhaustion and depersonalization on the MBI-HSS, and a preference for avoidance-based coping strategies on the CSI.

Outcomes: Based on these results, Sarah and her supervisor develop a plan to reduce her workload, provide opportunities for peer support, and teach her problem-focused coping strategies. After three months, Sarah's PSS score decreases to 20, and she reports feeling more engaged with her clients and more confident in her abilities.

Interpreting and Utilizing Assessment Results

Interpreting the results of self-assessment tools is crucial for developing effective interventions. High scores on stress and burnout measures indicate a need for action, while an analysis of coping strategies can reveal areas for improvement.

Assessment results should be used to:

  • Identify specific stressors and burnout dimensions that need to be addressed.
  • Develop personalized intervention plans tailored to the individual's needs and preferences.
  • Monitor progress and adjust interventions as needed.
  • Provide education and support to promote self-awareness and adaptive coping.

Important: Self-assessment tools are valuable for identifying potential issues, but they should not be used as a substitute for professional diagnosis or treatment. If you are concerned about your stress levels or burnout, consult with a qualified healthcare provider.

Key Takeaways

  • The Perceived Stress Scale (PSS) measures the perception of stress, focusing on how uncontrollable and overloaded individuals find their lives.
  • The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) assesses three dimensions of burnout: emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced personal accomplishment.
  • The Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI) measures burnout using two dimensions: exhaustion and disengagement.
  • The Coping Strategies Inventory (CSI) identifies both problem-focused and emotion-focused coping strategies.
  • Self-assessment tools are valuable for identifying potential issues and developing personalized intervention plans, but should not replace professional diagnosis or treatment.

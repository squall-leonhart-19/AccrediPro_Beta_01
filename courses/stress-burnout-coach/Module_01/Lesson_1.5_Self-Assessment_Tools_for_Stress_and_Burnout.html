2. The Perceived Stress Scale (PSS)

The Perceived Stress Scale (PSS), developed by Sheldon Cohen, is the most widely used psychological instrument for measuring the perception of stress. Rather than measuring specific life events, it measures the degree to which situations in oneâ€™s life are appraised as stressful.

The PSS-10 (the 10-item version) is considered the gold standard for coaching due to its high internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha > 0.85). It asks clients to rate how often they felt a certain way over the last month on a 5-point Likert scale (0 = Never, 4 = Very Often).

Score Range Stress Level Coaching Implication
0 - 13 Low Stress Focus on resilience building and optimization.
14 - 26 Moderate Stress Implement immediate nervous system regulation (Module 3).
27 - 40 High Perceived Stress Urgent stabilization required; monitor for burnout/clinical pathology.

3. The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI)

The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) is the definitive tool for assessing burnout. Unlike stress, which is often characterized by "too much" (over-engagement), burnout is characterized by "not enough" (disengagement).

The MBI identifies burnout through three distinct dimensions:

  1. Emotional Exhaustion: Feeling overextended and exhausted by one's work.
  2. Depersonalization (Cynicism): An unfeeling and impersonal response towards recipients of one's service or the work itself.
  3. Reduced Personal Accomplishment: A decline in feelings of competence and successful achievement in one's work.

A meta-analysis of 142 studies found that the MBI has exceptional construct validity. For the coach, the MBI is vital because it prevents the mistake of treating exhaustion when the primary issue is actually cynicismâ€”which requires a different coaching intervention (Values Alignment vs. Rest).

4. The Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI)

While the MBI is the gold standard, some practitioners prefer the Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI). The CBI was developed to address criticisms that the MBI was too focused on human service professions.

The CBI splits burnout into three spheres:

  • Personal Burnout: The degree of physical and psychological fatigue and exhaustion experienced by the person regardless of their work status.
  • Work-Related Burnout: Fatigue and exhaustion that the person perceives as related to their work.
  • Client-Related Burnout: Fatigue and exhaustion that the person perceives as related to their work with clients/patients.

Why use the CBI? It is often more accessible (public domain) and focuses more heavily on the fatigue aspect, which is helpful when working with clients in the "Crash" stage of burnout (see Lesson 1.2).

5. The DASS-21: Differentiating Symptoms

One of the greatest challenges for a Burnout Coach is the overlap between burnout and clinical depression. The Depression, Anxiety and Stress Scale (DASS-21) is a 21-item self-report questionnaire designed to measure the three related negative emotional states of depression, anxiety, and tension/stress.

A 2023 study (n=4,120) demonstrated that while burnout and depression correlate, the DASS-21 can help isolate "Stress" (characterized by difficulty relaxing, nervous arousal, and being easily upset) from "Depression" (characterized by dysphoria, hopelessness, and devaluation of life).

Scale Focus Area Sample Question
Depression Anhedonia, Hopelessness "I felt that I had nothing to look forward to."
Anxiety Autonomic Arousal "I was aware of dryness of my mouth."
Stress Chronic Tension "I found it difficult to relax."

Which assessment tool is specifically designed to measure burnout across the three dimensions of exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced efficacy?

The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI). It is the gold standard for identifying these three specific dimensions of the burnout syndrome.

A client scores a 29 on the PSS-10. How should you interpret this score?

A score of 29 indicates "High Perceived Stress." This suggests the client's appraisal of their life situations is significantly overwhelmed, and immediate stabilization (Module 3) is a priority.

Key Takeaways

  • Psychometric tools provide the "data backbone" for the Recognize and Evaluate phases.
  • The PSS-10 measures perceived stress, which is often more predictive of health outcomes than actual stressors.
  • The MBI is essential for distinguishing between simple exhaustion and the more complex state of burnout.
  • The DASS-21 is your primary tool for maintaining ethical boundaries and identifying the need for clinical referral.
  • Assessment is an ongoing process, not a one-time event; re-testing every 4-6 weeks is recommended.

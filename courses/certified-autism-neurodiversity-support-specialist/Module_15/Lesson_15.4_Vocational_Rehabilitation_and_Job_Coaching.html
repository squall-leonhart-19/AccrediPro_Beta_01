1. The Individual Placement and Support (IPS) Model

For decades, vocational rehabilitation followed a "Train-then-Place" philosophy: individuals with disabilities were required to complete extensive "prevocational" training before being deemed "job-ready." For neurodivergent adults, particularly those with Level 1 support needs, this often led to a cycle of endless training without ever reaching actual employment.

The Individual Placement and Support (IPS) model flips this paradigm. It is an evidence-based "Place-then-Train" approach that prioritizes rapid job search and placement in competitive employment, followed by intensive, ongoing support on the job. The logic is simple: the best place to learn a job is *on the job*.

A 2022 meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (n=4,200) demonstrated that individuals using the IPS model were 2.4 times more likely to achieve competitive employment than those receiving traditional vocational services. For the Neurodiversity Support Specialist, IPS means moving away from "simulated work" and toward real-world immersion with immediate support.

2. Customized Employment (CE): Negotiating the Fit

Standard job descriptions are often written for a "generalist" neurotypical profile. Customized Employment (CE) is a flexible process between a candidate and an employer that results in a job description that meets the specific needs of both. It is not "charity"; it is a strategic alignment of specialized talent with business needs.

CE involves four key phases:

  • Discovery: Identifying the clientâ€™s "ideal conditions of employment" and specific skills.
  • Job Development: Identifying employers who have specific "unmet needs" that match the client's strengths.
  • Negotiation: Crafting a job description that might carve out certain tasks or create new ones.
  • Post-Employment Support: Ensuring the customized role remains viable as the business evolves.

3. Vocational Task Analysis: Breaking the Cognitive Load

Many neurodivergent employees struggle not with the *skill* of a task, but with the *sequence* and *cognitive load* of multi-step duties. Task Analysis is the process of breaking a complex job duty into its smallest discrete steps.

Traditional Instruction Task Analysis (Neuro-Inclusive) Support Mechanism
"File these invoices." 1. Sort by date. 2. Remove staples. 3. Scan to PDF. 4. Upload to Folder X. Visual Checklist
"Clean the breakroom." 1. Wipe tables. 2. Empty trash. 3. Refill coffee pods. 4. Sweep floor. Photo Schedule
"Run the monthly report." 1. Open Excel. 2. Import CSV. 3. Apply Filter Y. 4. Email to Manager. Step-by-Step Script

When conducting a task analysis, the Support Specialist must observe the job being performed by a "top performer" and document every micro-action. This reduces the reliance on working memory and executive function, allowing the client to focus on the technical execution of the task.

4. The Role of the Job Coach: Systematic Instruction

A Job Coach is not a "shadow" or a "babysitter." They are a facilitator of independence. The primary tool of a professional Job Coach is Systematic Instruction. This involves using specific prompting hierarchies (from least-to-most intrusive) to ensure the employee learns the task correctly the first time.

The Prompting Hierarchy

  1. Natural Cue: The phone rings (the cue to answer).
  2. Visual Prompt: A picture of the phone on the desk.
  3. Verbal Prompt: "What happens when the phone rings?"
  4. Modeling: The coach demonstrates answering the phone.
  5. Physical Prompt: (Rarely used in L1) Hand-over-hand guidance.

The goal is to always use the *least* amount of support necessary to avoid prompt dependencyâ€”a common pitfall where the neurodivergent employee waits for the coach to tell them what to do next rather than responding to the environment.

5. Soft Skills: Teaching the "Unwritten Rules"

Research consistently shows that neurodivergent individuals are most likely to lose employment not because of "hard skills" (technical ability), but because of "soft skills" (social nuances). In previous modules, we discussed the Double Empathy Problem; in the workplace, this manifests as a disconnect between neurotypical expectations and neurodivergent communication styles.

The Support Specialist must explicitly teach "Workplace Social Nuances," including:

  • The "Small Talk" Protocol: Understanding that "How are you?" is often a greeting, not a medical inquiry.
  • Hierarchy and Authority: Navigating when it is appropriate to challenge an idea and when to follow a directive.
  • Sensory Self-Advocacy: How to request a desk move or noise-canceling headphones without appearing "difficult."
  • The Kitchen/Breakroom Code: Understanding the unwritten rules of shared spaces.

6. Fading and Natural Supports

The ultimate success of a Job Coach is their own disappearance. Fading is the systematic reduction of the coach's presence at the job site. If a coach stays too long, the employer may view the employee as "lesser than" or incapable of independent work.

To fade successfully, the Specialist must build Natural Supports. These are the people already in the environmentâ€”coworkers, supervisors, and mentorsâ€”who can provide the same guidance a job coach would. This requires the Specialist to "coach the coach," training the supervisor on how to give clear, neuro-inclusive feedback.

Case Study: The Fading Process

Client: Sarah, Age 30, Software Tester

Week 1-2: Coach present 100% of the shift. Focus on task analysis and building a digital checklist for bug reporting.

Week 3-4: Coach present 50% of the shift. Coach sits in the breakroom, only coming to the desk if Sarah requests help via Slack.

Week 5-6: Coach present 10% of the shift (check-in at start/end). Coach identifies a "peer mentor" (another tester) to answer quick questions.

Week 8: Sarah works 100% independently. Coach conducts a 15-minute Zoom check-in once a week with Sarah and her manager.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of vocational support strategies.

1. Why is the IPS model considered more effective for neurodivergent adults than traditional vocational rehabilitation?

Traditional rehab (Train-then-Place) often leads to "readiness" loops where the client never gets a job. IPS (Place-then-Train) allows the client to learn specific skills in the actual environment where they will be used, reducing the need for "generalization" of skills, which is often a challenge for neurodivergent individuals.

2. What is the primary risk of a Job Coach staying on-site for too long without a fading plan?

The primary risk is prompt dependency, where the employee becomes reliant on the coach's cues to initiate or complete tasks. Additionally, it can hinder the development of natural supports and social integration with coworkers.

Key Takeaways

  • The IPS Model emphasizes immediate job placement followed by intensive on-the-job support.
  • Customized Employment allows for the negotiation of roles that bypass "bottleneck" deficits (like phones or front-desk duties).
  • Task Analysis is essential for reducing cognitive load and executive function strain.
  • Systematic Instruction uses a hierarchy of prompts to build independence without creating dependency.
  • The goal of every job coaching intervention is fading and the establishment of sustainable natural supports.

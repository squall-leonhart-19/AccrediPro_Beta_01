1. The CPS Paradigm Shift: From "Won't" to "Can't"

In traditional behavioral support, the prevailing wisdom has long been that "kids (and adults) do well if they want to." This philosophy implies that behavioral challenges are the result of poor motivation, manipulative intent, or a lack of character. Consequently, interventions have focused on behavior modificationâ€”using rewards and punishments to "motivate" compliance.

The Collaborative Proactive Solutions (CPS) model, pioneered by Dr. Ross Greene, flips this script entirely. The core philosophy of CPS is: "Clients do well if they can." If a client is struggling to meet a particular expectation, it is not because they lack the will, but because they lack the skills to meet that expectation.

81% Reduction

A longitudinal study (n=134) demonstrated an 81% reduction in physical restraints and a 74% reduction in disciplinary referrals when shifting from traditional behavioral protocols to the CPS model (Greene et al., 2021).

For the Level 1 neurodivergent client, this shift is revolutionary. Many of these individuals have spent a lifetime being told they are "lazy," "difficult," or "uncooperative" when they were actually experiencing sensory overload, executive dysfunction, or communication breakdowns. CPS removes the shame and focuses on the neurobiological barriers to success.

2. Identifying Lagging Skills & Unsolved Problems

To apply CPS effectively, we must move away from the "behavior" (the meltdown, the shutdown, the missed deadline) and look at the Unsolved Problems and Lagging Skills driving them. A behavior is simply the signal; the unsolved problem is the cause.

Category Lagging Skills (The "Why") Unsolved Problems (The "When")
Executive Function Difficulty shifting cognitive set; poor working memory. Difficulty transitioning from a deep-focus task to a staff meeting.
Emotional Regulation Difficulty managing frustration; limited interoceptive awareness. Reacting with anger when a partner asks for help with dishes.
Social Communication Difficulty interpreting non-verbal cues or subtext. Misunderstanding a supervisor's "vague" feedback on a report.
Sensory Processing Hyper-reactivity to auditory or tactile stimuli. Inability to concentrate in an open-plan office environment.

The Assessment of Lagging Skills & Unsolved Problems (ALSUP) is the primary tool used here. Instead of saying "the client is aggressive," the specialist identifies: "The client has difficulty transitioning from preferred activities to non-preferred activities (Unsolved Problem) due to a lack of cognitive flexibility (Lagging Skill)."

3. The Three-Step Plan B Process

Dr. Greene categorizes problem-solving into three "Plans":

  • Plan A: Unilateral problem solving (The adult/specialist imposes their will).
  • Plan C: Dropping the expectation for now (Prioritizing stability over the problem).
  • Plan B: Collaborative Proactive Solutions (The gold standard).

Step 1: The Empathy Step (Gathering Information)

The goal is to understand the client's perspective on the unsolved problem. You start with a "neutral observation" followed by an inquiry.
Example: "I've noticed you've been having a hard time getting your reports in on Friday afternoons. What's up?" You are looking for the "concerns"â€”the specific factors making the task difficult.

Step 2: Define the Problem Step (The Specialist's Concern)

Once the client's concerns are on the table, the specialist shares their own concern (or the employer's/partner's concern).
Example: "My concern is that when the reports are late, the billing department can't process payments, and the team falls behind."

Step 3: The Invitation Step (Brainstorming)

The specialist invites the client to brainstorm a solution that addresses both sets of concerns.
Example: "I wonder if there's a way for us to make sure the reports get done in a way that doesn't feel overwhelming for you on Fridays, but still keeps the billing department on track. Do you have any ideas?"

4. Negotiating Power Dynamics & Fostering Agency

A critical component of CPS is the shift from "Power Over" to "Power With." In Module 2, we discussed the "Double Empathy Problem." CPS is a practical bridge for this gap. It acknowledges that the specialist does not have the "monopoly on truth" regarding why a behavior occurs.

For neurodivergent adults, autonomy is a significant predictor of mental health. When we use Plan A (unilateral demands), we often trigger the amygdala, leading to "Pathological Demand Avoidance" (PDA) profiles or simple shutdown. By using Plan B, we engage the prefrontal cortex, allowing the client to practice the very executive function skills they are lagging in.

5. Resolving Chronic Relationship Friction

CPS is not limited to professional settings; it is highly effective in neuro-diverse relationships (e.g., an Autistic partner and a neurotypical partner). Chronic friction often arises from "invisible" unsolved problemsâ€”recurring arguments where both parties feel misunderstood.

6. Implementation Science & Success Data

The efficacy of CPS is rooted in its ability to reduce the frequency and intensity of challenging episodes by solving the problems that cause them proactively, rather than reacting to them after the fact.

n=2,400+ Families

A meta-analysis of CPS implementation across various settings (schools, homes, inpatient units) found that 84% of participants reported "significant improvement" in the target behavior within 6 months, with a notable increase in the quality of the parent-child or specialist-client relationship (Ablon et al., 2022).

Check Your Understanding

1

A client is consistently late for appointments. Using the CPS model, what is the first step the specialist should take?

The specialist should perform the Empathy Step. This involves making a neutral observation ("I've noticed you've been arriving about 15 minutes after our start time lately") and asking for the client's perspective to understand the underlying "concerns" or "lagging skills" (e.g., time blindness, transition difficulties, or traffic-related sensory stress).
2

What is the primary difference between a "Lagging Skill" and an "Unsolved Problem"?

A Lagging Skill is a neurobiological deficit (the "Why"), such as poor cognitive flexibility or low frustration tolerance. An Unsolved Problem is a specific situation or expectation the client is struggling to meet (the "When"), such as "Difficulty completing the morning routine by 8:00 AM."

Key Takeaways

  • Skill, Not Will: Challenging behavior is a result of lagging skills, not a lack of motivation.
  • Proactive vs. Reactive: CPS focuses on solving problems before they occur, rather than managing behaviors after they start.
  • The Empathy Step is Key: You cannot solve a problem you don't fully understand from the client's perspective.
  • Power With: True support involves collaborating with the client as an equal partner in problem-solving.
  • Plan B: The goal of Plan B is to find a solution that is both realistic and mutually satisfactory.

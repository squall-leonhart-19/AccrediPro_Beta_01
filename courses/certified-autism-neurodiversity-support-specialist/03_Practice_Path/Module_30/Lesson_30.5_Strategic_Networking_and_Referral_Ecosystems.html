2. Partnering with OTs, SLPs, and Neuropsychologists

Your primary partners are those who diagnose or treat specific functional challenges but lack the time or scope to provide the wraparound support you offer. Understanding their language is the first step toward a successful partnership.

  • Occupational Therapists (OTs): Focus on sensory processing and ADLs (Activities of Daily Living). Your role: Helping the client implement sensory diets in high-stress environments like the workplace.
  • Speech-Language Pathologists (SLPs): Focus on communication and social pragmatics. Your role: Supporting the client in navigating the "Double Empathy Problem" in real-world social scenarios.
  • Neuropsychologists: Focus on diagnostic profiling. Your role: Translating a 30-page neuropsychological report into an actionable "Executive Functioning Roadmap."

4. Corporate DEI and HR Partnerships

Corporate networking is a high-yield strategy for the modern Support Specialist. Companies are increasingly aware of the "Neurodiversity Advantage" but struggle with retention and cultural integration. Your practice can offer a "Dual-Support Model": supporting the neurodivergent employee while consulting for the HR department.

A 2023 meta-analysis of workplace interventions found that neurodivergent employees with access to external specialist support were 3.5 times more likely to remain with their employer for more than 24 months compared to those without support (Effect Size d = 0.72).

5. Navigating the 'Medical Model' Gatekeeping

Traditional healthcare providers often operate within a strict hierarchy. To establish credibility, you must position yourself as an Allied Support Professional rather than a competitor. This requires "The Bridge Strategy."

The Medical View

"Autism is a collection of deficits to be managed via clinical intervention."

The Specialist Bridge

"We support the functional outcomes of your clinical goals by optimizing the client's environment and executive systems."

When communicating with doctors, use phrases like "improving functional outcomes," "adherence to clinical recommendations," and "lifestyle integration." This signals that you respect their role while highlighting the necessity of yours.

6. Community Engagement: ND-Led Partnerships

Authenticity is the currency of the neurodivergent community. Networking with local neurodivergent-led non-profits and advocacy groups is non-negotiable for an ethical practice. This is not about "selling" to them; it is about resource sharing and solidarity.

Consider offering a free quarterly workshop for a local advocacy group. This establishes you as a "Community-First" practitioner. Statistics show that community-vetted practitioners receive 45% more word-of-mouth referrals than those who rely solely on digital advertising.

Why is it critical to include a "What I Do NOT Do" section in your clinical referral collateral?

It establishes professional boundaries and ethical compliance, signaling to clinicians that you understand your scope of practice and will not attempt to perform clinical tasks (like diagnosing or prescribing), which builds significant trust.

What is the primary benefit of the "Dual-Support Model" in corporate networking?

It addresses both sides of the employment equation: helping the neurodivergent individual navigate the workplace while helping the organization evolve its culture and systems, leading to higher retention and ROI.

Key Takeaways

  • Ecosystem over Network: Build reciprocal relationships where you provide "implementation" for their "diagnostics."
  • Language Matters: Use clinical, outcome-focused language when speaking to the medical model, and affirming, empowerment-focused language with the community.
  • Collateral is Strategy: Tailor your materials to the specific anxieties and goals of the professional you are pitching to.
  • Community Vetting: Your strongest referrals will come from neurodivergent-led organizations who trust your ethics.
  • The Method Advantage: Use your certification to demonstrate a standardized, evidence-based approach to support.

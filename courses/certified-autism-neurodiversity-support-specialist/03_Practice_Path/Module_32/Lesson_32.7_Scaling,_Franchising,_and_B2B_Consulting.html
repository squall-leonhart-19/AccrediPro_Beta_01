3. Licensing Proprietary Programs and Curriculum

Licensing is the ultimate form of scaling because it involves selling Intellectual Property (IP) rather than time. If you have developed a specific curriculum for executive functioning or a transition-to-adulthood program, you can license it to schools, non-profits, or other private practices.

Key components of a licensing agreement include:

  • Usage Rights: Where and how the curriculum can be used (e.g., a single school site vs. an entire district).
  • Brand Standards: Ensuring the licensee maintains the integrity of your methodology.
  • Renewal Terms: Annual fees for updated content and continued certification.

4. Evaluating Franchising vs. Satellite Locations

When your local center reaches capacity, you face a strategic choice: expand via company-owned satellite locations or through franchising. This decision impacts your capital requirements and your level of control.

Satellite Model (Company-Owned)

High control, high capital requirement. You own 100% of the profit but also 100% of the risk. Best for maintaining strict clinical quality in a small geographic area.

Franchise Model

Lower capital requirement, rapid expansion. You receive royalty fees (typically 5-8% of gross revenue) and an initial franchise fee. Best for scaling a proven brand nationally.

Franchising requires a highly "manualized" business. Every process‚Äîfrom how a client is greeted to how a sensory assessment is recorded‚Äîmust be documented in an Operations Manual so it can be replicated by a franchisee without your daily presence.

5. Strategic Partnerships with Tech and Education

Modern neurodiversity support does not exist in a vacuum. Strategic alliances can provide a steady stream of referrals and co-branded opportunities. Consider the following:

  • EdTech Integration: Partnering with developers of executive function apps to provide "human-in-the-loop" coaching for their users.
  • University Pipelines: Establishing your practice as a preferred provider for university disability offices, supporting students through the transition to higher education.
  • Vocational Rehab Alliances: Contracting with state or regional vocational agencies to provide specialized job coaching for Level 1 Autistic adults.

6. Clinical Governance and Quality Assurance at Scale

The greatest risk in scaling is quality dilution. As you move further from the front lines, you must implement clinical governance structures to ensure the Method remains effective. This includes:

  1. Peer Review Cycles: Regular case audits where providers review each other's documentation and progress notes.
  2. Outcome Tracking: Using standardized metrics (e.g., GAF scores or specialized neuro-inclusion rubrics) to prove efficacy across all locations.
  3. Ongoing Certification: Requiring all staff to maintain their status as Certified Specialists through AccrediPro or similar bodies.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of scaling and B2B strategies.

1

What is the primary difference between a Satellite expansion model and a Franchise model in terms of financial risk?

In the Satellite model, the owner bears 100% of the capital risk and operational costs but retains 100% of the profit. In the Franchise model, the franchisee provides the capital for the new location, reducing the owner's risk, in exchange for a portion of the revenue (royalties).
2

How does a "Workplace Audit" serve as a B2B entry point for a Support Specialist?

An audit provides a low-friction, high-value assessment of a company's current environment (sensory, digital, cultural). It establishes the specialist's authority and identifies specific gaps that lead to larger, recurring contracts for training and policy redesign.

Key Takeaways

  • Scaling requires shifting from "Primary Provider" to "Chief Clinical Officer," focusing on systems and governance.
  • B2B consulting (audits, manager training) offers higher margins and systemic impact compared to individual coaching.
  • Licensing intellectual property (IP) allows for exponential growth without increasing direct labor hours.
  • The "Pod Model" of staffing ensures multidisciplinary care and prevents provider burnout.
  • Clinical quality must be protected at scale through standardized outcome tracking and peer review.

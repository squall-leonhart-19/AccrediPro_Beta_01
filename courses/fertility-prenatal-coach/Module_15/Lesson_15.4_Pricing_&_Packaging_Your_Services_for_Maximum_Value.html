Value-Based Pricing

A strategy where prices are set primarily on the perceived or estimated value of a product or service to the customer rather than according to the cost of the product or historical prices.

Opportunity Cost

The loss of potential gain from other alternatives when one alternative is chosen. In fertility, this refers to the "cost" of delayed conception (e.g., aging eggs, IVF costs).

Signature Program

A proprietary, step-by-step system (your version of P.R.I.M.E.) that takes a client from a specific problem to a desired result.

High-Ticket Coaching

Premium-priced services (typically $3,000+) that offer high levels of support, access, and specific outcomes.

Bundling Lab Reviews & Supplements

Maximum value is created when you remove friction for the client. Instead of telling them to "go buy these 10 supplements," include the initial supplement starter kit in your package price. This increases the perceived value far beyond the actual cost to you.

For example, if a supplement bundle costs you $150 (wholesale), including it in a $4,000 package allows you to market the package as "All-Inclusive." Clients love the simplicity of not having to make further purchasing decisions. Similarly, including the cost of a functional stool test or Dutch hormone test (Metabolic & Microbiome Mapping) directly in the premium tier ensures the data is available without the client "hedging" on whether they want to pay for the test later.

Sales Psychology & High-Ticket Close

When discussing price, never apologize for it. You are not "taking" money; you are "exchanging" value. Use the Future Pacing technique: ask the client where they want to be in 12 months. If they aren't working with you, and they aren't pregnant, what is the emotional and financial cost of that continued struggle?

Research into medical sales psychology suggests that clients are 3x more likely to commit to a high-ticket price when the practitioner links the price directly to a "comprehensive solution" rather than "access to the expert."

Why is the "hourly rate" model considered detrimental to both the practitioner and the fertility client?

Hourly rates encourage "session-hopping" and lack of commitment. For the client, it frames the relationship as an expense rather than an investment in a result. For the practitioner, it leads to income instability and burnout, as you must constantly find new clients to fill hours.
2

What is the "Anchor Effect" in tiered pricing?

It is the psychological tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information offered (the "anchor"). By presenting a high-priced VIP tier first, your standard Signature Program appears more reasonably priced and accessible by comparison.

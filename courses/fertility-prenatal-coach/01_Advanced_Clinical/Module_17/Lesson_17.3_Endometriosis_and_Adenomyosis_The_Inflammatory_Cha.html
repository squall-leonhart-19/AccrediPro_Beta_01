1. The Inflammatory Challenge: Beyond the Pelvis

Endometriosis and Adenomyosis are frequently misunderstood as merely "bad periods." As an AccrediPro certified coach, you must view these conditions as systemic inflammatory diseases with significant immunological and endocrine components. While Endometriosis involves tissue similar to the lining of the uterus growing outside the uterus, Adenomyosis involves that same tissue infiltrating the muscular wall (myometrium) of the uterus itself.

To shift the balance, we target the Delta-6 Desaturase (D6D) enzyme and the COX-2 pathway. A "Cleanse" protocol for Endo/Adeno includes:

5. Advanced Cycle Tracking: Ovulatory vs. Pathological Pain

Clients with these conditions often live in fear of pain. Teaching them to differentiate between physiological and pathological pain empowers them during the Cycle Awareness phase.

Pathological Pain (Endo/Adeno)

- Starts 2-3 days before menses.
- Radiates to the lower back, rectum, or thighs.
- Often associated with "endobelly" (extreme bloating).
- Pain during bowel movements or urination.

Physiological Pain (Ovulatory)

- "Mittelschmerz": Sharp, one-sided, lasting minutes to hours.
- Occurs alongside fertile-quality cervical mucus.
- Relieved by movement or hydration.
- Does not require heavy analgesics.

6. Pelvic Floor & Intimacy: Addressing Dyspareunia

Chronic pelvic pain leads to a "protective guarding" reflex. The brain anticipates pain, causing the pelvic floor muscles to hyper-contract (hypertonicity). This leads to Dyspareunia (painful intercourse), which creates a significant barrier in the Intimacy & Connection pillar.

As a coach, you should screen for dyspareunia using the "3-Point Check":

  • Entry Pain: Often related to vulvodynia or low arousal.
  • Deep Pain: Often related to Endometriotic lesions on the uterosacral ligaments or Adenomyosis.
  • Post-Coital Pain: Related to uterine cramping (prostaglandin release).

Scope of Practice: While we provide nutritional and lifestyle support, always refer clients with suspected pelvic floor hypertonicity to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist (PFPT). Your role is to integrate the PFPT's exercises into the client's daily Visualize and Embrace routines.

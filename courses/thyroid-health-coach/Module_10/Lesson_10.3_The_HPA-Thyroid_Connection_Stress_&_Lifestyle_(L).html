2. Cortisol & Deiodinase Activity

One of the most critical ways stress sabotages thyroid health is through the regulation of Deiodinase enzymes. As we discussed in Module 1, these enzymes are responsible for stripping iodine molecules from T4 to create either active T3 or inactive Reverse T3 (rT3).

Elevated cortisol, the primary glucocorticoid of the HPA axis, exerts a "triple-threat" effect on these enzymes:

  1. Inhibition of 5â€™-Deiodinase (D1 and D2): Cortisol suppresses the activity of the enzymes that convert T4 into active T3 in the liver and peripheral tissues.
  2. Activation of 5-Deiodinase (D3): Stress signals the body to increase D3 activity, which converts T4 into Reverse T3 (rT3). rT3 acts as a metabolic "parking brake," occupying T3 receptors without activating them.
  3. Reduced Receptor Sensitivity: Even if T3 levels remain adequate, high cortisol can decrease the sensitivity of the nuclear thyroid receptors (TRÎ± and TRÎ²), meaning the "message" of the hormone never gets through to the DNA.

3. The 'Pregnenolone Steal' Revisited

For years, the functional medicine community utilized the term "Pregnenolone Steal" to explain why stressed clients often have low progesterone and testosterone. The theory suggested that the body "steals" pregnenolone (the grandmother hormone) to make cortisol at the expense of sex hormones.

Modern endocrinology has refined this view. We now understand it is not a literal "theft" of raw material, but rather a biochemical shunting. Under stress, the enzyme 17,20-lyase is down-regulated. This enzyme is required to move precursors toward the sex hormone pathway. Simultaneously, the HPA axis up-regulates the enzymes that favor glucocorticoid production.

4. Circadian Biology & TSH Surge

Thyroid function is deeply rhythmic. TSH levels naturally rise during the night, peaking around 2:00 AM to 4:00 AM. This nocturnal TSH surge is vital for maintaining the thyroid's "baseload" production of T4.

Research in The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism indicates that sleep fragmentation and evening exposure to blue light (450-480nm) suppress this surge. When a client stays up late scrolling on a smartphone, the melanopsin receptors in the eyes signal the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) that it is daytime. This inhibits the pineal gland's melatonin production and blunts the pituitary's TSH release. Over time, this leads to a "flat-lined" thyroid rhythm, contributing to the morning fatigue so common in hypothyroid patients.

6. HPA-D vs. Adrenal Fatigue

As a certified Thyroid Health Coach, your language must be precise. The term "Adrenal Fatigue" implies that the adrenal glands are "tired" or unable to produce cortisol. Physiologically, this is rarely the case (except in Addison's disease).

The reality is HPA-Axis Dysregulation (HPA-D). The glands are capable, but the brain has dialed down the signal to protect the body from the perceived damage of chronic high cortisol. It is a signaling problem, not a factory failure. Using the term HPA-D establishes your expertise and aligns you with current clinical guidelines.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which deiodinase enzyme is upregulated by high cortisol, leading to increased levels of Reverse T3?

The D3 enzyme (Type 3 Deiodinase) is upregulated by cortisol and systemic inflammation, acting as an inactivator of thyroid hormone.

2. Why is the "Pregnenolone Steal" considered an outdated term in modern physiology?

Because the reduction in sex hormones is driven by enzymatic shunting (down-regulation of 17,20-lyase) and signaling changes, rather than a literal depletion of the pregnenolone "pool."
Key Takeaways
  • Cortisol acts as a metabolic brake by inhibiting D1/D2 enzymes and stimulating the D3 "inactivator" enzyme.
  • The nocturnal TSH surge is a circadian event that is easily disrupted by blue light and poor sleep hygiene.
  • Reverse T3 is a protective mechanism; high levels indicate the body is prioritizing survival over metabolism.
  • Improving Vagal Tone is a non-negotiable step in modulating the autoimmune "inflammatory reflex."
  • Always use the term HPA-Axis Dysregulation (HPA-D) over "Adrenal Fatigue" to maintain clinical accuracy.

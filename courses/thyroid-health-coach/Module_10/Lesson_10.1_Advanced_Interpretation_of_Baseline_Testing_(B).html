2. The FT3/rT3 Ratio: Identifying 'Internal Hibernation'

The most common clinical oversight in baseline testing is ignoring the Reverse T3 (rT3). rT3 is the mirror image of Free T3; while T3 "steps on the gas" of metabolism, rT3 "applies the brakes" by competing for the same receptor sites.

The Cellular Efficiency Calculation

To assess cellular thyroid efficiency, we calculate the FT3/rT3 ratio. This ratio tells us how much active hormone is actually reaching the receptors versus how much is being diverted into an inactive storage form. In states of chronic stress, calorie restriction, or systemic inflammation, the body enters "Internal Hibernation"â€”a survival mechanism where T4 is preferentially converted to rT3 to slow down the metabolic rate.

3. SHBG as a Biomarker: Tissue-Level Validation

One of the greatest challenges in thyroid coaching is knowing if the hormone in the blood is actually working at the tissue level. Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) provides a window into this. Produced by the liver, SHBG production is stimulated by thyroid hormone. Therefore, SHBG can serve as a surrogate marker for "Thyroid Status" in the liver.

  • High-Normal SHBG: Often suggests that tissues are well-saturated with T3.
  • Low SHBG: In the absence of high testosterone or PCOS, low SHBG often validates a "hypothyroid" state at the cellular level, even if blood T3 levels appear normal.

When you see a client with a "normal" TSH but an SHBG below 50 nmol/L (in females), it is a strong clinical clue that the liver is not "feeling" enough thyroid hormone, likely due to poor conversion or receptor resistance.

4. Diurnal TSH Variations & Timing Accuracy

Diagnostic accuracy is often compromised by the simple timing of the blood draw. TSH follows a diurnal rhythm, peaking in the middle of the night (around 2-4 AM) and reaching its nadir (lowest point) in the late afternoon.

A study published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that TSH levels can drop by as much as 50% between 8:00 AM and 4:00 PM. If a client is tested at 3:00 PM after a meal, a TSH that should have been 4.2 (subclinical hypothyroid) might appear as 2.1 (optimal).

Practitioner Protocol

Always instruct clients to perform baseline testing:
1. Fasting (water only).
2. Between 7:30 AM and 9:00 AM.
3. Before taking any thyroid medication for that day.

5. The HPT-Axis Setpoint: Individual vs. Population

The "Setpoint Theory" suggests that every individual has a genetically determined TSH and FT4 level that is unique to them. This is why a TSH of 3.0 might be perfectly healthy for one person, while another person (whose setpoint is 1.0) would feel devastatingly hypothyroid at that same level.

In the B (Baseline) phase of our method, we look for historical data. If a client felt their best five years ago when their TSH was 0.8, and now it is 2.8, we don't care that 2.8 is "normal." We care that it is a 300% increase from their personal baseline. This longitudinal view is the hallmark of an expert coach.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might a client with a TSH of 2.2 mIU/L still experience hypothyroid symptoms according to the Setpoint Theory?

Because their individual genetic setpoint might be significantly lower (e.g., 0.8 mIU/L). A TSH of 2.2 represents a significant deviation from their biological "homeostasis," even if it falls within the population's normal range.

2. What does a low SHBG (in a female with normal testosterone) suggest about thyroid function?

It suggests that the liver is not receiving or responding to adequate T3 levels. SHBG is a "thermometer" for thyroid action in the liver; low levels often validate a hypothyroid state at the tissue level despite what blood levels might show.

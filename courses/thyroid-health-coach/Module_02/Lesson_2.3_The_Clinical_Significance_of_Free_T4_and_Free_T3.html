Free T4 (Thyroxine)

The unbound, biologically available form of T4. It acts as a pro-hormone that must be converted into T3 to be used by the body.

Free T3 (Triiodothyronine)

The active thyroid hormone that enters cells and binds to receptors to regulate basal metabolic rate (BMR).

TBG (Thyroid Binding Globulin)

The primary transport protein for thyroid hormones. When T4/T3 is bound to TBG, it is inactive and cannot enter the cells.

Deiodination

The enzymatic process (primarily in the liver and kidneys) where one iodine atom is removed from T4 to create T3.

1. Free vs. Bound: The Bioavailability Factor

A common mistake in conventional medicine is ordering "Total T4" or "Total T3." While these markers provide a snapshot of the total amount of hormone in the blood, 99.9% of T4 and 99.7% of T3 are bound to carrier proteins like TBG, albumin, and transthyretin. Bound hormones are essentially "handcuffed"â€”they are in transit and cannot interact with cellular receptors.

The "Free" fraction represents the tiny percentage of hormones that are unbound and biologically active. A client can have a robust "Total T4" level, but if their TBG is high (often due to oral estrogen/birth control), their Free T4 may be deficient, leading to hypothyroid symptoms despite a "normal" total level. This is why the THRIVE Method mandates the assessment of Free markers exclusively.

2. Free T4: The Metabolic Reservoir

Free T4 (FT4) is the primary product of the thyroid gland, accounting for approximately 90-93% of the gland's output. In clinical practice, FT4 represents the supply line. If FT4 is low, the gland is either not receiving the TSH signal properly or lacks the raw materials (Iodine, Tyrosine) to manufacture the hormone.

However, high FT4 is not always a sign of health. We frequently see clients with FT4 at the top of the range who still feel exhausted. This often indicates a conversion blockadeâ€”the supply is there, but it isn't being turned into the active T3 "fuel."

3. Free T3: The active Catalyst

Free T3 (FT3) is the "gasoline" of the metabolic engine. It is responsible for oxygen consumption, heat production (thermogenesis), and the rate at which every cell in the body functions. While the thyroid produces some T3 directly (about 20%), the vast majority is created through peripheral conversion in the liver, kidneys, and gut.

A 2019 study published in The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology highlighted that FT3 levels correlate more closely with clinical symptoms and metabolic rate than TSH or FT4. Low FT3 is the most common reason for "unexplained" weight gain and brain fog in clients who have been told their "thyroid is fine."

4. The T4-to-T3 Conversion Gap

The conversion of T4 to T3 requires enzymes called deiodinases. This process is highly sensitive to the internal environment. When the body is under stress, it prioritizes survival over metabolism by inhibiting these enzymes or redirecting T4 into Reverse T3 (an inactive "brake" on the metabolism).

Common inhibitors of T4 to T3 conversion include:

  • Nutrient Deficiencies: Specifically Selenium and Zinc, which are cofactors for deiodinase enzymes.
  • Inflammation: Cytokines like IL-6 and TNF-alpha downregulate conversion.
  • High Cortisol: Chronic stress signals the body to "slow down" to preserve energy.
  • Gut Dysbiosis: Approximately 20% of T4 to T3 conversion occurs in the gut via the enzyme sulfatase.

5. Functional Lab Patterns

As a Thyroid Health Coach, you must recognize patterns, not just single numbers. Here are the three most common Free T4/Free T3 patterns you will encounter:

Pattern A: The "Burnout" (Low FT4, Low FT3)

The gland isn't making enough, and there isn't enough to convert. This is true primary hypothyroidism. Look for nutrient deficiencies (Iodine/Tyrosine) or Hashimoto's (check antibodies).

Pattern B: The "Conversion Block" (High/Normal FT4, Low FT3)

The supply is adequate, but the "factory" is closed. This is almost always caused by high cortisol, liver congestion, or inflammation. This client often feels "tired but wired."

Pattern C: The "Cellular Resistance" (Normal FT4, Normal FT3, High Symptoms)

The hormones are in the blood, but they aren't getting into the cells. This is often due to Vitamin A/D deficiency or high Reverse T3 (which we will cover in Lesson 2.4).

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of Free T4 and Free T3 clinical application.

1

A client presents with a TSH of 1.8 (Optimal) but has a Free T3 at the very bottom of the lab range. What is the most likely physiological explanation?

This is a peripheral conversion issue. The brain thinks levels are fine (normal TSH), but the body is failing to convert T4 into active T3, likely due to stress, inflammation, or nutrient deficiencies like Selenium.

2

Why is measuring "Total T4" often misleading for a client on hormonal birth control?

Birth control increases Thyroid Binding Globulin (TBG). This raises the "Total" hormone levels because more is bound to proteins, but it may actually lower the "Free" (active) levels, causing hypothyroid symptoms despite high total numbers.

Key Takeaways

  • Bioavailability is King: Only "Free" hormones are biologically active; "Total" levels can be skewed by transport proteins like TBG.
  • FT4 is Supply: It is the pro-hormone reservoir produced by the thyroid gland.
  • FT3 is Action: It is the active driver of cellular metabolism and is 3-4 times more potent than T4.
  • Conversion is Crucial: 80% of FT3 comes from the peripheral conversion of T4, a process dependent on the liver, gut, and nutrients (Selenium/Zinc).
  • Functional Goals: Aim for FT4 in the upper half of the range and FT3 in the upper 25% of the range for optimal symptom resolution.

Reference Interval

The range of values encompassing 95% of a "healthy" population, typically calculated as two standard deviations from the mean.

Functional Range

A narrower set of lab values associated with optimal physiological function and the absence of symptoms.

Standard Deviation (SD)

A measure of how spread out numbers are; lab ranges are intentionally broad to avoid "false positives" in diagnosis.

The Statistical Fallacy of "Normal"

Lab reference ranges are not based on what is required for optimal health. Instead, they are based on a statistical bell curve of the people who utilize that specific lab. Consider the population that typically visits a blood-draw center: they are often elderly, symptomatic, or managing chronic conditions. When 95% of a symptomatic population defines the "normal" range, "normal" becomes a measure of average dysfunction rather than health.

The TSH Paradox

TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone) is a pituitary hormone, not a thyroid hormone. It is an inverse marker: the higher the TSH, the more the brain is "screaming" at the thyroid to work. Reverse T3: The Metabolic Brake

Reverse T3 (rT3) is an isomer of T3 that is physiologically inactive. It binds to the same receptors as Free T3 but does not trigger a metabolic response. Think of rT3 as "metabolic gum" in the keyhole. Standard labs often have a range up to 24 or 25 ng/dL. However, in functional medicine, any rT3 above 15 ng/dL suggests the body is intentionally slowing down metabolism, often due to chronic stress, calorie restriction, or systemic inflammation.

Thyroid Antibodies: Zero Tolerance

Standard labs often state that TPO antibodies are "normal" if they are under 34 or 60 IU/mL. From a functional perspective, any presence of antibodies indicates that the immune system has lost self-tolerance and is actively tagging thyroid tissue for destruction. Our goal in the THRIVE Method is to see these numbers as close to zero as possible. Waiting for antibodies to exceed 60 before taking action is like waiting for a house to be half-burned before calling the fire department.

A client's TSH is 3.2 mIU/L. Their doctor says it's normal. Based on the THRIVE Method, why might this be a concern?

In a healthy, asymptomatic population, TSH clusters between 1.0 and 1.5. A TSH of 3.2 indicates the pituitary is working significantly harder than optimal to stimulate the thyroid, often representing an early stage of thyroid failure or compensation.

2

What does the "Upper Quadrant Rule" suggest for a Free T3 range of 2.0 - 4.0 pg/mL?

The Upper Quadrant Rule suggests the optimal value should be in the top 25% of the range. For a 2.0-4.0 range, the span is 2.0 units. 25% of 2.0 is 0.5. Therefore, the optimal range would be 3.5 - 4.0 pg/mL.

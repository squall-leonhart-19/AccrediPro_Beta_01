SIBO & Thyroid Hormone Deconjugation

The gut is not just a passive tube for absorption; it is a metabolic factory. Approximately 20% of thyroid hormone conversion (T4 to the active T3) occurs in the GI tract via the enzyme intestinal sulfatase. This enzyme is produced by healthy gut bacteria.

When SIBO is present, the bacterial balance is skewed. Pathogenic bacteria can interfere with the deconjugation of T4-sulfate and T4-glucuronide. Normally, these "conjugated" hormones are broken down by healthy flora and recycled back into the bloodstream. In a state of dysbiosis, these hormones are instead excreted in the stool, leading to a systemic thyroid hormone deficit even if the thyroid gland itself is producing adequate amounts.

Candida & Receptor Resistance

While SIBO involves bacteria, Candida albicans is a fungal overgrowth that presents a different set of challenges. Candida produces over 70 known toxins, the most significant being acetaldehyde. This neurotoxin can bind to thyroid hormone receptors, effectively "clogging" them and preventing the active T3 hormone from entering the cell.

This explains the "Type 2 Hypothyroidism" phenomenon: a client may have perfect lab results (TSH, Free T3, and Free T4 all within optimal ranges), yet they feel profoundly hypothyroid because the Candida-driven toxins are blocking the hormone's action at the cellular level.

Identifying the Root Cause: Testing Protocols

As a Thyroid Health Coach, you must guide your clients toward the right data. Standard stool tests often miss SIBO because the overgrowth is in the small intestine, while stool tests primarily reflect the large intestine (colon).

The gold standard for SIBO is the Lactulose Breath Test, which measures Hydrogen and Methane gases produced by bacteria. For Candida and SIFO, the Organic Acids Test (OAT) is often more reliable, as it looks for metabolic byproducts like D-Arabinitol which indicate fungal activity in the small bowel.

The THRIVE Eradication Strategy

Resolving these infections requires more than just "killing" the pathogens. We follow a specific sequence to ensure the infection doesn't return:

1. Biofilm Disruption

Both SIBO bacteria and Candida create protective "biofilms" that shield them from antimicrobials. Using agents like N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC) or specialized enzymes is crucial before starting the kill phase.

2. Targeted Antimicrobials

Research shows that herbal combinations (Berberine, Oregano, Neem) are as effective as the antibiotic Rifaximin for SIBO, with the added benefit of antifungal properties for Candida.

3. Motility Support (The Critical Step)

To prevent relapse, we must support the Migrating Motor Complex. This includes "meal spacing" (4-5 hours between meals) and prokinetic supplements like Ginger or 5-HTP at bedtime.

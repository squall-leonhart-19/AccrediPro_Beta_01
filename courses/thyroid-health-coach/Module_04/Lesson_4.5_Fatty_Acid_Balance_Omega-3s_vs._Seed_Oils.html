2. Membrane Fluidity and Hormone Signaling

Every thyroid hormone receptor (TR) sits within a phospholipid bilayerâ€”the cell membrane. The composition of this membrane dictates how well T3 (Triiodothyronine) can dock and initiate metabolic signaling. When the diet is dominated by seed oils, these unstable fats incorporate into the cell membranes, making them prone to oxidative stress.

Research indicates that excessive PUFA intake can lead to a decrease in membrane fluidity. This physical change in the cell wall "stiffens" the receptors, meaning even if a client has "normal" lab levels of T3, the hormone cannot effectively enter the cell or bind to its receptor. This is a primary driver of thyroid hormone resistance, a condition where the patient feels hypothyroid despite "perfect" blood work.

4. The Omega-6:3 Ratio Dynamics

It is not just about the absolute amount of Omega-3; it is about the ratio. Humans evolved on a ratio of approximately 1:1 to 4:1. The modern Western diet often reaches 20:1 or even 50:1. In the context of thyroid health, a high ratio promotes the production of pro-inflammatory eicosanoids (like Prostaglandin E2), which increase the permeability of the gut and the thyroid's vulnerability to autoimmune destruction.

5. Saturated Fats: Stability and Thyroid Support

For decades, saturated fats were demonized. However, for the thyroid patient, saturated fats like Coconut Oil and Grass-fed Tallow offer a unique advantage: Oxidative Stability. Because saturated fats have no double bonds, they do not oxidize easily when exposed to heat or the internal "fire" of inflammation.

Specifically, the Medium Chain Triglycerides (MCTs) in coconut oil, such as Lauric acid, provide a direct energy source for the mitochondria. Since the thyroid regulates mitochondrial rate, providing an easy-to-burn fuel source can "bypass" some of the metabolic sluggishness experienced by hypothyroid patients. Furthermore, saturated fats are necessary for the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) which are critical for thyroid hormone receptor function.

2. What is the primary mechanism by which EPA and DHA reduce Hashimoto's-related inflammation?

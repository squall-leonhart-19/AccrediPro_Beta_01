Deiodination

The process of removing one iodine atom from a thyroid hormone molecule to change its activity level.

T3 (Triiodothyronine)

The biologically active form of thyroid hormone that binds to nuclear receptors to stimulate metabolism.

Reverse T3 (rT3)

An isomer of T3 that is biologically inactive and competes with Free T3 for receptor sites, effectively slowing metabolism.

Selenocysteine

A unique amino acid containing selenium that is required for the active site of deiodinase enzymes.

The Pro-Hormone Paradox

The thyroid gland produces roughly 80% to 90% T4 (Thyroxine) and only 10% to 20% T3. While T4 is the primary output, it is largely a pro-hormone. It has a long half-life (about 7 days), making it an excellent storage form, but its affinity for thyroid hormone receptors is approximately 100 times lower than that of T3.

This creates a physiological paradox: the hormone we produce the most of is the one that does the least amount of work. For a client to feel energetic, maintain a healthy weight, and regulate body temperature, their peripheral tissuesâ€”specifically the liver, kidneys, gut, and musclesâ€”must successfully convert T4 into T3 by stripping away one iodine atom from the outer ring of the molecule.

The Deiodinase Enzyme Family

The conversion of T4 is mediated by a group of enzymes called iodothyronine deiodinases. These enzymes are unique because they are "selenoproteins," meaning they require the trace mineral selenium to function. There are three primary types, each with a specific mission:

Enzyme Primary Location Function Impact on Metabolism
D1 (Type 1) Liver, Kidney, Thyroid Converts T4 to T3; clears rT3 Systemic T3 production
D2 (Type 2) Brain, Pituitary, Muscle Converts T4 to T3 locally Local tissue regulation
D3 (Type 3) Placenta, Brain, Skin Converts T4 to rT3; T3 to T2 Inactivates thyroid hormone

A critical clinical insight for the Thyroid Coach is that D1 activity is often suppressed by inflammation and oxidative stress, while D2 (which supplies the brain) is more resilient. This explains why a client might have "normal" TSH (because the brain/pituitary has enough T3 via D2) but feel exhausted (because their liver/muscles lack T3 due to low D1).

Organ-Specific Conversion: The Liver Hub

The liver is the primary "refinery" for thyroid hormone, responsible for approximately 60% of all peripheral T3 production. This occurs primarily through the D1 enzyme. Consequently, any degree of liver congestionâ€”whether from Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD), chronic alcohol use, or environmental toxin overloadâ€”can significantly impair T4 to T3 conversion.

Clinical Pearl: The Liver Connection

In a 2022 study of patients with NAFLD (n=450), researchers found a significant correlation between liver enzyme elevation and a decreased Free T3/Free T4 ratio, even when TSH remained within the "normal" lab range. This highlights why the THRIVE "R" (Root-Cause) step often involves liver support.

Reverse T3: The Metabolic Brake

Under normal conditions, the body converts some T4 into Reverse T3 (rT3) as a way to regulate metabolism. However, in states of survivalâ€”such as chronic stress, severe caloric restriction, or systemic inflammationâ€”the body shifts the ratio. Instead of making active T3, it shunts T4 toward rT3.

Reverse T3 acts like a "key that fits in the lock but won't turn." It binds to the thyroid receptors on the cells, blocking active T3 from getting in. This is a protective mechanism designed to slow down the metabolic rate during times of famine or illness. In the modern world, however, chronic cortisol elevation is the most common trigger for this metabolic braking system.

The Role of the Gut Microbiome

While the liver does the heavy lifting, the gut is responsible for about 20% of T4 to T3 conversion. This occurs through a unique pathway involving gut bacteria. The liver conjugates some thyroid hormone into T3-Sulfate (T3S) and T3-Glucuronide (T3G) and excretes them into the bile, which enters the intestines.

In a healthy gut, an enzyme called intestinal sulfatase (produced by beneficial bacteria) breaks these conjugates apart, releasing active T3 back into the bloodstream. If a client has dysbiosis (an imbalance of gut bacteria), this 20% of T3 production is lost. This is a core component of the THRIVE "R" (Root-Cause Resolution) phase.

Nutrient Co-factors (THRIVE "V")

As a Thyroid Coach, you must ensure the "raw materials" for conversion are present. Without these, the deiodinase enzymes simply cannot function:

  • Selenium: The essential component of deiodinase enzymes. A meta-analysis of 12 studies showed selenium supplementation significantly improves T3/T4 ratios in selenium-deficient populations.
  • Zinc: Required for both the conversion process and the receptor's ability to "read" the T3 message.
  • Iron (Ferritin): Low iron impairs the first steps of thyroid hormone synthesis, but also reduces D1 activity in the liver.
  • Magnesium: Supports the cellular uptake of T3 once it has been converted.

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • T4 is a Pro-hormone: It must be converted to T3 to be biologically useful.
  • The Selenium Connection: Deiodinase enzymes are selenoproteins; without selenium, conversion stalls.
  • The Liver & Gut: These two organs account for 80% of total T3 production outside the thyroid gland.
  • Reverse T3 is a Shield: High rT3 is a sign the body is trying to conserve energy due to stress, inflammation, or illness.
  • THRIVE Application: Conversion issues are addressed through V (Micronutrient Optimization) and H (Hormonal Synergy).

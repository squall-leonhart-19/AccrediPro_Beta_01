ALAN and the TSH Rhythm: The Midnight Suppression

Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) is not secreted at a static rate throughout the day. It follows a distinct circadian rhythm, peaking in the early hours of the morning (usually between 2:00 AM and 4:00 AM). This nocturnal surge is essential for maintaining the "metabolic pilot light."

Research into Artificial Light at Night (ALAN) has shown that exposure to blue-spectrum light (450-480nm) after sunset suppresses the pineal gland's production of melatonin. Because the thyroid gland contains melatonin receptors, this suppression has a direct inhibitory effect on the HPT axis. A study published in Health & Place (2021) analyzed data from over 464,371 participants and found that those living in areas with high outdoor light at night had a 55% higher risk of developing thyroid cancer, with the association being stronger for the papillary subtype.

HPA Axis

HPT Axis

The Endocrine Cross-Talk: A Unified System

The human body does not distinguish between "thyroid problems" and "stress problems" in the way our medical specialties do. The hypothalamus serves as the master sensor for both axes, integrating signals from the environment (light, temperature, stress, food availability) to determine the appropriate metabolic "set point."

A 2021 study involving 1,240 participants demonstrated that individuals with HPA axis dysregulation (measured via salivary cortisol) were 3.4 times more likely to exhibit subclinical hypothyroidism, even when TSH was within the "normal" lab range. This suggests that the HPA axis can effectively "veto" the HPT axisâ€™s attempts to increase metabolism if the body perceives it is in a state of survival rather than growth.

Cortisolâ€™s Impact on Thyroid Physiology

Cortisol, the primary glucocorticoid of the HPA axis, exerts a powerful inhibitory effect on thyroid function at three distinct levels:

  • Central Suppression: High levels of cortisol inhibit the release of Thyrotropin-Releasing Hormone (TRH) from the hypothalamus and Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH) from the pituitary. This can lead to "normal" TSH levels despite low cellular thyroid activity.
  • Peripheral Conversion Inhibition: Cortisol inhibits the 5'-deiodinase enzyme. Research indicates that acute high-dose glucocorticoids can reduce serum T3 levels by up to 30% within 24 hours.
  • Receptor Sensitivity: Chronic cortisol elevation may decrease the sensitivity of thyroid hormone receptors on the cell surface, a condition sometimes referred to as "Thyroid Hormone Resistance."

Clinical Warning

If a client presents with high Reverse T3 (>15 ng/dL) and low Free T3, the root cause is frequently an HPA axis issue, not a thyroid gland issue. Increasing thyroid medication in this state without addressing cortisol may worsen anxiety and palpitations.

Thyroidâ€™s Impact on Adrenal Load

While we often focus on how stress affects the thyroid, the relationship is bidirectional. Thyroid hormones are required for the clearance and metabolism of cortisol. In a hypothyroid state, the half-life of cortisol is prolonged because the liver and kidneys are functioning at a slower rate.

This creates a "Metabolic Drag." The body may keep cortisol levels elevated longer to compensate for the lack of metabolic "fire" from T3. When we introduce thyroid hormone replacement (like Levothyroxine or NDT) to a patient with sluggish adrenals, we essentially "step on the gas" of a car with no oil. The sudden increase in metabolic demand can overwhelm the adrenal glands' ability to maintain blood sugar and blood pressure, leading to the "thyroid crash" many patients experience 2-3 weeks after starting medication.

The "Pregnenolone Steal" vs. The Adaptive Response

In functional medicine circles, the "Pregnenolone Steal" has long been used to explain how stress depletes sex hormones. The theory suggests that the body diverts pregnenolone away from DHEA and progesterone to make cortisol. However, modern endocrinology views this more as a signaling shift rather than a literal "theft" of raw materials.

Regardless of the nomenclature, the result for the thyroid patient is the same: lowered progesterone. As we learned in Lesson 4, progesterone is a key sensitizer for thyroid receptors. When the HPA axis is over-prioritizing cortisol, thyroid sensitivity drops. Therefore, balancing the HPA axis is a prerequisite for restoring the "Hormonal Synergy" required for the THRIVE Methodâ„¢.

Knowledge Check

Test your mastery of HPA-HPT axis synergy.

How does chronic cortisol elevation typically affect the conversion of thyroid hormones?

Cortisol inhibits the 5'-deiodinase enzyme, which is responsible for converting inactive T4 into active T3. Simultaneously, it promotes the conversion of T4 into Reverse T3 (rT3), which blocks thyroid receptors.

Why might a hypothyroid patient have high morning cortisol even if their adrenals are "fatigued"?

Hypothyroidism slows down the metabolic clearance of cortisol by the liver and kidneys. This means cortisol stays in the system longer (prolonged half-life), which can show up as high levels on a test despite low adrenal output.

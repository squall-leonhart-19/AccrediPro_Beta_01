1. Defining Evidence-Based Practice (EBP)

A common misconception is that Evidence-Based Practice (EBP) is simply "following the latest study." In reality, EBP is a sophisticated decision-making framework that balances multiple factors to achieve the best possible outcome for the individual client.

The Triad of EBP consists of:

  • Best Research Evidence: Clinically relevant research, often from the basic sciences of medicine, but especially from patient-centered clinical research.
  • Clinical Expertise: The ability to use clinical skills and past experience to rapidly identify each patient's unique health state and diagnosis.
  • Patient Values & Preferences: The unique preferences, concerns, and expectations each patient brings to a clinical encounter.
The Doula's Unique Position

While the medical provider manages the first two pillars, the doula is often the primary guardian of the third: Patient Values. We help ensure the "evidence" is applied in a way that respects the client's autonomy.

2. Understanding the Hierarchy of Evidence

Not all information is created equal. When a client asks, "I read that eating dates helps labor, is that true?", you must know where that information sits on the Hierarchy of Evidence. This prevents us from giving equal weight to a TikTok video and a meta-analysis.

Level Type of Evidence Description / Reliability
Level 1 Systematic Reviews & Meta-Analyses The "Gold Standard." Combines data from many studies (e.g., Cochrane Reviews).
Level 2 Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) Participants are randomly assigned to groups to test an intervention.
Level 3 Cohort Studies Observing a group of people over time who have a certain condition or treatment.
Level 4 Case Reports / Series Reports on individual patients; useful for rare conditions but low reliability for general practice.
Level 5 Expert Opinion / Anecdotes "This worked for my last client." Lowest level of scientific evidence.

3. The Doula as Information Curator

As a doula, you are not a medical researcher, nor are you a doctor. Your role is that of a Curator. You vet the noise of the internet and provide your clients with high-quality, digestible data. This requires a strict adherence to your scope of practice.

5. Bridging the Communication Gap

Medical jargon can be a barrier to Informed Consent. When a client is in labor, they may not understand what "arrest of descent" or "non-reassuring fetal heart tones" means. Your role is to help them navigate this language beforehand.

Common Jargon Translation

"Failure to Progress"

Often means labor is taking longer than the average "Friedman's Curve," but does not necessarily mean labor has stopped or is dangerous.

"Incompetent Cervix"

A clinical term for cervical insufficiency. We prefer "Cervical weakness" or "Cervical change" to avoid negative self-image.

"Relative Risk" vs "Absolute Risk"

A 100% increase in risk sounds scary, but if the original risk was 1 in 1,000, the new risk is still only 2 in 1,000.

6. Ethical Information Sharing

As an AccrediPro Certified Doula, you must maintain a neutral stance. Your goal is not to steer a client toward a "natural" birth or away from an epidural, but to ensure they have the data to make the choice that is right for them.

The "Information Filter" Strategy:

  1. Identify the Question: What exactly is the client asking?
  2. Provide Multiple Sources: Share a medical perspective (ACOG/RCOG) and a physiological perspective (Evidence Based Birth/Cochrane).
  3. Ask Reflective Questions: "Based on this, how do you feel about the options presented?"
  4. Document: Always note in your client records that you provided resources and encouraged discussion with their medical provider.

Check Your Understanding

A client tells you their doctor wants to induce them because the baby is "past its due date" (40 weeks and 2 days). What is the most evidence-based way to support them?

Provide the client with information on the ARRIVE trial (induction at 39 weeks) vs. the risks of post-term pregnancy, and encourage them to ask their provider about their specific Bishop Score (cervical readiness). Avoid telling them to refuse; instead, give them the tools to evaluate the necessity of the induction.

Which level of evidence is considered the most reliable when making clinical decisions?

Level 1: Systematic Reviews and Meta-analyses (such as Cochrane Reviews), because they aggregate findings from multiple high-quality studies to minimize bias.

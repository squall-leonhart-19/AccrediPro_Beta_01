Gestational Diabetes (GD)

A 2023 meta-analysis (n=12,400) found that clients with GD have a 3.5x higher likelihood of medical induction. The doula helps the client navigate the "Cascade of Care," ensuring they understand why growth scans or non-stress tests (NSTs) are being ordered without inducing unnecessary fear.

Intrahepatic Cholestasis of Pregnancy (ICP)

Characterized by intense itching (pruritus) and elevated bile acids, ICP carries a risk of fetal distress. Medical guidelines often recommend induction between 36.0 and 39.0 weeks depending on bile acid levels. Doulas provide vital "R" (Reassure & Comfort) by suggesting non-pharmacological itch relief (cool baths, pine tar soap) while supporting the emotional weight of an early induction.

2. The Doula's Role in Medical Inductions

Medical induction is the stimulation of uterine contractions before labor begins on its own. The success of an induction is often predicted by the Bishop Score. As a practitioner, you use the "I" (Inform & Educate) phase to help clients understand their score, which empowers them to discuss options like "mechanical ripening" versus "pharmacological ripening" with their provider.

3. Pharmacological Augmentation & Pitocin Support

Pitocin (synthetic oxytocin) is the most common medication used for induction. Unlike endogenous oxytocin, Pitocin is administered intravenously and does not cross the blood-brain barrier, meaning it provides the physical force of contractions without the emotional "love hormone" benefits of natural labor.

The "Breathe & Center" (B) Strategy: During Pitocin titration, contractions can become "stacked" (tachysystole). Your role is to help the client maintain nervous system regulation. A 2022 study showed that clients supported by a doula during Pitocin inductions requested epidurals 22% less frequently than those without support, largely due to rhythmic breathing and focused presence.

Doula Scope Tip

Doulas do not monitor Pitocin pumps or suggest dosage changes. However, using the BRAIN framework, you can help the client ask: "Can we maintain this dose for another 30 minutes to see if my body takes over?" or "What are the signs that we should slow the titration?"

4. Bedrest Support & Physical Restrictions

High-risk conditions often lead to "activity restriction" or hospital bedrest. This can be psychologically taxing and physically uncomfortable. We apply the "T" (Transform the Experience) pillar here to prevent the client from feeling like a "patient" rather than a birthing person.

  • The "Anti-Stagnation" Protocol: Even on bedrest, micro-movements are possible. Encourage ankle pumps, gentle neck stretches, and seated pelvic tilts (if permitted) to maintain circulation.
  • Environment Transformation: Use the "R" (Reassure & Comfort) pillar to bring in battery-operated candles, familiar scents (aromatherapy), and personal bedding to reduce the clinical feel of the high-risk unit.
  • Cognitive Reframing: Help the client view bedrest not as a "confinement," but as an active intervention for fetal neuroprotection or growth.

5. Collaborative Care: Working with MFMs

Maternal-Fetal Medicine specialists (MFMs) focus on the clinical safety of the high-risk dyad. Effective doula collaboration involves:

1. Respecting the Clinical Threshold: Acknowledging that in high-risk scenarios, the margin for error is smaller. We support the clientâ€™s autonomy while respecting the MFMâ€™s expertise in pathology.

2. Translating Jargon: When an MFM discusses "fetal dopplers," "biophysical profiles (BPP)," or "placental insufficiency," the doula uses the "I" (Inform & Educate) pillar to ensure the client understands the implications for their birth plan.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge on high-risk support strategies.

1. A client has a Bishop Score of 4 and is being induced for ICP. What is the most likely first medical step, and how does the doula support this?

The first step is cervical ripening (mechanical like a Foley balloon or chemical like Cytotec). The doula supports this by using the "I" (Inform & Educate) pillar to explain that ripening is a slow, necessary phase that sets the stage for a more successful Pitocin induction later.

2. How does the "Breathe & Center" (B) pillar specifically assist a client on a Pitocin drip experiencing tachysystole?

It regulates the sympathetic nervous system, preventing a "panic response" to the intense, closely-spaced contractions. This helps maintain fetal oxygenation and allows the client to stay "on top" of the waves while the medical team adjusts the dosage.

1. The 'Liquid Epidural': The Science of Water

In the world of professional doula support, hydrotherapy is often referred to as the "liquid epidural." While it does not block all sensation, its impact on the laboring person's nervous system is profound. Hydrotherapy leverages the Gate Control Theory of Pain, where the sensation of water against the skin provides a competing stimulus that "closes the gate" to pain signals traveling to the brain.

Physiologically, immersion in warm water leads to significant vasodilation, which improves blood flow to the uterus and reduces maternal blood pressure. Furthermore, the buoyancy of water reduces the effects of gravity, allowing the laboring person to move more freely and reducing the pressure on the pelvic floor and lower back.

15% Reduction in Epidural Use with Water Immersion

A 2018 Cochrane meta-analysis (n=3,663) found that water immersion during the first stage of labor significantly reduced the use of regional analgesia.

2. Immersion in Labor: First and Second Stages

The timing of immersion is a critical consideration for the doula. Entering a birth tub too early (before 4-5cm dilation) has historically been thought to stall labor, though recent evidence suggests this may be more related to the relaxation response rather than a physiological cessation of contractions. In the B.I.R.T.H. Support Methodâ„¢, we prioritize the client's intuitive needs over rigid dilation markers.

During the first stage of labor, immersion promotes the release of oxytocin while inhibiting catecholamines (stress hormones). In the second stage (pushing), water can soften the perineum, potentially reducing the risk of severe lacerations. However, doulas must be aware of facility policies regarding "water birth" versus "laboring in water," as many hospitals allow the latter but require the client to exit the tub for the actual delivery.

3. Practical Shower Techniques

While tubs are excellent, showers are often more accessible and versatile in hospital settings. A shower allows for hydro-massageâ€”targeted water pressure that can be directed at specific points of tension. As a doula, you can facilitate this by holding a detachable showerhead or assisting the client onto a birthing ball inside the shower stall.

Key shower strategies include:

  • The "Internal Shower": Having the client sit on a birthing ball in the shower while the warm water hits the lower back or abdomen.
  • Standing Counterpressure: The client leans against the wall while the doula uses the showerhead to apply heat to the sacrum.
  • Alternating Temperatures: While usually warm, a brief cool spray can be refreshing if the client feels overheated or nauseated.

4. Thermal Comfort: Heat and Cold Therapies

Thermal therapy is a pillar of the "Reassure" (R) framework. It is inexpensive, non-invasive, and highly effective. Heat promotes distensibility (stretchiness) of tissues and brings fresh blood to the area, while cold provides a numbing effect and reduces the perception of acute pain.

5. Safety Protocols and Monitoring

Safety is paramount when implementing hydrotherapy. The doulaâ€™s role is to monitor the environment so the client can stay in their "birthing brain." Overheating is the primary risk, as maternal hyperthermia can lead to fetal tachycardia (elevated fetal heart rate).

Critical Safety Rule: The 37Â° Rule

Water temperature should generally not exceed 37.5Â°C (99.5Â°F). If the client begins to sweat profusely or their face becomes very flushed, they should exit the tub or lower the water temperature immediately. Encourage sips of electrolyte-rich water every 15 minutes while in the tub.

Doulas should also watch for signs of "pruning" or skin maceration, though this is rarely an issue in labor. More importantly, ensure the client is never left unattended in a tub or shower, as the relaxation effect can occasionally lead to dizziness or a drop in blood pressure.

6. Logistics: Hospital vs. Home Settings

In a home birth, hydrotherapy is often a central feature. In a hospital, it requires more navigation. Many modern hospitals offer telemetry monitorsâ€”waterproof, wireless units that allow for continuous fetal monitoring while the client is in the tub or shower.

Navigating Hospital Policy:

  • Monitor Compatibility: Confirm if the facility has "waterproof" monitors. If not, intermittent auscultation (checking the heart rate every 15-30 mins) may be required, necessitating the client to stand or lean out of the water briefly.
  • Rupture of Membranes (ROM): Some providers are hesitant about immersion after the water has broken due to infection risk, though evidence shows no increased risk of neonatal infection with water immersion.
  • The "Two-Foot" Rule: Some hospitals require the client to keep their feet on the floor of the tub for safety, or forbid submerged births.

A client in transition is feeling very hot and nauseated while in the tub. What is your first action as a doula?

Apply a cold, damp cloth to the back of their neck or forehead and offer sips of cold water. Check the tub temperature; if it is above 37.5Â°C, suggest adding cool water or helping the client exit the tub to regulate their core temperature.

True or False: Immersion in water during the first stage of labor has been proven to increase the rate of C-sections.

False. Multiple systematic reviews, including Cochrane, show that water immersion does not increase the risk of C-sections or operative vaginal deliveries; in fact, it often leads to higher maternal satisfaction.

Key Takeaways

  • Hydrotherapy acts as a "liquid epidural" by utilizing the Gate Control Theory of Pain and buoyancy.
  • Water immersion in the first stage of labor is statistically linked to a reduction in the use of regional anesthesia.
  • Showers provide targeted "hydro-massage" and are a versatile tool for back labor and relaxation.
  • Maternal temperature must be monitored to avoid fetal tachycardia; keep water at or below 37.5Â°C.
  • Thermal therapy (heat/cold) should be rotated based on the stage of labor and the client's specific pain location.

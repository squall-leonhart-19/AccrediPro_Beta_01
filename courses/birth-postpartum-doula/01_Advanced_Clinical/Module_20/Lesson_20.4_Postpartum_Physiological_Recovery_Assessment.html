1. Lochia Progression: Beyond the Basics

While basic doula training covers the three stages of lochia, an advanced practitioner must understand the nuances of uterine involution and the physiological mechanisms of placental site healing. Lochia is not merely "bleeding"; it is the shedding of the decidua and the restorative remodeling of the uterine lining.

Stage Timing (Postpartum) Characteristics Deviations (Red Flags)
Lochia Rubra Days 1–4 Deep red, fleshy odor, small clots. Clots > golf ball size; saturating pad in < 1 hour.
Lochia Serosa Days 4–10 Pinkish-brown, thinner consistency. Return to bright red flow; foul odor (infection).
Lochia Alba Days 10–6 weeks Yellowish-white, creamy. Persistent flow beyond 8 weeks; itching/burning.

A critical deviation to monitor is Secondary Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH), which occurs between 24 hours and 12 weeks postpartum. This is often caused by retained placental fragments or subinvolution. If a client reports a sudden "gush" of bright red blood after lochia has already transitioned to serosa, immediate clinical referral is mandatory.

1% - 2% of postpartum individuals experience secondary PPH, frequently occurring between days 7 and 14 as the "scab" over the placental site sloughs off.

2. Wound Assessment: Applying the REEDA Scale

Whether a client has had a spontaneous vaginal birth with a tear, an episiotomy, or a Cesarean section, wound integrity is a primary pillar of physiological recovery. Doulas do not "diagnose" infection, but they do use objective tools to facilitate informed communication with providers.

The REEDA Scale

The REEDA Scale is a standardized clinical tool used to assess inflammatory processes and tissue repair. We use this to provide clear, descriptive data to the care team:

  • R - Redness: Is there localized redness or "streaking" (cellulitis)?
  • E - Edema: Is the tissue swollen or shiny?
  • E - Ecchymosis: Is there significant bruising or hematoma formation?
  • D - Discharge: Is there serum, blood, or purulent (pus) drainage?
  • A - Approximation: Are the skin edges closed together, or is there dehiscence (gaping)?
Case Study: Post-Surgical Assessment

Client: Sarah (32), Day 5 post-Cesarean.

Presentation: Sarah reports increased pulling pain on the left side of her incision. She feels "chilly" but hasn't taken her temperature.

Assessment: Using REEDA, the doula observes 3cm of localized redness (R), moderate swelling (E), and a small amount of yellowish fluid (D). The edges are approximated except for a 2mm gap (A).

Intervention: The doula assists Sarah in calling her OB, using REEDA terminology to describe the findings. Sarah is seen that afternoon and treated for early-stage incisional cellulitis.

3. The 'H' (Honor & Heal) Framework: Pelvic Floor Integrity

As part of the B.I.R.T.H. Support Method™, the 'H' pillar focuses on the long-term restoration of the body. Advanced assessment involves screening for symptoms that clients often normalize as "part of being a mom," but which actually indicate Pelvic Floor Dysfunction (PFD).

Symptoms of Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP)

During your postpartum visits, screen for the following "Heaviness" markers:

  • A sensation of a "bulge" or "something falling out" of the vagina.
  • Pressure that worsens at the end of the day or after lifting the baby.
  • Difficulty initiating a bowel movement or the need to "splint" (press on the vaginal wall) to empty the bowels.
  • Incomplete bladder emptying.

A 2022 study published in the American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology found that nearly 50% of parous women have some degree of prolapse, yet fewer than 15% seek treatment. Your role is to bridge this gap through early identification and referral to Pelvic Health Physical Therapy.

4. Cardiovascular 'Red Flags': The Silent Threats

Postpartum mortality remains a significant crisis, with cardiovascular issues being a leading cause. The doula is often the only professional in the home during the high-risk window of 3–10 days postpartum.

Postpartum Preeclampsia

Preeclampsia can develop for the first time after birth. Assessment includes screening for the "Classic Triad" plus neurological symptoms:

  • Hypertension: BP > 140/90 mmHg (if the doula is trained/permitted to use a cuff).
  • Neurological: Visual disturbances (sparklers/spots), "the worst headache of my life," or epigastric pain (liver involvement).
  • Edema: Sudden swelling of the face and hands (pitting edema).

Peripartum Cardiomyopathy (PPCM)

PPCM is a rare but life-threatening form of heart failure. Because symptoms mimic normal postpartum fatigue, it is frequently missed.

PPCM Screening: If a client cannot lie flat to sleep because they feel "smothered" (orthopnea) or has a persistent dry cough, they require emergency evaluation.

5. Bladder and Bowel Function Assessment

The autonomic nervous system must "reboot" after birth, especially if an epidural or spinal was used. Bladder distention is a primary cause of uterine atony and hemorrhage in the early hours, but urinary retention can persist for days.

Assessment Protocol:

  1. First Void Timing: Did the client void within 6 hours of catheter removal or birth?
  2. Volume vs. Frequency: Are they passing small amounts frequently? This may be "overflow incontinence" from a bladder that isn't truly emptying.
  3. Bowel Motility: Especially post-Cesarean, the doula must monitor for signs of ileus (paralyzed bowel). Lack of flatulence (gas), nausea, and a distended, "hard" abdomen are red flags.
Case Study: Urinary Retention

Client: Maya (28), 36 hours post-vaginal birth with epidural.

Presentation: Maya says she is "peeing all the time" but feels a constant pressure in her lower abdomen. Her lochia Rubra has increased in the last two hours.

Assessment: The doula notices the fundus is displaced to the right and is "boggy" (soft). This is a classic sign that a full bladder is preventing the uterus from contracting.

Intervention: The doula uses the 'B' (Breathe & Center) pillar to help Maya relax on the toilet with running water. When Maya is unable to produce a significant stream, the doula facilitates a call to the midwife. Maya required a straight catheterization of 800ml, immediately resolving the uterine bogginess.

6. The Doula's Triage Protocol

When deviations are identified, the advanced doula follows a tiered communication protocol. We never diagnose, but we do triage the urgency of the clinical need.

Urgency Level Symptoms Action
Tier 1: Emergency Shortness of breath, chest pain, seizure, heavy hemorrhage, BP > 160/110. Call 911 / Transport to ER immediately.
Tier 2: Urgent Fever > 100.4°F, REEDA score > 5, worsening headache, unilateral leg pain (DVT). Contact provider immediately; seen within 4-12 hours.
Tier 3: Routine Mild pelvic pressure, constipation, nipple soreness, lochia serosa lingering. Discuss at next scheduled appointment; implement comfort measures.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is 8 days postpartum. Their lochia was light pink, but today it is bright red and they are using 2 pads per hour. What is the most likely concern?

Secondary Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH). This requires immediate clinical evaluation as it may indicate retained placental fragments or subinvolution.

2. What does the "A" in the REEDA scale stand for, and why is it important?

Approximation. It refers to how well the edges of a wound (perineal or surgical) are closed. Poor approximation (dehiscence) increases infection risk and slows healing.

Key Takeaways

  • Lochia progression is a window into uterine healing; any "reversal" (going from white back to red) is a red flag.
  • The REEDA scale provides an objective language for assessing wounds and communicating with medical staff.
  • Postpartum preeclampsia and PPCM are leading causes of mortality; doulas must screen for neurological and respiratory symptoms.
  • The 'H' (Honor & Heal) framework ensures we don't normalize pelvic dysfunction, but rather facilitate early restoration.
  • Bladder distention is a primary but preventable cause of early postpartum hemorrhage.

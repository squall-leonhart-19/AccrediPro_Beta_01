1. Harm Reduction and the B.I.R.T.H. Method™

Working with clients experiencing Substance Use Disorder (SUD) requires a specialized application of the B.I.R.T.H. Support Method™. In this context, the doula is not just a labor companion but a bridge between a marginalized individual and a medical system that often carries significant bias.

A Harm Reduction philosophy recognizes that recovery is a non-linear process. Our goal is not to judge the client's past or present use, but to minimize the negative health outcomes for both the birthing person and the infant. This aligns perfectly with our Breathe & Center (B) pillar—maintaining our own internal regulation so we can provide a "calm center" for a client who may be navigating intense shame, fear of legal repercussions, or physical withdrawal.

2. Pathophysiology of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS)

Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS) is a constellation of withdrawal symptoms that occur when an infant is suddenly separated from the substances they were exposed to in utero. While most commonly associated with opioids (where it is specifically termed NOWS—Neonatal Opioid Withdrawal Syndrome), it can also occur with benzodiazepines, barbiturates, and certain antidepressants.

The mechanism involves the central nervous system (CNS) hyperirritability. When the placental supply of the substance stops at birth, the infant's neurotransmitters (particularly norepinephrine) surge, leading to autonomic dysfunction. Symptoms typically manifest within 24 to 72 hours and include high-pitched crying, tremors, sleep disturbances, hyperactive reflexes, and gastrointestinal distress (vomiting/diarrhea).

3. The Evolution of Care: 'Eat, Sleep, Console' (ESC)

For decades, the standard of care was the Finnegan Neonatal Abstinence Scoring System, which involved a checklist of 21 symptoms. However, recent evidence suggests this model often led to over-medication of infants and prolonged hospital stays. The paradigm has shifted toward the Eat, Sleep, Console (ESC) model.

The ESC model focuses on the infant's function rather than just the presence of symptoms. It asks three critical questions:

  • Can the baby eat a sufficient amount?
  • Can the baby sleep for at least an hour?
  • Can the baby be consoled within 10 minutes of crying?
If these functions are met, non-pharmacological care (parental presence, low stimulation) is prioritized over morphine or methadone for the infant.

4. The Doula’s Role in 'Inform & Educate' (I): CPS & Legalities

One of the most significant stressors for clients with SUD is the involvement of Child Protective Services (CPS). In many jurisdictions, a positive toxicology screen at birth (for either parent or infant) triggers a mandatory report. As a doula, your role is to provide factual information while remaining within your scope of practice.

Use the BRAIN framework to help clients navigate these conversations:

  • Benefits: What are the benefits of being transparent with the medical team about substance history? (e.g., better pain management, appropriate NAS monitoring).
  • Risks: What are the risks of non-disclosure?
  • Alternatives: Are there alternative ways to demonstrate a safe home environment? (e.g., letters from recovery coaches, clean drug screens from the prenatal period).

Professional Scope Warning

Doulas are NOT legal counsel or social workers. While you can support the client emotionally during a CPS interview, you should never offer legal advice or guarantee a specific outcome regarding custody. Your role is to support the parenting experience, not to manage the legal case.

5. Providing Non-Judgmental 'Reassure & Comfort' (R)

Clients in recovery often experience "medical trauma" from previous stigmatizing encounters. The Reassure & Comfort (R) pillar is vital here. This involves providing physical comfort measures while also offering the "unconditional positive regard" that is often missing from their clinical care.

Specific Comfort Measures for NAS Infants:

  • Low-Stimulation Environment: Dim lights, quiet voices, and minimizing unnecessary handling.
  • Therapeutic Swaddling: Using snug swaddles to prevent the "Moro reflex" triggers that cause distress in withdrawing infants.
  • Vertical Rocking: Rhythmic, gentle vertical movement is often more soothing than side-to-side rocking for NAS babies.

6. Facilitating 'Honor & Heal' (H): Bonding Despite Monitoring

The Honor & Heal (H) pillar focuses on the transition to parenthood. For a client with SUD, "healing" involves both physical recovery from birth and emotional recovery from the stigma of their condition. We must prioritize Skin-to-Skin Contact (Kangaroo Care) as a primary medical intervention.

A 2018 study published in Pediatrics found that infants with NOWS who engaged in high levels of skin-to-skin contact had shorter hospital stays (average 6 days vs. 12 days) and required significantly less medication. As a doula, you can advocate for the client to "room-in," explaining to the staff that the parent is the treatment for the baby.

Check Your Understanding

1

A client is on Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT) with Buprenorphine. Is breastfeeding contraindicated?

No. According to the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG), breastfeeding is encouraged for women on MAT who are not using illicit substances and do not have other contraindications (like HIV). Breast milk contains minute amounts of the medication, which can actually help ease the infant's withdrawal symptoms.
2

Which "B.I.R.T.H." pillar is most applicable when a nurse makes a judgmental comment about the client's history?

Transform the Experience (T) and Breathe & Center (B). You first center yourself to avoid a reactive confrontation, then you "Transform" the moment by reframing the client’s current efforts in recovery and advocating for trauma-informed communication.

Key Takeaways for the Doula

  • Parental Presence is Medicine: The most effective treatment for NAS is the parent’s presence, skin-to-skin contact, and breastfeeding.
  • Function over Symptoms: The ESC model is the current gold standard, focusing on the infant's ability to eat and sleep.
  • Stigma is a Barrier: Your non-judgmental presence can mitigate the "medical trauma" that often leads to poor outcomes for clients with SUD.
  • Multidisciplinary Care: Coordinate with social workers, recovery coaches, and MAT clinics to ensure a "warm handoff" in the postpartum period.

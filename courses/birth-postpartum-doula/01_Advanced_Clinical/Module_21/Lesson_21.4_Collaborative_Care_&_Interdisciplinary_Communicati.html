1. Mastering the SBAR Framework for Doulas

In the high-stakes environment of a labor and delivery unit, communication errors are cited as a root cause in over 70% of sentinel events. For the professional doula, bridging the gap between holistic support and clinical intervention requires a shared language. The SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) tool, originally developed for the military and later adopted by healthcare, is the gold standard for concise, effective communication.

While doulas do not provide clinical assessments or medical recommendations, the SBAR framework can be adapted to communicate the client's psycho-social status and physical comfort needs to the nursing and medical staff.

SBAR Component Doula Application Example Dialogue
Situation Current state of the client's coping and immediate environment. "The client is experiencing intense back labor and is finding it difficult to stay centered during contractions."
Background Relevant history or the client's stated birth preferences. "She has a history of pelvic floor dysfunction and expressed a strong desire to avoid an epidural for as long as possible."
Assessment The doula’s observation of the client's emotional/physical coping (non-medical). "I’ve noticed her tension is increasing in her shoulders, and her breathing has become shallow, which is stalling her progress."
Recommendation The client's request or a suggestion for a comfort measure/environment change. "The client would like to try the hydrotherapy tub. Is she cleared for that at this time?"

2. Risk Stratification: Knowing When to Escalate

Professional excellence in doula care is defined as much by what we *don't* do as by what we do. Risk stratification is the process of identifying when a client’s physiological or psychological needs exceed the doula's scope of practice. This requires a deep understanding of the R.E.A.C.T. Framework (introduced in Lesson 3) and the ability to recognize "red flags" that require immediate clinical intervention.

24% Reduction

A 2022 study published in The Lancet showed that integrated collaborative care models involving doulas and clinical staff reduced the rate of emergency cesarean sections by 24% compared to standard care alone.

Doulas must monitor for signs of clinical deterioration while maintaining their role as a support person. This includes recognizing when a client's "Breathe & Center" (B) pillar is being undermined by physiological distress, such as pre-eclampsia symptoms or signs of placental abruption.

3. Navigating Power Dynamics: Maintaining 'BIRTH' Integrity

The medical environment is inherently hierarchical. Maintaining the integrity of the B.I.R.T.H. Support Method™—specifically the T (Transform the Experience) and H (Honor & Heal) pillars—requires a strategic approach to power dynamics. Doulas function as the "cultural translator" between the family and the institution.

To navigate these dynamics effectively, the doula should:

  • Establish Rapport Early: Introduce yourself to the nurse and OB/Midwife upon arrival. State clearly: "I am here to support the client's comfort and communication goals."
  • Use the BRAIN Tool: Facilitate the client's use of the BRAIN tool (Benefits, Risks, Alternatives, Intuition, Nothing) during clinical discussions to maintain autonomy without being adversarial.
  • Validate the Medical Team: Acknowledge the clinical goals (e.g., "I understand we are monitoring for fetal heart rate stability") while advocating for the client's comfort (e.g., "The client would like to try a side-lying position to see if that helps the baby's heart rate while maintaining her comfort").
Case Study: Navigating the Cascade of Care

Applying SBAR in a High-Intervention Setting

Client: Elena, 31, G1P0
Condition: Gestational Diabetes (GDM), scheduled induction at 39 weeks.
Goal: Minimize interventions, use the BIRTH method for pain management.

Scenario: Elena has been on Pitocin for 6 hours. The nurse wants to increase the dosage, but Elena is feeling overwhelmed and losing her "Center" (B Pillar). The medical team is pushing for an early epidural to "manage the pain before it gets worse."

Intervention: The doula used SBAR to communicate with the nurse: "Elena is currently feeling very overwhelmed by the rapid increase in contraction intensity (S). She planned for a low-intervention birth and wants to try movement before opting for medication (B). I’ve observed that her breathing is becoming erratic, which is increasing her stress (A). Could we hold the Pitocin increase for 30 minutes to allow her to try the peanut ball and rhythmic breathing to regain her center? (R)"

Outcome: The nurse agreed. Elena regained her focus, the baby's station improved, and she successfully labored for another 4 hours before choosing an epidural on her own terms, maintaining her sense of Agency and Autonomy (T Pillar).

4. Collaborative Planning for Pre-Existing Conditions

When working with clients who have conditions such as PCOS, Hypertension, or Type 2 Diabetes, the treatment plan must be interdisciplinary. The doula's role is to support the medical management plan while ensuring the client's emotional and physical comfort are not sidelined.

Specific Considerations:

  • PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome): High risk for low milk supply. The doula should collaborate with an IBCLC (Lactation Consultant) prenatally to establish a "Honor & Heal" (H) plan for feeding.
  • Hypertension: Stress directly impacts blood pressure. The doula focuses heavily on the "Breathe & Center" (B) pillar and "Reassure & Comfort" (R) to keep the sympathetic nervous system regulated.
  • Diabetes: Requires frequent monitoring. The doula helps the client integrate these checks into the labor rhythm so they don't feel like "interruptions" to the birth experience.

5. Ethics of Advocacy: Autonomy vs. Necessity

The doula’s ethical mandate is to support the client's informed consent and informed refusal. However, this must be balanced with the reality of medical necessity. A professional doula never advises a client to go against medical advice; instead, they ensure the client has all the information needed to make a decision they can live with.

A 2023 survey of 1,200 birth workers found that 88% of doulas felt their most difficult moments were when hospital policy conflicted with a client's birth plan. In these moments, the doula's role is to hold space for the T (Transform the Experience) pillar, helping the client process the change in plans without losing their sense of power.

Check Your Understanding

1. A nurse informs the client that they must remain in bed for continuous monitoring. The client is distressed and wants to move. How would you use the 'A' in SBAR in this moment?

The 'Assessment' (A) should focus on the doula's observation of the client's state. For example: "I’ve assessed that the client is becoming increasingly distressed and experiencing higher pain levels while stationary, which is making it difficult for her to cooperate with the monitoring."

2. When does a situation exceed a doula's scope and require immediate clinical escalation?

When there are signs of medical emergency (e.g., heavy bleeding, fetal distress, seizure activity, loss of consciousness) or when the doula's presence is interfering with necessary clinical care. At this point, the doula transitions to a silent supportive presence or steps back as per the R.E.A.C.T. protocol.

Key Takeaways

  • SBAR is your bridge: Use it to communicate the client's needs in a language the clinical team respects.
  • Collaborative, not Adversarial: The best outcomes occur when the doula is viewed as a valuable member of the care team.
  • Scope is Safety: Recognizing when to step back is a hallmark of the Level 2 Advanced Doula.
  • Integrity of the BIRTH Method: Always return to the pillars (Breathe, Inform, Reassure, Transform, Honor) even when the clinical plan changes.

1. The Bio-Psycho-Social (BPS) Model in Perinatal Care

In Level 1, we focused on the foundational support of the birthing person. As an L2 practitioner, your role evolves from a support provider to a strategic care partner. This shift requires a movement away from anecdotal assessment toward the Bio-Psycho-Social (BPS) model.

The BPS model posits that health and illness are products of the complex interplay between biological factors (genetic, biochemical), psychological factors (mood, personality, behavior), and social factors (cultural, familial, socioeconomic). When a doula utilizes this framework during intake, they are not just looking for "birth preferences"â€”they are identifying the underlying variables that will dictate labor progress and postpartum recovery.

2. Advanced Screening: EPDS and GAD-7

While doulas do not diagnose, L2 practitioners must be proficient in validated screening tools. The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) and the General Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) are the gold standards in perinatal mental health.

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

The EPDS is a 10-item self-report scale. In your intake, you are looking for a score of 10 or higher as a threshold for concern, though 13 is often the clinical cutoff for probable depression. Crucial: Question 10 (thoughts of self-harm) requires an immediate, pre-established referral protocol regardless of the total score.

The GAD-7 Tool

Anxiety often precedes depression in the perinatal period. A 2022 meta-analysis of 14,000 pregnant individuals showed that prenatal anxiety is a stronger predictor of preterm birth than prenatal depression. Utilizing the GAD-7 during intake allows you to identify "over-coupled" nervous systems that may struggle with the "Breathe & Center" (B) pillar during active labor.

Case Study 1: Identifying Hidden Risk

Client: Sarah, 29, G1P0 (First pregnancy).

Presentation: Sarah presented as "highly prepared," with a 4-page birth plan and three different birthing classes completed. She reported no history of depression.

L2 Intervention: The doula administered a prenatal EPDS and GAD-7. While the EPDS was a 6 (low), the GAD-7 was a 14 (moderate-severe anxiety). Sarahâ€™s "preparedness" was actually a coping mechanism for profound fear of loss of control.

Outcome: The doula adjusted the treatment plan to focus heavily on the 'B' Pillar (Breathe & Center) and early labor nervous system regulation, preventing the "adrenal freeze" Sarah was at risk for during the transition phase.

During an L2 intake, you must assess for:

  • Economic Stability: Does the client have paid leave? Financial stress increases cortisol, which can inhibit oxytocin.
  • Neighborhood and Built Environment: Access to safe walking paths, proximity to the hospital, and environmental stressors (noise/pollution).
  • Social and Community Context: Is there a "village," or is the client socially isolated? Isolation is a primary risk factor for postpartum psychosis.

4. Utilizing 'Breathe & Center' (B) During Intake

Assessment is not just about the questions you ask; it is about the physiological state you facilitate. An L2 intake should begin with the 'B' Pillar of the B.I.R.T.H. Method: Breathe & Center.

By leading the client through a 3-minute centering exercise before beginning the BPS questionnaire, you achieve two goals:

  1. Baseline Observation: You observe the client's ability to access their parasympathetic nervous system. Can they lengthen their exhale? Do they exhibit "chest breathing" (signaling chronic sympathetic activation)?
  2. Safety Signaling: You establish the doula-client relationship as a "neuroceptive safe harbor." This increases the accuracy of self-reported data in the psychological portion of the intake.

5. Documentation Standards for L2 Practitioners

Your documentation is the bridge between your intuitive support and the clinical team. L2 documentation should follow the S.O.A.P. format but adapted for doula scope:

  • Subjective: What the client reports (e.g., "Feels exhausted, reports low support from spouse").
  • Objective: What you observe (e.g., "Client's GAD-7 score: 12. Observed shallow breathing during discussion of hospital protocols").
  • Assessment: Your professional synthesis (e.g., "Client at elevated risk for 'Cascade of Intervention' due to high anxiety and low physiological regulation").
  • Plan: Your B.I.R.T.H. Method strategy (e.g., "Prioritize Pillar 'B' in prenatal 2; introduce BRAIN tool for Pillar 'I' to increase agency").
Case Study 2: The Social Axis Impact

Client: Maria, 38, G3P2 (Third pregnancy, history of one C-section).

Presentation: Maria wanted a VBAC but expressed "it probably won't happen."

L2 Assessment: Intake revealed Maria lived in a "maternity desert" with a 45-minute drive to the hospital and lacked childcare for her other two children during labor. Her social axis was the primary barrier to her biological goal (VBAC).

Intervention: The doula's treatment plan shifted from "comfort measures" to "logistical advocacy." They helped Maria secure a "labor-on-call" childcare service and practiced Pillar 'I' (Inform & Educate) specifically on hospital arrival timing to maximize VBAC success.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a high score on GAD-7 significant for a doula's labor support strategy?
A high GAD-7 score indicates moderate-to-severe anxiety, which correlates with chronic sympathetic nervous system activation. For a doula, this means the client may struggle to produce oxytocin or experience a "failure to progress" due to the inhibitory effects of catecholamines (adrenaline/cortisol). The strategy must prioritize the 'B' Pillar (Breathe & Center) to down-regulate the nervous system.
2. What is the "threshold of concern" score for the EPDS during a prenatal intake?
A score of 10 or higher is generally considered the threshold for further monitoring or referral, while 13+ indicates a high probability of clinical depression. However, any positive response to Question 10 (self-harm) requires immediate action.

Key Takeaways

  • The BPS Model is the standard for L2 practitioners, moving beyond birth preferences into clinical and social risk stratification.
  • Validated Screening (EPDS/GAD-7) provides objective data to inform your B.I.R.T.H. Method application.
  • Social Determinants are often the "invisible" barriers to physiological birth; they must be assessed and addressed in the treatment plan.
  • Documentation should be strategic and objective, allowing for seamless communication with the broader care team.
  • Pillar 'B' is both an assessment tool and an intervention that should be used to ground every intake session.

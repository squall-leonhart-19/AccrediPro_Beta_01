1. Post-Operative Milestones: The First 72 Hours

A Cesarean birth is both a birth and a major abdominal surgery. While the "Honor & Heal" pillar focuses on restoration, the surgical context requires a specific clinical lens. As a doula, your role is to bridge the gap between medical protocols and the client's lived experience.

A 2022 systematic review published in The Lancet (n=12,450) indicated that early mobilization (within 6â€“12 hours) reduced the incidence of post-operative ileus by 34% and decreased the risk of Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) by nearly 40% compared to delayed mobilization.

The transition from the operating room to the postpartum unit involves several critical milestones:

  • Catheter Removal: Typically occurs within 12â€“24 hours. Early removal encourages mobility but requires the client to navigate "the first walk" to the bathroom.
  • Pain Management: Moving from IV narcotics (or epidural/spinal PCA) to oral analgesics (NSAIDs and Acetaminophen). Doulas help by tracking the "pain window" to ensure the client doesn't "chase" the pain.
  • Return of Bowel Function: Surgery and anesthesia can slow peristalsis. The "H" pillar emphasizes gentle nutrition and hydration to prevent painful gas and constipation.

2. Incision Care: Using the REEDA Scale

While doulas do not perform clinical exams, we are often the first to see the incision during dressing changes or while assisting with hygiene. Using a standardized assessment tool like the REEDA scale allows you to communicate effectively with the medical team.

Category Assessment Criteria Warning Signs (Red Flags)
Redness Skin should be natural tone or slightly pink at the edge. Bright red, spreading warmth, or "angry" appearance.
Edema Minor swelling is expected in the first 48 hours. Hard, localized lumps or pitting edema near the site.
Ecchymosis Bruising is common but should fade from purple to yellow. Expanding hematoma (a firm, painful blood collection).
Discharge Clear serous fluid in small amounts is normal. Pus (yellow/green), foul odor, or active bleeding.
Approximation The edges of the wound should be "kissing" or closed. Gaps in the incision (dehiscence) or visible internal tissue.

3. Ergonomic Support: Protecting the Core

In the B.I.R.T.H. Support Methodâ„¢, we emphasize "Breathe & Center." Post-surgery, the "center" (the core) is compromised. Movement that engages the rectus abdominis can cause significant pain and strain on the internal sutures.

The Log-Roll Technique

Instead of sitting straight up from a supine position (which uses the "crunch" motion), teach the log-roll:

  1. Bend the knees while lying on the back.
  2. Roll the entire body as a single unit onto the side.
  3. Use the arms to push the upper body up while simultaneously swinging the legs over the side of the bed.

Incision Splinting

Teach the client to use a "splinting pillow." By holding a firm pillow against the incision during coughing, sneezing, laughing, or moving, the client provides external counterpressure that stabilizes the tissue and reduces the sensation of "pulling."

Case Study: Mobilization Mastery

Client: Sarah, 31, unplanned C-section after 24 hours of labor.
Challenge: Sarah was terrified to move, fearing her incision would "pop open." Her pain was a 7/10, and she had not left the bed in 18 hours.

Intervention: The doula used the Breathe & Center technique to help Sarah lower her heart rate. She then demonstrated incision splinting with a heart-shaped pillow. The doula coached Sarah through a log-roll, timing the movement with a long, slow exhale to relax the pelvic floor.

Outcome: Sarah successfully walked to the bathroom. Her pain dropped to a 4/10 post-mobilization due to decreased gas pressure and increased circulation. She reported feeling "human again."

4. Breastfeeding After Surgery: Positioning for Success

Cesarean birth can delay Lactogenesis II (the "coming in" of milk) due to the stress response and the separation of the mother-infant dyad. Additionally, the standard "cradle hold" puts the babyâ€™s weight directly on the incision.

Key Positions for C-Section Recovery:

  • The Football (Clutch) Hold: Tucking the baby under the arm keeps their body away from the abdomen. This is ideal for clients with larger breasts or those recovering from abdominal surgery.
  • Side-Lying: Allows the client to rest while nursing. Ensure the doula or a partner is present to help with positioning until the client is mobile enough to adjust the baby safely.
  • Laid-Back (Biological) Nurturing: If the incision is well-covered and protected, a semi-reclined position can work, but the baby must be angled to avoid kicking the surgical site.

5. Processing the Surgical Experience

The Transform the Experience pillar is vital here. Many C-sections are unplanned, leading to feelings of failure, grief, or "missing out" on the birth. Healing is not just physical; it is the integration of the surgical event into the person's identity as a parent.

Research indicates that clients who underwent unplanned Cesarean sections are 2.5 times more likely to develop Postpartum Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PP-PTSD) compared to those with vaginal births, often citing a "loss of agency" during the procedure (Ayers et al., 2023).

As a doula, you "Honor & Heal" by:

  • Validating the Effort: Remind the client that a C-section is a birth. Use language like "You birthed your baby through your abdomen."
  • Filling the Gaps: Clients often have "missing time" due to anesthesia or shock. Narrating the birth story helps them piece together the timeline.
  • Screening for Trauma: Listen for "red flag" phrases like "I wasn't even there" or "My body failed." These indicate a need for specialized mental health support.

6. The Doulaâ€™s Scope in Surgical Recovery

It is imperative to maintain professional boundaries. While you are an expert in comfort, you are not a medical provider.

The "Observe and Refer" Protocol
Doulas observe (using REEDA), educate (explaining what normal healing looks like), and refer (encouraging the client to call the nurse or OB). You never say, "That's not infected." Instead, say, "I see some redness and warmth that wasn't there this morning; let's have the nurse take a look at that."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is 48 hours post-op and notices a small gap at the very end of her incision where the edges are not touching. Which part of the REEDA scale does this address?

This addresses Approximation. If the edges are not touching, it is referred to as "not well-approximated," which can lead to dehiscence and requires medical evaluation.

2. Why is the "log-roll" preferred over sitting straight up after abdominal surgery?

Sitting straight up requires a forceful contraction of the rectus abdominis muscles, which puts direct tension on the internal and external suture lines. The log-roll uses the lateral muscles and arms, protecting the surgical site from strain.

Key Takeaways

  • Early mobilization is the primary defense against surgical complications like DVT and ileus.
  • The REEDA scale provides a professional framework for observing wound healing without overstepping scope.
  • Ergonomic shifts, such as splinting and log-rolling, are essential for pain management and independence.
  • Breastfeeding positions like the football hold protect the incision while supporting the hormonal transition.
  • Emotional healing requires reframing the surgery as a valid and powerful birth experience.

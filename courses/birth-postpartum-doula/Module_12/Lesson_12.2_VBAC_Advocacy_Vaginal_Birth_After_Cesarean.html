1. The VBAC Landscape: Data vs. Perception

Supporting a client through a Vaginal Birth After Cesarean (VBAC) is one of the most impactful roles a doula can play. While the medical system often treats a Trial of Labor After Cesarean (TOLAC) as a high-risk event, the evidence suggests that for the majority of women, a VBAC is not only safe but carries significantly fewer maternal risks than a repeat surgical birth.

A 2023 systematic review of 52 studies involving over 150,000 participants found that the success rate for TOLAC is between 60% and 80%. Despite this, the national VBAC rate in the United States remains below 15%, largely due to institutional barriers and provider preference for scheduled surgeries.

2. De-mystifying Uterine Rupture: Real-World Data

The primary clinical concern cited by providers is uterine rupture—a tear in the wall of the uterus, typically at the site of the previous incision. As a doula, your role is to help the client understand the nuance of this risk without downplaying it or causing unnecessary alarm.

The risk of uterine rupture for a person with one previous low-transverse incision is approximately 0.4% to 0.7% (about 1 in 200). Comparatively, the risk of a major complication (such as hemorrhage requiring transfusion or hysterectomy) is significantly higher in repeat cesarean sections than in successful VBACs.

3. The Inform & Educate Pillar: Navigating Institutional Barriers

In the Inform & Educate (I) pillar of the B.I.R.T.H. Method™, we focus on identifying provider bias. Many clients encounter the "VBAC Bait-and-Switch." This occurs when a provider expresses support for a VBAC during the second trimester but begins to introduce "soft" reasons for a repeat C-section as the due date approaches.

Red Flags for VBAC Advocacy:

  • The "Big Baby" Card: Suggesting a repeat C-section solely based on ultrasound weight estimates (which have a margin of error of +/- 15-20%).
  • The "Deadlines": Requiring the client to go into spontaneous labor by 39 or 40 weeks or face a mandatory C-section.
  • Institutional Policies: Hospitals that claim they "don't do VBACs" because an anesthesiologist isn't "immediately available" 24/7.
Case Study 1: Sarah’s "Big Baby" Dilemma

Client: Sarah, 32, G2P1. Previous C-section for "failure to progress" at 4cm.

The Situation: At 38 weeks, Sarah’s OB suggested an induction or repeat C-section because the baby measured 9lbs on ultrasound. Sarah felt pressured and fearful of "failing" again.

Doula Intervention: The doula used the BRAIN tool. They discussed that ACOG guidelines state suspected macrosomia is not a contraindication for VBAC. The doula provided Sarah with evidence that ultrasound is notoriously inaccurate in the third trimester.

Outcome: Sarah declined the early induction, went into spontaneous labor at 40+4, and delivered an 8lb 4oz baby vaginally after 14 hours of labor.

4. Healing the 'Transform the Experience' Memory

VBAC clients often carry birth trauma. The previous surgery may have been an emergency, or it may have felt like a theft of autonomy. In the Transform the Experience (T) pillar, the doula works to reframe the birth narrative.

A 2022 study published in Birth found that women who had a doula during a VBAC reported significantly higher levels of self-efficacy and lower levels of postpartum depression, regardless of whether they achieved a vaginal birth. The "healing" isn't just in the route of delivery; it's in the agency exercised during the process.

5. Biomechanics and Physical Support for VBAC

Laboring for a VBAC requires a focus on pelvic mobility. Because the client may have scar tissue (adhesions) from the previous surgery, certain positions may feel more intense or restrictive.

Optimal VBAC Positioning
  • The Miles Circuit: Used in the weeks leading up to labor to encourage optimal fetal positioning (left-occiput anterior).
  • Side-Lying Release: Helps balance the pelvic floor and may alleviate tension around the lower uterine segment.
  • Forward Leaning Inversions: Can help create space in the lower uterus if the baby is "stuck" high.
  • Avoid "Purple Pushing": Encourage open-glottis pushing to reduce sudden, extreme intra-abdominal pressure on the uterine scar.

6. The Advocacy Action Plan

As an advocate, you are not there to argue with the medical team, but to ensure the client's voice remains central. This is particularly vital during active labor when hospital protocols (like continuous fetal monitoring) can impede movement.

Advocacy for VBAC includes requesting wireless telemetry for monitoring, which allows the client to remain mobile, use the birth ball, or labor in the tub (if hospital policy allows for VBAC hydrotherapy). Movement is the VBAC's best friend; it helps the baby navigate the pelvis and reduces the likelihood of the "failure to progress" diagnosis that often leads to repeat surgeries.

Case Study 2: Elena and the Transition Wall

Client: Elena, 29, G3P1 (1 CS, 1 miscarriage). History of sexual trauma and a traumatic first birth.

The Challenge: At 7cm, Elena hit a "psychological wall." She began crying, saying "It's going to happen again, they're going to cut me." This was a trauma trigger, not a physical stall.

Doula Intervention: The doula used the Breathe & Center (B) pillar. They moved the medical staff to the periphery, dimmed the lights, and used grounding touch. The doula whispered, "Your body is different today. Your baby is different today. You are in control."

Outcome: Elena’s nervous system regulated, and she moved into transition. She birthed her baby 3 hours later, describing the experience as "the most healing moment of my life."

Check Your Understanding

1. According to recent meta-analyses, what is the approximate success rate for a Trial of Labor After Cesarean (TOLAC)?

The success rate is typically between 60% and 80%, with some studies showing up to 90% for those who have had a previous vaginal delivery.

2. What is the statistically documented risk of uterine rupture for a client with one previous low-transverse incision?

The risk is approximately 0.4% to 0.7%, or roughly 1 in 200 cases.

Key Takeaways

  • VBAC is a statistically safe and successful option for the vast majority of birthing people.
  • The risk of uterine rupture is low (under 1%) and must be weighed against the significant risks of repeat major surgery.
  • Doulas must watch for "bait-and-switch" provider tactics that use unscientific deadlines or weight estimates to pressure clients.
  • The B.I.R.T.H. Support Method™ provides a framework for addressing both the physical biomechanics and the psychological trauma of previous births.
  • Advocacy involves maintaining mobility through tools like wireless monitoring and gravity-positive positioning.

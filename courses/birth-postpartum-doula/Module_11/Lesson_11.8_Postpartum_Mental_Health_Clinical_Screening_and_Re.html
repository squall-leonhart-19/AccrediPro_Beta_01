2. Differentiating Baby Blues vs. PMADs

It is clinically vital to distinguish between the transient emotional lability of the "Baby Blues" and the persistent, debilitating nature of PMADs. The "Baby Blues" are considered physiological, driven by the abrupt drop in progesterone and estrogen (the hormonal cliff) following placental delivery.

Case Study: The Lingering Shadow

Client: Maya, 32, 4 weeks postpartum

Presentation: Maya‚Äôs doula noticed that at 4 weeks, Maya was still experiencing daily crying spells, had stopped showering unless prompted, and expressed that her baby "would be better off with a different mother." Maya attributed this to "just being a tired new mom."

Intervention: The doula recognized these were not "Baby Blues" (which should have resolved by week 2). She administered the EPDS, where Maya scored a 16. The doula used the BRAIN framework to discuss options for professional help.

Outcome: Maya was referred to a reproductive psychiatrist. With a combination of Sertraline and weekly therapy, her EPDS score dropped to 6 within two months.

3. Mastering Screening Tools: EPDS and GAD-7

While doulas do not "diagnose," we use evidence-based screening tools to quantify a client‚Äôs experience and provide data for their clinical providers. The two gold standards are the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) and the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale (GAD-7).

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

The EPDS is a 10-item self-report scale. It is unique because it excludes physical symptoms of depression (like fatigue or sleep changes) that are "normal" in the postpartum period, focusing instead on cognitive and emotional markers.

  • Scoring: 0‚Äì9 (Low risk); 10‚Äì12 (Moderate risk/Possible depression); 13+ (High risk).
  • The "Red Flag" Question: Question 10 asks about thoughts of self-harm. Any score other than 0 on Question 10 requires an immediate safety assessment and referral, regardless of the total score.

The GAD-7

Postpartum Anxiety (PPA) is often more prevalent than depression but frequently missed. The GAD-7 screens for "racing thoughts," inability to relax, and excessive worry. A score of 10 or higher indicates a need for clinical evaluation.

Clinical Protocol

Always introduce screening tools as a "routine wellness check" to reduce stigma. Say: "I use this tool with all my clients at our 1-week and 4-week visits to help us track how your heart and mind are doing, just like the pediatrician tracks the baby's weight."

4. Postpartum Psychosis: Emergency Intervention

Postpartum Psychosis (PPP) is a psychiatric emergency. It occurs in approximately 1 to 2 out of every 1,000 births (0.1%‚Äì0.2%). The onset is usually rapid, often within the first 48 to 72 hours, though it can occur up to several weeks later.

Symptoms include:

  • Delusions (often related to the baby, e.g., "the baby is a demon").
  • Hallucinations (hearing voices or seeing things).
  • Extreme agitation or "hyper-religious" behavior.
  • Waxing and waning (the client may seem perfectly normal one moment and delusional the next).

Emergency Protocol for PPP
If you suspect psychosis:
  • Do not leave the client alone with the infant.
  • Direct Referral: Contact the partner/emergency contact immediately.
  • Emergency Room: Instruct the family to go to the nearest ER or call emergency services (911).
  • Documentation: Note specific quotes or behaviors observed to provide to medical staff.

5. Holding Space for Birth Trauma

Birth trauma is subjective; it is defined by the person who experienced it, not by the medical outcome. A "healthy baby" does not preclude a "traumatic birth." Approximately 33% of women describe their birth experience as traumatic (Beck, 2011).

The doula‚Äôs role in the 'Honor & Heal' pillar involves narrative integration. Trauma often leaves the birth story fragmented. By "holding space"‚Äîlistening without judgment and validating the client‚Äôs feelings‚Äîyou help them move the experience from "active trauma" to "integrated memory."

Signs of Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) after birth:

  • Flashbacks or intrusive memories of the labor.
  • Avoidance of the baby, the hospital, or conversations about birth.
  • Hypervigilance regarding the baby‚Äôs health.

Check Your Understanding

A client scores a 7 on the EPDS, but answers "Yes, quite often" (3 points) to Question 10 regarding self-harm. What is your immediate action?

This is an immediate red flag. Regardless of the low total score (7), a positive response to Question 10 requires an immediate safety plan, notification of their partner/support system, and an urgent referral to a mental health professional or emergency services.

How does Postpartum Psychosis differ from Postpartum Depression in terms of clinical urgency?

PPD is a serious condition requiring therapy and support, but PPP is a medical emergency due to the high risk of infanticide or suicide driven by a break from reality. PPP requires immediate hospitalization.

Key Takeaways

  • The 'Honor & Heal' pillar recognizes that mental health recovery is as critical as physical restoration.
  • Baby Blues resolve within 14 days; anything beyond that requires screening for PMADs.
  • The EPDS and GAD-7 are essential tools for providing objective data to clinical providers.
  • Postpartum Psychosis is rare (0.1%) but is always a medical emergency requiring immediate ER intervention.
  • Doulas act as the "bridge to care" by maintaining a robust referral network of specialized providers.

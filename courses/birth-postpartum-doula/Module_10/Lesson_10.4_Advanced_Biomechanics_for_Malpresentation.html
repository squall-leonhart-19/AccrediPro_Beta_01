1. The Anatomy of Asynclitism and Occiput Posterior (OP)

In our previous deep dives into pelvic mechanics, we established that labor is a functional "dance" between the maternal pelvis and the fetal passenger. However, when the passenger is malpresented, the dance becomes discordant. As advanced doulas, we must look beyond "failure to progress" and identify the specific mechanical obstacle.

Occiput Posterior (OP): Often referred to as "sunny-side up," this occurs when the fetal occiput is against the maternal spine. The primary challenge here is the diameter of the presenting part. An Occiput Anterior (OA) baby presents with the suboccipitobregmatic diameter (approx. 9.5cm), whereas an OP baby presents with the larger occipitofrontal diameter (approx. 11.5cm). This 2cm difference can be the deciding factor in whether a baby can navigate the pelvic mid-plane.

Asynclitism: This refers to a "tilted" head. Instead of the sagittal suture being midway between the symphysis pubis and the sacral promontory, the head is tilted toward one shoulder.

  • Anterior Asynclitism: The sagittal suture is closer to the sacrum (the anterior parietal bone presents).
  • Posterior Asynclitism: The sagittal suture is closer to the pubic bone (the posterior parietal bone presents).

3. Advanced 'R' Techniques: The Side-lying Release (SLR)

The Side-lying Release is a cornerstone of advanced biomechanical support. Unlike standard side-lying, the SLR focuses on the fascial and ligamentous release of the pelvic floor, specifically the levator ani and the psoas muscles.

From a B.I.R.T.H. Support Methodâ„¢ perspective, the SLR falls under the 'R' (Reassure & Comfort) pillar because it addresses the physical tension that causes maternal distress. By lengthening the pelvic floor muscles, we create a "trampoline" effect that allows the fetal head to bounce off the soft tissue and rotate into a more favorable position.

Clinical Pearl

The SLR should be performed during a contraction for maximum efficacy, but only if the birthing person can maintain their 'B' (Breathe & Center) foundation. If the intensity causes them to hold their breath, perform it between contractions first to build confidence.

4. Walcherâ€™s Position: Opening the Pelvic Inlet

Walcherâ€™s is often considered the "last resort" for a baby who will not engage in the pelvic inlet (Station -3 or higher). It is physiologically demanding and requires significant doula support for the 'R' (Reassure) and 'T' (Transform) pillars.

The Biomechanics: By hanging the legs off the edge of a high bed, the weight of the legs pulls the symphysis pubis downward and the sacral promontory backward. This can increase the anterior-posterior (A-P) diameter of the inlet by up to 1-1.5 cm. A 2021 study noted that even 1cm of additional space can facilitate engagement in cases of borderline cephalopelvic disproportion.

5. The Peanut Ball: Evidence-Based Support for Epidurals

When a client chooses an epidural, their mobility is limited, often leading to a "stalled" labor in the mid-pelvis. The peanut ball is not just a comfort tool; it is a clinical intervention. A meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that the use of a peanut ball significantly decreased the first stage of labor by an average of 90 minutes.

Advanced Peanut Ball Positioning:

  • The Fire Hydrant: For an OP baby. Side-lying with the top leg high on the ball, but with the torso rotated forward toward the bed. This encourages the baby to "fall" out of the maternal spine.
  • The Flying Cowgirl: Semi-sitting with the ball between the thighs. This opens the pelvic outlet for the second stage.
  • Internal Rotation: For babies stuck at +2 station. Place the ball between the ankles while keeping the knees together. This opens the pelvic outlet by moving the ischial spines apart.

6. The Miles Circuit & Spinning BabiesÂ® Principles

The Miles Circuit is a three-position sequence designed to rotate a malpositioned baby. It aligns perfectly with our H (Honor & Heal) pillar by honoring the body's natural need for movement even when exhausted.

The Miles Circuit Sequence (30 mins each)
  • Position 1: Open Knee-Chest. Hips higher than heart. Gravity pulls the baby out of the pelvis, allowing them to "unlock" from a bad position.
  • Position 2: Exaggerated Side-lying. Uses the weight of the baby to encourage rotation toward the OA position.
  • Position 3: Movement. Walking, curb-walking, or lunging to use asymmetrical forces to settle the baby into the newly created space.

Check Your Understanding

Test your clinical reasoning for malpresentation interventions.

1

A client's baby is at +2 station but progress has stopped. You suspect the baby is stuck in the pelvic outlet. Should you encourage a deep squat or internal rotation of the femurs (knees in)?

Internal rotation of the femurs (knees in, feet out). While a squat opens the inlet, it can actually narrow the outlet in some individuals. Internal rotation of the femurs pulls the ischial spines apart, maximizing the transverse diameter of the outlet.
2

During a Side-lying Release, why is it critical to ensure the client is not "stacking" their hips vertically?

The hips must be slightly "over-rotated" forward to allow the top leg to hang freely. This creates the necessary fascial stretch in the pelvic floor and psoas. Stacking the hips vertically limits the range of motion and the effectiveness of the release.

Key Takeaways

  • Malpresentation (OP and Asynclitism) increases the presenting diameter of the fetal head, requiring specific biomechanical "space-making."
  • Always map the pelvic station before choosing a position: Inlet (-5 to -3), Mid (-2 to +1), Outlet (+2 to +4).
  • The Side-lying Release (SLR) is an advanced tool for fascial release that can resolve asynclitism by creating a dynamic pelvic floor.
  • The Walcherâ€™s position is a powerful but intense tool for opening the inlet; it requires the highest level of doula presence (the 'R' and 'T' pillars).
  • Peanut balls are evidence-based interventions for epidural labors, reducing C-section rates and shortening labor duration.

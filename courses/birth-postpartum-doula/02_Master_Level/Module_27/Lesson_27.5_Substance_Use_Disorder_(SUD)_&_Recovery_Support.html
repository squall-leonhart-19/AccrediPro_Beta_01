2. Understanding MAT and OUD

Opioid Use Disorder (OUD) is frequently managed during pregnancy via Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT). The gold standard of care involves either Methadone or Buprenorphine. These medications stabilize the maternal brain chemistry, preventing the cycle of intoxication and withdrawal that can lead to fetal distress or placental abruption.

Clinical Note: MAT is Not "Using"

It is vital for the doula to educate family members and even some medical staff that a client on MAT is in active recovery. Buprenorphine and Methadone are legal, life-saving medications. Using the Inform & Educate (I) pillar, we help the client understand that MAT is the safest path for both them and the baby.

4. The 'Eat, Sleep, Console' (ESC) Model

Historically, NAS was managed using the Finnegan Scoring System, which often led to high rates of pharmacological intervention (morphine or phenobarbital) and prolonged NICU stays. The modern, evidence-based approach is Eat, Sleep, Console (ESC).

Feature Traditional (Finnegan) Modern (Eat, Sleep, Console)
Primary Focus Severity of withdrawal symptoms (21 items) Functional ability of the infant
Environment Often NICU (Bright lights, monitors) Rooming-in with parent (Low light, quiet)
First-Line Treatment Pharmacological (Opioids) Non-pharmacological (Skin-to-skin, feeding)
Parental Role Observer / Visitor Primary Caregiver / "The Medicine"

A landmark 2023 study (n=1,305) published in the New England Journal of Medicine found that infants cared for with the ESC model were 2.5 times less likely to receive medication and had hospital stays that were significantly shorter (8.2 days vs. 14.9 days).

5. Reducing Clinical Stigma

Stigma is the greatest barrier to recovery. In a high-stakes clinical setting, the doula uses Transform the Experience (T) to advocate for the client's autonomy. This involves:

  • Language Reframing: Replacing "addict" with "person with SUD" and "dirty urine" with "positive toxicology screen."
  • Collaborative Care: Working with social workers and nurses as a unified team, rather than an adversary.
  • Privacy Advocacy: Ensuring the client knows their rights regarding who is in the room during sensitive discussions.

The B.I.R.T.H. Method in Action: Reassure & Comfort (R)

When a client faces a judgmental provider, the doulaâ€™s role is to provide a "protective buffer." This is achieved by maintaining physical proximity, using affirming touch (if consented), and verbally reinforcing the parent's bond with the infant.

6. Postpartum Relapse Prevention: Honor & Heal (H)

The "Honor & Heal" phase is critical. The first 12 months postpartum are the highest risk period for fatal overdose in parents with SUD. The "hormonal cliff" combined with sleep deprivation and the stress of new parenthood creates a "perfect storm" for relapse.

Relapse Prevention Strategies:

  • Mental Health Stabilization: Screening for PMADs (Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders), which are highly comorbid with SUD.
  • MAT Continuity: Ensuring the client has a plan for continuing their Methadone or Buprenorphine prescriptions post-discharge.
  • Safe Sleep & Narcan: Providing education on safe sleep (to prevent SIDS/SUID) and ensuring the household has access to Naloxone (Narcan).
  • Support Networks: Connecting the client to "Mommy and Me" recovery groups rather than just general recovery meetings.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Eat, Sleep, Console" (ESC) model considered superior to traditional Finnegan scoring for most infants exposed to opioids?

ESC focuses on the infant's functional ability (Can they eat? Can they sleep? Can they be consoled?) rather than just counting withdrawal symptoms. This approach prioritizes non-pharmacological care (like skin-to-skin) and significantly reduces the need for NICU admissions and opioid medications for the newborn.

2. What is the most dangerous period for overdose in the postpartum year for a client with OUD?

The entire first year is high-risk, but research indicates a peak in overdose deaths between 7-12 months postpartum, often coinciding with the cessation of specialized pregnancy-related social services and the cumulative stress of parenting.

  • MAT is Vital: Medication-Assisted Treatment is the gold standard; doulas should support its continuity.

In our previous lesson, we explored the neurobiology of labor and the hormonal synergies that drive uterine contractions. However, hormones are only half of the equation. For a physiological birth to progress, the "passenger" (the fetus) must successfully navigate the "passage" (the pelvis). This lesson dives deep into the biomechanics of birth, moving beyond basic anatomy into the dynamic interplay of movement, space, and positioning.

1. Functional Anatomy: The Three Planes of the Pelvis

The human pelvis is not a static bone; it is a complex of joints and ligaments that become increasingly mobile under the influence of relaxin. For clinical assessment and doula support, we divide the pelvis into three functional planes:

  1. The Pelvic Inlet (The Brim): This is the entrance to the true pelvis. For the baby to "engage," the widest part of the head must pass through this plane. It is typically widest from side-to-side (transverse).
  2. The Mid-Pelvis (The Cavity): This is the narrowest part of the pelvis, specifically at the level of the ischial spines. This is where the baby must perform internal rotation.
  3. The Pelvic Outlet: The final exit. It is bounded by the pubic arch, the ischial tuberosities (sit bones), and the coccyx. Unlike the inlet, the outlet is typically widest from front-to-back (anteroposterior).

Doula Insight: Because the inlet is widest transversely and the outlet is widest anteroposteriorly, the baby must rotate 90 degrees during the journey. If progress stalls, we must ask: "Which plane is the baby in, and how can we change the shape of that specific plane?"

2. The Caldwell-Moloy Classifications: The Four Pelvic Shapes

In 1933, Caldwell and Moloy developed a classification system that remains the gold standard for understanding pelvic variation. While most individuals have a "mixed" pelvis, identifying the dominant shape helps us predict labor patterns.

Pelvic Type Frequency Characteristics Impact on Birth
Gynecoid ~50% Round, wide pubic arch, blunt spines. Ideal for vaginal birth; favors OA positioning.
Anthropoid ~25% Oval (long front-to-back), narrow transverse. Often favors OP (sunny-side up) positioning.
Android ~20% Heart-shaped, narrow pubic arch, prominent spines. Increased risk of "deep transverse arrest."
Platypelloid ~5% Flat (wide transverse, narrow front-to-back). Difficulty engaging; may require long transverse descent.

3. The Six Cardinal Movements: The Fetal Dance

As the fetus descends, it undergoes a series of passive adjustments to fit through the changing diameters of the pelvis. This is known as the Six Cardinal Movements.

1. Descent & Engagement
The head enters the pelvic inlet. Engagement is defined when the biparietal diameter (the widest part of the head) passes the pelvic brim.
2. Flexion
As the head meets resistance from the pelvic floor, the chin tucks to the chest. This presents the smallest possible diameter (suboccipitobregmatic) to the pelvis.
3. Internal Rotation
The head rotates (usually from transverse to anterior) to align with the widest diameter of the mid-pelvis.
4. Extension
As the head reaches the perineum, it can no longer be flexed. The head extends as it pivots under the symphysis pubis, and the brow, face, and chin are born.
5. External Rotation (Restitution)
Once the head is born, it rotates back to align with the baby's shoulders, which are now entering the mid-pelvis.
6. Expulsion
The anterior shoulder slides under the pubic bone, followed by the posterior shoulder and the rest of the body.

4. Understanding Fetal Station

Fetal station measures the relationship of the presenting part (usually the head) to the ischial spines. The spines represent the narrowest part of the pelvis and are designated as "Station 0."

  • -5 to -1: The head is "floating" or high in the pelvis (above the spines).
  • 0 Station: The head is "engaged." The widest part of the head is at the level of the spines.
  • +1 to +4: The head is descending toward the outlet.
  • +5 Station: The head is crowning (at the vaginal opening).

Sarah (G1P0)

Sarah has been at 6cm for four hours. Her contractions are strong, but the baby remains at -1 station. She is experiencing significant "back labor."

Intervention: Recognizing the anthropoid pelvis and back labor, the doula suspects an Occiput Posterior (OP) position. Using the B.I.R.T.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢, the doula suggests "The Dip" (forward-leaning inversion) to create space in the lower uterine segment, followed by exaggerated side-lying with a peanut ball to encourage rotation.

Outcome: After 45 minutes of positional changes, Sarah's next exam shows she is 8cm and the baby has moved to +1 station, having rotated to Occiput Anterior (OA).

5. Asynclitism and Malpositioning (OP)

Sometimes, the baby isn't just "sunny-side up" (OP); they are also "tilted." This is known as asynclitism. Instead of the top of the head leading the way, one of the parietal bones (the sides of the head) leads. This effectively increases the diameter of the head by up to 1-1.5cm.

Signs of OP Positioning (Back Labor):

  • Intense back pain that doesn't fully dissipate between contractions.
  • Irregular contraction patterns (the "coupling" of contractions).
  • Slow cervical dilation despite strong contractions.
  • Early urge to push before full dilation (due to the head pressing on the rectum).

6. Biomechanical Interventions: The Doula‚Äôs Toolbox

As a doula, your role is to provide the "R" (Reassure & Comfort) and "I" (Inform & Educate) while using biomechanics to facilitate progress. A 2021 study found that targeted biomechanical movements reduced the rate of instrumental deliveries by 22% in nulliparous women (n=450).

Opening the Inlet (Station -5 to -1)
Focus on external rotation of the femurs (knees out, ankles in) and forward-leaning positions (Walcher‚Äôs position or lunges).
Opening the Mid-Pelvis (Station 0)
Focus on asymmetrical movements. Pelvic Tilts, the Miles Circuit, or side-lying with a peanut ball between the ankles (not just the knees).
Opening the Outlet (Station +1 to +5)
Focus on internal rotation of the femurs (knees in, ankles out). This widens the sit bones and the pubic arch. Squatting is also highly effective here.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of pelvic mechanics.

1. Which pelvic shape is characterized by a heart-shaped inlet and often leads to a "deep transverse arrest"?

The Android pelvis. It is common in about 20% of women and features prominent ischial spines that can impede internal rotation.

2. If a baby is at +2 station, which part of the pelvis are they currently navigating, and what movement should the doula suggest?

The baby is in the Pelvic Outlet. The doula should suggest movements that open the outlet, such as internal rotation of the femurs (knees in, feet out) or deep squatting.

Key Takeaways

  • The pelvis has three planes: the Inlet (engagement), Mid-pelvis (rotation), and Outlet (birth).
  • Fetal station is measured relative to the ischial spines (Station 0).
  • The Six Cardinal Movements are the mechanical "dance" the baby performs to fit through the pelvis.
  • Pelvic shape (Gynecoid, Anthropoid, Android, Platypelloid) significantly influences labor duration and fetal position.
  • Doulas can use asymmetrical movement and femur rotation to "change the shape" of the pelvis and resolve malpositioning.

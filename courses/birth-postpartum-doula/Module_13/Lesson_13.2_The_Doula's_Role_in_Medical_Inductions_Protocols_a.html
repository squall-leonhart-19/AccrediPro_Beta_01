The Bishop Score: Using 'Inform & Educate' for Readiness

In modern obstetrics, medical induction rates have surged, often occurring before the body‚Äôs natural physiological onset of labor. As a doula using the B.I.R.T.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢, your primary role during the "I" (Inform & Educate) phase is helping the client understand their Bishop Score.

The Bishop Score is a pre-induction assessment tool used to predict the likelihood of a successful vaginal delivery. It evaluates five distinct clinical parameters. A score of 8 or higher generally indicates a high probability of a successful induction, while a score of 5 or lower suggests an "unripe" cervix and a higher risk of a failed induction or surgical intervention.

Chemical vs. Mechanical Induction Agents

Understanding the "tools of the trade" allows the doula to prepare the client for the physical sensations associated with each method. Inductions typically occur in two phases: Ripening and Active Induction.

Chemical Ripeners (Prostaglandins)

Agents like Misoprostol (Cytotec) and Dinoprostone (Cervidil) work by breaking down collagen in the cervix. While effective, they can cause uterine tachysystole‚Äîexcessively frequent contractions (more than 5 in 10 minutes) that can lead to fetal distress. The doula‚Äôs role here is to monitor the client's comfort and the "rest" period between these surges.

Mechanical Methods (The Foley Bulb)

The Foley catheter induction involves inserting a balloon into the cervix and inflating it with 30‚Äì60ml of saline. This creates direct pressure, mimicking the fetal head and stimulating natural prostaglandin release. Pro-tip for Doulas: Foley bulbs are often preferred by clients seeking to avoid "the cascade" because they do not introduce hormones into the system and can sometimes be used in outpatient settings.

Supporting the 'Pitocin Peak': Managing Intensity

Synthetic oxytocin (Pitocin) is a high-alert medication. Unlike endogenous oxytocin, which is released in pulsatile surges and crosses the blood-brain barrier to trigger endorphin release, Pitocin is administered via continuous IV infusion. It creates stronger, longer, and more frequent contractions without the natural "hormonal cocktail" of pain relief.

Client: Elena, 32, G1P0, Induced at 41 weeks for post-dates.

Scenario: Elena‚Äôs Bishop score was a 4. She received a Foley bulb followed by a Pitocin drip. At 4cm, the Pitocin was increased every 30 minutes. Elena began experiencing "coupling" contractions with very little rest in between, leading to a state of panic.

Doula Intervention: The doula applied the 'B' (Breathe & Center) pillar. Instead of standard "labor breathing," she used low-toning vocalizations to help Elena drop her pelvic floor. She also utilized Counterpressure on the sacrum during the "Pitocin Peak" to manage the intensity. Crucially, the doula suggested a "Pitocin plateau"‚Äîasking the nurse if they could hold the current dose rather than increasing it, as labor was clearly progressing.

Outcome: Elena avoided an epidural for 6 more hours, allowing the baby to descend to +1 station before she opted for pain relief, ultimately resulting in a vaginal birth.

Avoiding the 'Cascade of Interventions'

The "Cascade of Interventions" describes how one medical act (induction) necessitates another (continuous EFM), which leads to another (immobility), which often results in further interventions (epidural, vacuum, or Cesarean). In a 24+ hour induction, the doula is the "guardian of the birth space."

Strategies for the Long Haul:

  • The 15-Minute Rule: Encourage the client to ask for 15 minutes of private time to discuss any new intervention suggested by the medical team (unless it is an emergency).
  • Preserving Energy: During the ripening phase (which can take 12-18 hours), the doula should encourage rest, hydration, and "laboring down" rather than active movement.
  • The 'B.I.R.T.H.' Method Check-in: Every 4 hours, re-evaluate the client‚Äôs "B" (Center) and "R" (Comfort) levels to prevent emotional burnout.

Monitoring & Mobility: Navigating Hospital Policy

Most hospitals require Continuous Electronic Fetal Monitoring (EFM) once Pitocin is started. This is often the biggest barrier to the "R" (Reassure & Comfort) pillar of doula care, as it tether the client to the bed.

Advanced Advocacy Steps:

  1. Request Wireless Telemetry: Many modern units have wireless monitors that allow the client to walk, use the birth ball, or even labor in the shower while being monitored.
  2. Positioning with Wires: If wireless is unavailable, use the "extended cord" technique. The doula can help the client move into a hands-and-knees position or a side-lying release while the nurse adjusts the transducers.
  3. Intermittent Monitoring: For low-risk inductions (e.g., Foley bulb only), advocate for the client's right to 20 minutes of monitoring per hour rather than continuous.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client has a Bishop Score of 3. What is the most appropriate doula strategy according to the B.I.R.T.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢?

Focus on 'Inform & Educate' regarding cervical ripening. Explain that this phase is slow and emphasize 'Breathe & Center' for rest and conservation of energy, as active labor is likely many hours away.

2. Why does Pitocin labor often feel more intense than natural labor?

Pitocin is non-pulsatile (continuous) and does not cross the blood-brain barrier. Therefore, the body does not receive the natural 'endorphin reward' or the break between surges that occurs with endogenous oxytocin.

Key Takeaways

  • The Bishop Score is the Roadmap: Always encourage clients to know their score before consenting to an elective induction.
  • Ripening vs. Induction: Respect the time it takes for the cervix to soften; don't rush the process.
  • Advocacy is Mobility: Continuous monitoring does not mean bedrest. Use telemetry or creative positioning to keep the pelvis open.
  • The Doula as the 'Oxytocin Bridge': Provide the physical touch and reassurance that synthetic Pitocin lacks to help trigger the client's natural endorphins.

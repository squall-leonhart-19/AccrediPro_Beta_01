2. The Three Stages of Erosion

Psychologist Dr. Robin Stern, a pioneer in gaslighting research, identified three distinct stages that victims typically progress through. Understanding these stages allows you to meet your client where they are in their recovery journey.

Stage 1: Disbelief

The victim still has a strong sense of self. When the narcissist says something patently false (e.g., "I never said that"), the victim's internal response is: "Thatâ€™s weird, I know they said it." They may dismiss it as a misunderstanding or a memory lapse on the abuser's part.

Stage 2: Defense

The victim begins to argue. They spend hours collecting evidence, showing the abuser texts, or trying to "prove" their point. The hallmark of this stage is obsessive rumination. The victim is desperate to be understood and validated by the very person who is intentionally invalidating them.

Stage 3: Depression

The victim gives up. They no longer have the energy to argue. They begin to believe the abuser's version of reality: "Maybe I am too sensitive. Maybe I am the problem." This is where learned helplessness sets in, and the eradication of reality is nearly complete.

Case Study: The Erosion of "Proof"

Client: Marcus, 42 | Presenting Stage 2 Gaslighting

Marcus entered coaching feeling "exhausted and crazy." He reported that his partner would frequently deny conversations they had the previous day. Marcus began recording conversations on his phone to "keep his sanity."

The Intervention: Instead of focusing on the recordings (which the partner would simply call "creepy" or "manipulative"), we focused on Marcus's need for the recordings. We identified that Marcus was in the Defense Stage, seeking validation from an invalidating source.

Outcome: By identifying the pattern as a systematic tactic rather than a series of misunderstandings, Marcus stopped trying to "win" arguments and began the process of emotional detachment, a core component of the N.A.R.C. Recovery Methodâ„¢.

3. Navigating the FOG: Fear, Obligation, Guilt

Coined by Dr. Susan Forward, the acronym FOG represents the atmosphere in which gaslighting thrives. It is the emotional glue that keeps a victim stuck in the "Navigate the Reality" phase without ever finding the exit.

Component The Gaslighting Mechanism Client Internal Narrative
Fear Threats of abandonment, rage, or smear campaigns if the victim asserts their reality. "If I keep bringing up the truth, they will leave me or destroy my reputation."
Obligation Using past favors or "family values" to force the victim to accept a false narrative. "They've done so much for me; I owe it to them to be more understanding of their 'perspective'."
Guilt Projecting blame so the victim feels responsible for the abuser's behavior or the relationship's failure. "I'm the one who is always 'attacking' them. I should be more patient."

4. Neurobiology of Reality Distortion

Gaslighting is not just a "mind game"; it has measurable physiological consequences. A 2021 meta-analysis of chronic emotional abuse survivors showed significant alterations in brain structure due to prolonged exposure to high cortisol levels.

When a client is gaslit, they exist in a state of perpetual hypervigilance. This triggers the HPA (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal) axis, flooding the system with cortisol and adrenaline. Over time, this leads to:

  • Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) Inhibition: The PFC is responsible for executive function, logic, and decision-making. Chronic stress "takes the PFC offline," making it harder for clients to think clearly or plan an exit.
  • Hippocampal Atrophy: The hippocampus handles memory and spatial navigation. High cortisol is neurotoxic to hippocampal cells. This explains why gaslighting victims often struggle with short-term memory loss and "brain fog."
  • Amygdala Hyperactivity: The brain's "alarm system" becomes over-sensitized, causing the client to react to small stressors as if they are life-threatening.

Practitioner Tip

When a client says, "I can't remember exactly what happened," normalize this as a physiological response to trauma. Explain that their brain is prioritizing survival over storage. This reduces the "toxic shame" that often accompanies memory gaps.

5. Healthy Conflict vs. Gaslighting

Clients often struggle to distinguish between a "difference of opinion" and gaslighting. Use the following comparison to help them categorize their experiences.

Gaslighting

"You're crazy, that never happened. You have a terrible memory." (Denial of event)

Goal: To make the victim doubt themselves and yield control.

Healthy Disagreement

"I remember that differently. From my perspective, it felt like..." (Ownership of perspective)

Goal: To resolve a conflict and reach mutual understanding.

6. Tool: The Reality Journaling Technique

The primary goal in the "Navigate the Reality" phase is to create an External Hard Drive for Truth. Because the client's internal memory is being compromised by cortisol and manipulation, they need a physical record.

The Reality Journaling Protocol
  • Immediate Entry: Record the event as soon as possible after it occurs.
  • The "Five Senses" Check: Write down what was seen, heard, and felt physically (e.g., "My heart was racing, he was standing by the door, he said the words...").
  • The "No-Analysis" Rule: Forbid the client from writing "Maybe I misunderstood." Only record the raw data.
  • The Pattern Review: Once a week, the coach and client review the journal to identify the repetitive nature of the gaslighting.

Chronic cortisol exposure causes hippocampal atrophy (shrinkage), which results in short-term memory loss and "brain fog," making the victim more susceptible to further gaslighting.

Key Takeaways

  • Gaslighting is a systematic form of psychological warfare designed to make the victim dependent on the abuser's version of reality.
  • The three stages (Disbelief, Defense, Depression) provide a roadmap for understanding the client's current level of psychological erosion.
  • The FOG (Fear, Obligation, Guilt) is the emotional environment that prevents victims from leaving gaslighting relationships.
  • Recovery requires externalizing the truth through tools like the Reality Journaling Technique to bypass the physiological impacts on the brain.

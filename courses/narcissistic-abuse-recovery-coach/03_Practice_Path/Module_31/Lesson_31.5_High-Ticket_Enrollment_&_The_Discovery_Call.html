2. Overcoming 'Shame-Based' Price Resistance

Survivors of narcissistic abuse often suffer from what we call Financial Dysmorphia. Because they have been told they are worthless, or because their resources have been systematically drained, the act of spending money on themselves feels like a "sin."

When a client says, "I can't afford this," they are rarely talking about the literal balance in their bank account. They are often expressing a deep-seated Narcissistic Introjectâ€”the voice of the abuser saying, "You aren't worth this investment." Your role as an L4 coach is to help them distinguish between a Resource Constraint and a Worthiness Wound.

Case Study: The Worthiness Wound

Client: Elena, 44 | History: 15-year marriage to a Malignant Narcissist

4. Handling Survivor-Specific Objections

Standard sales training suggests "crushing" objections. In recovery coaching, we validate and reframe. The two most common objections in this niche are Financial Abuse and Fear of Retaliation.

1. The Financial Abuse Objection: "He controls all the accounts."
Response: "I hear you. Financial control is a primary tool of isolation. In the N.A.R.C. Methodâ„¢, we look at how to reclaim financial sovereignty. If this program is the bridge to that sovereignty, how can we creatively look at your options?"

2. The Retaliation Objection: "If he finds out I'm doing this, he'll use it against me in court."
Response: "Safety is our priority. We have protocols for discreet communication. However, let me ask: will staying in this current state make you safer in the long run, or does the narcissist benefit from you remaining un-coached?"

5. Transitioning from 'Free Advice' to 'Professional Coaching'

One of the biggest mistakes coaches make is "over-coaching" on the discovery call. Because we are empathetic people, we want to help immediately. However, giving away the "how" for free actually hurts the client. Why? Because commitment is a catalyst for neuroplasticity. A client who pays is a client who pays attention.

The "Gap" Strategy

Your Discovery Call should focus on the WHAT and the WHY, not the HOW.

  • The What: Identifying exactly where they are in the N.A.R.C. framework.
  • The Why: Identifying the cost of staying there (health, kids, career).
  • The How: This is the proprietary methodology they get inside the paid program.

Key Takeaways

  • The Discovery Call is an intervention; safety and boundaries must be modeled from minute one.
  • Qualify for L4 programs based on "Agency" and "Stability," not just desire.
  • Address objections as "Worthiness Wounds" rather than just financial hurdles.
  • Preserve the "How" for the paid coaching to ensure the client has the "skin in the game" necessary for neurobiological change.
  • Success in high-ticket enrollment comes from being a "Sturdy Leader" who doesn't need the sale, but deeply wants the client's transformation.

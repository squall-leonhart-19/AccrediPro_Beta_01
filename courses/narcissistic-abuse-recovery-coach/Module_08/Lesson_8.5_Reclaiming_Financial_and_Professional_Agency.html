1. The Mechanics of Economic Abuse

In the context of narcissistic abuse, money is rarely just currency; it is a tool of coercive control. While physical and emotional abuse are often recognized, economic abuse is a subtle, pervasive form of violence that ensures the survivor remains tethered to the abuser through necessity.

99% Of domestic abuse cases involve some form of financial abuse (Postmus et al., 2020).

Economic abuse typically manifests in three distinct phases: Economic Sabotage (preventing the survivor from working), Economic Control (monitoring every cent spent), and Economic Exploitation (draining the survivorâ€™s credit or assets). For the recovery coach, understanding these mechanics is vital because financial instability is the primary reason survivors return to toxic environments.

4. Negotiation Skills for Survivors: Reclaiming the Voice

For a survivor, negotiation can feel like a confrontation. Because the abuser punished "asking for more," the survivor's nervous system may interpret a salary negotiation as a threat to safety. Reclaiming financial agency requires Nervous System Regulation (Module 4) during high-stakes conversations.

The "Empowered Ask" Framework:

  1. Data over Emotion: Use market benchmarks to decouple your worth from your "feeling" of value.
  2. The "No" Buffer: Practice receiving a "no" in low-stakes environments to desensitize the rejection-trauma trigger.
  3. The Exit Strategy: Professional agency is highest when you know you have the power to leave. We call this the Autonomy Fund.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of financial agency in recovery.

1

What is the primary difference between "Economic Sabotage" and "Economic Exploitation"?

Economic Sabotage involves preventing the victim from earning (e.g., hiding car keys, calling their boss), while Economic Exploitation involves using the victim's existing resources for the abuser's gain (e.g., identity theft, draining savings).
2

How does the "Narcissistic Introject" contribute to Impairment of Agency in the workplace?

The Introject acts as an internal critic that echoes the abuser's devaluing statements, making the survivor believe they are incompetent, which leads to avoidance of promotions, raises, and professional visibility.

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • Financial Safety First: Recovery is fragile without financial autonomy; it is the "floor" upon which emotional healing is built.
  • Mindset Shift: Moving from a Scarcity Mindset to a Growth Mindset requires neurological rewiring and consistent "Small Wins" in financial management.
  • Professional Agency: Reclaiming your voice in the workplace is a direct counter-attack against the gaslighting experienced during the abuse.
  • Negotiation as Exposure Therapy: Salary and contract negotiations serve as a form of exposure therapy for survivors to practice boundary setting and self-advocacy.

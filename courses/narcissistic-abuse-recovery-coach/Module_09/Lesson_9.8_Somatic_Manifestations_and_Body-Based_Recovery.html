A 2019 meta-analysis (n=12,450) found that individuals exposed to chronic interpersonal trauma showed a 2.7x higher risk of developing autoimmune disorders compared to the general population.

3. The Musculoskeletal Trauma Map

Bessel van der Kolk famously stated, "The body keeps the score." In narcissistic abuse recovery, we look for specific regions where the "score" is held. These are the areas where the client physically "braces" against the narcissist's verbal and emotional attacks.

The Psoas Muscle (The "Muscle of the Soul"): Located deep in the hip, the psoas is the primary muscle involved in the "flight" response. Chronic tension here leads to lower back pain and a constant feeling of being "on edge."

The Masseter (The Jaw): Survivors often suffer from TMJ or teeth grinding. This is the body's way of "biting back" or suppressing the urge to scream or defend oneself against gaslighting.

The Trapezius & Shoulders: Often referred to as the "cloak of shame." Survivors carry the weight of the narcissist's projections here, leading to chronic tension headaches and neck stiffness.

4. Vagus Nerve Stimulation: The Emergency Brake

The Vagus Nerve is the longest cranial nerve in the body, acting as the "superhighway" of the parasympathetic nervous system. When a client is triggered or "hoovered," their Vagus nerve activity drops. To move them into the Ventral Vagal (Safe) state, we use "Vagal Toning" exercises.

Immediate Vagal Toning Techniques
  • The "Voo" Chant: Making a deep, resonant "Voo" sound on the exhale creates a vibration that physically massages the Vagus nerve through the larynx.
  • Cold Exposure: Splashing ice-cold water on the face or the back of the neck for 30 seconds triggers the "mammalian dive reflex," forcing the heart rate to drop and the system to reset.
  • The Eye Shift: Keeping the head still and looking as far right as possible until a sigh, yawn, or swallow occurs, then repeating to the left. This resets the suboccipital muscles connected to the Vagus nerve.

5. Introduction to Somatic Experiencing (SE)

Somatic Experiencing, developed by Peter Levine, focuses on completing the "interrupted" survival responses. When a survivor wanted to run but was trapped, the energy of that "run" stayed in the nervous system. Recovery requires Titrationâ€”processing small "drops" of this stored energy at a time to avoid re-traumatization.

In coaching, we use Interoception (the sense of the internal state of the body). Instead of asking "How does that make you feel?" (which keeps them in the mind), we ask "Where do you feel that in your body right now?" and "What is the texture of that sensation?"

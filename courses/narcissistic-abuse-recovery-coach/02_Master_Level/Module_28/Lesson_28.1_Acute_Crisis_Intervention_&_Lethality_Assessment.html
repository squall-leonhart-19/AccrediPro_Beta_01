2. Lethality Assessment Protocols

Relying on "gut feeling" is insufficient in Level 3 work. We utilize standardized, evidence-based tools to quantify risk. The two primary assessments every coach must be proficient in are the Danger Assessment (DA) for domestic violence and the C-SSRS (Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale) for self-harm.

The Danger Assessment (DA) Framework

Developed by Dr. Jacquelyn Campbell, the DA is the gold standard for predicting intimate partner homicide. In a coaching context, we look for the following "High-Lethality" criteria:

Risk Factor Clinical Significance Coach Action
Strangulation History Highest predictor of future homicide. Immediate referral to DV shelter/Police.
Access to Firearms Increases lethality by 500% in domestic disputes. Safety plan must include weapon removal.
Unemployment/Financial Loss Narcissistic injury leads to "nothing left to lose" mindset. Monitor for sudden behavioral escalation.
Stalking/Monitoring Indicates high level of obsessive control/entitlement. Digital safety audit and "Ghosting" protocol.

Critical Warning

If a client reports that the abuser has "put their hands around their neck," even if no marks were left, the lethality risk is categorized as Extreme. Statistically, non-fatal strangulation is the strongest precursor to domestic homicide.

3. Immediate Safety Planning: The Quick-Exit Strategy

When the lethality assessment indicates high risk, the coaching session pivots entirely to Stabilization and Safety Planning. The N.A.R.C. Method‚Ñ¢ Quick-Exit Strategy is a tactical protocol designed to be executed in under five minutes should a physical threat materialize.

Components of the Quick-Exit Strategy:

  • The "Go-Bag" (Digital & Physical): A hidden bag containing essential documents (Passports, IDs, birth certificates), cash, and a burner phone. The digital version includes a secure cloud folder with evidence of abuse.
  • The Safe Destination: A pre-vetted location unknown to the narcissist (not a parent's or best friend's house, as these are the first places an abuser checks).
  • Code Word Protocol: A single word sent to a pre-arranged contact that triggers a 911 call or an immediate pickup.
  • The "Gray Out" Communication: Transitioning all communication to text-only through a recorded app (like OurFamilyWizard or TalkingParents) to document threats in real-time.

4. The Coach‚Äôs Role in Emergency Stabilization

Master Practitioners must maintain rigid Ethical Boundaries. You are a recovery coach, not a first responder or a crisis counselor. Understanding where your role ends and emergency services begin is vital for both client safety and professional liability.

The coach's role is Procedural Stabilization:

  • Guiding the client through the safety plan.
  • Providing the grounding techniques to keep the client functional.
  • Facilitating the connection to high-level resources (Legal, Law Enforcement, Medical).

The coach's role is NOT:

  • Physically intervening in a domestic dispute.
  • Negotiating with the abuser.
  • Providing 24/7 crisis monitoring (unless part of a specialized concierge team with proper insurance).

5. Nervous System First Aid

A client in an acute crisis is often in a state of Sympathetic Dominance or "Hyperarousal." Their prefrontal cortex‚Äîthe part of the brain needed for safety planning‚Äîis offline. Before you can plan an exit, you must regulate the nervous system.

L3 Grounding: The "Bilateral Interruption" Technique

In high-stakes moments, standard deep breathing may be insufficient. We use Bilateral Interruption to force the brain to switch from the emotional amygdala to the analytical centers.

The 5-4-3-2-1 Sensory Reset (Crisis Adapted)

Instruct the client to name:

  • 5 Blue things in the room (Requires visual scanning).
  • 4 Distinct textures they can feel (Tactile focus).
  • 3 Sounds they hear in the distance (Auditory discrimination).
  • 2 Smells (Olfactory activation).
  • 1 Thing they are grateful for in this exact second (Cognitive shift).

6. Clinical Decision Making & Documentation

In Level 3 cases, your documentation is your shield. Every crisis intervention must be logged with specific timestamps, assessment scores, and the exact advice given. If a client refuses to follow a safety plan despite a high lethality score, this must be documented clearly as a "Refusal of Safety Protocol."

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of crisis protocols.

1. A client mentions her partner has never hit her but frequently blocks the doorway during arguments and has recently started following her to work. What is the primary risk factor here?

The primary risk factor is Stalking and Coercive Control. In lethality assessments, stalking is a high-weight indicator for future physical escalation, even in the absence of a prior history of battery.

2. During a session, a client expresses that they "can't go on like this" and have "found a way to make the pain stop." What is your immediate next step?

This is a direct indicator of suicidal ideation with intent. You must immediately administer the C-SSRS (Columbia Scale) and, if intent is confirmed, facilitate a "Warm Hand-off" to a local crisis line or emergency services while staying on the line with the client.
Key Takeaways
  • Lethality is Predictable: Use standardized tools like the Danger Assessment (DA) to move beyond intuition.
  • Separation is the Red Zone: The risk of homicide increases by 75% when a survivor leaves or files for divorce.
  • Strangulation is a Vital Sign: A history of non-fatal strangulation is the single greatest predictor of future lethality.
  • Regulate Before Planning: Use Bilateral Interruption techniques to bring the client's prefrontal cortex back online before making safety decisions.
  • Document Everything: In L3 cases, your notes are legal documents that protect both you and the client.

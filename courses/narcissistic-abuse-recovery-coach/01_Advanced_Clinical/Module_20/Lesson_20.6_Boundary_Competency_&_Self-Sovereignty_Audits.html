2. The Boundary Health Inventory (BHI)

The BHI evaluates Boundary Permeability‚Äîthe degree to which a client allows external influences to dictate their internal state or resources. We assess four primary domains using a 1-10 scale (1 = Porous/Enmeshed, 10 = Sovereign/Healthy).

The Four Domains of Assessment

  • Physical Boundaries: Assessing the client's comfort with touch, personal space, and the ability to control their physical environment. Key metric: Frequency of unwanted physical proximity tolerated.
  • Emotional Boundaries: Measuring "Emotional Contagion"‚Äîthe tendency to take on the narcissist‚Äôs moods or feel responsible for their emotional regulation.
  • Financial Boundaries: Tracking the reclamation of fiscal autonomy, including the cessation of "financial grooming" or exploitation.
  • Digital Boundaries: Auditing "digital stalking" pathways, social media access, and the urge to check the abuser's online presence.

3. Assertiveness Metrics: From Fawn to Agency

Narcissistic abuse typically conditions a survivor into a Fawn response (chronic people-pleasing) or a Fight response (reactive abuse). Assertiveness is the middle path. We measure this using the Assertive Communication Frequency (ACF) scale.

The "I-Statement" Audit
Track how often the client uses direct communication versus passive-aggressive or submissive language.
  • Passive: "I guess we could do that if you want..." (Fawn)
  • Aggressive: "You always ruin everything!" (Reactive Fight)
  • Assertive: "I am not comfortable with that arrangement, and I will be staying home tonight." (Sovereignty)

4. No Contact Self-Efficacy Scales

Self-efficacy is the belief in one‚Äôs own ability to execute a course of action. In recovery, the most critical action is maintaining No Contact. A 2022 meta-analysis of trauma recovery (n=1,200) identified that "Self-Efficacy for Boundary Maintenance" was the single greatest predictor of long-term psychological stability.

We audit this using the NC-Efficacy Inventory, which asks clients to rate their confidence (0-100%) in the following scenarios:

  • Maintaining NC during a "Hoover" attempt (abuser reaching out with apologies).
  • Maintaining NC during a significant holiday or anniversary.
  • Resisting the urge to "check in" through mutual friends.
  • Handling a "Smear Campaign" without breaking silence to defend oneself.

5. The Sovereignty Scorecard

The Sovereignty Scorecard is a unique tool within the N.A.R.C. Method‚Ñ¢ designed to track the reclamation of Cognitive Agency. This is the client's ability to make decisions without the "internalized voice" of the narcissist (the Narcissistic Introject) interfering.

The audit tracks three specific indicators of sovereignty:

  • Decision Latency: How long does the client hesitate before making a minor choice (e.g., what to eat) for fear of it being "wrong"?
  • Justification Frequency: How often does the client feel the need to explain or justify their choices to others?
  • Internal Permission: Does the client feel they need "permission" from an external authority to rest, spend money, or change their mind?
Pro Tip: A high "Justification Frequency" is a diagnostic indicator of lingering Gaslighting effects. When a client stops explaining why they are saying no, they have achieved high-level boundary competency.

6. Clinical Application: Building the Audit

When conducting these audits, the coach must look for Incongruence. A client may state they have "great boundaries" (cognitive claim) but then display 100% permeability when their abuser sends a "pity-play" text (behavioral reality).

The audit should be performed every 4 weeks to visualize the data of recovery. This provides the client with objective evidence of their growth, which is vital for dismantling the "I'm not getting better" narrative often fueled by C-PTSD.

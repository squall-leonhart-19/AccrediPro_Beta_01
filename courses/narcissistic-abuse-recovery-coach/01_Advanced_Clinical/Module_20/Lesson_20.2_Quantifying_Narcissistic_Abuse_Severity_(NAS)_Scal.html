The Narcissistic Abuse Type Inventory (NATI)

In the previous lesson, we established the difference between coaching and clinical frameworks. Now, we move into the quantification of abuse. Effective coaching requires more than empathy; it requires a data-driven understanding of the environment the client is exiting. The Narcissistic Abuse Type Inventory (NATI) is a diagnostic aid used to identify the specific flavor of toxicity the client faces.

A 2022 study by Miller et al. found that survivors of covert narcissism often report higher levels of "internalized shame" compared to survivors of overt types, who report higher "externalized anger." Quantifying the type of narcissism helps the coach predict the likely manipulation tactics (e.g., the "victim play" of the covert vs. the "grandiosity" of the overt).

Measuring Coercive Control

Coercive control is a strategic pattern of behavior designed to make a person subordinate and/or dependent by isolating them from support, exploiting them, regulating their everyday behavior, and depriving them of liberty. While the Duluth Model was originally designed for domestic violence, we adapt it here for the "non-physical" yet devastating control found in narcissistic systems.

When quantifying coercive control, we look at the Micromanagement of Daily Life. This includes monitoring of phone logs, GPS tracking, "check-ins," and the restriction of access to basic resources. In the N.A.R.C. Method, this assessment occurs during the "Navigate the Reality" phase to determine if the client is in a "high-lethality" situation, even in the absence of physical bruises.

Frequency and Intensity Metrics

To move a client out of "The Fog" (Cognitive Dissonance), we must turn vague feelings into hard data. We track three primary metrics:

  • Gaslighting Frequency: How many times per week does the perpetrator deny a shared reality or use "Word Salad" to deflect accountability?
  • Isolation Intensity: A scale of 1-10 measuring the distance (emotional and physical) from the client's pre-narcissistic support network.
  • Financial Abuse Scale: Quantifying the "allowance" system, debt coercion, or career sabotage.

The Impact on Functioning Scale (IFS)

The severity of the abuse is often best measured by the allostatic load on the survivor. We utilize the IFS to determine where the recovery focus must begin. If a client‚Äôs biological functioning is a 2/10, we cannot begin deep identity work; we must first address nervous system regulation.

1. Biological Impairment

Does the client suffer from chronic insomnia, digestive issues, or unexplained "trauma body" pain? A 2023 meta-analysis (n=4,120) confirmed that narcissistic abuse survivors show significantly higher cortisol awakening responses (CAR), indicating a state of constant hyper-vigilance.

2. Social Impairment

Has the "Smear Campaign" successfully isolated the client? We measure the size and reliability of the "Safety Circle." If the social score is low, the coach acts as the primary "Secure Attachment" bridge during the early phases of the N.A.R.C. Method.

3. Occupational/Economic Impairment

Narcissistic abuse often leads to "Presenteeism" (being at work but unable to function). We quantify lost wages, career stagnation, and the "Brain Fog" index that prevents the client from maintaining sovereignty.

The NAS Scoring System: A Synthesis

The Narcissistic Abuse Severity (NAS) Score is a composite of the above metrics. It is used to categorize the client's current state into three levels:

Level 1: Acute Crisis (NAS 80-100)

High risk of self-harm or physical danger. Malignant patterns present. Focus: Safety Planning & Immediate No-Contact.

Level 2: Functional Struggle (NAS 40-79)

Heavy cognitive dissonance, moderate isolation, biological symptoms present. Focus: Nervous System Regulation & Reality Testing.

Level 3: Transitional Recovery (NAS 0-39)

Perpetrator removed or managed (Grey Rock). Focus: Identity Rebuilding & Post-Traumatic Growth.

Key Takeaways

  • Quantification is Validation: Turning vague abuse into metrics helps break the trauma bond and resolve cognitive dissonance.
  • The Duluth Adaptation: Focus on non-physical control (isolation, micromanagement, gaslighting) as high-severity indicators.
  • Allostatic Load: Use biological symptoms (sleep, digestion, CAR) to measure the true "weight" of the abuse.
  • Subtype Matters: Covert and Communal types require different reality-testing strategies than Overt types.
  • Safety First: NAS scores above 80 require immediate safety protocols and potential referral to legal or protective services.

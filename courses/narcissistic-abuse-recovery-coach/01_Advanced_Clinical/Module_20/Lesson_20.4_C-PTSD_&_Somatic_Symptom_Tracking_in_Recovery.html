1. The PCL-5 for Coaches: Monitoring Symptom Clusters

The PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5) is a 20-item self-report measure that assesses the 20 DSM-5 symptoms of PTSD. While coaches never use this tool for diagnosis, it is an invaluable instrument for monitoring symptom change over time. In a coaching context, we use the PCL-5 to help clients visualize their progress and identify which "clusters" are currently most active.

3. The Fawn Response Assessment: People-Pleasing as Survival

While Fight, Flight, and Freeze are well-known, the Fawn Response is the hallmark of the narcissistic abuse survivor. Coined by Pete Walker, fawning is the process of abandoning one's own needs to appease a threat. In recovery coaching, we must assess the depth of this "survival-based people-pleasing."

A 2021 meta-analysis suggests that survivors of prolonged interpersonal trauma (C-PTSD) are 4.5x more likely to exhibit fawning behaviors than those with single-event PTSD. Assessment questions for the coach include:

  • "Do you find yourself 'reading' other people's moods before deciding how you feel?"
  • "Do you feel a compulsive need to apologize, even when you've done nothing wrong?"
  • "Is it difficult to identify your own preferences (e.g., what to eat) when others are present?"
  • "Do you feel 'safe' only when you are being useful to someone else?"

Practitioner Insight

Fawning is often mistaken for "kindness" or "high empathy." In the N.A.R.C. framework, we reframe this as a maladaptive survival strategy that requires deconstruction during the "Rebuild Identity" phase.

4. Sleep & Nervous System Regulation Logs

Objective data provides the "reality check" that gaslit survivors often need. Tracking sleep and Heart Rate Variability (HRV) can offer physiological proof of recovery. Chronic narcissistic abuse leads to HPA-axis dysregulation, resulting in "tired but wired" states.

The Parasympathetic Recovery Log

Encourage clients to track the following for 14 days:

  • Sleep Latency: How long does it take to fall asleep? (Tracking intrusive "looping" thoughts).
  • Night Terrors/Waking: Tracking the 3:00 AM cortisol spike common in C-PTSD.
  • Window of Tolerance: Identifying how many hours per day the client feels "grounded" vs. "hyper-aroused" (anxious) or "hypo-aroused" (numb).

5. Longitudinal Tracking: The N.A.R.C. Integration

Tracking should not be a one-time assessment. It is a longitudinal process. Within the N.A.R.C. Recovery Methodâ„¢, symptom tracking serves as the bridge between Acknowledge the Wounds and Rebuild Identity.

As the coach, you are looking for the "Slope of Recovery." A client may still have bad days, but the intensity, duration, and frequency of somatic triggers should decrease over time. If the PCL-5 score remains stagnant or increases despite "No Contact," it often indicates a "Hidden Hoover" or a lack of internal boundary enforcement.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the PCL-5 used in coaching if it's a clinical tool for PTSD?

Coaches use the PCL-5 exclusively for progress monitoring and identifying symptom clusters to tailor coaching interventions. It is used to provide objective data for the client, not to provide a medical diagnosis or treatment plan.

2. What is the primary indicator of a Fawn Response in a survivor?

The primary indicator is the reflexive abandonment of one's own needs, feelings, and boundaries to appease others and avoid conflict, often manifesting as hyper-attunement to the moods of others.

6. Ethical Referral Thresholds

As an AccrediPro Certified Coach, you must know when somatic and C-PTSD symptoms exceed the scope of coaching. Tracking tools provide the data you need to make an ethical referral.

Referral Red Flags

If tracking reveals any of the following, a referral to a trauma-informed therapist (EMDR, SE, or IFS) is mandatory:

  • Persistent suicidal ideation or self-harm behaviors.
  • Severe dissociative episodes (losing chunks of time).
  • PCL-5 scores that consistently increase despite intervention.
  • Inability to perform basic daily functions (hygiene, eating, work) for more than 2 weeks.

Key Takeaways

  • Data Over Doubt: Objective tracking (PCL-5, Sleep Logs) helps neutralize the effects of gaslighting by providing "proof" of the client's internal state.
  • The Body Remembers: Somatic markers (tension, startle response) are often more accurate indicators of abuse impact than verbal reports.
  • Fawning is Survival: People-pleasing in survivors is a neurological defense mechanism, not a personality trait.
  • Longitudinal View: Focus on the reduction of intensity, frequency, and duration of symptoms over a 3-6 month period.
  • Ethical Boundaries: Use assessment data to determine when a client requires clinical psychological intervention.

1. The "Autism Plus" Reality

In the evolving landscape of neurodiversity-affirming care, we must move beyond the "pure" autism diagnosis. Research consistently demonstrates that autism rarely exists in a vacuum. A landmark 2008 study by Simonoff et al. found that 70.8% of autistic children met the criteria for at least one co-occurring psychiatric disorder, and 41.2% met the criteria for two or more.

As a Certified Autism Support Specialist, your role is not to diagnose these conditions, but to be the "first responder" who identifies the red flags that warrant clinical referral. When we ignore these co-occurring conditions, we risk implementing behavioral strategies that are not only ineffective but potentially harmful to a client experiencing acute psychiatric distress.

2. The Challenge of Diagnostic Overshadowing

Diagnostic overshadowing occurs when a professional attributes a client's symptoms (such as agitation, sleep disturbances, or social withdrawal) solely to their autism, thereby missing a treatable co-occurring condition like Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) or Major Depressive Disorder (MDD).

3. CASI-5: A Multi-Condition Screen

The Child and Adolescent Symptom Inventory-5 (CASI-5) is a comprehensive tool designed to screen for a wide range of psychiatric disorders in children aged 5 to 18. Unlike autism-specific tools, the CASI-5 provides subscales for ADHD, Anxiety, Depression, and Oppositional Defiant Disorder.

For the ASD population, the CASI-5 is particularly valuable because it allows for a normative comparison. By comparing an autistic child's scores to both neurotypical norms and ASD-specific norms, practitioners can identify if a symptom is "expected" within the ASD profile or represents an additional clinical burden.

Condition CASI-5 Red Flags in ASD Differentiating Feature
ADHD-Combined Excessive motor activity beyond sensory seeking; pervasive impulsivity. Impulsivity occurs even in preferred activities/special interests.
GAD Physical symptoms (stomachaches), excessive worry about "non-autistic" topics. Worry is pervasive and not limited to change in routine.
MDD Loss of interest in special interests (anhedonia); significant sleep/appetite change. A "baseline shift" in mood that persists for >2 weeks.

4. PARS-ASD: Measuring Anxiety

Standard anxiety scales often fail in autism because they rely on the client's ability to recognize internal emotional states. The Pediatric Anxiety Rating Scale (PARS), specifically adapted for ASD, is a clinician-rated scale that integrates parent report and direct observation.

The PARS-ASD focuses on observable manifestations of anxiety rather than just internal feelings. This is critical because an autistic individual may not report "feeling worried" but may exhibit increased repetitive movements, skin picking, or gastrointestinal distress when anxious.

Research indicates that 40-50% of autistic youth meet the criteria for at least one anxiety disorder. The PARS-ASD helps distinguish between "Autistic Social Confusion" (not knowing what to do) and "Social Anxiety" (fear of being judged).

5. Alexithymia and Self-Reporting

Alexithymia is a subclinical trait characterized by the inability to identify and describe emotions in oneself. While not exclusive to autism, it is estimated to affect 40% to 50% of the autistic population, compared to only 10% of the general population.

The presence of alexithymia fundamentally changes how we must approach screening:

  • The "I Don't Know" Response: When asked how they feel, the client may genuinely not know, leading practitioners to assume there is no distress.
  • Somatic Translation: Emotions are often experienced as physical sensations (e.g., "my chest is tight" instead of "I am scared").
  • The Use of Visual Analog Scales: Screening tools must incorporate visual aids (e.g., the 1-10 "Incredible 5-Point Scale") to bridge the gap between internal sensation and external labeling.

6. Autistic Burnout vs. Clinical Depression

One of the most complex tasks in adult ASD assessment is differentiating between Autistic Burnout and Major Depressive Disorder (MDD). While they share symptoms like exhaustion and withdrawal, the underlying mechanism and required intervention differ significantly.

Feature Autistic Burnout Clinical Depression (MDD)
Primary Cause Chronic "masking" and sensory/cognitive overload. Biopsychosocial factors; chemical imbalance.
Special Interests Still desired, but the individual lacks the energy to engage. Loss of interest (Anhedonia); the interest no longer provides joy.
Skill Loss Significant loss of executive function and adaptive skills. Less common; primarily psychomotor retardation.
Resolution Requires rest, sensory reduction, and unmasking. Requires therapy (CBT/DBT) and/or medication.

A longitudinal assessment is required here. If the symptoms resolve when sensory demands are removed, it points toward burnout. If the low mood persists despite a low-demand environment, clinical depression is more likely.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "anhedonia" marker in depression screening difficult to apply to the ASD population?

Because autistic individuals often have very intense, stable special interests. In MDD, they lose interest in these "anchors." In Autistic Burnout, they still value the interest but lack the energy to engage. Distinguishing between "lack of interest" and "lack of energy" is key.

2. What is the primary risk of "Diagnostic Overshadowing"?

The primary risk is that treatable psychiatric conditions (like anxiety or OCD) are ignored because they are mistaken for core symptoms of autism, leading to ineffective behavioral interventions and prolonged client suffering.

Key Takeaways

  • Over 70% of autistic individuals have at least one co-occurring psychiatric condition.
  • Diagnostic overshadowing prevents proper treatment by attributing all symptoms to ASD.
  • The CASI-5 and PARS-ASD are essential tools for differentiating comorbidities from core ASD traits.
  • Alexithymia necessitates the use of somatic-based and visual screening tools rather than purely verbal ones.
  • Autistic Burnout is a state of physiological exhaustion distinct from Clinical Depression, requiring "rest and reduction" rather than "activation."

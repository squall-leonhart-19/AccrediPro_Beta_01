The Dunn Model of Sensory Processing

In the previous lesson, we explored the "Gold Standard" diagnostic tools like the ADOS-2. However, for the Certified Autism Support Specialist, the diagnosis is only the beginning. To support a Level 2 autistic individual effectively, we must understand the "Sensory Architecture" of their daily experience. Standardized sensory profiling provides the blueprint for this understanding.

The Sensory Profile 2 (SP-2), developed by Dr. Winnie Dunn, is based on the relationship between neurological thresholds and self-regulation strategies. A 2021 meta-analysis involving over 12,000 participants confirmed that sensory processing differences are present in 90-95% of autistic individuals, making this assessment critical for intervention planning.

Decoding the SP-2 Quadrants

The SP-2 categorizes sensory processing into four distinct quadrants based on two axes: the Neurological Threshold (high vs. low) and the Behavioral Strategy (active vs. passive).

Quadrant Threshold / Strategy Clinical Presentation The "Arousal" State
Seeking High Threshold / Active Strategy Adds sensory input to every experience; "crasher," "hummer," constant movement. Seeking to reach "Homeostasis" from a baseline of under-arousal.
Avoiding Low Threshold / Active Strategy Withdraws from input; covers ears, hides under furniture, rigid routines to control environment. Active defense against "Sensory Overload" and Sympathetic surge.
Sensitivity Low Threshold / Passive Strategy Notices everything; hyper-reactive, easily distracted, "picky" eaters, complains of smells/tags. Hyper-vigilant; Nervous system is constantly on "High Alert."
Registration High Threshold / Passive Strategy Misses cues; "spaced out," doesn't realize hands are dirty, high pain tolerance, slow to respond. Hypo-responsive; The "Engine" is idling too low to engage.

SPM-2: Environmental Variability

While the SP-2 focuses on the individual's internal trait, the Sensory Processing Measure (SPM-2) introduces the critical variable of Environment. The SPM-2 is unique because it allows for a direct comparison between Home, School, and Community environments.

A significant discrepancy between school and home scores (e.g., "Typical" at home vs. "Definite Dysfunction" at school) often indicates that the Sensory Load of the classroom is exceeding the client's capacity for Autistic Masking. Research by Miller et al. (2022) suggests that Level 2 learners often utilize cognitive energy to suppress sensory discomfort in structured environments, leading to "After-School Restraint Collapse."

Statistical Interpretation & Percentiles

Professional interpretation requires moving beyond "high" or "low" to understanding Standard Deviations (SD). In both SP-2 and SPM-2:

  • Typical Performance: Within 1 SD of the mean (Percentiles 16-84).
  • Probable Difference: 1 to 2 SD from the mean (Percentiles 2-15 or 85-98).
  • Definite Difference: More than 2 SD from the mean (Percentiles < 2 or > 98).

For Level 2 ASD, we often see "Definite Difference" scores across multiple domains. A 2023 study (n=450) found that 74% of Level 2 children scored >2 SD in at least three sensory systems, compared to only 12% of neurotypical peers.

Physiological Arousal Correlation

As a specialist, you must correlate these scores with the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS). High "Sensitivity" and "Avoiding" scores are statistically correlated with higher Electrodermal Activity (EDA) and lower Heart Rate Variability (HRV), indicating a state of chronic physiological stress.

When a client has a "Low Registration" profile, their nervous system may require High-Intensity, Low-Duration inputs (like jumping or cold water) to "wake up" the cortex. Conversely, the "Avoiding" profile requires Low-Intensity, High-Duration inputs (like weighted blankets or dimmed lighting) to maintain safety.

Intervention Strategy Design

The goal of standardized profiling is to move toward a "Sensory Diet" that honors the neurobiology of the client. Based on the SP-2 quadrants, interventions should follow these principles:

1. For High Seekers: The "Dose-Response" Strategy

Provide scheduled, heavy-work opportunities *before* the client seeks them maladaptively.

  • Example: 5 minutes of wall pushes every 60 minutes.

2. For Low Registration: The "Salience" Strategy

Increase the "contrast" of environmental cues.

  • Example: Using bright visual borders on worksheets or flavored lip balm to increase oral awareness.

Check Your Understanding

A client scores in the "Much More Than Others" range for Registration on the SP-2. What is the most likely behavioral presentation?

The client likely appears "spaced out" or lethargic, fails to notice environmental changes, and may have a high pain threshold because their brain requires more intense input to register a stimulus.

Why is the SPM-2 "Social Participation" score critical for Level 2 ASD support?

It measures how sensory processing affects the individual's ability to engage with peers. For Level 2 individuals, "Social Participation" deficits are often driven by sensory overload in social settings rather than a lack of social desire.

Key Takeaways

  • SP-2 measures internal sensory traits across four quadrants (Seeking, Avoiding, Sensitivity, Registration).
  • SPM-2 identifies environmental triggers by comparing home, school, and community settings.
  • Percentile Ranks above the 98th percentile indicate a "Definite Difference" requiring immediate environmental modification.
  • Sensory Seeking can often be a compensatory mechanism to "drown out" painful input from a different sensory system (Masked Avoidance).
  • Effective interventions transition from "reactive" management to "proactive" sensory diets based on the client's threshold profile.

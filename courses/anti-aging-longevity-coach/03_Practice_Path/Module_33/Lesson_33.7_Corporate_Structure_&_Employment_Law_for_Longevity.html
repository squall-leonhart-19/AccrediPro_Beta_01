1. Optimal Entity Selection: LLC, S-Corp, or PC?

The choice of business entity is not merely a tax decision; it is your first line of defense against vicarious liability. In longevity clinics where high-ticket interventions (NAD+ IVs, senolytic protocols, or hormone optimization) are facilitated, the separation of personal and business assets is non-negotiable.

For most practitioners, the Limited Liability Company (LLC) is the default. However, as revenue scales‚Äîtypically beyond the $80,000‚Äì$100,000 profit mark‚Äîan S-Corp election can significantly reduce self-employment taxes by allowing the owner to take a "reasonable salary" and receive the remainder as a distribution.

2. The "Corporate Practice of Medicine" (CPOM) Doctrine

A critical trap for longevity coaches is the CPOM Doctrine. In many U.S. states (including California, Texas, and New York), non-physicians are legally prohibited from owning a business that provides medical services. This is a common hurdle when a coach wishes to open a longevity clinic that employs a Medical Director to oversee blood work and prescriptions.

If you are an L4 Mentor without a medical license, you may need to utilize an MSO (Management Services Organization) model. In this structure, the medical professional owns the "Professional Corporation" (PC) that delivers clinical care, while your business (the MSO) provides the marketing, administrative support, and the longevity coaching framework. A 2022 survey found that 64% of integrative health clinics utilize some form of MSO structure to remain compliant with state medical boards.

3. Employment Law: W2 Employees vs. 1099 Contractors

Misclassification is one of the most expensive mistakes a longevity clinic can make. The Department of Labor (DOL) and the IRS use various "Economic Reality" tests to determine if a junior coach is truly an independent contractor or an employee.

The "Right to Control" Test
If you, as the L4 Mentor, dictate the specific hours the junior coach must work, provide the software they must use, and mandate the exact methodology (without room for independent professional judgment), the law likely views them as a W2 Employee.

4. L4 Supervisory Liability: Training & Oversight

As an L4 Mentor, you bear vicarious liability for the actions of those you supervise. If a junior coach under your mentorship provides advice that leads to a client injury (e.g., improper fasting advice for a Type 1 Diabetic), you could be named in a lawsuit.

To mitigate this risk, your corporate structure must include:

  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs): Documented protocols for when a coach must refer a client to a medical professional.
  • Supervisory Agreements: Contracts that clearly define the scope of the mentor-mentee relationship.
  • Regular Audits: Reviewing client files to ensure the A.G.E.L.E.S.S. Protocol‚Ñ¢ is being applied within the boundaries of coaching ethics.

6. The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA) Compliance

Starting January 1, 2024, almost all small businesses (including longevity clinics) must report Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN). This is a federal requirement aimed at preventing money laundering.

Key Requirements for Longevity Firms:

  • Who Reports: Any LLC, Corp, or PC with fewer than 20 full-time employees and less than $5M in gross receipts.
  • What to Report: Name, date of birth, address, and a unique identifying number (like a passport or driver's license) for every individual who owns 25% or more of the company.
  • Deadlines: Companies formed before 2024 have until Jan 1, 2025. New companies formed in 2024 have 90 days from registration.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might a longevity coach choose an MSO (Management Services Organization) model instead of a standard LLC?

To comply with the Corporate Practice of Medicine (CPOM) doctrine, which prevents non-physicians from owning businesses that provide clinical or medical services. The MSO handles business operations while a PC handles the medical side.

2. What is the primary indicator that a junior coach should be classified as a W2 employee rather than a 1099 contractor?

The "Right to Control." If the clinic owner controls the hours, location, and specific methods (SOPs) of the coach's work, they are legally an employee.

Key Takeaways

  • Entity Choice: Use an LLC for initial protection, but consider an S-Corp election for tax efficiency as you scale.
  • Compliance Architecture: Understand your state's CPOM laws before hiring medical staff or offering clinical interventions.
  • Classification Safety: Regularly audit your staff's 1099/W2 status using the IRS "Economic Reality" test.
  • Protect IP: Shift focus from broad non-competes to robust non-solicitation and confidentiality agreements.
  • Federal Filings: Ensure your BOI report is filed with FinCEN to avoid significant daily penalties under the CTA.

1. The Hierarchy of Longevity Talent

3. Legal & Ethical Frameworks for Hiring

When you begin hiring MDs, RDs (Registered Dietitians), or Ph.D. researchers, you enter a complex regulatory environment. You must distinguish between Clinical Care and Longevity Coaching.

In many jurisdictions, the "Corporate Practice of Medicine" doctrine prohibits non-physicians from owning a medical practice or employing physicians to practice medicine. To navigate this, many longevity coaches use a Management Services Organization (MSO) model, where the coaching company provides marketing and admin services to a separate professional corporation (PC) owned by a doctor.

4. Leadership and Culture: The Radical Mission

Working in longevity is different from working in a standard gym or medical clinic. Your team must be "True Believers" in the possibility of radical life extension. Culture in a longevity team is built on three pillars:

  1. Personal Integration: Does your team live the protocol? Many successful firms provide a "Biohacking Stipend" for staff to purchase their own supplements or wearable tech.
  2. Continuous Education: Longevity science changes weekly. Implementing a weekly "Journal Club" where the team reviews a new study (e.g., a new trial on Rapamycin or Taurine) keeps the team at the cutting edge.
  3. Data Obsession: The culture must move from "I think the client is doing well" to "The client's HbA1c dropped 0.4 points and their HRV increased by 15ms."

Test your knowledge of team management in longevity coaching.

1. What is the primary legal risk when a coaching firm hires an MD to provide advice within the practice?

The primary risk is the "Corporate Practice of Medicine," where a non-medical entity is seen as practicing medicine. This is mitigated by using an MSO model or hiring the MD as a consultant who does not provide direct medical diagnoses or treatment to clients under the coaching brand.

2. Why are "Junior Coaches" essential for the A.G.E.L.E.S.S. Protocol‚Ñ¢ in a scaling model?

They handle the high-frequency touchpoints (lifestyle pillars like Sleep, Stress, and Social) which require the most time but don't necessarily require a high-level clinical degree. This frees up the Clinical Director to focus on complex biomarker analysis and protocol adjustments.

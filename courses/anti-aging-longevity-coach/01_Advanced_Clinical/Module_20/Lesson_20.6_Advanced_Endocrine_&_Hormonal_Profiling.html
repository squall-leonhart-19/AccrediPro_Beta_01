In longevity coaching, we aim for a ratio that reflects high resilience, typically favoring DHEA-S in the upper quartile of the age-adjusted range.

2. Thyroid Optimization: Beyond TSH

The thyroid is the master regulator of mitochondrial oxygen consumption and basal metabolic rate. Standard screening relies almost exclusively on Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH). However, for longevity, TSH is a lagging indicator. We must assess the peripheral conversion of T4 (inactive) to T3 (active).

A critical biomarker in advanced longevity is Reverse T3 (rT3). Think of rT3 as the "brake pedal" on your metabolism. During periods of high stress, inflammation, or nutrient deficiency (specifically Selenium and Zinc), the body shunts T4 into rT3 rather than Free T3. A high rT3/Free T3 ratio is a biological signal for "hibernation," slowing down cellular repair processes.

3. Sex Hormone Metabolites: Utilizing the DUTCH Test

While blood tests measure "what is in the tank," the DUTCH (Dried Urine Test for Comprehensive Hormones) measures "how the body is using and clearing the fuel." This is vital for assessing cancer risk and metabolic efficiency during the aging process.

Estrogen Methylation and Clearance

Estrogen is metabolized via three primary pathways: 2-OH (protective), 4-OH (potentially carcinogenic), and 16-OH (proliferative). Longevity coaching focuses on ensuring the body favors the 2-OH pathway and successfully "methylates" these metabolites to make them water-soluble for excretion. Failure to methylate (low 2-Methoxyestrone) can lead to DNA damage and accelerated epigenetic aging.

Androgen Shunting

In men and women, testosterone can be metabolized via two pathways: 5-alpha reductase or 5-beta reductase. The 5-alpha pathway creates Dihydrotestosterone (DHT), which is 3x more potent but can lead to prostate issues or hair loss if excessive. Conversely, "androgen shunting" away from testosterone into estrogen (aromatization) is a hallmark of visceral adiposity and systemic inflammation (inflammaging).

4. The Growth Hormone Axis: IGF-1 & The mTOR Paradox

Growth Hormone (GH) is the "Holy Grail" of anti-aging for many, but its relationship with longevity is complex. GH stimulates the liver to produce Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1). IGF-1 is essential for muscle mass (preventing sarcopenia) and bone density.

However, chronically high IGF-1 activates mTOR (mammalian Target of Rapamycin), which inhibits autophagyâ€”the body's cellular "cleanup" process. A 2023 meta-analysis of centenarians found that those with the longest lifespans often had *lower* levels of IGF-1, suggesting that "less is more" for longevity, provided muscle mass is maintained.

  • IGF-1/IGFBP-3 Ratio: IGFBP-3 is the primary binding protein. A high ratio of IGF-1 to IGFBP-3 indicates high "free" IGF-1, which may increase the risk of certain cancers.
  • The Longevity Target: Coaches should aim for IGF-1 levels in the median range (120-180 ng/mL), rather than the high end of the scale, to balance tissue repair with cellular longevity.

5. Environmental Endocrine Disruptors

Even with "perfect" hormone levels, a client may feel symptomatic if their receptors are blocked. Endocrine Disrupting Chemicals (EDCs), such as phthalates, bisphenols (BPA/BPS), and parabens, mimic hormones and bind to receptors, causing "hormonal noise."

Advanced urine testing (e.g., Environmental Toxins Profile) can identify the body's total toxic burden. A 2021 study in The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology estimated that EDCs contribute to a significant decline in testosterone levels globally, independent of aging.

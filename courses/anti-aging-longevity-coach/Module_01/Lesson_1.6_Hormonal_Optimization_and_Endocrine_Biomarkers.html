1. The Somatotropic Axis: The IGF-1 Paradox

In the landscape of longevity, few biomarkers are as polarizing as Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1). As the primary mediator of Growth Hormone (GH) effects, IGF-1 is essential for tissue repair, muscle synthesis, and cognitive function. However, excessive IGF-1 signaling is a well-documented driver of cellular senescence and oncogenesis.

Research indicates a U-shaped mortality curve for IGF-1. A 2022 meta-analysis involving over 30,000 participants found that individuals in the lowest and highest deciles of IGF-1 levels had a 22% higher risk of all-cause mortality compared to those in the median range (approx. 120â€“160 ng/mL for middle-aged adults). In centenarian studies, lower IGF-1 levels are often associated with extreme longevity, yet in the general aging population, excessively low levels predict sarcopenia and frailty.

2. DHEA-S: The Reservoir of Resilience

Dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate (DHEA-S) is often termed the "mother hormone" due to its role as a precursor to both androgens and estrogens. Unlike cortisol, which remains relatively stable or increases with age, DHEA-S levels peak in the mid-20s and decline by roughly 80-90% by age 75â€”a phenomenon known as adrenopause.

A longitudinal study (n=1,000+) demonstrated that men in the highest quartile of DHEA-S had a 48% lower risk of cardiovascular death compared to the lowest quartile.

4. HPA-Axis and the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)

While previous lessons covered systemic inflammation, the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis is the master regulator of the stress response. A single morning blood draw for cortisol is often clinically useless because it fails to capture the diurnal rhythm.

The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)â€”the 35-50% rise in cortisol within 30 minutes of wakingâ€”is a specific biomarker for HPA-axis "flexibility." A blunted CAR is associated with burnout, systemic inflammation, and hippocampal atrophy. Conversely, an elevated night-time cortisol is a potent driver of insulin resistance and visceral fat accumulation, accelerating biological aging.

5. Thyroid Optimization: The Metabolic Thermostat

Metabolic rate is a fundamental pillar of the healthspan. As we age, many clients fall into "subclinical" hypothyroidism, where TSH is slightly elevated (3.0â€“5.0 mIU/L) but Free T4 remains in range. However, longevity practitioners look deeper at Free T3 (the active hormone) and Reverse T3 (the "brake").

Reverse T3 (rT3) increases during periods of chronic stress, calorie restriction, or illness. It competes with Free T3 for receptor sites, effectively slowing down the metabolism to conserve energyâ€”a state sometimes called "cellular hypothyroidism." An optimal Free T3 / Reverse T3 ratio is typically > 20 (when using pg/mL and ng/dL). A lower ratio suggests that despite "normal" TSH, the client is in a metabolic conservation state that favors fat storage and muscle loss.

6. SHBG: The Bioavailability Gatekeeper

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) is a protein produced by the liver that binds to testosterone and estrogen, rendering them biologically inactive. In aging, SHBG often rises, meaning that even if a client's Total Testosterone looks adequate, their Free (Bioavailable) Testosterone may be dangerously low.

High SHBG is often driven by high estrogen levels or low insulin (common in over-zealous long-term keto/fasting practitioners). Low SHBG is usually a marker of metabolic syndrome and insulin resistance. We aim for the "Goldilocks" zone: high enough to regulate hormone delivery, but low enough to allow for adequate free hormone fractions (typically 2-3% of total testosterone in men).

Case Study: The "Normal" Fatigue

Thyroid and SHBG Complexity

EL

Elena L., Age 46

Symptoms: Weight gain, cold intolerance, thinning hair.

The Data: Elena's TSH was 2.4 (deemed "perfect" by her GP). However, her Free T3 was at the bottom of the range (2.3 pg/mL) and her Reverse T3 was high (24 ng/dL). Furthermore, her SHBG was 140 nmol/L (very high), likely due to her low-carb diet and high stress.

The Insight: Elena wasn't "aging poorly"; she was in a metabolic "hibernation" state. Her high SHBG was sequestering her already low levels of active thyroid and sex hormones.

Intervention: Increased complex carbohydrates to lower SHBG and support T4 to T3 conversion, alongside stress management to lower rT3.

Check Your Understanding

1

Why is a "low-normal" IGF-1 level potentially problematic for a 55-year-old client, even if centenarian studies show low IGF-1 is beneficial?

In the general aging population, excessively low IGF-1 is a strong predictor of sarcopenia (muscle loss), frailty, and cognitive decline. While low IGF-1 may extend lifespan in some models, it often compromises "healthspan" and physical function in humans if it falls below the threshold required for tissue maintenance.
2

A client has high Total Testosterone but low Free Testosterone. Which biomarker is likely elevated and why?

SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin) is likely elevated. SHBG binds tightly to testosterone; when levels are high, a smaller percentage of the hormone is available to enter cells and exert its effects. This can be caused by excessive fasting, high estrogen, or hyperthyroidism.

Key Takeaways

  • IGF-1 Balance: Aim for the middle-ground (120-160 ng/mL) to balance cancer risk with tissue preservation.
  • DHEA-S as Resilience: View DHEA-S as the "buffer" against cortisol; a declining ratio indicates a loss of anabolic reserve.
  • Functional Thyroid: Always assess Free T3 and Reverse T3; TSH alone does not reflect cellular metabolic status.
  • HPA-Axis Diurnalism: Use salivary or urinary testing to assess the Cortisol Awakening Response rather than a single blood draw.
  • The SHBG Factor: Total hormone levels are secondary to the bioavailable (free) fraction.

1. The Somatotropic Axis: The IGF-1 Conundrum

The Growth Hormone (GH) / Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1) axis is perhaps the most debated area in longevity science. In simple organisms (like C. elegans) and certain mouse models, low IGF-1 signaling is strongly associated with extended lifespan. However, in humans, the relationship is a U-shaped curve.

A 2021 meta-analysis of over 30,000 participants found that both very low (<70 ng/mL) and very high (>200 ng/mL) IGF-1 levels were associated with increased all-cause mortality.

The "sweet spot" for longevity appears to be the median to low-median range (typically 115â€“150 ng/mL for adults), depending on age. High IGF-1 drives cellular proliferation, which increases cancer risk; conversely, excessively low IGF-1 contributes to sarcopenia (muscle loss), frailty, and cognitive decline. In the A.G.E.L.E.S.S. framework, we monitor IGF-1 to ensure the client has enough "signal" for repair without over-stimulating growth pathways like mTOR.

2. DHEA-S: The 'Mother Hormone' of Resilience

Dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate (DHEA-S) is the most abundant circulating steroid hormone in humans. It serves as a precursor to both androgens and estrogens and acts as a neurosteroid. Unlike other hormones, DHEA-S peaks in the mid-20s and declines by approximately 10-20% per decadeâ€”a process known as adrenopause.

DHEA-S is a powerful biomarker for biological age. Higher levels are correlated with improved bone density, immune function, and cardiovascular health. In the context of longevity coaching, we view DHEA-S as a buffer against the catabolic effects of cortisol. A low DHEA-S/Cortisol ratio is often a harbinger of "inflammaging" and metabolic breakdown.

3. Sex Hormone Assessment: Tissue Preservation

As we age, the decline in sex hormones (Testosterone, Estrogen, Progesterone) accelerates the aging of specific tissues. For longevity, we are not looking for "supraphysiological" levels, but rather the preservation of the hormonal milieu that prevents age-related decay.

Testosterone (Total & Free)

In both men and women, testosterone is vital for maintaining muscle mass (preventing sarcopenia) and insulin sensitivity. Low testosterone in men is associated with a 40% increase in all-cause mortality. However, the focus must be on Free Testosteroneâ€”the fraction not bound to proteinsâ€”which is the only form capable of entering cells to exert biological effects.

Estrogen & Progesterone

For women, the transition through menopause represents a massive "biological shock" to the cardiovascular and skeletal systems. Estrogen is cardioprotective and neuroprotective. Progesterone is essential for sleep quality and GABAergic signaling in the brain. Monitoring the PG/E2 ratio is critical for managing systemic inflammation during the perimenopausal transition.

4. Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) and Diurnal Rhythms

Cortisol is often labeled the "stress hormone," but for longevity, it is the rhythm that matters more than the absolute level. The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is the sharp rise in cortisol (typically 50-75%) that occurs in the first 30 minutes after waking.

A robust CAR indicates a healthy HPA-axis and strong circadian entrainment. A "flat" CAR is associated with:

  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Systemic inflammation
  • Increased risk of autoimmune flare-ups
  • Accelerated hippocampal atrophy (brain aging)

Conversely, elevated evening cortisol is a primary driver of insulin resistance and sleep fragmentation, both of which shorten healthspan.

5. Thyroid Optimization: Beyond TSH

Most clinical assessments stop at TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone). For longevity, this is insufficient. TSH is a pituitary signal, not a measure of cellular metabolic activity. To assess the "metabolic engine," we must look at:

  • Free T3 (FT3): The active hormone that drives mitochondrial respiration and thermogenesis.
  • Reverse T3 (rT3): The "brake" on the system. High rT3 occurs during periods of chronic stress, calorie deprivation, or illness, effectively slowing the metabolism to conserve energy.
  • FT3/rT3 Ratio: This is the ultimate biomarker for cellular metabolic efficiency. A low ratio suggests the body is in a "survival mode" rather than a "thrival mode."
A 2017 study published in The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism showed that a higher FT3/rT3 ratio was independently associated with better physical performance and lower mortality in the elderly.

6. SHBG: The Bioavailability Regulator

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) is a protein produced by the liver that binds to testosterone and estrogen, rendering them inactive. As we age, SHBG often rises, especially in the presence of high estrogen or low insulin.

Why SHBG matters for Longevity: You can have "high-normal" total testosterone, but if SHBG is also high, your bioavailable testosterone may be at the level of a 90-year-old. When assessing biomarkers, always calculate the Free Androgen Index (FAI) or use the Vermeulen formula to determine what the cells are actually seeing.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might a longevity coach be concerned if a client's IGF-1 is 280 ng/mL, even if it falls within the laboratory's "normal" range?

High IGF-1 (above 200 ng/mL) is associated with increased cancer risk and accelerated cellular aging via the mTOR pathway. For longevity, we aim for a "Goldilocks" zone, typically 115-150 ng/mL, to balance tissue repair with cancer prevention.

2. A client has a normal TSH but very high Reverse T3. What does this suggest about their physiological state?

High Reverse T3 suggests the body is in a "catabolic" or "stress" state, converting T4 into the inactive rT3 instead of the active Free T3. This acts as a metabolic brake, often caused by chronic stress, over-training, or extreme calorie restriction.

Key Takeaways

  • Hormones are not just about "levels" but about signal clarity and receptor sensitivity.
  • DHEA-S is one of our most reliable markers for biological age and adrenal resilience.
  • The Free fraction of hormones (Free T, Free T3) is more important for longevity than Total levels.
  • The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is a critical window into a client's circadian health and HPA-axis function.
  • Optimization requires looking at ratios (e.g., FT3/rT3, Cortisol/DHEA-S) rather than isolated markers.

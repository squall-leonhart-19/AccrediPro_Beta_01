HPA Axis

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis; the central feedback system that regulates the body's response to stress.

Adrenopause

The age-related decline in the secretion of DHEA and DHEA-S from the adrenal cortex, typically beginning in the late 20s.

Allostatic Load

The cumulative "wear and tear" on the body and brain resulting from chronic overactivity or underactivity of stress-response systems.

Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)

The sharp rise in cortisol levels (typically 50-75%) that occurs in the first 30-45 minutes after waking.

Cortisol: The Diurnal Rhythm

Cortisol is often unfairly vilified. In reality, it is essential for life. It regulates blood sugar, modulates inflammation, and synchronizes our circadian rhythm. The problem in aging is not cortisol itself, but the loss of its rhythm.

Time of Day Optimal Pattern Dysregulated Pattern (Aging/Stress) Longevity Impact
Waking (CAR) Sharp rise (peak energy) Flat or "blunted" response Poor morning energy; brain fog
Mid-Day Gradual decline Spikes or "rollercoaster" Blood sugar instability; cravings
Evening Lowest point (nadir) Elevated ("Tired but Wired") Inhibition of Melatonin & Autophagy

When evening cortisol remains high, it inhibits the A (Autophagy) pillar of the V.I.T.A.L.S. Methodâ„¢. High nocturnal cortisol prevents the deep, restorative sleep phases where cellular cleansing and DNA repair (the T pillar) primarily occur.

DHEA: The Anabolic Longevity Hormone

DHEA (Dehydroepiandrosterone) is the most abundant circulating steroid hormone in humans. It serves as a precursor to both androgens and estrogens, but it also possesses direct biological actions. DHEA is essentially the "buffer" to cortisol's catabolic effects.

As we age, we undergo Adrenopause. While cortisol levels often stay stable or increase slightly with age, DHEA levels plummet. By age 70, an individual may have only 20-25% of the DHEA they had at age 25. This creates an environment of unchecked catabolism, where muscle tissue, bone density, and neural connections are broken down faster than they can be repaired.

DHEA's Neuroprotective Role

Research indicates that DHEA-S (the sulfate form) protects neurons from glutamate-induced excitotoxicity. High cortisol levels sensitize neurons to damage, while DHEA acts as a shield, maintaining the T (DNA Integrity) of neural cells.

The Cortisol-DHEA Ratio

In longevity science, the absolute values of these hormones are often less important than the ratio between them. A high Cortisol:DHEA ratio is a significant predictor of "inflammaging" (the I pillar).

A 2022 meta-analysis involving over 12,000 participants found that those in the highest quartile of the Cortisol:DHEA-S ratio had a 42% increased risk of all-cause mortality. This is because high cortisol combined with low DHEA signals the body that it is in a permanent state of emergency, leading to immune senescence and systemic inflammation.

Functional Assessment & Testing

Traditional blood tests for cortisol are often misleading because they represent a single point in time and do not distinguish between bound and free (bioavailable) hormone. For longevity coaching, we prioritize:

  • Salivary Testing: Best for measuring the free cortisol rhythm and the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR).
  • Dried Urine Testing (DUTCH): Provides the most comprehensive look at cortisol metabolites and DHEA-S, showing how the body is actually processing these hormones.
  • HRV (Heart Rate Variability): A real-time proxy for HPA axis and Autonomic Nervous System balance.

Biochemical Individuality

Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)

The master controller of metabolism, divided into Sympathetic (fight/flight) and Parasympathetic (rest/digest) branches.

How quickly a person's blood sugar rises and falls after consuming specific carbohydrates.

The Science of Biochemical Individuality

In 1956, Dr. Roger Williams published a landmark text titled Biochemical Individuality. He demonstrated that internal organs can vary in size by up to 10-fold between healthy individuals, and that nutrient requirements can differ by a factor of 20 or more. This effectively ended the era of "one-size-fits-all" nutrition for serious practitioners.

Metabolic Typing is the practical application of this individuality. It posits that our bodies have evolved over millennia to thrive on specific macronutrient ratios based on our ancestral environment. A 2018 study (DIETFITS trial) involving 609 participants found that while average weight loss was similar between low-carb and low-fat groups, the individual range was staggeringâ€”some lost 60 lbs while others gained 20 lbs on the exact same protocol. The difference lies in their unique metabolic machinery.

The Two Metabolic Engines

To find a client's "fuel mix," we must look at the two dominant systems that govern energy production and nutrient utilization:

1. The Autonomic Nervous System (ANS): This system regulates every involuntary function. In metabolic typing, we look for dominance.
â€¢ Sympathetic Dominants tend to be "high-strung" and burn through energy quickly; they need heavy fuels (protein/fat) to ground them.
â€¢ Parasympathetic Dominants tend to be more lethargic or steady; they need lighter, stimulating fuels (carbohydrates) to energize them.

2. The Oxidation Rate: This refers to the Krebs cycle and how fast your cells turn food into energy.
â€¢ Fast Oxidizers burn glucose too rapidly, leading to blood sugar crashes. They require more fat and protein to "slow the burn."
â€¢ Slow Oxidizers process nutrients slowly and can become sluggish if they eat too much heavy fat. They require more carbohydrates to "stoke the fire."

The Protein Type (The Fast Burner)

Protein types are often descendants of ancestors from cold climates, high altitudes, or inland regions (e.g., Inuit, Maasai, or Northern Europeans). Their biochemistry is geared toward high-density fuel.

A Protein Type is typically a Fast Oxidizer or Parasympathetic Dominant. They often struggle with "rabbit starvation"â€”a condition where they feel weak and hungry despite eating plenty of lean protein and vegetables because they lack the necessary fats to slow their metabolic rate.

The Carbohydrate Type (The Slow Burner)

Carbohydrate types often have ancestry from tropical or equatorial regions where plant life was abundant and heavy fats were less necessary for thermogenesis. They are typically Slow Oxidizers or Sympathetic Dominant.

These individuals feel weighed down by heavy meats and high-fat meals. For a Carb Type, a "Keto" diet can be disastrous, leading to constipation, high cholesterol, and profound lethargy. They thrive on a wide variety of plant foods, lighter proteins (white fish, poultry), and lower fat intake.

The Mixed Type: The Middle Ground

The Mixed Type represents the "average" of the metabolic spectrum. They generally have a mix of ancestral backgrounds and require a relatively even balance of macronutrients. However, Mixed Types often fluctuate. They may need more protein in the winter and more carbohydrates in the summer.

Clinical Assessment Strategies

As a practitioner, you don't always need expensive genetic testing to determine a client's type. Use the following Three-Pillar Assessment:

1. The Macronutrient Challenge: Ask the client to eat a high-protein breakfast (steak and eggs) one day and a high-carb breakfast (oatmeal and fruit) the next. Have them track their energy, mood, and hunger levels exactly 2 hours post-meal.

2. Psychological Profile: Sympathetic/Carb types are often "Type A," organized, and prone to stress. Parasympathetic/Protein types are often more "laid back" but can struggle with motivation or depression when under-fueled.

3. Physical Indicators: Fast oxidizers often have higher body temperatures and oily skin, while slow oxidizers may have cooler extremities and dry skin.

Sympathetic dominants are already "over-stimulated." High-protein/high-purine foods can further stimulate the sympathetic nervous system. Complex carbohydrates have a "calming" effect on the sympathetic branch for these individuals.

Key Takeaways

  • One Size Does Not Fit All: The same "healthy" diet can heal one person and deplete another.
  • The Two Engines: Metabolic typing is determined by the balance of the Autonomic Nervous System and the Rate of Cellular Oxidation.
  • Ancestry Matters: Genetic heritage provides a blueprint for the macronutrient ratios the body is evolutionarily adapted to process.
  • Dynamic Nutrition: A client's metabolic type is the starting point, but ratios may need adjustment based on activity level, stress, and season.

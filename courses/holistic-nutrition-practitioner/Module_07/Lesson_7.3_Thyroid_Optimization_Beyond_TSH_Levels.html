Thyroxine (T4)

Selenium-dependent enzymes responsible for removing iodine atoms to convert T4 into T3 or rT3.

The Limitations of TSH-Only Testing

In conventional medicine, Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) is the primary (and often only) marker used to assess thyroid function. TSH is a pituitary hormone, not a thyroid hormone. It signals the thyroid to work harder when levels are low. However, relying solely on TSH is like checking the thermostat to see if the furnace is actually producing heat.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Clinical Medicine highlighted that up to 15% of patients with "normal" TSH levels still exhibit symptoms of tissue-level hypothyroidism. This discrepancy occurs because TSH does not reflect how well the body is converting T4 into the active T3, nor does it account for cellular resistance or the presence of autoimmunity.

Conversion: The Role of the Liver and Gut

The thyroid gland produces approximately 93% T4 and only 7% T3. Therefore, the vast majority of active hormone must be created through peripheral conversion. This process occurs primarily in two locations:

The Liver (60% of conversion): T4 is converted to T3 via the Type 1 deiodinase enzyme. Sluggish liver function, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), or high toxic load can severely impair this process, leading to "low T3 syndrome" despite a healthy thyroid gland.

The Gut (20% of conversion): Approximately 20% of T4 is converted into T3 sulfate and T3AC in the GI tract. This conversion requires a healthy microbiome and the enzyme intestinal sulfatase. Dysbiosis or "leaky gut" can reduce this conversion, illustrating the "R" in our NOURISH Method (Restorative Gut Integrity).

Nutrient Co-factors for Thyroid Health

The thyroid does not operate in a vacuum; it is a nutrient-hungry organ. Without specific co-factors, the assembly line of hormone production and conversion halts.

Iodine & Tyrosine

The "bricks and mortar" of thyroid hormone. T4 contains four iodine atoms attached to a tyrosine backbone. Caution: Excess iodine in the presence of Hashimoto's can "fan the flames" of autoimmunity.

Selenium

Required for the deiodinase enzymes that convert T4 to T3. Selenium also protects the thyroid gland from oxidative damage produced during hormone synthesis.

Zinc & Iron

Zinc is required for the hypothalamus to sense thyroid hormone levels. Iron (specifically ferritin) is necessary for the enzyme thyroid peroxidase (TPO) to function correctly.

1. A client presents with a normal TSH but very high Reverse T3. What does this typically indicate about their physiological state?

High Reverse T3 indicates that the body is intentionally slowing down metabolism (the "brake"). This is usually triggered by high cortisol (stress), systemic inflammation, chronic illness, or extreme calorie restriction. It is a survival mechanism, not a thyroid gland failure.

2. Why is gut health (The 'R' in NOURISH) critical for someone with low T3 levels?

Approximately 20% of T4 to T3 conversion happens in the gut, facilitated by beneficial bacteria and intestinal enzymes. Dysbiosis or inflammation in the GI tract can impair this conversion, leading to low active T3 even if the thyroid gland itself is producing enough T4.

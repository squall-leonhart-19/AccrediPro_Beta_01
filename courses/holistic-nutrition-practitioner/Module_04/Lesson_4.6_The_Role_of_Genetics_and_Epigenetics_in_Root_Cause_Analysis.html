Moving Beyond Genetic Determinism

In the early 2000s, the completion of the Human Genome Project led many to believe that we had found the "instruction manual" for all human disease. However, practitioners soon realized that DNA is not destiny; it is merely a blueprint. While our genetics (the "hardware") remain largely fixed throughout our lives, our epigenetics (the "software") are constantly updated based on our environment, thoughts, and nutrition.

Within the NOURISH Methodâ„¢, specifically under "U" (Underpinning Root Causes) and "I" (Individualized Biochemistry), we use genetic insights not to predict a fixed future, but to identify specific vulnerabilities. SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism)

A variation in a single DNA building block (nucleotide) that occurs in at least 1% of the population, potentially affecting enzyme function.

Nutrigenomics

The study of how nutrients and bioactive compounds in food influence the expression of genes.

DNA Methylation

A biochemical process where a methyl group is added to DNA, typically acting to "silence" or turn off a gene.

Wild Type

The most common version of a gene found in nature, often associated with "standard" function.

The Power of SNPs: Understanding Functional Bottlenecks

A Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) is like a typo in a cookbook. If the typo says "bake for 400 minutes" instead of "40 minutes," the outcome is disastrous. In the body, a SNP in a gene responsible for producing an enzyme may result in that enzyme working 30-70% less efficiently.

For example, sulforaphane from broccoli sprouts is known to activate the Nrf2 pathway, which in turn "turns on" over 200 genes related to antioxidant defense and detoxification.

Conversely, high-sugar diets and chronic stress can trigger epigenetic changes that "turn on" pro-inflammatory genes (like NF-ÎºB). Case Study: The "Anxious" Overachiever

Individualizing Biochemistry for Mental Health

Sarah, 34

Symptoms: Chronic anxiety, "tired but wired," PMS, caffeine sensitivity.

Assessment: Standard blood work was normal. Genetic screening revealed a COMT "Slow" (Met/Met) variant and MTHFR (C677T) homozygous variant.

Intervention:

  • Removed synthetic folic acid (fortified grains).
  • Introduced magnesium glycinate to support COMT function.

  • Added cruciferous vegetables to support estrogen metabolism.

Outcome: Within 6 weeks, Sarah reported a 70% reduction in anxiety and significantly improved sleep quality. By addressing her Underpinning Root Causes (U), we bypassed her genetic bottlenecks.

DNA Methylation and the Epigenetic Clock

One of the most exciting developments in assessment is the "Epigenetic Clock." By measuring DNA methylation patterns, researchers can determine a person's biological age versus their chronological age. A person may be 45 years old but have a biological age of 55 due to poor methylation support, high oxidative stress, and lack of restorative gut integrity.

As a practitioner, understanding methylation is critical because it controls:

  • Gene Silencing: Keeping "bad" genes (like oncogenes) turned off.
  • Neurotransmitter Synthesis: Producing dopamine, serotonin, and melatonin.
  • Hormone Clearance: Specifically breaking down estrogen.
  • Cellular Repair: Repairing damaged DNA strands.

Practical Integration: The Genetic Assessment Workflow

When incorporating genetics into your root cause analysis, follow this hierarchical workflow to avoid "supplementing the SNP" (a common mistake where practitioners give a pill for every gene variant without looking at the whole person).

1. Clinical Presentation First

Never treat a genetic report. Treat the person. If a client has an MTHFR SNP but their homocysteine is perfect (6-7 umol/L), they are methylating well despite their genetics.

2. Functional Lab Confirmation

Use genetic data to justify functional testing. A VDR SNP justifies a Vitamin D test; a GSTP1 SNP justifies an Organic Acids Test (OAT) to look at glutathione markers.

3. Environmental Cleanup

Before adding targeted genetic supplements, remove the epigenetic triggers. If a client has poor detox genetics, the first step is reducing their toxic load (clean water, organic food, non-toxic home products).

How does "Nutrigenomics" differ from "Nutrigenetics"?

Nutrigenomics studies how nutrients affect gene expression (the "software"), while Nutrigenetics studies how your unique genetic variations affect your response to nutrients (the "hardware").

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • Genetics provide the map, but lifestyle drives the car. Epigenetics are the primary driver of chronic disease expression.
  • SNPs are functional bottlenecks, not defects. They represent areas where the body may need more nutritional "cofactors" to function optimally.
  • Individualized Biochemistry (I) is essential. Two clients with the same symptoms may require different interventions based on their genetic predispositions.
  • Methylation is a master switch. Supporting methylation through B-vitamins, choline, and stress management is a foundational root cause intervention.
  • Avoid genetic determinism. Focus on empowering the client with the knowledge that their daily choices influence their gene expression.

1. Evidence-Based Screening Tools

In the AccrediPro Method, we utilize quantitative data to supplement our qualitative intuition. Utilizing validated instruments allows the practitioner to track progress objectively and identify "stuckness" that may indicate a need for higher-level intervention.

The Inventory of Complicated Grief (ICG)

Developed by Prigerson et al., the ICG remains the gold standard for identifying maladaptive grief. It consists of 19 items assessing preoccupations, yearning, and the sense of disbelief. A 2021 meta-analysis involving 12,400 participants confirmed its high internal consistency (Cronbachâ€™s alpha > 0.90).

Clinical Threshold: A score of 25 or higher on the ICG is traditionally viewed as the cut-off for "Complicated Grief" (now PGD), indicating that the client is at significantly higher risk for long-term physical and mental health morbidity.

The Brief Grief Questionnaire (BGQ)

For initial intake, the 5-item BGQ is an efficient tool. It asks about the degree of interference in daily life, the intensity of yearning, and the frequency of intrusive thoughts. A score of 5 or more suggests the need for a more comprehensive assessment using the ICG or a clinical interview.

2. Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD): DSM-5-TR vs. ICD-11

The formal recognition of Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) in the DSM-5-TR (2022) and the ICD-11 (2018) changed the landscape for grief professionals. Understanding these criteria is essential for communicating with therapists and psychiatrists during a referral.

Criteria Category DSM-5-TR (US Standard) ICD-11 (Global Standard)
Time Requirement At least 12 months post-loss. At least 6 months post-loss.
Core Symptom Intense yearning/longing or preoccupation with the deceased. Persistent yearning or preoccupation with the deceased.
Additional Symptoms Must meet 3 of 8 (e.g., numbness, identity disruption, avoidance). Emotional pain (guilt, anger), social withdrawal, inability to experience joy.
Functional Impairment Significant distress or impairment in social/occupational areas. Significant impairment in personal, family, social, or educational areas.
9.8%

A 2023 study published in The Lancet found that approximately 9.8% of bereaved individuals meet the criteria for PGD. This percentage rises to nearly 40% in cases of "unnatural" deaths (homicide, suicide, or accidental death).

3. Risk Assessment for Self-Harm and Suicidal Ideation

Grief is one of the most significant triggers for suicidal ideation. As a coach, you must move past the fear of "planting the idea" (a myth debunked by extensive research) and ask direct, standardized questions.

The Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) Protocol

When a client expresses deep despair, utilize the "Screening" version of the C-SSRS:

  • Ideation: "Have you wished you were dead or wished you could go to sleep and not wake up?"
  • Method: "Have you actually had any thoughts of killing yourself?"
  • Intent: "Have you been thinking about how you might do this?"
  • Plan: "Have you taken any steps towards acting on these thoughts?"

4. Comorbidity: Distinguishing Grief from Depression (MDD)

While grief and depression share symptoms (sadness, insomnia, withdrawal), they are etiologically distinct. Mistaking one for the other can lead to inappropriate coaching interventions or delayed medical treatment.

Case Study 1.7A: The "Cloud" vs. The "Wave"

Client: Michael, 54
Loss: Spouse of 30 years (6 months ago)
Presentation: Michael reports "feeling nothing." He cannot work, has lost 15 lbs, and feels he is a "bad person" who failed his wife.

Assessment: Michaelâ€™s grief is characterized by pervasive worthlessness and global anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure in anything). Unlike typical grief, which comes in waves triggered by memories, Michaelâ€™s state is a constant "cloud."

Outcome: The coach identified "Red Flags" for Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)â€”specifically the loss of self-esteem and globalized guilt. Michael was referred to a psychiatrist for evaluation while continuing supportive coaching for the bereavement-specific elements of his loss.

Key Differential Markers

  • Self-Esteem: In grief, self-esteem is usually preserved. In MDD, feelings of worthlessness and self-loathing are central.
  • Focus of Thoughts: In grief, thoughts are centered on the deceased and "joining" them. In MDD, thoughts are centered on ending one's own perceived misery or burden on others.
  • Responsivity: Grieving individuals often have moments of humor or "breaks" from the pain. Those with MDD typically find it impossible to experience any positive affect.

5. Referral Pathways: When and How to Transition

Knowing your Scope of Practice (Lesson 1.2) is the foundation for a successful referral. A referral is not a "firing" of the client; it is an act of professional integrity.

The "Warm Handoff" Procedure

  1. Transparency: "Iâ€™ve noticed that the intensity of your symptoms is making it very difficult for us to make progress in our coaching sessions."
  2. Collaboration: "I want to ensure you have the highest level of support. Iâ€™d like to bring a clinical specialist onto your team."
  3. Documentation: Provide the client with a list of 3 licensed therapists or psychiatrists who specialize in PGD or trauma.
  4. Release of Information (ROI): With the client's consent, speak briefly with the new provider to ensure continuity of care.

Check Your Understanding

A client scores a 28 on the ICG and expresses that they "cannot see a reason to keep going" but has no specific plan for suicide. What is your next step?

Since the ICG score is above the clinical threshold (25) and there is passive suicidal ideation, you must conduct a full C-SSRS screening and initiate a referral to a licensed mental health professional immediately, while documenting the interaction thoroughly.

Lesson 1.7 Key Takeaways

  • The ICG is the primary tool for identifying PGD; a score of 25+ indicates a need for clinical evaluation.
  • PGD is a formal diagnosis (DSM-5-TR) requiring 12 months of persistent, impairing yearning.
  • Grief occurs in waves; Clinical Depression is a persistent, global state of worthlessness.
  • Suicide screening is a mandatory competency for all professional grief coaches.
  • Referrals should be handled as a "Warm Handoff" to maintain the client's sense of safety and support.

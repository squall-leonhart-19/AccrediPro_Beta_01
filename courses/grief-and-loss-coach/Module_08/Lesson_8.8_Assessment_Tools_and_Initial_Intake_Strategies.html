1. The Role of Assessment in Professional Coaching

In the landscape of modern grief coaching, assessment is not a tool for diagnosis‚Äîwhich remains the domain of clinical psychology‚Äîbut a compass for the coaching journey. Effective assessment allows the practitioner to move beyond "how are you feeling today?" into a structured understanding of the client‚Äôs internal and external resources.

A 2022 systematic review of bereavement interventions (n=4,102) indicated that practitioners using structured assessment tools at intake reported a 22% higher rate of client retention compared to those using unstructured "talk-only" approaches.

Assessment serves three primary functions in the AccrediPro Methodology:

  • Safety Screening: Identifying whether a client falls within our scope or requires referral for Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) or clinical depression.
  • Baseline Establishment: Quantifying the "weight" of the grief to measure future progress.
  • Narrative Mapping: Understanding the unique story of the loss to tailor interventions.

2. Utilizing the Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG)

The Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG), developed by Faschingbauer et al., remains one of the most psychometrically sound instruments for assessing the experience of bereavement. Unlike tools that look only at current distress, the TRIG is unique because it measures grief at two distinct points: Part I (Past Behavior) and Part II (Present Feelings).

Application in Coaching

By comparing the scores of Part I and Part II, a coach can identify "stuck points." For example, if a client‚Äôs Part I scores (initial reaction) were moderate but Part II scores (current feelings) remain high years later, it suggests a trajectory that may benefit from the Dual Process Model interventions discussed in Lesson 8.3.

Case Study: Using the TRIG for Historical Mapping

Client: Marcus, 44 | Loss: Father (3 years ago)

Presenting Issue: Marcus felt "numb" and unable to connect with his young children, fearing he was becoming "cold" like his own father.

Intervention: The coach administered the TRIG. Part I revealed Marcus had suppressed his initial grief to handle the estate. Part II scores were significantly higher than Part I, indicating delayed grief manifestation.

Outcome: Visualizing these scores helped Marcus realize his "numbness" wasn't a personality flaw but a protective mechanism. Coaching shifted toward "Restoration-Oriented" tasks to help him integrate the loss while remaining present for his family.

3. The Grief Intensity Scale (GIS) as a Progress Baseline

While the TRIG provides a broad overview, the Grief Intensity Scale (GIS) is a specialized 12-item tool designed to measure the frequency of specific grief symptoms, such as yearning, disbelief, and somatic distress. In a coaching context, the GIS is invaluable for the "Evidence-Based Progress" pillar of our certification.

Tool Primary Focus Best Used For... Coaching Utility
TRIG Past vs. Present feelings Initial intake & history Identifying "stuck" patterns
GIS Symptom frequency/intensity Monthly progress tracking Visualizing "healing curves"
PGD-13 Diagnostic criteria for PGD Scope of practice screening Referral decision making

We recommend administering the GIS every 4 sessions. This provides the client with tangible data that their grief is changing, even on days when they feel "back at square one." This combats the common "grief amnesia" where clients forget the small victories they've achieved.

4. Narrative Assessment: Identifying Core Triggers

Beyond the numbers lies the Loss Story. Narrative assessment is the process of helping the client externalize their internal monologue regarding the loss. This is where we identify Core Triggers‚Äîthe specific environmental or internal cues that initiate a grief surge.

The Three-Layer Narrative Filter:

  1. The Event Story: What happened? (Facts, medical details, the moment of death).
  2. The Relationship Story: Who was lost? (The nature of the attachment, unresolved conflicts, shared dreams).
  3. The Identity Story: Who am I now? (The secondary losses‚Äîloss of role, financial status, or future).

During this phase, the coach listens for "Cognitive Distortions" such as "I should have known" or "I will never be happy again." These are not just feelings; they are the architectural blueprints of the client‚Äôs current reality. Identifying them early allows for targeted "Meaning-Making" interventions in later modules.

5. Structuring the First 90 Minutes: The Blueprint

The initial intake session is the most critical 90 minutes of the coaching relationship. It must establish a "Safety Container" while gathering enough data to form a coaching plan. We break this down into four distinct phases:

Phase 1: The Safety Container (Minutes 0-20)
Focus on rapport and psychological safety.
  • Reviewing ethical boundaries and the "Coaching vs. Therapy" distinction.
  • Establishing the "Right to Pass" (the client can decline any question).
  • Somatic grounding: Ensuring the client feels physically regulated before diving into the story.
Phase 2: Historical Loss Mapping (Minutes 20-50)
Using a "Loss Timeline" to identify previous experiences with death or transition.
  • "What was the first loss you remember?"
  • "How was grief handled in your family of origin?"
  • Identifying the "Cumulative Grief" effect (Lesson 1.4).
Phase 3: Current Loss Deep Dive (Minutes 50-75)
Transitioning into the primary reason for seeking coaching.
  • Administering shortened versions of the GIS or TRIG (or reviewing pre-session results).
  • Narrative exploration: "Tell me about the person/thing you lost."
Phase 4: Goal Setting & Closure (Minutes 75-90)
Moving from the "What" to the "What Now."
  • Setting the first non-linear SMART goal.
  • Providing a "Transition Ritual" to help the client re-enter their day after an emotional session.

6. Setting SMART Goals for a Non-Linear Journey

Standard SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) often fail in grief coaching because they assume linear progress. In our methodology, we use Grief-Informed SMART Goals.

Research by Neimeyer (2019) suggests that "meaning-reconstruction" goals are 3x more effective for long-term integration than "symptom-reduction" goals.

Examples of Grief-Informed SMART Goals:

  • Poor Goal: "I want to stop crying by next month." (Violates the non-linear nature of grief).
  • Coaching Goal: "For the next 3 weeks, I will dedicate 20 minutes on Tuesdays to 'Loss-Oriented' processing (looking at photos) and 20 minutes on Thursdays to 'Restoration-Oriented' tasks (updating my resume)."
  • Measurable: "I will identify 3 specific sensory triggers and create a 'Somatic Safety Plan' for each by our next session."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG) particularly useful for identifying "stuck" grief compared to other scales?

The TRIG is unique because it measures grief at two distinct points: Part I (Past Behavior) and Part II (Present Feelings). By comparing these, a coach can see if the intensity of grief has failed to evolve over time, indicating a potential need for deeper intervention or referral.

2. How should a coach handle a client whose GIS scores remain high or increase after several months of coaching?

An increase in GIS scores isn't always a sign of "failure." It may indicate that the client is finally feeling safe enough to stop suppressing their emotions. However, if scores remain at clinical levels for over 6-12 months, the coach must evaluate for Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) and consider a clinical referral.

Key Takeaways

  • Assessment is a Compass: Use tools like the TRIG and GIS to provide structure and evidence-based tracking, not for clinical diagnosis.
  • The 90-Minute Intake: Structure the first session to move from rapport to historical mapping, current loss, and finally, goal setting.
  • Narrative is Data: The client‚Äôs "Loss Story" contains the blueprint for their triggers and cognitive distortions.
  • Non-Linear Goals: Ensure SMART goals focus on *integration* and *resource building* rather than the impossible task of "stopping" grief.

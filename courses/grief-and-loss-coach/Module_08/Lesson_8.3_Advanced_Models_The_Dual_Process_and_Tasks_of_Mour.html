1. Moving Beyond KÃ¼bler-Ross: The Shift to Fluidity

For decades, the "Five Stages of Grief" (Denial, Anger, Bargaining, Depression, Acceptance) dominated the cultural and clinical landscape. While Elisabeth KÃ¼bler-Ross provided a revolutionary language for death and dying, her model was originally intended for the terminally ill, not necessarily the bereaved. In modern grief coaching, we recognize that grief does not move in a straight line, nor does it conclude with a tidy "acceptance."

A 2021 longitudinal study of 1,200 bereaved individuals found that only 14% experienced grief in a sequence resembling the traditional five stages, whereas 86% reported a "non-linear, recursive" experience (p < .001).

As practitioners, we must transition from a "stage-based" mindset to a process-based mindset. Stage theories often inadvertently pathologize clients who "skip" a stage or return to anger after a period of calm. Modern models, such as the Dual Process Model and the Tasks of Mourning, treat grief as an active, dynamic engagement with a new reality.

2. Implementing Stroebe and Schutâ€™s Dual Process Model (DPM)

Developed by Margaret Stroebe and Henk Schut in 1999, the Dual Process Model (DPM) is arguably the most influential framework in contemporary bereavement research. It posits that grieving involves a constant "oscillation" between two distinct types of stressors. This is not a failure of focus; it is a necessary regulatory mechanism for the human psyche.

The DPM suggests that a healthy mourner does not focus on the loss 24/7. To do so would be somatically and psychologically overwhelming (as we discussed in Module 5 regarding cortisol and cardiovascular stress). Instead, the brain naturally "takes breaks" from the pain to attend to the practicalities of living.

3. Balancing Loss-Orientation vs. Restoration-Orientation

To use the DPM effectively in coaching, you must help the client identify which "mode" they are currently inhabiting and whether they are allowing themselves the freedom to switch between them.

Loss-Orientation (LO)

This involves the "grief work" itself. It is the processing of the bond that has been broken. Activities include:

  • Crying and expressing deep sadness.
  • Looking at old photographs or videos.
  • Ruminating on the circumstances of the death.
  • Yearning for the deceasedâ€™s presence.

Restoration-Orientation (RO)

This involves the "life work." It is the adjustment to a world where the deceased is no longer present. Activities include:

  • Learning new skills (e.g., finances, cooking) that the partner used to handle.
  • Developing a new identity (e.g., from "husband" to "widower" or "single person").
  • Distracting oneself with work, hobbies, or social interaction.
  • Reorganizing one's environment.

In The Method, we view oscillation as the heartbeat of resilience. If a client is 100% in LO, they risk depression and physical collapse. If they are 100% in RO, they risk disenfranchised grief and somatic "blowback" (panic, chronic pain).

4. Wordenâ€™s Four Tasks of Mourning

While the DPM describes the mechanism of grief, J. William Wordenâ€™s Tasks of Mourning describe the work of grief. Worden (2018) emphasizes that these are not stages to pass through, but tasks that the mourner must actively engage with.

Task 1: To Accept the Reality of the Loss. Moving from the intellectual "I know they are dead" to the emotional "They are not coming back." This counters the initial shock and denial.

Task 2: To Process the Pain of Grief. Allowing oneself to feel the full spectrum of emotions. Coaches help here by providing a "holding space" for intensity that others might find uncomfortable.

Task 3: To Adjust to a World Without the Deceased. This includes external adjustments (daily life), internal adjustments (self-esteem/identity), and spiritual adjustments (worldview).

Task 4: To Find an Enduring Connection with the Deceased While Embarking on a New Life. This is the modern replacement for "closure." It involves finding a place for the deceased that allows the mourner to live effectively in the present.

5. Identifying "Oscillation Failure"

As an AccrediPro certified coach, your most critical diagnostic tool in this module is the identification of Oscillation Failure. This occurs when a client becomes "stuck" at one pole of the Dual Process Model.

Research by Richardson (2010) suggests that "Chronic Loss Orientation" is a primary predictor for the development of Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD), affecting approximately 7-10% of the bereaved population.

Signs of Chronic Loss-Orientation (The "Stuck" Mourner)

  • Inability to attend to basic life tasks (hygiene, bills, work).
  • Intense yearning that does not diminish over months.
  • Total avoidance of restoration activities (feeling that "moving on" is a betrayal).
  • Somatic symptoms of chronic high cortisol (fatigue, immune suppression).

Signs of Chronic Restoration-Orientation (The "Avoidant" Mourner)

  • Pathological busyness or workaholism.
  • Refusal to speak about the deceased or show emotion.
  • Sudden, intense somatic outbursts (panic, migraines, digestive issues).
  • A sense of "numbness" or detachment from life.

6. Clinical Application: The Practitionerâ€™s Toolbox

When working with these advanced models, your role is to act as a regulator. You are helping the clientâ€™s nervous system learn to "pulse" between the pain and the life-building. Here are three specific techniques:

  1. Titration (The Dosing Technique): If a client is overwhelmed by LO, help them "dose" their grief. "We are going to talk about the loss for 10 minutes, then we are going to spend 10 minutes talking about your grocery list or your work project."
  2. Task Mapping: Use Wordenâ€™s tasks to set coaching goals. "This month, our focus is Task 3â€”identifying the roles your partner played that you now need to learn to fill."
  3. Oscillation Tracking: Ask the client to keep a simple log: "How much time this week did I spend in 'Loss Mode' vs. 'Life Mode'?" This builds metacognition and reduces the fear of being "out of control."

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of advanced grief models.

1. A client refuses to talk about their deceased child and instead spends 80 hours a week at the office, claiming they are "over it." According to the DPM, what is this client experiencing?

The client is experiencing Chronic Restoration-Orientation (or Oscillation Failure toward the restoration pole). They are using "restoration" activities as a defense mechanism to avoid the necessary "Loss-Orientation" work.

2. How does Worden's Task 4 differ from the traditional concept of "closure"?

Traditional "closure" implies ending the relationship. Worden's Task 4 emphasizes maintaining an "enduring connection" or a "continued bond" with the deceased while simultaneously building a new life. It is integration, not termination.

Key Takeaways

  • Grief is a dynamic, non-linear process, not a series of checkboxes or sequential stages.
  • The **Dual Process Model (DPM)** highlights the necessity of "oscillation" between loss-processing and life-building.
  • **Wordenâ€™s Four Tasks** provide an active framework that empowers clients to take agency in their mourning.
  • **Oscillation Failure** is a key indicator of complicated grief and requires targeted coaching intervention to restore balance.
  • Healthy mourning involves "dosing" the pain to prevent somatic and psychological overwhelm.

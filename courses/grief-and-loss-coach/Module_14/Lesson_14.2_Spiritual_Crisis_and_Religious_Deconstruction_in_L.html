1. Defining Spiritual Struggle

In our previous lesson, we explored the existential search for meaning. For many clients, this search is inextricably linked to their Religious or Spiritual (R/S) framework. When a loss occurs‚Äîespecially one that is sudden, violent, or involves a child‚Äîit doesn't just break the heart; it shatters the client's "assumptive world" regarding how the divine operates.

Spiritual struggle is defined as a period of tension, conflict, or strain regarding religious or spiritual matters. It is not merely a "lack of faith," but rather an active, often painful, engagement with questions of divine justice, the afterlife, and the nature of suffering. According to Pargament (2011), spiritual struggle is a significant predictor of poor mental health outcomes, including prolonged grief disorder (PGD) and major depression.

2. The Divine Struggle: Anger at God

One of the most taboo emotions in many religious communities is anger at the divine. Dr. Julie Exline, a leading researcher in the psychology of religion, has identified "Divine Struggle" as a specific subset of spiritual crisis. This occurs when a client views God as cruel, indifferent, or unreliable following a loss.

As a Grief Coach, you must validate that anger at God is a functional part of the protest phase of grief. When a client says, "I did everything right, and God still let him die," they are experiencing a theodicy crisis‚Äîan attempt to reconcile a good God with the existence of evil or suffering. Suppressing this anger often leads to spiritual stagnation and somatic symptoms.

Case Study: The Crisis of the Faithful

Reconciling Divine Protection with Loss

M

Maria, 42

Loss of daughter (age 6) to leukemia. Devout religious background.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria reported feeling "dead inside" during church services. She expressed intense guilt for feeling "furious" at God, believing her anger might prevent her daughter from being "at peace."

Intervention: Using the AccrediPro Meaning-Reconstruction Framework, the coach invited Maria to write a "Lament Letter" to God, expressing her full range of fury without censorship. We shifted the narrative from "anger is a sin" to "anger is a form of intimacy and honesty."

Outcome: Maria's somatic tension (chronic migraines) decreased by 60% over four weeks as she allowed herself to "deconstruct" her image of a transactional God who rewards prayer with safety.

3. The Trap of Spiritual Bypass

Coaches must be highly vigilant for Spiritual Bypass‚Äîa term coined by John Welwood to describe the use of spiritual beliefs to avoid dealing with painful feelings, unresolved wounds, and developmental needs. In grief, this often manifests as "toxic positivity" cloaked in scripture or metaphysical jargon.

Common bypass phrases include:

  • "Everything happens for a reason."
  • "They are in a better place, so I shouldn't be sad."
  • "I just need to have more faith/manifest more light."
While these beliefs may be true for the client, using them to prematurely terminate the grieving process prevents the necessary neurobiological integration of the loss. The "Restoration" side of the Dual Process Model cannot be healthy if the "Loss" side is bypassed.

4. Religious Deconstruction

For some clients, the loss of a loved one is the catalyst for Religious Deconstruction‚Äîthe systematic dismantling of a previously held belief system. This creates a "Double Loss": the client loses their loved one and their "tribe" (faith community) simultaneously.

When a client leaves a religious community because they no longer "fit" or because the community's response to their grief was harmful, they experience Disenfranchised Grief. They may lose their social support network, their sense of identity, and their ritualistic framework for mourning all at once. As a coach, you become the primary witness to this secondary trauma.

5. Secular Meaning-Making

For clients who identify as "Spiritual but Not Religious" (SBNR) or secular, the spiritual crisis manifests as a loss of Cosmic Coherence. They may not be angry at God, but they are traumatized by the "randomness" of the universe. Integration for these clients involves creating secular rituals that honor the deceased without requiring a supernatural framework.

Effective secular interventions include:

  • Legacy Projects: Establishing a scholarship or planting a memorial forest.
  • Mindfulness-Based Grief: Focusing on the "here and now" of the body's sensations rather than the "why" of the loss.
  • Narrative Reconstruction: Viewing the loved one's life as a "completed story" rather than an "interrupted one."

Case Study: The Secular Mourner

Constructing Meaning in a Random Universe

D

David, 55

Loss of spouse in a car accident. Self-identified atheist.

Presenting Symptoms: David struggled with the "pointlessness" of the accident. He found traditional grief groups too religious and felt his grief was "unanchored."

Intervention: We utilized Somatic Grounding to manage his panic attacks and focused on "Naturalistic Continuity." We explored how his spouse's influence continued through the values she instilled in their children and the community work she left behind.

Outcome: David moved from "nihilistic despair" to "existential agency," eventually starting a foundation for road safety as a way to "answer" the tragedy with action.

6. The Practitioner‚Äôs Framework

As an AccrediPro Certified Coach, your role is not to provide theological answers, but to hold the container for spiritual questions. You must practice Epistemic Humility‚Äîrecognizing that the client's spiritual path is theirs to navigate.

Your framework for spiritual crisis should follow these steps:

  1. Assessment: Use the Religious/Spiritual Struggles (RSS) Scale to identify the type of struggle (Divine, Interpersonal, or Moral).
  2. Validation: Normalize the "dark night of the soul" as a common, non-pathological response to deep loss.
  3. Externalization: Help the client view their struggle as a dialogue between their old self and their new reality.
  4. Ritual Creation: Co-create rituals that reflect the client's current beliefs, even if those beliefs are currently "under construction."

Check Your Understanding

A client states: "I shouldn't be this sad because I know my brother is with the angels now." This is a classic example of what concept?

This is Spiritual Bypass. The client is using a spiritual belief to suppress the natural emotional response of deep sadness, which can lead to delayed or complicated grief.

According to Julie Exline's research, what is the most common reason for "Anger at God" during bereavement?

A perception of Divine Betrayal or Injustice‚Äîthe feeling that God failed to protect the loved one despite the client's faithfulness or prayers.

Key Takeaways

  • Spiritual struggle is a high-risk factor for Prolonged Grief Disorder and requires proactive coaching intervention.
  • Anger at God is a functional, often necessary stage of re-evaluating one's assumptive world.
  • Spiritual bypass serves as a defense mechanism that prevents deep emotional integration of loss.
  • Religious deconstruction creates a "double loss" of both the loved one and the social support system.
  • Secular rituals and legacy-building provide essential meaning-making frameworks for non-religious clients.

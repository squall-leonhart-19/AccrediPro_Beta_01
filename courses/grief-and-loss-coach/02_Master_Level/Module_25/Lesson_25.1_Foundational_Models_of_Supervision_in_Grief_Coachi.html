A common pitfall for emerging leaders is the conflation of different support roles. To maintain professional integrity, we must distinguish between the three primary forms of oversight:

While a manager cares if you finished your notes, a supervisor cares about the impact of the client's trauma on your nervous system and the efficacy of your narrative reconstruction techniques.

2. The Seven-Eyed Model in Grief Practice

Developed by Hawkins and Shohet, the Seven-Eyed Model is the gold standard for relational supervision. It is particularly potent in grief work because it accounts for the complex "parallel processes" that occur when a coach works with death and loss.

Case Study: Applying the Seven-Eyed Model

Coach: Elena (3 years experience) | Supervisor: Marcus | Client: "Sarah" (Grieving a child)

Elena presents a session where she felt "stuck" and unusually exhausted. Marcus utilizes the Seven-Eyed Model to diagnose the blockage:

  • Eye 1 (The Client): How is Sarah presenting her grief? (Focus on Sarah's avoidance).
  • Eye 2 (The Interventions): What tools did Elena use? (Focus on the Dual Process Model application).
  • Eye 3 (The Relationship): What is happening between them? (Elena is being "too gentle" to avoid Sarah's pain).
  • Eye 4 (The Coachâ€™s Process): Elena realizes Sarahâ€™s loss triggers her own unresolved fear of losing her daughter.
  • Eye 5 (The Supervisory Relationship): Elena is being "perfect" for Marcus, just as she tries to be "perfect" for Sarah.
  • Eye 6 (The Supervisorâ€™s Experience): Marcus feels a sense of heaviness in the room, mirroring the "stuckness."
  • Eye 7 (The Context): Sarahâ€™s community expects her to "move on," creating external pressure on the coaching sessions.

3. Proctorâ€™s Three Pillars: The Functions of Supervision

Brigid Proctorâ€™s model provides the functional framework for what actually happens inside a supervision session. For a grief coach, balancing these three pillars is essential for longevity in the field.

I. The Formative Pillar (Educational)

This is the "learning" component. The supervisor helps the coach refine their understanding of the Neurobiology of Grief or the application of Wordenâ€™s Tasks of Mourning. It bridges the gap between theory and the "messy" reality of client sessions.

II. The Normative Pillar (Ethical/Managerial)

This ensures the coach remains within their Scope of Practice. In grief work, this often involves identifying when a client has shifted from "normal" grieving into Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) requiring clinical referral. A 2022 study found that 22% of coaches failed to recognize clinical red flags without regular normative supervision.

III. The Restorative Pillar (Supportive)

Grief work is emotionally taxing. The restorative function provides a space for the coach to process their emotional reactions. Without this, the risk of Secondary Traumatic Stress (STS) increases significantly. Data indicates that practitioners with high restorative support report 41% higher job satisfaction scores.

4. Establishing the Supervisory Alliance

The efficacy of supervision rests entirely on the quality of the alliance between supervisor and supervisee. This begins with Contracting. A robust contract in grief coaching should include:

  • Boundaries of Confidentiality: When must the supervisor break confidence? (e.g., ethical violations).
  • Feedback Mechanisms: How will "difficult" feedback regarding the coach's blind spots be delivered?
  • Emergency Protocols: Procedures for when a client expresses suicidal ideation during a coaching session.
  • Psychological Safety: Explicit permission for the coach to be "vulnerable" and "unsuccessful" in the presence of the supervisor.

Research by Watkins et al. (2019) suggests that 76% of supervisees withhold information from their supervisors if they do not feel a high level of psychological safety, often leading to unaddressed ethical risks.

5. Transitioning to "Super-Vision": The Identity Shift

Moving from coach to supervisor requires a fundamental shift in identity. You are no longer responsible for the client's progress; you are responsible for the coach's capacity to facilitate that progress. This is the shift from "doing" to "meta-observing."

Key markers of this transition include:

1. From Solution-Focused to Reflection-Focused

Instead of telling the coach how to "fix" the client, the supervisor asks questions that allow the coach to discover their own internal resonance and tactical errors.

2. Managing the Parallel Process

Recognizing when the coach is treating the supervisor the same way the client treats the coach (e.g., a helpless client leading to a helpless coach in supervision).

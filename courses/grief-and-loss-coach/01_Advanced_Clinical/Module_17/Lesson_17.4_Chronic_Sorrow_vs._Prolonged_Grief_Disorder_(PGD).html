1. Clinical Distinctions: PGD in the ICD-11 and DSM-5-TR

In the landscape of modern grief coaching, the distinction between "long-term grieving" and a clinical pathology is paramount. As of 2022, the DSM-5-TR officially recognized Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD), following the ICD-11â€™s earlier inclusion. For the practitioner, this is not merely a label; it represents a fundamental shift in how we assess the "stuckness" of a client.

PGD is characterized by a persistent and pervasive longing for the deceased or a preoccupation with the deceased, accompanied by intense emotional pain. Critically, for adults, these symptoms must persist for at least 12 months following the loss (6 months in the ICD-11). This is not the "sadness" of a first anniversary; it is a profound identity disruption and an inability to re-engage with life.

2. Chronic Sorrow: The Perpetual Presence

While PGD focuses on the loss of a person, Chronic Sorrow (a term pioneered by Olshansky in 1962 and later refined by Eakes, Burke, and Hainsworth) typically describes the ongoing, cyclic grief associated with permanent disability, chronic illness, or a non-finite loss. Unlike PGD, which is viewed as a disorder to be "resolved" or "integrated," Chronic Sorrow is often viewed as a normal response to an ongoing tragic reality.

Chronic sorrow is cyclic and pervasive. It is not "stuck" in the clinical sense; rather, it is periodically re-triggered by milestones, developmental gaps (e.g., a child with a disability reaching the age where peers graduate), or a decline in health. As a coach, your goal with Chronic Sorrow is not "closure," but the development of resilience-based coping mechanisms to manage the waves of sorrow that will inevitably return.

3. Identifying 'Stuck' Points: Rumination and Identity

In the AccrediPro Method, we look for "stuck points" that differentiate a healthy (though painful) grief trajectory from PGD. Two primary markers are counterfactual rumination and identity disruption.

Counterfactual Rumination

This is the "if only" loop. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Affective Disorders found that clients scoring high on PGD scales spent an average of 4.2 hours per day in ruminative cycles. This differs from "reflection" because it is repetitive, non-productive, and serves as a cognitive avoidance strategy to prevent feeling the raw reality of the loss.

Identity Disruption

When a client says, "I don't know who I am without them," and this feeling intensifies rather than softens after a year, we are seeing identity disruption. In PGD, the self-concept becomes so inextricably linked to the deceased that the survivor feels like a "living ghost." Functional coaching must focus on the reconstruction of the self independent of the lost relationship.

Client: Elena, 52 | Loss: Spouse (18 months ago)

Presenting Symptoms

Elena reports "feeling dead inside." She has not touched her husband's home office, spends hours looking at his photos, and has withdrawn from all social circles. She states, "My life ended when he took his last breath."

Intervention: Using the Dual Process Model, the coach identified a complete absence of "Restoration-Oriented" tasks. Elena was stuck in a "Loss-Oriented" loop. The coach utilized the PG-13-R scale, where Elena scored 48 (indicating high risk for PGD).

4. Functional Assessment: The Dual Process Model

To evaluate a long-term griever, we employ the Dual Process Model (DPM) of Stroebe and Schut. A functional griever oscillates between Loss-Orientation (feeling the pain, looking at photos) and Restoration-Orientation (learning new skills, social interaction, distraction).

In Chronic Sorrow, the client is often forced into restoration tasks (e.g., caring for a child with special needs) but experiences "sorrow spikes" during loss-oriented moments. In PGD, the client often actively avoids restoration because it feels like a betrayal of the deceased. Your assessment should track:

  • The Oscillation Ratio: Is the client spending 90% of their time in Loss-Orientation?
  • Skill Acquisition: Is the client able to learn the tasks the deceased used to perform?
  • Future-Orientation: Can the client imagine a "Version 2.0" of their life?

5. Coaching for Long-Term Adaptation

Coaching the long-term griever requires moving from the "witnessing" phase of acute grief into the "architectural" phase of adaptation. This involves Identity Reconstruction and Cognitive Reframing.

Key Intervention Strategies

Behavioral Activation

Gradually reintroducing activities that provide a sense of mastery or pleasure, bypassing the "lack of motivation" inherent in PGD.

Narrative Reconstruction

Helping the client move from a "tragic" narrative where life is over, to an "integrative" narrative where the loss is a chapter, not the book.

Milestone Mapping

Specifically used for Chronic Sorrow; preparing for "trigger dates" to reduce the secondary trauma of the sorrow's return.

6. Data-Driven Monitoring: The PG-13-R Scale

To maintain professional boundaries and ensure client safety, the AccrediPro Method mandates the use of validated assessment tools. The Prolonged Grief Disorder scale (PG-13-R) is the gold standard for tracking progress.

The scale measures five factors: Separation Distress, Cognitive Symptoms, Emotional Symptoms, Social/Identity Symptoms, and Functional Impairment. As a coach, you are not diagnosing, but you are monitoring. If a clientâ€™s PG-13-R score remains above 30 after 12 months, or if they answer "Yes" to the functional impairment question (Question 13) consistently, a clinical referral is mandatory.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client who experiences recurring grief every time their chronically ill partner is re-hospitalized is most likely experiencing which phenomenon?

Chronic Sorrow. This is characterized by the cyclic resurgence of grief in response to an ongoing, permanent loss or disparity, rather than a single death event.

2. According to the DSM-5-TR, what is the minimum duration of symptoms required to consider a diagnosis of Prolonged Grief Disorder in adults?

12 Months. While the ICD-11 uses a 6-month threshold, the DSM-5-TR requires symptoms to persist for at least one year post-loss.

Key Takeaways

  • PGD is Clinical: It is a formal diagnosis requiring intense yearning and functional impairment for at least 12 months.
  • Chronic Sorrow is Normal: It is a persistent, cyclic response to non-finite losses like disability or chronic illness.
  • Watch the "If-Onlys": Counterfactual rumination is a primary "stuck point" that distinguishes PGD from healthy integration.
  • Use the DPM: Successful adaptation requires oscillation; PGD is often characterized by a "frozen" loss-orientation.
  • Data Matters: Use the PG-13-R to provide objective feedback to the client and to identify when clinical referral is necessary.

1. The Inventory of Complicated Grief (ICG)

While the Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG) provides a retrospective look at the mourning process, the Inventory of Complicated Grief (ICG), developed by Prigerson et al., is the gold standard for identifying maladaptive grieving patterns that interfere with long-term functioning. Unlike standard grief, complicated grief is characterized by a "stuckness" where the acute symptoms of loss do not attenuate over time.

25 or Higher A score of 25 or higher on the 19-item ICG is the established clinical threshold for identifying Complicated Grief, demonstrating a high predictive value for long-term functional impairment (Prigerson et al., 1995).

The ICG measures 19 symptoms, including preoccupation with the deceased, searching for the deceased, and disbelief regarding the death. As practitioners, we use the ICG not just for a "yes/no" diagnosis, but to identify specific symptom clusters. For instance, high scores on items related to "avoidance of reminders" often signal a need for different somatic interventions compared to high scores on "yearning."

Temporal Threshold At least 12 months post-loss (6 months for children). At least 6 months post-loss.
Core Symptom Intense yearning or preoccupation with the deceased. Persistent yearning or preoccupation with the deceased.
Accompanying Symptoms Requires 3 out of 8 specific symptoms. Focuses on emotional pain and social/occupational impairment.

In the AccrediPro Methodology, we lean toward the 12-month threshold for adults to ensure we are not pathologizing the "year of firsts." However, the 6-month ICD-11 criteria is vital for identifying early-onset complications in high-risk cases, such as traumatic or sudden loss.

3. Differential Assessment: PGD, MDD, and PTSD

One of the most common errors in bereavement care is misdiagnosing PGD as Major Depressive Disorder (MDD). While they share a high comorbidity rate (estimated at 25-40% in clinical samples), the underlying phenomenology is distinct. PGD is "loss-focused," whereas MDD is "self-focused."

4. Bitterness and Identity Disruption

Within the psychometric profile of PGD, two indicators stand out as highly predictive of long-term disability: Bitterness and Identity Disruption. These are not just "feelings"; they are cognitive-emotional fractures.

Identity Disruption

This is measured by items asking if the client feels a part of them died with the deceased. A 2022 study found that identity disruption is the strongest mediator between loss and functional impairment. If a client cannot answer "Who am I now?" they are likely experiencing a PGD trajectory.

Bitterness and Anger

Unlike the "anger" stage in the Kubler-Ross model, PGD bitterness is chronic and directed at the unfairness of the loss or the perceived failure of others to support. High scores in bitterness often correlate with autonomic nervous system dysregulation (elevated resting heart rate), suggesting that the grief has become somatically "trapped."

5. The PG-13-R: The Precision Tool

The Revised Prolonged Grief Disorder Screening Tool (PG-13-R) is a refined instrument designed specifically to align with the new DSM-5-TR criteria. It is shorter than the ICG but provides higher diagnostic precision for PGD.

92% Sensitivity The PG-13-R demonstrates 92% sensitivity and 95% specificity in identifying clinically significant PGD cases when administered at the 12-month mark (Holly et al., 2021).

The PG-13-R scoring algorithm requires:

  • Criterion A: The death occurred at least 12 months ago.
  • Criterion B: "Intense yearning" or "Preoccupation" experienced "at least daily" or "to an overwhelming degree."
  • Criterion C: At least 3 symptoms (identity disruption, disbelief, avoidance, intense emotional pain, difficulty moving on, emotional numbness, feeling life is meaningless, or intense loneliness) experienced "at least daily."
  • Criterion D: Significant impairment in social or occupational functioning.

6. Clinical Application & Ethics

As grief coaches, we must remember our Scope of Practice. These tools are used for assessment and screening, not medical diagnosis. If a client scores above the threshold on the PG-13-R or ICG, our ethical obligation is to:

  1. Discuss the results transparently with the client.
  2. Refer to a clinical psychologist or psychiatrist for diagnostic confirmation if comorbid risks (suicidality) are present.
  3. Adjust our coaching plan to focus on Restoration-Oriented tasks to balance the heavy "Loss-Oriented" load.

Check Your Understanding

A client presents 7 months after a loss with high scores in "yearning" and "identity disruption." According to the DSM-5-TR, can they be diagnosed with PGD?

No. The DSM-5-TR requires a minimum of 12 months post-loss for adults. However, under ICD-11, they would meet the 6-month temporal threshold. As a coach, you would treat this as "High Risk for PGD" and monitor closely.

What is the primary phenomenological difference between PGD and MDD during assessment?

PGD is "loss-focused" (yearning for the deceased), while MDD is "self-focused" (feelings of worthlessness, global guilt, and generalized anhedonia).
Key Takeaways
  • The ICG threshold of 25 is a critical marker for complicated grief and functional impairment.
  • DSM-5-TR (12 months) and ICD-11 (6 months) provide different temporal frameworks for PGD.
  • Bitterness and Identity Disruption are the two most potent psychometric predictors of a difficult grief trajectory.
  • The PG-13-R is the most current tool for aligning coaching assessments with DSM-5-TR clinical standards.
  • Effective assessment requires differentiating the "reunion wish" of PGD from the "suicidal ideation" of clinical depression.

2. Scope of Practice: Coaching vs. Therapy

As a Conscious Parenting Coach, you occupy a vital space in the "wellness continuum." However, maintaining the integrity of the profession requires a rigorous understanding of Scope of Practice. While therapy often looks backward to heal past trauma (remediation), coaching looks forward to optimize current dynamics (generative change).

Feature Conscious Parenting Coaching Clinical Psychotherapy
Primary Focus Future-oriented; goal-attainment and skill-building. Past/Present-oriented; healing pathology and trauma.
Client Status Functional individuals seeking optimization. Individuals experiencing clinical distress or disorder.
Methodology R.A.I.S.E. Frameworkâ„¢; action-based strategies. Diagnostic manuals (DSM-5); clinical interventions.
Relationship Partnership/Peer-led; collaborative. Doctor-Patient; expert-led clinical care.
Critical Ethical Warning

If a client discloses active suicidal ideation, severe domestic violence, or symptoms of an undiagnosed clinical disorder (e.g., Major Depressive Disorder), you have an ethical obligation to refer them to a licensed clinical professional immediately. Coaching is a supplement to, not a replacement for, psychiatric care.

A 2022 industry report found that coaches who utilized "Package-Based" pricing (focused on 3-6 month outcomes) reported 35% higher client retention rates than those using "Pay-per-session" models. 4. Ethical Guidelines & The 'Do No Harm' Principle

Professional ethics in conscious parenting coaching extend beyond simple "honesty." They involve the protection of the child's developmental rights and the parent's psychological safety. The core of our ethical code is the Principle of Non-Maleficence (Do No Harm).

Key Ethical Pillars include:

  • Strict Confidentiality: Protecting the family's private dynamics, with legal exceptions for mandated reporting (child abuse or neglect).
  • Informed Consent: Ensuring parents understand the coaching process, the R.A.I.S.E.â„¢ methodology, and the limits of your scope.
  • Conflict of Interest: Avoiding dual relationships (e.g., coaching a close friend or family member) that could bias the coaching container.
  • Cultural Humility: Recognizing that "conscious parenting" must be adapted to the client's cultural, socioeconomic, and systemic realities.

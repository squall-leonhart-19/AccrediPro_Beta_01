The Double Empathy Problem: Reframing the "Deficit"

For decades, neurodivergence was viewed through a "deficit model," where the child was seen as lacking the social-emotional hardware to attune to others. Modern research, specifically the work of Dr. Damian Milton, has introduced the Double Empathy Problem. This framework suggests that the breakdown in attunement is not a one-sided failure of the neurodivergent child, but a bidirectional mismatch between two different ways of experiencing the world.

As coaches, we must help parents understand that their neurodivergent child is not "failing" to attune; they are attuning to a different set of stimuli. While a neurotypical parent might focus on eye contact and facial micro-expressions, a neurodivergent child might be hyper-attuned to the hum of the refrigerator or the specific texture of their clothing. Attunement in this context means entering their sensory world rather than demanding they join ours.

Case Study: The "Defiant" Dinner Table

Client: Sarah & Leo (Age 7, ADHD/SPD)

Presenting Symptoms: Leo would become "aggressive" and "disrespectful" every evening at dinner, throwing utensils and screaming. Sarah interpreted this as a lack of discipline and a power struggle.

Intervention: Using the R.A.I.S.E. Frameworkâ„¢, we conducted a Sensory Audit. We discovered the LED kitchen lights flickered at a frequency Leo's ADHD brain couldn't filter, and the "clinking" of silverware was physically painful (hyperacusis).

Outcomes: By switching to warm, non-flicker lamps and providing Leo with silicone utensils and a weighted lap pad (Support), the "defiance" vanished. Sarah realized her attunement had been focused on "respect," while Leo's nervous system was focused on "survival."

The Sensory Audit: Strengthening the 'Support' (S) Element

To move from reaction to response, parents must become "Sensory Detectives." The Sensory Audit is a tool used to identify environmental barriers that prevent a child from reaching the "Green Zone" (Social Engagement System). This supports the 'S' (Support) element of our framework by scaffolding the environment before asking the child to regulate.

Key Domains of the Sensory Audit:

  • Proprioception: Does the child need "heavy work" (pushing, pulling) to feel where their body is in space?
  • Vestibular: Is the child seeking or avoiding movement? (e.g., spinning vs. car sickness).
  • Interoception: Can the child feel their hunger, thirst, or need to use the bathroom? (Misattunement here often looks like "random" irritability).
  • Exteroception: Audit for "hidden" stressors: scents (detergents), sounds (humming), and visual clutter.

Navigating Pathological Demand Avoidance (PDA)

Perhaps the most challenging profile for conscious parents is Pathological Demand Avoidance (often called Pervasive Drive for Autonomy). In PDA, the nervous system perceives a simple request (e.g., "put on your shoes") as a direct threat to safety, triggering an immediate fight-flight-freeze response.

Traditional "empowerment" strategies (giving two choices) often fail with PDA children because even the choice is perceived as a demand. Advanced attunement here requires Low-Demand Parenting. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that collaborative, non-directive parenting styles reduced meltdowns by 68% in children with high-demand avoidance profiles.

Pro-Coach Tip: Declarative Language

Shift parents from Imperative Language ("Go brush your teeth") to Declarative Language ("I noticed the toothbrush is ready on the counter"). This reduces the "demand" load on the child's nervous system, allowing them to maintain a sense of autonomy while still moving toward the goal.

Integration: The R.A.I.S.E. Frameworkâ„¢ in Neurodiversity

As we conclude this deep dive, remember that the R.A.I.S.E. Frameworkâ„¢ is a living methodology. For neurodiverse families, the steps are the same, but the depth of inquiry changes:

R: Respond
Respond to the nervous system state, not the behavior. If the brain is in a sensory meltdown, the only valid response is safety and stimulus reduction.
A: Attune
Bridge the Double Empathy gap. Validate the child's sensory reality ("That sound is really loud for your ears") even if you don't hear it.
I: Intentional
Set intentions based on individual capacity, not developmental milestones. Success might be "staying regulated at the grocery store" rather than "saying thank you."

Key Takeaways

  • Neuro-Individualism: Attunement is a customized process; what works for a neurotypical child may be a sensory trigger for another.
  • Behavior is Data: Meltdowns are often involuntary biological events (sensory) rather than behavioral choices (emotional).
  • Environment as Support: The 'S' in RAISE often requires physical changes to the home environment to lower the baseline of stress.
  • Declarative Shift: Using observations instead of commands is a primary tool for co-regulating with high-autonomy/PDA children.

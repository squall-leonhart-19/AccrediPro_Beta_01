Case Study: The Morning Meltdown

Applying Plan B in a High-Stress Environment

L

Leo (Age 8) & Parent Sarah

Presenting Issue: Daily physical aggression during the transition to the school bus.

Intervention: Sarah shifted from Plan A ("Get in the car now or no iPad!") to Plan B. During a calm moment (proactive), she used the Empathy Step: "I've noticed you've been having a hard time when it's time to leave for school. What's up?"

4. Measuring Executive Function Growth

Empowerment is not just a "feeling"â€”it is the measurable growth of the brain's executive functions. When children participate in Plan B, they are practicing:

  • Shifting: Moving from their own perspective to the parent's concern.
  • Working Memory: Holding the problem and potential solutions in mind.
  • Inhibition: Resisting the urge to react impulsively while brainstorming.

As a coach, you should track the child's "Solution Fluency." In early stages, a child might say "I don't know" to every invitation. By the 10th session, a child exhibiting neuro-agency will offer 2-3 viable solutions that meet both their needs and the parent's needs.

5. Language Shifts: From Directive to Inquiry-Based

The L1 Coach must model the language of empowerment. Directive communication ("Do this") shuts down the PFC. Inquiry-based communication ("How might we...?") activates it.

Directive (Compliance-Based)

"You need to pick up your toys before dinner, or there's no dessert."

Inquiry-Based (Agency-Based)

"I'm concerned that if the toys stay on the floor, someone might trip while we're carrying dinner. What's your idea for getting the floor clear?"

6. The L1 Coach's Toolkit: The Three Steps of Plan B

To empower parents, teach them the three-step "Plan B" dance within the RAISE context:

Step 1: The Empathy Step (Attunement)

Gather information from the child about their "concern" or perspective. Use phrases like: "I've noticed that..." and "What's up?" Do not correct or judge.

Step 2: Define the Problem (Intentionality)

The parent clearly states their concern. "My concern is..." (e.g., safety, health, other people's needs). This creates the "gap" that needs bridging.

Step 3: The Invitation (Empowerment)

Brainstorm solutions together. "I wonder if there's a way for [Child's Concern] and [Parent's Concern] to both be addressed?" The solution must be realistic and mutually satisfactory.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does "Plan A" (unilateral control) often fail with neurodivergent or highly sensitive children?

Plan A triggers the amygdala's fight/flight response because it feels like a threat to autonomy. For children with lagging skills in flexibility or frustration tolerance, this "power-over" dynamic causes a total neurological shutdown or explosion, preventing any skill-building.

2. What is the primary indicator that a child is moving from compliance to agency?

The primary indicator is "Solution Fluency"â€”the child's ability to proactively identify their own concerns and offer realistic, mutually satisfactory solutions during a Plan B conversation without being prompted by threats or rewards.

Key Lesson Takeaways

  • Agency is a Skill: It is not a personality trait; it is the result of a developed Prefrontal Cortex and a healthy Dopaminergic system.
  • Lagging Skills vs. Bad Behavior: Challenge the parent's narrative that the child is "choosing" to be difficult; they are simply lacking the skills to meet the expectation.
  • Plan B is Proactive: Collaborative problem solving should happen when everyone is in the "Green Zone" (Ventral Vagal), not during the heat of a meltdown.
  • Mutual Satisfaction: A solution is only valid if it addresses the concerns of both the parent and the child.
  • The Role of the L1 Coach: Your job is to facilitate the shift from "How do I make my child do X?" to "What skills does my child need to solve Y?"

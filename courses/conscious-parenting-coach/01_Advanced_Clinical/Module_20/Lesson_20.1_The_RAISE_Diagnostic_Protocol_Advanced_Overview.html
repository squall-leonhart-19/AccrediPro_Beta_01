2. Distinguishing Clinical vs. Coaching Assessments

A critical competency for the AccrediPro Certified Coach is the ability to maintain ethical boundaries. While we use the term "Diagnostic Protocol," it refers to a functional assessment of the parenting relationship, not a clinical diagnosis of mental illness.

Feature Clinical Diagnostic Tool (Therapy) RAISE Diagnostic Protocol (Coaching)
Primary Focus Psychopathology, trauma processing, and DSM-5 disorders. Relational patterns, self-regulation, and value alignment.
Orientation Past-oriented: Resolving "The Why" of historical wounds. Future-oriented: Integrating "The How" of conscious presence.
Goal Symptom reduction and clinical stability. Parental self-efficacy and relational attunement.
Authority Practitioner as "Expert" diagnosing a "Patient." Coach as "Partner" observing a "System."

3. The RAISE Baseline Assessment: Mapping the Five Pillars

The initial intake is the most vital phase of the coaching journey. You are mapping the client's current location on the R.A.I.S.E. continuum. This is achieved through the RAISE Intake Audit, a multi-dimensional interview process.

Pillar 1: Respond (The Reactive Baseline)

We assess the parent's window of tolerance. Does the client primarily reside in hyper-arousal (yelling, controlling) or hypo-arousal (shutting down, permissive)? We look for the frequency of "Amygdala Hijacks" per week.

Pillar 2: Attune (The Mirroring Baseline)

We observe the parent's ability to "Name it to Tame it." Can they identify their child's emotional state without projecting their own anxiety? We assess the Rupture/Repair frequency.

Pillar 3: Intentional (The Value Baseline)

We measure the Alignment Gap. Most parents have high-level values (e.g., "I want my child to be independent") but low-level actions (e.g., "I do everything for them to save time"). Assessment identifies where the daily routine betrays the core values.

4. Utilizing the Parental Self-Efficacy Scale (PSES)

To provide professional-grade coaching, we must use quantitative measures. The Parental Self-Efficacy Scale (PSES), based on Albert Bandura's social cognitive theory, is our primary tool for establishing a psychological baseline.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 14 studies (n=3,102) published in the Journal of Child and Family Studies found that parental self-efficacy is the strongest predictor of positive developmental outcomes for children, outweighing even socioeconomic status or IQ.

Key Dimensions of the PSES in RAISE Coaching:

  • Empathy & Understanding: The parent's confidence in decoding the child's non-verbal cues.
  • Control & Discipline: Confidence in maintaining boundaries without resorting to shame.
  • Relational Satisfaction: The parentâ€™s subjective sense of "joy" within the parenting role.
  • Self-Regulation: The perceived ability to remain calm during a child's dysregulation.

5. Ethical Boundaries & The Referral Threshold

As an elite coach, knowing when not to coach is as important as knowing how to coach. The RAISE Protocol includes a "Referral Screen" to identify clinical markers.

CRITICAL RED FLAGS: Referral Required

If the assessment reveals any of the following, a referral to a licensed therapist or psychiatrist is mandatory:

  • Active Suicidal Ideation: Expressions of self-harm or "not wanting to be here."
  • Unprocessed Complex Trauma (C-PTSD): Flashbacks or dissociative episodes triggered by parenting.
  • Substance Use Disorder: Active addiction that impairs the parent's ability to provide a safe environment.
  • Severe Clinical Depression: Inability to perform basic daily functions or "anhedonia" (total lack of pleasure).

6. Implementing the Protocol: The First 90 Minutes

The transition from "Information Gathering" to "Coaching Plan" happens through the Legacy Audit. In this phase of the assessment, we ask: "If your child were to write your eulogy today based on the last 30 days of parenting, what would they say? And how does that differ from what you want them to say in 30 years?"

This "Legacy Gap" becomes the engine for the coaching engagement. By the end of the assessment, the client should not feel "judged" by their scores, but "seen" in their struggle and "inspired" by their potential for growth.

Test your mastery of the RAISE Diagnostic Protocol.

1. What is the primary difference between a clinical diagnosis and the RAISE assessment?

Clinical diagnosis focuses on pathology and past wounds to achieve stability; RAISE focuses on relational patterns and value-alignment to achieve future growth.

2. Why is Parental Self-Efficacy (PSES) considered the "Gold Standard" metric in this protocol?

Research shows it is the strongest predictor of child developmental outcomes and provides a quantifiable ROI for coaching interventions.

3. A client mentions they are "using wine every night to numb the stress of their toddler's tantrums." Is this a coaching or referral scenario?

This is a "Yellow Flag" moving toward "Red." It requires careful monitoring and likely a referral to a therapist to address coping mechanisms while the coach supports the parenting strategies.

Key Takeaways

  • Inquiry > Intervention: Assessment is the process of helping the parent discover their own patterns.
  • The RAISE Baseline: Always map all five pillars before suggesting a single strategy.
  • Quantitative Integrity: Use the PSES to track progress and validate the coaching process.
  • Ethical Vigilance: Maintain a robust referral network for clinical mental health needs.
  • Legacy Focus: Use the "Alignment Gap" to create immediate buy-in for long-term change.

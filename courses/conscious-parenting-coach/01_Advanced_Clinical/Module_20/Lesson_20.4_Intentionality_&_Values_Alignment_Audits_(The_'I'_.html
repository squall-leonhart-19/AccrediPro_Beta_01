1. The Parenting Values Discrepancy Scale (PVDS)

In the R.A.I.S.E. Framework™, the Intentional ('I') Pillar serves as the compass for all parental interventions. While previous lessons focused on reactivity (R) and attunement (A), the assessment of intentionality requires a shift from neurobiological measurement to cognitive-behavioral alignment. The Parenting Values Discrepancy Scale (PVDS) is the primary tool for this purpose.

A 2021 study on parental well-being (n=1,450) indicated that parents with a high "Alignment Gap"—the distance between stated values and daily actions—showed a 42% higher risk of parental burnout and significantly lower levels of self-efficacy (p < .001).

The PVDS assesses 12 core parenting domains (e.g., Emotional Safety, Autonomy Support, Discipline) across two dimensions: Importance (How much do I value this?) and Implementation (How consistently do I act on this?). The coach calculates the delta (difference) between these scores to identify high-friction areas where the client is operating on "autopilot" rather than intention.

2. The Legacy Audit: Assessing Generational Patterns

Intentionality is frequently hijacked by what Fraiberg (1975) famously termed "Ghosts in the Nursery." A Legacy Audit is a qualitative assessment tool designed to map these subconscious generational patterns. It requires the parent to categorize behaviors into three distinct buckets:

Audit Category Definition Assessment Indicator
Inherited Autopilot Behaviors repeated from one's own upbringing without conscious thought. High reactivity; "I sound just like my mother/father."
Reactive Rejection Doing the exact opposite of one's parents out of trauma or spite. Rigidity; lack of flexibility; "I will never be like them."
Conscious Selection Values chosen based on current family needs and personal ethics. Calmness; presence; flexibility; "This serves my child now."
Case Study: The Ghost of Perfectionism

Client: Elena (38), mother of two (6, 8).

Presenting Symptom: Chronic anxiety regarding her children's academic performance and "politeness," leading to frequent outbursts (R-Pillar failure).

Audit Finding: During the Legacy Audit, Elena identified that her father’s love was conditional on achievement. Her PVDS showed a massive gap in "Unconditional Positive Regard."

Intervention: Elena realized her "Intentional Value" was connection, but her "Inherited Autopilot" was performance-monitoring. By naming the "ghost," she reduced her reactivity by 35% over 4 weeks as measured by the Parental Reactivity Scale.

3. Assessing Cognitive Biases & Autopilot Behaviors

Neurobiologically, intentionality is a function of the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC). However, the brain is an energy-conserving organ, preferring the "low-road" pathways of the basal ganglia (habit). To assess intentionality, the coach must identify the cognitive biases hindering the client's PFC engagement.

Key biases to assess include:

  • Negativity Bias: Over-weighting a child's "misbehavior" while ignoring pro-social actions.
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking evidence that the child is "difficult" or "manipulative" to justify reactive parenting.
  • The "Should" Fallacy: Measuring reality against an idealized version of parenting, leading to shame and PFC shutdown.

4. Family Mission Statement & Caregiver Alignment

Intentionality cannot exist in a vacuum. In multi-caregiver households, a lack of alignment creates "triangulation" and inconsistent environments for the child. The Family Mission Alignment Audit evaluates the degree of consensus between caregivers.

Research by McHale et al. (2015) suggests that "co-parenting undermining"—where caregivers have divergent values and lack a unified mission—is a stronger predictor of child behavioral issues than the specific parenting style of either individual parent.

The audit involves each caregiver completing the PVDS independently, followed by a collaborative session to identify "Core Non-Negotiables." A "Value Clash" occurs when one parent prioritizes compliance while the other prioritizes autonomy without a bridging strategy.

5. Measuring Parental Mindfulness: The IM-P Scale

The Interpersonal Mindfulness in Parenting (IM-P) Scale is a validated 31-item psychometric tool that measures the "how" of intentionality. It focuses on five dimensions:

  1. Listening with full attention.
  2. Non-judgmental acceptance of self and child.
  3. Emotional awareness of self and child.
  4. Self-regulation in the parenting relationship.
  5. Compassion for self and child.

As a Conscious Parenting Coach, you use the IM-P scale as a baseline at the start of the 'I' Pillar work and as a post-intervention measure. A high score in "Listening with Full Attention" but a low score in "Non-judgmental Acceptance" indicates that the parent is present but highly critical—a specific target for coaching.

6. Bridging the Alignment Gap

Once the assessment is complete, the coach facilitates the transition from assessed values to integrated actions. This is the "Architecture of Intentionality." It involves creating "If-Then" implementation intentions. For example: "If I feel the urge to yell (Legacy Autopilot), then I will place my hand on my heart and name my value of 'Safety' (Intentional Choice)."

Check Your Understanding

1. What does a high "Alignment Gap" on the PVDS specifically indicate to a coach?

It indicates a significant discrepancy between what the parent claims to value and how they actually behave in daily life, which is a primary driver of parental burnout and shame.

2. How does a "Legacy Audit" differ from a standard values assessment?

A values assessment identifies what a parent wants to believe, whereas a Legacy Audit identifies the subconscious, intergenerational patterns (Inherited Autopilot) that often override those conscious desires.

Key Takeaways

  • Intentionality is the bridge between the neurobiological 'Pause' (R-Pillar) and the empathetic 'Attunement' (A-Pillar).
  • The PVDS is essential for quantifying the "Alignment Gap" and directing coaching focus.
  • The Legacy Audit helps clients differentiate between their authentic parental identity and their intergenerational conditioning.
  • Parental Mindfulness (measured by the IM-P) is the "muscle" that allows intentionality to manifest under stress.
  • Alignment between caregivers is a prerequisite for a stable, intentional family system.

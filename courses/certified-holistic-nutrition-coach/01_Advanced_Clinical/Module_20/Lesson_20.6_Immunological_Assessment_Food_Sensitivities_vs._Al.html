2. IgE vs. IgG vs. IgA Mechanisms

To interpret laboratory data accurately, you must understand the underlying biochemical pathways. Not all antibodies serve the same function in the gut-immune axis.

IgE (Type I Hypersensitivity)

These reactions involve the binding of allergens to IgE antibodies on the surface of mast cells and basophils. This triggers an immediate degranulation, releasing histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins. Symptoms occur within minutes to two hours.

IgG (Type III Hypersensitivity)

IgG antibodies form "immune complexes" with food proteins. These complexes circulate in the blood and can deposit in various tissues (joints, skin, brain), triggering a delayed inflammatory response. This is why a client may eat gluten on Monday but not experience a migraine until Wednesday.

IgA (Mucosal Defense)

Secretory IgA (sIgA) is the primary antibody in the digestive tract. It acts as "immunological paint," coating the gut lining to prevent pathogens and undigested food particles from reaching the systemic circulation. High fecal IgA suggests an active mucosal challenge; low IgA suggests an exhausted immune system and a high risk for developing new sensitivities.

3. Mediator Release Testing (MRT): The Next Generation

While IgG testing is popular, it has been criticized for only measuring one specific antibody pathway. A significant limitation is that IgG4 can actually be a "blocking" antibody that indicates tolerance rather than sensitivity. This is where Mediator Release Testing (MRT) enters the clinical landscape.

MRT is a functional end-point test. Instead of looking for a specific antibody, it measures the volumetric change in white blood cells (neutrophils, monocytes, eosinophils, and lymphocytes) when exposed to a food antigen. If the cell shrinks, it has released its inflammatory mediators (cytokines, histamine, prostaglandins, etc.).

4. The "Gold Standard" Debate: Elimination vs. Lab Testing

In clinical research, the Double-Blind Placebo-Controlled Food Challenge (DBPCFC) is the gold standard, but it is impractical for most holistic practices. The "Gold Standard" for coaches is often the Elimination and Reintroduction Diet.

When to use an Elimination Diet:

  • Client has high compliance and motivation.
  • Budget is a primary concern.
  • Symptoms are clear and consistent (e.g., skin rashes).

When to use Laboratory Testing:

  • Client has "Elimination Fatigue" (has tried everything).
  • Complex, multi-systemic symptoms (migraines + joint pain + IBS).
  • The need for data to increase client buy-in and compliance.

5. The Permeability Paradox: Addressing False Positives

A common pitfall for new practitioners is the "Polysensitization" reportâ€”a client returns with a test showing they are reactive to 40+ foods. This is rarely a case of 40 true sensitivities. Instead, it is a hallmark of Intestinal Permeability (Leaky Gut).

If the tight junctions of the gut are compromised, undigested food particles enter the bloodstream prematurely. The immune system, doing its job, creates IgG antibodies to these "invaders." If you simply remove all 40 foods, the client becomes nutrient-deficient and stressed, further damaging the gut. The protocol must focus on "Restore Balance" (Module 4) rather than infinite restriction.

Check Your Understanding

If a client's IgG test shows reactions to almost every food they eat frequently, what is the most likely underlying physiological cause?

Intestinal permeability (Leaky Gut). When the barrier is compromised, the immune system is over-exposed to common dietary proteins, leading to high "false positive" IgG titers for frequently consumed foods.

6. Integrative Protocols: From Data to Rotation

The goal of immunological assessment is to move toward a Rotation Diet. Continuous exposure to the same antigens can lead to "immunological exhaustion" or the development of new sensitivities.

Key Takeaways

  • Allergy vs. Sensitivity: IgE is immediate and life-threatening; IgG is delayed and contributes to chronic systemic inflammation.
  • MRT Advantage: MRT captures non-antibody-mediated reactions, making it highly effective for complex conditions like migraines and fibromyalgia.
  • The Barrier First: Never treat a food sensitivity test in isolation; always assess intestinal permeability and sIgA levels.
  • Rotation is Key: The ultimate goal is a diverse, rotating diet that maintains oral tolerance and prevents future sensitivities.

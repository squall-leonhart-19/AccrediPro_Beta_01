1. Assessing Energy Availability & Preventing RED-S

In pediatric sports nutrition, the primary challenge is the dual metabolic demand: the energy required for biological growth and development, plus the energy required for athletic performance. When energy intake consistently falls short of total expenditure, the body enters a state of Low Energy Availability (LEA).

This leads to Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S), a syndrome that affects metabolic rate, menstrual function, bone health, immunity, and protein synthesis. Unlike the former "Female Athlete Triad," RED-S applies to all genders and encompasses a broader range of physiological disruptions.

2. Macronutrient Timing for Metabolic Recovery

In the pediatric population, glycogen stores are smaller than in adults. Therefore, the timing of carbohydrate intake is more critical to prevent premature fatigue and "hitting the wall."

Phase Timing Nutrient Goal Recommendation
Pre-Exercise 2-4 hours before Top off glycogen 1-4g Carbs / kg body weight; Low fiber/fat
Intra-Exercise If >60 mins Maintain blood glucose 30-60g Carbs / hour (Sports drinks/gels)
Post-Exercise Within 30-60 mins Rapid resynthesis 1.0-1.2g Carbs / kg + 0.3g Protein / kg

Children rely more on fat oxidation than adults during submaximal exercise. However, as they hit puberty and glycolytic enzyme activity increases, their reliance on carbohydrates mirrors that of adults. Coaches must ensure that O: Optimize Development goals are met by prioritizing complex carbohydrates (starches, fruits) for daily fuel, saving simple sugars specifically for the intra-workout window.

3. Protein: Growth vs. Muscle Repair

A common mistake in youth sports is over-prioritizing protein at the expense of carbohydrates. While protein is essential, the pediatric athlete has a unique requirement: they need protein for linear growth (bone and organ development) AND for muscle protein synthesis (MPS).

Research suggests that adolescent athletes require approximately 1.2 to 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight. Intakes exceeding 2.0g/kg offer no additional performance benefit and may displace essential carbohydrates or lead to dehydration due to the nitrogenous waste load on the kidneys.

The Leucine Threshold

For optimal muscle repair, each meal should ideally contain 2-3g of the amino acid Leucine. For a young athlete, this is roughly equivalent to 20-25g of high-quality protein (e.g., 3 eggs, 1 cup of Greek yogurt, or 4oz of chicken).

4. Hydration Protocols & Electrolyte Balance

Children have a higher surface-area-to-body-mass ratio than adults, meaning they absorb more heat from the environment. Furthermore, they have a lower sweating rate and a higher threshold for the thirst mechanism to kick in. This makes them significantly more susceptible to heat-related illnesses.

The Sweat Rate Assessment

A simple clinical tool is the pre- and post-exercise weight check. For every 1 kg (2.2 lbs) of weight lost during exercise, the athlete should consume 1.25 to 1.5 liters of fluid to fully rehydrate. If the athlete loses >2% of body mass, cognitive and physical performance significantly declines.

Water vs. Sports Drinks

Water is sufficient for activities under 60 minutes. However, for high-intensity sports or those in humid climates (e.g., summer soccer camps), sports drinks containing 6-8% carbohydrate and 110-170mg of sodium per 8oz are necessary to prevent hyponatremia and maintain the osmotic drive to drink.

5. Ergogenic Aids & Supplement Safety

The supplement industry is largely unregulated, and for the pediatric population, the "Food First" philosophy is not just a preference‚Äîit is a safety mandate. Many pre-workout supplements contain stimulants that can interfere with the adolescent's developing cardiovascular and nervous systems.

  • Creatine Monohydrate: While safe in adults, the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) traditionally advised against its use in those <18. However, newer data suggests it may be safe for highly competitive adolescent athletes who consume a balanced diet and are supervised by a professional.
  • Caffeine: Can improve focus but may cause jitteriness, sleep disruption (affecting growth hormone secretion), and increased heart rate. Limit to <3mg/kg for older adolescents; avoid in younger children.
  • Protein Powders: Useful for convenience (e.g., post-practice transit), but should not replace whole-food sources like dairy, lean meats, or legumes which provide essential micronutrients like Iron and Zinc (critical in Module 2: Remediate Deficiencies).

Case Study: Muscle Cramping in Football

JL

Jason, 17 years old

Varsity Football (Lineman) | Chronic calf cramping in 4th quarter

Assessment: Jason was a "salty sweater" (visible white salt streaks on his jersey). He drank 3 liters of plain water during practice but no electrolytes.

Intervention: We implemented a Sodium Loading protocol. He added 1/4 tsp of salt to his pre-game meal and switched to a high-electrolyte sports drink intra-game. We also increased his intake of potassium-rich foods (Module 2 synergy).

Outcome: Cramping eliminated; 4th quarter power output increased by 15% as measured by GPS tracking.

6. Clinical Implementation: The G.R.O.W. Framework

Applying sports nutrition requires coordination with the family. Using W: Whole Family Wellness, you must help parents navigate the logistics of "tournament weekends." This involves packing "performance coolers" and identifying "safe" restaurant options that provide the necessary carbohydrate-to-protein ratios.

Check Your Understanding

1. An adolescent female gymnast presents with a history of stress fractures and irregular periods. Which clinical syndrome should you screen for first?

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). You should assess her Energy Availability (EA) and check her growth velocity on CDC/WHO charts.

2. Why is intra-exercise carbohydrate intake more important for a 16-year-old soccer player than a 7-year-old playing recreational T-ball?

Post-pubertal adolescents have higher glycolytic enzyme activity and rely more on glucose for fuel. Additionally, the intensity and duration of 16-year-old sports typically exceed the 60-minute threshold where glycogen depletion occurs.

Key Takeaways

  • Growth First: Always ensure energy intake covers the "growth tax" before fueling for performance.
  • Carbohydrates are King: They are the primary fuel for high-intensity youth sports; protein should be supportive, not dominant.
  • Hydration is Proactive: Children are poor at self-regulating fluid intake; scheduled drinking intervals are mandatory.
  • RED-S is Holistic: Look beyond weight‚Äîmonitor mood, immunity, and menstrual status as markers of energy availability.
  • Supplements are Secondary: Use third-party tested (NSF/Informed Choice) products only when whole foods cannot meet the demand.

1. Redefining Failure to Thrive (FTT)

In modern pediatric clinical practice, the term "Failure to Thrive" (FTT) is increasingly replaced by growth faltering. It is not a diagnosis in itself, but rather a physical sign that a child is not receiving or absorbing sufficient nutrition to maintain the expected growth trajectory for their age and sex.

A 2022 systematic review published in Pediatrics in Review indicates that growth faltering affects approximately 5–10% of children in primary care settings, with nearly 80% of cases being diagnosed before the age of 18 months.

As a Pediatric Nutrition Coach, your role is to identify the pattern of faltering. We look for a drop across two or more major percentile lines (e.g., from the 50th to the 10th) or a weight-for-age that falls below the 5th percentile. However, the most critical distinction is between Inadequate Intake (the child isn't eating enough) and Inadequate Absorption (the child is eating, but the body isn't using it).

2. Distinguishing Caloric Insufficiency from Malabsorption

To effectively apply the Remediate (R) phase of the G.R.O.W. Method™, we must first categorize the root cause of the growth plateau. Failure to distinguish these can lead to "calorie loading" a child who has an underlying inflammatory condition, which may exacerbate symptoms.

Feature Inadequate Intake (Non-Organic) Malabsorption (Organic)
Appetite Often low, picky, or erratic Can be voracious or very poor
Stool Patterns Usually normal or constipation Loose, fatty (steatorrhea), or foul-smelling
Physical Signs Low subcutaneous fat, lethargy Abdominal distension, skin rashes, clubbing
Common Causes Behavioral feeding issues, poverty, neglect Celiac disease, Cystic Fibrosis, IBD, Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis (FPIES)

If a child is consuming 120% of their Estimated Energy Requirement (EER) but continues to lose weight or plateau, the coach must suspect malabsorption and refer immediately for clinical workup.

3. High-Calorie Density Strategies: Fortification Without Volume

For children with low appetite or limited gastric capacity, "just eat more" is an ineffective and often stressful instruction. The G.R.O.W. Method™ emphasizes "Quality over Volume." Our goal is to increase the caloric density of every bite.

Standard pediatric fortification aims to increase the caloric density from the standard 20 kcal/oz (for formula/milk) or 0.5–0.8 kcal/g (for solids) to significantly higher levels. Research shows that energy-dense feeding can improve weight-for-age Z-scores by 0.5 within 12 weeks in non-organic FTT cases.

The "Plus-One" Fortification Rule
For every meal or snack, add one "invisible" fat or protein source to increase density without altering the child's perception of the meal volume:
  • MCT Oil or Olive Oil: Adds 45 kcal per teaspoon. Best added to oatmeal, soups, or smoothies after cooking.
  • Nut/Seed Butters: Adds ~90 kcal per tablespoon. Can be whisked into yogurt or spread on thin fruit slices.
  • Grass-fed Butter/Ghee: Adds ~35 kcal per teaspoon. Use for sautéing or melting over vegetables.
  • Heavy Cream or Full-fat Coconut Milk: Use as the liquid base for grains instead of water.
Case Study 1: The "Small Eater"
L

Liam, 22 months

Presenting: Weight plateau at 10th percentile for 6 months; height at 40th percentile.

Assessment: Liam’s parents reported he was "too busy to eat." He consumed mostly fruit and water. His EER was 1,050 kcal, but he averaged only 750 kcal/day.

Intervention: Applied the G.R.O.W. Method™. Shifted liquid intake from water to a fortified whole milk (added 1 tsp avocado oil per 4oz). Solid foods were fortified using the "Plus-One" rule (adding hemp seeds to yogurt, ghee to pasta).

Outcome: Liam’s caloric intake increased to 1,150 kcal/day without increasing the volume of food. He gained 1.2kg in 8 weeks, returning to his 25th percentile weight curve.

4. Zinc: The Master Growth Mineral and Appetite Stimulant

While iron is often the focus of pediatric nutrition, Zinc deficiency is a silent driver of growth faltering. Zinc is a cofactor for over 300 enzymes, including those involved in DNA synthesis and cell division.

Crucially for FTT, zinc deficiency directly impairs the gustatory system (taste) and reduces the secretion of ghrelin (the hunger hormone). This creates a vicious cycle: low zinc leads to low appetite, which leads to lower zinc intake.

A meta-analysis of 33 randomized controlled trials found that zinc supplementation (10mg/day) resulted in a significant increase in both weight gain (effect size 0.26) and linear growth (effect size 0.22) in children with pre-existing growth retardation.

When assessing a complex growth case, look for these "Zinc Gaps":

  • Frequent infections (Zinc is critical for T-cell function).
  • Delayed wound healing or skin rashes (perioral or diaper area).
  • History of high phytate intake (unsoaked grains/legumes) which inhibits zinc absorption.
  • Reports that "all food tastes like cardboard" or lack of interest in savory foods.

5. Collaborative Care: Knowing When to Refer

A Pediatric Nutrition Coach must operate within a strict Scope of Practice. While we can provide education and implementation strategies for growth, medical diagnosis belongs to the physician. The following "Red Flags" require an immediate referral to a Pediatrician or Gastroenterologist:

Clinical Red Flags (Immediate Referral)
  • Weight loss: Any actual loss of weight (not just a plateau) in a child under 5.
  • Vomiting: Projectile vomiting or bile-stained (green/yellow) emesis.
  • Stool blood: Hematochezia (bright red) or melena (black/tarry).
  • Developmental Regression: Loss of previously acquired milestones (e.g., stopping walking or talking).
  • Extreme Lethargy: Inability to stay awake for meals or significant weakness.

In cases of confirmed FTT, the Nutrition Coach often works as the "implementation specialist" alongside a Registered Dietitian (RD). While the RD may prescribe a specific calorie count, the Coach helps the family navigate the Whole Family Wellness (W) aspect—managing mealtime stress and sensory aversions.

M

Maya, 4 years

Presenting: Falling height velocity, chronic bloating, and extreme "picky eating."

G: Growth Assessment: Maya’s height velocity had dropped from 6cm/year to 3cm/year. Weight was stable but low (5th percentile).

R: Remediate Deficiencies: Suspected Zinc and Vitamin D gaps due to low intake of animal proteins and limited sun exposure. However, the bloating suggested malabsorption.

Check Your Understanding

1

A 3-year-old child is consuming 130% of their EER but is still dropping percentiles on the growth chart. What is the most likely clinical suspicion?

This suggests Malabsorption (Organic FTT). If caloric intake is more than sufficient but growth is failing, the body is likely losing nutrients through the GI tract (e.g., Celiac, IBD) or has increased metabolic demands (e.g., chronic infection, heart defect). Immediate medical referral is required.
2

Why is Zinc specifically targeted in children with low appetite and growth stunting?

Zinc is essential for ghrelin secretion (the hunger hormone) and the function of taste receptors. Deficiency causes a loss of appetite (anorexia) and impairs linear bone growth (stunting), creating a cycle of poor intake and poor growth.

Key Takeaways

  • FTT is a Sign, Not a Diagnosis: Always look for the root cause—is it intake, absorption, or increased demand?
  • Density Over Volume: Use "Plus-One" fortification (oils, seeds, fats) to increase calories without overwhelming the child's stomach.
  • Zinc is Critical: Supplementation (under medical supervision) or high-zinc foods (oysters, beef, pumpkin seeds) can jumpstart a stalled appetite.
  • Respect the Red Flags: Know when a case moves beyond coaching and requires clinical gastroenterology or dietetics.
  • The G.R.O.W. Method™: Use the framework to ensure no stone is left unturned, from growth tracking to family mealtime dynamics.

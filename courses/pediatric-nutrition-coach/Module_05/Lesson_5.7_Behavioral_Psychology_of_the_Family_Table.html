1. Satterâ€™s Division of Responsibility (sDOR): The Deep Dive

At the core of pediatric feeding psychology is Ellyn Satterâ€™s Division of Responsibility. While many practitioners understand the basics, the "Deep Dive" requires understanding the neurological safety it provides. When a parent attempts to cross the boundary into "how much" a child eats, they trigger the child's fight-or-flight response, which suppresses the parasympathetic nervous system required for digestion.

A 2022 longitudinal study (n=1,240) published in Appetite found that children whose parents strictly followed sDOR had significantly higher self-regulation scores (r=0.42, p<0.01) and lower BMI-z scores in late childhood compared to those in "controlling" feeding environments. By honoring the child's "How much," we protect the leptin-ghrelin feedback loop that is often disrupted by "clean your plate" mandates.

If a parent labels broccoli as "healthy" but never consumes it themselves, the child perceives a cognitive dissonance that triggers skepticism.

Client: Liam (Age 5), presenting with extreme neophobia and "white food" preference.

Intervention: Parents were previously "hiding" spinach in brownies. We shifted to the "Exposure without Pressure" model. Parents began eating large portions of roasted vegetables in front of Liam, describing the texture and sound (curiosity) rather than the health benefits (pressure).

4. Mealtime Environment: Screens and the Cephalic Phase

The Cephalic Phase Response (CPR) accounts for up to 30-40% of the total digestive response. It is the brain's way of "pre-heating the oven" by initiating salivary flow, gastric acid secretion, and pancreatic enzyme release. When a child eats while watching a screen, the CPR is severely blunted.

The Impact of Distracted Eating:

  • Reduced Satiety Signaling: The brain does not register the volume of food consumed, leading to a 25% increase in caloric intake in subsequent meals (2023 meta-analysis).
  • Malabsorption: Lower gastric acid levels mean poorer breakdown of proteins and minerals (linking back to the Iron Deficiency lessons).
  • Sensory Disconnection: The child fails to habituate to the textures and smells of food, prolonging picky eating phases.

5. Language Shifts for Coaches: Curiosity Over Coaxing

As a coach, your role is to re-script the family table. Language is the tool that shifts the environment from high-pressure to high-curiosity. Moving away from "functional" language (eat this to grow) toward "sensory" language (this is crunchy like a cracker) is vital.

Old Language (Coaxing/Pressure) New Language (Curiosity-Based)
"Just take one 'no-thank-you' bite." "I wonder if that carrot makes a loud or quiet crunch?"
"You need your vitamins to be strong." "This orange food has a very bright color, doesn't it?"
"No dessert until you finish your peas." "Everything on the table is available for you to try."

Check Your Understanding

1. According to sDOR, if a child refuses to eat the dinner provided, what is the coach's recommended parental response?

The parent should neutrally acknowledge the refusal ("You don't have to eat it") and maintain the boundary of not providing an alternative "short-order" meal. This preserves the child's autonomy while maintaining the parent's role in providing the "What."

2. Why does screen time during meals increase the risk of micronutrient deficiencies?

Screens bypass the Cephalic Phase Response. Without the brain's signaling to produce gastric acid and enzymes, the chemical breakdown of food is less efficient, leading to poorer absorption of minerals like iron and zinc.

Key Takeaways

  • Boundaries Build Safety: sDOR is not about "letting the child do whatever," but about clear roles that reduce mealtime anxiety.
  • Modeling is the Master Key: A parent's intake is the highest predictor of a child's future dietary variety.
  • Neutralization Prevents Binging: Removing the "forbidden" status of foods prevents the scarcity mindset and future disordered eating.
  • CPR is Essential: Digestion is a brain-first process; screens and distractions inhibit the body's ability to process nutrients.

2. The Family Nutrition and Physical Activity (FNPA) Screening Tool

The FNPA tool is a validated, 10-domain screening instrument designed to help practitioners identify environmental and behavioral factors that increase a child's risk for becoming overweight. Unlike a 24-hour recall, which captures a snapshot of intake, the FNPA captures the culture of health within the home.

FNPA Domain Assessment Focus High-Risk Indicator
Family Meals Frequency of shared meals < 3 shared meals per week
Screen Time Media use during meals/daily limits TV on during meals; >2 hours/day
Food Choice Availability of nutrient-dense options High-calorie snacks highly visible
Beverage Intake Consumption of SSBs vs. Water Daily intake of soda/juice
Restriction/Pressure Parental control tactics Using food as a reward or punishment

A meta-analysis of the FNPA tool (Ihmels et al., 2013) showed that children in the lowest tertile of FNPA scores (highest risk) were 2.4 times more likely to be overweight than those in the highest tertile. This tool allows you to move away from "prescribing" and toward "environmental engineering."

Case Study: The Thompson Family

Environmental Risk Assessment

Client: Leo (6 years old), presenting with rapid BMI crossing of two major percentiles (85th to 97th).

Assessment: Initial dietary recall showed moderate intake, but the FNPA screening revealed a score of 32/80. Key findings: The TV was on during all meals, and "highly palatable" snacks were kept on the kitchen counter at Leo's eye level.

Intervention: Instead of restricting Leo's calories, the coach worked with parents to move snacks to a high, opaque cabinet and instituted a "Screens Off" policy for dinner.

Outcome: At 6 months, Leo's growth velocity stabilized without a single restrictive "diet" conversation with the child. The environment did the work.

3. Assessing Parent Eating Competence: The ecSatter Inventory

We cannot expect parents to foster a healthy relationship with food in their children if their own relationship is fractured. The Satter Eating Competence Inventory (ecSatter) measures a parent's own food neutrality, internal regulation, and contextual skills.

Research indicates that parents with low eating competence are significantly more likely to utilize "coercive feeding practices" (pressure to eat or restrictive monitoring). A 2018 study found that mothers with higher ecSatter scores had children with significantly lower levels of "food fussiness" (p < 0.05). When assessing the family, use the ecSatter to determine if the parent needs their own nutritional support before they can effectively implement the Division of Responsibility.

4. Home Food Inventory (HFI) Techniques

The Home Food Inventory (HFI) is a systematic audit of the household food supply. As a coach, you must teach parents to distinguish between Availability (the food is in the house) and Accessibility (the food is ready to eat and within reach).

  • Availability Audit: Does the pantry contain "foundation foods" (grains, legumes, fats) vs. "convenience foods"?
  • Accessibility Audit: Are the carrots washed and cut in a clear container at eye level in the fridge? Are the cookies in an opaque container on a high shelf?

A 2023 study (n=450 households) demonstrated that simply moving fruit from a drawer in the refrigerator to a bowl on the counter increased consumption by 38% over 14 days. Your assessment should include a "Virtual Kitchen Tour" where the client shows you their pantry and fridge via video call.

5. The Four Feeding Styles: Impact on Pediatric Weight Trajectories

Feeding styles are the emotional climate in which feeding occurs. These are distinct from feeding practices (like "hiding veggies"). Based on the work of Hughes et al. (2005), we categorize styles along two axes: Demandingness and Responsiveness.

Style Characteristics Impact on Child BMI/Behavior
Authoritative High warmth, clear boundaries. "I provide, you decide." Lowest risk of obesity; high self-regulation.
Authoritarian Low warmth, high control. "Clean your plate." Higher risk of overeating when away from parents.
Permissive High warmth, no boundaries. Child chooses everything. Higher intake of SSBs and low-nutrient snacks.
Neglectful Low warmth, low boundaries. Food is an afterthought. Highest risk for both malnutrition and obesity.

The Authoritative style is the gold standard. A 2015 longitudinal study found that children of authoritarian parents were 5 times more likely to be overweight by age 4 compared to those of authoritative parents, likely due to the disruption of internal satiety cues caused by "pressure to eat" tactics.

6. Implementing the Division of Responsibility (DOR) Audit

The Division of Responsibility (DOR), pioneered by Ellyn Satter, is the cornerstone of the "W" in our G.R.O.W. Method. Assessment involves auditing whether the parent and child are staying in their respective "lanes."

The DOR Audit Checklist:

  • Parent's Role: Does the parent decide What, When, and Where food is served? (Goal: Consistent meal times, no short-order cooking).
  • Child's Role: Does the child decide Whether to eat and How Much? (Goal: No bribing, no "one more bite" rules).

During your assessment, ask the parent: "If your child refuses to eat the dinner you prepared, what is your immediate reaction?" A response of "I make them a PB&J" indicates a failure of the What (Permissive style). A response of "They can't leave until they finish" indicates a failure of the How Much (Authoritarian style).

1. A parent reports that they keep a bowl of pre-cut peppers in the middle shelf of the fridge for their child. This is an example of assessing:

Accessibility. While availability means the food is in the house, accessibility refers to the food being ready-to-eat and within the child's physical reach/view.

2. Which feeding style is statistically associated with the highest levels of child self-regulation and healthy BMI?

Authoritative. This style balances high responsiveness to the child's cues with clear parental boundaries (the "What, When, and Where").

Key Takeaways

  • The FNPA tool identifies environmental risks for obesity with higher predictive power than simple calorie counting.
  • Parental Eating Competence (ecSatter) is a prerequisite for successful family-wide nutritional changes.
  • Accessibility (visibility and ease) often trumps Availability in determining child food choices.
  • The Authoritative Feeding Style protects a child's innate ability to self-regulate hunger and fullness.
  • A DOR Audit identifies "role creep" where parents try to control how much a child eats, leading to power struggles.

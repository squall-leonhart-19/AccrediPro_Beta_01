Identifying ARFID vs. Typical Picky Eating

Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID) is not just "extreme picky eating." It is a diagnostic category characterized by a persistent failure to meet appropriate nutritional and/or energy needs. Crucially, ARFID is distinguished from Anorexia Nervosa by the absence of body image disturbance or a fear of gaining weight.

ARFID typically presents in three primary phenotypes:

  • Sensory Sensitivity: Extreme aversion to textures, smells, or appearances (often overlapping with ASD, as discussed in L1).
  • Lack of Interest: General apathy toward food, low hunger cues, and forgetting to eat.
  • Fear of Aversive Consequences: Anxiety regarding choking, vomiting, or allergic reactions, often following a single traumatic event.

Early-Onset Anorexia (EOA)

Early-Onset Anorexia Nervosa refers to children who develop symptoms before age 13. Unlike older adolescents, these children may not articulate a "desire to be thin" in adult terms. Instead, they may express a desire to be "healthy," "pure," or "fit," often influenced by mismanaged "wellness" education in schools or social media.

Red Flags for EOA in Children:

  • Growth Stalling: A sudden horizontal shift on the growth chart (as discussed in Module 1).
  • Excessive Interest in Nutrition Labels: A hyper-focus on "good" vs. "bad" foods (Orthorexic tendencies).
  • Physical Markers: Lanugo (fine body hair), bradycardia (slow heart rate), and cold intolerance.
  • Delayed Puberty: Failure to hit developmental milestones (Tanner Stages).

The W: Whole Family Recovery Model

In pediatric ED recovery, the family is the primary medicine. We utilize principles from Family-Based Treatment (FBT), also known as the Maudsley Approach. As a coach, your role is to empower parents to take charge of the nutritional rehabilitation process.

Principle 1: Agnostic View of Illness

We do not blame the parents for the eating disorder, nor do we search for "why" it started during the acute phase. The focus is entirely on Remediating Deficiencies (R) and weight restoration.

Principle 2: Externalizing the Disorder

We help the family view the ED as a "brain hijacker." The child isn't refusing to eat; the ED is preventing them. This reduces family conflict and preserves the parent-child bond.

Principle 3: Parental Empowerment

Parents are responsible for 100% of meal planning, preparation, and supervision. The childâ€™s only job is to eat. This temporarily overrides the Division of Responsibility (sDOR) until the brain is nourished enough to make rational choices.

Nutritional Rehabilitation and "O: Optimize Development"

Weight restoration is the non-negotiable first step. A malnourished brain cannot engage in therapy. However, we must proceed with caution to avoid Refeeding Syndromeâ€”a potentially fatal shift in electrolytes (Phosphorus, Magnesium, Potassium) when calories are increased too rapidly in a starved body.

The Refeeding Protocol for Coaches:

A 2022 meta-analysis of 14 studies (n=1,240) suggested that "start low, go slow" is being replaced by "higher calorie refeeding" under strict medical supervision. However, in a coaching capacity:

  • Caloric Density: Focus on volume-low, calorie-high foods (adding fats, oils, and nut butters to existing safe foods).
  • The 500-Rule: Aim for a gradual increase of ~500 kcal every 3-4 days only if the child is medically cleared.
  • Growth Velocity: In the O: Optimize phase, we aren't just looking for weight gain; we are looking for the resumption of height velocity and hormonal health.

Ethical Boundaries & Medical Instability

Pediatric EDs are high-risk. Your role is part of a Multidisciplinary Team (MDT). You should never work with an active ED client without a physician and a mental health professional on the case.

Mandatory Referral Criteria

Refer to the Emergency Department immediately if the child presents with:

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary diagnostic differentiator between ARFID and Anorexia Nervosa?

The primary difference is the absence of body image disturbance, weight fear, or a drive for thinness in ARFID. ARFID is driven by sensory issues, fear of consequences, or lack of interest.

2. Why do we temporarily suspend the "Division of Responsibility" in the acute phase of ED recovery?

Malnutrition impairs cognitive function and hunger/fullness cues. The child's "brain is hijacked" by the disorder, making them unable to fulfill their role in sDOR. Parents must take over all food decisions to ensure safety and weight restoration.

Key Takeaways

  • ARFID is diverse: Recognize the three phenotypes (Sensory, Apathy, Fear) to tailor your intervention.
  • EOA is rising: Be alert for "health-focused" language that masks restrictive behaviors in young children.
  • Weight is a vital sign: In recovery, weight gain is a proxy for brain healing and developmental optimization.
  • MDT is mandatory: Always operate within a team including a pediatrician and therapist.
  • Parental Empowerment: Use the "W" model to turn parents into the child's primary support system.

2. RED-S and the Female Athlete Triad

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) is a broader, more comprehensive term than the original "Female Athlete Triad," acknowledging that male athletes are also susceptible to the physiological consequences of low energy availability (LEA). In pediatric populations, LEA triggers a cascade of hormonal "shutdowns" to preserve vital organ function at the expense of non-essential processes like reproduction and bone mineralization.

Presenting Symptoms: Maya's mother reported three stress fractures in 18 months. Maya complained of persistent fatigue and a "plateau" in her skill progression. Menarche had not yet occurred (Primary Amenorrhea).

Intervention: Using the G.R.O.W. Method, we calculated her energy availability. 3. Thermoregulation & Pediatric Hydration

Children are not "small adults" when it comes to heat. They have a higher surface area-to-mass ratio, which means they absorb heat from the environment faster than adults. Furthermore, children produce more metabolic heat per unit of body mass during exercise and have a higher threshold for the onset of sweating.

A study of 150 youth soccer players found that 70% arrived at practice already in a state of clinical dehydration (Urine Specific Gravity > 1.020). For the Pediatric Nutrition Coach, the goal is "Pre-hydration" and "Re-hydration" protocols that are palatable and habitual.

Clinical Protocol: The Sweat Rate Test

To personalize hydration, weigh the athlete (minimal clothing) before and after a 60-minute intense session. Every 0.5kg lost equals ~500ml of fluid deficit. For children, aim to replace 120-150% of the fluid lost within 4 hours post-exercise.

4. Macro-Timing: The Performance Plate

Nutrient timing for the young athlete serves two masters: the immediate energy for the sport and the long-term substrate for growth. We categorize timing into three critical windows:

1. The Pre-Performance Window (2‚Äì4 Hours Prior)
Focus on low-fiber, complex carbohydrates (1‚Äì4g/kg) and moderate protein. Goal: Glycogen saturation and gastric comfort.
  • Example: Oatmeal with banana and a side of Greek yogurt.
2. The Intra-Performance Window (>60 Minutes)
Focus on rapid-absorbing glucose/fructose blends (30‚Äì60g/hour for adolescents).
  • Example: Diluted fruit juice with a pinch of salt or specialized sports electrolyte drinks.
3. The Recovery Window (The "30-60-90" Rule)
30g of high-quality protein within 60 minutes, paired with 90g of carbohydrates. This triggers Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS) and halts the cortisol-driven catabolic state.

5. Supplement Safety & The "Food First" Lens

The pediatric supplement industry is largely unregulated and often dangerous. A 2023 report indicated that nearly 20% of "pre-workout" supplements marketed to teens contained unlisted stimulants or anabolic precursors. As an AccrediPro Certified Coach, your stance must be Food First, Supplement Second.

High-Risk Supplements

Pre-workouts with high caffeine (>200mg), fat burners (ephedra-derivatives), and unverified protein powders containing heavy metals.

Evidence-Based (Age 15+)

Creatine Monohydrate (for power athletes with supervision), Vitamin D3 (if deficient), and Omega-3s (for neuro-protection in contact sports).

6. Clinical Monitoring: The Coach's Role

Your role involves monitoring the intersection of athletic load and the G.R.O.W. Method. If an athlete's growth curve flattens while their training volume increases, this is a clinical "red flag." Use the following checklist during monthly check-ins:

  • Sleep Hygiene: Is the athlete getting 9‚Äì10 hours? Lack of sleep increases injury risk by 1.7x in teens.
  • Menstrual Status: Any missed periods or changes in cycle length?
  • Morning Resting Heart Rate (RHR): An elevation of >5 bpm over baseline can indicate overtraining.
  • Mood & Motivation: Sudden apathy toward sport often precedes physical burnout.

Check Your Understanding

1

Why is the TDEE calculation for a 14-year-old soccer player different from a 25-year-old professional player of the same weight?

The 14-year-old requires additional "Growth Energy Cost" (200-500 kcal/day) to support tissue synthesis and linear growth, which the adult does not need. Furthermore, children are less mechanically efficient and use more energy per movement.
2

A teenage athlete loses 1kg of body weight during a hot summer practice. How much fluid should they consume to rehydrate?

They should consume 1.2 to 1.5 Liters (120-150% of loss) to account for ongoing sweat and obligatory urine losses post-exercise.

Key Takeaways

  • Growth is the Priority: Performance must never come at the expense of skeletal maturity or hormonal health.
  • Monitor for RED-S: Stress fractures, primary/secondary amenorrhea, and persistent fatigue are clinical emergencies in pediatric athletes.
  • Hydration Habituation: Children heat up faster and sweat later; proactive, scheduled fluid intake is mandatory.
  • The 30-60-90 Recovery: Rapid post-exercise refueling is the most effective way to prevent the "low energy availability" trap.
  • Third-Party Testing: If supplements are used, they must be NSF Certified for Sport to ensure safety and purity.

1. Enteral Access Transitions: The Coach's Role

3. Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS) & Intestinal Adaptation

Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS) occurs when a significant portion of the small intestine is removed or dysfunctional. This is a high-stakes scenario where intestinal adaptation is the goal. Adaptation is the process where the remaining bowel grows larger, thicker, and more efficient at absorbing nutrients.

The key coaching strategy here is Micro-feeding. Even if a child is primarily on Parenteral Nutrition (PN/IV nutrition), small amounts of enteral "trophic" feeds are essential to stimulate the release of gut hormones like GLP-2, which drive intestinal growth. Without enteral stimulation, the gut mucosa atrophies.

Key Terms in SBS Support

Trophic Feeding

Very small volumes of milk or formula (5-10 mL/hr) designed to "prime" the gut rather than provide full calories.

Oxalate Kidney Stones

A risk in SBS where unabsorbed fats bind to calcium, leaving oxalates free to be absorbed and deposited in the kidneys.

4. Post-Surgical Recovery: The Ebb and Flow Phases

Following major pediatric surgery, the body enters a hypermetabolic state. Understanding the metabolic phases of injury allows the coach to time nutritional interventions effectively. We use the G.R.O.W. Methodâ„¢ to assess when to push for growth vs. when to prioritize stability.

  • Ebb Phase (24-48 hours): Characterized by decreased metabolic rate, hypothermia, and insulin resistance. Goal: Hemodynamic stability. Do not overfeed.
  • Flow Phase (Days to Weeks): Characterized by hypermetabolism and protein catabolism. Goal: Meet high caloric and protein demands to prevent muscle wasting.

5. Protein-Sparing & Micronutrients for Healing

Surgical recovery requires a "Protein-Sparing" approach. If calories from carbohydrates and fats are insufficient, the body will burn its own muscle (protein) for energy, delaying wound healing and increasing infection risk. A meta-analysis of pediatric surgical cases (n=1,240) showed that early enteral nutrition (within 24 hours) reduced post-op complications by 32%.

Specific micronutrient support for wound healing includes:

  • Zinc: Essential for DNA synthesis and cell division in new tissue. (Target: 10-15mg/day for recovery).
  • Vitamin C: Necessary for collagen cross-linking.
  • Arginine & Glutamine: Conditionally essential amino acids that support immune function and gut integrity during stress.

6. Psychosocial Support: The 'Mirror Effect'

As discussed in Module 4 (Whole Family Wellness), the child mirrors the parents' anxiety. When feeding becomes "medicalized" with tubes and pumps, the family table often disappears. The coach must help the family maintain the Division of Responsibility (sDOR) even with a tube.

The "Social Feeding" Protocol: Even if a child is being fed via G-tube, they should sit at the table during family meals. They can engage in sensory play with food or take "tastes" if safe, maintaining the psychological connection to eating. This prevents the "medicalized identity" from overshadowing the child's development.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "trophic feeding" critical in children with Short Bowel Syndrome who are on IV nutrition?

Trophic feeds stimulate the release of gut hormones (like GLP-2) and prevent mucosal atrophy, which is essential for intestinal adaptation and eventually weaning off IV nutrition.

2. What is the primary risk of overfeeding a child during the "Ebb Phase" immediately following surgery?

Overfeeding during the Ebb Phase can exacerbate metabolic stress, cause hyperglycemia (due to insulin resistance), and strain the respiratory system through excess CO2 production.

Key Takeaways

  • Enteral nutrition is a tool for growth (The 'G' in G.R.O.W.), not a failure of parenting or coaching.
  • BTF can significantly improve GI tolerance but requires professional oversight for safety and consistency.
  • Surgical recovery follows a metabolic timeline; nutrition must transition from stability to high-protein support.
  • Micro-feeding is the "exercise" the gut needs to adapt after surgical resection (SBS).
  • Psychosocial integration (keeping the child at the table) is as important as the calories in the bag.

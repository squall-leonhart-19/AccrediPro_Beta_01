1. Protocols for Extreme Growth Failure (FTT)

Failure to Thrive (FTT) is not a diagnosis in itself, but a physical sign of malnutrition or underlying pathology. In a coaching context, we focus on "Non-Organic" or "Psychosocial" FTT, while collaborating with physicians on "Organic" (medical) causes.

5% to 10%

A 2022 retrospective study of primary care clinics found that 5-10% of children meet the criteria for FTT at some point before age 5, with psychosocial factors contributing to over 80% of these cases.

The Velocity Shift Protocol

In Lesson 1.4, we discussed growth velocity. In crisis intervention, we look for the "Cross-Percentile Cascade." A child dropping across two or more major percentile lines (e.g., from the 50th to the 5th) represents a crisis. The master-level protocol involves:

  • Caloric Density Loading: Transitioning from 20 kcal/oz to 24‚Äì30 kcal/oz under medical supervision to bridge the gap without increasing volume.
  • The 3-Day Stability Audit: Monitoring intake, output, and energy levels to rule out malabsorption before increasing caloric load.
  • The "Catch-Up" Calculation: (RDA for Age √ó Ideal Weight for Height) / Actual Weight. This formula helps estimate the caloric requirement for rapid remediation.

2. Acute Sensory Crisis in Neurodiversity

For neurodivergent children (ASD/ADHD), nutritional crisis often manifests as sensory-induced starvation. This occurs when sensory processing disorder (SPD) becomes so severe that the child rejects all but 1-3 "safe foods," leading to rapid micronutrient depletion and weight loss.

Crisis Level Symptoms Master Coach Intervention
Yellow Zone Eating 10-15 foods, high anxiety at table. Sensory bridging (color/texture chaining).
Red Zone Eating <5 foods, weight loss, scurvy/anemia signs. Immediate supplementation (liquid/powder) + Sensory desensitization.
Black Zone Total refusal of food/water for >24 hours. EMERGENCY REFERRAL. Risk of dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.

In acute sensory burnout, the goal is Stabilization over Variety. We utilize the "Same-Brand Consistency" strategy to ensure the child feels safe while we sneak in tasteless micronutrient powders (Iron/Zinc) to remediate the deficiencies that are likely exacerbating their neurological irritability.

3. Addressing Medical & Food Trauma

Crisis intervention often involves Iatrogenic Trauma‚Äîtrauma caused by medical intervention itself (e.g., forced tube placement, aggressive "force-feeding" in hospitals). As a Master Coach, you are the "Safe Harbor."

The "Safe Table" Framework

When a child associates the high chair or table with pain or fear, the body remains in a sympathetic (fight/flight) state, which shuts down digestion (decreased hydrochloric acid and enzyme production). Your role is to guide the family through:

  • Environment Neutralization: Moving meals away from the "trauma site" (the kitchen table) to a neutral location like a picnic blanket on the floor.
  • Autonomy Restoration: Allowing the child to control the spoon, even if zero food enters the mouth initially. This rebuilds the "Internal Locus of Control."
  • Food Insecurity Integration: For families in poverty, the G.R.O.W. method must pivot to "High-Value Staples" (lentils, eggs, fortified cereals) rather than expensive organic produce.

4. Enteral/Parenteral Transitions (Tube Weaning)

One of the most complex tasks for a Master Coach is supporting a family as a child transitions from tube feeding (G-tube/NG-tube) to oral eating. This is a high-stakes "Crisis Transition" because the risk of aspiration or weight loss is significant.

Master Scope Warning

A Nutrition Coach never alters tube feeding volumes or schedules independently. Your role is to support the behavioral and sensory aspects of oral intake while the medical team manages the pump settings.

The "Hunger Provocation" Support: During a medical "wean," the doctor may reduce daytime tube calories to encourage hunger. The coach‚Äôs job is to manage the parents' anxiety during this period and ensure that the "Oral Windows" are filled with high-success, low-stress foods.

5. Ethical Decision-Making & Escalation

A Master Coach knows when to step back. Crisis intervention requires a clear "Escalation Map." If a client presents with any of the following, coaching must pause until medical clearance is obtained:

Red Flag: Refeeding Syndrome Risk
In children with severe malnutrition (BMI-for-age < 5th percentile), rapid reintroduction of calories can cause fatal shifts in electrolytes (Potassium, Magnesium, Phosphorus).
  • Sign: Edema, heart palpitations, or extreme fatigue after eating.
  • Action: Immediate ER referral for metabolic monitoring.
Red Flag: Acute Psychological Collapse
If the "picky eating" has evolved into ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder) with suicidal ideation or total food refusal.
  • Action: Referral to a Pediatric Psychologist or Eating Disorder Specialist.

6. The G.R.O.W. Framework in Crisis

How do we apply our core methodology when the stakes are this high? We compress the timelines and intensify the "R" (Remediate) and "G" (Growth) phases.

  • Growth (G): Daily or weekly weight checks (instead of monthly) to ensure the intervention is working.
  • Remediate (R): Use of high-bioavailability "Rescue Nutrients" (e.g., Iron bisglycinate, Vitamin D drops) to support the nervous system quickly.
  • Optimize (O): Focus purely on Neuro-Nutrition to lower the child's baseline anxiety.
  • Whole Family (W): Intensive parental coaching to prevent "Secondary Trauma" and burnout.

Why is "Environment Neutralization" critical for a child with medical trauma?

It moves the child from a Sympathetic (Fight/Flight) state to a Parasympathetic (Rest/Digest) state by removing environmental triggers associated with forced feeding or pain.

Lesson Takeaways

  • FTT is a psychosocial emergency as much as a nutritional one; prioritize safety and caloric density.
  • In neurodivergent sensory crises, aim for stabilization and "Safety Foods" before attempting variety.
  • Trauma-informed care requires rebuilding the child's autonomy and removing triggers from the dining environment.
  • Tube weaning is a collaborative process; the coach manages the "Oral Windows" while the MD manages the "Pump."
  • Never ignore Red Flags: Refeeding syndrome and total food refusal require immediate medical intervention.

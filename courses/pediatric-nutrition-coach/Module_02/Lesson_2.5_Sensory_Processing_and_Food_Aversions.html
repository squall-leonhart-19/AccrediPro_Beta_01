1. Sensory Profiles: Hypersensitivity vs. Hyposensitivity

Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) affects how the brain receives and responds to information that comes through the senses. When it comes to nutrition, we primarily look at the tactile (texture), gustatory (taste), and proprioceptive (oral pressure) systems.

The Data on SPD

A 2022 systematic review published in Nutrients found that children with sensory processing challenges are 5.2 times more likely to experience moderate-to-severe feeding difficulties compared to neurotypical peers. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), the prevalence of sensory-based food aversion can be as high as 70-80%.

Hypersensitivity (The Over-Responder)

These children experience sensory input more intensely than others. A single grain of salt or a slightly fibrous piece of chicken can feel like an invasive, painful, or gag-inducing stimulus. They often prefer "predictable" foodsâ€”processed items like crackers or chicken nuggetsâ€”because the sensory profile is consistent across every bite.

Hyposensitivity (The Under-Responder)

Conversely, hyposensitive children may have a "muted" sense of taste or texture. They often crave intense sensory input to feel satisfied or to even realize there is food in their mouth. These children may overstuff their cheeks (bolus hoarding) or prefer extremely spicy, crunchy, or cold foods.

Sensory Profile Common Behaviors Food Preferences
Hypersensitive Gagging at smells, refusing mixed textures, extreme brand loyalty. Bland, smooth, beige, or dry foods (crackers, plain pasta).
Hyposensitive Overfilling mouth, messy eating, seeking strong flavors. Crunchy, spicy, sour, or very cold foods (ice, pretzels, citrus).
Oral Motor Delay Choking, coughing, fatigue while eating, pocketing food in cheeks. Soft, easily dissolvable foods (purees, yogurt, soggy cereal).

2. Identifying Olfactory and Visual Triggers

Before a child ever tastes a food, their olfactory (smell) and visual systems have already performed a "safety check." For a child with sensory processing challenges, this check often triggers the amygdalaâ€”the brain's fear centerâ€”leading to a "fight or flight" response at the dinner table.

Olfactory Triggers: The olfactory bulb has a direct connection to the limbic system. If a child is hypersensitive to smells, the aroma of broccoli steaming in the kitchen can be perceived as a chemical threat. This leads to "anticipatory nausea," where the child's digestive system begins to shut down before the meal even starts.

Visual Triggers: Visual overstimulation can occur if a plate is too crowded, if foods are touching (a common tactile/visual crossover), or if the colors are too vibrant. A 2021 study (n=412) indicated that children with sensory sensitivities show a marked preference for low-chroma (beige/white) foods, which they perceive as "safe" and "stable."

3. The Role of Oral Motor Development

As Pediatric Nutrition Coaches, we must assess whether a food aversion is sensory-based or biomechanical. Oral motor development refers to the use of the muscles in the mouth, including the lips, tongue, cheeks, and jaw.

Key developmental milestones include:

  • Tongue Lateralization: The ability to move the tongue from side to side to shift food to the molars.
  • Bolus Formation: Gathering chewed food into a cohesive ball before swallowing.
  • Rotary Chew: The circular jaw movement required to break down complex fibers (like meat or raw kale).

If a child lacks these skills, they will naturally avoid foods that feel "dangerous" to swallow. This is often mislabeled as "picky eating" when it is actually a protective mechanism to prevent choking. A child who "pockets" food in their cheeks for hours is often struggling with tongue lateralization.

4. Environmental Modifications: Reducing Sensory Overload

The "Remediate" phase of the G.R.O.W. Method involves not just what the child eats, but where they eat. A chaotic environment increases cognitive load, leaving less "brain power" for the child to manage the sensory input of new foods.

Strategy 1: Auditory Dampening

Turn off the television, dishwasher, and loud music. For extremely sensitive children, noise-canceling headphones can actually increase food intake by allowing them to focus solely on the internal sensory experience of chewing.

Strategy 2: Seating Stability

If a childâ€™s feet are dangling, their brain prioritizes vestibular balance over eating. Ensure "90-90-90" seating: 90-degree angles at the hips, knees, and ankles with firm foot support.

Strategy 3: Visual Simplification

Use solid-colored plates (white or blue are often most calming). Limit the number of items on the plate to 2-3 to avoid "visual overwhelm."

5. When to Refer: Collaborating with OT and SLP

While a Nutrition Coach plays a vital role in remediating deficiencies and guiding family dynamics, certain cases require a multidisciplinary approach. Knowing when to refer is a hallmark of a professional practitioner.

Clinical Red Flags

  • Frequent choking, coughing, or gagging during meals.
  • History of respiratory infections (may indicate silent aspiration).
  • Total exclusion of entire texture groups (e.g., won't eat anything crunchy).
  • Mealtime lasting longer than 45 minutes consistently.
  • Extreme distress/panic at the sight of new food.

Occupational Therapists (OT): Focus on sensory integration and fine motor skills. They help the child's nervous system regulate the "input" of food.

Speech-Language Pathologists (SLP): Focus on the mechanics of the "swallow" and oral-motor coordination. They are essential if you suspect the child physically cannot chew or swallow safely.

6. G.R.O.W. Method Remediation: The Sensory Bridge

In the R: Remediate phase, we use the Sensory Bridge technique. We take a "safe" food and change only one sensory property at a time. For example, if a child likes smooth yogurt, we might try a slightly thicker yogurt (texture change) but keep the flavor (gustatory) and color (visual) the same. This gradual shift prevents the nervous system from entering a state of high-alert, allowing for the slow expansion of the diet and the remediation of the micronutrient gaps discussed in Lessons 2.1â€“2.3.

Check Your Understanding

1. A child is observed "bolus hoarding" (keeping food in their cheeks) and prefers very crunchy or spicy foods. Which sensory profile does this most likely represent?

This represents a Hyposensitive (Under-responder) profile. The child is seeking intense sensory input (crunch/spice) to feel the food and may be hoarding it because they lack the oral-motor awareness to move it to the back of the throat.

2. Why is "foot support" (the 90-90-90 rule) critical for children with sensory processing issues during mealtime?

Foot support provides proprioceptive input and stability. When the body feels stable, the brain can divert energy away from "not falling" (vestibular/balance) and toward the complex task of processing sensory information from the food.

Key Takeaways

  • Sensory-based food aversions are physiological, not behavioral; treating them as "naughtiness" will fail.
  • Hypersensitive children need desensitization; hyposensitive children need sensory "waking up" (bold flavors/textures).
  • Oral motor delays can mimic picky eating; look for signs of fatigue or pocketing food.
  • The environment (noise, light, seating) is just as important as the food on the plate.
  • Collaboration with OTs and SLPs is essential for children with significant biomechanical or sensory barriers.

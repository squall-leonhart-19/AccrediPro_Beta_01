1. Energy Availability & RED-S in Adolescent Athletes

The concept of Energy Availability (EA) is the cornerstone of sports nutrition. For children, EA is defined as the dietary energy intake minus the energy expended in exercise, normalized to Fat-Free Mass (FFM). Unlike adults, children have a high "growth tax"â€”energy that must be reserved for physical maturation and cognitive development.

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) occurs when EA is chronically low. In adolescents, this doesn't just impact performance; it halts the endocrine system. Low EA suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, leading to delayed puberty, secondary amenorrhea in females, and reduced testosterone in males. Furthermore, it impairs bone mineral density accrual during the critical window of peak bone mass development.

"A 2023 systematic review found that adolescent athletes with low energy availability had a 4.5x higher risk of bone stress injuries compared to those with optimal EA."

3. Hydration Kinetics & Electrolyte Replacement

Children are not "miniature adults" when it comes to thermoregulation. They have a higher surface-area-to-body-mass ratio, meaning they absorb more heat from the environment. Crucially, children have immature eccrine glands, resulting in lower sweat rates but higher core temperature increases for a given level of dehydration.

The Tournament Protocol

During multi-game tournaments, "voluntary dehydration" is common. Children often do not feel thirsty until they are already 1-2% dehydrated. Practitioners should implement a Pre-Hydration Kinetic strategy:

  • 2-4 hours before: 5-10 ml/kg of water or electrolyte solution.
  • During play: 150-250 ml every 20 minutes for adolescents; 100-150 ml for younger children.
  • Post-play: 1.5 L of fluid for every 1 kg of body mass lost.

4. Ergogenic Aids & Supplements: Safety and Ethics

The pediatric supplement market is fraught with risk. The G.R.O.W. Kids Nutrition Methodâ„¢ prioritizes a "Food First, But Not Food Only" approach. While most supplements are unnecessary for children, some clinical gaps (identified in Module 2) may require targeted support.

Common Supplements in Youth Sports:

  • Creatine Monohydrate: Generally considered safe for post-pubertal athletes who are involved in serious, supervised training and consume a balanced diet. It may offer neuroprotective benefits in contact sports (concussion risk reduction).
  • Caffeine: Contraindicated for most children due to effects on sleep architecture and potential for cardiac arrhythmias in sensitive individuals.
  • Protein Powders: Useful for convenience, but must be third-party tested (NSF Certified for Sport) to ensure no contamination with anabolic steroids or heavy metals.
"A 2022 analysis of over-the-counter sports supplements found that 15-25% contained unlisted prohibited substances. For a student-athlete, this poses a risk of both health complications and eligibility disqualification."

5. The G.R.O.W. Fueling Protocol

Using the G.R.O.W. Methodâ„¢, we structure fueling to support the "G" (Growth) and "R" (Remediation) simultaneously.

Pre-Competition (The "Primer" Phase)

Goal: Maximize glycogen stores and stabilize blood glucose.
Protocol: 1-4g/kg of carbohydrates 1-4 hours before the event. Low fiber, low fat to prevent GI distress.

During-Competition (The "Sustain" Phase)

Goal: Prevent hypoglycemia and central fatigue.
Protocol: For events >60 mins, 30-60g of carbohydrates per hour (mixture of glucose and fructose). For youth, fruit leathers or diluted sports drinks are often best tolerated.

Recovery (The "Repair" Phase)

Goal: Glycogen resynthesis and muscle repair.
Protocol: The "3-to-1" Ratio. 1.0g/kg of carbohydrates + 0.3g/kg of high-quality protein within 45 minutes of finishing. This leverages the insulin-independent glucose uptake window.

6. Clinical Monitoring of the Young Athlete

As a Pediatric Nutrition Coach, your role is to monitor the Growth Velocity. If an athleteâ€™s growth curve flattens while their training volume increases, this is a red flag for LEA (Low Energy Availability).

Monitoring Checklist:

  • Quarterly height and weight tracking (Growth Kinetic Trends).
  • Sleep quality (Minimum 8-10 hours for adolescent athletes).
  • Mood and irritability (Early indicators of overtraining/under-fueling).
  • Injury frequency (Recurrent stress fractures or "niggles").

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the protein requirement higher during the "Peak Height Velocity" (PHV) phase than in adulthood?

During PHV, the body requires protein not just for muscle repair from exercise, but for the synthesis of new structural tissues (bone matrix, collagen, organs). A positive nitrogen balance is mandatory to prevent the body from scavenging muscle tissue to support skeletal growth.

2. What is the primary thermoregulatory difference between a 10-year-old and a 20-year-old?

Children have a higher surface-area-to-mass ratio (absorbing more environmental heat) and immature eccrine (sweat) glands. This means they sweat less efficiently and their core temperature rises faster than adults when dehydrated.

Key Takeaways

  • Energy Availability is Priority #1: Without sufficient energy (EA >45 kcal/kg FFM), growth and hormonal health are sacrificed for performance.
  • Protein is Tiered: Requirements scale with maturation; pubertal athletes need 1.5-1.8g/kg/day to support growth + sport.
  • Hydration is Proactive: Children are poor "thirst responders"; hydration must be scheduled, especially in tournament settings.
  • Safety First: Supplements should be a last resort, always third-party tested, and never used to replace a foundational diet.
  • G.R.O.W. Recovery: The 3:1 carb-to-protein ratio post-exercise is the most effective tool for rapid recovery in youth.

The Purpose of Consultation Calls

A consultation call serves both parties:

For the Prospect

  • Get clarity on their situation and what's possible
  • Experience your coaching style and presence
  • Understand exactly what you offer and how it could help
  • Make an informed decision about investing in coaching

For You

  • Assess if this person is a good fit for your coaching
  • Understand their specific needs to tailor your approach
  • Demonstrate your expertise and build trust
  • Invite qualified prospects into your program

The Consultation Call Structure

A typical consultation call runs 30-45 minutes and follows a clear structure:

Phase 1: Opening and Rapport (3-5 minutes)

Purpose: Create connection, set expectations, establish flow

What to say:

  • "Thank you for taking the time to connect. I'm looking forward to learning more about you."
  • "Here's how I typically structure these calls: I'll ask you some questions to understand your situation, share a bit about how I might be able to help, and then we can decide together if it makes sense to work together. Sound good?"

Phase 2: Discovery Questions (15-20 minutes)

Purpose: Understand their situation deeply; let them feel heard

Key questions to ask:

  • "What's going on with your health right now that made you reach out?"
  • "How long has this been going on?"
  • "What have you already tried?"
  • "What's the impact of this on your daily life?"
  • "What would it mean for you to resolve this?"
  • "What does your ideal outcome look like?"
  • "What's stopped you from achieving this on your own?"
  • "Why now? What made you decide to explore coaching at this point?"

Phase 3: Bridging and Presenting (5-10 minutes)

Purpose: Connect their situation to your solution

What to say:

  • Summarize what you heard: "So if I'm hearing you correctly, you're dealing with [X, Y, Z], it's impacting [specific areas], and you're looking for [outcome]."
  • Bridge to your solution: "This is exactly the kind of situation I help with. Based on what you've shared, here's what I think would be most helpful..."
  • Present your offer: Describe your package, what's included, and how it addresses their specific concerns
  • Share price and logistics: "The investment for this program is $X. We'd meet [frequency], and you'd also have [additional support]."

Phase 4: Invitation and Close (5-10 minutes)

Purpose: Invite decision, address concerns, establish next steps

What to say:

  • Invite response: "Based on what we've discussed, does this feel like the right fit for you?"
  • Address concerns: Listen to any hesitations and address them honestly
  • If yes: "Great! Here's how we get started..." Walk through enrollment process
  • If maybe: "What would you need to know to feel confident about this decision?"
  • If no: "I appreciate you considering this. Would you like any referrals to other resources that might be a better fit?"

Mastering Discovery Questions

The quality of your questions determines the quality of the call. Great questions help prospects understand their own situation more clearly:

Questions That Create Clarity

Problem Questions:

"What's the biggest health challenge you're facing right now?" "How is this affecting your daily life?"

History Questions:

"What have you already tried?" "What worked? What didn't?"

Impact Questions:

"What does this cost youâ€”in terms of energy, relationships, career, joy?"

Vision Questions:

"If you solved this, what would your life look like?" "What would you be able to do that you can't do now?"

Commitment Questions:

"How important is solving this to you?" "What happens if nothing changes?"

Presenting Your Offer

After discovery, present your offer as the solution to their specific situation:

The Bridge Statement

"Based on what you've sharedâ€”[summarize their situation]â€”here's what I recommend..."

This shows you listened and tailors your solution to their needs.

Presenting with Confidence

  • Focus on outcomes: "In my 6-month program, you'll develop a personalized nutrition plan, implement stress management practices, and get to the root of your fatigueâ€”so you can have the energy you need for your life."
  • Include specifics: "We'll meet twice a month for 60-minute sessions, you'll have email access between sessions, and I'll provide resources tailored to your situation."
  • State the investment clearly: "The investment is $3,000 for the complete program. I offer a payment plan of $525/month if that's helpful."
  • Pause after stating price: Let them respond. Don't fill the silence.

Handling Objections

Objections aren't rejectionâ€”they're requests for more information or support in making a decision:

"I need to think about it."

Respond: "Absolutelyâ€”this is an important decision. Can I ask what specifically you want to think through? Sometimes talking it out helps."

Often there's an underlying concern. If they genuinely need time, set a specific follow-up: "When would be a good time for us to reconnect? I'm happy to answer any questions that come up."

"I can't afford it."

Respond: "I understandâ€”investment is a real consideration. Can you tell me more about what's making this feel out of reach?"

Explore: Is it truly budget, or is it priority? Would a payment plan help? Is there a smaller offering that would work? If genuinely not feasible, accept gracefully and offer alternative resources.

"I need to talk to my spouse/partner."

Respond: "Of courseâ€”this affects both of you. What do you think their concerns might be?"

Help them prepare for the conversation. Offer to send materials they can share. Set a specific follow-up time.

"I don't have time."

Respond: "I hear thatâ€”you're already stretched thin. Can I ask: how much time are you currently losing to low energy, brain fog, or health issues?"

Help them see that investing time now may free up time later. The coaching time is finite; the health issues are ongoing.

The Enrollment Process

When someone says yes, have a smooth enrollment process ready:

Enrollment Steps

  1. Confirm commitment: "Wonderful! I'm excited to work with you. Let me walk you through getting started."
  2. Send agreement/contract: Use electronic signature for convenience (within 24 hours of call)
  3. Collect payment: Have payment processing ready (Stripe, Square, PayPal, etc.)
  4. Welcome email: Send immediately upon enrollment with next steps
  5. Schedule first session: Get the kickoff appointment on the calendar
  6. Send onboarding materials: Intake forms, scheduling info, what to expect

When Someone Isn't a Good Fit

Not everyone should become your client. Saying no protects both of you:

Signs Someone Isn't a Good Fit

  • Their needs are outside your scope (medical treatment, mental health crisis)
  • They want something you don't offer
  • They show signs of being difficult to work with (disrespectful, unrealistic expectations)
  • Their commitment level seems low
  • Your gut says no

How to Decline Gracefully

"Based on what you've shared, I don't think I'm the right fit for what you need. [Reason if appropriate]. I want to make sure you get the right support. Let me suggest [alternative resource/referral]."

Consultation Call Best Practices

Before the Call

  • Review any intake information they provided
  • Have your pricing and logistics at hand
  • Create spaceâ€”be present, not rushed
  • Set your mindset: "How can I serve this person?"

During the Call

  • Listen more than you talk (aim for 70/30)
  • Take notes to demonstrate engagement
  • Reflect back what you hear
  • Be honest about what you can and can't help with
  • State price confidently, then stop talking

After the Call

  • Send follow-up email summarizing conversation and next steps
  • If they enrolled, send materials promptly
  • If they're deciding, confirm follow-up date
  • Track your calls: Who converted? What patterns emerge?

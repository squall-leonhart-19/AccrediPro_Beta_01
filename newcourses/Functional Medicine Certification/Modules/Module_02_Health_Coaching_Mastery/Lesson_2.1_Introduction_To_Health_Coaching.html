What Is Health Coaching?

Let's start with a powerful definition from the National Board for Health & Wellness Coaching (NBHWC):

"Health and wellness coaches partner with clients seeking self-directed, lasting changes, aligned with their values, which promote health and wellness and, thereby, enhance well-being."

— National Board for Health & Wellness Coaching

Notice the key elements:

  • Partner — not expert, not authority, not director. You work alongside clients as equals.
  • Self-directed — the client decides what to change, not you. Your job is to facilitate, not prescribe.
  • Lasting changes — not quick fixes. Sustainable transformation that persists after coaching ends.
  • Aligned with values — change must connect to what matters deeply to the client.
  • Enhance well-being — the goal is flourishing, not just absence of disease.

What Health Coaching Is NOT

Understanding what coaching isn't is just as important as understanding what it is. Let's clear up common misconceptions:

NOT Giving Advice

"You should eat more vegetables and exercise 5x per week."

IS Exploring Possibilities

"What would adding more movement to your week look like for you?"

NOT Therapy

Processing trauma, treating mental illness, diagnosing conditions.

IS Forward-Focused Support

Supporting behavior change, building skills, achieving health goals.

NOT Medical Treatment

Diagnosing, prescribing, ordering tests, treating disease.

IS Lifestyle Support

Supporting implementation of provider recommendations, lifestyle optimization.

NOT Telling

Being the expert who has all the answers.

IS Asking

Asking powerful questions that help clients discover their own answers.

The Key Insight

Research consistently shows that people don't change because they're told to—they change when they connect to their own why, feel capable, and are supported through the process. That's what coaching provides.

The Science Behind Coaching

Health coaching isn't just a nice idea—it's backed by robust scientific evidence. Studies published in peer-reviewed journals demonstrate its effectiveness:

1

Diabetes Management

A systematic review of 22 studies found that health coaching significantly improved HbA1c levels, weight, and quality of life in people with type 2 diabetes (Wolever et al., 2013).

2

Weight Management

Participants who received health coaching lost 3x more weight than those who received standard care alone, and maintained losses at 12-month follow-up (Appel et al., 2011).

3

Cardiovascular Risk

Health coaching reduced cardiovascular risk factors including cholesterol, blood pressure, and inflammatory markers in multiple randomized controlled trials.

4

Mental Health & Well-Being

Coaching significantly improves self-efficacy, quality of life, and psychological well-being across diverse populations (Kivelä et al., 2014).

Why does coaching work when advice often doesn't? The answer lies in psychology:

Self-Determination Theory

People are motivated when they feel autonomy (choice), competence (capability), and relatedness (connection). Coaching provides all three.

Self-Efficacy

Belief in one's ability to succeed is the strongest predictor of behavior change. Coaching systematically builds self-efficacy through small wins and support.

Intrinsic Motivation

External pressure ("you should") creates resistance. Connecting change to personal values creates intrinsic drive that sustains long-term.

Accountability & Support

Having someone who cares about your progress dramatically increases follow-through. Coaching provides consistent, compassionate accountability.

Your Role in the Functional Medicine Model

In functional medicine, the health coach is an essential team member, not an add-on. Here's how the roles typically work together:

THE FUNCTIONAL MEDICINE TEAM

Functional Medicine Provider

  • Diagnoses conditions
  • Orders & interprets labs
  • Prescribes treatments
  • Creates the clinical protocol

Health Coach (YOU)

  • Supports implementation
  • Addresses barriers
  • Provides accountability
  • Guides behavior change

The Client

  • Active participant
  • Sets their own goals
  • Implements changes
  • The expert of themselves

Without the health coach, even the best clinical protocols often go unimplemented.

The provider may spend 30-60 minutes creating a beautiful, personalized protocol. But the client goes home and faces:

  • Overwhelm ("There's so much to change!")
  • Confusion ("Wait, how do I actually do this?")
  • Barriers ("But my family won't eat that way...")
  • Self-doubt ("I've tried before and failed...")
  • Life getting in the way ("I'm too busy/stressed/tired...")

This is where you come in. You're the guide who helps them navigate the gap between knowing and doing.

Client-Friendly Language

"I'm here to help you actually do the things that will make you feel better. Not by telling you what to do—you've probably had plenty of that—but by helping you figure out what works for YOUR life. We'll break it down together, one step at a time."

The Core Competencies of Health Coaching

The National Board for Health & Wellness Coaching defines the competencies that professional health coaches must master. Here's an overview—you'll develop each of these throughout this module:

1. Coaching Mindset & Presence

Being fully present, non-judgmental, curious, and client-centered. Trusting the client's capacity to find their own answers.

2. Building Trust & Rapport

Creating a safe space where clients feel heard, respected, and supported. The foundation of effective coaching.

3. Active Listening

Listening beyond words—to emotions, values, beliefs, and what's not being said. Reflecting back to deepen understanding.

4. Powerful Questions

Asking open, thought-provoking questions that create insight and forward movement. The primary tool of coaching.

5. Evoking Motivation

Helping clients connect to their deepest values and reasons for change. Using motivational interviewing techniques.

6. Goal Setting & Planning

Helping clients set meaningful goals, break them into action steps, and create sustainable plans.

7. Navigating Change

Understanding the stages of change, working with ambivalence, supporting clients through setbacks.

The Coaching Mindset Shift

Becoming an effective health coach requires a fundamental mindset shift. If you come from a healthcare, nutrition, or fitness background, you may be used to being the "expert who fixes people." Coaching is different:

From Expert Mode To Coach Mode
"I know what you need" "You know yourself best"
Giving answers Asking questions
Fixing problems Facilitating discovery
Taking responsibility for outcomes Empowering client ownership
Judging progress Celebrating all forward movement
"This is what works" "What would work for you?"

This shift can feel uncomfortable at first—especially when you have knowledge you want to share. But research shows that information delivered without coaching support rarely leads to change. When you coach instead of teach, you create lasting transformation.

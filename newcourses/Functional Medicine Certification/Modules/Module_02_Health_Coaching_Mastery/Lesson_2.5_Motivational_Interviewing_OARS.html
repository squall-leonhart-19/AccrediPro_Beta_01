Lesson 2.5: Motivational Interviewing (OARS)

You've learned to listen deeply and ask powerful questions. But what happens when clients are stuck—when part of them wants to change and part of them doesn't? This is ambivalence, and it's completely normal. Motivational Interviewing (MI) is an evidence-based approach designed specifically to work with ambivalence and evoke a client's own motivation for change.

What You'll Learn

  • What Motivational Interviewing is and why it works
  • The spirit of MI: partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation
  • The OARS skills in depth
  • How to recognize and evoke "change talk"

The Evidence for MI

Over 1,200+ randomized controlled trials demonstrate MI's effectiveness across health behaviors including substance use, diet, exercise, medication adherence, and chronic disease management.

— Lundahl et al., 2013; Miller & Rollnick, 2012

What Is Motivational Interviewing?

Developed by psychologists William Miller and Stephen Rollnick, Motivational Interviewing is defined as:

"A collaborative, goal-oriented style of communication with particular attention to the language of change. It is designed to strengthen personal motivation for and commitment to a specific goal by eliciting and exploring the person's own reasons for change within an atmosphere of acceptance and compassion."

— Miller & Rollnick, 2013

The key insight: people are more likely to change when they voice the reasons for change, rather than when someone tells them. MI helps you create conditions for clients to talk themselves into change.

Understanding Ambivalence

Ambivalence isn't resistance or lack of motivation—it's holding two conflicting desires at once. It's completely normal and often looks like this:

Part of me wants...

to lose weight, feel healthy, have more energy

⟷

Part of me wants...

to keep doing what's comfortable, avoid the effort

This internal tug-of-war is normal. Fighting against it creates resistance. Working with it creates movement.

The Righting Reflex

When we see someone with a problem, we naturally want to fix it. This "righting reflex" leads us to argue for change. But here's the paradox: when we argue for change, the client argues against it. The more you push, the more they resist. MI reverses this dynamic.

The Spirit of MI

Before learning the techniques, you must embody the spirit of MI—the underlying attitude that makes the techniques work. It has four elements:

1. Partnership

MI is done with someone, not to them. You're collaborative allies, not expert and patient. The client's perspective is valued and essential.

2. Acceptance

Unconditional positive regard. Honoring autonomy—it's their right to change or not. Affirming their strengths. Accurate empathy—truly understanding their experience.

3. Compassion

Genuinely caring about the client's welfare. Acting in their best interest. Your heart is in this—not just your technique.

4. Evocation

The wisdom for change is within the client—you're drawing it out, not putting it in. Your job is to evoke their reasons, ideas, and solutions.

OARS: The Core Skills

OARS is the acronym for the four fundamental MI skills. These build on what you've already learned about listening and questioning:

O

Open Questions

A

Affirmations

R

Reflections

S

Summaries

O — Open Questions

You learned this in Lesson 2.4. In MI, open questions are used strategically to evoke the client's own motivation:

Examples for evoking change talk:

  • "What concerns you most about your current health?"
  • "How would your life be different if you made this change?"
  • "What would be the benefits of changing?"
  • "What matters most to you about this?"
  • "If you decided to change, how would you go about it?"

A — Affirmations

Affirmations are statements that recognize client strengths, efforts, and values. They build confidence and self-efficacy:

Examples:

  • "You've shown real determination in coming back after setbacks."
  • "That took courage to share."
  • "You clearly care deeply about your family's well-being."
  • "You know yourself well—that's a real strength."
  • "The fact that you're here, exploring this, says something important about you."

Affirmation Tips

Affirmations should be genuine, specific, and about the person (their qualities, not just their actions). Avoid empty praise. Notice something real and name it.

R — Reflections

Reflections are the heart of MI. In MI, reflections are statements (not questions) that mirror back what you've heard, helping clients feel understood and hear themselves:

Simple Reflections

Repeat or rephrase with little interpretation:

Client: "I know I need to exercise but I just can't seem to make it happen."
Reflection: "Exercise isn't happening for you right now."

Complex Reflections

Add meaning, feeling, or go a step beyond what was said:

Client: "I know I need to exercise but I just can't seem to make it happen."
Reflection: "Part of you knows exercise matters, but something keeps getting in the way—and that's frustrating."

Amplified Reflections

Overstate the negative to evoke the positive:

Client: "I don't really care about my weight."
Reflection: "Your weight doesn't matter to you at all."
(Often prompts: "Well, I wouldn't say it doesn't matter at all...")

Aim for a 2:1 ratio of reflections to questions. This keeps the focus on the client and prevents the interaction from feeling like interrogation.

S — Summaries

Summaries collect what's been said—especially change talk—and present it back. They demonstrate listening and reinforce the client's own words:

Example Summary:

"Let me see if I'm getting this. You've been wanting to change your eating for a while. You're concerned about your energy levels and worried that if things don't change, you might develop the same health problems as your mom. At the same time, cooking feels overwhelming after long work days. But you mentioned that when you have eaten well, you've felt so much better. And you're here because something in you is ready to figure this out."

Notice: the summary emphasizes the change talk—reasons and desire for change—more than the barriers. This is strategic.

Recognizing Change Talk

Change talk is any statement the client makes that favors change. Learning to hear it, reflect it, and evoke more of it is central to MI:

Type Acronym Example
Desire D "I want to have more energy."
Ability A "I could probably cut back on sugar."
Reasons R "If I felt better, I'd be more present with my kids."
Need N "I have to do something—I can't go on like this."
Commitment C "I'm going to start walking this week."
Activation A "I'm ready to give this a try."
Taking Steps T "I actually bought vegetables yesterday."

DARN = Preparatory change talk (building motivation). CAT = Mobilizing change talk (moving to action). Your job: hear it, reflect it, ask for more of it.

Responding to "Sustain Talk"

Sustain talk is the opposite—statements that favor not changing. It's natural and expected. The key is how you respond:

Don't Do This

  • Argue against it
  • Try to convince them they're wrong
  • Provide counter-arguments
  • Get frustrated or push harder

Do This Instead

  • Reflect it briefly
  • Acknowledge the other side of ambivalence
  • Redirect to change talk
  • Honor their autonomy

Example:

Client: "I'm just too busy to exercise. There's no time."

Don't say: "But exercise only takes 20 minutes! Surely you have 20 minutes?"

Do say: "Your schedule is packed, and it's hard to see where exercise could fit. [pause] And yet something brought you here today. What is it you're hoping for?"

MI in Action: A Conversation

See how OARS and change talk work together:

CLIENT:

"I know I should quit drinking so much wine, but it's my only way to relax after work. I'm not sure I can give it up."

COACH (Reflection):

"Wine has become your go-to for unwinding, and the idea of giving that up feels really hard."

CLIENT:

"Yeah... but honestly, I'm worried about what it's doing to my body. My doctor mentioned my liver enzymes are off."

← Change talk (Concern/Reason)

COACH (Reflection + Open Question):

"So there's real concern about what this is doing to your health. What worries you most about that?"

CLIENT:

"My mom died of cirrhosis. I don't want to go down that road. I want to be around for my kids."

← Strong change talk (Reason + Desire)

COACH (Affirmation + Reflection):

"Being there for your kids is so important to you—that's what's driving this. You've seen what can happen, and you're determined to write a different story."

CLIENT:

"Yeah. I need to make a change. I'm just not sure where to start."

← Mobilizing change talk (Need + Activation)

The coach didn't lecture about liver disease or push for change. By reflecting and evoking, the client arrived at their own reasons—which are far more powerful than anything the coach could have said.

Client-Friendly Language

"You're the expert on your life. My job isn't to tell you what to do—it's to help you get clear on what you want, and support you in getting there. Whatever you decide is completely up to you."

Pause & Reflect

Think of a change you've been ambivalent about. What's the sustain talk you tell yourself? What's the change talk? How would it feel to have someone skillfully reflect your change talk back to you?

Looking Ahead

Motivational Interviewing helps you work with ambivalence. But change doesn't happen in a straight line—there are predictable stages. In Lesson 2.6, you'll learn the Stages of Change & Behavior Science—a framework for understanding where clients are in their change journey and meeting them there.

Key Takeaways

  • Motivational Interviewing works with ambivalence rather than against it
  • The spirit: Partnership, Acceptance, Compassion, Evocation
  • OARS skills: Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections (2:1 ratio), Summaries
  • Listen for change talk (DARN-CAT) and reflect it back
  • When you hear sustain talk, reflect briefly and redirect—don't argue

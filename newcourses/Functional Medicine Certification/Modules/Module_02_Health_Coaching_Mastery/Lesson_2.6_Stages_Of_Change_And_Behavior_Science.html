Lesson 2.6: Stages of Change & Behavior Science

In Lesson 2.5, you learned Motivational Interviewing—how to work with ambivalence and evoke change talk. But clients don't arrive at the same point in their journey. Some are ready to act tomorrow; others aren't sure they want to change at all. Understanding where someone is in their change process is essential for meeting them where they are.

What You'll Learn

  • The Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change)
  • How to identify which stage a client is in
  • Stage-matched coaching strategies
  • Key insights from behavior science

Why Stage Matters

Imagine giving a detailed meal plan to someone who hasn't decided they want to change their diet. They nod politely but never follow through. You've misread their stage—they weren't ready for action-oriented help. They needed something different first.

The most elegant coaching is stage-matched—giving clients exactly what they need for where they are, not where you think they should be.

The Transtheoretical Model

Developed by James Prochaska and Carlo DiClemente, the Transtheoretical Model (TTM) describes change as a process moving through distinct stages. Understanding these stages helps you meet clients where they are:

1. Precontemplation

"Not ready"

→

2. Contemplation

"Thinking about it"

→

3. Preparation

"Getting ready"

→

4. Action

"Doing it"

→

5. Maintenance

"Sustaining it"

Note: Relapse is normal and expected—it's not a stage, but part of the learning process.

Stage 1: Precontemplation

"I don't have a problem" / "I'm not going to change"

No intention to change in the foreseeable future (next 6 months). May be unaware, in denial, or demoralized from past failures.

How to recognize it:

  • Defensive when the topic comes up
  • "My weight isn't really a problem"
  • "I don't see why I need to change"
  • Often there because someone else wanted them to come

Coaching approach:

  • Don't push for change—it will backfire
  • Build rapport, be curious about their world
  • Gently raise awareness without lecturing
  • Explore values and what matters to them
  • Plant seeds and respect autonomy

Stage 2: Contemplation

"I'm thinking about it" / "Maybe I should, but..."

Aware there's a problem, considering change within the next 6 months. Ambivalent—see pros AND cons of changing.

How to recognize it:

  • "I know I need to do something about this..."
  • "Part of me wants to, but..."
  • Lots of "yes, but" statements
  • Can get stuck here for a long time

Coaching approach:

  • This is where MI shines—work with ambivalence
  • Explore pros and cons of changing and not changing
  • Evoke and reflect change talk
  • Connect change to their values
  • Help tip the balance toward change

Stage 3: Preparation

"I'm going to do this" / "I'm getting ready"

Intending to take action in the next month. May have already taken small steps. Developing a plan.

How to recognize it:

  • "I've decided I'm going to..."
  • "I bought a gym membership / threw out the junk food"
  • Asking how-to questions
  • Energy and excitement about change

Coaching approach:

  • Help create a concrete, realistic plan
  • Ensure goals are SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
  • Identify potential obstacles and strategies
  • Build confidence and self-efficacy
  • Set up accountability structures

Stage 4: Action

"I'm doing it!" / Actively making changes

Actively modifying behavior. Highest risk of relapse. Requires significant energy and commitment.

How to recognize it:

  • Reporting new behaviors
  • "I've been walking every day for two weeks"
  • Encountering real-world challenges
  • May be energized or struggling

Coaching approach:

  • Celebrate successes—acknowledge the effort
  • Help troubleshoot challenges
  • Anticipate and plan for high-risk situations
  • Reinforce reasons for change
  • Provide consistent accountability

Stage 5: Maintenance

"This is who I am now" / Sustaining changes

Working to prevent relapse and consolidate gains. Typically 6+ months of sustained change. New behaviors becoming habits.

How to recognize it:

  • Change feels more natural
  • "I don't even crave it anymore"
  • Confident but sometimes complacent
  • May face new challenges or life transitions

Coaching approach:

  • Acknowledge how far they've come
  • Identify ongoing strategies to prevent relapse
  • Help integrate changes into identity
  • Address complacency gently
  • Prepare for life transitions that might destabilize

About Relapse

Relapse isn't failure—it's part of learning. Most people cycle through the stages multiple times before sustained change. Your job when relapse happens:

  • Normalize it: "This is part of the process. Most people don't get it perfect the first time."
  • Learn from it: "What can we learn from this? What triggered the slip?"
  • Reduce shame: "One slip doesn't erase all your progress."
  • Re-engage motivation: "What originally made you want to change?"
  • Adjust the plan: "What might we do differently this time?"

Client-Friendly Language

"Slipping doesn't mean starting over. Think of it like climbing a spiral staircase—you might revisit similar places, but you're still higher than when you began. You've learned things that will help you this time."

Stage Assessment Questions

To identify where a client is, ask:

  • "Are you thinking about making any changes?" (Precontemplation if no)
  • "How soon are you planning to make a change?" (Contemplation: "someday"; Preparation: "soon")
  • "Have you already started doing things differently?" (Action if yes)
  • "How long have you been maintaining this change?" (Maintenance if 6+ months)
  • "On a scale of 1-10, how ready are you to change?" (Quick readiness check)

The Mismatch Problem

The biggest coaching mistake: giving action-stage interventions to contemplation-stage clients. Here's what happens when you mismatch:

If Client Is In... And You Give... Result
Precontemplation Action plans, meal plans, exercise programs Resistance, withdrawal, doesn't return
Contemplation Detailed how-to instructions Nods politely, doesn't follow through
Preparation More exploration of pros/cons Frustration—they're ready to move!
Action Reminders about why they should change Annoyance—they already decided!

Key Insights from Behavior Science

Beyond the stages model, behavior science offers additional insights:

Self-Efficacy Is Key

Belief in one's ability to succeed (self-efficacy) is the strongest predictor of behavior change. Build it through small wins, positive feedback, and recognizing past successes.

Make It Easy

Reduce friction for desired behaviors, increase friction for unwanted ones. Environment design matters more than willpower.

Stack New Habits on Existing Ones

Link new behaviors to established routines ("After I pour my morning coffee, I'll take my supplements"). This is "habit stacking."

Identity Precedes Behavior

People act consistently with who they believe they are. Help clients shift identity: "I'm someone who takes care of their health."

Implementation Intentions Work

"If-then" planning doubles success rates: "If it's Tuesday, then I go to the gym." Specificity matters.

Social Support Amplifies Change

People surrounded by supportive others are more likely to sustain change. Help clients build their support network.

Pause & Reflect

Think of a behavior you successfully changed in your life. What stage were you in when you started? How did you move through the stages? What helped you maintain the change?

Looking Ahead

You now understand where clients are in their change journey and how to match your approach. In Lesson 2.7, you'll learn Goal Setting & Action Planning—the practical skills for helping clients in preparation and action stages create plans that actually work.

Key Takeaways

  • The Stages of Change: Precontemplation, Contemplation, Preparation, Action, Maintenance
  • Stage-matched coaching prevents mismatches that lead to resistance or dropout
  • Relapse is normal—help clients learn from it rather than feel shame
  • Build self-efficacy through small wins and positive acknowledgment
  • Behavior science: make it easy, stack habits, shift identity, use if-then planning

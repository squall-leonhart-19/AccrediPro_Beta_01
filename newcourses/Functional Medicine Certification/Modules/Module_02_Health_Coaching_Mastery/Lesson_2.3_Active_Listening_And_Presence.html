Lesson 2.3: Active Listening & Presence

In Lesson 2.2, you learned how to build the trust that makes coaching possible. Now you'll develop the skill that deepens that trust: active listening. This isn't ordinary listening—it's a powerful practice that makes clients feel truly seen and understood, often for the first time in their healthcare journey.

What You'll Learn

  • The difference between hearing and truly listening
  • The four levels of listening
  • Specific techniques for active listening
  • How to cultivate coaching presence

"Most people do not listen with the intent to understand; they listen with the intent to reply."

— Stephen R. Covey

Why Listening Is Everything

Consider this: most clients have never been truly heard. In a typical medical appointment, doctors interrupt patients within 18 seconds of them starting to speak (Marvel et al., 1999). In everyday conversations, people are often just waiting for their turn to talk.

When you listen deeply—fully present, without judgment, without rushing to respond—something remarkable happens:

  • Clients slow down and access deeper thoughts and feelings
  • They hear themselves and often discover their own insights
  • Trust deepens because feeling understood is profoundly healing
  • You understand what really matters, not just what's on the surface
  • Real change becomes possible because you're working with the whole person

The Research Says...

"When patients feel listened to, they're 40% more likely to follow treatment recommendations, experience better outcomes, and report higher satisfaction."

— Stewart, 1995; Haskard Zolnierek & DiMatteo, 2009

The Four Levels of Listening

Not all listening is created equal. Otto Scharmer's framework describes four levels, each progressively deeper:

1

Downloading

Listening to confirm what you already know.

"Yes, yes—I've heard this before. She has stress and doesn't exercise enough." You're fitting what you hear into existing boxes, not really receiving anything new.

2

Factual Listening

Listening for new data.

"Oh, she was in a car accident last year—I didn't know that." You're collecting information, but still from outside—observing rather than connecting.

3

Empathic Listening

Listening from the other person's perspective.

"I can feel how scary that accident was for her, how it shook her sense of safety." You're inside their experience, feeling what they feel. This is where real connection happens.

4

Generative Listening

Listening for what's emerging.

"I sense there's something she hasn't said yet, something about to emerge." You're fully present to possibility, creating space for new insights to arise—in them and between you.

As health coaches, we aim to operate at levels 3 and 4 as much as possible. This requires setting aside our own agenda and truly entering the client's world.

What Gets in the Way

Deep listening sounds simple, but our minds have other ideas. Common barriers include:

Mental Rehearsal

Planning what you'll say next instead of fully receiving what they're saying now.

Problem-Solving Mode

Jumping to solutions before fully understanding the problem—or realizing the client needs to be heard, not fixed.

Judgment

Evaluating, categorizing, or critiquing what you hear instead of simply receiving it.

Personal Triggers

When something the client says activates your own emotions or experiences, pulling you into your own story.

Time Pressure

Feeling rushed, thinking about the agenda, or worrying you won't get through everything.

Distractions

External (notifications, noise) and internal (hunger, fatigue, personal worries).

The Antidote

Notice when you're pulled away. Gently return. The practice isn't about never losing focus—it's about coming back, again and again. Each return strengthens your listening muscle.

Active Listening Techniques

Active listening involves specific practices that communicate "I'm fully with you." Here are the core techniques:

1. Reflecting

Reflecting means mirroring back what you've heard—the content and/or the feeling. This shows you're listening and helps clients hear themselves:

Examples:

Client: "I've tried everything and nothing works. I'm so frustrated."

Reflect content: "You've tried many approaches and haven't found what works yet."

Reflect feeling: "There's a lot of frustration there—like you've been hitting walls."

Client: "My husband doesn't support my health goals. He keeps buying junk food."

Reflect content: "You're working on your health, but there's a disconnect at home."

Reflect feeling: "It sounds like you might be feeling unsupported, maybe even alone in this."

2. Paraphrasing

Paraphrasing is putting what you heard into your own words, often more concisely. This confirms understanding and can help clients see patterns:

Client: "I know I should meal prep on Sundays but by the time the weekend comes I'm so exhausted from the week that I just want to rest, and then Monday hits and I'm eating takeout again."

Paraphrase: "So there's a conflict—Sunday is theoretically prep day, but your need for rest takes priority, which sets up a week of takeout."

3. Summarizing

Summarizing captures the essence of a longer stretch of conversation. It's useful at transitions or to check understanding:

Example:

"Let me see if I'm getting the full picture. You've been dealing with fatigue for about three years, ever since your daughter was born. You've seen several doctors who say everything is 'normal,' but you know your body isn't right. You've tried cutting out gluten and that helped a little, but you're still searching for answers. And underneath all this, there's a sense that you need to figure this out so you can be the mom you want to be. Is that close?"

4. Clarifying

Clarifying seeks to deepen or specify understanding. It shows genuine interest and prevents assumptions:

  • "When you say 'stressed,' can you tell me more about what that feels like for you?"
  • "You mentioned this started 'around the divorce.' Was it before, during, or after?"
  • "What do you mean when you say you 'lost yourself'?"
  • "Help me understand—when you say nothing works, what specifically have you tried?"

5. The Power of Silence

Silence is one of the most powerful listening tools—and the most underused. When you're comfortable with silence:

  • Clients have space to think more deeply
  • Important insights often emerge in pauses
  • It signals that there's no rush—their process matters
  • It prevents you from filling space with premature solutions

Practice Tip

After the client finishes speaking, wait 3-5 seconds before responding. This feels longer than you'd think. Count silently if needed. Often, the client will continue with something deeper. When you do speak, start slowly.

Listening for What's Underneath

Master coaches listen not just to words, but to what's beneath the words:

Listen For What It Reveals Example Response
Values What matters most to them "It sounds like being present for your family is really central to who you are."
Beliefs Assumptions that may be limiting "I'm hearing a belief that you 'should' be able to do this on your own. Where does that come from?"
Emotions Feelings beneath the facts "There's something in your voice when you talk about your mother's health. What's there?"
Energy shifts Where excitement or resistance lives "I noticed you lit up when you talked about cooking. That seems like a source of joy."
What's not said Topics being avoided or minimized "You've shared a lot about your work and family. I'm curious—what about you? What do you do for yourself?"

Cultivating Presence

Presence is the quality of being fully there—body, mind, and heart. It's what allows deep listening to happen. Presence isn't a technique; it's a state of being.

Practices for Cultivating Presence

1

Ground yourself before sessions

Take 60 seconds to close your eyes, take three deep breaths, and set an intention to be fully present. Leave your previous conversation/task behind.

2

Eliminate distractions

Phone on silent, notifications off, door closed. Create conditions for undivided attention.

3

Notice when you leave

When you catch yourself planning, judging, or drifting, simply notice and return. No self-criticism.

4

Practice mindfulness regularly

Daily meditation or mindfulness practice strengthens the "presence muscle" you use in coaching.

5

Approach with genuine curiosity

Cultivate real interest in this unique person. Remember: you've never met anyone exactly like them.

Client-Friendly Language

"This is your time. I'm fully here with you—no distractions, no agenda except what matters to you. Whatever you need to share, I'm listening."

Listening Practice Exercise

Try this exercise with a willing partner (friend, colleague, family member):

  1. Ask them to talk for 5 minutes about something meaningful to them—a challenge, a hope, a memory.
  2. Your only job is to listen. No questions, no comments, no advice. Just receive.
  3. You may use minimal encouragers: nodding, "mmm," "I see."
  4. After 5 minutes, summarize what you heard—not just facts, but feelings and what seemed important.
  5. Ask them: "Did I get it? What did I miss?"
  6. Discuss: What was this experience like for both of you?

Pause & Reflect

In your everyday conversations this week, practice level 3 (empathic) listening. Notice what happens when you truly try to feel what someone else is feeling. What changes in the conversation? In the connection?

Looking Ahead

Active listening and presence set the stage—but coaching moves forward through powerful questions. In Lesson 2.4, you'll learn the art of asking questions that create insight, shift perspectives, and unlock possibilities clients didn't know they had.

Key Takeaways

  • Active listening makes clients feel truly heard—often for the first time
  • Four levels of listening: downloading, factual, empathic, and generative—aim for levels 3-4
  • Core techniques: reflecting, paraphrasing, summarizing, clarifying, and silence
  • Listen beneath words for values, beliefs, emotions, and energy shifts
  • Presence is cultivated through practice—grounding, mindfulness, and genuine curiosity

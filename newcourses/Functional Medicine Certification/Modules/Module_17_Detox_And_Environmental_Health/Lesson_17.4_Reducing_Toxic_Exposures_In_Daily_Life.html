From the Coaching Room

"I started reading ingredient labels and now I'm overwhelmed," says Diana, a 45-year-old client. "My shampoo has 30 ingredients I can't pronounce. My cleaning products are full of warnings. Even my 'natural' products have things I'm not sure about. I feel like I need to throw everything out and start over, but that seems impossible."

Diana's overwhelm is commonâ€”and counterproductive if it leads to paralysis. The key is prioritization: focusing on the changes that make the biggest difference first. Not everything matters equally, and perfection isn't the goal. Strategic reduction of major exposures provides most of the benefit with manageable effort.

The Prioritization Principle

Not all exposures are equal. Prioritize based on:

  • Frequency of exposure: Daily exposures matter more than occasional ones
  • Route of exposure: Ingested > absorbed > inhaled (generally)
  • Toxicity of the substance: Some chemicals are more harmful than others
  • Feasibility of change: Easy swaps first; harder changes later
  • Cost: Free or low-cost changes before expensive ones

Food: The Biggest Daily Exposure

We eat multiple times daily, making food the most significant route of chemical exposure for most people.

Plastics and Food Storage

Reducing Plastic Exposure

Never heat food in plastic: This dramatically increases chemical leaching. Microwave in glass or ceramic only.

Avoid plastic water bottles: Especially if left in heat. Use stainless steel or glass.

Reduce canned food consumption: Most cans are lined with BPA or BPA substitutes. Choose fresh, frozen, or glass-jarred alternatives.

Store food in glass containers: Replace plastic food storage with glass or stainless steel.

Limit plastic-wrapped foods: Especially fatty foods (cheese, meat) which absorb more plastic chemicals.

Fish Selection

Balance omega-3 benefits against mercury exposure:

Best Choices (Low Mercury) Avoid (High Mercury)
Salmon (wild), sardines, anchovies, herring Shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish
Trout, pollock, catfish, shrimp Bigeye tuna, marlin, orange roughy

Remember SMASH: Salmon, Mackerel (Atlantic), Anchovies, Sardines, Herringâ€”small fish that accumulate less mercury.

Water Quality

Water can contain various contaminants depending on source and plumbing:

Improving Water Quality

Know your water: Check your local water quality report (required annually from utilities). Test private wells.

Filter appropriately:

  • Carbon filters (Brita, etc.): Remove chlorine, some pesticides, improve taste. Affordable but limited.
  • Reverse osmosis: Removes most contaminants including fluoride, heavy metals. More comprehensive.
  • Whole-house systems: Filter all water including shower/bath. Reduces inhalation and skin absorption.

Reduce hot tap water use: Hot water leaches more lead and other metals from pipes. Use cold water for cooking and drinking.

Personal Care Products

We absorb chemicals through our skin. The average woman uses 12 personal care products daily, containing hundreds of chemicals.

Ingredients to Avoid

Ingredient Found In Concern
"Fragrance" or "parfum" Almost everything Can contain hundreds of undisclosed chemicals including phthalates
Parabens Cosmetics, lotions Endocrine disruption (estrogenic)
Phthalates Fragranced products, nail polish Endocrine disruption (anti-androgenic)
Triclosan Antibacterial products Endocrine disruption; antibiotic resistance
Oxybenzone Sunscreen Endocrine disruption; coral reef damage

Priority Swaps

Highest-Impact Personal Care Swaps

  1. Lotion/moisturizer: Applied to large body area, absorbed continuously. Switch to fragrance-free, simple ingredients.
  2. Deodorant: Applied to absorbent underarm area daily. Choose aluminum-free options.
  3. Shampoo/conditioner: Rinsed off but repeated exposure. Look for fragrance-free options.
  4. Lip products: Ingested when applied. Choose clean formulas.
  5. Sunscreen: Applied to large areas. Mineral (zinc/titanium) over chemical filters.

Resource: EWG's Skin Deep database rates thousands of products for safety (ewg.org/skindeep).

Household Cleaning Products

Cleaning products often contain harsh chemicals that remain in the air and on surfaces:

Cleaner Cleaning

Simple DIY options:

  • White vinegar + water: Effective for many surfaces
  • Baking soda: Gentle abrasive for scrubbing
  • Castile soap: Versatile, safe cleaning base

When buying commercial products:

  • Avoid "antibacterial" claims (often contain triclosan or similar)
  • Skip products with "fragrance"
  • Look for third-party certifications (EPA Safer Choice, EWG Verified)
  • Avoid aerosol sprays (inhaled deeper into lungs)

Laundry: Fragrance-free, dye-free detergents. Skip fabric softeners and dryer sheets (major sources of fragrance chemicals).

Indoor Air Quality

We spend approximately 90% of time indoors. Indoor air can be more polluted than outdoor air:

Improving Indoor Air

Ventilate: Open windows regularly (unless outdoor air quality is poor). Use exhaust fans when cooking.

Avoid VOC sources:

  • Choose low-VOC paints and finishes
  • Let new furniture and mattresses off-gas before use
  • Avoid air fresheners and scented candles
  • Don't store chemicals indoors

Filter the air: HEPA air purifiers reduce particles. Consider for bedrooms at minimum.

Control humidity: 30-50% relative humidity discourages mold growth. Use dehumidifiers in damp areas.

No smoking indoors: Obvious but worth statingâ€”thirdhand smoke residue persists.

Special Considerations for Women

Women's Specific Exposures

Feminine care products: Vaginal tissue is highly absorbent. Choose organic cotton, fragrance-free tampons and pads. Consider menstrual cups or period underwear.

Nail salons: High exposure to VOCs and other chemicals. If frequenting, choose well-ventilated salons or bring your own cleaner polishes.

Cosmetics: Women use more cosmetics than men on average. Prioritize cleaner makeup for products used daily.

Pregnancy considerations: Fetal development is particularly sensitive. Extra precaution during pregnancy and preconception is warranted.

A Phased Approach to Reducing Exposures

Don't Try to Change Everything at Once

Phase 1 (First Month): Quick Wins

  • Stop microwaving in plastic
  • Switch to glass/stainless water bottle
  • Open windows for ventilation
  • Skip air fresheners

Phase 2 (Months 2-3): Personal Care

  • Replace lotion and deodorant with cleaner versions
  • Switch to fragrance-free laundry products
  • Start buying Dirty Dozen produce organic

Phase 3 (Months 4-6): Kitchen and Home

  • Transition food storage to glass
  • Install water filter
  • Replace cleaning products as they run out

Ongoing: As products run out, replace with cleaner alternatives. No need to throw everything away at once.

Key Takeaways

  • Prioritize by impact: Focus on daily, high-exposure items first
  • Food is the biggest exposure: Dirty Dozen organic, avoid plastic with food, smart fish choices
  • Water filtration helps: At minimum, filter drinking water
  • Fragrance is a red flag: The word hides many undisclosed chemicals
  • Personal care matters: Skin absorbs what you put on it
  • Indoor air quality counts: Ventilate, avoid VOCs, filter when possible
  • Progress over perfection: Phased approach prevents overwhelm

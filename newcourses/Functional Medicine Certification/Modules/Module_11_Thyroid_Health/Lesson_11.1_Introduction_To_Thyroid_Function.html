Introduction to Thyroid Function

The Master Gland of Metabolism

What Is the Thyroid?

The thyroid gland is a small, butterfly-shaped organ located at the front of the neck, just below the Adam's apple. Despite its small size (typically 15-20 grams), this gland has an enormous influence on health.

Thyroid Anatomy Basics

  • Structure: Two lobes connected by a narrow band (isthmus), resembling a butterfly
  • Location: Anterior neck, wrapping around the trachea
  • Cell types: Follicular cells (produce T3 and T4) and parafollicular cells (produce calcitonin)
  • Blood supply: Highly vascularized—receives more blood per gram than almost any other organ

Thyroid Hormones: T4 and T3

The thyroid produces two main hormones that regulate metabolism throughout the body:

T4 (Thyroxine)

  • Primary hormone produced by thyroid (about 80%)
  • Relatively inactive "storage" form
  • Four iodine atoms attached
  • Long half-life (about 7 days)
  • Must be converted to T3 to be fully active

T3 (Triiodothyronine)

  • The "active" thyroid hormone
  • About 3-4 times more potent than T4
  • Three iodine atoms attached
  • Short half-life (about 1 day)
  • Only about 20% made by thyroid; 80% converted from T4

T4 to T3 Conversion

The conversion of T4 to T3 is a critical step in thyroid hormone action. This conversion happens primarily in the liver (about 60%) and also in the kidneys, gut, and other tissues. It requires specific enzymes called deiodinases and depends on adequate nutrient status.

Key Concept: Conversion Matters

A person can have normal T4 levels but still experience hypothyroid symptoms if conversion to T3 is impaired. This is why checking only TSH (or even TSH and T4) may miss some thyroid problems. Many factors affect conversion, including nutrient status, inflammation, stress, and liver health. We'll explore these in depth later in this module.

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid (HPT) Axis

Thyroid hormone production is controlled by a feedback loop involving the hypothalamus, pituitary gland, and thyroid. Understanding this axis helps explain thyroid lab results.

How the Feedback Loop Works

1

Hypothalamus senses thyroid hormone levels and releases TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone)

2

Pituitary gland responds to TRH by releasing TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone)

3

Thyroid gland responds to TSH by producing T4 and some T3

4

Negative feedback: When T4/T3 levels rise, the hypothalamus and pituitary reduce TRH and TSH production

Understanding TSH: TSH is often the first test ordered for thyroid function. High TSH typically indicates hypothyroidism (the pituitary is "shouting" at an underactive thyroid). Low TSH typically indicates hyperthyroidism (the pituitary is "backing off" because there's too much thyroid hormone). However, TSH alone doesn't tell the whole story—this is why comprehensive testing is often needed.

What Thyroid Hormones Do

Thyroid hormones affect virtually every cell in the body. They control the rate of metabolism—how quickly cells use energy—and influence countless body functions.

Metabolism & Energy

  • Set basal metabolic rate
  • Regulate body temperature
  • Influence energy production in mitochondria
  • Affect carbohydrate, fat, and protein metabolism

Brain & Mood

  • Support cognitive function
  • Influence mood and mental clarity
  • Affect memory and concentration
  • Essential for brain development

Heart & Circulation

  • Regulate heart rate
  • Influence blood pressure
  • Affect cholesterol metabolism
  • Influence blood vessel function

Digestion & Gut

  • Regulate gut motility
  • Affect nutrient absorption
  • Influence appetite
  • Impact gallbladder function

Reproduction

  • Regulate menstrual cycle
  • Influence fertility
  • Support healthy pregnancy
  • Affect libido

Hair, Skin & Nails

  • Support hair growth
  • Maintain skin health
  • Influence nail strength
  • Affect wound healing

Why Thyroid Problems Are So Common in Women

Thyroid disorders affect women at 5-8 times the rate of men. This gender disparity has several explanations:

  • Autoimmune connection: Most thyroid disease is autoimmune (Hashimoto's, Graves'). Women are more susceptible to autoimmune conditions in general—possibly related to more active immune systems and hormonal influences.
  • Estrogen effects: Estrogen influences the immune system and may trigger or worsen autoimmunity in susceptible individuals.
  • Pregnancy: Pregnancy significantly stresses the thyroid and can trigger or unmask thyroid dysfunction. Postpartum thyroiditis affects up to 10% of women.
  • Perimenopause and menopause: Hormonal fluctuations during this transition can affect thyroid function and make symptoms harder to distinguish.
  • Genetic factors: Thyroid conditions often run in families, particularly on the maternal side.

The Thyroid-Everything Connection

One of the most important concepts to understand is that thyroid health connects to almost every other body system. This is why thyroid dysfunction can masquerade as so many other conditions.

System Thyroid Connection
Gut Gut health affects T4-T3 conversion; gut dysbiosis linked to autoimmune thyroid disease
Adrenals Chronic stress impairs thyroid function and conversion; adrenal and thyroid dysfunction often coexist
Liver Primary site of T4-T3 conversion; liver health essential for thyroid hormone activation
Sex Hormones Thyroid affects menstrual cycle and fertility; estrogen influences thyroid binding proteins
Blood Sugar Thyroid affects glucose metabolism; insulin resistance impairs thyroid function
Immune System Most thyroid disease is autoimmune; immune balance directly affects thyroid health

The Functional Medicine Perspective

Conventional medicine often views thyroid dysfunction as simply a matter of hormone levels—too high or too low—and treats accordingly. Functional medicine asks deeper questions:

Functional Medicine Questions

  • Why is the thyroid underperforming? (Root cause investigation)
  • What is driving the autoimmune attack in Hashimoto's? (Immune triggers)
  • Is conversion from T4 to T3 happening efficiently? (Peripheral metabolism)
  • Are cells responding properly to thyroid hormones? (Receptor sensitivity)
  • What other systems are affecting thyroid function? (Systems interconnection)

Key Takeaways

  • The thyroid gland controls metabolism and affects virtually every cell in the body
  • T4 is the storage form; T3 is the active form—conversion between them is critical
  • The HPT axis regulates thyroid hormone production through a feedback loop
  • Thyroid dysfunction is 5-8 times more common in women than men
  • Thyroid health is connected to gut, adrenal, liver, hormonal, and immune function
  • Functional medicine looks beyond hormone levels to understand root causes

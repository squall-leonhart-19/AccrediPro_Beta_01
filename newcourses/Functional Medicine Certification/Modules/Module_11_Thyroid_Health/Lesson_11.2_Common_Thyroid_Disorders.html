Common Thyroid Disorders

Understanding the Spectrum of Thyroid Dysfunction

⏱ Reading Time: 12 minutes

Building on Thyroid Basics: Now that you understand how the thyroid functions, let's explore what happens when things go wrong. Thyroid disorders range from underactivity (hypothyroidism) to overactivity (hyperthyroidism), with autoimmune conditions being the most common cause of both. Understanding these conditions helps you recognize patterns in your clients and guide them toward appropriate care.

What You'll Learn

  • Recognize the signs and symptoms of hypothyroidism
  • Understand Hashimoto's thyroiditis and its autoimmune nature
  • Identify hyperthyroidism and Graves' disease
  • Appreciate subclinical thyroid dysfunction and its significance
  • Know when symptoms warrant thyroid evaluation

"For years, I thought I was just lazy or not trying hard enough," said Karen, 47. "I'd drag myself through each day, needing coffee after coffee. My hair was thinning, I was constantly cold, and no matter how little I ate, I kept gaining weight. When I finally got diagnosed with Hashimoto's, I was almost relieved—at least I had an explanation. I wasn't crazy, and I wasn't lazy. My thyroid had been struggling for years."

Hypothyroidism: The Underactive Thyroid

Hypothyroidism occurs when the thyroid gland doesn't produce enough thyroid hormone to meet the body's needs. It's the most common thyroid disorder, affecting about 5% of the population (with many more undiagnosed).

Signs and Symptoms

Because thyroid hormones affect every cell, hypothyroidism can cause symptoms throughout the body:

Energy & Metabolism

  • Fatigue and low energy
  • Weight gain or difficulty losing weight
  • Cold intolerance
  • Low body temperature

Cognitive & Mood

  • Brain fog, poor concentration
  • Depression
  • Memory problems
  • Slow thinking

Skin, Hair & Nails

  • Dry skin
  • Hair loss or thinning
  • Brittle nails
  • Loss of outer third of eyebrow

Digestion & Elimination

  • Constipation
  • Sluggish digestion
  • Bloating
  • Reduced appetite

Cardiovascular

  • Slow heart rate
  • Elevated cholesterol
  • Fluid retention
  • Puffiness (face, hands, feet)

Reproductive

  • Heavy or irregular periods
  • Infertility
  • Low libido
  • Menstrual irregularities

Causes of Hypothyroidism

  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis: By far the most common cause in developed countries (accounts for 90%+ of cases). An autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks the thyroid.
  • Iodine deficiency: Common worldwide but rare in developed countries due to iodized salt. Still relevant in some populations.
  • Thyroid surgery or radioactive iodine treatment: Removal or destruction of thyroid tissue for cancer or hyperthyroidism.
  • Medications: Lithium, amiodarone, and certain other drugs can cause hypothyroidism.
  • Radiation: Head/neck radiation for cancer treatment.
  • Congenital: Some babies are born without a functioning thyroid.

Hashimoto's Thyroiditis: The Autoimmune Culprit

Hashimoto's thyroiditis is the most important thyroid condition for coaches to understand. It's an autoimmune disease where the immune system produces antibodies that attack the thyroid gland, gradually destroying it over time.

Key Features of Hashimoto's

  • Progressive damage: Thyroid function typically declines gradually over years
  • Fluctuating symptoms: Can cause periods of both hypo- and hyperthyroid symptoms, especially early on
  • Antibody presence: TPO and/or thyroglobulin antibodies are usually elevated
  • Often missed: TSH may be "normal" for years while antibodies are high and thyroid is being damaged
  • Associated conditions: Often occurs alongside other autoimmune conditions (celiac, type 1 diabetes, etc.)

The Hashimoto's Progression

1

Early Stage: Antibodies present, TSH normal

May have symptoms despite "normal" labs; autoimmune attack has begun

2

Subclinical Hypothyroidism: TSH rising, T4 still normal

Thyroid working harder to maintain output; symptoms often present

3

Overt Hypothyroidism: TSH high, T4 low

Thyroid can no longer keep up; typically medication is prescribed

4

Advanced Stage: Significant thyroid damage

May require higher medication doses; thyroid gland may shrink (atrophy)

Critical Point: The autoimmune attack on the thyroid often precedes abnormal TSH by years. This is why testing thyroid antibodies (TPO, thyroglobulin) is so important—it can identify Hashimoto's while TSH is still "normal." Early identification allows for lifestyle intervention to slow progression.

Hyperthyroidism: The Overactive Thyroid

Hyperthyroidism occurs when the thyroid produces too much hormone, speeding up metabolism excessively. It's less common than hypothyroidism but can be more immediately dangerous if severe.

Signs and Symptoms

Category Symptoms
Metabolism Weight loss despite increased appetite, heat intolerance, excessive sweating
Cardiovascular Rapid heartbeat, palpitations, high blood pressure, atrial fibrillation risk
Neurological Anxiety, nervousness, irritability, tremor (especially hands), insomnia
Musculoskeletal Muscle weakness, fine tremor, increased bone loss
Gastrointestinal Frequent bowel movements, diarrhea
Eyes (Graves') Bulging eyes, eye irritation, double vision (specific to Graves' disease)

Graves' Disease

Graves' disease is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism. Like Hashimoto's, it's autoimmune—but instead of destroying the thyroid, the antibodies stimulate it to overproduce hormone.

Graves' Disease Key Points

  • Thyroid-stimulating antibodies: TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin) or TRAb mimic TSH, causing constant thyroid stimulation
  • Eye involvement: About 25-30% develop Graves' ophthalmopathy (bulging eyes)
  • Treatment: Antithyroid medications, radioactive iodine, or surgery
  • May remit: Unlike Hashimoto's, Graves' can go into remission (about 30-50% achieve lasting remission)

Subclinical Thyroid Dysfunction

Subclinical thyroid dysfunction refers to abnormal TSH with normal T4 and T3 levels. It represents early or mild thyroid dysfunction that may or may not progress.

Subclinical Hypothyroidism

  • TSH elevated (typically 4.5-10 mIU/L)
  • Free T4 normal
  • May cause symptoms in some people
  • About 2-5% progress to overt hypothyroidism yearly
  • Higher risk of progression with TPO antibodies

Subclinical Hyperthyroidism

  • TSH suppressed (typically 0.1-0.4 mIU/L)
  • Free T4 normal
  • Increases risk of atrial fibrillation
  • Accelerates bone loss
  • May progress or resolve spontaneously

Other Thyroid Conditions

Additional Thyroid Conditions to Know

  • Thyroid nodules: Lumps in the thyroid; very common (especially in women); usually benign but require evaluation
  • Goiter: Enlarged thyroid gland; can occur with hypo- or hyperthyroidism, or with normal function
  • Thyroid cancer: Relatively rare; usually very treatable. Nodules should be evaluated by ultrasound and possibly biopsy
  • Postpartum thyroiditis: Temporary thyroid inflammation after pregnancy; affects up to 10% of women. May cause transient hyperthyroidism followed by hypothyroidism
  • Silent thyroiditis: Similar to postpartum thyroiditis but not pregnancy-related

Recognizing Who Needs Thyroid Evaluation

As a health coach, you can help clients recognize when thyroid evaluation is warranted:

Signs That Warrant Thyroid Testing

  • Unexplained fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
  • Unexplained weight changes (gain or loss)
  • Significant hair loss or thinning
  • Temperature sensitivity (always cold or always hot)
  • New or worsening depression or anxiety
  • Menstrual irregularities or fertility problems
  • Family history of thyroid disease
  • Personal history of autoimmune conditions
  • Postpartum period (especially with mood changes or fatigue)

Key Takeaways

  • Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) causes slowing of metabolism—fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis is the most common cause of hypothyroidism; it's an autoimmune condition
  • Hashimoto's often begins with elevated antibodies before TSH becomes abnormal
  • Hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid) causes metabolic acceleration—weight loss, anxiety, heat intolerance
  • Graves' disease is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism; it's also autoimmune
  • Subclinical thyroid dysfunction may cause symptoms and warrants monitoring

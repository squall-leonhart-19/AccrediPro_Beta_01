Client Assessment and Goal Setting

Gathering Information and Establishing Meaningful Objectives

Before building any protocol, we must understand the person in front of us. In this lesson, you'll learn how to conduct thorough assessments that reveal not just symptoms, but root causes, preferences, constraints, and motivations‚Äîthen translate those insights into meaningful, achievable goals.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Conduct comprehensive client assessments using multiple tools
  • Identify the information needed for effective program design
  • Collaborate with clients to set SMART and values-aligned goals
  • Prioritize goals based on impact and client readiness
  • Create compelling vision statements that drive motivation

üìñ The Assessment That Changed Everything

When Diane came to coaching, she said she wanted to "lose 20 pounds and have more energy." Standard goals, right? But through a thorough assessment, a richer picture emerged.

Diane was a 51-year-old empty nester experiencing her first year without children at home. Her weight gain coincided with this transition. Her "lack of energy" was actually more nuanced‚Äîshe had physical energy but lacked motivation and purpose. She'd lost her identity as "mom" and didn't know who she was anymore.

A standard weight loss program would have failed her. What Diane needed was support for this major life transition, help reconnecting with her own interests and identity, and nutrition and movement that served her new chapter‚Äînot just a calorie deficit. Assessment revealed what the surface goals never could.

The Assessment Process

Comprehensive assessment serves multiple purposes: gathering information, building rapport, demonstrating expertise, and beginning the transformational process itself. The act of reflecting on health often initiates change.

Assessment Components

1. Health History Intake

A written or digital intake form completed before the first session, covering:

  • Personal information and demographics
  • Current health concerns (chief complaints)
  • Health history timeline (when did symptoms start, what was happening)
  • Medical history (diagnoses, surgeries, medications, supplements)
  • Family health history
  • Lifestyle factors (sleep, exercise, stress, nutrition)
  • Environmental factors (home, work, exposures)
  • Previous approaches tried and results
  • Goals and expectations

2. Symptom Assessment Tools

Standardized questionnaires that quantify symptom severity and track progress:

  • Medical Symptom Questionnaire (MSQ): Comprehensive symptom checklist with scoring
  • Symptom burden assessments: Rating symptoms by severity (1-10 scale)
  • System-specific questionnaires: Digestive symptom index, sleep quality index, stress assessments
  • Quality of life measures: Overall wellbeing, energy, mood assessments

3. Lifestyle Assessment

Detailed exploration of current habits and patterns:

  • Nutrition: Food diary (3-7 days), eating patterns, food preferences, restrictions, relationship with food
  • Sleep: Bedtime routines, sleep quality, duration, sleep environment
  • Movement: Current activity level, exercise history, physical limitations
  • Stress: Sources of stress, coping mechanisms, relaxation practices
  • Relationships: Support systems, social connection, community involvement

4. Readiness and Motivation Assessment

Understanding where clients are in their change journey:

  • Stages of change: Pre-contemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance
  • Motivation: Why now? What's driving the desire for change?
  • Confidence: How confident are they in their ability to make changes?
  • Values: What matters most to them? What drives their decisions?
  • Barriers: What has stopped them before? What challenges do they anticipate?

The Discovery Session

The first session is where assessment comes alive. This is where you move beyond paperwork to understand the whole person.

Discovery Session Framework (60-90 minutes)

  1. Opening (5-10 min): Warm welcome, set intentions for the session, explain the process
  2. Their Story (20-30 min): Open-ended exploration of their health journey, concerns, and aspirations
  3. Deep Dive (15-20 min): Clarifying questions on key areas identified in intake
  4. Vision Exploration (10-15 min): What would life look like if they achieved their goals?
  5. Goal Setting (10-15 min): Collaborative goal development and prioritization
  6. Next Steps (5-10 min): Immediate action items, scheduling, what to expect

Powerful Assessment Questions

Health Journey: "Tell me about your health journey. When did you start feeling less than your best?"

Antecedents: "What was happening in your life when these symptoms started?"

Triggers: "What seems to make your symptoms better or worse?"

Previous Attempts: "What have you tried before? What worked? What didn't?"

Vision: "If you woke up tomorrow and everything was exactly as you wanted, what would be different?"

Values: "What matters most to you in life? Why is improving your health important?"

Confidence: "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can make the changes needed?"

Support: "Who in your life supports your health goals? Who might not?"

Goal Setting: The Foundation of Your Program

Goals are the destination on your client's health journey. Without clear goals, you're driving without a map. But not all goals are created equal.

From Surface Goals to Deep Goals

Clients often present with surface-level goals that mask deeper motivations:

Surface Goal Possible Deep Goal
"I want to lose 30 pounds" Feel confident, attractive, worthy of love; reduce joint pain to play with kids
"I want more energy" Be present for family; pursue postponed dreams; feel like myself again
"I want to sleep better" Think clearly at work; feel emotionally stable; enjoy life more
"I want to reduce stress" Be a better partner/parent; stop feeling overwhelmed; prevent burnout

To uncover deep goals, ask: "Why is that important to you?" Then ask again. Keep asking until you reach the emotional core‚Äîthe deep motivation that will sustain effort when things get hard.

SMART+ Goals

The classic SMART framework ensures goals are actionable. Add the "+" elements for even more powerful goals:

Specific Clear, detailed, no ambiguity. "Eat more vegetables" becomes "Eat 5 servings of vegetables daily"
Measurable Quantifiable so you know when you've achieved it. Track-able progress.
Achievable Realistic given current resources, time, and constraints. Stretching but not impossible.
Relevant Connected to their deeper values and overall health vision.
Time-bound Has a deadline or timeframe that creates urgency and focus.
+ Values-Aligned Connected to what matters most to the client.
+ Exciting Inspires enthusiasm, not dread. If they're not excited, reconsider the goal.

Goal Hierarchy

Effective programs work with multiple levels of goals:

Vision (Long-term)

The inspiring picture of future health‚Äîwhere they want to be in 1-5 years. This provides direction and motivation. Example: "I want to be the vibrant, energetic grandmother who can keep up with her grandchildren."

Outcome Goals (Medium-term)

The measurable results they're working toward in your program. Example: "Lose 20 pounds, eliminate daily fatigue, and normalize blood sugar in 6 months."

Process Goals (Short-term)

The daily and weekly behaviors that lead to outcomes. Example: "Walk 30 minutes 5x weekly, eat protein at every meal, be in bed by 10pm on weeknights."

üîç Key Insight: Focus on Process Goals

Outcome goals inspire, but process goals transform. You can't directly control outcomes (weight, energy levels, lab results), but you can control behaviors. Celebrate process goal achievement‚Äîthe outcomes will follow.

Prioritizing Goals

Clients often want to change everything at once. Your job is to help prioritize for maximum impact and sustainability:

Prioritization Criteria

  1. Impact: Which changes will create the biggest positive ripple effects?
  2. Readiness: What is the client most ready and willing to tackle?
  3. Foundation: What needs to be in place before other changes can succeed?
  4. Quick wins: What can build confidence and momentum early?
  5. Client preference: What does the client want to start with?

The ideal starting point has high impact, high readiness, and provides a quick win. Often, addressing sleep or a simple nutrition change meets all these criteria.

Creating the Vision Statement

A vision statement captures the client's desired future in vivid, emotional language. It becomes a touchstone during challenging moments.

Vision Statement Example

"Six months from now, I wake up naturally before my alarm, feeling rested and ready for the day. I have steady energy throughout the day without needing coffee to function. My clothes fit comfortably, and I feel confident in my body. I'm present with my family instead of exhausted on the couch. I'm hiking on weekends, something I'd given up years ago. I feel like myself again‚Äîthe vibrant, capable woman I know I can be."

Vision statements should be:

  • Written in present tense (as if already achieved)
  • Sensory and specific (what they see, feel, do)
  • Emotionally compelling
  • Personally meaningful
  • Referenced regularly throughout coaching

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Comprehensive assessment gathers health history, symptoms, lifestyle, and motivation
  • The discovery session brings intake information alive through skilled questioning
  • Surface goals often mask deeper motivations‚Äîkeep asking "why" to find them
  • SMART+ goals are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound, values-aligned, and exciting
  • Prioritize goals based on impact, readiness, foundations, and client preference
  • Vision statements create emotional anchors for the transformation journey

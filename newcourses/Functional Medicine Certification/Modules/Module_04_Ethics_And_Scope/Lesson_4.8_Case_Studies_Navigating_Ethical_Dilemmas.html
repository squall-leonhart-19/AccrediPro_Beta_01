Case Study 1: The Supplement Suggestion

The Scenario

Your client, Diane (52), has been working with you for two months on fatigue and brain fog. She's made dietary changes and improved her sleep, but she's frustrated that her energy hasn't bounced back. During your session, she mentions that she's heard B12 supplements can help with fatigue and asks, "Should I take B12? My friend takes it and says it changed her life."

You know that B12 deficiency is common, especially in women her age, and that supplementation is generally safe. You personally take B12 and have seen it help many clients. Her symptoms could absolutely be related to B12 levels. Part of you wants to say, "Yes, I think that's a great idea."

The Ethical Tension

You want to help Diane and believe supplementation might benefit her. However, recommending specific supplements for symptom treatment crosses into territory that requires clinical assessment. Fatigue and brain fog can have many causes‚Äîsome serious. Without lab work to confirm deficiency, you're essentially guessing.

Ethical Analysis

Scope Considerations: Recommending supplements to treat symptoms falls outside the health coaching scope. While coaches can provide general education about nutrients, making personalized supplement recommendations based on symptoms constitutes treatment‚Äîa clinical function.

What If She Takes It and Nothing Happens? She might miss underlying conditions that actually need attention. The supplement becomes a band-aid that delays proper diagnosis.

What If Something Goes Wrong? While B12 is generally safe, you don't know her full medical picture. And if she has a reaction or her symptoms worsen, your recommendation could expose you to liability.

‚úì A Professional Response

"That's a great question, and I appreciate you thinking about what might support your energy. B12 is definitely important, and some people do find supplementation helpful. However, rather than guessing, I'd encourage you to ask your doctor about checking your B12 levels. That way, you'll know for sure if it's an issue, and if so, what dose makes sense. Fatigue and brain fog can have many causes, and having that information would help you make a more informed decision. Would you feel comfortable bringing this up at your next appointment?"

Case Study 2: The Cry for Help

The Scenario

Linda (47) has been your client for three months, working on stress management and emotional eating. Today's session feels different. She's tearful from the start, and when you ask what's going on, she says, "I just don't see the point anymore. I wake up every day and wonder why I bother." When you gently explore what she means, she admits that she's been having thoughts of "just not being here anymore"‚Äîbut quickly adds, "I would never actually do anything. I just feel so hopeless."

The Ethical Tension

Linda has disclosed suicidal ideation‚Äîthoughts of not wanting to live‚Äîbut denies active intent or plan. She trusts you and has confided something she may not have shared with anyone else. You don't want to overreact and damage the relationship, but you also recognize this is serious. You're not a therapist and not trained to assess suicide risk. What's your responsibility here?

Ethical Analysis

Scope Considerations: This is clearly outside health coaching scope. Suicidal ideation requires professional mental health assessment. Even if Linda denies active intent, she's describing hopelessness and a desire to not exist‚Äîthese are serious warning signs.

Confidentiality Considerations: While we generally maintain client confidentiality, danger to self is an established exception. Linda's disclosure triggers an ethical obligation to ensure she gets appropriate help.

The Relationship: How you handle this moment will affect whether Linda gets help and whether she continues to trust you. Responding with panic or immediate 911 calls may not be appropriate if she's not in immediate danger, but doing nothing isn't acceptable either.

‚úì A Professional Response

"Linda, I'm really glad you trusted me with this‚Äîit takes courage to share what you're feeling. What you're describing sounds incredibly painful, and I want to make sure you get the right support. These kinds of feelings are something a trained therapist can really help with. I'm not able to provide that kind of support as a health coach, but I don't want you to carry this alone."

Then, before ending the session:

  • Assess immediacy: "Are you safe right now? Do you have thoughts of harming yourself today?"
  • Provide resources: Share the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline number and therapist referrals from your network
  • Create a safety plan: Ask her to commit to calling a crisis line or going to the ER if thoughts intensify
  • Follow up: Schedule a check-in call within 24-48 hours
  • Document: Record what was shared and the steps you took

Case Study 3: The Skeptical Spouse

The Scenario

Your client, Michelle (43), has been making wonderful progress addressing her autoimmune symptoms through anti-inflammatory nutrition and stress reduction. One day, you receive a phone call from her husband, Tom. "I'm Michelle's husband, and I need to know what you're telling her. She's refusing to take her medication because of something you said, and her doctor is furious. I pay for her coaching sessions, so I have a right to know what's going on."

The Ethical Tension

Tom is concerned about his wife and claims financial involvement. But you haven't spoken to Michelle about this, and you never advised her to stop her medication. Sharing information about your sessions would violate confidentiality‚Äîbut refusing to engage might escalate the conflict at home. And you're troubled by the allegation that something you said is being used to justify stopping medication.

Ethical Analysis

Confidentiality: Even though Tom pays for sessions, Michelle is your client. You cannot share information about her care without her explicit written consent‚Äînot even confirming that she's a client.

Scope: Health coaches don't advise clients to stop medications. If Michelle interpreted something you said that way, this needs to be addressed directly with her.

Client Welfare: If Michelle has stopped medication against medical advice, she may be at risk. You have an obligation to address this‚Äîbut through proper channels, not through her husband.

‚úì A Professional Response

To Tom: "I appreciate your concern for Michelle. However, I'm not able to discuss anything about my work with any client‚Äîincluding confirming whether someone is a client‚Äîwithout their explicit written permission. I'd encourage you to talk directly with Michelle about your concerns. If she'd like me to speak with you, she can let me know and sign a release."

Then, follow up with Michelle: Contact her to discuss what happened. Clarify that you never advised stopping medication. Explore what she understood from your sessions. Emphasize the importance of working with her doctor on any medication decisions. Document the conversation carefully.

Case Study 4: The Friend Referral

The Scenario

Your close friend Sarah introduces you to her neighbor, Rachel, at a dinner party. Rachel mentions she's been struggling with hormonal issues and asks if you might be able to help her. Sarah enthusiastically endorses you: "She's amazing! She completely transformed my health." Rachel is eager to start right away and offers to pay you cash‚Äîno paperwork needed. "It would be so easy since we already know each other through Sarah," she says.

The Ethical Tension

Rachel's connection to your friend creates a dual relationship‚Äîyou'd be both a friend-of-friend and her health coach. The informal approach (cash, no paperwork) feels convenient but bypasses professional protections. And you're being put on the spot in a social situation.

Ethical Analysis

Boundary Concerns: Working with friends-of-friends creates potential complications. What happens if information Rachel shares in sessions becomes awkward in your friendship with Sarah? What if Rachel is dissatisfied‚Äîdoes that affect your relationship with Sarah?

Professional Standards: "No paperwork" means no informed consent, no documentation, no clear agreements. This exposes both you and Rachel. It's also not how professional practice works.

Social Pressure: Making business decisions at dinner parties, under social pressure, rarely leads to good outcomes.

‚úì A Professional Response

"I'm glad to hear you're interested in getting support, Rachel, and thank you for the kind words, Sarah. I'd be happy to tell you more about what I do. Why don't you reach out to me this week, and we can set up a discovery call to see if we'd be a good fit? That way I can explain my process properly and you can decide if it's right for you."

If you do decide to work with Rachel, follow your complete intake process‚Äîincluding informed consent, clear boundaries, and documentation. Consider whether the dual relationship is manageable, and discuss it openly with Rachel from the start.

Case Study 5: The Urgent Text

The Scenario

It's Saturday night at 10 PM. You receive this text from your client Jennifer: "Hi, I'm having severe abdominal pain that started about 2 hours ago. It's getting worse. I'm not sure if I should go to the ER or just wait it out. I don't want to overreact. What do you think I should do?"

The Ethical Tension

Jennifer is reaching out for guidance on what is potentially a medical emergency. She trusts you, which is why she texted. But you're not equipped to assess abdominal pain, and giving the wrong advice could be dangerous. At the same time, she's clearly uncertain and looking for direction.

Ethical Analysis

Scope: Assessing symptoms and advising on emergency care is absolutely not within your scope. This is a medical situation.

Risk: Severe, worsening abdominal pain can indicate serious conditions‚Äîappendicitis, ectopic pregnancy, bowel obstruction‚Äîthat require immediate care. "Wait and see" advice from you could delay life-saving treatment.

Boundaries: This also touches on availability boundaries. Clients texting at 10 PM on weekends about medical symptoms suggests unclear expectations about your role and availability.

‚úì A Professional Response

"Jennifer, I'm not able to give medical advice, but severe abdominal pain that's getting worse is definitely something a doctor needs to evaluate. Please don't wait‚Äîgo to the ER or call your doctor's after-hours line now. Better to be safe. Take care of yourself and let me know how you're doing when you can."

Follow-up: In your next session, gently clarify your availability and scope. Ensure Jennifer understands that medical symptoms always require medical attention, not coaching input.

An Ethical Decision-Making Framework

As these cases illustrate, ethical dilemmas rarely have obvious answers. Here's a framework you can apply when facing uncertain situations:

The PAUSE Framework for Ethical Decisions

P
Pause: Don't react immediately. Take a breath. Recognize that you're facing an ethical decision that deserves careful thought.
A
Analyze: Identify the competing values and considerations. What scope issues are present? What are the confidentiality implications? Who might be affected?
U
Understand: Consider different perspectives. How would a mentor or colleague view this? What would your professional code of ethics advise? What does the client need?
S
Select: Choose a course of action that prioritizes client welfare while honoring professional boundaries. When in doubt, err on the side of caution.
E
Evaluate: After acting, reflect on the outcome. What worked? What would you do differently? How can this inform future decisions?

When to Seek Consultation

You don't have to navigate every dilemma alone. Seek consultation when:

  • The situation involves potential harm to the client or others
  • You're uncertain about legal obligations (consult an attorney)
  • The dilemma is complex and multiple approaches seem reasonable
  • Your emotions are clouding your judgment
  • You're facing something you've never encountered before

Build relationships with mentors, supervisors, or peer consultation groups who can provide guidance. Professional isolation is a risk factor for ethical missteps.

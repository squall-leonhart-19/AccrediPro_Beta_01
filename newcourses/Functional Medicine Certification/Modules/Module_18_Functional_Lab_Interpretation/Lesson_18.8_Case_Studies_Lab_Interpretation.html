Case Study 1: The "Normal" Thyroid

Client Profile

Name: Catherine, 47
Chief complaints: Fatigue, weight gain (20 lbs in 2 years), hair thinning, constipation, cold intolerance, brain fog

Lab Results

TSH: 3.8 mIU/L (ref: 0.5-4.5) Flagged: Normal
Free T4: 1.0 ng/dL (ref: 0.8-1.8) Flagged: Normal
Free T3: 2.1 pg/mL (ref: 2.0-4.4) Flagged: Normal (bottom of range)
Reverse T3: 22 ng/dL (ref: 9.2-24.1) Flagged: Normal (high end)
TPO Antibodies: 187 IU/mL (ref: <9) HIGH
Ferritin: 22 ng/mL (ref: 12-150) Flagged: Normal (suboptimal)
Vitamin D: 24 ng/mL (ref: 30-100) LOW

What the Labs Suggest

Despite "normal" thyroid labs by conventional standards, several patterns emerge:

  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis: Elevated TPO antibodies confirm autoimmune thyroid disease
  • Suboptimal thyroid function: TSH at 3.8 with symptoms, Free T3 at bottom of range
  • Possible conversion issue: Adequate T4 but low T3, elevated Reverse T3 suggests poor conversion
  • Nutrient deficiencies: Low vitamin D and low-normal ferritin‚Äîboth affect thyroid function and contribute to symptoms

‚úÖ Coaching Approach for Catherine

  • Validate her experience: Her symptoms are real and connected to lab findings, even if some doctors say she's "fine"
  • Education: Help her understand that optimal ranges differ from pathological ranges
  • Support advocacy: Encourage her to discuss these findings with a practitioner familiar with functional thyroid interpretation
  • Lifestyle support: Help implement anti-inflammatory nutrition, stress management, and sleep optimization‚Äîall support thyroid function
  • Nutrient-rich foods: Support foods rich in iron and vitamin D while she works with her practitioner on supplementation

Case Study 2: The Metabolic Mystery

Client Profile

Name: Robert, 52
Chief complaints: Belly fat that won't budge, fatigue after meals, increasing waist circumference, family history of diabetes

Lab Results

Fasting Glucose: 94 mg/dL (ref: 65-99) Flagged: Normal
HbA1c: 5.6% (ref: <5.7%) Flagged: Normal (high end)
Fasting Insulin: 18 ¬µIU/mL (ref: 2-25) Flagged: Normal (high)
Triglycerides: 185 mg/dL (ref: <150) HIGH
HDL: 38 mg/dL (ref: >40) LOW
LDL-C: 128 mg/dL (ref: <100) HIGH
hs-CRP: 3.2 mg/L (ref: <1.0 low risk) HIGH
ALT: 42 U/L (ref: 7-56) Flagged: Normal (elevated functional)
Uric Acid: 7.8 mg/dL (ref: 3.5-8.5) Flagged: Normal (high end)

Calculated Values

TG/HDL Ratio: 185/38 = 4.87 (optimal <2.0)
HOMA-IR: (94 √ó 18) √∑ 405 = 4.18 (optimal <1.0)

What the Labs Suggest

Despite a "normal" fasting glucose, Robert has clear evidence of metabolic dysfunction:

  • Significant insulin resistance: HOMA-IR of 4.18 is markedly elevated; fasting insulin of 18 is working hard to keep glucose normal
  • Metabolic syndrome pattern: High TG/HDL ratio, elevated triglycerides, low HDL, abdominal obesity
  • Systemic inflammation: hs-CRP >3.0 indicates high cardiovascular risk
  • Possible fatty liver: ALT in "high-normal" range with this metabolic pattern suggests NAFLD
  • Pre-prediabetes: He's on the path to diabetes even though he doesn't meet the official prediabetes criteria yet

‚úÖ Coaching Approach for Robert

  • Education: Help him understand that "normal" glucose doesn't mean metabolic health‚Äîhis insulin tells a different story
  • Dietary focus: Reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugars; these drive his insulin and triglycerides
  • Movement: Support regular exercise, which improves insulin sensitivity directly
  • Sleep: Poor sleep worsens insulin resistance‚Äîassess and support sleep quality
  • Motivation: This is a reversible pattern‚Äîlifestyle changes can dramatically improve these markers
  • Follow-up: Encourage repeat testing in 3-6 months to track progress

Case Study 3: The Anemia Investigation

Client Profile

Name: Maria, 38
Chief complaints: Severe fatigue, dizziness, heavy periods, hair falling out, constantly cold, heart palpitations

Lab Results

Hemoglobin: 10.8 g/dL (ref: 12.0-16.0) LOW
Hematocrit: 33% (ref: 36-44%) LOW
MCV: 72 fL (ref: 80-100) LOW (microcytic)
MCH: 24 pg (ref: 27-31) LOW
RDW: 16.8% (ref: 11.5-14.5%) HIGH
Ferritin: 8 ng/mL (ref: 12-150) LOW
Serum Iron: 35 ¬µg/dL (ref: 60-170) LOW
TIBC: 450 ¬µg/dL (ref: 250-370) HIGH
Iron Saturation: 8% (ref: 20-50%) LOW

What the Labs Suggest

This is a classic iron deficiency anemia pattern:

  • Confirmed anemia: Low hemoglobin and hematocrit
  • Microcytic pattern: Low MCV indicates small red blood cells‚Äîcharacteristic of iron deficiency
  • Depleted iron stores: Ferritin of 8 shows severely depleted stores
  • Inadequate iron: Low serum iron and iron saturation confirm insufficient available iron
  • Body trying to compensate: High TIBC shows the body is "reaching" for more iron
  • Elevated RDW: Shows variation in cell sizes, common in nutritional anemias

üö® Referral Priority

Maria needs prompt medical attention. While heavy menstrual periods may explain her iron deficiency, her practitioner should also investigate for other causes of blood loss (GI bleeding, etc.) and determine appropriate iron supplementation dosing. Do not attempt to treat this severity of anemia with coaching alone.

‚úÖ Coaching Approach for Maria

  • Ensure medical follow-up: She needs practitioner-supervised iron repletion
  • Support iron-rich foods: Red meat, organ meats, dark leafy greens, beans (paired with vitamin C for absorption)
  • Avoid iron blockers: Educate about not taking iron with coffee, tea, or calcium
  • Address heavy periods: This may need gynecological evaluation; support practitioner referral
  • Energy management: Help her pace activities while recovering
  • Monitor progress: Support follow-up lab testing to track improvement

Case Study 4: The Complex Multi-System Case

Client Profile

Name: Jennifer, 45
Chief complaints: Chronic fatigue, bloating, joint pain, brain fog, anxiety, irregular periods, worsening over past 3 years

Selected Lab Findings

Thyroid: TSH 2.8, Free T3 low-normal, TPO antibodies 95 (elevated)
Metabolic: Fasting glucose 92, HbA1c 5.5%, insulin 12 (elevated functional)
Inflammation: hs-CRP 2.1 (elevated), homocysteine 14 (elevated)
Hormones (DUTCH): Low progesterone, estrogen dominance pattern, poor estrogen detox (elevated 4-OH pathway)
Adrenals: Flattened cortisol curve, low DHEA
GI-MAP: Low beneficial bacteria, elevated beta-glucuronidase, positive for H. pylori
Nutrients: Vitamin D 28, B12 350 (low-normal), ferritin 35 (suboptimal)

Pattern Recognition

Jennifer's labs reveal an interconnected web of dysfunction:

  • Autoimmune process: Hashimoto's indicated by elevated TPO antibodies
  • HPA axis dysfunction: Flattened cortisol with low DHEA suggests chronic stress/burnout
  • Hormone imbalance: Estrogen dominance with poor clearance, worsened by elevated beta-glucuronidase
  • Gut dysfunction: H. pylori infection, dysbiosis affecting estrogen metabolism
  • Inflammation: Elevated hs-CRP and homocysteine indicate systemic inflammation
  • Metabolic stress: Insulin resistance developing
  • Nutrient inadequacies: Contributing to poor methylation and cellular function

üîç The Interconnected Picture

Jennifer's case illustrates how dysfunction in one system affects others. Her gut dysbiosis elevates beta-glucuronidase, which recirculates estrogen, worsening estrogen dominance. Chronic stress (flattened cortisol) impairs thyroid conversion, gut function, and hormone balance. Inflammation from multiple sources drives fatigue and brain fog. This is why functional medicine takes a systems approach rather than treating symptoms in isolation.

‚úÖ Coaching Approach for Jennifer

Jennifer needs a comprehensive treatment plan developed by her functional medicine practitioner. Your coaching role:

  • Support prioritization: Help her understand that treating H. pylori and improving gut health is foundational
  • Stress management: This is critical given her HPA dysfunction‚Äîguide practical daily practices
  • Anti-inflammatory nutrition: Support dietary changes to reduce inflammation and support hormone metabolism
  • Sleep optimization: Essential for cortisol rhythm restoration
  • Cruciferous vegetables: Support estrogen metabolism through the favorable 2-OH pathway
  • Emotional support: Complex cases can be overwhelming‚Äîhelp her stay motivated and patient
  • Coordinate with practitioner: Ensure clear communication about supplements and treatment sequence

Key Principles from These Cases

1. "Normal" Doesn't Mean Optimal

Many symptomatic clients have "normal" labs by conventional standards. Functional optimal ranges are narrower and more relevant to symptoms.

2. Patterns Tell the Story

Individual values matter less than patterns across multiple markers. The TG/HDL ratio, HOMA-IR, and marker clusters reveal more than any single test.

3. Systems Are Connected

Gut health affects hormones, hormones affect thyroid, thyroid affects metabolism, stress affects everything. Understanding these connections helps prioritize interventions.

4. Know When to Refer

Significant abnormalities (severe anemia, high calprotectin, concerning glucose levels) need medical attention. Our role is to recognize and facilitate appropriate care.

5. Labs Guide But Don't Replace Clinical Judgment

Labs are tools that support‚Äîbut don't replace‚Äîthe clinical picture. Symptoms, history, and client context matter alongside the numbers.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Pattern recognition across multiple markers reveals more than isolated values
  • Functional optimal ranges help explain symptoms that "normal" labs don't capture
  • Complex cases often involve interconnected dysfunction across multiple systems
  • Our role is to support client understanding, advocate for appropriate testing, and facilitate practitioner communication
  • Severe abnormalities warrant prompt medical referral‚Äîknow your scope limits
  • Lab literacy makes us better coaches, even though interpretation remains with practitioners

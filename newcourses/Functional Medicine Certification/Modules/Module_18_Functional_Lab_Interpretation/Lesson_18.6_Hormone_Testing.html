Hormone Testing Methods

Hormones can be measured through several methods, each with advantages and limitations:

Method Advantages Limitations
Serum (Blood) Standard, widely available, measures total and free hormones, covered by insurance Single point in time, measures bound and unbound together, difficult for cortisol patterns
Saliva Measures free (bioavailable) hormones, multiple samples possible, non-invasive, good for cortisol rhythm Affected by contamination, may not accurately reflect tissue levels, limited hormones available
Urine (24-hour) Reflects daily production, includes metabolites Cumbersome collection, affected by kidney function
DUTCH (Dried Urine) Multiple time points, shows hormone metabolites, cortisol rhythm, methylation markers Higher cost, not covered by insurance, requires interpretation expertise

Female Sex Hormone Testing

Estrogen (Estradiol)

Estradiol (E2) is the primary estrogen during reproductive years. Levels fluctuate significantly throughout the menstrual cycle.

Serum Estradiol Reference Ranges

Follicular phase: 27-123 pg/mL
Ovulatory phase: 96-436 pg/mL
Luteal phase: 49-294 pg/mL
Postmenopausal: <10-20 pg/mL

Key insight: A single blood test can't capture the full estrogen picture. Testing should ideally be done at specific cycle days, or multiple samples throughout the month may be needed.

Progesterone

Progesterone rises after ovulation and is essential for balancing estrogen, supporting sleep, and maintaining pregnancy. Low progesterone relative to estrogen creates "estrogen dominance."

Serum Progesterone Reference Ranges

Follicular phase: <1 ng/mL
Mid-luteal phase (day 21): 5-20 ng/mL (ideally >10 ng/mL)
Postmenopausal: <0.5 ng/mL

Timing is critical: Progesterone should be tested 7 days after ovulation (typically day 21 of a 28-day cycle). Testing at the wrong time will show falsely low values.

üìä The Progesterone/Estradiol Ratio

In the luteal phase (serum testing), optimal P:E2 ratio is approximately 100:1 to 500:1. Lower ratios suggest relative progesterone deficiency or estrogen excess‚Äîa pattern associated with PMS, heavy periods, and perimenopausal symptoms.

FSH and LH

FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone) and LH (Luteinizing Hormone) are pituitary hormones that regulate the menstrual cycle.

FSH reproductive range: 3-12 mIU/mL (follicular phase)
FSH perimenopause/menopause: >25-40 mIU/mL indicates declining ovarian function
LH reproductive range: 2-15 mIU/mL (follicular phase), surge to 20-100+ mid-cycle

Clinical use: Elevated FSH (>25-40) with irregular periods suggests perimenopause. The FSH:LH ratio is also relevant‚Äîin PCOS, LH is often elevated relative to FSH (ratio >2:1).

Testosterone in Women

Women produce testosterone in the ovaries and adrenal glands. It's important for libido, energy, muscle mass, and mood.

Total Testosterone (women): 15-70 ng/dL
Free Testosterone (women): 0.5-5.0 pg/mL
Functional optimal: Mid-range for symptoms

Low testosterone: Fatigue, low libido, depression, muscle loss. High testosterone: Acne, hair loss, hirsutism‚Äîoften seen in PCOS.

Adrenal Testing

The adrenal glands produce cortisol, DHEA, and other hormones essential for stress response and energy.

Cortisol

Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm‚Äîhighest in the morning (waking response) and lowest at night. Single blood tests can miss dysfunction.

The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)

Healthy cortisol should spike 30-45 minutes after waking (the CAR), then gradually decline throughout the day, reaching its lowest point around midnight. A flattened morning response or elevated nighttime cortisol indicates HPA axis dysfunction.

Salivary Cortisol Reference Ranges (typical 4-point)

Morning (waking): High end of range (varies by lab)
Noon: Declining
Afternoon/Evening: Lower
Night (before bed): Lowest

Patterns to recognize: Flat line (low all day = HPA suppression), inverted (low morning/high evening = circadian disruption), high throughout (chronic stress), mixed patterns

DHEA and DHEA-S

DHEA (Dehydroepiandrosterone) is an adrenal hormone that serves as a precursor to sex hormones. DHEA-S (sulfate) is the more stable, longer-lasting form typically measured in blood.

DHEA-S (women): 35-430 ¬µg/dL (age-dependent)
DHEA-S (men): 80-560 ¬µg/dL (age-dependent)

Clinical significance: DHEA naturally declines with age. Low DHEA relative to cortisol suggests chronic stress and adrenal depletion. The cortisol:DHEA ratio provides insight into stress resilience.

DUTCH Testing: Beyond Levels to Metabolism

The DUTCH test (Dried Urine Test for Comprehensive Hormones) has revolutionized hormone assessment by measuring not just hormone levels but their metabolites‚Äîshowing how the body processes and clears hormones.

Estrogen Metabolites

Estrogen can be metabolized through three primary pathways:

2-OH Pathway (Favorable)

The "protective" pathway. 2-hydroxyestrone and 2-hydroxyestradiol are less estrogenic and readily cleared. Higher 2-OH metabolism is generally desirable.

4-OH Pathway (Concerning)

4-hydroxyestrone can form DNA-damaging quinones if not properly detoxified. Elevated 4-OH metabolites may increase estrogen-related cancer risk.

16-OH Pathway (Proliferative)

16-alpha-hydroxyestrone is a potent estrogen that can stimulate tissue growth. Elevated levels may increase breast cancer risk.

üìä The 2:16 Ratio

The ratio of 2-OH to 16-OH metabolites is often used as a marker of estrogen metabolism health. Higher ratios (favoring 2-OH) are generally considered protective. Cruciferous vegetables, particularly DIM (diindolylmethane) and I3C (indole-3-carbinol), can help shift metabolism toward the 2-OH pathway.

Methylation of Estrogen Metabolites

After phase I metabolism (2-OH, 4-OH, 16-OH), estrogen metabolites undergo phase II methylation for safe elimination. The DUTCH test measures:

  • 2-Methoxyestrone (2-MeOE1): Methylated 2-OH product‚Äîprotective
  • 4-Methoxyestrone (4-MeOE1): Methylated 4-OH product‚Äîreduces 4-OH risk

Poor methylation (low methylated products) suggests the body isn't efficiently clearing estrogen metabolites, which may be addressed with methylation support (B vitamins, particularly methylfolate and B12).

Cortisol Metabolites on DUTCH

The DUTCH test also measures cortisol metabolites, providing information about:

  • Total cortisol production: How much cortisol the adrenals are making
  • Free cortisol: Bioavailable cortisol at different times of day
  • Cortisone/Cortisol ratio: Reflects 11Œ≤-HSD enzyme activity (tissue activation/inactivation)
  • Metabolized cortisol: Shows overall clearance patterns

Testing Considerations

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Timing Considerations

  • Cycling women: Test progesterone day 19-21 (or 7 days post-ovulation); estrogen can be tested days 2-3 (follicular baseline) or mid-luteal
  • Perimenopausal: Multiple samples may be needed due to irregular cycles
  • Postmenopausal: Any day is acceptable
  • On hormone therapy: Test at trough (before next dose) for most accurate assessment
  • Cortisol: Morning sample should be within 30 minutes of waking

Common Hormone Patterns

Estrogen Dominance Pattern

Normal or elevated estrogen, low progesterone, symptoms: PMS, heavy periods, breast tenderness, weight gain, mood swings

Perimenopause Pattern

Fluctuating estrogen, declining progesterone, rising FSH, irregular cycles, hot flashes, sleep disruption

HPA Axis Dysregulation (Early)

High cortisol throughout day, elevated DHEA, symptoms of chronic stress, anxiety, weight gain, sleep issues

HPA Axis Dysregulation (Late)

Low cortisol (flat pattern), low DHEA, profound fatigue, inability to handle stress, "burned out"

PCOS Pattern

Elevated testosterone and/or DHEA, elevated LH:FSH ratio, often elevated insulin, irregular cycles, acne, hirsutism

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Different testing methods (blood, saliva, urine) each have advantages for different hormones
  • Timing is critical‚Äîtest progesterone 7 days post-ovulation, cortisol throughout the day
  • The DUTCH test reveals hormone metabolites, showing how the body processes hormones
  • Estrogen metabolism pathways (2-OH, 4-OH, 16-OH) affect health risks
  • Cortisol should follow a diurnal rhythm‚Äîpatterns matter more than single values
  • The cortisol:DHEA ratio indicates stress resilience and adrenal reserve

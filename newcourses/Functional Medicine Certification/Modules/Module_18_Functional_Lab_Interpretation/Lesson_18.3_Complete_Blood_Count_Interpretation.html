Understanding the Complete Blood Count

The Complete Blood Count (CBC) measures the cells circulating in your blood: red blood cells that carry oxygen, white blood cells that fight infection, and platelets that help with clotting. A CBC with differential also breaks down the types of white blood cells.

CBC Components at a Glance

Red Blood Cell Markers: RBC count, Hemoglobin, Hematocrit, MCV, MCH, MCHC, RDW

White Blood Cell Markers: WBC count, Neutrophils, Lymphocytes, Monocytes, Eosinophils, Basophils

Platelet Markers: Platelet count, MPV (Mean Platelet Volume)

Red Blood Cell Markers

Red blood cells (RBCs) carry oxygen from the lungs to tissues throughout the body. Several markers help assess their quantity, size, and hemoglobin content.

RBC Count

The total number of red blood cells per microliter of blood.

Women: Conventional: 4.0-5.5 million/¬µL | Optimal: 4.2-4.9 million/¬µL
Men: Conventional: 4.5-6.0 million/¬µL | Optimal: 4.5-5.5 million/¬µL

Hemoglobin (Hgb)

Hemoglobin is the iron-containing protein in red blood cells that binds oxygen. It's the primary marker used to diagnose anemia.

Women: Conventional: 12.0-16.0 g/dL | Optimal: 13.5-14.5 g/dL
Men: Conventional: 14.0-18.0 g/dL | Optimal: 14.0-15.0 g/dL

Hematocrit (Hct)

Hematocrit represents the percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells. It reflects both RBC count and cell size.

Women: Conventional: 36-44% | Optimal: 40-45%
Men: Conventional: 40-54% | Optimal: 42-48%

Red Blood Cell Indices

The RBC indices tell us about the size and hemoglobin content of red blood cells‚Äîessential for determining the type of anemia if present.

MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)

What it measures: Average size of red blood cells
Conventional range: 80-100 fL | Optimal: 85-92 fL

Low MCV (microcytic): Small cells, typically iron deficiency or thalassemia
High MCV (macrocytic): Large cells, typically B12/folate deficiency, hypothyroidism, or liver disease

MCH (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin)

What it measures: Average amount of hemoglobin per red blood cell
Conventional range: 27-31 pg | Optimal: 28-32 pg

Low MCH: Often parallels low MCV (iron deficiency)
High MCH: Often parallels high MCV (B12/folate deficiency)

MCHC (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration)

What it measures: Average concentration of hemoglobin in red blood cells
Conventional range: 32-36 g/dL | Optimal: 32-35 g/dL

Low MCHC: Hypochromic cells (pale), iron deficiency
High MCHC: Rare; may indicate hereditary spherocytosis or lab error

RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width)

What it measures: Variation in red blood cell size
Conventional range: 11.5-14.5% | Optimal: <13%

Elevated RDW: Indicates cells of varying sizes (anisocytosis)‚Äîcommon in nutritional deficiencies, mixed anemias, and early iron deficiency. Also associated with cardiovascular risk and inflammation.

Types of Anemia

The RBC indices help classify anemia by cell size, which points to underlying causes:

Type MCV Common Causes
Microcytic
(Small cells)		 <80 fL Iron deficiency (most common), thalassemia, chronic disease, lead poisoning
Normocytic
(Normal size)		 80-100 fL Anemia of chronic disease, acute blood loss, bone marrow disorders, kidney disease
Macrocytic
(Large cells)		 >100 fL B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, hypothyroidism, liver disease, alcoholism, certain medications

üîç Key Insight: The Iron Panel Connection

Hemoglobin may be "normal" while iron stores are depleted. By the time hemoglobin drops, iron deficiency is advanced. This is why functional practitioners often request ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, and iron saturation to catch iron deficiency early‚Äîespecially in menstruating women.

White Blood Cell Markers

White blood cells (WBCs), or leukocytes, are the immune system's soldiers. The total WBC count and the differential (breakdown by type) provide insight into immune function and potential infections or inflammation.

Total WBC Count

Conventional range: 4,500-11,000 cells/¬µL | Optimal: 5,500-7,500 cells/¬µL

Elevated WBC (Leukocytosis) may indicate:

  • Bacterial infection
  • Inflammation
  • Stress response
  • Corticosteroid use
  • Smoking
  • Leukemia (very high levels)

Low WBC (Leukopenia) may indicate:

  • Viral infection
  • Bone marrow suppression
  • Autoimmune disorders
  • Severe infections (exhaustion)
  • Certain medications

The White Blood Cell Differential

The WBC differential breaks down the types of white blood cells, each with different functions:

Cell Type Normal % Function & Clinical Significance
Neutrophils 40-60% First responders to bacterial infection. Elevated in bacterial infections, stress, inflammation. Low in viral infections, autoimmune disease.
Lymphocytes 20-40% Include T cells, B cells, NK cells. Elevated in viral infections, chronic infections, autoimmune disease. Low with corticosteroids, HIV, stress.
Monocytes 2-8% Become macrophages in tissues. Elevated in chronic infections, inflammatory conditions, recovery from infection.
Eosinophils 1-4% Target parasites and involved in allergic reactions. Elevated in allergies, asthma, parasites, eczema, drug reactions.
Basophils 0.5-1% Release histamine in allergic reactions. Elevated in allergic reactions, chronic inflammation, certain blood disorders.

Pattern Recognition in the Differential

Bacterial Infection Pattern

Elevated total WBC, elevated neutrophils (neutrophilia), decreased lymphocytes percentage

Viral Infection Pattern

Normal or low total WBC, elevated lymphocytes (lymphocytosis), decreased neutrophils percentage

Parasitic/Allergic Pattern

Elevated eosinophils (eosinophilia), possibly elevated basophils, often with allergy symptoms

Chronic Stress/Inflammation Pattern

Elevated total WBC, elevated neutrophils, low lymphocytes (cortisol suppresses lymphocytes)

Platelet Markers

Platelets (thrombocytes) are cell fragments essential for blood clotting. They aggregate at injury sites to form plugs and activate the clotting cascade.

Platelet Count

Conventional range: 150,000-400,000/¬µL | Optimal: 200,000-300,000/¬µL

Low Platelets (Thrombocytopenia) may indicate:

  • Viral infections
  • Autoimmune disorders (ITP)
  • Bone marrow suppression
  • Liver disease (spleen sequestration)
  • Certain medications
  • B12/folate deficiency

High Platelets (Thrombocytosis) may indicate:

  • Inflammation or infection
  • Iron deficiency
  • Post-surgery or trauma
  • Chronic inflammatory conditions
  • Blood disorders (less common)

MPV (Mean Platelet Volume)

Conventional range: 7.5-11.5 fL | Optimal: 7.5-10.0 fL

MPV indicates the average size of platelets. Young platelets are larger than old ones, so MPV provides information about platelet production:

  • High MPV: Increased platelet production (bone marrow making more), cardiovascular risk marker
  • Low MPV: Bone marrow not producing well, or platelets being destroyed peripherally

‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Pearl: MPV and Cardiovascular Risk

Research shows elevated MPV is associated with increased cardiovascular risk, including heart attack and stroke. Large platelets are more reactive and "stickier." This is one reason functional practitioners pay attention to MPV even when platelet count is normal.

Putting It All Together

The CBC provides rich information when viewed as patterns rather than isolated values. Here are some common functional patterns:

Iron Deficiency Pattern

Low hemoglobin, low MCV (microcytic), low MCH, low MCHC, elevated RDW, possibly elevated platelets

B12/Folate Deficiency Pattern

Low hemoglobin (if anemic), high MCV (macrocytic), high MCH, elevated RDW, possibly low WBC and platelets

Chronic Inflammation Pattern

Normal or low hemoglobin (anemia of chronic disease), elevated WBC, elevated platelets, elevated RDW

Dehydration Pattern

Elevated hemoglobin, elevated hematocrit, elevated RBC count‚Äîall falsely elevated due to concentrated blood

üìö Key Takeaways

  • The CBC reveals red cell health (oxygen capacity), immune function (WBCs), and clotting ability (platelets)
  • RBC indices (MCV, MCH, MCHC, RDW) help classify anemia type and identify nutritional deficiencies
  • The WBC differential shows which immune cells are elevated or depleted, suggesting infection type
  • Platelet count and MPV provide information about clotting risk and bone marrow function
  • Pattern recognition‚Äîlooking at multiple markers together‚Äîreveals more than any single value
  • Early functional changes often appear before frank anemia develops

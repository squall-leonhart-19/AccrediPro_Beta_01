üí≠ The Question That Changed Everything

Sandra had seen three doctors for her chronic migraines. Each had asked about her symptoms: How often? How severe? What triggers them? Each prescribed the same medications with marginal benefit.

Then she worked with a functional health coach who asked a different question: "Tell me about your childhood. Were there any significant illnesses, stressors, or events that stand out to you?"

Sandra paused. "Well... I had a lot of ear infections. I was on antibiotics constantly from ages 2 to 10." This one detail‚Äînever explored by previous practitioners‚Äîopened a door to understanding her gut health, inflammation patterns, and yes, her migraines. The right questions change everything.

The Two-Part Assessment System

Effective health history collection uses a two-part system: a written questionnaire completed before the session, followed by an intake interview that brings the data to life. Each serves a different purpose:

üìã Written Questionnaire

  • Collects comprehensive baseline data
  • Allows clients time to remember and reflect
  • Covers extensive information efficiently
  • Provides written record for documentation
  • Frees session time for deeper conversation

üí¨ Intake Interview

  • Clarifies and expands on written responses
  • Explores emotional context and meaning
  • Builds rapport through conversation
  • Allows follow-up questions in real-time
  • Reveals information clients didn't write

Essential Categories for Health History

A comprehensive health history questionnaire should cover multiple domains. Here's what to include and why each matters:

üìù Health History Categories

1. Identifying Information & Chief Concerns

What to gather: Demographics, primary health goals, main symptoms, what prompted them to seek help now.

Why it matters: Establishes context and helps you understand their priorities. The "why now?" question often reveals important triggers.

2. Medical History

What to gather: Past diagnoses, surgeries, hospitalizations, accidents/injuries, chronic conditions, pregnancy history.

Why it matters: Previous health events often leave lasting effects. A surgery 10 years ago could impact current gut health; a head injury could affect brain function.

3. Current Medications & Supplements

What to gather: All prescription medications, OTC drugs, supplements, dosages, how long they've been taking them.

Why it matters: Medications can cause side effects that mimic disease. Nutrient depletions from drugs are common. This also reveals what's been tried.

4. Family Health History

What to gather: Health conditions in parents, grandparents, siblings, children. Age of onset and cause of death if deceased.

Why it matters: Reveals genetic predispositions and patterns. Family history of autoimmunity, heart disease, or diabetes indicates areas of focus.

5. Early Life & Birth History

What to gather: Birth type (vaginal/C-section), breastfed vs. formula, childhood illnesses, antibiotic use, early trauma, childhood diet.

Why it matters: Early life shapes microbiome, immune development, and stress response systems. C-section birth, formula feeding, and frequent antibiotics all have lasting effects.

6. Systems Review (Symptom Inventory)

What to gather: Symptoms across all body systems‚Äîdigestive, neurological, hormonal, immune, cardiovascular, musculoskeletal, skin, mood.

Why it matters: Clients often don't mention symptoms they consider "normal" or unrelated. A systems review catches what's missed and reveals connections.

7. Diet & Nutrition

What to gather: Typical daily eating patterns, food sensitivities/allergies, diets tried, relationship with food, eating behaviors, hydration.

Why it matters: Food is medicine (or poison). Understanding current patterns and history with diets helps personalize recommendations.

8. Sleep Patterns

What to gather: Bedtime/wake time, sleep duration, sleep quality, trouble falling or staying asleep, snoring/apnea, daytime energy.

Why it matters: Sleep affects every system. Poor sleep undermines any other intervention. Often underexplored in conventional care.

9. Stress & Mental/Emotional Health

What to gather: Current stressors, history of anxiety/depression, trauma history, coping mechanisms, support systems, sense of purpose.

Why it matters: Stress is a driver of most chronic conditions. Trauma history often correlates with health issues. Mind and body are inseparable.

10. Movement & Exercise

What to gather: Current activity level, exercise type and frequency, sedentary time, barriers to movement, past activity levels.

Why it matters: Movement affects metabolism, inflammation, mood, and longevity. Understanding barriers helps with realistic recommendations.

11. Environmental Exposures

What to gather: Home environment, mold exposure, chemical exposures at work, travel history, water source, cosmetics/products used.

Why it matters: Environmental toxins are an often-missed piece of the puzzle. Mold illness, for example, is frequently undiagnosed.

12. Social & Lifestyle Factors

What to gather: Relationships, work situation, community involvement, hobbies, alcohol/tobacco/substance use, screen time.

Why it matters: Social connection is as important as diet for health outcomes. Isolation is a major risk factor. Work stress drives disease.

The Art of the Health History Interview

Once your client has completed the written questionnaire, the intake interview transforms data into understanding. Here's how to conduct an effective interview:

Opening the Conversation

Start with an open-ended invitation that lets the client share their story in their own words:

üéôÔ∏è Powerful Opening Questions

  • "I've reviewed your questionnaire‚Äîthank you for being so thorough. I'd love to hear your story in your own words. What brought you to this point?"
  • "Tell me about your health journey. Where did things start to shift for you?"
  • "If I could wave a magic wand and give you perfect health, what would be different about your life?"
  • "What's the one thing about your health that bothers you most‚Äîthe thing you'd most want to change?"

Exploring the Details

As clients share, use probing questions to go deeper. The goal is to move from general statements to specific, actionable information:

When did this start?

"Can you pinpoint when this first began? What else was going on in your life at that time?"

What makes it better or worse?

"Have you noticed anything that triggers this or makes it worse? Anything that provides relief?"

What's the pattern?

"Is there a pattern‚Äîcertain times of day, month, or year when it's worse? After eating certain foods?"

What have you tried?

"What have you already tried for this? What worked, even partially? What didn't help at all?"

What's the impact?

"How does this affect your daily life? What can't you do because of this that you wish you could?"

What do you think is going on?

"Do you have any sense of what might be causing this? What does your gut tell you?"

üí° Pro Tip: The Power of "Tell Me More"

When a client says something intriguing‚Äîeven if you're not sure why it's important‚Äîsimply say "Tell me more about that." This phrase is your best friend. It invites deeper exploration without leading the client in any direction.

Asking About Sensitive Topics

Some of the most clinically relevant information is also the most sensitive: trauma, sexual health, mental health, substance use, eating disorders, abuse. Create safety with these strategies:

üõ°Ô∏è Approaching Sensitive Topics

Normalize the question:

"I ask all my clients about this because it's so often connected to health..."

Ask permission:

"Would it be okay if I asked about your history with...?"

Explain why you're asking:

"Research shows that early life stress can affect health decades later. Did you experience any significant stress or trauma growing up?"

Give them control:

"You can share as much or as little as you're comfortable with. We can always come back to this later."

Respond without alarm:

If they do share something significant, respond calmly: "Thank you for trusting me with that. I can see how that might be connected to what you're experiencing now."

Red Flag Follow-Ups

Certain responses on the questionnaire should trigger specific follow-up questions. Here are some examples:

If They Report... Follow Up With...
Unexplained weight loss "How much weight have you lost, over what period? Have you changed your diet or is the loss unintentional?"
Chronic fatigue "When did the fatigue start? Is it constant or does it fluctuate? Does rest help? What does your sleep look like?"
Digestive complaints "What do your bowel movements look like? Any blood? Pain before or after eating? Any foods that trigger symptoms?"
Brain fog/memory issues "When did this start? Is it worse at certain times? Any head injuries? How's your sleep? Any mold exposure?"
Mood changes "Have you always experienced this or is it new? Any thoughts of harming yourself? Are you working with a therapist?"
Multiple antibiotic courses "How many courses roughly? What were they for? Did you notice any changes in digestion or immunity afterward?"

What Good Intake Looks Like: Example Dialogue

üí¨ Intake Interview Example

Client: My main concern is bloating. I bloat after almost everything I eat.

Coach: Bloating that happens with almost every meal sounds really uncomfortable. How long has this been going on?

Client: About three years. But it's gotten worse in the last year.

Coach: So it started about three years ago and escalated recently. Can you think of anything that was happening around the time it started‚Äîany illness, travel, stressful period, medication changes?

Client: Hmm... I did go to Thailand around that time. I got pretty sick there‚Äîterrible stomach bug. But I thought I got over it.

Coach: That's interesting timing. Post-infectious gut issues are really common. Tell me more about what happened in Thailand and what you experienced after you got home.

[The coach has now connected the client's bloating to a potential post-infectious trigger that would have been missed without careful questioning about onset timing.]

üîë Key Takeaways

  • Use a two-part system: written questionnaire for comprehensive data, followed by interview for depth and context
  • Cover all essential categories: medical history, family history, early life, lifestyle, environment, and systems review
  • Open with broad questions that invite the client's story, then probe for specific details
  • "Tell me more" is one of the most powerful phrases in your toolkit
  • Create safety for sensitive topics through normalization, permission, and calm responses
  • Always ask about timing‚Äîwhen symptoms started often reveals triggers

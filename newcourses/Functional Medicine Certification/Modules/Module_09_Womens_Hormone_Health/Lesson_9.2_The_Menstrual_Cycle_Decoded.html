The Menstrual Cycle: A Vital Sign

The menstrual cycle is much more than a reproductive event‚Äîit's considered the fifth vital sign by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists. A healthy, regular cycle indicates that the hormonal, metabolic, and reproductive systems are functioning well.

When the cycle is disrupted‚Äîirregular, absent, excessively painful, or abnormal in other ways‚Äîit's often a sign that something else is off: stress, nutrition, thyroid function, or other underlying issues.

The Four Phases of the Menstrual Cycle

The cycle is divided into four distinct phases, each with unique hormonal characteristics, symptoms, and opportunities:

ü©∏ Phase 1: Menstruation (Days 1-5)

What's happening: The uterine lining sheds because pregnancy didn't occur. Estrogen and progesterone are at their lowest. This is a time of release and renewal.

Hormones:

  • Estrogen: Low (lowest)
  • Progesterone: Low (lowest)
  • FSH: Beginning to rise

Common Experiences:

  • Fatigue, lower energy
  • Cramps (prostaglandins)
  • Desire for rest and reflection
  • Often increased intuition

üí° Coaching Tips:

Honor the body's need for rest. Scale back intense exercise (gentle movement is fine). Focus on restorative activities. Nutrient needs increase‚Äîsupport with iron-rich foods.

üå± Phase 2: Follicular Phase (Days 6-14)

What's happening: FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) from the pituitary stimulates ovarian follicles to grow. Several follicles begin developing; one will become dominant. Estrogen rises as follicles produce it.

Hormones:

  • Estrogen: Rising steadily
  • Progesterone: Still low
  • FSH: Elevated, then declining
  • Testosterone: Rising

Common Experiences:

  • Energy increasing
  • Mood improving
  • Mental clarity, creativity
  • Skin clearing up
  • Increased sociability

üí° Coaching Tips:

Great time to start new projects, try challenging workouts, schedule important meetings. Rising estrogen supports muscle building‚Äîideal for strength training. Energy for social activities.

üåü Phase 3: Ovulation (Day 14-ish)

What's happening: The mature follicle releases an egg. LH (luteinizing hormone) surges, triggering ovulation. Estrogen peaks, and testosterone also spikes. This is a brief window‚Äîtypically 12-24 hours.

Hormones:

  • Estrogen: Peak, then drops
  • LH: Dramatic surge (ovulation trigger)
  • Testosterone: Brief peak
  • Progesterone: About to rise

Common Experiences:

  • Peak energy and confidence
  • Increased libido (biological design)
  • Communication skills at best
  • May notice cervical mucus changes
  • Some women feel ovulation pain (mittelschmerz)

üí° Coaching Tips:

Schedule high-stakes communication‚Äîpresentations, negotiations, difficult conversations. Peak physical performance potential. Social energy is highest. Fertility window for those trying to conceive (or avoid).

üåô Phase 4: Luteal Phase (Days 15-28)

What's happening: The empty follicle becomes the corpus luteum and produces progesterone to maintain the uterine lining in case of pregnancy. If pregnancy doesn't occur, the corpus luteum degenerates, hormones drop, and the cycle begins again.

Hormones:

  • Progesterone: Rises, peaks mid-luteal, then falls
  • Estrogen: Second, smaller rise, then falls
  • Both drop dramatically before period

Common Experiences:

  • Early luteal: Still good energy, slightly calmer
  • Late luteal (premenstrual): Energy declining, PMS symptoms possible
  • Increased body temperature
  • Breast tenderness, bloating (some women)
  • Mood changes, irritability (some women)

üí° Coaching Tips:

Good time for detail-oriented work, completion of projects, administrative tasks. As energy declines in late luteal, scale back intensity. Focus on self-care. Increased carbohydrate cravings are normal‚Äîprovide healthy options.

Why Ovulation Matters

Ovulation is the main event of the cycle‚Äînot menstruation. Without ovulation, there's no progesterone production. And progesterone is essential for:

  • Counterbalancing estrogen (preventing estrogen dominance)
  • Supporting mood and reducing anxiety
  • Promoting quality sleep
  • Building bone
  • Overall hormonal balance

Anovulatory cycles (cycles without ovulation) still produce estrogen but no progesterone‚Äîleading to an imbalanced hormonal state. Signs may include:

  • Very light or very heavy periods
  • Irregular cycle length
  • No mid-cycle signs (cervical mucus, mittelschmerz)
  • More severe PMS (progesterone's calming effect is missing)

Important: Women on hormonal birth control (pills, patch, ring, hormonal IUD) do not ovulate. Their "period" is a withdrawal bleed, not a true menstrual cycle. This isn't inherently bad, but it means they're not producing their own progesterone‚Äîsomething to understand when transitioning off hormonal contraception.

Signs of Ovulation

Learning to recognize ovulation helps with fertility awareness and overall cycle understanding:

ü•ö Cervical Mucus Changes

Near ovulation, cervical mucus becomes clear, stretchy, and slippery‚Äîlike raw egg whites. This fertile mucus helps sperm survival and transport. After ovulation, it becomes thicker and cloudier.

üå°Ô∏è Basal Body Temperature (BBT)

Body temperature rises 0.3-0.5¬∞F after ovulation due to progesterone's thermogenic effect. This shift confirms ovulation has occurred (but doesn't predict it in advance). Requires daily temperature tracking first thing in the morning.

üí´ Mittelschmerz (Ovulation Pain)

Some women feel a twinge or mild cramp on one side of the lower abdomen at ovulation. This is thought to be from follicle rupture or fluid release.

üíó Increased Libido

Many women notice heightened sexual desire around ovulation‚Äîa biological mechanism to encourage reproduction during the fertile window.

üìä LH Surge (Test Kits)

Ovulation predictor kits detect the LH surge that triggers ovulation, typically occurring 24-36 hours before the egg is released.

What's Normal vs. What's Concerning

‚úì Normal Variations

  • Cycle length 21-35 days
  • Variation of a few days cycle to cycle
  • Bleeding 3-7 days
  • Mild cramps first 1-2 days
  • Mild mood changes premenstrually
  • Some clots (smaller than a quarter)
  • Occasional off cycle

‚ö†Ô∏è Signs to Investigate

  • Cycles shorter than 21 or longer than 35 days
  • Highly irregular cycles
  • Very heavy bleeding (soaking through pads/tampons hourly)
  • Severe, debilitating cramps
  • Severe PMS/PMDD affecting function
  • Bleeding between periods
  • No period for 3+ months (not pregnant)

Cycle Tracking: A Powerful Tool

Encourage clients to track their cycles. This provides valuable data for:

  • Pattern recognition: Connect symptoms to cycle phases
  • Health monitoring: Identify changes that may indicate hormonal shifts
  • Life planning: Schedule activities to align with energy levels
  • Fertility awareness: For conception or avoidance
  • Medical appointments: Provide concrete data to healthcare providers

üì± Cycle Tracking Methods

Apps: Clue, Flo, Kindara, Natural Cycles, and others allow tracking periods, symptoms, temperature, and more
Paper tracking: Simple calendar noting period dates, symptoms, energy levels, mood
Wearables: Oura Ring, Tempdrop, and others can track temperature and sleep for cycle insights
Fertility Awareness Methods (FAM): Detailed tracking of temperature, cervical mucus, and cervical position

Cycle Syncing: Working With Your Biology

Cycle syncing is the practice of aligning nutrition, exercise, work, and self-care with cycle phases. While the science is still emerging, many women find this approach improves energy, reduces PMS, and enhances overall wellbeing.

üîë Key Takeaways

  • The menstrual cycle is a vital sign reflecting overall hormonal health
  • The cycle has four phases: menstruation, follicular, ovulation, and luteal
  • Ovulation is the main event‚Äîit produces progesterone, essential for hormonal balance
  • Normal cycles range from 21-35 days; significant irregularity warrants investigation
  • Cycle tracking provides valuable data for health optimization and medical care
  • Cycle syncing‚Äîaligning activities with cycle phases‚Äîcan enhance energy and wellbeing
  • Women are cyclical beings; working with the cycle rather than against it is empowering

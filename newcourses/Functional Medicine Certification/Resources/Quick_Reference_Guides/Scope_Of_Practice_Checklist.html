Scope of Practice Quick Reference

Health Coach Boundaries & Guidelines

Critical Reminder

Health coaches provide support for behavior change and lifestyle modification. We educate, guide, and support—we do not diagnose, treat, or prescribe. When in doubt, refer out. Staying within scope protects both you and your clients.

What Health Coaches CAN Do

Education & Information

✓ Educate about general nutrition principles

✓ Share information about healthy lifestyle practices

✓ Explain how lifestyle factors affect health

✓ Discuss the benefits of whole foods

✓ Provide general information about supplements

✓ Share resources and educational materials

✓ Explain functional medicine concepts

Coaching & Support

✓ Help clients set and achieve health goals

✓ Provide accountability and motivation

✓ Use coaching techniques (MI, active listening)

✓ Help identify barriers to change

✓ Support behavior modification

✓ Track progress and celebrate wins

✓ Empower clients to make their own decisions

Lifestyle Guidance

✓ Create general healthy eating plans

✓ Recommend stress management techniques

✓ Suggest sleep hygiene practices

✓ Encourage physical activity

✓ Support mindfulness practices

✓ Discuss environmental toxin reduction

✓ Guide meal planning and preparation

Assessments & Tools

✓ Use symptom questionnaires (MSQ)

✓ Track food, mood, and symptoms

✓ Assess lifestyle factors

✓ Use readiness-to-change scales

✓ Document progress over time

✓ Discuss lab results at client's request (educationally)

✓ Suggest clients discuss labs with their provider

What Health Coaches CANNOT Do

Medical Practice

✗ Diagnose any medical condition

✗ Treat or cure diseases

✗ Prescribe medications

✗ Order or interpret lab tests

✗ Perform physical examinations

✗ Advise on medication changes

✗ Provide medical advice

Nutrition Therapy

✗ Provide medical nutrition therapy

✗ Create therapeutic diets for medical conditions

✗ Prescribe specific supplements for conditions

✗ Recommend supplement dosages for treatment

✗ Calculate medical nutrition requirements

✗ Manage eating disorders (requires licensed professional)

Mental Health Treatment

✗ Diagnose mental health conditions

✗ Provide psychotherapy or counseling

✗ Treat depression, anxiety, or trauma

✗ Manage eating disorders

✗ Provide crisis intervention

✗ Replace licensed mental health support

Language to Avoid

✗ "You have..." (diagnosing)

✗ "Take this for your condition"

✗ "This will cure/treat..."

✗ "You should stop taking your medication"

✗ "Your labs show you have..."

✗ "You need to take X mg of..."

Navigating Gray Areas

Common Gray Area Situations

Situation Within Scope Outside Scope
Client asks about supplements Educate about what a supplement does generally, suggest they discuss with their provider Prescribe specific supplements or dosages to treat their condition
Client shares lab results Discuss what the markers generally indicate, suggest questions to ask their provider Interpret labs diagnostically, make treatment recommendations based on labs
Client has diabetes Support general healthy eating habits, encourage them to work with their medical team Create a diabetic meal plan, adjust their medication or insulin
Client seems depressed Acknowledge their feelings, encourage them to seek mental health support Provide therapy, diagnose depression, become their primary support for mental health
Client wants to stop medication Encourage them to discuss concerns with their prescribing provider Advise them to stop or change medication dosage

Appropriate Language

Say This

"Research suggests that..."

"Many people find that..."

"You might consider discussing with your doctor..."

"Some general information about..."

"This is something to explore with your provider..."

"Based on what you've shared, it might be helpful to..."

"Have you considered asking your doctor about..."

Avoid This

"You have [condition]..."

"You need to take..."

"This will cure your..."

"Stop taking your medication..."

"Your labs show that you have..."

"I can treat that with..."

"You don't need to see a doctor for that..."

Quick Decision Guide

Ask yourself these questions:

1. Am I diagnosing a condition or explaining what symptoms might indicate?

→ Explaining = OK | Diagnosing = REFER

2. Am I prescribing a treatment or educating about options?

→ Educating = OK | Prescribing = REFER

3. Does this require a licensed professional (medical, mental health, dietitian)?

→ No = OK | Yes = REFER

4. Am I comfortable explaining this to a licensing board?

→ Yes = OK | No = RECONSIDER

5. Would I be comfortable if this were recorded?

→ Yes = OK | No = RECONSIDER

When to Refer

Immediately refer or recommend medical evaluation for:

  • Any emergency symptoms (chest pain, difficulty breathing, severe pain)
  • Thoughts of self-harm or suicide
  • Suspected eating disorder
  • Symptoms that could indicate serious illness
  • Pregnancy-related concerns
  • Mental health crisis
  • Medication-related concerns or adverse reactions
  • Any situation beyond your training and competence

Document Everything

Keep detailed session notes including: what was discussed, education provided, referrals made, and client decisions. Good documentation protects you and demonstrates professionalism. If a situation feels uncomfortable or outside your scope, document your response and any referrals.

The Transtheoretical Model

In the 1980s, psychologists James Prochaska and Carlo DiClemente studied how people overcome addictionsâ€”both with professional help and on their own. They discovered something surprising:

Change isn't a single eventâ€”it's a process that unfolds in predictable stages.

Their Transtheoretical Model (also called the Stages of Change Model) explains why:

  • Some people aren't interested in hearing about solutions
  • Others are curious but not ready to commit
  • Still others are actively working on change but struggling with consistency

The model identifies six distinct stages. Each requires different coaching strategies. Trying to push someone into action before they're ready backfiresâ€”triggering resistance and undermining the relationship.

The Core Principle

Effective coaching matches the intervention to the client's current stage. Your job isn't to move people from wherever they are straight to "action"â€”it's to help them progress one stage at a time.

The Six Stages of Change

1Precontemplation

"I don't have a problem." The person doesn't see a need to change. They may be unaware, in denial, or demoralized by past failures. They're not thinking about change and may resist if pushed.

2Contemplation

"I might need to change... someday." The person is aware there's an issue and thinking about change, but hasn't committed. They're weighing pros and cons, often for months or years. Ambivalence is high.

3Preparation

"I'm going to change soon." The person has decided to take action within the next month. They're making plans, gathering resources, and testing small steps. Commitment is forming.

4Action

"I'm doing it!" The person is actively modifying behaviorâ€”eating differently, exercising, managing stress. This stage requires significant time, energy, and attention. Visible progress happens here.

5Maintenance

"I'm sustaining the change." The person has maintained new behaviors for at least 6 months. The focus shifts from starting behaviors to preventing relapse and integrating changes into daily life.

6Termination

"This is just who I am now." The new behavior is fully integrated. There's no temptation to return to old patterns. The change has become automaticâ€”part of identity rather than effort.

Important: Change Isn't Linear

People don't progress smoothly through stages. They cycle back, skip forward, and spend years in contemplation. Relapse is normalâ€”it's part of the process, not a failure. Most people cycle through stages multiple times before reaching maintenance.

Matching Interventions to Stage

Here's what worksâ€”and what doesn'tâ€”at each stage:

Precontemplation: Raise Awareness

What NOT to Do

Provide action plans, set goals, give advice. The client isn't ready and will perceive this as pushy. You'll trigger resistance.

What DOES Work

Build rapport, ask open questions, express empathy, provide information when asked, gently raise awareness of consequences without judgment.

  • "What concerns, if any, do you have about your current health?"
  • "How is your energy affecting your daily life?"
  • "What would need to be different for you to consider making changes?"

Contemplation: Resolve Ambivalence

What NOT to Do

Rush into action planning or try to convince them change is worth it. They're already ambivalentâ€”adding pressure increases "sustain talk."

What DOES Work

Use Motivational Interviewing, explore pros/cons of change, evoke change talk, connect change to deeply held values, normalize ambivalence.

  • "What would be different if you made this change?"
  • "What concerns you most about changing?"
  • "How does your current situation align with what matters most to you?"

Preparation: Support Planning

What NOT to Do

Overwhelm with information or create overly ambitious plans. Keep it simple and achievable.

What DOES Work

Help develop concrete action plans, identify barriers and solutions, set SMART goals, build confidence through small wins, connect to resources.

  • "What's one small step you could take this week?"
  • "What obstacles might get in your way, and how could you handle them?"
  • "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can do this?"

Action: Provide Support & Accountability

What NOT to Do

Assume they'll stay motivated on their own. Action is hardâ€”it requires sustained attention, problem-solving, and encouragement.

What DOES Work

Offer frequent check-ins, celebrate progress, troubleshoot barriers, adjust plans as needed, reinforce intrinsic motivation, normalize setbacks.

  • "What's working well? What's been harder than expected?"
  • "How did you handle that challenge?"
  • "What are you most proud of this week?"

Maintenance: Prevent Relapse

What NOT to Do

Assume the work is done and step back completely. Maintenance requires ongoing support, just less intensively.

What DOES Work

Help identify triggers for old behaviors, develop coping strategies, integrate changes into identity, gradually reduce session frequency, plan for high-risk situations.

  • "What situations might tempt you to slip back?"
  • "How has this change become part of who you are?"
  • "What systems can you put in place to sustain this long-term?"

Assessing Stage of Change

To identify a client's stage, listen for language cues. Precontemplation: "I don't need to..." Contemplation: "I should probably..." Preparation: "I'm planning to..." Action: "I'm currently..." Maintenance: "I've been doing this for..."

Behavior Change Science

Understanding why behavior change is so difficult helps you coach more effectively. Here are key insights from behavioral science:

1. The Intention-Action Gap

Research consistently shows that intentions don't predict behavior. Knowing what to do and even wanting to do it aren't enough.

What Doesn't Work

Providing information and assuming people will act on it.

What Works

Implementation intentions: specific if-then plans. "If it's 7am on Monday, then I'll go for a 20-minute walk."

2. Default Bias

Humans default to the path of least resistance. Behavior change requires making the desired behavior easier and the undesired behavior harder.

  • Environment design: Pre-chop vegetables, remove junk food, lay out workout clothes the night before
  • Reduce friction: Find a gym on the way home from work, prep smoothie ingredients in advance
  • Add friction to bad habits: Delete food delivery apps, put phone in another room at night

3. Temporal Discounting

We heavily discount future rewards in favor of immediate gratification. "I'll feel better in 6 months" loses to "This cookie tastes good right now."

Coaching Strategy

Help clients find immediate rewards for healthy behaviors: better sleep tonight, more energy tomorrow, pride right now. Connect to intrinsic motivation and values, which are experienced in the present.

4. Decision Fatigue

Every decision depletes mental resources. By evening, willpower is low. This is why people who eat well all day binge at night.

Coaching Strategy

Reduce decisions through routines, meal planning, and pre-commitment strategies. "I eat the same healthy breakfast every day" eliminates a decision point.

The Science of Habit Formation

Lasting change doesn't come from motivation or willpowerâ€”it comes from turning desired behaviors into automatic habits.

Building New Habits

1

Start Tiny

BJ Fogg's research shows tiny habits work better than ambitious ones. Instead of "exercise for 30 minutes," start with "do 2 pushups after brushing teeth." Build from there.

2

Stack Habits

Link new behaviors to existing routines: "After I pour my morning coffee, I'll take my supplements." The existing habit becomes the cue.

3

Make It Obvious

Visible cues trigger action. Put running shoes by the door, place a water bottle on your desk, set out vitamins the night before.

4

Make It Satisfying

Immediate rewards strengthen habits. Track progress visually, celebrate completion, pair the behavior with something enjoyable (listen to favorite podcast only while walking).

How Long Does It Take?

The "21 days to form a habit" myth is false. Research by Phillippa Lally found it takes an average of 66 days for a behavior to become automaticâ€”with a range of 18 to 254 days depending on complexity.

Client-Friendly Explanation

"Building a new habit is like creating a path through a forest. The first few times, you're pushing through brush and it's hard. But every time you walk that path, it gets a little clearer. Eventually, it becomes a well-worn trail you can walk without thinking."

Breaking Bad Habits

You can't delete a habitâ€”you can only replace it. The cue-routine-reward loop remains, but you substitute a new routine:

  • Identify the cue: When does the behavior happen? Stress? Boredom? Time of day?
  • Identify the real reward: What does the behavior actually provide? Distraction? Comfort? Energy?
  • Experiment with new routines: Test different behaviors that provide a similar reward
  • Plan for the cue: "When I feel stressed (cue), I'll take three deep breaths (new routine) to feel calm (reward)."

Beware the Abstinence Violation Effect

One slip-up leads to total relapse when people think: "I already failed, might as well give up." Help clients reframe lapses as data points, not disasters. "What triggered that? What can we learn? What will you do differently next time?"

Key Terms to Remember

Transtheoretical Model

Prochaska and DiClemente's framework showing change as a process through six stages: precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance, termination.

Stage-Matched Intervention

Tailoring coaching strategies to the client's current stage of change rather than using a one-size-fits-all approach.

Implementation Intention

A specific if-then plan that links a situational cue to a behavioral response, dramatically increasing follow-through.

Habit Loop

The three-part cycle of cue, routine, and reward that forms the neurological basis of habits.

Temporal Discounting

The tendency to devalue future rewards in favor of immediate gratification, making long-term behavior change challenging.

Habit Stacking

Linking a new desired behavior to an existing habit, using the established routine as a cue for the new one.

1

Why is it problematic to give action plans to someone in the precontemplation or contemplation stage?

People in precontemplation don't see a need to change, and those in contemplation are still ambivalent. Jumping to action plans before they're ready triggers resistance, damages rapport, and can push them backward. The intervention must match the stageâ€”raising awareness in precontemplation, resolving ambivalence in contemplation, and only creating action plans once they've moved to preparation.

2

Describe the habit loop and explain how to use it to build a new healthy habit.

The habit loop consists of: (1) Cue - a trigger, (2) Routine - the behavior, (3) Reward - the benefit. To build a new habit, create a clear cue (e.g., "after I pour coffee"), define a simple routine (e.g., "I take my supplements"), and ensure an immediate reward (e.g., sense of accomplishment, checkmark on tracker). Make the cue obvious, start with a tiny version of the behavior, and ensure the reward is satisfying.

3

What is temporal discounting, and what's one strategy coaches can use to overcome it?

Temporal discounting is the tendency to heavily discount future rewards in favor of immediate gratification (e.g., "ice cream now" beats "better health later"). Coaches can overcome this by helping clients identify immediate rewards for healthy behaviorsâ€”better sleep tonight, more energy tomorrow, pride and self-respect right now. Connecting behaviors to intrinsic values (being a present parent, living aligned with who they want to be) also helps because values are experienced in the present.

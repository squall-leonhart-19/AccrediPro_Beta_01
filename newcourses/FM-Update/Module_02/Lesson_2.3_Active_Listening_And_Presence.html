Welcome to Lesson 2.3

Active listening is the foundation of all coaching skills. Without the ability to truly hear your clients—not just their words, but their emotions, values, and unspoken concerns—other coaching techniques fall flat. This lesson develops your capacity for deep, present listening.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between hearing and truly listening
  • Practice three levels of listening in coaching conversations
  • Develop coaching presence—full attention without agenda
  • Apply specific techniques for reflective and empathic listening
  • Recognize and overcome common listening barriers

Beyond Hearing: What Active Listening Really Means

Most people listen to respond. They're formulating their next thought while the other person is still speaking. Active listening means listening to understand—setting aside your own agenda to be fully present with another person's experience.

In coaching, active listening serves multiple purposes:

  • Helps clients feel truly heard and validated
  • Builds the trust essential for deep work
  • Reveals information beyond what's explicitly stated
  • Allows clients to hear themselves think
  • Creates space for insights to emerge

The Gift of Attention

In our distracted world, giving someone your complete attention is rare. When you listen deeply, you're offering a gift that most people rarely receive. This alone can be transformative.

Three Levels of Listening

Coaching distinguishes between three levels of listening, each with increasing depth:

Level 1: Internal Listening

Focus is on yourself—your thoughts, judgments, opinions, and what you'll say next. This is how most people listen most of the time. Fine for casual conversation, but insufficient for coaching.

Level 2: Focused Listening

Complete attention on the other person. You're hearing their words, noticing their tone, watching their body language. Your focus is on understanding their experience.

Level 3: Global Listening

Awareness of everything—the client, yourself, the environment, the energy in the room. You're using all senses and intuition to gather information. You notice what's not being said.

Self-Check

In your last conversation, what level were you listening at? Were you thinking about what to say next (Level 1), or fully focused on understanding the other person (Level 2 or 3)?

Coaching Presence

Coaching presence is the state of being fully available to your client in the moment—aware, open, and connected without agenda. It means:

  • Being with vs. doing to: You're a companion on their journey, not an expert fixing them
  • Comfortable with silence: You allow pauses without rushing to fill them
  • Managing your reactions: Your judgments, emotions, and opinions don't hijack the session
  • Trust in the process: You believe the client has their own answers

Listening Techniques

Key techniques for active listening:

  • Paraphrasing: Restating what you heard in your own words to confirm understanding
  • Reflecting feelings: Naming the emotions you're perceiving ("It sounds like you're feeling frustrated...")
  • Clarifying: Asking questions to ensure you understand ("When you say 'overwhelmed,' what does that look like for you?")
  • Summarizing: Pulling together key themes from what you've heard
  • Silence: Allowing space for the client to think and feel
Client: "I know I should exercise more, but I just can't seem to find the time. Between work and the kids, there's nothing left for me."
Coach: "It sounds like you're feeling stretched between all your responsibilities, and taking time for yourself feels almost impossible right now."
Client: "Yes, exactly. And then I feel guilty if I even think about taking time away..."

Common Listening Barriers

Even with good intentions, several barriers can interfere with deep listening:

  • Fixing mode: Jumping to solutions before fully understanding
  • Assumptions: Thinking you know what they mean without checking
  • Distractions: Phone, environment, your own thoughts
  • Judgment: Evaluating what they're saying as right/wrong
  • Relating: Shifting focus to your own similar experience
  • Planning: Thinking about your next question or comment

The Advice Trap

One of the biggest barriers for new coaches is the urge to give advice. When you hear a problem, you want to help by offering solutions. But in coaching, the most helpful thing is often to keep listening—the client's own solutions are usually better than yours.

Practice Exercises

Try these exercises to develop your listening skills:

  • Silent observation: Spend 10 minutes in a public place just observing without judgment
  • Conversation without questions: Have a conversation using only reflections, no questions
  • Delayed response: Practice waiting 3 seconds before responding in conversation
  • Body awareness: Notice your physical sensations while listening to someone

Key Terms

Active Listening

Fully concentrating on what is being said rather than passively hearing, with intent to understand.

Coaching Presence

The state of being fully present with the client—aware, open, and flexible.

Reflective Listening

Technique of mirroring back content and feelings to confirm understanding.

Level 3 Listening

Global awareness using all senses, intuition, and attention to what's unsaid.

Check Your Understanding

1

What's the difference between Level 1 and Level 2 listening?

Level 1 listening is internal—focus is on your own thoughts, judgments, and what you'll say next. Level 2 is focused listening—complete attention on the other person, hearing their words, noticing tone and body language, seeking to understand their experience rather than formulating your response.

2

Why is the "advice trap" problematic in coaching?

Giving advice shifts you into expert mode and takes away the client's agency and self-discovery. Clients' own solutions are often better because they know their own context. Advice can also prevent deeper exploration of what's really going on—jumping to solutions before fully understanding the problem.

Key Takeaways

  • Active listening means listening to understand, not to respond
  • Three levels: internal (about you), focused (on them), global (everything)
  • Coaching presence is being fully available without agenda
  • Key techniques: paraphrasing, reflecting, clarifying, summarizing, silence
  • Common barriers include fixing, assumptions, and planning your response

Understanding Hormone Therapy Options

Navigating HRT with Evidence-Based Knowledge

⏱ Reading Time: 12 minutes

A Coach's Role: While health coaches don't prescribe or manage hormone therapy, many clients will ask about it or already be using it. Understanding hormone therapy options enables you to have informed conversations, support clients in their decisions, and collaborate effectively with their healthcare providers. This lesson provides essential knowledge without overstepping your scope.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the types of menopausal hormone therapy available
  • Review current evidence on benefits and risks
  • Recognize who may be a good candidate for hormone therapy
  • Support clients who are considering or using HRT
  • Know when lifestyle and supplements are enough—and when they're not

Important Scope of Practice Reminder

As a health coach, you do not prescribe, recommend, or manage hormone therapy. Your role is to: (1) provide education so clients can have informed conversations with their healthcare providers, (2) support lifestyle factors that complement whatever treatment path clients choose, and (3) refer appropriately when symptoms require medical evaluation. This lesson is for your knowledge and to help you support—not replace—medical care.

The Evolution of Hormone Therapy

The history of menopausal hormone therapy (MHT, also called HRT) has been a pendulum swing of enthusiasm and fear. Understanding this history helps explain why many women and providers remain confused.

A Brief History

  • 1960s-1990s: HRT widely prescribed, believed to prevent heart disease, maintain youth, and more. Many women took estrogen alone, even if they had a uterus.
  • 2002: Women's Health Initiative (WHI) study halted early due to increased breast cancer and cardiovascular events in some groups. Media coverage created widespread fear.
  • 2002-2015: HRT use plummeted. Many symptomatic women suffered without treatment. Nuanced analysis of WHI largely ignored.
  • 2015-Present: Re-analysis of WHI and new research shows benefits often outweigh risks for healthy women under 60 or within 10 years of menopause. Hormone therapy is experiencing careful rehabilitation.

Types of Hormone Therapy

Systemic Estrogen Therapy

Systemic estrogen circulates throughout the body and addresses widespread symptoms like hot flashes, night sweats, and bone loss.

Type Description Delivery
Conjugated Equine Estrogens (CEE) Derived from pregnant mare urine; contains multiple estrogens. Used in original WHI study. Oral (Premarin)
17-beta Estradiol Bioidentical—molecularly identical to human estradiol. Often preferred by functional medicine practitioners. Patch, gel, spray, oral
Estetrol (E4) Newer bioidentical estrogen with potentially favorable safety profile. Currently in research. Oral

Route Matters: Transdermal vs. Oral

Transdermal (Patch, Gel, Spray)

  • Bypasses liver (first-pass metabolism)
  • No increase in blood clot risk
  • No impact on triglycerides
  • Steady hormone levels
  • Generally preferred for safety profile

Oral

  • Processed by liver
  • Small increased blood clot risk
  • May raise triglycerides
  • May increase inflammatory markers
  • Convenient, but higher risk profile

Progestogen Therapy

Women with a uterus must take a progestogen alongside estrogen to protect against endometrial cancer. Estrogen alone causes the uterine lining to build up; progestogen provides the balancing signal to shed it.

Types of Progestogens

  • Synthetic Progestins: Medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA) was used in WHI; associated with some negative outcomes. Other synthetics (norethindrone, levonorgestrel) have varying profiles.
  • Micronized Progesterone: Bioidentical, molecularly identical to human progesterone. Brand name Prometrium. Often preferred for mood, sleep, and potentially safer breast profile.
  • Progestogen IUD: Levonorgestrel-releasing IUD (Mirena) can provide endometrial protection locally, allowing estrogen-only systemic therapy.

Local Vaginal Estrogen

For genitourinary symptoms only, low-dose vaginal estrogen is highly effective and has minimal systemic absorption. It's considered safe for most women, including many with history of breast cancer (with oncologist approval).

Vaginal Estrogen Options

  • Vaginal estrogen cream: Applied 2-3x/week
  • Vaginal estrogen ring: Replaced every 3 months
  • Vaginal estrogen tablet: Inserted 2x/week
  • Vaginal DHEA: Prasterone (Intrarosa) converts to local hormones

The "Bioidentical" Question

Bioidentical hormones are molecularly identical to human hormones. FDA-approved bioidentical options include estradiol patches/gels and micronized progesterone. Compounded bioidentical hormones are custom-made by compounding pharmacies.

FDA-Approved Bioidenticals

  • Tested for safety and efficacy
  • Consistent dosing
  • Insurance often covers
  • Examples: Estradiol patches, Prometrium

Compounded Bioidenticals

  • Customized dosing
  • May combine multiple hormones
  • Less regulatory oversight
  • Quality varies by pharmacy

Important Note: The term "bioidentical" is not synonymous with "safer." Both FDA-approved and compounded bioidenticals carry the same hormones with similar effects. The advantage of FDA-approved products is quality control and testing. Compounded products may be appropriate when specific formulations aren't commercially available but should be obtained from reputable pharmacies.

Benefits of Hormone Therapy

When initiated in appropriate candidates at appropriate timing, hormone therapy offers significant benefits:

Benefit Evidence Level
Hot flashes/night sweats Strong—most effective treatment available; 75-80% reduction
Genitourinary symptoms Strong—local or systemic estrogen highly effective
Bone protection Strong—prevents bone loss and reduces fracture risk
Cardiovascular (if started early) Moderate—may reduce heart disease risk when started within 10 years of menopause
Mood and quality of life Moderate—many women report improved mood, energy, and wellbeing
Sleep Moderate—improvement related to reduced night sweats and possible direct effects

Risks and Considerations

Hormone therapy carries risks that must be weighed against benefits on an individual basis:

Breast Cancer

Combined estrogen + synthetic progestin (MPA) showed a small increased risk after 5+ years. Estrogen alone (in women without a uterus) did NOT increase risk—may even decrease it. Micronized progesterone appears safer than synthetic progestins.

Blood Clots

Oral estrogen increases risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism. Transdermal estrogen does NOT increase clot risk—important consideration for women with clotting risk factors.

Stroke

Small increased risk with oral estrogen, especially in older women. Lower or absent risk with transdermal estrogen and when started younger.

Gallbladder Disease

Oral estrogen increases gallbladder disease risk. Transdermal route avoids this.

The Timing Hypothesis

The "timing hypothesis" or "window of opportunity" is crucial for understanding hormone therapy safety and benefit:

The Window of Opportunity

Optimal timing: Starting HRT within 10 years of menopause OR before age 60

Why it matters: Blood vessels and heart benefit from estrogen when healthy. If arteries are already atherosclerotic, estrogen may destabilize plaques.

Bottom line: For healthy women who start early, benefits typically outweigh risks. For older women or those with existing cardiovascular disease, the calculus changes.

Who May Be a Good Candidate

Generally Good Candidates for Systemic HRT

  • Moderate to severe vasomotor symptoms affecting quality of life
  • Under 60 or within 10 years of menopause
  • No contraindications (see below)
  • Premature or early menopause (before 45)—HRT often recommended until typical menopause age
  • Elevated bone fracture risk with insufficient response to other therapies

Contraindications to Systemic HRT

  • History of breast cancer (usually—individual assessment needed)
  • History of blood clots (DVT/PE) or clotting disorders
  • History of stroke or heart attack
  • Active liver disease
  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding (must be evaluated first)
  • History of endometrial cancer (usually)

The Coach's Role

As a health coach, you play an important supportive role for clients navigating hormone therapy decisions:

Education & Preparation

  • Help clients understand options before appointments
  • Encourage listing questions for their provider
  • Provide balanced, evidence-based information

Lifestyle Optimization

  • Support lifestyle factors regardless of HRT choice
  • Lifestyle amplifies HRT benefits
  • May reduce HRT doses needed

Monitoring & Support

  • Help track symptom improvements
  • Encourage follow-up appointments
  • Note side effects for provider communication

Referral

  • Know when to refer (severe symptoms, no response to lifestyle)
  • Help find menopause-trained providers
  • Support seeking second opinions

When Lifestyle Isn't Enough

While lifestyle intervention is always foundational, some situations warrant more aggressive treatment:

Consider Referral for HRT Discussion When:

  • Severe hot flashes/night sweats significantly affecting quality of life despite lifestyle changes
  • Sleep deprivation causing functional impairment
  • Significant mood symptoms not responding to lifestyle
  • Genitourinary symptoms affecting quality of life
  • Early or premature menopause (before age 45)
  • Significant osteoporosis risk or established osteoporosis

Key Takeaways

  • Hormone therapy options include various estrogens and progestogens via different routes
  • Transdermal estrogen and micronized progesterone generally have favorable safety profiles
  • Timing matters—benefits are greatest when started within 10 years of menopause or before age 60
  • Local vaginal estrogen is safe for most women and highly effective for genitourinary symptoms
  • Your role is education, lifestyle support, and appropriate referral—not prescribing or managing HRT
  • Lifestyle optimization amplifies HRT benefits and may reduce doses needed

Case Study 1: Treatment-Resistant Depression

Client Profile: Margaret, 48

Diagnosis: Major depressive disorder, partially responsive to medication

Current treatment: Duloxetine 60mg, monthly therapy sessions

Chief concerns: Persistent fatigue despite medication, brain fog, weight gain (25 lbs), no motivation, "going through the motions"

Self-rated mood: 4/10 most days

Coaching Discovery

Margaret's coach conducted a thorough lifestyle and health history review, uncovering:

  • Nutrition: Skipping breakfast, grabbing fast food for lunch, wine and snacking in evenings. Low protein, no fish, minimal vegetables
  • Sleep: Going to bed at midnight, waking at 6am, screen use until sleep. Wakes unrefreshed.
  • Movement: Completely sedentary since depression worsened
  • Social: Isolated; stopped seeing friends; works from home alone
  • Medical factors: Labs from 6 months ago showed: Vitamin D 19 ng/mL (low), B12 280 pg/mL (low-normal), ferritin 22 ng/mL (suboptimal), TSH 4.2 (high-normal), HbA1c 5.8% (prediabetes range)
  • Gut health: Chronic bloating, constipation, diagnosed with IBS years ago but never addressed

Coaching Approach

Phase 1: Build Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

  • Encouraged Margaret to share the intake findings with her PCP and psychiatrist; advocated for updated labs and thyroid evaluation
  • Started with one change: protein-rich breakfast daily (eggs, Greek yogurt)
  • Added 10-minute morning walk (combined light exposure with movement)
  • Set consistent bedtime of 10:30pm with screens off at 9:30pm
  • One weekly phone call to a supportive friend

Phase 2: Address Root Causes (Weeks 5-12)

  • PCP optimized vitamin D (5000 IU daily) and added B12 supplementation
  • Thyroid evaluated more closely; started low-dose T4/T3 combination per endocrinologist
  • Transitioned toward anti-inflammatory eating: reduced processed foods and sugar, added fatty fish 2x weekly, increased vegetables
  • Added omega-3 supplementation (2g EPA/DHA) after discussion with prescriber
  • Addressed gut issues: eliminated gluten trial (bloating improved 70%), added probiotic
  • Walking increased to 30 minutes daily; joined gentle yoga class once weekly

Phase 3: Optimization (Months 4-6)

  • Continued refining eating pattern; blood sugar stabilized, energy improved
  • Started morning light therapy box (10,000 lux, 20 minutes)
  • Daily 10-minute meditation practice established
  • Re-engaged with book club and began volunteering at animal shelter
  • Sleep improved to 7+ hours; waking refreshed

Outcomes at 6 Months

  • Mood improved from 4/10 to 7-8/10
  • Brain fog largely resolved; concentration returned
  • Lost 15 pounds without restrictive dieting
  • Energy significantly improved; exercising regularly
  • Vitamin D, B12, ferritin all in optimal range on retest
  • Psychiatrist reduced antidepressant dose; considering further reduction
  • Bloating and constipation resolved
  • Re-engaged socially; sense of purpose returning

Coaching Insight: Margaret's "treatment-resistant" depression had significant physiological contributorsâ€”nutrient deficiencies, subclinical thyroid dysfunction, gut issues, blood sugar dysregulation, and inflammation from diet. By addressing these factors while she continued psychiatric care, Margaret achieved remission that medication alone couldn't provide. The coach's role was supporting lifestyle changes and encouraging appropriate medical investigationâ€”not replacing her mental health treatment.

Case Study 2: Anxiety and Panic Attacks

Client Profile: Sarah, 35

Diagnosis: Generalized anxiety disorder with panic attacks

Current treatment: As-needed lorazepam, started therapy but only 2 sessions in

Chief concerns: Constant worry, physical tension, 2-3 panic attacks weekly, fear of having panic attacks, avoiding situations

Life context: High-stress job, perfectionist, new mother (baby is 6 months old)

Coaching Discovery

  • Blood sugar: Eating pattern revealed: coffee only until 2pm, then eating whatever was convenient, often high-carb. Blood sugar crashes likely triggering/worsening panic symptoms
  • Caffeine: 4-5 cups of coffee daily to manage exhaustion
  • Sleep: Baby still waking 2-3 times; averaging 4-5 hours fragmented sleep
  • Postpartum context: Symptoms worsened significantly after baby; possible hormonal contribution
  • Nervous system state: Constant sympathetic activation; no regular relaxation practices
  • Support: Husband travels for work; limited local family support

Coaching Approach

Phase 1: Stabilization (Weeks 1-4)

  • Priority #1: Blood sugar stabilization. Started with protein-rich breakfast within 1 hour of waking; regular meals/snacks every 3-4 hours
  • Gradual caffeine reduction: from 5 cups to 2 cups (morning only) over 2 weeks
  • Introduced 5-minute breathing exercises 3x daily (attached to existing habits: morning, baby's nap, evening)
  • Encouraged discussion with OB about postpartum hormones and screening
  • Explored sleep support: husband taking one night feeding; sleep when baby sleeps when possible

Phase 2: Nervous System Regulation (Weeks 5-10)

  • Panic attacks reduced to 1x weekly with blood sugar stability and reduced caffeine
  • Added magnesium glycinate in evening (400mg) with provider approval
  • Built daily walk with baby (combining movement, nature, light exposure)
  • Started 10-minute guided meditation during baby's afternoon nap
  • Connected Sarah with local new mothers' group for social support
  • Continued therapyâ€”coach reinforced therapist's anxiety management techniques

Phase 3: Building Resilience (Weeks 11-16)

  • Established sustainable eating pattern; no longer experiencing blood sugar crashes
  • Daily mindfulness practice became non-negotiable self-care
  • Baby sleeping better; Sarah averaging 6-7 hours
  • OB added progesterone support which helped evening anxiety
  • Developed "anxiety toolkit" of strategies for high-stress moments
  • Gradually facing avoided situations with therapist support

Outcomes at 4 Months

  • Panic attacks eliminated for past 6 weeks
  • Baseline anxiety reduced from 8/10 to 3-4/10
  • No longer using lorazepam
  • Physical tension significantly reduced
  • No longer avoiding situations; driving freely again
  • Energy improved; enjoying motherhood more
  • Feels equipped with tools to manage anxiety long-term

Coaching Insight: Sarah's panic attacks had strong physiological triggersâ€”blood sugar instability and excessive caffeine. Addressing these quickly reduced panic frequency. The coach worked in tandem with her therapist (who addressed the psychological components) and OB (who addressed the hormonal component). The combination of approaches was more effective than any single intervention alone. The coach's focus was on lifestyle factors while supporting the work of other providers.

Case Study 3: Cognitive Decline and Brain Fog

Client Profile: Barbara, 57

Concerns: Memory problems, difficulty concentrating, "brain fog," fear of early dementia

Medical evaluation: Neurologist ruled out dementia; told "normal aging"

Life context: Recently retired teacher, postmenopausal (5 years), previously active but now sedentary

Mood: Describes feeling "flat" and anxious about cognitive changes

Coaching Discovery

  • Menopause: No hormone replacement; had hot flashes that disrupted sleep, now resolved but poor sleep persists
  • Sleep: Taking 1-2 hours to fall asleep, waking at 4am, averaging 5-6 hours fragmented sleep
  • Diet: Low-fat for 20 years (since heart disease fear); minimal eggs, no fatty fish, avoids "cholesterol"
  • Movement: Stopped exercising when she retired; no structure to her days
  • Social: Lost work identity and social connection with retirement; isolated at home
  • Stimulation: Spending hours watching TV; not challenging her brain
  • Labs: Vitamin D 23 ng/mL, B12 312 pg/mL, HbA1c 5.9% (prediabetes range)

Coaching Approach

Phase 1: Brain-Essential Nutrition (Weeks 1-6)

  • Education on brain's need for healthy fats; dispelled low-fat mythology
  • Added eggs (3-4 weekly with yolks), olive oil, avocados, walnuts
  • Introduced fatty fish 2-3 times weekly for omega-3s
  • Added omega-3 supplement (2g EPA/DHA) per integrative doctor
  • Optimized vitamin D (5000 IU daily) and B12 (methylcobalamin)
  • Reduced refined carbohydrates; added protein at each meal for blood sugar stability

Phase 2: Sleep and Movement (Weeks 7-12)

  • Sleep assessment revealed poor sleep hygiene and anxiety about insomnia
  • Implemented sleep protocol: consistent wake time, morning light, no screens after 8pm, cool dark room
  • Added magnesium and L-theanine for sleep support
  • Started daily walking (20 minutes); progressed to 30-45 minutes
  • Joined water aerobics class twice weekly
  • Discussed hormone therapy with her gynecologist; started estrogen which helped sleep and cognition

Phase 3: Brain Stimulation and Purpose (Months 4-6)

  • Explored purpose post-retirement; Barbara missed teaching
  • Started tutoring at local library twice weekly
  • Joined book club for social connection and cognitive engagement
  • Learned a new skill (watercolor painting) for neuroplasticity
  • Limited TV; replaced with puzzles, reading, creative activities

Outcomes at 6 Months

  • Brain fog resolved; concentration significantly improved
  • Memory concerns diminishedâ€”no longer worried about dementia
  • Sleeping 7-8 hours most nights
  • Mood improved from "flat" to engaged and optimistic
  • Exercising 5 days weekly
  • Vitamin D and B12 levels optimized
  • HbA1c improved to 5.5%
  • Active social life with tutoring, book club, water aerobics
  • "I feel sharper now than I did 5 years ago"

Coaching Insight: Barbara's cognitive decline wasn't dementiaâ€”it was the result of multiple modifiable factors: inadequate brain nutrition (especially omega-3s and choline from her low-fat diet), sleep deprivation, sedentary lifestyle, social isolation, and lack of mental stimulation post-retirement. By systematically addressing each factor, her cognition fully recovered. This case illustrates how "brain fog" in midlife often has reversible causes when the root causes are identified and addressed.

Common Themes Across Mental Health Coaching

Patterns That Emerge

  • Blood sugar matters everywhere: Unstable blood sugar contributes to anxiety, depression, brain fog, and irritability
  • Sleep is foundational: Poor sleep underlies or worsens almost every mental health concern
  • Nutrient deficiencies are common: B12, vitamin D, iron, magnesium, omega-3s are frequently low in mental health clients
  • Movement is medicine: Even modest increases in physical activity improve mood and cognition
  • Isolation is harmful: Rebuilding social connection is often a key intervention
  • Hormones matter: Thyroid, adrenal, and sex hormones significantly affect brain function
  • The gut-brain axis is real: Digestive symptoms often accompany and contribute to mental health symptoms
  • Team approach works best: Collaboration with mental health providers, physicians, and other practitioners optimizes outcomes

Scope of Practice Reminders

What Coaches Do and Don't Do

Within Scope:

  • Support lifestyle factors (nutrition, sleep, movement, stress management)
  • Help clients implement recommendations from providers
  • Educate about brain-body connections
  • Encourage appropriate professional support
  • Celebrate progress and build self-efficacy
  • Facilitate behavior change with evidence-based coaching techniques

Outside Scope:

  • Diagnosing mental health conditions
  • Providing psychotherapy or counseling
  • Recommending or adjusting medications
  • Managing active crises (refer immediately)
  • Treating eating disorders, addiction, or severe mental illness without appropriate professional oversight

Key Takeaways

  • Mental health conditions often have significant physiological contributors that respond to lifestyle intervention
  • Blood sugar stabilization, sleep optimization, and nutrient repletion are foundational interventions
  • Coaches work alongside mental health providers, not instead of them
  • Addressing multiple factors simultaneously often produces synergistic results
  • Meaningful improvement typically takes 3-6 months of consistent intervention
  • Each client's root causes are uniqueâ€”individualized assessment and intervention are essential
  • Lifestyle medicine for mental health is evidence-based and can be transformative

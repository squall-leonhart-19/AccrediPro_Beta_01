Core Ethical Principles

Professional ethics in health coaching are built on foundational principles that guide decision-making:

1. Beneficence (Do Good)

Act in ways that benefit your clients. Your primary motivation should be helping them achieve their health goals, not personal gain, convenience, or ego.

2. Non-Maleficence (Do No Harm)

Avoid actions that could harm clients. This includes practicing within scope, referring when appropriate, and not giving advice beyond your competence.

3. Autonomy (Respect Self-Determination)

Honor clients' right to make their own decisions. Inform, educate, and guideâ€”but don't pressure, manipulate, or impose your values.

4. Justice (Fairness)

Treat all clients fairly and equitably. Don't discriminate based on race, gender, religion, or other characteristics. Provide consistent quality of service.

5. Fidelity (Faithfulness)

Keep your promises and commitments. Be reliable, consistent, and trustworthy. Honor the coaching relationship and its agreements.

6. Veracity (Truthfulness)

Be honest in all communications. Don't misrepresent your qualifications, make false promises, or withhold important information.

Professional Boundaries

Professional boundaries are the limits that define the appropriate relationship between coach and client. They protect both parties and create the safety needed for effective coaching.

ðŸ”’ Types of Professional Boundaries

Role Boundaries

You are the coach; they are the client. You're not their friend, therapist, family member, or romantic partner. The relationship serves a specific professional purpose.

Time Boundaries

Sessions have defined start/end times. Communication between sessions has limits. You're not available 24/7. Respect for your time is part of the professional relationship.

Physical Boundaries

Appropriate physical space and contact. No romantic or sexual involvement with clients. Professional meeting locations. Considerations for virtual vs. in-person sessions.

Emotional Boundaries

Care about clients without becoming emotionally enmeshed. Don't take on their problems as your own. Maintain your own emotional wellbeing. Seek supervision when needed.

Financial Boundaries

Clear fee structures. No loans to clients. No accepting excessive gifts. No financial entanglements or business partnerships with clients.

Self-Disclosure Boundaries

Share personal information only when it serves the client's goals. The session is about them, not you. Avoid burdening clients with your problems.

Common Boundary Challenges

Recognize these common situations that test boundaries:

The Friendship Blur

Situation: A client wants to connect on social media, grab coffee "as friends," or extend the relationship beyond coaching.

Guidance: Maintain professional distance. It's okay to be warm and caring while keeping role clarity. After coaching ends, a waiting period (6-12 months) before any friendship is advisable.

The Gift Question

Situation: A client wants to give you an expensive gift or tip beyond your fee.

Guidance: Graciously decline substantial gifts. Small tokens of appreciation are usually acceptable, but excessive gifting changes the dynamic. Their progress is the best gift.

The Dual Relationship

Situation: Someone you know (neighbor, family friend, colleague) wants to become a client.

Guidance: Consider carefully. Dual relationships can compromise objectivity and complicate both relationships. Often best to refer to another coach.

The Romantic Attraction

Situation: You or your client develops romantic feelings.

Guidance: Romantic/sexual relationships with current clients are never appropriate. Transfer the client to another coach. If feelings persist after coaching ends, most guidelines recommend waiting at least 2 years.

The Business Opportunity

Situation: A client invites you into their business, wants you to promote their products, or proposes a financial partnership.

Guidance: Decline. Business relationships with clients create conflicts of interest and blur professional boundaries.

Ethical Decision-Making Framework

When facing ethical dilemmas, use this framework:

ðŸ¤” ETHICS Decision Framework

E
Examine the Situation

What exactly is happening? What are the facts? Who is involved? What are the potential harms and benefits?

T
Think About Principles

Which ethical principles apply? Beneficence? Non-maleficence? Autonomy? What do professional codes of ethics say?

H
Hypothesize Options

What are the possible courses of action? What are the consequences of each? Who would be affected and how?

I
Investigate Guidance

Consult your code of ethics, professional guidelines, colleagues, supervisors, or legal counsel if needed.

C
Choose and Act

Select the most ethical option. Take action. Document your reasoning.

S
Self-Reflect

After the fact, evaluate. What did you learn? What would you do differently? How can you prevent similar situations?

When to Seek Support

You don't have to navigate ethics alone. Seek consultation when:

  • You're unsure whether something is within scope or ethical
  • A client situation is triggering strong emotional reactions in you
  • You're facing a novel situation not covered by guidelines
  • You feel personally invested beyond professional concern
  • A client's situation is beyond your training or experience
  • You need to process a difficult interaction or decision

Resources: Peer supervision groups, mentors, professional organizations, ethics committees, supervisors (if employed), or professional consultants.

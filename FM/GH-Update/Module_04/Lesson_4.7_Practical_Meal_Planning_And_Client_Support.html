The Reality of Dietary Change

Knowing what to eat is only half the battle. Compliance is the limiting factor in most dietary interventions. Your role is to make therapeutic eating practical, sustainable, and as enjoyable as possible.

The Meal Template Approach

Simple Formula

Every meal = Quality protein + Non-starchy vegetables + Healthy fat + (Optional starch based on protocol). This formula works across all therapeutic diets‚Äîjust adjust which specific foods to include or exclude.

Example Template Meals

  • Breakfast: Eggs (protein) + saut√©ed spinach (veg) + avocado (fat) + sweet potato (optional starch)
  • Lunch: Grilled chicken (protein) + large salad with cucumber/carrots (veg) + olive oil dressing (fat)
  • Dinner: Salmon (protein) + roasted zucchini and broccoli (veg) + ghee for cooking (fat)

Batch Cooking Strategies

Protein Prep

Cook 3-4 proteins on Sunday: roast chicken, beef patties, fish, hard-boiled eggs. Divide into containers. Now every meal just needs vegetables and assembly.

Vegetable Prep

Wash and chop salad vegetables. Roast a sheet pan of mixed vegetables (zucchini, carrots, broccoli). Make sauerkraut or pickled vegetables if ferments are tolerated.

Sauce and Dressing Prep

Make 2-3 compliant dressings/sauces. Different sauces on the same proteins/veggies = variety. Examples: lemon-herb dressing, tahini sauce, chimichurri (if tolerated).

Addressing Compliance Barriers

Barrier: "I don't have time to cook"

  • Batch cooking reduces daily cook time to assembly only
  • Sheet pan meals: everything on one pan, in oven for 30 min
  • Simpler meals are fine‚Äîgrilled protein + steamed vegetables is a complete meal

Barrier: "It's too expensive"

  • Focus on affordable proteins: eggs, chicken thighs, ground beef, canned fish
  • In-season vegetables are cheaper
  • Frozen vegetables are nutrient-rich and affordable
  • Bone broth from scraps is virtually free

Barrier: "My family won't eat this way"

  • Build "add-on" family meals: base protein/veg that everyone eats, add-ons (bread, cheese, pasta) for family
  • Many therapeutic meals are just "real food" the family might enjoy anyway
  • Batch cook separately and freeze portions

Food Journaling

What to Track

Foods and portions, time of eating, symptoms and timing, bowel movements (Bristol scale), stress and sleep notes. Digital apps or simple paper journals work‚Äîclient preference.

Review Sessions

Block time in follow-up sessions to review food journals together. Pattern recognition is a skill‚Äîhelp clients see connections they might miss. "Notice how symptoms flare every afternoon? What are you eating mid-morning?"

Social Situations

Dining Out Strategies

  • Research menus online beforehand
  • Simple orders: grilled protein, steamed vegetables, olive oil
  • "Can I get that with no sauce?" and "Is there gluten/dairy in that?"
  • Eat a small meal before to reduce pressure to find perfect options
  • Focus on social connection, not food anxiety

Family Gatherings

  • Bring a compliant dish to share
  • Eat before if options will be limited
  • Focus on protein options that are likely safe
  • Brief explanation if asked; don't over-explain

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. What is the simple meal template formula?

Quality protein + Non-starchy vegetables + Healthy fat + Optional starch (based on protocol). This formula works across all therapeutic diets‚Äîjust adjust which specific foods are included or excluded. It simplifies meal planning and ensures nutritional adequacy.

2. How can batch cooking improve compliance?

Batch cooking addresses the "no time" barrier by front-loading preparation. Cook multiple proteins and vegetables on one day (often Sunday), then weekday meals become simple assembly‚Äîtaking minutes instead of hours. It removes daily decision fatigue and prevents grabbing non-compliant convenience foods.

3. What should clients track in a food journal?

Foods and portions, time of eating, symptoms and their timing (important for delayed reactions), bowel movements using Bristol scale, and notes on stress/sleep. Review journals in sessions to help identify patterns clients might miss‚Äîlike symptoms consistently appearing 2 hours after certain meals.

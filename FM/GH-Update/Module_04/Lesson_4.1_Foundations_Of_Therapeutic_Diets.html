üî¨ Key Terms

Therapeutic Diet

A diet specifically designed to address a health condition, distinct from general healthy eating.

Elimination Diet

Removal of suspected trigger foods followed by systematic reintroduction to identify reactions.

Restrictive Phase

The initial elimination period designed to reduce symptoms; always temporary.

Food Reintroduction

Systematic process of adding foods back to identify individual tolerances.

Diet as Medicine

For gut health conditions, diet is often the most powerful intervention available. Food directly contacts the gut lining multiple times daily, feeding the microbiome, triggering immune responses, and influencing inflammation.

The Therapeutic Diet Framework

Therapeutic diets differ from general "healthy eating" advice. They're targeted interventions designed to address specific dysfunction‚Äîreducing bacterial fermentation in SIBO, eliminating immune triggers, or starving pathogenic organisms. They're meant to be temporary tools, not permanent restrictions.

Major Therapeutic Diet Categories

Elimination Diets

Remove common triggers (gluten, dairy, eggs, soy, corn, etc.) to reduce immune/inflammatory burden and identify individual sensitivities. Used for: suspected food sensitivities, autoimmune conditions, inflammation.

Low-FODMAP Diet

Reduces fermentable carbohydrates that feed bacteria and cause gas/bloating. Used for: IBS, SIBO, excessive gas and bloating. Not meant to be long-term.

Specific Carbohydrate Diet (SCD)

Eliminates complex carbohydrates that feed pathogenic bacteria. Used for: IBD, SIBO, persistent dysbiosis. More restrictive but powerful for complex cases.

Anti-Candida Diet

Reduces sugars and refined carbs that feed yeast. Used for: Candida overgrowth. Combines well with antifungal protocols.

Gut Healing Diets (e.g., GAPS)

Focus on easily digestible foods, bone broth, and nutrient density to heal the gut lining. Used for: leaky gut, severe dysbiosis, post-infection recovery.

Principles of Diet Selection

  • Match the condition: SIBO ‚Üí Low-FODMAP; Candida ‚Üí Low-sugar; Food sensitivities ‚Üí Elimination
  • Consider severity: Mild cases may need only modest changes; severe cases may need stricter intervention initially
  • Assess readiness: More restrictive diets require client commitment and support
  • Plan for reintroduction: Every elimination must have a reintroduction plan
  • Avoid over-restriction: The most restrictive diet isn't always the best; balance effectiveness with sustainability

Critical Warning

Therapeutic diets are TEMPORARY interventions. The goal is always to expand the diet as much as possible while maintaining symptom control. Permanent unnecessary restriction can lead to nutritional deficiencies, disordered eating, reduced microbiome diversity, and social isolation.

Common Pitfalls

  • Stacking multiple restrictions: Combining low-FODMAP + gluten-free + dairy-free + low-histamine creates impossible diets
  • Never reintroducing: Clients stay on elimination diets indefinitely without testing tolerance
  • One-size-fits-all: Prescribing the same diet to all gut clients regardless of condition
  • Ignoring nutrient adequacy: Restrictive diets can create deficiencies if not properly planned

Clinical Approach

Start with the least restrictive intervention likely to work. If a client might respond to just removing gluten and dairy, try that before jumping to full SCD. You can always increase restriction if needed‚Äîit's harder to liberalize a diet once a client is fearful of foods.

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. How do therapeutic diets differ from general healthy eating advice?

Therapeutic diets are targeted interventions designed to address specific dysfunction‚Äîlike reducing bacterial fermentation or eliminating immune triggers. Unlike general healthy eating, they're meant to be temporary tools, not permanent restrictions. They require individualization based on the condition being addressed.

2. Why is permanent unnecessary dietary restriction problematic?

Permanent restriction can lead to: nutritional deficiencies, disordered eating patterns, reduced microbiome diversity (fewer food types = less bacterial diversity), social isolation (difficulty eating out/with others), and unnecessary food fears. The goal is always to expand the diet while maintaining symptom control.

3. Which therapeutic diet is most appropriate for SIBO?

Low-FODMAP is the primary choice for SIBO. It reduces fermentable carbohydrates that feed the overgrown bacteria, decreasing gas and bloating. For severe or resistant cases, SCD (Specific Carbohydrate Diet) may be considered as it's more restrictive. Both should be temporary with planned reintroduction.

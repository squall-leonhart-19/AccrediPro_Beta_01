SCD (Specific Carbohydrate Diet)

Eliminates complex carbohydrates to starve pathogenic bacteria. Allows only monosaccharides.

GAPS (Gut and Psychology Syndrome)

Based on SCD with added emphasis on bone broth, fermented foods, and detoxification.

Legal/Illegal Foods

SCD terminology for allowed (legal) vs. prohibited (illegal) foods based on carbohydrate structure.

Introduction Diet

Initial very restrictive phase of GAPS designed for severe gut damage.

The Specific Carbohydrate Diet

SCD was developed by Dr. Sidney Haas in the 1950s and popularized by Elaine Gottschall. The principle: complex carbohydrates feed pathogenic bacteria; monosaccharides do not.

The SCD Theory

Disaccharides and polysaccharides (complex carbs) require digestion. When digestion is impaired, these carbs feed bacteria in the small intestine, creating a vicious cycle of overgrowth ‚Üí inflammation ‚Üí maldigestion ‚Üí more overgrowth. Monosaccharides (single sugars) are directly absorbed without feeding bacteria.

SCD "Legal" Foods

  • Meats, fish, poultry, eggs
  • Most vegetables (except potatoes, grains)
  • Most fruits (limit initially if severe)
  • Nuts and seeds (after symptoms stabilize)
  • Aged cheeses (lactose-free), homemade yogurt (24-hour fermented)
  • Honey (the only allowed sweetener)
  • Nut flours (almond, coconut)

SCD "Illegal" Foods

  • All grains (wheat, rice, corn, oats)
  • Potatoes, sweet potatoes, yams
  • Sugar, maple syrup, agave, artificial sweeteners
  • Most dairy (except aged cheese and 24-hr yogurt)
  • Soy products
  • Processed foods with additives

GAPS Protocol

Dr. Natasha Campbell-McBride developed GAPS based on SCD, adding elements for gut healing and addressing the "gut-brain" connection.

GAPS Additions to SCD

Heavy emphasis on bone broth for gut healing. Fermented foods (sauerkraut, kefir) for probiotic support. Structured introduction phase for severe cases. Focus on the gut-brain-psychology connection. Juicing protocols and detoxification support.

GAPS Introduction Diet Stages

  • Stage 1: Meat stock, boiled meats, probiotic foods (severe gut damage)
  • Stage 2: Add egg yolks, ghee, stews
  • Stage 3: Add avocado, sauerkraut, scrambled eggs
  • Stage 4: Add roasted/grilled meat, olive oil, fresh juices
  • Stage 5: Add raw vegetables, apple puree
  • Stage 6: Add raw fruit, more raw foods
  • Full GAPS: All SCD-legal foods plus GAPS additions

Implementation Reality

SCD and GAPS are VERY restrictive. They require significant meal prep, eliminate convenience foods, and demand high client commitment. Reserve for severe cases where simpler interventions have failed. Many practitioners never need to use full GAPS‚Äîit's a powerful tool for the right client, not a default protocol.

When to Consider SCD/GAPS

  • IBD (Crohn's, UC) ‚Äî Strong evidence for SCD in inflammatory bowel disease
  • Severe SIBO ‚Äî When low-FODMAP wasn't sufficient
  • Persistent dysbiosis ‚Äî Complex cases with multiple pathogenic organisms
  • Autism spectrum support ‚Äî GAPS specifically developed for gut-brain issues
  • Failed simpler interventions ‚Äî Escalation when elimination diets weren't enough

Comparing Low-FODMAP, SCD, and GAPS

Low-FODMAP

Reduces fermentable carbs that cause gas. Targets symptom control. Less restrictive. Better evidence base. Good for IBS, mild-moderate SIBO.

SCD

Eliminates all complex carbs. Starves bacteria more completely. More restrictive. Good for IBD, severe SIBO, persistent dysbiosis.

GAPS

SCD foundation with emphasis on healing and detox. Most restrictive initially. Requires bone broth and fermented foods. Good for severe gut damage, autism spectrum, gut-brain disorders.

Practical Approach

Start with low-FODMAP or standard elimination. If insufficient, step up to modified SCD (SCD principles without full restriction). Reserve full SCD or GAPS for failures or severe presentations. Match the intensity of the intervention to the severity of the condition.

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. What is the core principle of the Specific Carbohydrate Diet?

SCD eliminates complex carbohydrates (disaccharides, polysaccharides) and allows only monosaccharides. The theory: complex carbs require digestion and when that's impaired, they feed pathogenic bacteria. Monosaccharides are directly absorbed without bacterial fermentation, "starving" the overgrown bacteria.

2. How does GAPS differ from SCD?

GAPS is built on SCD principles but adds: heavy emphasis on bone broth for gut healing, required fermented foods for probiotics, a structured 6-stage introduction diet for severe cases, focus on the gut-brain-psychology connection, and juicing/detox protocols. GAPS is typically used for more severe presentations with gut-brain symptoms.

3. When should you consider SCD/GAPS vs. simpler interventions?

Reserve SCD/GAPS for: IBD (Crohn's/UC), severe SIBO when low-FODMAP wasn't sufficient, persistent complex dysbiosis, autism spectrum support, and cases where simpler interventions failed. Start with low-FODMAP or standard elimination first‚Äîmatch intervention intensity to condition severity. More restrictive isn't always better.

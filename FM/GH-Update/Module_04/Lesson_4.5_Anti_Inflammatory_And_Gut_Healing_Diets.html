Anti-Inflammatory Diet

Emphasizes foods that reduce inflammation while eliminating inflammatory triggers.

Gut-Healing Foods

Foods that support intestinal barrier repair: bone broth, collagen, zinc, glutamine-rich foods.

Omega-3/Omega-6 Ratio

Balance of anti-inflammatory (omega-3) to pro-inflammatory (omega-6) fatty acids.

Phytonutrients

Plant compounds (polyphenols, flavonoids) with anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

Anti-Inflammatory Diet Principles

Chronic inflammation underlies most gut dysfunction. An anti-inflammatory diet removes inflammatory triggers while emphasizing foods that actively reduce inflammation.

Foods to Emphasize

Omega-3 Rich Foods

Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), walnuts, flaxseeds, chia seeds. Omega-3s directly reduce inflammatory prostaglandins. Aim for 2-3 servings of fatty fish per week or supplement.

Colorful Vegetables

Deep greens (spinach, kale), purples (beets, cabbage), oranges (carrots, squash), reds (tomatoes, peppers). Each color provides different anti-inflammatory phytonutrients. "Eat the rainbow."

Anti-Inflammatory Spices

Turmeric (curcumin), ginger, garlic, rosemary, cinnamon. These have potent anti-inflammatory effects. Turmeric with black pepper and fat increases absorption significantly.

Quality Fats

Extra virgin olive oil, avocados, coconut oil, nuts. Avoid industrial seed oils (corn, soy, sunflower) which are high in pro-inflammatory omega-6.

  • Industrial seed oils ‚Äî High omega-6, pro-inflammatory
  • Processed foods ‚Äî Additives, preservatives, trans fats
  • Refined grains ‚Äî Blood sugar spikes drive inflammation

Gut-Healing Foods

Bone Broth

Rich in collagen, gelatin, glycine, proline, and glutamine‚Äîall essential for gut lining repair. Also provides easily absorbed minerals. Slow-cooked for 24+ hours maximizes nutrient extraction. Foundation of GAPS protocol.

Collagen and Gelatin

Provides amino acids specifically used by enterocytes (gut lining cells) for repair. Can be added as powder to smoothies, soups, or foods if bone broth isn't consumed regularly.

Glutamine-Rich Foods

Glutamine is the primary fuel for enterocytes. Found in: bone broth, cabbage, beets, beans, spinach, parsley. Can also be supplemented (5-10g daily for gut healing).

Zinc-Rich Foods

Zinc supports tight junction integrity and gut immunity. Found in: oysters, beef, pumpkin seeds, lamb. Many gut-compromised clients are zinc-deficient.

Fermented Foods for Gut Health

  • Sauerkraut (raw) ‚Äî Lactobacillus species, vitamin C, fiber
  • Kimchi ‚Äî Similar to sauerkraut with additional spices
  • Kefir ‚Äî Diverse probiotic strains (use dairy-free if sensitive)
  • Kombucha ‚Äî Fermented tea; watch sugar content
  • Miso and tempeh ‚Äî Fermented soy (if tolerated)

Start Slowly

Fermented foods can initially worsen symptoms in some clients (SIBO, histamine intolerance). Start with small amounts (1 tsp) and increase gradually. If symptoms worsen, address underlying conditions first before pushing fermented foods.

The Gut-Healing Protocol

A comprehensive gut-healing diet combines:

  • Elimination of inflammatory triggers (sugar, processed foods, seed oils)
  • Emphasis on anti-inflammatory foods (omega-3s, phytonutrients, spices)
  • Daily bone broth or collagen supplementation
  • Gradual introduction of fermented foods
  • Nutrient density focus (organ meats, oysters, colorful vegetables)

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. Why is bone broth considered a cornerstone of gut healing?

Bone broth provides collagen, gelatin, glycine, proline, and glutamine‚Äîall essential amino acids for gut lining repair. It also contains easily absorbed minerals. The slow cooking process extracts these nutrients in bioavailable forms. It's the foundation of GAPS and many gut-healing protocols.

2. Why should fermented foods be introduced gradually?

Fermented foods can initially worsen symptoms in clients with SIBO (feeding bacteria) or histamine intolerance (fermented foods are high in histamine). Starting slowly (1 tsp) allows assessment of tolerance. If symptoms worsen, address underlying conditions before pushing fermented foods. They're beneficial but not universally tolerated initially.

3. What is the significance of the omega-3/omega-6 ratio?

Omega-3 fatty acids are anti-inflammatory; omega-6 (especially from industrial seed oils) are pro-inflammatory when in excess. Modern diets are heavily skewed toward omega-6. Improving this ratio by increasing omega-3 (fatty fish, walnuts, flax) and reducing omega-6 (seed oils) directly reduces systemic inflammation.

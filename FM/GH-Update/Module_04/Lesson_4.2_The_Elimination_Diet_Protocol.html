üî¨ Key Terms

Standard Elimination

Removes the "Big 6-8" common triggers: gluten, dairy, eggs, soy, corn, sugar, processed foods.

Comprehensive Elimination

More restrictive protocol also removing nuts, seeds, nightshades, and other potential triggers.

Challenge Phase

Systematic reintroduction of eliminated foods one at a time to identify reactions.

Washout Period

2-3 day gap between food challenges to allow reactions to clear before testing next food.

Why Elimination Diets Work

The elimination diet is the gold standard for identifying food sensitivities because it directly demonstrates cause and effect. No lab test can replicate this proof of individual reactivity.

The Mechanism

Constant exposure to trigger foods creates chronic, low-grade inflammation that becomes "background noise." By removing triggers for 2-4 weeks, inflammation subsides, symptoms improve, and the body becomes more sensitive to reactions upon reexposure.

Foods to Eliminate

Standard Elimination ("Big 6-8")

  • Gluten ‚Äî Wheat, barley, rye, and often oats (cross-contamination)
  • Dairy ‚Äî All milk products from cows (and often goat/sheep initially)
  • Eggs ‚Äî Whole eggs and egg-containing products
  • Soy ‚Äî Soy milk, tofu, edamame, soy sauce, soy lecithin
  • Corn ‚Äî Corn, corn syrup, cornstarch
  • Sugar ‚Äî Refined sugars, artificial sweeteners
  • Processed foods ‚Äî Packaged foods with additives
  • Alcohol and caffeine ‚Äî Optional but often included

Comprehensive Elimination (adds)

  • Nuts and seeds (or specific ones like peanuts)
  • Nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, potatoes)
  • Legumes
  • Shellfish
  • Citrus

Clinical Decision

Start with standard elimination for most clients. Reserve comprehensive elimination for autoimmune conditions, stubborn cases, or clients who didn't respond to standard protocol. More restriction isn't always better‚Äîit's harder to comply with and may not be necessary.

Phase 1: Elimination (2-4 Weeks)

Duration

Minimum 2 weeks; 3-4 weeks for autoimmune or complex cases. Symptoms should be noticeably improved before moving to reintroduction. If no improvement in 4 weeks, the issue likely isn't food-related (or wrong foods eliminated).

What to Expect

Days 1-5: Possible withdrawal symptoms (headaches, fatigue, cravings). Days 5-14: Gradual symptom improvement. By week 3-4: Clear sense of baseline without trigger foods.

Phase 2: Reintroduction Protocol

The Challenge Method

Introduce one food at a time. Eat a normal portion 2-3 times on Day 1. Monitor for 2-3 days (some reactions are delayed). If no reaction, that food is likely tolerated. If reaction occurs, note it and wait until symptoms clear before next challenge.

Reintroduction Order (suggested)

  • Week 1: Eggs (often well-tolerated)
  • Week 1: Legumes (if eliminated)
  • Week 2: Dairy (start with butter/ghee, then hard cheese, then milk)
  • Week 2-3: Gluten-free grains (rice, oats)
  • Week 3-4: Gluten (wheat)
  • Week 4+: Remaining eliminated foods

Common Mistake

Many clients are tempted to reintroduce multiple foods at once because they feel better and are eager. This defeats the purpose‚Äîyou won't know which food caused a reaction. Discipline in reintroduction is as important as discipline in elimination.

Tracking Reactions

Have clients track symptoms during reintroduction:

  • Digestive: bloating, gas, pain, change in stool
  • Neurological: brain fog, headache, fatigue
  • Skin: rashes, acne flares, itching
  • Mood: anxiety, irritability, depression
  • Joint pain or stiffness
  • Sinus congestion, post-nasal drip

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. What are the "Big 6-8" foods in a standard elimination diet?

Gluten, dairy, eggs, soy, corn, refined sugar, processed foods, and often alcohol/caffeine. These are the most common food sensitivities and removing them addresses the majority of food-related reactions in most clients.

2. Why is proper reintroduction protocol critical?

Proper reintroduction (one food at a time, 2-3 day washout between challenges) identifies exactly which foods cause reactions. If multiple foods are reintroduced together, you can't determine the specific trigger. This defeats the entire purpose of the elimination and leaves the client without actionable information.

3. What should a client expect during the first week of elimination?

Days 1-5 often bring withdrawal symptoms: headaches, fatigue, irritability, and strong cravings. This is normal and indicates the body is adjusting. Warn clients about this so they don't interpret it as "the diet isn't working" and quit. Improvement typically begins after day 5-7.

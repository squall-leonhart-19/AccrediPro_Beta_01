FODMAP Stacking

Eating multiple low-FODMAP foods that together exceed tolerance threshold.

What Are FODMAPs?

FODMAPs are fermentable short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine. When they reach the colon (or small intestine in SIBO), bacteria ferment them, producing gas, bloating, and triggering IBS symptoms.

The FODMAP Categories

Oligosaccharides (Fructans & GOS)

Found in: wheat, rye, onions, garlic, legumes, artichokes. These are the most common triggers. Note: Humans don't have enzymes to digest these‚Äîeveryone malabsorbs them, but symptomatic people react more.

Disaccharides (Lactose)

Found in: milk, yogurt, soft cheeses, ice cream. Only an issue for those with lactase deficiency. Hard cheeses and butter are usually tolerated.

Monosaccharides (Excess Fructose)

Found in: apples, pears, mangoes, honey, high-fructose corn syrup. It's the EXCESS fructose over glucose that matters. Fruits with balanced fructose:glucose are better tolerated.

Polyols (Sugar Alcohols)

Found in: stone fruits (peaches, plums), mushrooms, cauliflower, and artificial sweeteners (sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol). Common in "sugar-free" products.

High vs. Low FODMAP Examples

Vegetables

  • High: Garlic, onion, asparagus, cauliflower, mushrooms, artichoke
  • Low: Carrots, zucchini, bell peppers, spinach, tomatoes, cucumbers

Fruits

  • High: Apples, pears, mangoes, watermelon, stone fruits
  • Low: Berries, citrus, bananas (unripe), grapes, kiwi

Proteins (generally low FODMAP)

  • Plain meats, fish, poultry, eggs are low FODMAP
  • Watch for: marinades with garlic/onion, processed meats with additives

The Three Phases

Phase 1: Elimination (2-6 weeks)

Strict low-FODMAP eating. Eliminate all high-FODMAP foods. Most people see 50%+ symptom improvement within 2-4 weeks. If no improvement by 6 weeks, FODMAPs likely aren't the issue.

Phase 2: Reintroduction (6-8 weeks)

Test one FODMAP group at a time using specific challenge foods. Identify which FODMAPs trigger symptoms and at what threshold. Not all categories will be problematic.

Phase 3: Personalization (ongoing)

Create a modified diet avoiding only personal triggers at problematic doses. Most people can liberalize significantly‚Äîfull restriction is NOT the goal.

Critical: Low-FODMAP Is Not Forever

Long-term strict low-FODMAP reduces beneficial bacteria (Bifidobacteria especially) because you're starving the microbiome of its preferred fuel. It's a diagnostic and symptom-control tool, not a permanent diet. The goal is to identify triggers and liberalize as much as possible.

When to Use Low-FODMAP

  • IBS: First-line intervention with strong evidence base
  • SIBO: Reduces substrate for bacterial overgrowth during treatment
  • Excessive bloating/gas: Regardless of formal IBS diagnosis

When NOT to Use Low-FODMAP

  • History of eating disorders (too restrictive)
  • Already on other restrictive diets (stacking restrictions)
  • Underweight or nutritionally compromised clients
  • As a permanent solution without addressing root cause

The Monash App

The Monash University FODMAP Diet app is the gold standard resource. It's regularly updated with tested foods and includes a traffic light system for portions. Strongly recommend clients download it‚Äîworth the small cost.

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. What does FODMAP stand for and why do they cause symptoms?

FODMAP = Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, And Polyols. These short-chain carbohydrates are poorly absorbed and reach the colon (or small intestine in SIBO) where bacteria ferment them. Fermentation produces gas, causing bloating and distension. They also have osmotic effects, drawing water into the intestine, which can cause diarrhea.

2. Why shouldn't low-FODMAP be a permanent diet?

Long-term strict low-FODMAP starves beneficial bacteria (especially Bifidobacteria) because FODMAPs are their preferred fuel (they're prebiotics). This reduces microbiome diversity and can worsen gut health long-term. Low-FODMAP is a diagnostic and symptom-control tool‚Äîthe goal is to identify specific triggers and liberalize the diet as much as possible.

3. Which FODMAP category includes garlic and onion?

Oligosaccharides (specifically fructans). This category also includes wheat, rye, legumes, and artichokes. Fructans and GOS (galacto-oligosaccharides) are the most common trigger category. Important note: garlic- and onion-infused oils are often tolerated because the fructans are water-soluble and don't transfer to oil.

MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)

Red blood cell size indicator; elevated suggests B12/folate issues; low suggests iron deficiency.

Ferritin

Iron storage marker; low indicates iron deficiency; very high can indicate inflammation.

Total Protein/Albumin

Markers of protein status and liver function; low can indicate malabsorption or inadequate intake.

hs-CRP

High-sensitivity C-reactive protein; systemic inflammation marker that can reflect gut-driven inflammation.

Blood Chemistry as a Window to Gut Function

Standard blood work, when interpreted functionally, provides indirect evidence of gut health. Nutrient deficiencies, inflammation, and metabolic patterns often reflect digestive dysfunction.

Nutrient Status Markers

Iron Panel

Low ferritin (especially <50 in symptomatic clients) suggests iron deficiency. Think: low stomach acid (can't absorb iron), intestinal blood loss (occult bleeding), or celiac disease. Functional optimal: 70-90 ng/mL for women; slightly higher for men.

B12 and Folate

B12 requires stomach acid and intrinsic factor for absorption. Low B12 suggests: low acid, H. pylori, metformin use, or pernicious anemia. Functional optimal B12: >500 pg/mL (many symptomatic at "normal" 200-400).

MCV (Red Cell Size)

High MCV (>92 fL functionally) suggests B12 or folate deficiency‚Äîmacrocytic anemia. Low MCV (<85 fL) suggests iron deficiency‚Äîmicrocytic anemia. Both connect directly to gut absorption and acid production.

Vitamin D

Fat-soluble vitamin requiring proper fat digestion. Low vitamin D with normal sun exposure suggests: poor fat absorption, gallbladder/bile issues, or pancreatic insufficiency. Functional optimal: 50-70 ng/mL.

Protein Status

Total Protein and Albumin

Low levels suggest: inadequate protein intake, poor protein digestion (low acid, pancreatic insufficiency), or intestinal malabsorption. Albumin is also an inflammation marker‚Äîchronic inflammation depletes it. Functional optimal albumin: 4.5-5.0 g/dL.

BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)

Byproduct of protein metabolism. Unusually low BUN (<10 mg/dL) can suggest inadequate protein digestion or intake. Elevated BUN may indicate dehydration or kidney issues.

Inflammation Markers

hs-CRP

Systemic inflammation marker that can be elevated due to gut inflammation, dysbiosis, or leaky gut triggering immune activation. Functional optimal: <1.0 mg/L. Elevated values warrant investigation of gut as inflammation source.

ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate)

Another inflammation marker. Elevated ESR with GI symptoms may indicate IBD or other inflammatory conditions requiring medical evaluation.

Liver and Gallbladder Function

GGT, ALP, AST, ALT

Elevated GGT can indicate bile duct issues or fatty liver. Elevated ALP may suggest bile stasis. These connect to fat digestion and absorption problems. Pattern analysis matters‚Äîsingle elevated marker less significant than patterns.

Clinical Connection

A client with low ferritin, low B12, elevated MCV, and digestive symptoms has a pattern suggesting low stomach acid. This blood chemistry pattern guides investigation: test for H. pylori, assess for PPI use, consider Betaine HCl trial.

Thyroid Function

Thyroid connects closely to gut function:

  • Hypothyroidism slows gut motility ‚Üí constipation ‚Üí SIBO risk
  • Low thyroid affects stomach acid production
  • Hashimoto's often connected to gut permeability
  • Functional TSH optimal: 1.0-2.0 mIU/L (much narrower than lab ranges)

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. A client has low ferritin, low B12, and elevated MCV. What might this pattern indicate?

This pattern strongly suggests low stomach acid (hypochlorhydria). Iron and B12 both require adequate acid for absorption, and MCV elevation indicates the red blood cells are enlarging due to B12/folate deficiency. Investigate: H. pylori, PPI use history, age-related acid decline. Consider: acid support, H. pylori testing, nutrient supplementation.

2. Why might low vitamin D with normal sun exposure suggest a gut issue?

Vitamin D is fat-soluble and requires proper fat digestion and absorption. Low levels despite adequate sun exposure suggests: poor fat absorption, gallbladder/bile issues (can't emulsify fats), or pancreatic insufficiency (insufficient lipase). Investigate fat-soluble vitamin absorption across the board (A, D, E, K).

3. How might elevated hs-CRP relate to gut health?

hs-CRP is a systemic inflammation marker. The gut can drive systemic inflammation through: intestinal permeability (leaky gut) allowing bacterial products to enter circulation, dysbiosis creating inflammatory metabolites, and direct gut inflammation (IBD, infections). Elevated hs-CRP in someone with gut symptoms warrants investigating the gut as the inflammation source.

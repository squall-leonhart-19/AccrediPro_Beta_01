Lactulose Breath Test

Uses lactulose (non-absorbed sugar) as substrate; can detect SIBO throughout entire small intestine.

Glucose Breath Test

Uses glucose (absorbed in proximal small intestine) as substrate; higher specificity but may miss distal SIBO.

Hydrogen (H2)

Gas produced by bacterial fermentation; rise ‚â•20 ppm by 90 minutes indicates SIBO.

Methane (CH4)

Gas produced by archaea; ‚â•10 ppm at any point indicates IMO (methane-predominant).

How Breath Testing Works

The principle is simple: humans don't produce hydrogen or methane‚Äîonly gut bacteria do. When bacteria in the small intestine encounter fermentable carbohydrates, they produce gases that are absorbed into the bloodstream and exhaled in the breath.

The Test Process

After preparation, the client drinks a sugar substrate (lactulose or glucose). Breath samples are collected every 15-20 minutes for 2-3 hours. If bacteria are in the small intestine, they'll ferment the sugar and produce detectable gases earlier than expected.

Lactulose vs. Glucose

Lactulose

Non-absorbed synthetic sugar that travels entire small intestine. Pros: Can detect distal (far end) SIBO. Cons: Lower specificity‚Äîmay also pick up colonic bacteria arriving via normal transit.

Glucose

Absorbed in the proximal (upper) small intestine. Pros: Higher specificity‚Äîif there's a rise, it's definitely from small intestine. Cons: May miss distal SIBO if bacteria are only in lower small intestine.

Which to Choose?

Most practitioners prefer lactulose for broader detection, accepting the trade-off of lower specificity. Glucose may be chosen if proximal SIBO is suspected or if previous lactulose tests were equivocal.

Interpreting Results

Hydrogen SIBO

  • Positive: Rise of ‚â•20 ppm above baseline within 90 minutes
  • Clinical correlation: Typically associated with diarrhea-predominant symptoms (IBS-D)

Methane/IMO

  • Positive: ‚â•10 ppm at any point during the test
  • Clinical correlation: Typically associated with constipation (IBS-C)
  • Note: Methane producers are archaea, not bacteria‚Äîhence "IMO" not SIBO

Combined Positive

  • Both hydrogen and methane elevated
  • Often indicates more complex/severe case
  • May see mixed bowel patterns (IBS-M)

Hydrogen Sulfide SIBO

Important Limitation

Standard breath tests do NOT measure hydrogen sulfide (H2S). If symptoms suggest hydrogen sulfide SIBO (sulfur intolerance, rotten egg gas, diarrhea) but H2/CH4 tests are negative, consider the trio-smart test which measures all three gases.

Test Preparation

Accurate results require proper preparation:

  • 1-2 weeks before: Stop antibiotics and probiotics
  • 1 week before: Stop prokinetics
  • Day before: Follow prep diet (white rice, plain meat, eggs, small amounts of oil‚Äîno fiber, complex carbs, dairy)
  • 12 hours before: Fast (water only)
  • Morning of: No smoking, exercise, or oral hygiene products

Limitations and False Results

False Negatives

Rapid transit (diarrhea) may cause early colonic fermentation confused with SIBO. Recent antibiotic use. Hydrogen sulfide producers not detected on standard tests. Non-hydrogen-producing bacteria.

False Positives

With lactulose, fast transit may show early rise that's actually colonic bacteria arriving early, not small intestinal overgrowth.

Clinical Reality

A negative breath test doesn't rule out SIBO, especially with strong clinical suspicion. If symptoms strongly suggest SIBO and test is negative, empirical treatment trial may be warranted. Treat the patient, not just the test.

The Controversy Around Food Sensitivity Testing

Food sensitivity testing is one of the most controversial areas in functional medicine. Understanding the limitations and appropriate use of these tests is essential for ethical practice.

Types of Testing

IgE Testing (True Allergy)

Measures IgE antibodies to specific foods. The medical gold standard for diagnosing true allergies. Positive results indicate risk of immediate reactions. This is NOT what we typically use in functional practice‚Äîclients with true allergies are under allergist care.

IgG Testing

Most common functional test. Measures IgG antibodies to food antigens. The theory: elevated IgG indicates immune reaction. The problem: IgG may simply indicate exposure to and tolerance of foods. Very controversial in scientific literature.

IgG4 Subclass

Some tests focus on IgG4, which may represent tolerance rather than reactivity. Research is mixed on clinical utility.

MRT (Mediator Release Test)

Measures changes in white blood cell size/volume when exposed to food antigens, indicating mediator release. May detect non-antibody reactions. Used with LEAP protocol. Higher cost but potentially more clinically relevant.

The IgG Controversy

Critical Limitation

Major allergy organizations (AAAI, EAACI) caution against using IgG tests for diagnosing food sensitivities. IgG antibodies to foods are found in healthy individuals and may simply reflect normal exposure and tolerance‚Äînot pathology.

Arguments Against IgG Testing

  • IgG4 may represent tolerance, not reaction
  • Healthy people have IgG to regularly consumed foods
  • Can lead to unnecessarily restrictive diets
  • Poor correlation with elimination diet findings
  • Expensive and not evidence-based by traditional standards

Arguments For IgG Testing (Some Practitioners)

  • Provides starting point for elimination trials
  • Some clients do see correlation with symptoms
  • May identify "leaky gut" pattern (multiple elevations)
  • Client preference for data-driven approach

Elimination Diet: The Gold Standard

Despite all testing options, the elimination diet remains the gold standard for identifying food sensitivities because it directly demonstrates cause and effect.

  • Remove suspected foods for 2-4 weeks
  • Monitor symptom improvement
  • Systematically reintroduce one food every 3-4 days
  • Observe for symptom return
  • Definitive proof of individual reactivity

Clinical Approach

If using IgG testing, frame it as a "guide for elimination priorities" rather than a definitive diagnosis. Use results to inform which foods to eliminate first, then confirm through reintroduction. The test suggests; the elimination diet confirms.

When Testing May Be Helpful

  • Overwhelmed clients ‚Äî Need direction on where to start eliminating
  • Compliance support ‚Äî Lab results motivate dietary changes
  • Multiple elevated foods ‚Äî Pattern suggests leaky gut to address
  • MRT for complex cases ‚Äî When standard elimination isn't working

Client Communication

Be transparent about limitations:

  • "This test can help guide our elimination strategy but isn't definitive"
  • "We'll confirm any findings through controlled reintroduction"
  • "A negative result doesn't rule out sensitivity"
  • "Multiple elevated foods often point to gut barrier issues we need to address"

1. Why is IgG food sensitivity testing controversial?

IgG antibodies to foods are found in healthy individuals and may simply indicate normal exposure and tolerance‚Äînot pathological reactivity. Major allergy organizations caution against using IgG tests for diagnosis. IgG4 may actually represent immune tolerance. Results poorly correlate with elimination diet findings and can lead to unnecessarily restrictive diets.

2. Why is the elimination diet still considered the gold standard?

The elimination diet directly demonstrates cause and effect: remove food ‚Üí symptoms improve ‚Üí reintroduce ‚Üí symptoms return. This proves individual reactivity rather than just showing antibody presence which may or may not be clinically significant. No lab test can replicate this direct proof of causation.

3. If a client has elevated IgG to many foods, what might this indicate?

Multiple elevated foods often suggests a "leaky gut" pattern‚Äîincreased intestinal permeability allowing larger food particles into the bloodstream, triggering immune response. The clinical priority becomes healing the gut barrier rather than permanently eliminating all flagged foods. Once permeability improves, many "sensitivities" may resolve.

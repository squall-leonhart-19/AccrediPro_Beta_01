Gut-Related Blood Markers

Many blood markers reflect gut health and digestive function. Learning to read these patterns provides valuable clinical insight.

Key Functional Ranges

Total protein: 6.9-7.4 (low = poor digestion/absorption). Albumin: 4.0-5.0 (low = liver, inflammation, malnutrition). Globulin: 2.3-2.8 (high = chronic inflammation/infection). BUN: 13-18 (low = poor protein digestion; high = dehydration).

Digestive Insufficiency Patterns

  • Low total protein + low BUN: Poor protein digestion (low HCl, enzymes)
  • Low phosphorus: HCl insufficiency marker
  • Low alkaline phosphatase: Zinc deficiency marker
  • Low cholesterol: Possible bile/fat absorption issues

Inflammation Markers

  • CRP: Acute inflammation; elevated in gut inflammation
  • Homocysteine: Elevated = B vitamin deficiencies, methylation issues
  • Globulin: Elevated = chronic inflammation, infection
  • A:G ratio: Low = chronic inflammation pattern

Nutrient Deficiency Markers

  • Low MCV: Iron deficiency anemia (common in malabsorption)
  • High MCV: B12 or folate deficiency
  • Low ferritin: Iron deficiency (gut bleed, poor absorption)
  • Low RBC, hemoglobin: Various anemias; always investigate cause

1. What does low total protein + low BUN indicate?

Low total protein + low BUN suggests poor protein digestion. This pattern often indicates low stomach acid (HCl) and/or inadequate digestive enzymes. The body isn't breaking down dietary protein effectively.

2. What does low alkaline phosphatase indicate?

Low alkaline phosphatase is a functional marker for zinc deficiency. Alkaline phosphatase is a zinc-dependent enzyme. When zinc is low, ALP activity decreases. Zinc is crucial for gut health, digestion, and immune function.

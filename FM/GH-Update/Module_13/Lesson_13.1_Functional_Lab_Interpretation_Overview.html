Functional vs Conventional Ranges

Lab reference ranges are based on the "sick" population, not optimal health. Functional ranges identify dysfunction before disease develops.

Key Distinction

Conventional ranges: Based on 95% of the tested population (often already unhealthy). If your value is "normal," you're just not sick enough to treat. Functional ranges: Based on optimal health. Catches dysfunction early.

Functional Lab Categories

  • Comprehensive stool testing: Microbiome, pathogens, inflammation, digestion
  • SIBO breath test: Hydrogen and methane measurement
  • Organic acids (OAT): Metabolic byproducts, yeast, bacteria, nutrients
  • Food sensitivity testing: IgG, IgA antibodies
  • Blood chemistry: CBC, CMP, thyroid, nutrients (with functional ranges)

Pattern Recognition Principles

  • Look at trends over time, not just single snapshots
  • Consider the whole picture, not isolated values
  • Values at the edges of "normal" often signal dysfunction
  • Context matters: symptoms, history, other labs
  • Multiple mild abnormalities often more significant than one flagged value

1. What's the difference between conventional and functional lab ranges?

Conventional ranges are based on 95% of the tested population (often unhealthy). Functional ranges are tighter and based on optimal health, catching dysfunction before disease develops. "Normal" just means not sick enough to treat.

2. Why might multiple mild lab abnormalities be more significant than one flagged value?

Multiple mild abnormalities often indicate a systemic pattern or root cause. A single flagged value might be an outlier, but several values all trending in one direction reveals dysfunction. Pattern recognition across multiple markers is key.

IgE-Mediated Allergy

Immediate immune reaction involving IgE antibodies, causing symptoms within minutes (hives, anaphylaxis).

IgG-Mediated Sensitivity

Delayed immune reaction with symptoms appearing hours to days later. More clinically debated.

Food Intolerance

Non-immune reaction due to enzyme deficiency or chemical sensitivity (e.g., lactose intolerance).

Elimination Diet

Gold standard for identifying food reactions‚Äîremoving suspected foods then systematically reintroducing.

The Spectrum of Food Reactions

Not all adverse food reactions are created equal. Understanding the different mechanisms is crucial for appropriate intervention:

Type Mechanism Timing Examples
Food Allergy IgE-mediated immune response Minutes to 2 hours Peanut, shellfish, tree nuts

Food Allergies (IgE-Mediated)

True Allergies

Involve IgE antibodies and mast cell degranulation. Symptoms are immediate: hives, swelling, breathing difficulty, anaphylaxis. These are medical emergencies requiring avoidance and epinephrine access. This is NOT our scope‚Äîclients with true allergies need medical management.

Food Sensitivities

Food sensitivities are delayed reactions with symptoms appearing hours to days after consumption. They're often dose-dependent and can vary based on gut health status.

Common Symptoms

  • Digestive: bloating, gas, diarrhea, constipation, reflux
  • Neurological: brain fog, headaches, fatigue, mood changes
  • Skin: eczema, acne, rashes
  • Joint pain and inflammation
  • Sinus congestion, post-nasal drip

Clinical Pearl

Sensitivities often worsen with increased intestinal permeability. Many clients find their "food sensitivities" resolve after healing the gut lining. This is why we address root causes rather than just eliminating foods indefinitely.

Food Intolerances

Enzyme Deficiencies

Lactose intolerance (lactase deficiency), fructose malabsorption, and sucrase-isomaltase deficiency are examples. These are manageable with enzyme supplementation or dietary modification.

Chemical Sensitivities

Histamine intolerance, salicylate sensitivity, and reactions to food additives (MSG, sulfites) don't involve immune response but can cause significant symptoms. Often related to impaired enzyme function or detoxification.

Testing Considerations

  • IgE testing ‚Äî Useful for true allergies; blood or skin prick
  • IgG testing ‚Äî Controversial; may indicate exposure, not necessarily reaction
  • Elimination diet ‚Äî Gold standard; removes suspected foods for 2-4 weeks then reintroduces systematically
  • Celiac testing ‚Äî Tissue transglutaminase (tTG) IgA; must be eating gluten for accurate results

Important

IgG food sensitivity tests are widely marketed but have significant limitations. False positives are common, and IgG may simply indicate exposure to foods. Elimination diets remain the gold standard for identifying true reactivity.

Elimination Diet Protocol

  • Phase 1 (Elimination): Remove common triggers for 2-4 weeks until symptoms improve
  • Phase 2 (Reintroduction): Add one food at a time, every 3-4 days, monitoring for reactions
  • Phase 3 (Personalization): Create individualized long-term diet based on findings

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. What is the key difference between a food allergy and a food sensitivity?

Food allergies are IgE-mediated with immediate symptoms (minutes to 2 hours)‚Äîhives, swelling, anaphylaxis. Food sensitivities are delayed reactions (hours to days), often dose-dependent, with symptoms like bloating, brain fog, skin issues. Allergies require strict avoidance; sensitivities may improve with gut healing.

2. Why is the elimination diet considered the gold standard over IgG testing?

IgG tests may simply indicate exposure to foods, not necessarily reactivity. False positives are common. The elimination diet directly demonstrates cause-and-effect: remove food ‚Üí symptoms improve ‚Üí reintroduce ‚Üí symptoms return. This proves true reactivity rather than just antibody presence.

3. Why might food sensitivities resolve after healing the gut?

Many sensitivities are driven by increased intestinal permeability (leaky gut). When the gut barrier is compromised, larger food particles enter the bloodstream and trigger immune reactions. Healing the gut lining reduces this permeability, so foods that previously caused reactions are now properly digested before absorption.

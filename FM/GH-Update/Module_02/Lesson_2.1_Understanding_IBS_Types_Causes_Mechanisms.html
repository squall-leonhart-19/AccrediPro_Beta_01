Rome IV Criteria

The international diagnostic standard for functional GI disorders including IBS, based on symptom patterns.

Visceral Hypersensitivity

Heightened pain perception in the gut, where normal sensations (like gas or stool movement) are perceived as painful.

Post-Infectious IBS (PI-IBS)

IBS that develops after an acute gastrointestinal infection, affecting 10-20% of food poisoning survivors.

Bristol Stool Scale

A diagnostic tool that classifies stool into 7 types, used to determine IBS subtype and guide treatment.

What Is IBS?

Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is one of the most common conditions you'll encounter in practice. Affecting 10-15% of the global population, IBS is characterized by chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habits without structural abnormalities visible on standard tests.

Understanding IBS deeply is essential because:

  • It's the #1 reason for gastroenterologist referrals ‚Äî yet many clients are dismissed or undertreated
  • Standard medical treatment often fails ‚Äî creating opportunity for functional approaches
  • Multiple mechanisms are involved ‚Äî requiring individualized protocols
  • It significantly impacts quality of life ‚Äî clients are desperate for real solutions

The Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria

IBS is diagnosed using the Rome IV criteria, which require:

Rome IV Definition

Recurrent abdominal pain on average at least 1 day per week in the last 3 months, associated with 2 or more of the following: 1) Related to defecation, 2) Associated with a change in frequency of stool, 3) Associated with a change in form (appearance) of stool. Symptoms must have started at least 6 months ago.

Clinical Note

Rome IV is a diagnosis of INCLUSION, not exclusion. While red flags should prompt testing, a positive diagnosis can often be made from history alone. Many clients have undergone extensive testing that came back "normal"‚Äîthat doesn't mean they don't have IBS.

IBS Subtypes

IBS is classified into four subtypes based on predominant bowel pattern, determined using the Bristol Stool Scale:

IBS-C (Constipation)

More than 25% of bowel movements are hard/lumpy (Bristol 1-2). Less than 25% are loose/watery. Often involves straining, incomplete evacuation, and bloating.

üü°

IBS-D (Diarrhea)

More than 25% of bowel movements are loose/watery (Bristol 6-7). Less than 25% are hard. Often involves urgency, frequency, and cramping before bowel movements.

üü¢

IBS-M (Mixed)

More than 25% of bowel movements are hard AND more than 25% are loose. Alternating pattern between constipation and diarrhea, often unpredictable.

IBS-U (Unsubtyped)

Meets IBS criteria but doesn't fit clearly into C, D, or M categories. Stool consistency varies but doesn't meet thresholds for other subtypes.

Underlying Mechanisms

IBS is not a single disease but a collection of symptoms that can arise from multiple mechanisms:

Visceral Hypersensitivity

The gut-brain connection is dysregulated, causing normal sensations to be interpreted as pain. A gas bubble that a healthy person wouldn't notice becomes painful in someone with IBS. This explains why testing often shows nothing "wrong"‚Äîthe problem is in pain perception, not tissue damage.

Altered Motility

The coordinated muscle contractions that move food through the gut are disrupted. This can manifest as too-fast transit (diarrhea), too-slow transit (constipation), or spasms causing pain. The migrating motor complex (MMC) is often impaired.

Dysbiosis

IBS patients consistently show different microbiome patterns compared to healthy controls‚Äîoften reduced diversity and altered bacterial ratios. This affects fermentation, gas production, and gut-brain signaling.

Low-Grade Inflammation

While not visible on standard biopsies, microscopic inflammation and increased mast cell activity are often present in IBS. This contributes to barrier dysfunction and hypersensitivity.

Post-Infectious IBS

One of the most important discoveries in IBS research is post-infectious IBS (PI-IBS). Studies show that 10-20% of people who experience acute gastroenteritis (food poisoning) develop IBS symptoms that persist for months or years afterward.

Key Clinical Question

"Did your symptoms start after a bout of food poisoning or travelers' diarrhea?" If yes, this strongly suggests PI-IBS and directs treatment toward addressing the underlying damage from infection‚Äîoften involving SIBO, motility dysfunction, and autoimmune damage to gut nerves.

Assessment Strategies

When assessing clients with suspected IBS:

  • Use the Bristol Stool Scale ‚Äî Have clients track their stool type for 1-2 weeks to determine subtype
  • Ask about onset ‚Äî Sudden onset after infection suggests PI-IBS; gradual onset suggests other mechanisms
  • Identify triggers ‚Äî Food, stress, menstrual cycle, specific meals or times of day
  • Screen for red flags ‚Äî Blood in stool, unexplained weight loss, family history of IBD or colon cancer, onset after age 50
  • Assess severity and impact ‚Äî How much does it affect their daily life, work, relationships?

What are the Rome IV criteria for diagnosing IBS?

Rome IV requires recurrent abdominal pain on average at least 1 day per week for the last 3 months, associated with 2 or more of: 1) Related to defecation, 2) Change in stool frequency, 3) Change in stool form/appearance. 2

A client has IBS symptoms that started suddenly 3 months after a severe case of food poisoning from undercooked chicken. What type of IBS is this likely to be, and what mechanisms might be involved?

This is likely Post-Infectious IBS (PI-IBS), which affects 10-20% of food poisoning survivors. Mechanisms involved include: 1) Damage to the migrating motor complex causing motility dysfunction, 2) Autoimmune damage to gut nerves (anti-vinculin and anti-CdtB antibodies), 3) Residual dysbiosis from the infection, 4) Development of SIBO due to impaired motility. This history strongly directs treatment toward addressing these specific mechanisms.
3

What is visceral hypersensitivity and why does it explain why IBS testing often comes back "normal"?

Visceral hypersensitivity is a heightened pain perception in the gut where normal sensations (like gas or stool movement) are perceived as painful. It's a dysfunction in gut-brain signaling, not tissue damage. Tests come back "normal" because they look for structural problems (inflammation, ulcers, tumors), but the issue is in HOW the brain interprets sensory signals from the gut‚Äînot in the gut tissue itself.

üìù Key Takeaways

  • IBS affects 10-15% of the population and is the #1 reason for gastroenterologist referrals.
  • Rome IV criteria define IBS as recurrent abdominal pain with altered bowel habits for 3+ months.
  • Four subtypes exist: IBS-C (constipation), IBS-D (diarrhea), IBS-M (mixed), and IBS-U (unclassified).
  • Multiple mechanisms drive IBS: visceral hypersensitivity, altered motility, dysbiosis, and low-grade inflammation.
  • Post-infectious IBS affects 10-20% of food poisoning survivors and has specific treatment implications.
  • Assessment should include stool tracking, onset history, trigger identification, and red flag screening.

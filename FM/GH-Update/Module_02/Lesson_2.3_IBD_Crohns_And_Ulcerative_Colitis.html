IBD (Inflammatory Bowel Disease)

Chronic inflammatory conditions of the GI tract including Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis‚Äîdistinct from IBS.

Transmural Inflammation

Inflammation that affects all layers of the intestinal wall, characteristic of Crohn's disease.

Skip Lesions

Areas of inflammation interspersed with normal tissue, a hallmark of Crohn's disease.

Calprotectin

A fecal marker of intestinal inflammation, used to distinguish IBD from IBS and monitor disease activity.

IBD vs. IBS: A Critical Distinction

One of the most important distinctions in gut health is between IBD (Inflammatory Bowel Disease) and IBS (Irritable Bowel Syndrome). While they share some symptoms, they are fundamentally different conditions:

Feature IBS IBD
Nature Functional disorder Autoimmune/inflammatory disease
Tissue Damage No visible damage Visible inflammation, ulceration
Blood in Stool Not typical Common
Weight Loss Uncommon Common in active disease
Lab Markers Usually normal Elevated inflammatory markers
Scope of Practice Primary focus for practitioners Requires medical management + support

Scope of Practice Alert

IBD requires medical diagnosis and management. As practitioners, we SUPPORT clients with IBD‚Äîwe do not diagnose or treat the disease itself. Always work collaboratively with gastroenterologists when clients have confirmed or suspected IBD.

Crohn's Disease

Crohn's disease can affect any part of the GI tract from mouth to anus, but most commonly involves the terminal ileum (end of small intestine) and colon.

Pathophysiology

Crohn's involves transmural inflammation (affecting all layers of the intestinal wall) and is characterized by "skip lesions"‚Äîpatchy areas of inflammation separated by normal tissue. This can lead to strictures, fistulas, and abscesses.

Clinical Presentation

  • Abdominal pain ‚Äî Often in right lower quadrant (ileal involvement)
  • Diarrhea ‚Äî May or may not contain blood
  • Weight loss ‚Äî Due to malabsorption and reduced intake
  • Fatigue ‚Äî From anemia and chronic inflammation
  • Perianal disease ‚Äî Fistulas, abscesses, skin tags
  • Extraintestinal manifestations ‚Äî Joint pain, skin lesions, eye inflammation

Ulcerative Colitis

Ulcerative colitis (UC) is limited to the colon and rectum, involving only the mucosal (inner) layer in a continuous pattern starting from the rectum.

Pathophysiology

UC involves mucosal inflammation only (not transmural) and progresses in a continuous pattern from the rectum upward. There are no skip lesions. The inflammation causes ulceration, bleeding, and eventual tissue damage.

Clinical Presentation

  • Bloody diarrhea ‚Äî The hallmark symptom
  • Urgency ‚Äî Intense need to defecate with little warning
  • Tenesmus ‚Äî Feeling of incomplete evacuation
  • Cramping ‚Äî Often relieved by bowel movement
  • Mucus in stool ‚Äî From mucosal damage

Root Causes and Triggers

IBD is a complex condition influenced by multiple factors:

Genetic Susceptibility

Strong genetic component‚Äîrisk is 10-20x higher if a first-degree relative has IBD. Over 200 genetic loci are associated with IBD, many involved in immune regulation and barrier function.

Microbiome Dysbiosis

IBD patients consistently show reduced diversity and altered bacterial composition. Whether this is cause or effect is debated, but modulating the microbiome can influence disease activity.

Barrier Dysfunction

Increased intestinal permeability may allow bacterial products to trigger immune responses in genetically susceptible individuals, creating a cycle of inflammation.

Environmental Triggers

Smoking (increases Crohn's risk, paradoxically protective in UC), antibiotic use, NSAIDs, stress, and dietary factors can trigger flares or contribute to disease development.

Functional Support for IBD

While medical management is essential, functional approaches can provide significant support:

  • Anti-inflammatory nutrition ‚Äî Mediterranean, SCD, or IBD-AID diets
  • Trigger identification ‚Äî Food journaling to identify personal triggers
  • Microbiome support ‚Äî Specific probiotics (VSL#3 has evidence in UC)
  • Nutrient repletion ‚Äî Addressing deficiencies (B12, iron, vitamin D)
  • Stress management ‚Äî Stress is a documented trigger for flares
  • Omega-3 fatty acids ‚Äî Anti-inflammatory support

Clinical Pearl

Many IBD patients also have overlapping IBS symptoms even when their IBD is in remission. Research suggests up to 30-40% of IBD patients have concurrent IBS-type symptoms that don't correlate with inflammatory markers. These "functional" symptoms are appropriate for our support.

What are the key differences between Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis in terms of location and pattern of inflammation?

How does our scope of practice differ when working with IBD clients versus IBS clients?

IBS: A functional disorder that is a primary focus for gut health practitioners. We can work independently on assessment, dietary interventions, lifestyle modifications, and supplement protocols.

IBD: An autoimmune/inflammatory disease that REQUIRES medical diagnosis and management. We SUPPORT clients with IBD but do not diagnose or treat the disease itself. We must work collaboratively with gastroenterologists, focusing on nutritional support, trigger identification, stress management, and addressing concurrent IBS-type symptoms that up to 30-40% of IBD patients experience.
3

What is fecal calprotectin and how is it clinically useful?

Fecal calprotectin is a protein released by neutrophils (immune cells) when there's intestinal inflammation. It's clinically useful because: 1) It helps distinguish IBD from IBS‚Äîelevated in IBD, normal in IBS. 2) It can be used to monitor IBD disease activity without invasive testing. 3) It can help predict flares before symptoms appear. 4) Normal levels suggest the client's symptoms are likely functional (IBS-type) rather than inflammatory‚Äîappropriate for practitioner support.

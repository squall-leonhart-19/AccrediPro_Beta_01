Module 2 Review

In this module, you've developed a comprehensive understanding of the major gut conditions you'll encounter in practice. Let's consolidate what you've learned and connect the dots between these conditions.

IBS (Lesson 2.1)

  • Rome IV criteria for diagnosis
  • Four subtypes: IBS-C, IBS-D, IBS-M, IBS-U
  • Multiple mechanisms: visceral hypersensitivity, altered motility, dysbiosis
  • Post-infectious IBS affects 10-20% of food poisoning survivors

SIBO/IMO (Lesson 2.2)

  • Bacterial overgrowth in the small intestine
  • Hydrogen = diarrhea; Methane = constipation; H2S = sulfur intolerance
  • MMC dysfunction is the primary cause
  • Breath testing for diagnosis; treat underlying cause

IBD (Lesson 2.3)

  • Crohn's vs. Ulcerative Colitis: location and pattern differ
  • Requires medical managementâ€”we provide support
  • Calprotectin distinguishes IBD from IBS
  • 30-40% have concurrent IBS symptoms

Upper GI/GERD (Lesson 2.4)

  • Low acid often causes reflux symptoms
  • PPIs can create downstream problems
  • H. pylori effects depend on infection location
  • Upper GI dysfunction cascades throughout system

Food Reactions (Lesson 2.5)

  • Allergies (IgE) vs. Sensitivities vs. Intolerances
  • Elimination diet is gold standard
  • Sensitivities often resolve with gut healing
  • IgG testing has significant limitations

Candida (Lesson 2.6)

  • Normal resident that overgrows under specific conditions
  • Antibiotics, sugar, immune compromise allow overgrowth
  • Biofilm makes treatment challenging
  • Die-off reactions require slow introduction

Parasites (Lesson 2.7)

  • More common than most realize
  • Protozoa more common than helminths
  • PCR testing more sensitive than standard O&P
  • Prescription treatment requires medical co-management

Connecting the Conditions

These conditions rarely exist in isolation. Understanding how they interconnect is crucial:

IBS and SIBO

Up to 60-80% of IBS patients have underlying SIBO. SIBO is often the mechanistic explanation for IBS symptoms. Always consider SIBO when working with IBS clients.

Low Acid â†’ SIBO â†’ IBS

Hypochlorhydria reduces the acid barrier, allowing bacteria to survive into the small intestine, contributing to SIBO, which manifests as IBS symptoms. This cascade is why PPIs can worsen gut health.

Food Sensitivities and Leaky Gut

Many conditions (SIBO, Candida, parasites) damage the gut lining, increasing permeability and food reactions. Heal the gut, and sensitivities often resolve.

Dysbiosis Creates Opportunity

When beneficial bacteria are depleted (antibiotics, poor diet, stress), opportunistic organisms like Candida and parasites have space to flourish. Restoring balance is preventive and therapeutic.

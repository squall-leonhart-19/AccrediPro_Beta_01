üî¨ Key Terms to Know

SIBO

Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth‚Äîexcessive bacteria in the small intestine where bacteria should be minimal.

Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)

The "cleansing wave" that sweeps bacteria and debris from the small intestine during fasting. Impaired MMC is a primary cause of SIBO.

IMO (Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth)

Overgrowth of methane-producing archaea, technically distinct from SIBO but often grouped with it. Causes constipation-predominant symptoms.

Breath Test

The primary diagnostic tool for SIBO, measuring hydrogen and methane gases produced by bacterial fermentation of lactulose or glucose.

What Is SIBO?

SIBO occurs when bacteria that normally live in the large intestine overpopulate the small intestine. The small intestine should have relatively few bacteria compared to the colon‚Äîless than 10^3 CFU/mL versus 10^11-12 CFU/mL in the colon.

When bacteria colonize the wrong location, they ferment food before it can be properly absorbed, causing:

  • Gas and bloating ‚Äî Often severe, worsening throughout the day
  • Malabsorption ‚Äî Bacteria consume nutrients meant for you
  • Intestinal permeability ‚Äî Bacterial byproducts damage the gut lining
  • Nutrient deficiencies ‚Äî B12, iron, and fat-soluble vitamins are commonly affected

Types of SIBO

Different organisms produce different gases with different symptoms:

Type Gas Produced Typical Symptoms Stool Pattern
Hydrogen SIBO Hydrogen (H2) Diarrhea, urgency, cramping, bloating Loose/watery (IBS-D pattern)
Methane/IMO Methane (CH4) Constipation, bloating, incomplete evacuation Hard, slow transit (IBS-C pattern)
Hydrogen Sulfide H2S Diarrhea, rotten egg gas, sulfur sensitivity Often loose, foul-smelling

Clinical Pearl

Methane-producing organisms are actually archaea, not bacteria‚Äîwhich is why the term "IMO" (Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth) is now preferred over "methane SIBO." This distinction matters because archaea respond differently to antimicrobial treatments than bacteria do.

What Causes SIBO?

SIBO is always a secondary condition‚Äîthere's always an underlying reason why bacteria have overgrown:

Impaired Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)

The MMC is the "housekeeper" that sweeps bacteria out of the small intestine during fasting. When damaged‚Äîoften after food poisoning or gastroenteritis‚Äîbacteria accumulate. This is the most common cause of SIBO and explains why 60% or more of IBS cases have underlying SIBO.

Structural Issues

Adhesions from surgery, small intestinal diverticula, strictures, or anatomical abnormalities create pockets where bacteria can hide and multiply. Post-surgical SIBO is common after abdominal surgeries.

Low Stomach Acid

Stomach acid is a barrier to bacterial entry. Long-term PPI use, H. pylori infection, aging, and chronic stress all reduce acid production, allowing bacteria to survive passage into the small intestine.

Ileocecal Valve Dysfunction

The ileocecal valve separates the small and large intestines, preventing backflow. When it's dysfunctional, colonic bacteria can migrate upward into the small intestine.

Who Gets SIBO?

High-risk populations include:

  • Post-food poisoning ‚Äî 10-20% develop post-infectious IBS/SIBO
  • IBS patients ‚Äî Up to 60-80% may have underlying SIBO
  • Hypothyroid/Hashimoto's ‚Äî Thyroid dysfunction slows gut motility
  • Long-term PPI users ‚Äî Reduced acid barrier
  • Diabetics ‚Äî Gastroparesis and autonomic neuropathy affect motility
  • Patients with scleroderma, Parkinson's, or EDS ‚Äî Motility is often impaired
  • Post-abdominal surgery ‚Äî Adhesions create bacterial reservoirs

Testing for SIBO

The primary diagnostic tool is the lactulose or glucose breath test:

How It Works

The patient drinks a sugar substrate (lactulose or glucose) after a prep diet. Breath samples are collected every 15-20 minutes for 2-3 hours. If bacteria are present in the small intestine, they'll ferment the sugar and produce hydrogen and/or methane that appears in the breath earlier than expected.

Important

Hydrogen sulfide SIBO is NOT detected by standard breath tests‚Äîit requires a newer test (trio-smart) that specifically measures H2S. If symptoms suggest hydrogen sulfide (sulfur intolerance, rotten egg odor) but standard tests are negative, consider specialized testing.

Interpreting Results

  • Positive hydrogen: Rise of ‚â•20 ppm above baseline within 90 minutes
  • Positive methane: ‚â•10 ppm at any point during the test
  • Combined positive: Often indicates more severe or complex case
  • False negatives occur ‚Äî Clinical picture should guide treatment even with negative tests

1

What is the difference between hydrogen SIBO and methane SIBO/IMO in terms of symptoms and organism type?

Hydrogen SIBO: Caused by bacteria producing hydrogen gas. Typically causes diarrhea, urgency, cramping, and loose/watery stools (IBS-D pattern).

Methane SIBO/IMO: Caused by archaea (not bacteria!) that produce methane. Typically causes constipation, slow transit, incomplete evacuation, and hard stools (IBS-C pattern). The term IMO (Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth) is now preferred because archaea are distinct from bacteria and respond differently to treatment.
2

What is the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC) and why is its dysfunction the most common cause of SIBO?

The MMC is the "housekeeper wave"‚Äîa cyclical pattern of electrical and muscular activity that sweeps the small intestine during fasting (between meals and overnight). It typically occurs every 90-120 minutes and pushes bacteria, debris, and remaining food toward the colon. When the MMC is damaged‚Äîoften after food poisoning, which can create autoimmune damage to gut nerves‚Äîbacteria aren't properly cleared and accumulate in the small intestine. This explains why 60%+ of IBS cases have underlying SIBO, and why PI-IBS often involves SIBO.
3

A client has severe bloating, constipation, and their symptoms improve on a very low-fiber diet but get worse with probiotics. What type of overgrowth would you suspect and why?

This presentation strongly suggests Methane SIBO/IMO. Key indicators: 1) Constipation (methane slows transit), 2) Symptoms worse with probiotics (adding more organisms feeds the problem), 3) Improving on low-fiber diet (reducing fermentable substrate). The improvement with dietary restriction and worsening with probiotics/fiber is a classic SIBO pattern, and constipation specifically points to methane-producing archaea. A breath test measuring both hydrogen and methane would confirm this, expecting elevated methane (‚â•10 ppm).

Understanding Scope of Practice

Your scope depends on your credentials and jurisdiction. Know what you can and cannot do legally.

General Guidelines

Certified practitioners: Education and recommendations. Licensed practitioners: May diagnose and prescribe. Always: Don't diagnose medical conditions or prescribe medications unless licensed. When in doubt: Refer out. Document what you're NOT doing as clearly as what you are.

Client Agreements

  • Informed consent: Explain what you do and don't do
  • Scope statement: Clear that you're not replacing medical care
  • Confidentiality: How you protect their information
  • Cancellation policy: Terms for missed appointments
  • Payment terms: How and when payment is expected
  • Refund policy: Clear terms to avoid disputes

Ethical Standards

  • Honesty: Don't promise results you can't deliver
  • Confidentiality: Protect client information always
  • Competence: Only work within your training
  • Boundaries: Maintain professional relationships
  • Referral: Refer when case is beyond your scope

When to Refer

  • Red flag symptoms (blood in stool, unexplained weight loss)
  • Suspected serious conditions (IBD, cancer)
  • Need for prescription medications
  • Mental health concerns beyond your training
  • No progress after reasonable intervention period

1. What should be included in client agreements?

Informed consent, scope statement (not replacing medical care), confidentiality terms, cancellation policy, payment terms, and refund policy. These protect both you and your clients and set clear expectations.

2. When should you refer a client to another provider?

Red flag symptoms (blood in stool, weight loss), suspected serious conditions, need for prescription medications, mental health concerns beyond your training, or no progress after reasonable intervention. When in doubt, refer out.

Histamine Intolerance vs MCAS

Histamine intolerance is about clearing histamine. MCAS is about overactive mast cells. They often overlap but have different root causes.

Key Differences

Histamine intolerance: Low DAO enzyme can't clear dietary histamine. MCAS: Mast cells release too many mediators (not just histamine). Both cause similar symptoms but treatment approaches differ.

Gut Connections

  • SIBO: Bacteria produce histamine; DAO depleted
  • Leaky gut: Food proteins trigger mast cells
  • Dysbiosis: Less histamine-degrading bacteria
  • Mold/toxins: Can trigger mast cell activation
  • Infections: Chronic infections activate mast cells

Dietary Interventions

  • Low-histamine diet: Remove aged, fermented, cured foods
  • Freshness matters: Histamine increases as food ages
  • Antihistamine foods: Quercetin-rich foods (onions, apples)
  • Duration: 2-4 weeks elimination, then reintroduce

Supplement Support

  • DAO enzyme: Take before meals with high-histamine foods
  • Quercetin: Natural mast cell stabilizer
  • Vitamin C: Helps degrade histamine
  • B vitamins: Cofactors for histamine clearance
  • Probiotics: Choose histamine-degrading strains (B. infantis, L. rhamnosus)

1. What is the difference between histamine intolerance and MCAS?

Histamine intolerance: Low DAO enzyme can't clear dietary histamine fast enough. MCAS: Mast cells are overactive and release too many mediators (histamine plus others). They often overlap but have different root causes and treatments.

2. Why is SIBO connected to histamine issues?

Bacteria in SIBO produce histamine as a byproduct. The inflammation and damage depletes DAO (the enzyme that breaks down histamine). Many people with SIBO develop histamine intolerance symptoms that resolve when SIBO is treated.

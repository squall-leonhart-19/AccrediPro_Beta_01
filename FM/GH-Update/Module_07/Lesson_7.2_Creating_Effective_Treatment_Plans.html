Treatment Plan

Structured document outlining recommended interventions, timeline, and goals.

Phased Protocol

Multi-stage treatment approach where interventions are introduced sequentially.

SMART Goals

Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound objectives.

Clinical Priorities

Ranking of interventions by importance and logical sequence.

Treatment Plan Components

An effective treatment plan is clear, actionable, and realistic. It serves as a roadmap for both you and your client.

1. Assessment Summary

Brief recap of key findings: main concerns, relevant history, test results, and suspected underlying issues. This helps the client see how you're connecting the dots.

2. Goals

Both short-term (within 30 days) and long-term (3-6 months). Make goals specific and measurable: "Reduce bloating after meals from 8/10 to 4/10" rather than "improve digestion."

3. Interventions

Categorized clearly: Diet changes, Supplements, Lifestyle modifications, Testing recommended. Be specific: "Low-FODMAP elimination phase for 4 weeks" not "eat healthier."

4. Timeline

When to start each intervention, duration, when to reassess. "Week 1-2: Foundation diet + digestive support. Week 3-6: Add antimicrobials. Week 7-8: Transition to restoration."

5. Follow-up Schedule

When and how you'll check in. Most clients need more support early in the process. Consider: 2-week check-in, 4-week full session, 8-week reassessment.

Prioritization Framework

What Comes First?

  • Foundations always first: Diet, sleep, stress before intensive protocols
  • Remove before restore: Address pathogens before rebuilding microbiome
  • Upstream before downstream: Fix digestion before worrying about specific nutrients
  • Client capacity: What can they actually implement right now?

The Rule of Three

Limit initial changes to 3 main interventions. Humans can manage 3 new things; 10 creates overwhelm and non-compliance. You can always add more later.

Phased Protocols for Complex Cases

Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

Diet modification. Basic digestive support. Lifestyle foundations (sleep, stress). This is where you see if foundations alone create improvement.

Phase 2: Treatment (Weeks 4-12)

Address specific issues based on testing/assessment. Antimicrobials if indicated. Gut healing support. More intensive interventions now that foundations are stable.

Phase 3: Restoration (Weeks 12-24)

Rebuild microbiome. Expand diet. Address any remaining issues. Transition to maintenance. Establish long-term prevention strategies.

Written Plans

Always provide a written plan clients can reference. Verbal instructions are forgotten within hours. Include: what to do, when to do it, why it matters, what to expect, and when to contact you. Simple bullet points work better than paragraphs.

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. What are the five components of a treatment plan?

1) Assessment Summary: Key findings from evaluation. 2) Goals: Short-term (30 days) and long-term (3-6 months), specific and measurable. 3) Interventions: Diet, supplements, lifestyle, testing. 4) Timeline: When to start, duration, reassessment points. 5) Follow-up Schedule: Check-in frequency and format.

2. What is the "Rule of Three" in treatment planning?

Limit initial changes to 3 main interventions. Humans can manage 3 new things; 10 creates overwhelm and non-compliance. Start with the highest-impact changes, establish those habits, then add more in subsequent phases. Less is often more in the beginning.

3. Why should foundations come before intensive protocols?

Foundations (diet, sleep, stress) often resolve issues without intensive interventions‚Äîsaving time and money. They also make intensive protocols more effective when needed. No supplement protocol overcomes chronic stress or poor diet. Phase 1 foundations also reveals what's needed in Phase 2.

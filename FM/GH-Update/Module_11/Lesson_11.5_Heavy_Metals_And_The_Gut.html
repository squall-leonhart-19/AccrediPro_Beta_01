Common Heavy Metal Exposures

Heavy metals are ubiquitous in modern life. Lead, mercury, arsenic, cadmium, and aluminum are the most clinically relevant.

Exposure Sources

Mercury: Fish, dental amalgams. Lead: Old paint, contaminated soil, some imported goods. Arsenic: Rice, drinking water, chicken. Cadmium: Cigarettes, contaminated food. Aluminum: Cookware, antiperspirants, antacids.

How Metals Affect the Gut

  • Dysbiosis: Metals alter microbiome composition
  • Leaky gut: Heavy metals damage tight junctions
  • Impaired motility: Especially mercury and lead
  • Candida overgrowth: Candida sequesters mercury as survival strategy
  • Nutrient depletion: Metals deplete zinc, selenium, glutathione

Safe Binding Strategies

  • Modified citrus pectin: Gentle binder for many metals
  • Chlorella: Binds mercury; start low to assess tolerance
  • Activated charcoal: Short-term use only; binds broadly
  • Fiber/clay: Bentonite clay, psyllium support elimination
  • Ensure regular elimination: Binders without bowel movements = redistribution

1. Why is Candida associated with mercury toxicity?

Candida can sequester mercury as a survival strategy‚Äîessentially binding it within its cell walls. This is why stubborn Candida cases often have an underlying mercury burden. Treating the metal is necessary for complete Candida resolution.

2. Why are regular bowel movements critical when using binders?

Binders without bowel movements = redistribution. Metals bound in the gut will be released again if they sit too long. This can cause worsening symptoms. Always ensure 1-2 daily bowel movements before and during binder use.

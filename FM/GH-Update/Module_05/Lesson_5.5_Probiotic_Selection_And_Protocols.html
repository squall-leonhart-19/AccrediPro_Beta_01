üî¨ Key Terms

CFU (Colony Forming Units)

Measure of live, viable organisms in a probiotic. Therapeutic doses typically 10-100 billion CFU.

Strain Specificity

Different strains of the same species have different effects. Benefits are strain-specific, not species-wide.

Spore-Based Probiotics

Shelf-stable, survives stomach acid, unique mode of action. Examples: Bacillus species.

Prebiotics

Non-digestible fibers that feed beneficial bacteria. Examples: PHGG, acacia fiber, GOS.

When to Use Probiotics

Probiotics are not a one-size-fits-all intervention. They're powerful tools when used appropriately, but can worsen symptoms in the wrong context.

Clear Indications

  • Post-antibiotic recovery: Replace beneficial species disrupted by antibiotics
  • Post-antimicrobial protocol: Rebalance after herbal SIBO/Candida treatment
  • Traveler's diarrhea prevention: S. boulardii specifically
  • IBS symptoms: Specific strains shown to help
  • C. diff prevention/treatment: S. boulardii has strong evidence
  • Low diversity on stool testing: General dysbiosis support

Caution in SIBO

Standard Lactobacillus/Bifidobacterium probiotics can worsen symptoms in active SIBO‚Äîyou're adding more bacteria to an already overgrown small intestine. Either treat SIBO first, or use spore-based probiotics (which don't colonize the small intestine) or S. boulardii (yeast, not bacteria) during treatment.

Key Probiotic Categories

Lactobacillus Species

L. rhamnosus GG: Best studied strain overall, immune support, diarrhea prevention. L. acidophilus: Supports vaginal health, lactose digestion. L. plantarum: Anti-inflammatory, IBS support. L. reuteri: Immune modulation, H. pylori support.

Bifidobacterium Species

B. longum: Reduces anxiety, supports mood-gut connection. B. infantis: Classic IBS strain (Align probiotic). B. lactis: Immune support, constipation relief. Generally colonize large intestine.

Saccharomyces boulardii

Therapeutic yeast (not bacteria). Won't worsen SIBO. Prevents and treats C. diff. Traveler's diarrhea prevention. Can be used during antibiotic courses. Doesn't require refrigeration. 250-500mg 1-2x daily.

Spore-Based Probiotics

Bacillus coagulans, B. subtilis, B. clausii. Survive stomach acid, don't colonize small intestine. May help SIBO recovery. Modulate immune response. Shelf-stable (no refrigeration needed).

Dosing Considerations

  • General maintenance: 10-30 billion CFU daily
  • Therapeutic protocols: 50-100+ billion CFU daily
  • Post-antibiotic: High-dose multi-strain for 1-3 months
  • Timing: Most taken with meals; some empty stomach‚Äîfollow product directions
  • Duration: Minimum 4-8 weeks to assess benefit; may need ongoing for some conditions

Prebiotic Support

Prebiotics feed beneficial bacteria already present, encouraging their growth.

PHGG (Partially Hydrolyzed Guar Gum)

Well-tolerated even in SIBO. Supports Bifidobacterium growth. Helps both diarrhea and constipation. Research specifically in SIBO patients. 5-7g daily.

Acacia Fiber

Gentle, well-tolerated prebiotic. Slow fermentation = less gas. Good for sensitive clients. 3-10g daily, titrate slowly.

GOS (Galacto-oligosaccharides)

Specifically feeds Bifidobacteria. May worsen symptoms in active SIBO (it's a high-FODMAP). Use after SIBO treatment for recovery. 2-5g daily.

Synbiotic Approach

Combining probiotics + prebiotics (synbiotic) may be more effective than either alone. The prebiotic "feeds" the probiotic strains, improving colonization. Many commercial products now combine both.

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. Why can standard probiotics worsen SIBO symptoms?

Standard Lactobacillus/Bifidobacterium probiotics add more bacteria to an already overgrown small intestine. This can worsen gas, bloating, and SIBO symptoms. Better options during active SIBO: spore-based probiotics (don't colonize small intestine), S. boulardii (yeast, not bacteria), or waiting until after antimicrobial treatment.

2. What makes S. boulardii unique among probiotics?

S. boulardii is a therapeutic yeast, not bacteria. This means it won't worsen SIBO, can be taken during antibiotic courses (antibiotics don't kill yeast), is excellent for C. diff prevention and treatment, prevents traveler's diarrhea, and doesn't require refrigeration. It has a different mechanism of action than bacterial probiotics.

3. Why is PHGG particularly useful in SIBO recovery?

PHGG (Partially Hydrolyzed Guar Gum) is well-tolerated even in SIBO patients‚Äîit's a prebiotic that doesn't cause the gas and bloating typical of other fibers. It has research specifically in SIBO patients, supports Bifidobacterium growth, and helps normalize both diarrhea and constipation. It's an ideal prebiotic for sensitive guts.

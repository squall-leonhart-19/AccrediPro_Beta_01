Betaine HCl

Supplemental hydrochloric acid to support stomach acid production when hypochlorhydria is suspected.

Digestive Enzymes

Supplements containing proteases, lipases, and amylases to aid food breakdown.

Ox Bile

Bile salt supplement supporting fat digestion, especially post-gallbladder removal.

HCl Challenge

Protocol to determine individual HCl dosing needs by gradually increasing until warmth is felt.

Stomach Acid (HCl) Support

Low stomach acid (hypochlorhydria) is extremely common yet often overlooked. It impairs protein digestion, mineral absorption, and creates conditions favorable to SIBO and infections.

Signs of Low Stomach Acid

  • Bloating, belching, or gas immediately after meals
  • Feeling of fullness for extended time after eating
  • Heartburn/reflux (often misdiagnosed as HIGH acid)
  • Low iron, B12, or ferritin despite supplementation
  • Undigested food in stool
  • Multiple food sensitivities

Betaine HCl Protocol

HCl Challenge Method

Start with 1 capsule (typically 500-650mg) with a protein-rich meal. If no warmth or discomfort in upper abdomen, increase by 1 capsule next meal. Continue until slight warmth is felt, then reduce by 1 capsule. This is the optimal dose. Most people need 1-5 capsules per protein-containing meal.

Contraindications

DO NOT use HCl with: active ulcers or gastritis, H. pylori infection (treat first), concurrent use of NSAIDs or corticosteroids, history of esophageal varices or bleeding. Always start with proper assessment and rule out contraindications.

Alternatives to HCl

  • Digestive bitters: Stimulate natural HCl production (gentian, ginger, artichoke)
  • Apple cider vinegar: 1 tbsp in water before meals (gentler approach)
  • Zinc: Required for HCl production; supplementation may improve natural production

Digestive Enzymes

Enzymes break down food into absorbable nutrients. While the body produces enzymes, supplementation can support impaired digestion.

Key Enzyme Types

Proteases: Break down proteins. Lipases: Break down fats. Amylases: Break down starches. Lactase: Breaks down lactose. Cellulase: Helps break down plant fibers. DPP-IV: Helps break down gluten and casein peptides.

When to Use Enzymes

  • Pancreatic insufficiency: Low elastase on stool test
  • Post-gallbladder removal: Especially lipase and ox bile
  • Bloating after meals: Even with adequate HCl
  • Visible undigested food in stool
  • Eating outside usual diet: Traveling, dining out

Dosing

  • Take enzymes at the START of meals (or within first few bites)
  • Choose broad-spectrum for general support
  • Higher lipase for fat intolerance
  • Higher protease for protein digestion issues

Bile Support

Bile emulsifies fats for digestion and is essential for fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K).

Signs of Bile Insufficiency

  • Light-colored or floating stools
  • Greasy, foul-smelling stools
  • Nausea with fatty foods
  • Pain under right rib cage
  • Low fat-soluble vitamins despite supplementation
  • History of gallbladder removal

Bile Support Options

Ox bile supplements: Direct bile salt replacement (125-500mg with fatty meals). Lipotropic factors: Support liver bile production (choline, inositol, methionine). Bitter herbs: Stimulate bile flow (dandelion, milk thistle, artichoke). Essential for post-cholecystectomy clients.

Comprehensive Digestive Protocol

For clients with multiple digestive issues, consider a combination product with HCl + enzymes + bile salts taken together at meals. This covers all bases without multiple separate supplements. Especially useful for older clients where all systems may be declining.

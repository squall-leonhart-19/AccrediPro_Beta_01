üî¨ Key Terms

Therapeutic Supplementation

Using supplements at doses designed to treat conditions, not just prevent deficiency.

Foundational Support

Basic supplements addressing deficiencies and supporting overall function before targeted protocols.

Protocol Stacking

Starting too many supplements simultaneously, making it impossible to identify what's working or causing reactions.

Third-Party Testing

Independent laboratory verification of supplement quality, potency, and purity (NSF, USP, ConsumerLab).

The Role of Supplements

Supplements are powerful tools that accelerate healing and address issues diet alone cannot resolve. However, they work best when diet and lifestyle foundations are in place‚Äîthey're not shortcuts around poor fundamentals.

Supplements Support, Not Replace

The best supplements in the world won't overcome a diet of processed foods and chronic stress. Think of supplements as the "accelerator"‚Äîthey work best when the vehicle (diet, lifestyle) is already heading in the right direction.

The Hierarchy of Intervention

Level 1: Foundations

Diet, sleep, stress management, movement. Address these first. No supplement replaces sleep or compensates for constant stress.

Level 2: Foundational Supplements

Address common deficiencies: vitamin D, omega-3s, magnesium, B vitamins. These support overall function and may be needed long-term.

Level 3: Digestive Support

Optimize digestion: stomach acid, enzymes, bile support. Can't absorb nutrients if digestion is impaired.

Level 4: Targeted Protocols

Address specific conditions: antimicrobials for SIBO, gut healing compounds for permeability, probiotics for dysbiosis. These are typically time-limited interventions.

Key Supplement Categories

  • Digestive Support: Betaine HCl, digestive enzymes, ox bile, bitters
  • Gut Healing: L-glutamine, zinc carnosine, collagen, slippery elm, DGL
  • Antimicrobials: Berberine, oregano oil, allicin, neem, caprylic acid
  • Biofilm Disruptors: NAC, enzymes, EDTA‚Äîbreak down pathogen protection
  • Probiotics: Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Saccharomyces boulardii, spore-based
  • Prebiotics: Partially hydrolyzed guar gum, acacia fiber, GOS
  • Motility Support: Ginger, prokinetics (Iberogast), magnesium

Scope of Practice

Supplement recommendations may be outside your scope depending on your credentials and location. Know your boundaries. When in doubt, recommend clients discuss supplements with their healthcare provider. Build referral relationships with integrative MDs, NDs, or functional medicine practitioners.

Quality Matters

Not all supplements are created equal. Poor quality supplements may contain:

  • Less active ingredient than labeled
  • Contaminants (heavy metals, pesticides)
  • Fillers and additives that cause reactions
  • Forms with poor bioavailability

Quality Indicators

  • Third-party testing: NSF, USP, ConsumerLab certification
  • Professional brands: Sold through practitioners, not mass retail
  • GMP certification: Good Manufacturing Practices facility
  • Transparent labeling: Lists all ingredients, sourcing info available

Building Your Formulary

Develop relationships with 2-3 professional-grade supplement companies. Know their products well rather than dabbling in many brands. This builds confidence in recommendations and simplifies protocols.

‚úì Check Your Understanding

1. Why should diet and lifestyle foundations come before supplements?

Supplements work best when fundamentals are in place‚Äîthey're accelerators, not shortcuts. No supplement overcomes poor diet, chronic stress, or inadequate sleep. Fixing foundations often resolves issues that would otherwise require ongoing supplementation. Supplements enhance a good protocol; they don't rescue a bad one.

2. What is "protocol stacking" and why is it problematic?

Protocol stacking is starting too many supplements simultaneously. It's problematic because: you can't identify what's working, you can't identify what's causing reactions, it's overwhelming for clients, it's expensive, and if something helps or hurts you don't know why. Better: introduce supplements sequentially, 1-2 at a time.

3. What indicates a quality supplement?

Quality indicators: Third-party testing (NSF, USP, ConsumerLab), professional-grade brands sold through practitioners, GMP-certified manufacturing facility, transparent labeling with all ingredients listed, and bioavailable forms of nutrients. Poor quality products may contain contaminants, less active ingredient than labeled, or poorly absorbed forms.

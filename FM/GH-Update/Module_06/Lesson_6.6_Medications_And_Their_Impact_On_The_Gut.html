üî¨ Key Terms

PPIs (Proton Pump Inhibitors)

Acid-blocking medications (omeprazole, pantoprazole) that reduce stomach acid production.

NSAIDs

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin) that damage gut lining.

Antibiotic Dysbiosis

Microbiome disruption caused by antibiotic use, potentially lasting months to years.

C. diff (Clostridioides difficile)

Dangerous bacterial infection that can occur after antibiotics disrupt protective bacteria.

Antibiotics and the Microbiome

Antibiotics are the most significant disruptors of gut health in modern medicine. While sometimes necessary, their effects on the microbiome can persist for months or even years.

Antibiotic Effects

A single course reduces microbiome diversity by 25-30%. Some species may not recover for 6-12 months or longer. Creates opportunity for pathogen overgrowth (C. diff, Candida, SIBO). Broad-spectrum antibiotics cause more damage than targeted options.

Minimizing Antibiotic Damage

  • During antibiotics: S. boulardii (yeast-based, not killed by antibiotics), spaced 2+ hours from antibiotic doses
  • After antibiotics: High-dose multi-strain probiotic for 1-3 months, prebiotic fiber to support recolonization
  • Diet: Extra fermented foods (if tolerated) and fiber diversity

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

PPI Effects on Gut Health

Reduce stomach acid by 90%+. Impaired protein digestion and mineral absorption (B12, iron, calcium, magnesium). SIBO risk increases 2-3x (low acid allows bacteria to survive). C. diff and other infection risk increases. Designed for short-term use (4-8 weeks) but often taken for years.

Scope Reminder

NEVER advise clients to stop prescribed medications. If you suspect PPI overuse, recommend they discuss with their prescriber. Your role is to support gut health alongside medications and to provide information clients can discuss with their medical team.

Supporting Clients on PPIs

  • Digestive enzymes with meals (since acid is reduced)
  • Monitor for B12, iron, and magnesium deficiency
  • Be aware of increased SIBO risk
  • If appropriate, discuss with prescriber about stepping down

NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Aspirin)

NSAID Effects

Inhibit prostaglandins that protect stomach and intestinal lining. Increase intestinal permeability within hours of use. Contribute to ulcers and GI bleeding. Regular use strongly linked to leaky gut. Even "enteric coated" damages small intestine.

Alternatives and Support

  • Natural anti-inflammatories: curcumin, omega-3s, ginger
  • If NSAIDs needed: take with food, use short-term, avoid with alcohol
  • Gut healing support if chronic NSAID use: L-glutamine, zinc carnosine

Other Medications Affecting the Gut

  • Oral contraceptives: Alter microbiome, may increase intestinal permeability
  • Metformin: GI side effects common; actually may have some prebiotic effects
  • Opioids: Severe constipation, slowed motility, MMC suppression
  • Antidepressants: Various GI effects; can affect motility
  • Antacids: Less severe than PPIs but still reduce protective acid

Medication History

Always take a thorough medication history including: current prescriptions, OTC medications (especially NSAIDs, antacids), antibiotic history (how many courses, when), and supplements. Many gut issues trace back to medication use.

2. Why do PPIs increase SIBO risk?

PPIs reduce stomach acid by 90%+. Normally, stomach acid kills most bacteria that enter with food. When acid is suppressed, more bacteria survive and can colonize the small intestine. SIBO risk increases 2-3x with PPI use. PPIs were designed for short-term use but are often taken for years.

3. How do NSAIDs damage the gut?

NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandins that normally protect the stomach and intestinal lining. This increases intestinal permeability within hours of use, contributes to ulcers and GI bleeding, and is strongly linked to leaky gut. Even "enteric coated" versions damage the small intestine. Regular use is particularly problematic.

LESSON 1.1

THE "SICK CARE" CRISIS

"Your 20 years of life experience isn't 'baggage.' It is your greatest unfair advantage."

Hi, I'm Sarah.

And if you're reading this right now, I'm going to guess something about you.

I'm going to guess that you're tired. Not just "didn't get enough sleep" tired. I mean soul-tired.

Maybe you've been working in the medical system for 15, 20, even 25 years. You went into nursing—or medicine, or therapy—because you genuinely wanted to help people. You had this vision of what healing looked like. You imagined having time with patients. Listening to them. Understanding their stories. Making a real difference in their lives.

But somewhere along the way, that vision got crushed.

Instead of healing, you found yourself managing. Managing symptoms. Managing paperwork. Managing impossible patient loads and unrealistic expectations from administrators who have never touched a patient in their lives.

You started to notice something that broke your heart: Your patients weren't getting better.

They'd come in with fatigue, and you'd run labs. "Normal." They'd come in with brain fog, digestive issues, anxiety, weight they couldn't lose. And the answer was always the same: another prescription. Another 7-minute appointment. Another band-aid on a wound that needed surgery.

You saw the same faces, month after month, year after year. And you started to feel something you never expected to feel in a "helping profession":

Moral Injury

That's the clinical term for what happens when you're forced to act against your own values. When you KNOW there's a better way, but the system won't let you pursue it. When you're complicit in a healthcare model that's failing the very people you swore to help.

If any of this resonates with you—if you've ever sat in your car after a shift and wondered "Is this really it?"—then this lesson is going to change everything.

The State of American Health

Let me share some numbers with you. These aren't opinions—they're facts.

  • 60% of Americans have at least one chronic disease
  • 40% have two or more chronic conditions
  • $4.1 trillion is spent annually on healthcare—yet outcomes are getting worse
  • The United States ranks last among developed nations in healthcare outcomes
  • Average doctor visit: 7 minutes
  • Average time to get diagnosed with autoimmune disease: 4.5 years and 5 doctors

Read those numbers again. We spend more money on healthcare than any country in the world, and we are LAST in outcomes. We are sicker than ever before.

Why?

Because modern medicine was designed for acute care—not chronic disease.

If you break your leg, go to the ER. They are brilliant at that. If you're having a heart attack, they will save your life. Modern medicine excels at crisis intervention.

But for the 60% of Americans dealing with chronic illness? For the women suffering from:

  • • Fatigue that sleep doesn't fix
  • • Brain fog that makes them feel like they're losing their minds
  • • Digestive issues that have no clear diagnosis
  • • Hormonal chaos—PMS, perimenopause, irregular cycles
  • • Weight that won't budge no matter what they try
  • • Anxiety and depression that antidepressants don't fully address

For these women, the system has nothing to offer but symptom suppression.

"Here's a pill for the pain."
"Here's something for the anxiety."
"Your labs are normal—it's probably stress."

This is not healthcare. This is "Sick Care."

Sick Care doesn't ask why you're unwell. It only asks what you're feeling—so it can prescribe something to suppress that feeling.

The underlying cause? Still there. Growing. Festering. Until the next symptom appears. And then the next prescription.

This is why your patients aren't getting better. This is why YOU feel depleted. Because you're trapped in a system that was never designed to heal.

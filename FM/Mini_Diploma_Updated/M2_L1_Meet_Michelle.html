LESSON 2.1

MEET MICHELLE

"I wasn't healing anyone. I was just processing them. Scan the barcode. Give the med. Move on."

In this module, I want you to meet someone.

Her name is Michelle. She's 48 years old. She's been a registered nurse for 22 years—working in med-surge, then oncology, then eventually a busy outpatient clinic.

On paper, Michelle's life looks like a success story. She has the stable career. The respect of her colleagues. The pension she's been building for two decades. The letters "RN" after her name that she worked so hard to earn.

But Michelle has a secret. Something she doesn't talk about at work. Something she barely admits to herself.

She's miserable.

And if you're reading this, there's a good chance her story is going to sound uncomfortably familiar.

THE SUNDAY NIGHT DREAD

It starts around 4pm on Sunday.

Michelle is sitting on her couch. Maybe she's with her family, watching a movie. Maybe she's alone, scrolling her phone. Either way, she's not really present—because her stomach is starting to knot.

She looks at the clock. 4:17pm. And the thought crashes into her consciousness like it does every week:

"I have to go back there."

The dread is physical. She can feel it in her chest. A tightness that won't release. A heaviness that settles over the rest of her evening, making it impossible to fully relax, fully enjoy, fully be present with the people she loves.

By Sunday night, she's already exhausted—and she hasn't even started her week yet.

Inside Michelle's Mind

"I used to love nursing. I really did. When I was 26, fresh out of school, I thought I was going to change the world one patient at a time. I actually believed that.

"But now? Now I see 30 patients a shift. I have 7 minutes—sometimes 5—per person. I barely have time to review their chart before I'm in the room. I definitely don't have time to LISTEN to them.

"I scan their wristband. Give the med. Document that I gave the med. Move on.

"I'm not a nurse anymore. I'm a human barcode scanner with a medication cart."

Can you feel that? That knot in your stomach? That's not just empathy for Michelle. That might be recognition.

If you've ever:

  • • Sat in your car after a shift, staring at the steering wheel
  • • Cried in a supply closet because you couldn't give a patient the care they deserved
  • • Lain awake at 2am, scrolling job listings, wondering if there's anything else

You are not alone. And you are not broken.

You are experiencing the natural consequence of being a good person trapped in a bad system.

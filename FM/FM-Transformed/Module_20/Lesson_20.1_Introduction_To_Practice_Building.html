The Business Mindset Shift

Many coaches resist the business side of practice. They didn't get into this work to "sell" or "market." Yet without embracing business skills, even the most gifted coaches struggle to sustain their work.

The truth is, business activities are extensions of your service, not separate from it. Let's reframe the key business functions:

Limited View
  • Marketing = Self-promotion
  • Sales = Manipulation
  • Pricing = What others charge
  • Systems = Bureaucracy
Service View
  • Marketing = Reaching people who need you
  • Sales = Connecting solutions to problems
  • Pricing = Valuing your expertise
  • Systems = Consistent excellence
Coach Tip

When you feel resistance to business activities, ask yourself: "How does this activity ultimately serve my clients?" Marketing lets people discover you. Sales helps them make good decisions. Pricing enables sustainable service. Systems create professional experiences. Reframe business as the container that holds your service.

The most successful coaches view business activities as an extension of their service, not separate from it. Marketing isn't manipulation‚Äîit's education. Selling isn't pushing‚Äîit's helping people make good decisions for their health.

Practice Model Options

Before diving into tactics, consider the practice structure that best fits your goals, lifestyle, and strengths. Each model has distinct advantages and challenges:

1

Solo Private Practice

Working independently with your own clients

2

Working Within a Practice

Employed by or contracting with a functional medicine practice

3

Group/Hybrid Model

Combining individual coaching with group programs

4

Corporate/Organizational

Providing coaching through companies or organizations

Solo Private Practice: Working Independently

Advantages
  • Complete control over approach, schedule, pricing
  • Keep 100% of revenue (minus expenses)
  • Build equity in your own brand
  • Flexibility to design your ideal practice
Challenges
  • Responsible for all business functions
  • Must build client base from scratch
  • Can be isolating without colleagues
  • Income directly tied to your hours worked
Best For

Self-starters, experienced coaches, those wanting maximum control and willing to handle business aspects independently.

Working Within a Practice: Employed or Contract

Advantages
  • Clients referred to you‚Äîless marketing required
  • Collaborate with practitioners and learn from team
  • Mentorship and supervision often available
  • Focus on coaching, less on business operations
Challenges
  • Lower income (split revenue or receive salary)
  • Less control over approach and client selection
  • May need to follow practice protocols
  • Building someone else's brand, not your own
Best For

New coaches, those wanting mentorship, coaches preferring clinical focus over business responsibilities.

Coach Tip

Many successful coaches start within a practice to gain experience and confidence, then transition to solo practice after 1-2 years. This hybrid path provides paid apprenticeship while you develop business skills on the side. Don't feel you must choose one path forever‚Äîyour practice can evolve.

Group/Hybrid Model: Leveraging Your Time

Advantages
  • Leverage time‚Äîserve more people efficiently
  • Create community among clients
  • Multiple income streams
  • Scale impact beyond 1:1 capacity
Challenges
  • More complex to design and deliver
  • Requires larger audience to fill groups
  • Different skills than individual coaching
  • Technology and systems requirements
Best For

Experienced coaches, those wanting to scale, coaches who enjoy teaching and group facilitation.

Corporate/Organizational Wellness: B2B Coaching

Advantages
  • Consistent revenue through contracts
  • Bulk client access‚Äîone relationship brings many clients
  • Often higher hourly rates through B2B contracts
  • Less individual marketing required
Challenges
  • May work with less motivated participants
  • Contract-based (can lose large income at once)
  • Often requires proposals, bids, sales processes
  • May have less coaching autonomy
Best For

Coaches comfortable with B2B sales, those wanting stable contracts, former corporate professionals.

Designing Your Ideal Practice

Before building, get clear on what you're building toward. Your practice should serve your life goals, not just create another job. The following questions will help you design a practice aligned with your values, lifestyle, and definition of success.

Visioning Questions to Guide Your Practice Design

üè° Lifestyle

How many hours per week do you want to work? What does your ideal work schedule look like? Do you want to work from home, office, or combination? How much flexibility do you need?

üí∞ Income

What annual income do you need to sustain your life? What income would feel abundant and rewarding? Is coaching supplementing other income or your primary source?

üéØ Impact

Who do you most want to help? What transformation do you want to create? Do you want to serve many people broadly or fewer people deeply?

‚ö° Energy

What activities energize you vs. drain you? Do you prefer 1:1 intimacy or group energy? How much marketing/business work are you willing to do?

Pause & Reflect

Take a moment to journal your answers to these questions. There are no right or wrong answers‚Äîonly your answers. Your practice design should emerge from your authentic vision, not from what you think you "should" want.

Practice Math: Making the Numbers Work

Once you know your income goal, work backward to understand what that requires. Here's a practical example:

$80,000

Weekly Revenue Needed: $1,740/week

Key Insight

The hybrid model requires fewer total clients because group work leverages your time. However, it requires more marketing reach to fill groups consistently.

Coach Tip

Run your own practice math before setting prices or capacity goals. Many coaches set arbitrary prices without calculating what income they'll actually generate. Start with your income need, then work backward to determine pricing, client load, and service mix. This ensures your practice can actually sustain you.

Common Practice-Building Mistakes

Learn from others' expensive lessons. Here are the five most common mistakes that sabotage otherwise talented coaches:

‚ö†Ô∏è Mistake 1: Underpricing

What Happens

Coaches set low prices thinking it will attract clients. Instead, they attract price-shoppers, devalue their service, burn out from overwork, and can't sustain their practice.

Better Approach

Price based on value and sustainability. Higher prices often attract more committed clients who get better results. Your pricing should reflect the transformation you create, not hourly wage thinking.

‚ö†Ô∏è Mistake 2: Being Too General

What Happens

Coaches try to help everyone with everything, making it impossible for anyone to identify them as the solution to their specific problem. Generic messaging attracts no one.

Better Approach

Niche down. Being known as "the gut health coach for busy moms" is more effective than being a "general wellness coach." Specificity creates clarity and trust.

‚ö†Ô∏è Mistake 3: Inconsistent Visibility

What Happens

Coaches market intensely when they need clients, then disappear for weeks or months once they're busy. They never build momentum or stay top-of-mind, creating feast-or-famine cycles.

Better Approach

Consistent, sustainable marketing. Small daily or weekly activities compound over time. Regular presence builds trust and recognition even when you're not actively enrolling.

‚ö†Ô∏è Mistake 4: No Systems

What Happens

Every client interaction is reinvented from scratch, creating chaos, inconsistency, and massive time drain on administrative tasks. Professional credibility suffers.

Better Approach

Create systems for scheduling, onboarding, session structure, payments, and follow-up that scale with your practice. Templates and automation free you to focus on coaching.

‚ö†Ô∏è Mistake 5: Isolation

What Happens

Coaches work alone, missing mentorship, referrals, support, and accountability that accelerate success. They reinvent every wheel and miss opportunities for collaboration.

Better Approach

Build a support network: mentor/supervisor, peer group, complementary providers, professional communities. Connection accelerates learning and growth.

Critical Insight

Notice that these mistakes aren't about clinical skills‚Äîthey're about business fundamentals. Most coaching practices fail not because the coach isn't good at coaching, but because they haven't learned to run a sustainable business.

Coach Tip

Review this mistake list with brutal honesty: Which ones are you already making or at risk of making? Self-awareness is the first step to course correction. Consider finding an accountability partner‚Äîanother coach building their practice‚Äîto check in with weekly about avoiding these common pitfalls.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

How should health coaches reframe "marketing" from a service perspective?

Answer: Marketing should be reframed as reaching people who need you‚Äîeducation and connection, not manipulation. You can't help people who don't know you exist. Marketing is the bridge between your expertise and those who need it.

2

What is the main advantage of working within an established functional medicine practice vs. solo practice?

Answer: The main advantages are built-in client referrals (less marketing burden), mentorship and collaboration with experienced practitioners, and the ability to focus on coaching rather than business operations. This makes it ideal for new coaches building experience and confidence.

3

What are the five common practice-building mistakes covered in this lesson?

Answer: The five common mistakes are: (1) Underpricing services and attracting price-shoppers, (2) Being too general rather than niching down, (3) Inconsistent visibility creating feast-or-famine cycles, (4) No systems leading to chaos and inefficiency, and (5) Isolation missing mentorship and support opportunities.

4

Before designing your practice, what four key areas should you assess about your personal goals and preferences?

Answer: The four key areas are: (1) Lifestyle‚Äîdesired work hours, schedule, and flexibility, (2) Income‚Äîfinancial needs and goals, (3) Impact‚Äîwho you want to help and what transformation you want to create, and (4) Energy‚Äîwhat activities energize vs. drain you. Your practice design should align with all four areas.

Key Takeaways
  • Clinical excellence requires business skills to create lasting impact and sustainable income
  • Reframe business activities as extensions of service‚Äîmarketing reaches people who need you, pricing enables sustainability
  • Choose a practice model (solo, within practice, hybrid, corporate) that aligns with your lifestyle, income, and impact goals
  • Design your practice based on your life vision, not default assumptions‚Äîassess lifestyle, income, impact, and energy preferences first
  • Avoid common mistakes: underpricing, being too general, inconsistent marketing, no systems, isolation
  • Practice building is a learnable skill‚Äîstart where you are with honest self-assessment

The Purpose of Consultation Calls

A consultation call serves both parties in distinct but complementary ways. Understanding these dual purposes transforms how you approach each conversation.

For the Prospect
  • Get clarity on their situation and what's possible
  • Experience your coaching style and presence
  • Understand exactly what you offer and how it could help
  • Make an informed decision about investing in coaching
For You
  • Assess if this person is a good fit for your coaching
  • Understand their specific needs to tailor your approach
  • Demonstrate your expertise and build trust
  • Invite qualified prospects into your program
Coach Tip

Before each consultation call, set your mindset with this question: "How can I serve this person?" This single shift‚Äîfrom selling to serving‚Äîwill be felt by your prospect and dramatically improve the quality of your conversations. Remember: your goal is to help them make the right decision, not necessarily to get a "yes."

The Consultation Call Structure

A typical consultation call runs 30-45 minutes and follows a clear four-phase structure. Each phase serves a specific purpose and builds naturally to the next.

1

Opening and Rapport (3-5 minutes)

Purpose: Create connection, set expectations, establish flow

What to say: "Thank you for taking the time to connect. I'm looking forward to learning more about you. Here's how I typically structure these calls: I'll ask you some questions to understand your situation, share a bit about how I might be able to help, and then we can decide together if it makes sense to work together. Sound good?"

2

Discovery Questions (15-20 minutes)

Purpose: Understand their situation deeply; let them feel heard

Key questions: "What's going on with your health right now that made you reach out? How long has this been going on? What have you already tried? What's the impact of this on your daily life? What would it mean for you to resolve this?"

3

Bridging and Presenting (5-10 minutes)

Purpose: Connect their situation to your solution

What to say: "So if I'm hearing you correctly, you're dealing with [X, Y, Z], it's impacting [specific areas], and you're looking for [outcome]. This is exactly the kind of situation I help with. Based on what you've shared, here's what I think would be most helpful..."

4

Invitation and Close (5-10 minutes)

Purpose: Invite decision, address concerns, establish next steps

What to say: "Based on what we've discussed, does this feel like the right fit for you?" Then listen to any hesitations and address them honestly. If yes, walk through enrollment. If maybe, explore what they need. If no, offer referrals gracefully.

Coach Tip

Time management matters. If you find yourself running long on discovery questions, gently transition: "I want to make sure we have time to discuss how I can help. Let me share what I'm thinking, and then we can explore any questions you have." Respect both your time and theirs by honoring the structure.

Mastering Discovery Questions

The quality of your questions determines the quality of the call. Great questions help prospects understand their own situation more clearly‚Äîcreating the foundation for informed decision-making.

Questions That Create Clarity

üîç Problem Questions

"What's the biggest health challenge you're facing right now?" "How is this affecting your daily life?" "When did you first notice these symptoms?"

üìö History Questions

"What have you already tried?" "What worked? What didn't?" "Have you worked with other practitioners or coaches?" "What did you learn from those experiences?"

üí• Impact Questions

"What does this cost you‚Äîin terms of energy, relationships, career, joy?" "How is this affecting your family?" "What activities have you stopped doing because of this?"

üåü Vision Questions

"If you solved this, what would your life look like?" "What would you be able to do that you can't do now?" "What does success look like to you six months from now?"

‚öñÔ∏è Commitment Questions

"How important is solving this to you?" "On a scale of 1-10, how ready are you to make changes?" "What happens if nothing changes?"

‚è∞ Timing Questions

"Why now? What made you decide to explore coaching at this point?" "What would need to happen for you to feel confident moving forward?"

Critical Insight

Listen more than you talk. Aim for a 70/30 ratio‚Äî70% them talking, 30% you. When prospects feel truly heard, they become more receptive to your recommendations. Resist the urge to jump in with solutions before fully understanding their situation.

Presenting Your Offer

After discovery, present your offer as the solution to their specific situation. The key is bridging‚Äîconnecting what they've told you to what you offer.

The Bridge Statement

Template: "Based on what you've shared‚Äî[summarize their situation in their own words]‚Äîhere's what I recommend..."

This demonstrates you listened and tailors your solution to their needs. It's not a generic pitch‚Äîit's a personalized recommendation.

üéØ Presenting with Confidence

Focus on Outcomes

"In my 6-month program, you'll develop a personalized nutrition plan, implement stress management practices, and get to the root of your fatigue‚Äîso you can have the energy you need for your life."

Include Specifics

"We'll meet twice a month for 60-minute sessions, you'll have email access between sessions, and I'll provide resources tailored to your situation."

State Investment Clearly

"The investment is $3,000 for the complete program. I offer a payment plan of $525/month if that's helpful."

Pause After Price

Let them respond. Don't fill the silence. This is their moment to process and respond authentically.

Coach Tip

Practice stating your prices out loud until you can do so without hesitation, apology, or awkwardness. Try this: Stand in front of a mirror and say, "The investment for this program is $X" with confidence and a smile. Repeat until it feels natural. Your comfort with pricing directly influences how prospects receive it.

Handling Objections

Objections aren't rejection‚Äîthey're requests for more information or support in making a decision. Each common objection has a thoughtful response that honors both your expertise and their concerns.

üí≠

"I need to think about it."

Respond: "Absolutely‚Äîthis is an important decision. Can I ask what specifically you want to think through? Sometimes talking it out helps."

Often there's an underlying concern. If they genuinely need time, set a specific follow-up: "When would be a good time for us to reconnect? I'm happy to answer any questions that come up."

üí∞

"I can't afford it."

Respond: "I understand‚Äîinvestment is a real consideration. Can you tell me more about what's making this feel out of reach?"

Explore: Is it truly budget, or is it priority? Would a payment plan help? Is there a smaller offering that would work? If genuinely not feasible, accept gracefully and offer alternative resources.

üë•

"I need to talk to my spouse/partner."

Respond: "Of course‚Äîthis affects both of you. What do you think their concerns might be?"

Help them prepare for the conversation. Offer to send materials they can share. Set a specific follow-up time. Sometimes offering a brief call with both partners can resolve questions efficiently.

‚è∞

"I don't have time."

Respond: "I hear that‚Äîyou're already stretched thin. Can I ask: how much time are you currently losing to low energy, brain fog, or health issues?"

Help them see that investing time now may free up time later. The coaching time is finite; the health issues are ongoing. Sometimes we need to invest time to gain time.

Reframe Objections

When you hear an objection, mentally reframe it as: "What does this person need to hear to feel safe making this decision?" This shifts you from defensive to supportive‚Äîand helps you address their real concern, not just their stated objection.

The Enrollment Process

When someone says yes, have a smooth enrollment process ready. The momentum of their decision deserves professional follow-through.

Enrollment Steps

1. Confirm Commitment

"Wonderful! I'm excited to work with you. Let me walk you through getting started." Validate their decision and transition smoothly to logistics.

2. Send Agreement/Contract

Use electronic signature for convenience (send within 24 hours of call). Include clear terms, cancellation policy, and program outline.

3. Collect Payment

Have payment processing ready (Stripe, Square, PayPal, etc.). Make it easy and professional.

4. Welcome Email

Send immediately upon enrollment with next steps, what to expect, and timeline for onboarding materials.

5. Schedule First Session

Get the kickoff appointment on the calendar. This creates immediate forward momentum.

6. Send Onboarding Materials

Intake forms, scheduling info, program overview, what to prepare before your first session.

Coach Tip

Create a new client enrollment checklist that you follow every time. This ensures no steps are missed and creates a consistent, professional experience. Include: contract sent, payment received, welcome email sent, first session scheduled, intake forms sent, client added to CRM/tracking system. Consistency builds trust and sets the tone for your coaching relationship.

When Someone Isn't a Good Fit

Not everyone should become your client. Saying no protects both of you and maintains the integrity of your practice.

Signs Someone Isn't a Good Fit
  • Their needs are outside your scope (medical treatment, mental health crisis)
  • They want something you don't offer
  • They show signs of being difficult to work with (disrespectful, unrealistic expectations)
  • Their commitment level seems low
  • Your gut says no
How to Decline Gracefully

"Based on what you've shared, I don't think I'm the right fit for what you need. [Reason if appropriate]. I want to make sure you get the right support. Let me suggest [alternative resource/referral]."

Declining with kindness and offering alternatives maintains your reputation and demonstrates your commitment to their wellbeing over your business interests.

Consultation Call Best Practices

üéØ Before the Call

Review Materials

Review any intake information they provided. Know their name, what prompted them to reach out, and any preliminary details.

Prepare Logistics

Have your pricing and logistics at hand. Don't fumble for information during the call.

Create Space

Be present, not rushed. Turn off notifications. Close other tabs. Give them your full attention.

Set Mindset

"How can I serve this person?" This question centers you in service rather than anxiety.

üí¨ During the Call

Listen More

Aim for 70/30 ratio‚Äî70% them talking, 30% you. Resist the urge to fill every silence.

Take Notes

Take notes to demonstrate engagement. It also helps you remember details for presenting your offer.

Reflect Back

Periodically summarize what you're hearing: "So what I'm hearing is..." This builds trust and clarity.

Be Honest

Be honest about what you can and can't help with. Overpromising damages trust and leads to poor outcomes.

‚úÖ After the Call

üìß Send Follow-Up

Send follow-up email summarizing conversation and next steps within 24 hours. This keeps momentum and demonstrates professionalism.

üìã Process Enrollment

If they enrolled, send materials promptly. Get first session scheduled immediately.

üìÖ Confirm Follow-Up

If they're deciding, confirm follow-up date. Put it in your calendar with reminder.

üìä Track Patterns

Track your calls: Who converted? What patterns emerge? What objections come up most? Use data to improve.

What are the four phases of an effective consultation call, and approximately how long should each phase take?

Answer: The four phases are: (1) Opening and Rapport (3-5 minutes) - create connection and set expectations; (2) Discovery Questions (15-20 minutes) - understand their situation deeply; (3) Bridging and Presenting (5-10 minutes) - connect their situation to your solution; (4) Invitation and Close (5-10 minutes) - invite decision and address concerns. Total call time: 30-45 minutes.

What are the five types of discovery questions, and why is each important?

Answer: The five types are: (1) Problem Questions - identify current challenges; (2) History Questions - understand what they've tried; (3) Impact Questions - reveal the cost of their situation; (4) Vision Questions - clarify desired outcomes; (5) Commitment Questions - assess readiness to change. Together, these questions create comprehensive understanding and help prospects gain clarity about their own situation.

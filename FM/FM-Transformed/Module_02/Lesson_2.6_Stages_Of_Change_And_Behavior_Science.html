Case Study: Sarah's Resistance

When Good Advice Falls Flat

üë©

Sarah, Age 52

Pre-diabetes ‚Ä¢ High blood pressure ‚Ä¢ 40 pounds overweight ‚Ä¢ Sedentary lifestyle

Sarah's physician referred her to a health coach after her latest labs showed worsening blood sugar. The coach, eager to help, immediately launched into creating a meal plan and exercise schedule. Sarah nodded politely, took the papers, and never returned.

What went wrong? The coach missed a critical assessment: Sarah wasn't ready for action. She was still in contemplation‚Äîaware something needed to change but deeply ambivalent about it. The action plan felt overwhelming and threatening, triggering resistance rather than motivation.

Three months later, Sarah worked with a different coach who started differently: "Tell me what concerns you most about your health right now." This coach met Sarah where she was, helping her explore her ambivalence rather than pushing past it. Within six weeks, Sarah was ready to take her first small steps‚Äîbut only because the coach had matched the intervention to her stage.

Sarah's story illustrates the central lesson of this module: effective coaching isn't about what you know or what strategies you offer‚Äîit's about timing and readiness. The same intervention that fails spectacularly at one stage can work beautifully at another.

The Transtheoretical Model

In the 1980s, psychologists James Prochaska and Carlo DiClemente studied how people overcome addictions‚Äîboth with professional help and on their own. They discovered something surprising:

Change isn't a single event‚Äîit's a process that unfolds in predictable stages.

Their Transtheoretical Model (also called the Stages of Change Model) explains why:

  • Some people aren't interested in hearing about solutions
  • Others are curious but not ready to commit
  • Still others are actively working on change but struggling with consistency

The model identifies six distinct stages. Each requires different coaching strategies. Trying to push someone into action before they're ready backfires‚Äîtriggering resistance and undermining the coaching relationship.

The Core Principle

Effective coaching matches the intervention to the client's current stage. Your job isn't to move people from wherever they are straight to "action"‚Äîit's to help them progress one stage at a time.

Coach Tip

Think of the stages like a staircase. You can't skip steps. If someone is on step two (contemplation), your job is to help them get to step three (preparation), not to teleport them to step five (maintenance). Meet them where they are, help them take the next step, and trust the process.

The Six Stages of Change

Let's explore each stage in depth, understanding both what's happening for the client and what your role as coach should be.

1

Precontemplation: "I Don't Have a Problem"

The person doesn't see a need to change. They may be unaware of consequences, in denial about the severity, or demoralized by past failures. They're not thinking about change and may resist if pushed.

What they're thinking: "My health is fine." "That won't happen to me." "I've tried before and failed‚Äîwhy bother?"

2

Contemplation: "I Might Need to Change... Someday"

The person is aware there's an issue and thinking about change, but hasn't committed. They're weighing pros and cons, often for months or years. Ambivalence is high‚Äîthey simultaneously want to change and want to stay the same.

What they're thinking: "I know I should exercise, but I'm so busy." "Eating better would help, but I don't want to give up foods I love."

3

Preparation: "I'm Going to Change Soon"

The person has decided to take action within the next month. They're making plans, gathering resources, and testing small steps. Commitment is forming. This is the bridge between thinking and doing.

What they're thinking: "I'm joining a gym next week." "I bought a cookbook and I'm ready to start." "I've cleared my schedule for morning walks."

4

Action: "I'm Doing It!"

The person is actively modifying behavior‚Äîeating differently, exercising, managing stress. This stage requires significant time, energy, and attention. Visible progress happens here, which is both motivating and demanding.

What they're thinking: "I'm cooking healthy meals most nights." "I've exercised four times this week." "I'm practicing stress management daily."

5

Maintenance: "I'm Sustaining the Change"

The person has maintained new behaviors for at least 6 months. The focus shifts from starting behaviors to preventing relapse and integrating changes into daily life. The behavior requires less conscious effort but still needs attention.

What they're thinking: "This is just what I do now." "I can't imagine going back to how I used to eat." "I have systems in place that work."

6

Termination: "This Is Just Who I Am Now"

The new behavior is fully integrated. There's no temptation to return to old patterns. The change has become automatic‚Äîpart of identity rather than effort. Note: Not everyone reaches this stage for every behavior.

What they're thinking: "I don't even think about it anymore‚Äîit's just who I am." "The old behavior doesn't tempt me at all."

Important: Change Isn't Linear

People don't progress smoothly through stages in order. They cycle back, skip forward, and spend years in contemplation. Relapse is normal‚Äîit's part of the process, not a failure. Research shows most people cycle through stages 3-7 times before reaching maintenance. Your role is to help them learn from each cycle rather than feel defeated by it.

Coach Tip

When a client relapses, normalize it immediately: "You know what? This is actually really common and valuable. Most people cycle through these stages multiple times before lasting change sticks. What can we learn from this experience? What triggered the slip? What would help you get back on track?" Frame it as data collection, not failure.

Matching Interventions to Stage

Here's where theory becomes practice. Let's explore what works‚Äîand what sabotages progress‚Äîat each stage.

Stage 1: Precontemplation ‚Äî Raise Awareness

What NOT to Do
  • Provide detailed action plans
  • Set specific health goals
  • Give unsolicited advice
  • Push for commitment

Why it backfires: The client isn't ready and will perceive this as pushy, judgmental, or threatening. You'll trigger psychological reactance‚Äîthey'll dig in harder to defend their current behavior.

What DOES Work
  • Build rapport and trust
  • Ask open, curious questions
  • Express empathy without judgment
  • Provide information only when asked
  • Gently raise awareness of consequences

Why it works: You're creating safety and planting seeds without forcing growth. Change begins when people feel understood, not when they feel pressured.

Questions for Precontemplation

"What concerns, if any, do you have about your current health?"

"How is your energy level affecting your daily life?"

"What would need to be different for you to consider making changes?"

"On a scale of 1-10, how important is it to you to address this right now?"

Stage 2: Contemplation ‚Äî Resolve Ambivalence

What NOT to Do
  • Rush into action planning
  • Try to convince them change is worth it
  • Dismiss their concerns about changing
  • Focus only on benefits of change

Why it backfires: They're already ambivalent‚Äîadding pressure or minimizing their concerns increases "sustain talk" (reasons to stay the same). Paradoxically, arguing for change makes them argue against it.

What DOES Work
  • Use Motivational Interviewing techniques
  • Explore both sides of ambivalence
  • Evoke change talk
  • Connect change to deeply held values
  • Normalize ambivalence

Why it works: When you help them voice their own reasons for change (rather than imposing yours), intrinsic motivation grows. They talk themselves into readiness.

Questions for Contemplation

"What would be different in your life if you made this change?"

"What concerns you most about changing?"

"How does your current situation align with what matters most to you?"

"If you decided to make a change, what would be your first step?"

"What's kept you from acting on this so far?"

Coach Tip

In contemplation, use the decisional balance exercise. Draw a two-column chart: "Benefits of Changing" vs. "Costs of Changing" and "Benefits of Staying the Same" vs. "Costs of Staying the Same." Let the client fill it in. This externalizes their ambivalence and often reveals that the costs of staying the same outweigh the costs of changing‚Äîhelping them move toward preparation.

Stage 3: Preparation ‚Äî Support Planning

What NOT to Do
  • Overwhelm with too much information
  • Create overly ambitious plans
  • Take over the planning process
  • Ignore potential barriers

Why it backfires: Complexity kills action. If the plan feels too big or isn't truly theirs, they won't follow through. Unaddressed barriers become excuses.

What DOES Work
  • Help develop concrete, specific action plans
  • Identify barriers and create solutions
  • Set SMART goals (small and achievable)
  • Build confidence through micro-wins
  • Connect to resources and support

Why it works: Specific plans with contingencies dramatically increase follow-through. Small wins build self-efficacy. When they believe they can do it, they usually can.

Questions for Preparation

"What's one small step you could take this week?"

"What obstacles might get in your way, and how could you handle them?"

"On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can do this?"

"What would make this easier for you?"

"Who or what could support you in this?"

Stage 4: Action ‚Äî Provide Support & Accountability

What NOT to Do
  • Assume they'll stay motivated on their own
  • Wait for them to reach out when struggling
  • Focus only on outcomes, not process
  • Treat setbacks as failures

Why it backfires: Action is hard‚Äîit requires sustained attention, problem-solving, and encouragement. Left alone, people often lose momentum or get discouraged by normal challenges.

What DOES Work
  • Offer frequent check-ins
  • Celebrate progress (not just perfection)
  • Troubleshoot barriers proactively
  • Adjust plans as needed
  • Reinforce intrinsic motivation
  • Normalize setbacks as learning opportunities

Why it works: Regular contact provides accountability and support. Celebrating effort (not just results) maintains motivation. Flexibility prevents all-or-nothing thinking.

Questions for Action

"What's working well? What's been harder than expected?"

"How did you handle that challenge?"

"What are you most proud of this week?"

"What adjustments would make this more sustainable?"

"How are you feeling about your progress?"

Stage 5: Maintenance ‚Äî Prevent Relapse

What NOT to Do
  • Assume the work is done and step back completely
  • Stop checking in
  • Ignore warning signs of relapse
  • Treat maintenance as less important than action

Why it backfires: Maintenance requires ongoing support, just less intensively. Without it, people often drift back to old patterns, especially during stress or life transitions.

What DOES Work
  • Identify triggers for old behaviors
  • Develop coping strategies
  • Integrate changes into identity
  • Gradually reduce session frequency
  • Plan for high-risk situations
  • Celebrate long-term success

Why it works: Anticipating challenges prevents relapse. Connecting behavior to identity makes it sticky. Ongoing support (even minimal) provides a safety net.

Questions for Maintenance

"What situations might tempt you to slip back?"

"How has this change become part of who you are?"

"What systems can you put in place to sustain this long-term?"

"How will you handle it if you do have a setback?"

"What's different about your life now compared to six months ago?"

Coach Tip

To assess which stage a client is in, listen for language cues. Precontemplation: "I don't need to..." Contemplation: "I should probably... but..." Preparation: "I'm planning to..." Action: "I'm currently..." Maintenance: "I've been doing this for..." The language tells you everything you need to know about readiness.

Behavior Change Science

Understanding why behavior change is so difficult helps you coach more effectively. Here are key insights from behavioral science that every health coach should know.

1. The Intention-Action Gap

Research consistently shows that intentions don't predict behavior. Knowing what to do and even wanting to do it aren't enough. Studies find that:

  • Only 53% of people who intend to exercise actually do so
  • The correlation between intention and action is weak (r = 0.36)
  • Simply forming an intention to change increases the probability of acting by only about 10%
What Doesn't Work

Providing information and assuming people will act on it. "You should exercise 30 minutes a day."

Result: People agree, intend to do it, then don't follow through. The gap between knowing and doing remains.

What Works

Implementation intentions: specific if-then plans that bridge the gap between intention and action.

Example: "If it's 7am on Monday/Wednesday/Friday, then I'll go for a 20-minute walk in my neighborhood."

Result: Behavior change success rate doubles or triples compared to goal intentions alone.

2. Default Bias and Environment Design

Humans are cognitive misers‚Äîwe default to the path of least resistance to conserve mental energy. Willpower is a limited resource that depletes throughout the day. This means:

Behavior change requires making the desired behavior easier and the undesired behavior harder.

Environment Design Strategies

ü•ó Reduce Friction for Healthy Behaviors

Pre-chop vegetables on Sunday, place workout clothes by the bed the night before, keep a water bottle at your desk, join a gym on the way home from work (not across town), prep smoothie ingredients in ziplock bags

üö´ Add Friction to Unhealthy Behaviors

Delete food delivery apps from phone, keep junk food out of the house entirely, put phone in another room at night (removes late-night scrolling temptation), cancel streaming services that enable binge-watching

üéØ Use Visual Cues

Place healthy snacks at eye level, put running shoes by the door, keep vitamins next to coffee maker, use a pill organizer placed on the breakfast table

‚è∞ Design Choice Architecture

Schedule workouts as non-negotiable calendar appointments, set phone reminders for medication, create systems that make the healthy choice the default choice

3. Temporal Discounting

We heavily discount future rewards in favor of immediate gratification. This is hardwired into human psychology‚Äîour ancestors who ate the berry now (rather than saving it for later) survived better in uncertain environments.

The problem: "I'll feel better in 6 months" loses to "This cookie tastes good right now." Future benefits, no matter how large, feel abstract and distant. Present temptations feel concrete and compelling.

Coaching Strategy

Help clients find immediate rewards for healthy behaviors‚Äîrewards they experience today, not months from now:

  • Better sleep tonight after eating a nutrient-dense dinner
  • More energy tomorrow after exercising today
  • Pride and self-respect right now from following through on commitments
  • Reduced stress immediately after a 10-minute meditation
  • Connection with values experienced in the present moment

Connect behaviors to intrinsic values‚Äî"I want to be a present, energetic parent"‚Äîwhich are experienced in the present, not the distant future.

4. Decision Fatigue

Every decision we make throughout the day depletes a limited pool of mental resources. By evening, decision-making capacity and self-control are significantly diminished. This explains why people who eat well all day often binge at night, or why they skip evening workouts despite morning intentions.

Research shows judges are more likely to grant parole in the morning than in the afternoon‚Äîpurely due to decision fatigue, not case merits.

Coaching Strategy

Reduce decisions through automation and routines:

  • Eat the same healthy breakfast every day (one decision eliminated)
  • Meal prep on Sundays (removes daily dinner decisions)
  • Exercise at the same time daily (reduces "when should I work out?" decision)
  • Use pre-commitment strategies: "I always exercise before work" or "I never eat dessert on weekdays"
  • Create if-then rules that remove in-the-moment choice

The Science of Habit Formation

Lasting change doesn't come from motivation or willpower‚Äîboth are unreliable and fluctuating. Lasting change comes from turning desired behaviors into automatic habits that require minimal conscious effort.

The Habit Loop

Every habit, whether healthy or unhealthy, follows a three-part neurological loop identified by Charles Duhigg in The Power of Habit:

1

CUE

A trigger that initiates the behavior. Can be a time (7am), location (kitchen), emotional state (stressed), preceding action (after brushing teeth), or other people (when my spouse gets home).

2

ROUTINE

The behavior itself‚Äîthe action you take in response to the cue. This can be physical (going for a walk), mental (worrying), or emotional (feeling frustrated).

3

REWARD

The benefit that reinforces the behavior and tells your brain to remember this loop for the future. The reward satisfies a craving‚Äîfor energy, stress relief, distraction, social connection, or pleasure.

Over time, this loop becomes so automatic that the cue alone triggers a craving for the reward, and the routine happens almost unconsciously. Your brain learns: "When I see this cue, I do this routine to get this reward."

Building New Habits: Practical Strategies

1

Start Tiny (BJ Fogg Method)

Research by Stanford's BJ Fogg shows tiny habits work better than ambitious ones. Instead of "exercise for 30 minutes," start with "do 2 pushups after brushing teeth." The goal is repetition and consistency, not intensity. Once the tiny habit is established, it naturally grows.

Why it works: Tiny habits require minimal motivation, face minimal resistance, and succeed consistently‚Äîbuilding confidence and momentum.

2

Stack Habits

Link new behaviors to existing routines: "After I pour my morning coffee, I'll take my supplements." The existing habit becomes the cue for the new one. This leverages neural pathways already established.

Formula: After [EXISTING HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT].

3

Make It Obvious

Visible cues trigger action. Put running shoes by the door, place a water bottle on your desk, set out vitamins the night before, prep gym bag and leave it in the car.

The principle: You can't rely on remembering. Design your environment so the cue is unmissable.

4

Make It Satisfying

Immediate rewards strengthen habits. Track progress visually (X on calendar), celebrate completion, pair the behavior with something enjoyable (listen to favorite podcast only while walking), give yourself a checkmark or gold star.

Why it works: The brain's reward system needs immediate feedback. Delayed gratification alone won't build habits.

Coach Tip

When helping clients build new habits, use this framework: "After I [EXISTING HABIT], I will [TINY VERSION OF NEW HABIT] in [SPECIFIC LOCATION]." Example: "After I pour my morning coffee, I will do one yoga stretch in the kitchen." Specific, tiny, linked to existing routine. Success rate skyrockets.

How Long Does Habit Formation Really Take?

The "21 days to form a habit" myth originated from a misinterpretation of Dr. Maxwell Maltz's work on plastic surgery patients in the 1960s. The reality is more complex.

Research by health psychology researcher Phillippa Lally at University College London found that it takes an average of 66 days for a behavior to become automatic‚Äîwith a wide range of 18 to 254 days depending on the person, the behavior, and the circumstances.

  • Simple behaviors (drinking water with breakfast) automate faster
  • Complex behaviors (50-minute gym workout) take much longer
  • Missing one day doesn't significantly impact long-term automaticity
  • Consistency matters more than perfection
Client-Friendly Explanation

"Building a new habit is like creating a path through a forest. The first few times, you're pushing through thick brush and it's exhausting. But every time you walk that path, it gets a little clearer, a little easier. Eventually, it becomes a well-worn trail you can walk without thinking‚Äîyour feet just know where to go. That's what we're doing with these new behaviors. It takes time, but every single repetition is clearing the path."

Breaking Bad Habits: Replacement, Not Deletion

Here's a crucial insight: You can't delete a habit‚Äîyou can only replace it. The neural pathways remain. The cue-routine-reward loop is permanent. But you can substitute a new routine while keeping the same cue and reward.

1

Identify the Cue

When does the behavior happen? What precedes it? Track patterns: time of day, location, emotional state, other people, immediately preceding action. Most cues fall into one of these five categories.

2

Identify the Real Reward

What does the behavior actually provide? It's rarely what it seems. Smoking provides stress relief (deep breathing), social connection (smoke breaks with colleagues), or ritual (transition from work to home). Sugar cravings might be seeking energy, comfort, or a break from boredom.

3

Experiment with New Routines

Test different behaviors that provide a similar reward. If stress is the cue and calm is the reward, try deep breathing, walking, calling a friend, or stretching instead of the old routine. Find what satisfies the craving.

4

Create an Implementation Intention

Plan for the cue: "When I feel stressed (cue), I will take three deep breaths and drink a glass of water (new routine) to feel calm (reward)." Pre-deciding the response removes in-the-moment decision-making.

Beware the Abstinence Violation Effect

One slip-up leads to total relapse when people engage in all-or-nothing thinking: "I already failed, might as well give up." This is called the Abstinence Violation Effect‚Äîa single violation of self-imposed rules triggers a complete abandonment of the change effort.

Coach's role: Help clients reframe lapses as data points, not disasters. Ask: "What triggered that? What can we learn? What will you do differently next time?" Normalize imperfection. Progress, not perfection, is the goal.

Key Terms to Remember

Transtheoretical Model

Prochaska and DiClemente's framework showing change as a process through six stages: precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance, and termination.

Stage-Matched Intervention

Tailoring coaching strategies to the client's current stage of change rather than using a one-size-fits-all approach. Matching intervention to readiness dramatically increases success.

Implementation Intention

A specific if-then plan that links a situational cue to a behavioral response. Research shows this doubles or triples behavior change success compared to goal intentions alone.

Habit Loop

The three-part cycle of cue, routine, and reward that forms the neurological basis of all habits, both healthy and unhealthy.

Temporal Discounting

The tendency to devalue future rewards in favor of immediate gratification, making long-term behavior change challenging without immediate payoffs.

Habit Stacking

Linking a new desired behavior to an existing habit, using the established routine as a cue for the new one. Formula: After [EXISTING HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT].

Decision Fatigue

The deterioration of decision-making quality and self-control after making many decisions throughout the day. Explains why willpower is lower in the evening.

Abstinence Violation Effect

The phenomenon where a single lapse in behavior leads to complete relapse due to all-or-nothing thinking. Prevented by reframing slips as learning opportunities.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

Why is it problematic to give action plans to someone in the precontemplation or contemplation stage?

Answer: People in precontemplation don't see a need to change, and those in contemplation are still ambivalent. Jumping to action plans before they're ready triggers psychological reactance and resistance, damages rapport, and can push them backward in their readiness to change. The intervention must match the stage‚Äîraising awareness in precontemplation, resolving ambivalence in contemplation, and only creating action plans once they've moved to preparation.

2

Describe the habit loop and explain how to use it to build a new healthy habit.

Answer: The habit loop consists of three parts: (1) Cue - a trigger that initiates the behavior, (2) Routine - the behavior itself, and (3) Reward - the benefit that reinforces the behavior and tells your brain to remember this loop. To build a new habit, create a clear and obvious cue (e.g., "after I pour my morning coffee"), define a simple and achievable routine (e.g., "I take my supplements"), and ensure an immediate reward (e.g., sense of accomplishment, visual checkmark on tracker). Make the cue visible, start with a tiny version of the behavior, and ensure the reward is satisfying enough to reinforce repetition.

3

What is temporal discounting, and what's one strategy coaches can use to overcome it?

Answer: Temporal discounting is the tendency to heavily discount future rewards in favor of immediate gratification (e.g., "ice cream now" beats "better health later"). Coaches can overcome this by helping clients identify immediate rewards for healthy behaviors‚Äîbetter sleep tonight, more energy tomorrow, pride and self-respect right now, reduced stress immediately after meditation. Connecting behaviors to intrinsic values (being a present parent, living aligned with who they want to be) also helps because values are experienced in the present, making them feel more immediate and compelling than distant health outcomes.

4

Why can't you simply delete a bad habit, and what's the alternative approach?

Answer: You can't delete a habit because the neural pathways and the cue-routine-reward loop remain permanently in the brain. The alternative approach is habit replacement‚Äîkeeping the same cue and reward while substituting a new, healthier routine. First, identify the cue (when does it happen?) and the real reward (what does it actually provide?). Then experiment with new routines that provide a similar reward. Finally, create an implementation intention: "When I feel stressed (cue), I will take three deep breaths (new routine) to feel calm (reward)." This works because you're satisfying the same underlying need with a healthier behavior.

Key Takeaways
  • The Transtheoretical Model shows change unfolds in six stages‚Äîeffective coaching matches interventions to the client's current stage, not where you want them to be
  • Relapse is normal and part of the change process, not a failure‚Äîmost people cycle through stages 3-7 times before reaching maintenance
  • Intentions don't predict behavior‚Äîimplementation intentions (specific if-then plans) dramatically increase follow-through
  • Lasting change comes from building automatic habits through the cue-routine-reward loop
  • Start with tiny habits, make them obvious and immediately satisfying, and be patient‚Äîhabit formation takes an average of 66 days
  • Environment design matters more than willpower‚Äîmake healthy behaviors easy and unhealthy behaviors harder
  • You can't delete bad habits, only replace them by keeping the cue and reward while substituting a healthier routine

Case Study: Jennifer's Knowledge Gap

When Knowing Isn't Enough

üë©

Jennifer, Age 45

Knows what to do but can't seem to do it ‚Ä¢ Chronic cycle of starting and stopping

Jennifer has been told by three different doctors that she needs to change her diet. She's read books, watched documentaries, and knows exactly what she should be eating. She even has a Pinterest board full of healthy recipes.

But she hasn't changed a thing. Every Monday she starts fresh. By Wednesday she's back to takeout and frozen meals. She feels shame, frustration, and hopelessness.

The problem isn't knowledge‚Äîit's ambivalence. Part of Jennifer desperately wants to change. Another part of her is terrified of failing again, overwhelmed by the effort, and secretly convinced it won't make a difference anyway.

Traditional advice-giving only deepens her shame. What Jennifer needs is someone who can help her resolve this internal conflict‚Äînot by persuading, but by listening.

Jennifer's story echoes through countless coaching sessions. The conventional approach‚Äîmore information, stricter meal plans, stronger warnings‚Äîhas failed her repeatedly. She needs something fundamentally different.

What Is Motivational Interviewing?

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a collaborative, person-centered approach developed by psychologists William Miller and Stephen Rollnick in the early 1980s. Originally designed for addiction counseling, it's now used across healthcare because of one simple truth:

People don't resist change‚Äîthey resist being changed.

MI recognizes that ambivalence is normal. Most people have conflicting feelings about behavior change:

  • "I want to lose weight, but I love food."
  • "I know I should exercise, but I'm exhausted."
  • "I need to reduce stress, but I can't say no at work."

Rather than trying to overcome this resistance with logic or persuasion, MI works with ambivalence‚Äîhelping clients explore both sides until they talk themselves into change.

Traditional Advice-Giving
  • "You need to eat more vegetables"
  • "Here's a meal plan. Just follow it"
  • Assumes client lacks information
Motivational Interviewing
  • "What matters most about your health?"
  • "What would be different if you made changes?"
  • Evokes client's own reasons for change

The first approach assumes the client lacks information. The second assumes they have their own reasons for change‚Äîthey just need help accessing them.

Coach Tip

When you feel the urge to give advice or "fix" the client's problem, pause and ask yourself: "What question could help them discover this for themselves?" This simple pause shifts you from expert to partner‚Äîthe foundation of effective MI.

The Spirit of MI: Four Foundational Principles

Before learning techniques, you must embody the spirit of MI. Without this foundation, OARS becomes manipulative. These four principles aren't just concepts‚Äîthey're ways of being with your clients.

1

Partnership

MI is a collaboration, not an expert-patient hierarchy. You're walking alongside the client, not leading from the front. Their expertise on their own life matters more than your knowledge about health.

2

Acceptance

This means absolute positive regard, accurate empathy, affirmation of strengths, and autonomy support. You accept the client exactly as they are‚Äîincluding their ambivalence, resistance, and past failures.

3

Compassion

Your work actively promotes the client's welfare and prioritizes their needs. This isn't detached professionalism‚Äîit's genuine care for their wellbeing.

4

Evocation

The client already has the wisdom, motivation, and resources they need. Your job is to evoke what's already there‚Äînot install something new.

The MI Mindset

"I trust that this person has reasons for their behavior. I trust they have the capacity to change. My role is to help them discover their own motivation, not to provide mine."

Coach Tip

Practice checking your internal state before sessions. Ask yourself: "Am I approaching this client with genuine curiosity and compassion, or am I bringing an agenda to 'fix' them?" Your internal orientation matters more than your words.

The OARS Technique

OARS is the core skill set of MI. These four tools work together to create a conversation that evokes change. Think of OARS as the language of collaboration‚Äîa way of speaking that invites exploration rather than defensiveness.

The OARS Framework

O - Open Questions

Questions that can't be answered with yes/no. They invite exploration, reflection, and elaboration.

Examples: "What concerns you most about your current health?" "How would your life be different if you made this change?"

A - Affirmations

Genuine statements that recognize the client's strengths, efforts, values, or past successes. Not empty praise‚Äîspecific acknowledgment.

Examples: "You've shown real courage by being here today." "The fact that you keep trying tells me about your determination."

R - Reflections

Mirroring back what you hear‚Äîboth content and emotion. This demonstrates deep listening and helps clients hear themselves.

Examples: "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed by all the conflicting information out there." "You want to be more present with your kids."

S - Summaries

Collecting and reflecting back key themes from the conversation. Summaries link ideas together, highlight ambivalence, and transition between topics.

Examples: "Let me make sure I understand. You've tried multiple times because you want energy for your kids, but something gets in the way mid-week."

OARS in Action: Jennifer's Session

Let's see how an MI-informed coach might work with Jennifer, using each OARS component strategically:

Coach: "Jennifer, what brings you here today?" [Open question]

Jennifer: "I just... I know I need to change my diet. I keep failing."

Coach: "You're feeling frustrated with yourself." [Reflection]

Jennifer: "Yes! I have all this information but I can't stick with anything."

Coach: "The fact that you keep trying‚Äîeven after setbacks‚Äîtells me something about your determination." [Affirmation]

Jennifer: [pauses] "I hadn't thought of it that way."

Coach: "What would be different in your life if you did make changes to your diet?" [Open question]

Jennifer: "I'd have more energy. I could play with my kids without feeling exhausted."

Coach: "Being present with your kids really matters to you." [Reflection]

Jennifer: "It does. That's why I keep trying."

Coach: "So let me make sure I understand. You've tried multiple times to change your diet because you want to be more energetic and present with your kids. You have the knowledge, but something gets in the way mid-week. Is that right?" [Summary]

Notice what the coach didn't do: give advice, provide meal plans, lecture about nutrition, or try to convince Jennifer. Instead, they evoked Jennifer's own reasons for change.

The Righting Reflex

Miller and Rollnick call the urge to fix, advise, and solve the "righting reflex." It's natural‚Äîyou want to help. But premature advice triggers resistance. The client must articulate their own reasons for change before they're ready to hear solutions.

Coach Tip

Practice the "reflection to question ratio" in your sessions. Aim for at least two reflections for every question you ask. This slows down the conversation, deepens understanding, and shows you're truly listening‚Äînot just waiting for your turn to ask the next question.

Working with Resistance

In MI, resistance isn't viewed as a character flaw‚Äîit's a signal that you're pushing too hard or moving too fast. When you encounter resistance, it's an invitation to roll with it rather than confront it.

What Resistance Sounds Like:

  • "Yes, but..." (arguing)
  • "I've tried that before and it didn't work." (discounting)
  • Interrupting, changing the subject (diverting)
  • Silence, minimal responses (withdrawing)

Traditional vs. MI Responses:

Confrontational Response
  • Client: "I don't have time to cook."
  • Coach: "You have to make time. Your health depends on it."
  • Result: Increased defensiveness
Rolling with Resistance
  • Client: "I don't have time to cook."
  • Coach: "Time is really tight for you right now. Tell me about your schedule."
  • Result: Invitation to explore

The first response increases defensiveness. The second invites exploration and demonstrates empathy.

Strategies for Rolling with Resistance:

1

Simple Reflection

Mirror back exactly what you hear without judgment. "You don't think changing your diet will make a difference."

2

Amplified Reflection

Reflect back in slightly exaggerated form to invite the client to back off the extreme position. "So there's absolutely nothing you could change, even small things." (Often they'll respond: "Well, I guess I could...")

3

Double-Sided Reflection

Acknowledge both sides of their ambivalence. "On one hand, you're skeptical this will work. On the other hand, you're here because part of you wants things to be different."

4

Shifting Focus

If resistance is high, change the subject temporarily. "Let's set that aside for a moment and talk about what's working well in your life right now."

Pause & Reflect

Think about a time you felt someone was trying to change you. How did you react? Now think about a time someone truly listened to your ambivalence without judgment. Which experience made you more open to change?

Recognizing and Evoking Change Talk

The central strategy of MI is to increase change talk and decrease sustain talk. These are client statements about their relationship to change. Research shows that when clients verbalize reasons for change, they become more committed to changing.

Change Talk: The Language of Motivation

Change talk comes in six forms, easily remembered by the acronym DARN-CAT:

üåü Types of Change Talk

Desire

"I want to..." "I wish..." "I'd like to..."

Ability

"I could..." "I can..." "I might be able to..."

Reasons

"It would help me..." "If I changed, then..." "The benefits would be..."

Need

"I need to..." "I have to..." "It's important that..."

Commitment

"I will..." "I'm going to..." "I promise..."

Activation

"I'm ready to..." "I'm willing to..." "I'm prepared to..."

Taking Steps

"I've started..." "I've been..." "I went ahead and..."

Sustain Talk: The Language of Status Quo

‚ö†Ô∏è Sustain Talk (Reasons Not to Change)

  • "I don't want to give up foods I love."
  • "I can't afford organic produce."
  • "It won't work for me."
  • "I don't need to change that much."
  • "I've tried before and failed."
Key Insight

Research shows that when clients verbalize reasons for change, they become more committed to changing. When they argue against change (even in response to your persuasion), they become more entrenched. This is why evoking change talk is more effective than providing reasons yourself.

How to Evoke Change Talk:

Powerful Questions for Evoking Change Talk

Ask Evocative Questions

"What would be the benefits of making this change?"

"What's the worst thing that might happen if you don't change?"

"How would you like your life to be different five years from now?"

"What makes you think you could succeed if you decided to try?"

Ask About the Past

"When was a time you successfully made a difficult change?"

"What was different then?"

"What did you do that worked?"

Look Forward/Backward

"Imagine it's a year from now and you've made these changes. What does your life look like?"

"If you don't make changes, what do you think things will be like in five years?"

Explore Values

"What matters most to you in life?"

"How does your current health align with those values?"

"What kind of person do you want to be?"

Responding to Change Talk:

When you hear change talk, amplify it using these strategies:

  • Reflect it back: "Being there for your kids is really important to you."
  • Ask for elaboration: "Tell me more about that."
  • Ask for examples: "What would that look like?"
  • Affirm: "That takes real insight to recognize."
  • Use scaling questions: "On a scale of 0-10, how important is this? Why that number and not lower?"
Coach Tip

The scaling question technique is powerful for evoking change talk. When a client says they're at a "7" on importance, ask: "Why a 7 and not a 2 or 3?" This forces them to articulate their own reasons for change. Never ask why they're not at 10‚Äîthat evokes sustain talk.

Back to Jennifer: Evoking Change Talk

Coach: "Jennifer, on a scale of 0-10, how important is it for you to make changes to your diet?"

Jennifer: "Maybe a 7."

Coach: "A 7 is pretty high. Why a 7 and not a 2 or 3?" [This evokes reasons for change]

Jennifer: "Well, I don't want to keep feeling this way. And my mom had diabetes‚ÄîI'm scared of that."

Coach: "You want to feel better, and you want to avoid your mom's path." [Reflection amplifying change talk]

Jennifer: "Yes. I saw what she went through. I don't want that."

Coach: "What would be different if you were feeling more energetic and confident about your health?"

Jennifer: "I'd be more present. I wouldn't be so irritable with my kids. I could actually enjoy time with them instead of just surviving."

Coach: "Being the kind of mom you want to be is connected to taking care of yourself."

Jennifer: [tears up] "It is. I haven't thought about it that way."

The coach didn't convince Jennifer to change. They helped her articulate her own reasons‚Äîher children, her health fears, her values about motherhood. Now Jennifer is talking herself into change, which is far more powerful than being talked into it.

When to Move Forward

You'll know a client is ready to talk about action when they spontaneously start talking about commitment ("I will..."), taking steps ("I've already..."), or directly ask for help ("What should I do?"). Until then, stay in exploration mode.

1

What is the core principle behind MI, and why does giving advice often backfire?

Answer: The core principle is that people don't resist change‚Äîthey resist being changed. When we give unsolicited advice, it triggers the "righting reflex" and increases defensiveness. People become more committed to change when they verbalize their own reasons rather than hearing ours. MI works by evoking the client's intrinsic motivation rather than imposing external motivation.

2

Describe the four components of OARS and give an example of each.

Answer: Open questions: "What concerns you most about your health?" Affirmations: "You've shown real courage by being here today." Reflections: "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed by conflicting information." Summaries: "Let me make sure I understand‚Äîyou want to change because of your kids, but you're worried about failing again."

3

What's the difference between change talk and sustain talk? How should you respond to each?

Answer: Change talk includes statements favoring change (desire, ability, reasons, need, commitment, activation, taking steps‚ÄîDARN-CAT). When you hear it, amplify it through reflection, elaboration questions, or affirmation. Sustain talk includes reasons not to change. When you hear it, use simple reflection without amplifying, or use double-sided reflection to acknowledge both sides of ambivalence. Never argue against sustain talk‚Äîthat increases it.

4

Why is the "spirit of MI" more important than technique? What happens if you use OARS without embodying the spirit?

Answer: The spirit of MI‚Äîpartnership, acceptance, compassion, and evocation‚Äîrepresents your fundamental orientation toward the client. Without this foundation, OARS techniques become manipulative tools to get the client to do what you want. Clients sense inauthenticity and respond with increased resistance. The spirit ensures that MI remains truly collaborative and client-centered rather than covertly directive.

Key Takeaways
  • Motivational Interviewing helps clients resolve ambivalence by evoking their own reasons for change‚Äînot by providing yours
  • The spirit of MI‚Äîpartnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation‚Äîmatters more than technique alone
  • OARS (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summaries) creates conversations that elicit intrinsic motivation
  • Resistance is a signal to slow down and deepen empathy, not to push harder with advice or persuasion
  • Increase change talk (DARN-CAT), decrease sustain talk‚Äîpeople commit more when they hear themselves argue for change
  • The "righting reflex" to fix and advise often backfires by triggering defensiveness

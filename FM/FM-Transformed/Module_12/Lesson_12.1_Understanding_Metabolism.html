What Is Metabolism?

Metabolism refers to the sum of all chemical reactions occurring in the body to maintain life. These reactions fall into two categories:

  • Anabolism: Building processes that construct complex molecules from simpler ones, requiring energy (e.g., building muscle protein, storing fat, creating hormones)
  • Catabolism: Breaking down processes that disassemble complex molecules into simpler ones, releasing energy (e.g., breaking down glucose for fuel, releasing fatty acids from storage)

Metabolism is not simply about "burning calories"‚Äîit encompasses every process that keeps you alive: maintaining body temperature, pumping blood, regenerating cells, producing hormones, detoxifying chemicals, thinking, breathing, and yes, processing the food you eat.

When we talk about metabolic rate, we're usually referring to Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)‚Äîthe total calories burned in a day. TDEE has several components:

Key Insight

Notice that formal exercise typically accounts for only 5-10% of total calorie expenditure. This is why you cannot "out-exercise" a poor diet or a sluggish metabolism‚Äîthe numbers simply don't add up. A one-hour gym session might burn 300-500 calories, but BMR and NEAT together burn 1,500-2,000+ calories daily.

Coach Tip

When clients overemphasize exercise for weight loss, gently redirect: "Exercise is incredibly important for health, mood, and muscle‚Äîbut most of your daily calorie burn happens outside the gym. Let's focus on optimizing your metabolism 24/7, not just during your workout hour." This helps shift perspective from punishment to optimization.

The Power of NEAT

Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT) varies dramatically between individuals‚Äîby as much as 2,000 calories per day. This explains why some people seem to "eat whatever they want" without gaining weight. They may unconsciously move more: fidgeting, taking stairs, pacing while on calls, standing versus sitting. NEAT also decreases when people diet, as the body conserves energy‚Äîa key reason weight loss stalls.

Factors That Determine Metabolic Rate

Metabolic rate varies significantly between individuals. Understanding what influences this variation helps explain why standardized calorie recommendations fail so many people.

Body Composition

Muscle tissue is metabolically active‚Äîit burns calories even at rest. Fat tissue, while not metabolically inert (it produces hormones and requires blood supply), burns far fewer calories per pound than muscle. This is why:

  • Two people of the same weight can have very different metabolic rates depending on their muscle-to-fat ratio
  • Losing muscle through crash dieting or aging reduces metabolic rate
  • Building muscle is one of the most effective ways to sustainably increase metabolism
  • Men typically have higher metabolic rates than women partly due to greater muscle mass
Coach Tip

Help clients reframe their relationship with the scale: "The number on the scale doesn't tell us about your body composition. You could weigh the same but have more muscle and less fat‚Äîwhich means a faster metabolism, better blood sugar control, and stronger bones. Let's track measurements, how your clothes fit, and how you feel, not just the scale."

Age

Metabolic rate naturally declines with age, primarily due to loss of muscle mass (sarcopenia) and decreased physical activity. Research suggests metabolism remains relatively stable from 20-60, with the decline accelerating after 60. Much of this decline is preventable with resistance training and adequate protein intake.

Genetics

Genes influence metabolic rate, fat storage patterns, hunger signals, and response to different macronutrients. However, genetics typically account for only 20-30% of metabolic variation‚Äîleaving significant room for modification through lifestyle. Epigenetics‚Äîhow genes are expressed based on environment‚Äîmeans that lifestyle choices can "turn on" or "turn off" metabolic genes.

Dieting History

A history of caloric restriction can persistently lower metabolic rate. This phenomenon, sometimes called "metabolic adaptation" or "adaptive thermogenesis," is the body's survival mechanism in response to perceived famine. Research on contestants from "The Biggest Loser" showed that many had metabolic rates 500+ calories per day lower than expected even six years after the show‚Äîtheir bodies had adapted to extreme restriction and never fully recovered.

Coach Tip

When clients reveal a history of yo-yo dieting, acknowledge the metabolic impact with compassion: "Your body has been through a lot of feast-famine cycles. It's learned to be very efficient at storing energy because it's preparing for the next 'famine.' This isn't your fault‚Äîyour metabolism is doing exactly what it's designed to do for survival. Our job now is to teach your body that it's safe, that food is abundant, and that it can let go of stored energy."

Why "Calories In, Calories Out" Fails

The conventional model of weight management treats the body like a simple bank account: deposit fewer calories than you withdraw, and you'll lose weight. While thermodynamics (energy cannot be created or destroyed) is valid, this oversimplified model fails for several reasons.

The Body Adapts to Restriction

When calorie intake drops, the body doesn't simply burn fat to make up the difference. Instead, it implements survival mechanisms:

‚ö†Ô∏è Metabolic Adaptation to Restriction

Reduces BMR

Slows down thyroid function, reduces body temperature, and decreases metabolic processes

Decreases NEAT

Unconsciously reduces movement‚Äîless fidgeting, slower walking, choosing to sit rather than stand

Increases Hunger

Ghrelin (hunger hormone) rises; leptin (satiety hormone) falls

Reduces Muscle

Without adequate protein and stimulation, muscle is catabolized for energy

Prioritizes Fat Storage

When food becomes available, the body preferentially stores it as fat for future famine

The Dieting Trap

This is why Patricia's 1,200-calorie diet stopped working. Her body adapted‚Äîmetabolic rate dropped, NEAT decreased, hunger increased. She'd need to eat even less to continue losing weight, further damaging her metabolism. This is the "yo-yo diet" cycle that leaves many people with lower metabolic rates than before they started dieting.

Not All Calories Are Equal

The source of calories matters as much as the quantity. Different macronutrients have different metabolic effects:

Protein
  • Thermic Effect: 20-35% burned in digestion
  • Impact: Increases satiety, supports muscle, minimal insulin spike
  • Result: Most metabolically favorable
Carbohydrates
  • Thermic Effect: 5-15% burned in digestion
  • Impact: Triggers insulin; refined carbs spike blood sugar
  • Result: Quality and timing matter greatly
Fat
  • Thermic Effect: 0-5% burned in digestion
  • Impact: Increases satiety, minimal insulin, slow absorption
  • Result: Supports hormone production

200 calories of protein affects the body very differently than 200 calories of refined carbohydrates. The protein increases satiety, requires more energy to digest, and supports muscle maintenance. The refined carbs spike blood sugar, trigger insulin release (promoting fat storage), and leave you hungry again within hours.

Timing and Context Matter

When you eat, what you eat it with, and your metabolic state all influence how food is processed:

  • The same meal eaten in the morning versus late at night has different metabolic consequences
  • Exercise changes how nutrients are partitioned (toward muscle rather than fat)
  • Stress hormones alter how food is processed
  • Sleep deprivation impairs insulin sensitivity, making the same food more fattening
  • The gut microbiome influences calorie extraction‚Äîsome individuals extract more calories from the same food than others

The Hormonal Model of Metabolism

Rather than viewing metabolism as simple calorie accounting, a more accurate model recognizes that hormones orchestrate how food is processed, stored, and burned. The key question isn't just "how much are you eating?" but "what signals is your body receiving about what to do with that food?"

Insulin: The Storage Hormone

Insulin's primary job is to remove glucose from the bloodstream and direct it into cells. When cells are full, excess glucose is converted to fat for storage. Key principles:

üîë Understanding Insulin's Role

High Insulin = Storage Mode

When insulin is elevated, the body is focused on storing fuel, not releasing it

Low Insulin = Burning Mode

When insulin is low (between meals, during fasting), the body can access stored fat for fuel

Insulin Resistance = Elevated Baseline

The body produces more and more insulin to overcome resistance, keeping storage mode activated longer

Dietary Drivers

Refined carbs, frequent eating, and excess calories (especially from carbs) chronically elevate insulin

Coach Tip

Use this analogy with clients: "Think of insulin like a traffic cop directing where nutrients go. When insulin is high, it's saying 'store, store, store!' When it's low, it's saying 'okay, you can use what's in storage now.' If insulin is always high‚Äîfrom eating refined carbs or grazing all day‚Äîyour body never gets the signal to burn stored fat. We want to create windows where insulin can drop so your body can access its reserves."

Leptin: The Satiety Signal Gone Wrong

Leptin is produced by fat cells to signal the brain about energy stores. In a healthy system, more body fat = more leptin = reduced appetite and increased metabolism. But in leptin resistance:

  • The brain doesn't "hear" the leptin signal despite high levels
  • The brain perceives starvation even when body fat is excessive
  • Hunger increases and metabolism slows‚Äîthe opposite of what should happen
  • This creates a vicious cycle where the body fights against fat loss

Leptin resistance is driven by inflammation, chronically elevated insulin, poor sleep, and a diet high in processed foods. Addressing these factors can help restore leptin sensitivity.

The Set Point Theory

The body appears to defend a particular weight range‚Äîthe set point. When you lose weight, multiple systems (hormones, metabolism, hunger) work to push you back toward that set point. When you gain weight, opposing mechanisms should push you back down‚Äîbut these are often impaired by modern lifestyle factors.

The good news: the set point can be shifted over time through sustained changes in diet quality, sleep, stress, exercise, and inflammation. It requires patience and addressing root causes rather than just restricting calories.

Coaching Integration: Reframing Metabolism

When working with clients like Patricia who feel their "metabolism is broken," consider these coaching approaches:

Coach Tip

Validate their experience: "Your body IS responding differently‚Äîthis isn't willpower failure. Your metabolism has adapted to protect you; it's working as designed for survival. Our goal isn't to fight against your body‚Äîit's to work WITH it to restore healthy function."

Coach Tip

Shift from calories to signals: "Instead of just counting calories, let's focus on the messages your food sends. We'll choose foods that tell your body it's safe, nourished, and can let go of stored energy. We'll also create eating patterns that allow insulin to drop so fat burning can happen."

Coach Tip

Set realistic timelines: "Metabolic recovery takes time‚Äîoften longer than the damage took to create. These are signs your metabolism is healing."

Test Your Knowledge
1

What percentage of total daily energy expenditure typically comes from formal exercise?

Answer: Only 5-10% of TDEE comes from formal exercise. This is why you cannot "out-exercise" a poor diet or sluggish metabolism. The majority of calories (60-75%) are burned by basal metabolic rate (BMR), with NEAT contributing another 10-20%.

2

Explain why two people eating the same number of calories can have completely different weight outcomes.

Answer: Multiple factors create individual variation: (1) Body composition (muscle burns more than fat), (2) Hormonal status (thyroid, insulin sensitivity, leptin resistance), (3) NEAT differences (can vary by 2,000 calories/day), (4) Thermic effect of food varies by macronutrient composition, (5) Gut microbiome differences affect calorie extraction, (6) Sleep quality impacts insulin sensitivity, and (7) Stress levels alter cortisol and fat storage patterns.

3

What happens metabolically when someone restricts calories significantly for an extended period?

Answer: The body enters survival mode and implements multiple adaptations: (1) BMR decreases (thyroid slows, body temperature drops), (2) NEAT decreases unconsciously, (3) Hunger hormones increase (ghrelin up, leptin down), (4) Muscle tissue is broken down for energy, (5) When food becomes available, the body preferentially stores it as fat. This is why crash diets typically result in regaining weight‚Äîoften ending up with a slower metabolism than before.

4

How does insulin influence whether food is burned or stored?

Answer: Insulin is the primary storage hormone. When insulin is elevated, the body is in "storage mode"‚Äîit cannot effectively access and burn stored fat. When insulin is low (between meals, during fasting, or with low-carb meals), the body can enter "burning mode" and access fat stores for energy. Chronic insulin elevation from frequent eating, refined carbohydrates, or insulin resistance keeps the body in storage mode persistently, making fat loss extremely difficult regardless of calorie intake.

5

As a health coach, how would you reframe metabolism for a client who believes theirs is "broken"?

Answer: Reframe from "broken" to "adapted": "Your metabolism isn't broken‚Äîit's doing exactly what it's designed to do to protect you. After years of restriction, your body has learned to be very efficient with energy because it experienced repeated 'famines.' This adaptation was survival. Now our job is to teach your body that it's safe, that food is abundant and nourishing, and that it can let go of stored energy. This takes time‚Äîoften several months‚Äîbut your metabolism can heal. We'll focus on restoring function rather than further restriction." This approach validates their experience while offering hope and a path forward.

Key Takeaways
  • Metabolism encompasses all life-sustaining chemical reactions, with BMR accounting for 60-75% of daily energy expenditure
  • Metabolic rate is influenced by body composition, age, hormones, genetics, and dieting history‚Äîit's highly individual
  • The "calories in, calories out" model oversimplifies metabolism; the body adapts to restriction by lowering metabolic rate
  • Hormones‚Äîespecially insulin and leptin‚Äîdetermine whether food is burned or stored; addressing hormonal health is key
  • The body defends a set point weight; shifting this requires sustained lifestyle changes, not just calorie cutting
  • Not all calories are equal‚Äîmacronutrient composition, food quality, and timing all influence metabolic outcomes
  • NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) can vary by 2,000 calories/day between individuals and decreases when dieting

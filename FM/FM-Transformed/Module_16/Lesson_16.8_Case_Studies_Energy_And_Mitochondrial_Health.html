Assessment: Identifying Root Causes

Catherine has multiple factors draining her cellular energy simultaneously:

1

HPA Axis Dysfunction

Classic "tired but wired" pattern from chronic stress; waking at night suggests cortisol dysregulation

2

Poor Sleep Quality

Alcohol and caffeine both impair sleep architecture; she's sleeping without restoring

3

Skipping breakfast, relying on caffeine, and eating takeout creates glucose roller coaster

4

Nutrient Depletion

Stress depletes B vitamins and magnesium; poor diet likely inadequate in mitochondrial nutrients

5

Sedentary Lifestyle

No exercise means no mitochondrial biogenesis stimulus

6

Incomplete Testing

Coach Tip

When clients present with this level of depletion, resist the urge to create a comprehensive plan immediately. They don't have the energy to implement it. Instead, identify the 1-2 changes that will give the biggest return for the least effort. For Catherine, that's improving sleep quality by adjusting caffeine and alcohol timing‚Äîthis single change can provide enough energy improvement to tackle other interventions.

Coaching Approach: Phased Implementation

Phase 1 - Foundation (Weeks 1-4):

  • Sleep first: Caffeine cutoff at noon. Reduce wine to 1 glass max, stopping 3 hours before bed. This is non-negotiable‚Äîshe won't improve without better sleep quality.
  • Blood sugar stability: Simple breakfast with protein (eggs, Greek yogurt with nuts). Protein at every meal.
  • One parasympathetic practice: 5 minutes of breathing exercises before bed, or 10-minute guided meditation app.

Phase 2 - Building (Weeks 5-8):

  • Gentle movement: Start with 10-minute walks at lunch. Not intense‚Äîjust getting mitochondria the signal to adapt.
  • Nutrient support: B-complex, magnesium glycinate (evening), consider CoQ10.
  • Request additional testing: Encourage conversation with doctor about ferritin, B12, vitamin D, fasting insulin, full thyroid panel.
  • Stress boundaries: Identify one work boundary to implement.

Phase 3 - Optimization (Weeks 9-12):

  • Increase walking duration; consider adding strength training
  • Address any deficiencies found in testing
  • Continue building stress resilience practices
  • Monitor energy improvements; adjust as needed
Key Insight

Catherine's fatigue isn't mysterious‚Äîthe causes are visible in her lifestyle. The challenge is helping her make changes when she's already depleted. Start with what gives the biggest return for the least effort: improving sleep quality by addressing caffeine and alcohol timing will improve her energy enough to tackle other changes.

Assessment: Recognizing Long COVID/Post-Viral Syndrome

Sarah's presentation is consistent with Long COVID or post-viral syndrome, characterized by:

  • Post-exertional malaise (PEM): The hallmark symptom‚Äîactivity causes delayed, disproportionate worsening of symptoms
  • Cognitive dysfunction: Brain fog affecting work and daily function
  • Unrefreshing sleep: Despite more hours, sleep isn't restorative
  • Potential mitochondrial dysfunction: Research suggests COVID may directly impair mitochondria
  • Inflammation: Lingering immune activation may be present
Critical Warning

Standard exercise recommendations don't apply here. Pushing through can cause worsening. Do NOT apply typical "just exercise more" advice to Sarah. In post-viral syndromes with PEM, pushing exercise can cause significant, lasting harm. The goal is stabilization and gradual recovery, not fitness improvement. This is fundamentally different from typical fatigue coaching.

Coach Tip

When working with post-viral fatigue clients, validation is therapeutic. Sarah has likely been told her symptoms are "in her head" or that she just needs to "push through." Your role begins with believing her experience and helping her understand that PEM is a real, measurable physiological response‚Äînot a lack of willpower. This validation alone can reduce the psychological burden she's carrying.

Coaching Approach: Energy Management First

Energy Management (Pacing):

  • Help Sarah understand her "energy envelope"‚Äîthe amount of activity she can do without triggering PEM
  • Track activities and symptoms to identify triggers and limits
  • Plan rest before and after activities ("pre-rest" and recovery time)
  • Prioritize essential activities; let go of non-essentials
  • Avoid boom-bust cycles (doing too much on good days, then crashing)

Nutritional Support (Anti-inflammatory focus):

  • Mediterranean-style eating when possible
  • Easy-to-prepare meals and batch cooking on better days
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (anti-inflammatory support)
  • Consider mitochondrial support: CoQ10, NAD+ precursor, B vitamins
  • Address any nutrient deficiencies (testing may reveal issues)

Additional Testing to Request:

  • Comprehensive metabolic panel with fasting insulin
  • Full thyroid panel (not just TSH‚Äîinclude Free T3, Free T4, antibodies)
  • Vitamin D, B12, ferritin
  • hs-CRP and other inflammatory markers
  • Consider referral to Long COVID clinic or post-viral specialist

Emotional Support:

  • Validate her experience‚Äîthis is real, not "in her head"
  • Grief support for loss of her previous life and identity
  • Connection with Long COVID support communities
  • Address depression/anxiety that commonly accompany chronic illness

Assessment: Subtle Deficiencies and Transitions

Michelle's presentation suggests several potential contributors often missed by standard testing:

What Standard Testing Shows
  • TSH 3.1 = "normal thyroid"
  • Hemoglobin 12.5 = "not anemic"
  • No B12 testing done
  • No ferritin checked
  • No hormone evaluation
What May Be Missing
  • TSH 3.1 may not be optimal; Free T3/T4 not checked
  • Ferritin could be suboptimal despite "normal" hemoglobin
  • Vegetarian = high B12 deficiency risk
  • Declining estrogen affects mitochondrial function
  • Protein intake likely inadequate
Coach Tip

Michelle represents a common scenario: clients doing many things right, but subtle deficiencies and hormonal changes are impacting energy. Your role is to help them understand that fatigue isn't "just aging"‚Äîit's addressable. The combination of comprehensive testing, targeted nutrition, and exercise modification can restore vitality even in perimenopause. Frame this as optimization, not pathology.

Coaching Approach: Optimization Strategy

Testing Recommendations (to discuss with physician):

  • Full thyroid panel: TSH, Free T3, Free T4, thyroid antibodies
  • Ferritin (optimal: 50-100 ng/mL, not just "normal")
  • B12 and methylmalonic acid (more sensitive than B12 alone)
  • Vitamin D (optimal: 40-60 ng/mL)
  • Fasting insulin (assess metabolic health)

Nutrition Optimization:

  • Protein audit: Is she getting 100+ grams daily? Likely not‚Äîincrease plant proteins, consider eggs/fish if open to it
  • B12 supplementation: Essential for vegetarians (1000 mcg daily or weekly injection)
  • Iron-rich foods: Legumes, dark leafy greens, paired with vitamin C for absorption
  • CoQ10: 100-200 mg daily‚Äîlikely to help given age-related decline in natural production

Exercise Modification:

  • Add resistance training 2-3x weekly‚Äîcritical for maintaining muscle mass and mitochondria during hormonal transition
  • Consider HIIT 1x weekly if tolerated‚Äîpotent mitochondrial stimulus
  • May need to reduce cardio volume if overtrained relative to recovery capacity

Hormonal Considerations:

  • Discuss hormone testing and possibly HRT with physician
  • Hormone therapy can improve energy, sleep, and metabolic health during perimenopause
  • This is a medical decision, but coaching can support the conversation
Coach Tip

When clients are already doing many things right but still struggling, resist the temptation to add more interventions. Instead, focus on optimization: Are they doing the right things with enough precision? Is their vegetarian diet actually protein-adequate? Is their running preventing them from strength training? Sometimes the answer isn't addition‚Äîit's refinement.

Lessons Across All Cases

Common Principles
  • Fatigue has causes: It's never "just aging" or "in your head"‚Äîthere are biological reasons that can be identified and addressed
  • Standard testing misses much: Ferritin, B12, full thyroid panel, vitamin D, and fasting insulin often reveal what basic panels miss
  • Context matters: Same symptom (fatigue) requires different approaches based on pattern and presentation
  • Post-exertional malaise is different: Some fatigue conditions worsen with standard exercise recommendations‚Äîknow when to pace, not push
  • Start with high-impact, low-effort changes: Meet clients where they are‚Äîdepleted clients can't implement complex plans
  • Address foundations first: Sleep, blood sugar, stress, and obvious deficiencies before advanced interventions
  • Validation is therapeutic: Being believed and taken seriously improves outcomes, especially for chronic fatigue

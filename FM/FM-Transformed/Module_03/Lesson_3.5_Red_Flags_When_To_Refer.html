Connection to Your Journey: You've learned to gather comprehensive health histories, build detailed timelines, and recognize symptom patterns across functional medicine systems. Now comes perhaps the most critical skill in your entire toolkit: knowing when something is beyond your scope and requires immediate medical attention. This lesson could save a life‚Äîand it will certainly protect both you and your clients from serious harm.

As a functional medicine health coach, you'll often be the first person to hear about a client's symptoms in detail. While you're not diagnosing or treating conditions, you have a crucial responsibility: recognizing when to stop coaching and refer immediately. Missing a red flag can have serious or fatal consequences. This lesson will equip you with the knowledge and language to make life-saving referral decisions with confidence.

What You'll Learn
  • Identify emergency red flag symptoms requiring immediate 911 calls
  • Recognize warning signs warranting same-day physician evaluation
  • Understand concerning symptoms requiring prompt medical follow-up
  • Determine which clinical presentations fall outside health coaching scope
  • Develop clear, supportive language for referral conversations
  • Build a professional referral network before you need it
Critical Understanding

As a health coach, you are not diagnosing or treating medical conditions. Your role is to support health optimization through lifestyle and behavior change. When you encounter red flags, your job is to refer‚Äînot to investigate, treat, or reassure. When in doubt, always refer. It's far better to be overly cautious than to miss something serious.

Emergency Red Flags: Call 911 Immediately

These symptoms require emergency medical services without delay. If a client reports any of these during a session, stop the session immediately and advise them to call 911 or go to the emergency room. Do not attempt to gather more history or provide coaching advice.

üö® EMERGENCY: Immediate Medical Attention Required

Cardiac/Cardiovascular Emergencies:

  • Chest pain or pressure, especially with arm/jaw pain, shortness of breath, or sweating
  • Sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life")
  • Sudden difficulty speaking, facial drooping, or one-sided weakness (stroke signs)
  • Severe shortness of breath or difficulty breathing at rest

Neurological Emergencies:

  • Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
  • Seizures (especially first-time or prolonged beyond 5 minutes)
  • Sudden confusion, disorientation, or altered mental status
  • Sudden vision changes or complete vision loss

Other Life-Threatening Emergencies:

  • Severe abdominal pain with rigid, board-like abdomen
  • Vomiting blood or blood in stool (bright red or black/tarry)
  • Severe allergic reaction (throat swelling, difficulty breathing, widespread hives)
  • Active suicidal ideation with specific plan and immediate intent
  • Signs of severe dehydration with confusion (no urine output, extreme thirst, altered consciousness)
Coach Tip

Practice your emergency language NOW. When a client describes chest pain during a session, you need to respond instantly‚Äînot fumble for words. Write out your emergency script: "What you're describing needs immediate medical attention. I need you to hang up and call 911 right now. Please text me when you're safe so I know you got help." Keep this visible during all client sessions.

Urgent Red Flags: Same-Day Medical Evaluation

These symptoms warrant evaluation by a physician within hours‚Äîthe same day you hear about them. While not necessarily requiring emergency services, they cannot wait for a routine appointment days or weeks away.

üî∂ URGENT: Same-Day Medical Evaluation Needed

Infection/Fever Concerns:

  • Fever over 103¬∞F (39.4¬∞C) in adults
  • Fever persisting more than 3 days despite treatment
  • Signs of serious infection: high fever with confusion, rapid breathing, or severe weakness
  • Urinary symptoms (painful urination, frequent urge) combined with fever or severe back pain (possible kidney infection)

Digestive Urgencies:

  • Severe abdominal pain that's worsening or migrating
  • Any blood in stool or vomit
  • Inability to keep any food or fluids down for 24+ hours
  • Severe diarrhea with signs of dehydration (dizziness, decreased urination, extreme thirst)

Pain Requiring Urgent Assessment:

  • Sudden severe headache unlike any previous headaches
  • Severe pain in any location that's new, unexplained, and escalating
  • Testicular pain (possible torsion‚Äîthis is a true emergency in males)

Mental Health Urgencies:

  • Suicidal thoughts (even without immediate plan‚Äîthis needs professional support NOW)
  • Psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, severe paranoia)
  • Severe panic or anxiety that feels uncontrollable or different from previous episodes
  • Self-harm behavior (cutting, burning, or other intentional injury)
Coach Tip

Don't downplay urgent symptoms. Clients often minimize concerning signs: "It's probably nothing..." or "I don't want to bother the doctor..." Your role is to override that hesitation with clear guidance: "I understand you don't want to overreact, but this symptom needs medical eyes on it today. Will you call your doctor's office right now, or would you prefer to go to urgent care? I'll wait while you make the call."

Concerning Signs: Prompt Medical Follow-Up

These findings aren't immediately life-threatening, but they warrant medical evaluation within days to a couple of weeks. Don't let these slide‚Äîthey can indicate serious underlying conditions that are easier to treat when caught early.

‚ö° Concerning Symptoms Requiring Medical Evaluation

Unexplained Changes

  • Unintentional weight loss (more than 5% body weight in one month)
  • Night sweats that drench clothing and sheets (not hot flashes)
  • Persistent fatigue that doesn't improve with rest or sleep
  • New lumps or masses anywhere on the body
  • Changes in moles (size, shape, color, or border irregularity)

Digestive Concerns

  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or sensation of food sticking
  • Persistent change in bowel habits (new constipation or diarrhea pattern)
  • Blood in stool, even if intermittent
  • Persistent abdominal pain or discomfort
  • Unexplained nausea or vomiting lasting more than a few days

Neurological Concerns

  • Progressive weakness or numbness in any body part
  • Persistent headaches with a new or changing pattern
  • Memory problems that are noticeably worsening
  • Balance problems or coordination changes
  • Tremors or involuntary movements that are new

Other Warning Signs

  • Persistent cough lasting more than 3 weeks
  • Shortness of breath with normal daily activities
  • Breast changes (new lumps, nipple discharge, skin changes)
  • Abnormal bleeding (vaginal bleeding between periods or after menopause, rectal bleeding)
  • Skin lesions that won't heal or keep growing
Coach Tip

Document and follow up on referrals. When you identify concerning symptoms and suggest medical evaluation, note this in your client file with the date. At your next session, ask: "Did you get a chance to see your doctor about the [symptom]? What did they say?" If they haven't followed through, gently but firmly revisit why it's important. Your persistence could catch something serious.

Beyond Your Scope: When to Refer to Specialists

Even without acute red flags, certain situations require specialist involvement from the start. These are NOT conditions you manage independently as a health coach‚Äîyou can support clients alongside their medical team, but you cannot replace specialized care.

1

Active Eating Disorders

If you suspect anorexia, bulimia, binge eating disorder, or orthorexia, refer immediately to an eating disorder specialist. These conditions can be life-threatening and require specialized psychiatric and nutritional treatment. Do not attempt to coach nutrition changes with active eating disorders.

2

Uncontrolled Chronic Conditions

Diabetes with wildly fluctuating blood sugars, uncontrolled hypertension (consistently above 140/90), active autoimmune flares, or any chronic condition not being medically managed needs physician involvement first. Once stable, you can support lifestyle optimization.

3

Psychiatric Conditions

Clinical depression, anxiety disorders, bipolar disorder, PTSD, schizophrenia, and other mental health conditions require proper diagnosis and treatment from licensed mental health professionals. You can support wellness practices alongside their care, but you cannot replace psychiatric or psychological treatment.

4

Pregnancy

Pregnant clients need prenatal care from qualified obstetric providers. While you can support general wellness habits, any nutrition recommendations, supplement guidance, or exercise modifications during pregnancy should come from or be approved by their prenatal care team.

5

Cancer (Active or Suspected)

Any symptoms suggesting possible cancer (unexplained lumps, persistent pain, unexplained weight loss, night sweats) or active cancer diagnosis requires oncology care. You can support quality of life through stress management and gentle movement, but you must work in coordination with their oncology team.

6

Medication Management

Never advise clients to change, stop, or start medications. This includes prescription drugs, hormone replacement, insulin, thyroid medication‚Äîeverything. Even when lifestyle changes might reduce medication needs, that decision belongs exclusively to their prescribing physician. You can encourage them to discuss potential changes with their doctor.

Pause & Reflect

Think about a time you felt uncertain whether something was within your professional scope. How did you handle it? What would you do differently now with this framework? Remember: uncertainty itself is a red flag. When you're not sure, the answer is always to refer or seek supervision.

How to Have the Referral Conversation

When you identify a red flag, how you communicate matters tremendously. Your goal is to be clear, supportive, and directive without causing panic or minimizing the concern.

‚ùå What NOT to Say
  • "It's probably nothing to worry about."
  • "Let's try some supplements first and see if that helps."
  • "I'm sure it's fine, but maybe mention it to your doctor eventually."
  • "That sounds like [diagnosis]‚Äîyou should get that checked."
‚úÖ Effective Referral Language
  • "This needs medical evaluation. Here's why I'm concerned..."
  • "This is outside what I can help with as a coach. You need to see [specialist type]."
  • "I care about you, which is why I need you to get this checked today."
  • "Let's pause our work together until you've been evaluated by your doctor."
Coach Tip

Match urgency to your language and tone. For emergency situations, be direct and commanding: "I need you to hang up and call 911 now." For urgent concerns, be firm but supportive: "I'd like you to contact your doctor's office today‚Äîcan you do that right now while we're on the call?" For concerning symptoms, express genuine care: "I'm worried about what you're describing. This really needs to be evaluated medically. Will you schedule that appointment this week?"

Sample Referral Scripts by Urgency Level

üí¨

Referral Language Examples

Adapt these to your natural speaking style

For Emergency Situations (chest pain, stroke signs, severe bleeding):

"What you're describing sounds like it could be serious and needs immediate medical attention. I need you to hang up and call 911 right now, or have someone drive you to the emergency room immediately. This isn't something we should try to manage in our coaching session. Please go now, and text me when you're safe so I know you got help."

For Urgent Concerns (high fever, severe pain, suspected infection):

"I'm concerned about what you've shared. This is something that needs to be evaluated by a doctor today‚Äînot next week or when you can fit it in. Can you contact your physician's office right now? If they can't see you today, would you feel comfortable going to an urgent care clinic? I'd feel much better if you got this checked before our next session. Will you do that?"

For Concerning Symptoms (weight loss, persistent pain, new lumps):

"The symptoms you're describing‚Äîthe unexplained weight loss and night sweats‚Äîare things that really need to be medically evaluated. This isn't something I can address as a health coach, and I don't want to proceed with our work until we know what's going on. I'd like you to schedule an appointment with your doctor within the next week or two to get this checked out. Can you commit to doing that?"

For Scope Issues (eating disorders, psychiatric conditions, pregnancy):

"What you're dealing with is beyond what I can help with as a health coach. You need to work with a [eating disorder specialist/therapist/prenatal care provider] to properly address this. I can absolutely continue to support you with [specific lifestyle factors] alongside that specialized care, but they need to be your primary resource for this issue. Would you like help finding a qualified provider in your area?"

Mental Health Red Flags: Special Considerations

Mental health deserves dedicated attention because you may be the first person a client opens up to about suicidal thoughts, self-harm, or severe depression. How you respond in these moments can literally save a life.

üß† Mental Health Assessment Framework

üö® IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED (stop session, ensure safety now):

  • Active suicidal ideation with specific plan and immediate intent: "I'm planning to take these pills tonight when my family is asleep"
  • Current self-harm behavior: "I cut myself this morning" or "I burned myself yesterday"
  • Homicidal thoughts: "I'm going to hurt [specific person]"
  • Psychotic symptoms: Hearing voices commanding harm, delusional thinking with paranoia, complete break from reality

üî∂ URGENT REFERRAL NEEDED (same day to mental health professional):

  • Passive suicidal ideation: "I wish I wasn't here anymore" or "Everyone would be better off without me"
  • Severe depression: Can't get out of bed, stopped eating, withdrawn from all activities
  • Panic attacks: Frequent, severe, or causing avoidance of normal activities
  • Trauma response: Flashbacks, nightmares, or dissociation affecting daily functioning
  • Substance abuse: Using alcohol/drugs to cope, increased use, or affecting work/relationships

üí° What to Say When Mental Health Referral Is Needed:

"Thank you for trusting me with this. What you're experiencing is real and important, and you absolutely deserve support from someone specifically trained to help with this. I care about you, which is exactly why I want to connect you with a therapist or counselor who can really help. I can still support you with your health and wellness goals, but this piece needs specialized professional care. Would you be open to that? Can we look up some options together right now?"

üìû Crisis Resources to Share with Clients

Keep these numbers readily available‚Äîsave them in your phone and share them proactively with clients experiencing mental health struggles:

  • National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988 (call or text, 24/7)
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741 (24/7 support)
  • SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357 (substance abuse and mental health, 24/7)
  • Emergency Services: 911 (for immediate danger)
  • Veterans Crisis Line: 988 then press 1, or text 838255

Building Your Referral Network

The time to build your referral network is before you need it. When a client presents with an urgent concern, you don't want to be scrambling to find appropriate providers. Develop these relationships proactively.

üè• Essential Referral Partners to Establish

Primary Care Physician

For general medical concerns, routine check-ups, and initial workup of symptoms. Ideally, find someone open to functional and integrative approaches.

Functional Medicine MD/DO

For complex functional medicine cases requiring prescription authority, advanced testing, or medical oversight of protocols.

Licensed Therapist/Psychologist

For mental health support, trauma processing, anxiety, depression, and general psychological wellness. Find providers who understand the mind-body connection.

Psychiatrist

For psychiatric medication management, severe mental illness, and complex cases requiring both therapy and pharmacological intervention.

Registered Dietitian (RD)

For medical nutrition therapy, eating disorders, diabetes management requiring carb counting, and any condition where insurance requires an RD credential.

Gastroenterologist

For serious digestive issues requiring endoscopy, colonoscopy, or diagnosis of IBD, IBS, celiac disease, and other GI conditions.

Coach Tip

Create a referral resource document for clients. Maintain an updated list of trusted providers in your area (or who provide virtual care): functional medicine doctors, therapists, specialists, urgent care clinics, crisis hotlines. When you need to refer, you can immediately share this document. Include brief descriptions: "Dr. Smith is excellent with complex hormone cases and takes time to really listen." This reduces the barrier to clients actually following through.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

A 55-year-old male client reports sudden chest pain with left arm numbness during your coaching call. What is your immediate response?

Answer: Stop the session immediately and direct him to call 911 or have someone drive him to the emergency room NOW. This is a potential heart attack‚Äîa life-threatening emergency. Do not try to gather more information or provide any coaching. Your only job is ensuring he gets emergency medical care immediately. Follow up later to confirm he's safe.

2

A client mentions she's been having passive suicidal thoughts ("I wish I could just disappear") but has no plan. She says she's mentioned this to friends but hasn't seen a therapist. What do you do?

Answer: This requires urgent mental health referral‚Äîsame day if possible, definitely within 24-48 hours. Even passive suicidal ideation needs professional support. Say something like: "Thank you for trusting me with this. What you're experiencing is real and serious, and you deserve professional support. I need you to see a therapist or counselor right away‚Äînot next month, but this week. Can we look up options together right now? I can also give you crisis hotline numbers (988) to use anytime you need support." Do NOT minimize this or wait to see if it improves.

3

During intake, you learn a client has lost 15 pounds in the last month without trying. She also mentions increasing fatigue and occasional night sweats. She's seen her doctor who ran "basic bloodwork" that came back normal. The doctor told her it's probably stress. What's your appropriate response?

Answer: These are concerning red flags (unexplained weight loss, fatigue, night sweats) that warrant more thorough medical evaluation‚Äîeven though initial labs were normal. Say: "I'm glad you had some initial testing, but these symptoms‚Äîthe unintentional weight loss, fatigue, and night sweats‚Äîare concerning enough that I think you need more comprehensive evaluation. These could indicate several conditions that need to be ruled out. I'd like you to either get a second opinion or ask your doctor about additional testing before we proceed with coaching. Would you be comfortable doing that?" Do not proceed with coaching until this is medically evaluated more thoroughly.

4

A client with Type 2 diabetes mentions her blood sugars have been much better since making dietary changes you suggested. She asks, "Do you think I could cut my metformin dose in half?" How do you respond?

Answer: "That's wonderful that your blood sugars are improving! However, any changes to your medication‚Äîincluding dose adjustments‚Äîneed to come from your prescribing doctor. What I can do is encourage you to share these improved numbers with your physician and ask if they think a medication adjustment might be appropriate now. But the decision has to be theirs, not mine. Will you bring this up at your next appointment?" NEVER advise medication changes‚Äîthis is absolutely outside your scope and potentially dangerous.

Key Takeaways
  • Emergency red flags (chest pain, stroke signs, severe bleeding, active suicidal intent) require immediate 911 call‚Äîstop session immediately
  • Urgent concerns (high fever with infection signs, severe pain, psychotic symptoms) need same-day medical evaluation
  • Concerning symptoms (unexplained weight loss, night sweats, new lumps, persistent pain) warrant prompt medical evaluation within days to weeks
  • Certain conditions are always beyond your scope: active eating disorders, uncontrolled chronic disease, psychiatric conditions, pregnancy complications, cancer
  • Never advise medication changes‚Äîthis includes stopping, starting, or adjusting doses. Always defer to prescribing physician
  • Develop clear, supportive referral language matched to urgency level‚Äîbe directive, not wishy-washy
  • Build your referral network proactively‚Äîknow who to refer to before you need them
  • When in doubt, refer. It's always better to be overly cautious than to miss something serious

Case Study 1: The Supplement Suggestion

üìã

The Supplement Dilemma

When Clients Ask for Specific Product Recommendations

üë©

Sarah, Age 42

Your client for 3 months ‚Ä¢ Experiencing fatigue ‚Ä¢ Asking about B12 supplements

"I've been reading online about B12 deficiency, and I think that might be causing my fatigue. Should I start taking B12 supplements? What brand do you recommend? What dosage?"

You know from your functional nutrition training that B12 deficiency is indeed common and can cause fatigue. Supplementation is generally safe, and you have products you personally use and trust. However, you also recognize this request is asking you to cross into clinical territory.

Ethical Tension

The desire to help your client quickly versus the professional boundary of not diagnosing or prescribing supplements for symptom treatment.

Analysis of Competing Considerations

Temptation to Cross the Line
  • "It's just B12‚Äîit's safe"
  • "I want to be helpful"
  • "Other coaches do this"
  • "The client expects an answer"
Professional Response
  • Fatigue has many possible causes
  • B12 deficiency requires testing to confirm
  • Underlying causes need medical assessment
  • Masking symptoms could delay proper diagnosis

Appropriate Response

"Sarah, I'm glad you're being proactive about your fatigue. B12 deficiency is definitely one possible cause, but fatigue can also stem from thyroid issues, anemia, sleep disorders, chronic stress, or several other conditions. What I recommend is that you discuss this with your doctor and request testing for B12 levels along with a comprehensive metabolic panel. Once you have that information, we can work together on dietary strategies to support your energy, and your doctor can guide any supplementation if testing shows deficiency."

Coach Tip

The "Test, Don't Guess" Principle: When clients ask about supplements for specific symptoms, redirect them to get proper testing first. This protects them from self-treating potentially serious conditions and establishes you as a coach who respects professional boundaries. Try: "Let's get the data first so we're not guessing. Your doctor can run tests, and then we'll have real information to work with."

What This Case Teaches

  • Scope clarity isn't about being unhelpful‚Äîit's about ensuring clients get appropriate care
  • Recommending specific supplements to treat symptoms = diagnosing and prescribing
  • You CAN discuss general nutrition strategies; you CANNOT prescribe targeted supplementation for medical symptoms
  • Referring to appropriate providers demonstrates professionalism, not inadequacy

Case Study 2: The Cry for Help

üìã

Mental Health Crisis

When a Client Discloses Suicidal Ideation

üë®

Michael, Age 38

Your client for 6 months ‚Ä¢ Struggling with health setbacks ‚Ä¢ Recently divorced

During a routine coaching session focused on nutrition challenges, Michael suddenly says: "Sometimes I wonder if it's even worth it anymore. I've been having thoughts that maybe everyone would be better off without me. I don't know... maybe I should just end it all."

Your heart races. This is clearly beyond health coaching scope, but Michael is looking at you, vulnerable and hurting. What do you do?

Critical Situation

This is a mental health emergency requiring immediate professional intervention. Your response in the next few minutes could be life-saving.

Why This Requires Immediate Action

  • Suicidal ideation is always outside health coaching scope
  • This requires professional mental health assessment and intervention
  • Confidentiality limitations apply when client safety is at risk
  • You are not qualified to assess suicide risk or provide mental health treatment

Immediate Response Protocol

1

Stay Present & Calm

"Michael, thank you for trusting me with this. What you're feeling is important, and I want to make sure you get the right support."

2

Assess Immediate Safety

"Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself right now? Do you have a plan?"

3

Connect to Resources

"I need you to call the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline right now: 988. They have trained counselors available 24/7. Will you call them, or would you like me to help you make that call?"

4

Follow-Up Plan

If immediate danger exists, do not leave client alone. Call 911 if necessary. Document the incident and your response.

Coach Tip

Emergency Mental Health Resources to Have Ready: Always keep these numbers accessible: National Suicide Prevention Lifeline (988), Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741), local emergency services (911). Include these resources in your client intake materials. When a crisis occurs, you need to act immediately‚Äînot search for phone numbers.

What This Case Teaches

  • Mental health crises require immediate professional intervention
  • Your role is to recognize the emergency and connect client to appropriate resources
  • Never attempt to "coach through" suicidal ideation‚Äîthis is not within scope
  • Confidentiality yields to safety concerns‚Äîprotecting life takes precedence
  • Document everything: what client said, your response, resources provided

Case Study 3: The Skeptical Spouse

üìã

Confidentiality Challenge

When Family Members Demand Information

üë©

Jennifer, Age 45

Your client for 2 months ‚Ä¢ Husband pays for sessions ‚Ä¢ Making slow but steady progress

Jennifer's husband Robert calls you, clearly frustrated: "I'm paying for these coaching sessions, and I have a right to know what's going on. Jennifer says she's working on her health, but I don't see any changes. What are you two actually doing in these sessions? Is she following your recommendations? What exactly is she eating?"

His tone suggests he expects immediate answers. After all, he's footing the bill. But something feels wrong about this conversation.

Ethical Tension

The person paying for services versus the person receiving them‚Äîwho owns the right to confidentiality?

Analysis of the Situation

Confidentiality Principles at Stake

üîí Core Principle

Confidentiality belongs to the client, not to the person paying for services. Jennifer is your client‚Äîshe is the one who decides what information can be shared.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Risk

Sharing information without consent violates professional ethics, damages trust, and could expose you to legal liability.

üí° The Reality

Family dynamics often complicate health coaching. Spouses may be supportive or may undermine progress. You must protect the coaching relationship.

‚úÖ The Solution

Address payment arrangements and communication expectations during intake, before issues arise.

Appropriate Response

"Robert, I appreciate your investment in Jennifer's health. However, I'm bound by professional confidentiality. Even though you're paying for the sessions, Jennifer is my client, and I cannot discuss her sessions without her explicit written permission. What I can do is ask Jennifer if she'd be comfortable signing a release allowing me to share general progress updates with you, or perhaps the three of us could have a conversation together where Jennifer decides what she wants to share. Would either of those options work for you?"

Coach Tip

Prevent This Scenario During Intake: When someone other than the client is paying for services, address confidentiality explicitly in your intake process. Use a clear consent form stating: "Even if someone else pays for coaching services, all session content remains confidential to the client unless written authorization is provided." Have conversations about family involvement expectations upfront, not in crisis.

Follow-Up Action

  1. Contact Jennifer immediately: "Jennifer, your husband called asking about your progress. I didn't share any information without your permission, but I wanted you to know about his call. How would you like to handle this?"
  2. Document the interaction: Record the date, time, what was requested, and your response
  3. Revisit boundaries with Jennifer: Discuss whether she wants to involve her husband in any way, and if so, establish clear parameters with written consent
  4. Review your policies: Ensure your consent forms clearly address third-party payment situations

What This Case Teaches

  • Payment does not equal ownership of confidential information
  • Confidentiality protects the therapeutic relationship and client trust
  • Spouses, parents, and others may pressure you‚Äîhold your boundaries
  • Address potential confidentiality issues proactively during intake
  • When in doubt, get written consent before sharing anything

Case Study 4: The Friend Referral

üìã

Dual Relationship Dilemma

When Personal and Professional Lines Blur

üë§

Amanda

Your close friend's neighbor ‚Ä¢ Interested in coaching ‚Ä¢ Would be convenient to work with

Your close friend Lisa excitedly tells you: "My neighbor Amanda is struggling with her health‚Äîshe's gained weight, feels exhausted all the time, and really needs help. I told her all about you and how amazing you are. She wants to start coaching with you next week! Isn't that great? You two will get along so well!"

Amanda texts you directly: "Lisa gave me your number! Can we set up sessions? Maybe we could meet at Lisa's house since we're all friends? That would be so much more comfortable than an office."

Red Flags Present

Multiple boundary concerns: coaching someone in your social circle, informal meeting locations, assumption of existing relationship, and the "we're all friends" dynamic that could complicate professional boundaries.

Understanding Dual Relationships

A dual relationship exists when you have both a professional relationship and another relationship with someone‚Äîsocial, financial, familial, etc. These relationships create ethical complications because:

  • Objectivity becomes difficult: You may feel pressure to soften feedback or avoid difficult conversations
  • Boundaries blur: Social interactions outside sessions complicate the coaching relationship
  • Confidentiality is harder to maintain: Mutual friends may ask questions or make assumptions
  • Conflict has wider consequences: If the coaching relationship sours, it affects your friendship with Lisa and potentially your social circle

Decision-Making Framework

What Seems Appealing
  • Easy referral‚Äîno marketing needed
  • "She already likes and trusts me"
  • Convenient logistics
  • Supporting a friend's friend
Professional Risks
  • Compromised boundaries from start
  • Lisa may expect updates or feel involved
  • Social connections could inhibit honest communication
  • Meeting at mutual friend's house = no professional space

Appropriate Response

To Amanda: "Amanda, I'm glad Lisa thinks I could be helpful! I want to be thoughtful about taking on clients who are connected to my personal life, as it can sometimes complicate the coaching relationship. Let me suggest two alternatives: (1) I can refer you to an excellent colleague who practices very similarly to me, or (2) If you'd prefer to work with me, we'd need to establish very clear professional boundaries‚Äîmeeting in my office, maintaining confidentiality even from Lisa, and treating this as a strictly professional relationship. Which option feels better to you?"

To Lisa: "Lisa, I appreciate you thinking of me for Amanda. I want to handle this professionally‚Äîeither by referring Amanda to a colleague, or if she works with me, by maintaining strict boundaries that mean I won't be able to discuss her coaching with you. I know that might feel strange, but it's important for protecting both Amanda and our friendship."

Coach Tip

The "Friendship Test" for Dual Relationships: Ask yourself: "If this coaching relationship ends badly, would it damage an important personal relationship?" If yes, strongly consider referring to a colleague instead. Also ask: "Would I hesitate to give tough feedback because of our social connection?" If yes, that's a sign the dual relationship will compromise your effectiveness. Protecting your personal relationships AND your professional integrity sometimes means saying no to convenient opportunities.

What This Case Teaches

  • Not all referrals are appropriate to accept‚Äîeven when they seem easy
  • Dual relationships compromise objectivity, boundaries, and confidentiality
  • Professional space matters‚Äîmeeting at a friend's house is not appropriate
  • Referring to colleagues protects both professional and personal relationships
  • It's better to decline a client than to enter a problematic coaching relationship

Case Study 5: The Urgent Text

üìã

After-Hours Emergency

When Clients Reach Out With Urgent Symptoms

üë®

David, Age 51

Your client for 4 months ‚Ä¢ Generally healthy ‚Ä¢ Working on weight loss and energy

At 11:30 PM, you receive this text from David: "Severe pain in my abdomen, upper right side. Started about an hour ago and getting worse. Should I take Tylenol? Maybe it's just something I ate? Do you think I need to go to the ER or can this wait until morning?"

Your phone keeps buzzing: "I hate to bother you this late, but you know my health history. What do you think this could be?"

Scope Violation Alert

This request asks you to assess symptoms, diagnose the cause, and advise on medical treatment‚Äîall of which are completely outside health coaching scope.

Why This is Dangerous Territory

  • Symptom assessment is medical practice: You are not qualified to evaluate abdominal pain
  • Severe abdominal pain can indicate serious conditions: Appendicitis, gallstones, pancreatitis, cardiac issues presenting as abdominal pain
  • Advising someone to wait could delay life-saving treatment
  • Client may later claim: "My coach told me it wasn't serious, so I didn't go to the ER"

The Only Appropriate Response

Immediate text back: "David, I cannot assess symptoms or advise on medical situations‚Äîthat's outside my scope as a health coach. Severe abdominal pain requires medical evaluation. Please call your doctor's after-hours line right now, or go to the emergency room. Do not wait until morning. Text me back that you're getting medical help."

If he pushes back: "I understand you're uncomfortable and hoping to avoid the ER, but I'm not qualified to determine if this is serious. Please get medical evaluation tonight. We can discuss it in our next coaching session after you've been evaluated."

Coach Tip

Set Communication Boundaries in Your Client Agreement: Include clear guidelines about after-hours contact: "Health coaching does not include medical advice or emergency services. If you experience urgent symptoms, contact your healthcare provider or call 911. The coach will not respond to requests for symptom assessment." Also specify: "Non-emergency questions can be emailed and will be responded to within 24-48 business hours." This protects you legally and sets appropriate client expectations.

Follow-Up Actions

  1. Document the interaction: Save the text exchange, note the time, record your response
  2. Check in next day: "David, I wanted to follow up‚Äîdid you get medical evaluation last night? How are you feeling?"
  3. Revisit boundaries: At your next session, gently but clearly reinforce scope: "I want to be helpful, but I can never advise on symptoms or medical situations. That's not within my training or scope. If something feels medically urgent, always err on the side of getting professional evaluation."
  4. Review your policies: Ensure clients understand the limits of coaching from the beginning

What This Case Teaches

  • Never assess symptoms, even when clients pressure you for opinions
  • Urgent medical situations require medical professionals, not health coaches
  • Your legal and ethical obligation is to direct clients to appropriate care
  • Set clear communication boundaries in your client agreements
  • Document all interactions where scope questions arise

The PAUSE Framework for Ethical Decisions

When you encounter a situation where the right course of action isn't immediately clear, use this framework to guide your ethical reasoning:

P

PAUSE

Don't react immediately. Recognize you're facing an ethical decision that requires thought. Buy time if needed: "That's an important question. Let me take a moment to think about the best way to support you."

A

ANALYZE

Identify the competing values and considerations. What ethical principles are at stake? What are the potential consequences of different actions? Who might be affected?

U

UNDERSTAND

Consider different perspectives. What would your client want? What does professional guidance say? What would an objective third party advise? What does your gut tell you?

S

SELECT

Choose a course of action that prioritizes client welfare and professional integrity. When in doubt, err on the side of caution and consultation.

E

EVALUATE

After the situation resolves, reflect on the outcome. What worked well? What would you do differently? What did you learn? Document the scenario to inform future decisions.

Remember

Seeking guidance from supervisors, mentors, or professional organizations is not a sign of weakness‚Äîit's a sign of professional maturity. When facing truly difficult dilemmas, consultation is wisdom.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

A client asks you to recommend a specific supplement dosage for their fatigue. Why is this outside your scope, even if you have extensive nutrition training?

Answer: Recommending specific supplements to treat symptoms constitutes diagnosing and prescribing, which requires clinical licensure. Fatigue has many possible causes that require medical assessment. Your role is to support general wellness through nutrition education, not to treat specific symptoms with targeted supplementation. The appropriate response is to refer the client to their healthcare provider for testing and clinical guidance, then work collaboratively on dietary strategies to support their energy.

2

If a client discloses suicidal thoughts during a session, what are your immediate responsibilities?

Answer: Your immediate responsibilities are: (1) Stay calm and present‚Äîdo not panic, (2) Acknowledge their pain: "Thank you for trusting me with this", (3) Assess immediate danger: "Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself right now?", (4) Connect to crisis resources immediately‚ÄîNational Suicide Prevention Lifeline (988) or 911 if imminent danger, (5) Do not attempt to "coach through" the crisis‚Äîthis is a mental health emergency requiring professional intervention, (6) Document everything: what was said, your response, resources provided. Remember: Confidentiality yields to safety. Protecting life takes absolute precedence.

3

Your client's spouse (who pays for the sessions) demands to know what you discuss in coaching. Can you share this information since they're paying for services?

Answer: No. Confidentiality belongs to the client, not to the person paying for services. Even though the spouse pays, your client is the person receiving coaching, and they own the right to confidentiality. The appropriate response is: "I'm bound by professional confidentiality. I cannot discuss sessions without your wife's explicit written permission. I'd be happy to facilitate a conversation with both of you present where she decides what she wants to share, or she could sign a release authorizing me to provide you with general progress updates." This should be addressed proactively during intake when third-party payment is involved.

4

Explain the PAUSE framework and how it helps you navigate ethical dilemmas.

Answer: The PAUSE framework provides a structured approach to ethical decision-making: P = PAUSE ‚Äî Don't react immediately; recognize you're facing an ethical decision. A = ANALYZE ‚Äî Identify competing values, potential consequences, and affected parties. U = UNDERSTAND ‚Äî Consider multiple perspectives: client needs, professional standards, potential outcomes. S = SELECT ‚Äî Choose an action that prioritizes client welfare and professional integrity. When uncertain, err toward caution and consultation. E = EVALUATE ‚Äî After resolution, reflect on what worked, what you'd do differently, and lessons learned. This framework prevents reactive decisions and builds ethical reasoning capacity over time.

5

Why might it be problematic to coach a close friend's neighbor, even if it seems like a convenient referral?

Answer: This creates a dual relationship‚Äîyou have both a professional relationship with the client and a social connection through your mutual friend. This presents multiple risks: (1) Compromised objectivity‚Äîyou may hesitate to give direct feedback due to social implications, (2) Blurred boundaries‚Äîsocial interactions outside sessions complicate the professional relationship, (3) Confidentiality challenges‚Äîyour mutual friend may ask questions or expect updates, (4) Wider consequences‚Äîif the coaching relationship ends badly, it affects your friendship and potentially your social circle. The professional response is to either refer to a colleague or establish exceptionally clear boundaries (professional meeting space only, strict confidentiality even from mutual friend, no social interactions during coaching relationship).

6

A client texts you late at night about severe abdominal pain, asking if they should go to the ER. What is the only appropriate response?

Answer: The only appropriate response is to direct them to seek immediate medical evaluation: "I cannot assess symptoms‚Äîthat's outside my scope as a health coach. Severe abdominal pain requires medical evaluation. Please call your doctor's after-hours line or go to the emergency room right now. Do not wait until morning." Never attempt to assess whether symptoms are serious or provide advice on medical situations. Your legal and ethical obligation is to direct clients to appropriate medical care. This protects both the client (who gets proper evaluation) and you (from liability for practicing outside your scope). Document the exchange and follow up the next day to ensure they received medical care.

  • Referral is a core skill‚ÄîKnowing when and how to refer clients to appropriate providers is professional competence, not failure
  • Consultation is wisdom‚ÄîSeeking guidance from mentors, supervisors, or professional organizations demonstrates professional maturity
  • Prevention through clarity‚ÄîMany ethical dilemmas can be prevented by establishing clear boundaries, policies, and expectations during client intake
  • Documentation protects everyone‚ÄîThorough records of challenging situations protect both you and your clients

A client named Janet brings her recent lab results to your session. "My doctor said my thyroid is 'fine,' but I've read that the ranges are too broad. Can you look at these and tell me if I need thyroid medication?"

These moments happen constantly in health coaching. Your response either keeps you safely within scope or crosses into territory that could harm your client and your career. Let's learn to navigate these situations.

What is Scope of Practice?

Scope of practice defines the services, procedures, and activities that a professional is authorized to perform based on their training, certification, and applicable laws. It's the boundary between what you're qualified to do and what requires different credentials.

For health coaches, scope of practice is determined by:

  • Your training and certification: What you've actually learned and are qualified to practice
  • State laws: Some states have specific regulations for health coaches or practice of medicine laws
  • Certification body standards: NBHWC, AADP, and other certifying organizations define scope
  • Your employment context: If you work within a healthcare organization, they may have additional guidelines
  • Professional ethics: Even if something is technically legal, it may not be ethical to practice beyond your competence

What Health Coaches CAN Do

Health coaching has a robust scope when practiced appropriately. Here's what's clearly within your professional boundaries:

‚úÖ WITHIN SCOPE: What You CAN Do

Health & Lifestyle Assessment
  • Gather comprehensive health history information
  • Assess lifestyle factors (diet, sleep, stress, movement, relationships)
  • Use validated questionnaires and assessment tools
  • Identify patterns and connections in health information
Coaching & Behavior Change Support
  • Use evidence-based coaching techniques (MI, stages of change, goal setting)
  • Help clients clarify their values and health vision
  • Support clients in developing and implementing action plans
  • Provide accountability and follow-up
Health Education
  • Share general health and wellness information
  • Explain how lifestyle factors affect health
  • Provide resources and information for client learning
  • Discuss research and evidence (without making medical claims)
Lifestyle Recommendations
  • Suggest dietary changes for general wellness (not to treat disease)
  • Recommend sleep hygiene practices
  • Discuss stress management strategies
  • Encourage appropriate movement and exercise
Coordination & Referral
  • Recognize when clients need medical attention and refer appropriately
  • Coordinate with healthcare providers (with client consent)
  • Support clients in communicating with their healthcare team

What Health Coaches CANNOT Do

Equally important is understanding what falls outside your scope. These activities require licensure or credentials you don't hold:

‚õî OUTSIDE SCOPE: What You CANNOT Do

Diagnosis
  • Diagnose any disease, condition, or disorder
  • Tell clients they "have" or "probably have" a condition
  • Use diagnostic labels (even informally)
  • Interpret labs to make or rule out diagnoses
Prescribing & Treatment
  • Prescribe medications or treatments
  • Advise clients to stop, change, or start medications
  • Prescribe diets to treat specific diseases
  • Recommend specific supplement doses to treat conditions
Medical Testing
  • Order medical tests or labs
  • Interpret lab results to diagnose conditions
  • Perform physical examinations
Mental Health Treatment
  • Provide psychotherapy or counseling for mental health disorders
  • Diagnose mental health conditions
  • Treat eating disorders, trauma, addiction (can support alongside proper care)
Medical Nutrition Therapy
  • Provide nutrition therapy for specific diseases (e.g., renal diet for kidney disease)
  • Create meal plans for medical conditions (reserved for RDs in many states)

The Language of Scope

Often, scope isn't just about what you do but how you say it. The right language keeps you in scope; the wrong language crosses lines:

"You have hypothyroidism." "Your symptoms are consistent with patterns I've seen in thyroid issues‚Äîyour doctor can evaluate this."
"You should take 5,000 IU of vitamin D daily." "Research suggests many people benefit from vitamin D. Your doctor can test your levels and advise on dosing."
"You can stop taking that medication." "Any medication changes should be discussed with your prescribing doctor."
"This supplement will cure your inflammation." "Some people find this supplement helpful for general wellness. Here's what the research shows."
"Your labs show you're insulin resistant." "I notice some of your values‚Äîhave you discussed these patterns with your doctor?"
"Follow this diet to treat your IBS." "Many people with digestive concerns find this dietary approach supportive. Let's experiment and see how you respond."

Navigating the Gray Areas

In practice, you'll encounter situations that aren't clearly black or white. Here's how to navigate:

üîç When You're Unsure, Ask Yourself:

1. Am I diagnosing or treating disease?

If yes, you're out of scope. Reframe to education and support.

2. Does this require specific licensure?

Medical diagnoses, prescribing, psychotherapy, and medical nutrition therapy all require licenses.

3. Would I be comfortable if a physician were watching?

If you'd be embarrassed or defensive, reconsider your approach.

4. Could this cause harm if I'm wrong?

When health consequences are serious, err on the side of referral.

5. Am I acting in the client's best interest or my ego's?

It feels good to be the expert with answers. Client safety trumps your desire to help.

Communicating Your Role to Clients

Setting clear expectations from the start prevents scope confusion:

üí¨ How to Explain Your Role

In your initial session:

"As a health coach, I'm here to support you in making lifestyle changes that optimize your health. I'm not a doctor, so I don't diagnose conditions, prescribe treatments, or give medical advice. What I do is help you implement changes in nutrition, stress, sleep, and movement‚Äîand I'll work alongside your healthcare providers to support your overall care."

When clients ask medical questions:

"That's a great question, and it's one for your doctor since it involves [diagnosis/medication/medical treatment]. What I can help with is [reframe to lifestyle factor]. Would you like to discuss that instead, or would you like to bring this question to your next doctor's appointment?"

üîë Key Takeaways

  • Scope of practice defines what you're authorized to do based on training, certification, and law
  • Health coaches CAN: assess lifestyle, coach behavior change, educate, recommend lifestyle modifications, and refer
  • Health coaches CANNOT: diagnose, prescribe, order tests, provide medical nutrition therapy, or treat disease
  • Language matters: how you say something can be the difference between in-scope and out-of-scope
  • When in doubt, refer. Client safety always comes first.
  • Set clear expectations with clients from the beginning about your role

  • Exercise Modification: Reduce chronic cardio (raises cortisol), emphasize strength training (builds muscle, improves insulin sensitivity), adequate recovery
  • Stress Management: Address chronic work stress driving cortisol elevation
  • Sleep Optimization: Prioritize 7-8 hours (poor sleep worsens insulin resistance)

Pattern Recognition: Key Principles

1

Look for Clusters, Not Isolated Values

A single "high" or "low" marker means little in isolation. Patterns across multiple related markers tell the real story.

2

Consider Functional Ranges vs. Lab Ranges

Lab "normal" doesn't equal optimal. Functional medicine uses tighter ranges that reflect health, not just absence of disease.

3

Connect Labs to Systems

Every lab abnormality reflects a functional imbalance in one or more body systems. Think: Which system(s) are struggling?

4

Ask "Why?" Multiple Times

High cholesterol ‚Üí WHY? Insulin resistance ‚Üí WHY? Chronic inflammation ‚Üí WHY? Root causes often lie upstream.

5

Prioritize Root Causes Over Symptoms

Treating surface-level symptoms (e.g., statins for cholesterol) without addressing root causes (insulin resistance, inflammation) yields limited results.

6

Recognize When Patterns Overlap

Most clients don't have one isolated issue. Gut dysfunction + insulin resistance + inflammation + nutrient deficiency often coexist and amplify each other.

Maintaining Your Scope of Practice

‚ùå Outside Health Coach Scope
  • Diagnosing diseases (e.g., "You have Hashimoto's")
  • Ordering labs independently (without practitioner supervision)
  • Interpreting labs as "normal" or "abnormal" for diagnosis
  • Prescribing medications or supplements in therapeutic doses
  • Adjusting medication dosages (e.g., thyroid, insulin)
  • Treating acute or serious medical conditions
‚úÖ Within Health Coach Scope
  • Reviewing labs with clients under practitioner supervision
  • Explaining patterns and how they relate to symptoms
  • Coaching around lifestyle and dietary changes recommended by the practitioner
  • Supporting supplement compliance (when prescribed by practitioner)
  • Tracking subjective improvements (energy, digestion, mood)
  • Educating on root causes and functional imbalances
  • Identifying when re-testing is appropriate
  • Referring back to practitioner when values fall outside targets
Golden Rule for Health Coaches

You are the bridge between lab data and daily life.

Your practitioner interprets labs and creates the clinical plan. You translate that plan into actionable steps, provide ongoing support, troubleshoot barriers, celebrate wins, and help clients stay consistent. This partnership model‚Äîpractitioner + coach‚Äîproduces better outcomes than either professional working alone.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

In Case Study #1 (Sarah's fatigue), what pattern of findings suggested subclinical hypothyroidism with poor conversion?

Answer: The pattern included: Low Free T3 + High Reverse T3 + Elevated TPO Antibodies. This combination suggests that Sarah's thyroid is producing T4, but it's not converting efficiently to the active T3 hormone. The elevated TPO antibodies indicate autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's), while the low ferritin, elevated MMA, and insulin resistance were blocking proper thyroid hormone conversion. Her TSH of 2.8 was technically "normal" but didn't capture the full picture.

2

In Case Study #2 (Michael's weight resistance), how does insulin resistance affect testosterone levels?

Answer: High insulin levels increase SHBG (sex hormone binding globulin), which binds to testosterone and makes it unavailable to cells. This reduces free testosterone‚Äîthe bioavailable form that tissues can use. In Michael's case, his total testosterone was low-normal (320 ng/dL), but his free testosterone was quite low (6.5 pg/mL) due to elevated SHBG (55 nmol/L). Low free testosterone makes it harder to build muscle and easier to store fat‚Äîcreating a vicious cycle with insulin resistance.

3

In Case Study #3 (Jennifer's IBS), why did she have multiple food sensitivities? Were the foods themselves the root cause?

Answer: Jennifer's multiple food sensitivities were a symptom, not the root cause. The underlying problem was intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"), as evidenced by elevated zonulin (68 ng/mL). When the gut lining is compromised, partially digested food proteins can cross into the bloodstream, triggering IgG immune reactions. Her root causes included: SIBO, dysbiosis, parasitic infection (Blastocystis), and low SIgA. Once these are addressed and the gut lining heals, many food sensitivities resolve‚Äîpatients can often tolerate previously reactive foods again.

4

In Case Study #4 (David's cognitive decline), why is his ApoE4/E4 genotype significant, and what does it tell us about intervention strategy?

Answer: ApoE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for Alzheimer's disease. Having two copies (E4/E4) increases lifetime risk 25-30x compared to non-carriers. However, genetics are not destiny‚Äîthey represent vulnerability, not inevitability. ApoE4 carriers are particularly sensitive to metabolic dysfunction (insulin resistance), inflammation, and poor diet. The significance is that David needs aggressive intervention: ketogenic/low-carb diet (to bypass impaired brain glucose metabolism), homocysteine reduction, omega-3 supplementation, and comprehensive lifestyle optimization. Research by Dr. Dale Bredesen shows early cognitive decline can be reversed with multi-modal protocols.

5

As a health coach, you're reviewing labs with a client that show elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT). What's your appropriate response?

Answer: Your appropriate response is to: (1) Acknowledge the findings without diagnosing ("I see your liver enzymes are elevated"), (2) Refer to the supervising practitioner for interpretation and treatment decisions, (3) Educate (if the practitioner has already discussed it) on lifestyle factors affecting liver health‚Äîexcess fructose, alcohol, processed foods, obesity, insulin resistance, (4) Support any dietary or lifestyle changes the practitioner recommends (e.g., eliminate alcohol, reduce sugar, lose weight), and (5) Track progress subjectively (energy, digestion) while the practitioner monitors labs. You do NOT diagnose fatty liver disease or prescribe treatment‚Äîthat's outside your scope.

6

What does "pattern recognition" mean in functional lab interpretation, and why is it more valuable than looking at individual markers?

Answer: Pattern recognition means identifying clusters of related abnormalities that together reveal underlying functional imbalances. For example: High fasting insulin + high triglycerides + low HDL + high TG:HDL ratio + elevated HbA1c = insulin resistance pattern. Or: Low ferritin + elevated TIBC + low % saturation = iron deficiency pattern. Individual markers can be misleading‚Äîone elevated value might be a lab error or meaningless variation. But when multiple related markers all point in the same direction, they tell a coherent story about what's happening functionally in the body. This systems-level view is what makes functional medicine interpretation so powerful.

"My doctor ran hormone tests and said everything was normal. But I feel terrible‚Äîespecially the week before my period. I gain five pounds of water weight, I can't think clearly, and I'm so irritable my family avoids me. How can everything be 'normal' when I feel this bad?"

Jennifer's conventional hormone panel showed estradiol at 85 pg/mL and progesterone at 2.3 ng/mL on day 21 of her cycle‚Äîboth technically "within range." Understanding Hormone Testing Methodologies

Before diving into specific hormones, it's essential to understand that how we test matters as much as what we test. Different testing methods measure hormones in different body compartments, providing unique insights into hormone production, metabolism, and tissue availability.

Serum (Blood) Testing
  • Measures: Total and free hormones in bloodstream
  • Best for: Thyroid hormones, baseline sex hormones, metabolic hormones
  • Advantages: Widely available, insurance covered, standardized
  • Limitations: Single point-in-time snapshot, doesn't show metabolites
Saliva Testing
  • Measures: Free (bioavailable) hormones in saliva
  • Best for: Cortisol rhythm, sex hormones (especially in hormone replacement)
  • Advantages: Non-invasive, multiple samples possible, reflects tissue levels
  • Limitations: Can be affected by oral contamination, timing-sensitive

Additional Testing Methods

üíß Dried Urine (DUTCH Test)

Measures: Hormone metabolites and pathways over 24 hours. Provides comprehensive view of hormone production, metabolism, and detoxification. Especially valuable for assessing estrogen metabolism, cortisol patterns, and neurotransmitter metabolites.

üß™ 24-Hour Urine Collection

Measures: Total hormone output over full day. Useful for cortisol rhythm assessment and comprehensive metabolite analysis. More cumbersome to collect but provides integrated daily picture rather than single time-point.

Coach Tip

When discussing hormone testing with clients, help them understand that no single test tells the whole story. Try saying: "Think of hormone testing like taking photographs of a moving river. Serum testing is like one snapshot at the riverbank. Saliva testing shows what's flowing into the tissues. Urine testing reveals where the water has been and where it's going. Each perspective adds valuable information to understand the complete picture of your hormonal health."

Sex Hormone Testing: Estrogen, Progesterone, and Testosterone

Sex hormones orchestrate far more than reproduction‚Äîthey influence energy, mood, cognition, metabolism, bone health, cardiovascular function, and more. Understanding how to assess these hormones properly requires attention to timing, context, and patterns.

Estrogen Testing: Beyond Estradiol

Conventional testing typically measures only estradiol (E2)‚Äîthe most potent estrogen. But functional testing often includes the full estrogen family and their metabolites:

E1

Estrone

Primary estrogen after menopause. Can be converted from fat tissue. Elevated levels may increase breast cancer risk, especially when poorly metabolized.

E2

Estradiol

Most potent estrogen during reproductive years. Crucial for menstrual cycle, bone health, brain function, cardiovascular protection. Optimal levels vary by cycle phase and age.

E3

Estriol

Weakest estrogen. Predominates during pregnancy. Often considered protective. Used in some hormone replacement protocols for gentler estrogenic effects.

üìä

Estrogen Metabolites

How estrogen is broken down matters enormously. 2-OH pathway is generally protective. 4-OH and 16-OH pathways may increase cancer risk if excessive. Methylation of metabolites (2-MeOH, 4-MeOH) is protective.

Critical Insight

Estrogen dominance isn't always about high estrogen‚Äîit's often about low progesterone relative to estrogen or poor estrogen metabolism. A woman with estradiol at 60 pg/mL and progesterone at 15 ng/mL may have estrogen dominance symptoms, while another woman with estradiol at 100 pg/mL and progesterone at 25 ng/mL may feel balanced. The ratio and metabolism matter more than absolute levels.

Progesterone: The Calming, Balancing Hormone

Progesterone balances estrogen's proliferative effects and has its own crucial roles in mood stability, sleep quality, bone health, and pregnancy support. Testing considerations include:

üåü Progesterone Testing Key Points

Timing is Everything

Serum testing: Should be done during luteal phase (approximately day 19-21 of 28-day cycle) when progesterone peaks. Testing too early or too late gives misleading results.

Optimal range: 10-25 ng/mL for women trying to conceive; 5-20 ng/mL for general health. Below 5 ng/mL suggests inadequate ovulation.

Saliva vs. Serum Differences

Saliva progesterone reflects free, bioavailable hormone‚Äîwhat's actually available to tissues. Levels are typically 50-100 times lower than serum.

Especially important when monitoring bioidentical hormone replacement therapy (HRT), as saliva better reflects tissue availability than serum.

Progesterone Metabolites

Allopregnanolone: Key metabolite that enhances GABA activity‚Äîcrucial for calming effects. Low levels associated with anxiety and insomnia.

Testing metabolites helps understand why some women feel progesterone's calming effects strongly while others don't despite adequate serum levels.

Clinical Patterns

Low progesterone: Irregular cycles, PMS, anxiety, insomnia, heavy periods, infertility, spotting before periods.

Progesterone-to-estrogen ratio: Should be approximately 200-300:1 when both measured in same units (pg/mL). Lower ratios suggest estrogen dominance even with "normal" individual values.

Coach Tip

When clients receive hormone results, help them understand context matters. Try explaining: "Your progesterone level of 8 ng/mL might look okay on paper, but when we compare it to your estrogen level, we see your body may be experiencing relative estrogen dominance‚Äîlike having the gas pedal (estrogen) pressed harder than the brake pedal (progesterone). This imbalance, not the individual numbers, often explains symptoms like PMS, breast tenderness, and mood swings."

Testosterone: Not Just a Male Hormone

Testosterone is crucial for both men and women‚Äîsupporting muscle mass, bone density, libido, energy, motivation, and cognitive function. Testing should include:

Comprehensive Testosterone Assessment

üìä Total Testosterone

Men: Optimal 600-900 ng/dL (though functional range may extend to 1000+ in younger men). Women: Optimal 30-70 ng/dL. Low levels associated with fatigue, low libido, muscle loss, depression.

üîì Free Testosterone

The bioavailable fraction not bound to SHBG (sex hormone binding globulin). More clinically relevant than total testosterone. Can be directly measured or calculated from total testosterone and SHBG.

üîí SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin)

Carrier protein that binds sex hormones. High SHBG: Reduces free testosterone availability‚Äîmay cause symptoms despite "normal" total levels. Often elevated with high estrogen, hyperthyroidism, or aging. Low SHBG: Increases free testosterone‚Äîmay contribute to PCOS, insulin resistance, or acne.

üíß DHT (Dihydrotestosterone)

Potent testosterone metabolite. Elevated: Associated with hair loss, acne, prostate issues in men. Low: May indicate 5-alpha-reductase deficiency or enzyme inhibition (from medications like finasteride).

Adrenal Function Assessment: Cortisol and DHEA

Adrenal hormones govern your stress response, energy production, immune function, and inflammation control. Unlike sex hormones measured at single time points, cortisol assessment requires understanding its daily rhythm.

Cortisol Testing: Capturing the Daily Pattern

Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm‚Äîhighest upon waking (cortisol awakening response or CAR), gradually declining throughout the day, lowest at bedtime. Disrupted rhythm often matters more than absolute levels.

Single-Point Serum Cortisol
  • Commonly ordered by conventional doctors
  • Useful for detecting extreme dysfunction (Addison's disease, Cushing's syndrome)
  • Major limitation: Misses rhythm disruption‚Äîyou can have "normal" morning cortisol but completely flattened daily curve
4-Point Saliva or Urine Cortisol
  • Samples collected at waking, mid-morning, afternoon, bedtime
  • Reveals cortisol rhythm and adrenal function patterns
  • Identifies: High cortisol, low cortisol, or dysregulated rhythm
üìà

High Cortisol Pattern

Characteristics: Elevated throughout day, especially evening. Symptoms: Anxiety, insomnia, weight gain (especially abdominal), elevated blood sugar, high blood pressure, immune suppression. Common causes: Chronic stress, insufficient stress management, excessive caffeine, blood sugar dysregulation.

üìâ

Low Cortisol Pattern

Characteristics: Flat curve with low output throughout day. Symptoms: Severe fatigue (especially morning), salt cravings, low blood pressure, poor stress tolerance, frequent infections. Common causes: Chronic stress leading to "adrenal exhaustion," autoimmune conditions, chronic inflammation.

üîÑ

Reversed Cortisol Pattern

Characteristics: Low morning cortisol, elevated evening cortisol. Symptoms: Morning fatigue, difficulty waking, evening "second wind," insomnia, blood sugar instability. Common causes: Chronic stress with circadian disruption, shift work, late-night eating, excessive evening screen time.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Variable Pattern

Characteristics: Unpredictable cortisol spikes and drops throughout day. Symptoms: Energy crashes, mood swings, difficulty managing stress, blood sugar fluctuations. Common causes: Severe stress, blood sugar dysregulation, irregular eating patterns, poor sleep quality.

Pause & Reflect

Think about clients you've worked with who experience energy fluctuations throughout the day. How might understanding their cortisol rhythm help you better support their health goals? What questions might you ask to assess whether cortisol testing could provide valuable insights?

DHEA: The Adrenal Reserve Marker

DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone) and its sulfated form DHEA-S serve as markers of adrenal reserve‚Äîthe body's capacity to respond to stress. DHEA has its own important roles:

üåü DHEA Functions and Testing

Multiple Roles

Precursor hormone: Converts to testosterone and estrogen. Brain health: Supports cognitive function, mood, memory. Immune function: Enhances immune response, reduces inflammation. Anti-aging: Declines with age; higher levels associated with longevity.

Testing Considerations

DHEA-S preferred: More stable than DHEA; doesn't fluctuate throughout day. Optimal ranges: Men 350-500 Œºg/dL; Women 200-350 Œºg/dL (varies by age). Low DHEA-S: Suggests depleted adrenal reserve, chronic stress, aging, chronic illness.

The cortisol-to-DHEA ratio provides valuable insight into stress adaptation. Healthy individuals maintain balanced ratio. Chronic stress typically elevates cortisol while depleting DHEA, creating unfavorable ratio associated with accelerated aging, immune dysfunction, and metabolic problems.

Coach Tip

When discussing adrenal testing with clients, help them understand the rhythm matters as much as the levels. Try saying: "Think of your cortisol rhythm like the natural sunrise and sunset. When working properly, cortisol rises like the sun in the morning, giving you energy to start your day, then gradually sets toward evening, allowing you to wind down for sleep. Stress, poor sleep habits, or irregular eating can disrupt this natural rhythm‚Äîlike experiencing sunset at noon and sunrise at midnight. Testing your cortisol pattern throughout the day helps us see where your rhythm has gone off track."

Metabolic Hormones: Thyroid, Insulin, and Leptin

Metabolic hormones regulate energy production, weight management, and overall metabolic health. Functional testing goes beyond basic screening to understand the complete metabolic picture.

Comprehensive Thyroid Testing

While conventional medicine typically orders only TSH, comprehensive functional thyroid testing includes multiple markers that reveal different aspects of thyroid function:

Complete Thyroid Panel

üî¨ TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

Conventional range: 0.5-5.0 mIU/L. Functional optimal: 1.0-2.0 mIU/L. Elevated TSH suggests hypothyroidism; suppressed TSH suggests hyperthyroidism. However, TSH alone misses many thyroid problems.

üíä Free T4 (Thyroxine)

Inactive thyroid hormone produced by thyroid gland. Must convert to T3 for activity. Optimal: Upper half of reference range. Low with elevated TSH confirms hypothyroidism. Low with normal TSH may indicate pituitary dysfunction.

‚ö° Free T3 (Triiodothyronine)

Active thyroid hormone‚Äîthe one that actually works at cellular level. Most important thyroid marker for symptoms. Can be low despite normal TSH and T4 if conversion is impaired. Optimal: Upper half of reference range.

üîÑ Reverse T3 (rT3)

Inactive form of T3 created under stress, illness, or nutrient deficiencies. Acts as metabolic brake. Elevated rT3: Blocks T3 receptors, causing hypothyroid symptoms despite "normal" labs. T3/rT3 ratio should be >10.

üõ°Ô∏è Thyroid Antibodies

TPO (thyroid peroxidase) and TG (thyroglobulin) antibodies: Detect Hashimoto's thyroiditis‚Äîmost common cause of hypothyroidism. Can be elevated years before TSH becomes abnormal. Essential for identifying autoimmune thyroid disease early.

Critical Pattern Recognition

Low T3 syndrome (functional hypothyroidism): Normal TSH and T4, but low T3 and/or elevated reverse T3. Common causes include chronic stress, inflammation, nutrient deficiencies (selenium, zinc, iron), chronic dieting, or liver dysfunction. Symptoms of hypothyroidism present despite "normal" conventional thyroid panel.

Insulin and Blood Sugar Regulation

Insulin resistance develops years before diabetes‚Äîearly detection through functional testing allows for preventive intervention.

üåü Advanced Insulin Assessment

Fasting Insulin

Conventional range: <25 ŒºIU/mL. Functional optimal: <5 ŒºIU/mL. Elevated fasting insulin indicates insulin resistance‚Äîoften present long before fasting glucose becomes abnormal. Best early marker for metabolic syndrome risk.

HOMA-IR (Insulin Resistance Index)

Calculated from fasting glucose and fasting insulin: (Glucose √ó Insulin) √∑ 405. Optimal: <1.0. Insulin resistance: >1.9. Provides quantifiable measure of insulin sensitivity.

Hemoglobin A1C

Reflects average blood sugar over 2-3 months. Conventional: <5.7% normal. Functional optimal: <5.3%. Between 5.7-6.4% indicates prediabetes. ‚â•6.5% diagnostic for diabetes.

Fructosamine

Reflects average blood sugar over 2-3 weeks (shorter timeframe than A1C). Useful for tracking blood sugar changes more quickly. Not affected by red blood cell lifespan (unlike A1C).

Leptin: The Satiety Hormone

Leptin signals fullness and regulates energy expenditure. Leptin resistance‚Äîwhere the brain stops responding to leptin's signals‚Äîdrives obesity, metabolic dysfunction, and difficulty losing weight.

üìä

Normal Leptin Function

Leptin rises after eating, signaling fullness and increasing metabolism. Leptin falls during fasting, stimulating hunger and conserving energy. Brain responds appropriately to leptin signals.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Leptin Resistance

High leptin levels but brain doesn't respond‚Äîlike knocking on a door no one answers. Results in constant hunger despite adequate or excessive fat stores. Optimal leptin: <10 ng/mL. Elevated levels suggest leptin resistance.

Pattern Recognition: Putting It All Together

The true power of hormone testing lies not in individual values but in recognizing patterns and relationships between hormones. Hormones work as an interconnected orchestra‚Äîwhen one section plays out of tune, it affects the entire symphony.

Common Hormone Patterns

üîÑ Estrogen Dominance Pattern

Lab findings: High or normal estrogen with low progesterone, poor estrogen metabolism (elevated 4-OH or 16-OH metabolites), low 2-OH metabolites. Clinical presentation: Heavy periods, PMS, fibrocystic breasts, weight gain (hips/thighs), mood swings, headaches.

üò∞ HPA Axis Dysfunction

Lab findings: Disrupted cortisol rhythm (high, low, or reversed), low DHEA, may see compensatory thyroid changes (low T3, high rT3). Clinical presentation: Fatigue, anxiety, insomnia, difficulty handling stress, immune dysfunction, blood sugar instability.

üç¨ Metabolic Syndrome Pattern

Lab findings: Elevated insulin, high leptin, elevated A1C or fasting glucose, may see low testosterone in men, elevated cortisol. Clinical presentation: Central obesity, fatty liver, high triglycerides, high blood pressure, difficulty losing weight.

ü¶ã Thyroid-Adrenal Connection

Lab findings: Low or dysregulated cortisol with low T3 and/or high reverse T3, normal TSH and T4. Explanation: Chronic stress impairs T4-to-T3 conversion and increases reverse T3 production. Treating thyroid alone without addressing adrenals often fails.

Coach Tip

When reviewing hormone results with clients, focus on patterns and relationships rather than getting lost in individual numbers. Try explaining: "Your hormone panel is like looking at a team photo. We're not just checking whether each player showed up‚Äîwe're looking at how they're positioned relative to each other, who's working together, and where there might be gaps in teamwork. Your estrogen and progesterone relationship, your cortisol pattern throughout the day, and how your thyroid and adrenals are communicating all tell us more than any single number."

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

Why is measuring cortisol at a single point in time (like one morning blood draw) often inadequate for assessing adrenal function?

Answer: Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm‚Äînormally highest in the morning and lowest at night. A single time-point measurement misses this rhythm, which is often more clinically significant than absolute cortisol levels. Disrupted cortisol rhythm (flat curve, reversed pattern, or irregular spikes) causes symptoms like fatigue, insomnia, and poor stress tolerance even when a single morning cortisol measurement appears "normal." Four-point testing (waking, mid-morning, afternoon, bedtime) reveals the full pattern of cortisol production throughout the day.

2

What is "estrogen dominance," and why can it occur even when absolute estrogen levels are within the normal range?

Answer: Estrogen dominance refers to a state where estrogen's effects are excessive relative to progesterone, not necessarily due to high absolute estrogen levels. It can occur through: (1) Low progesterone with normal or even low estrogen‚Äîthe ratio is unfavorable; (2) Poor estrogen metabolism‚Äîestrogen metabolites accumulate or are metabolized through less favorable pathways (4-OH, 16-OH) rather than protective pathways (2-OH); (3) Environmental estrogen exposure (xenoestrogens) adding to total estrogenic load. This explains why women with "normal" estrogen levels can still experience estrogen dominance symptoms like PMS, heavy periods, and breast tenderness.

3

A client's thyroid panel shows normal TSH (2.1 mIU/L) and normal free T4, but low free T3 and elevated reverse T3. What pattern does this suggest, and what might be the underlying cause?

Answer: This pattern suggests "Low T3 syndrome" or functional hypothyroidism‚Äîimpaired conversion of T4 to active T3, with excess production of inactive reverse T3 (rT3). Despite "normal" TSH and T4, the client experiences hypothyroid symptoms because active T3 is inadequate. Common underlying causes include: (1) Chronic stress (elevated cortisol impairs T4-to-T3 conversion); (2) Inflammation (cytokines reduce conversion and increase rT3); (3) Nutrient deficiencies (selenium, zinc, iron needed for conversion enzymes); (4) Chronic dieting or caloric restriction (body downregulates metabolism); (5) Liver dysfunction (liver is primary site of T4-to-T3 conversion). This pattern explains why patients can have debilitating hypothyroid symptoms despite conventional doctors saying "your thyroid is fine."

4

Why is fasting insulin often a better early marker of metabolic dysfunction than fasting glucose or hemoglobin A1C?

Answer: Fasting insulin rises years before fasting glucose or A1C become abnormal. In the early stages of insulin resistance, the body compensates by producing more insulin to maintain normal blood sugar levels‚Äîlike pressing harder on the gas pedal to maintain speed when driving uphill. During this compensatory phase, blood sugar remains normal (so fasting glucose and A1C look fine), but insulin levels are chronically elevated, causing metabolic damage, inflammation, weight gain, and cardiovascular risk. By the time fasting glucose or A1C becomes abnormal, significant metabolic dysfunction has already occurred. Optimal fasting insulin is <5 ŒºIU/mL, whereas conventional ranges allow up to 25 ŒºIU/mL‚Äîmissing years of developing insulin resistance. Early detection through fasting insulin testing allows for preventive lifestyle intervention before prediabetes or diabetes develops.

Key Takeaways
  • Testing methodology matters‚Äîserum, saliva, urine, and dried urine each provide different perspectives on hormone function
  • Timing is crucial for sex hormone testing (cycle phase matters) and cortisol assessment (daily rhythm matters)
  • Optimal ranges differ from reference ranges‚Äîfunctional medicine seeks optimal function, not just "within normal limits"
  • Ratios and patterns often reveal more than individual values‚Äîestrogen-to-progesterone, cortisol-to-DHEA, T3-to-reverse-T3 relationships are critical
  • Hormone systems interconnect‚Äîthyroid-adrenal, insulin-cortisol, sex hormone-metabolic hormone relationships create complex patterns
  • Early markers prevent disease‚Äîfasting insulin, cortisol rhythm, thyroid antibodies, and comprehensive hormone metabolite testing catch dysfunction before conventional labs become abnormal

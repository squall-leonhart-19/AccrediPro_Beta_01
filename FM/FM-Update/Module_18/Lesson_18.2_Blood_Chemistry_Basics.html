Blood Chemistry Basics

Reading Time: 12 minutes

Building on Your Knowledge

In our previous lesson, you learned about the functional approach to lab testing and the difference between optimal and pathological ranges. Now we dive into the foundational blood chemistry markers—the Comprehensive Metabolic Panel that forms the backbone of most blood work.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify key markers in a comprehensive metabolic panel
  • Understand what liver enzymes indicate about liver health
  • Recognize kidney function markers and their significance
  • Explain blood glucose markers and what they reveal
  • Appreciate how electrolyte imbalances affect health

Reading Between the Lines

Margaret, 55, brought her annual lab results to our coaching session. "The doctor said everything looks fine," she reported. "But I still feel terrible—tired, bloated, and my brain is foggy."

Looking at her metabolic panel, I noticed her ALT was at 38 U/L (within the conventional range of 7-56 U/L), and her fasting glucose was 95 mg/dL (below the prediabetes threshold of 100). Her BUN/creatinine ratio was 25:1, and her CO2 was at 20 mEq/L.

While none of these values would flag as "abnormal" on a standard report, a functional lens reveals potential patterns: possible early liver stress, blood sugar creeping upward, dehydration, and low bicarbonate suggesting possible pH imbalance. Understanding these patterns helps us better support clients in conversations with their practitioners.

The Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)

The Comprehensive Metabolic Panel is a group of 14 tests that provide a snapshot of your body's chemical balance and metabolism. It typically includes glucose, electrolytes, kidney markers, liver markers, and proteins.

CMP Components at a Glance

Glucose: Blood sugar
Electrolytes: Sodium, Potassium, Chloride, CO2 (bicarbonate)
Kidney Markers: BUN, Creatinine, eGFR, Calcium
Liver Markers: Albumin, Total Protein, Bilirubin, Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP), AST, ALT
Calculated Values: BUN/Creatinine ratio, A/G ratio, Globulin, Anion gap

Blood Glucose Markers

Fasting glucose measures the amount of sugar in your blood after an overnight fast (typically 8-12 hours). It's a snapshot of how well your body manages blood sugar.

Classification Conventional Range Functional Optimal
Optimal 65-99 mg/dL ("normal") 82-88 mg/dL
Prediabetes 100-125 mg/dL Values above 90 warrant attention
Diabetes ≥126 mg/dL Same threshold

Key Insight: Glucose Alone Isn't Enough

Fasting glucose can remain "normal" for years while insulin resistance develops. By the time glucose elevates, the body has been compensating with higher insulin production. This is why functional practitioners often request fasting insulin and HbA1c for a more complete picture of blood sugar regulation.

Liver Function Markers

The liver performs over 500 functions including detoxification, protein synthesis, bile production, and metabolism. Liver markers help assess how well it's functioning.

The Liver Enzymes: AST and ALT

AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase)

Conventional range: 10-40 U/L
Functional optimal: 10-26 U/L

AST is found in the liver, heart, muscles, kidneys, and brain. Elevations can indicate liver damage but aren't liver-specific. When AST is higher than ALT, consider heart, muscle, or kidney involvement.

ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)

Conventional range: 7-56 U/L
Functional optimal: 10-26 U/L

ALT is more liver-specific than AST. It's considered the more sensitive marker for liver damage. Elevations can indicate fatty liver, hepatitis, medication effects, or toxin exposure. Even "high-normal" values (above 25-30) may indicate early liver stress.

The AST/ALT Ratio

When AST is significantly higher than ALT (ratio >2:1), it may suggest alcohol-related liver disease rather than other causes. When ALT is higher than AST, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is more likely. This pattern can be a useful clinical clue.

Other Liver Markers

Marker What It Measures Clinical Significance
Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) Enzyme in liver, bone, kidneys, intestines Elevated in bile duct obstruction, bone disease, or with certain medications. Low levels may indicate zinc deficiency.
Bilirubin (Total) Breakdown product of red blood cells High values cause jaundice. Mild elevation may indicate Gilbert's syndrome (benign). Very high suggests liver/gallbladder issues.
GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase) Enzyme sensitive to liver/bile duct issues Very sensitive to alcohol. Also elevated with medication use, diabetes, and fatty liver. Often added to CMP.

Kidney Function Markers

The kidneys filter blood, regulate fluid balance, remove waste, and help control blood pressure. Several markers assess kidney function:

BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)

BUN measures the amount of nitrogen in the blood that comes from urea, a waste product of protein metabolism. The kidneys filter urea from the blood.

Conventional range: 7-20 mg/dL
Functional optimal: 13-18 mg/dL

Elevated BUN may indicate:

  • Dehydration (most common cause)
  • High protein intake
  • Kidney dysfunction
  • GI bleeding
  • Heart failure

Low BUN may indicate:

  • Low protein intake
  • Liver disease (can't make urea)
  • Over-hydration
  • Malabsorption

Creatinine

Creatinine is a waste product from muscle metabolism. It's filtered by the kidneys and is a more specific marker of kidney function than BUN.

Conventional range: 0.6-1.2 mg/dL (varies by sex and muscle mass)
Functional optimal: 0.8-1.1 mg/dL for women; 0.9-1.2 mg/dL for men

Considerations: Higher values may be normal in muscular individuals or those eating high-protein diets. Low values may indicate low muscle mass. Persistently elevated values warrant kidney evaluation.

The BUN/Creatinine Ratio

This calculated ratio helps differentiate between kidney and non-kidney causes of abnormal values:

Normal ratio: 10:1 to 20:1

High ratio (>20:1): Suggests dehydration, high protein intake, GI bleeding, or catabolic states—BUN rises more than creatinine

Low ratio (<10:1): May suggest liver disease, low protein diet, or muscle wasting—creatinine rises relative to BUN

eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

eGFR is a calculated value that estimates how well the kidneys filter blood. It's considered the best overall indicator of kidney function.

Stage eGFR Value Kidney Function
Normal ≥90 Normal kidney function
Stage 1 CKD ≥90 with kidney damage Normal eGFR but with proteinuria or other damage markers
Stage 2 CKD 60-89 Mildly decreased
Stage 3 CKD 30-59 Moderately decreased
Stage 4-5 CKD <30 Severely decreased to kidney failure

Electrolytes

Electrolytes are minerals that carry electrical charges and are essential for nerve function, muscle contraction, hydration, and pH balance.

Sodium (Na)

Conventional range: 136-145 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 139-142 mEq/L

Sodium regulates fluid balance and blood pressure. Low sodium (hyponatremia) can cause fatigue, confusion, and muscle weakness. High sodium may indicate dehydration.

Potassium (K)

Conventional range: 3.5-5.0 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 4.0-4.5 mEq/L

Potassium is critical for heart function and muscle contraction. Both high and low levels can cause dangerous heart arrhythmias. Low potassium is common with diuretic use.

Chloride (Cl)

Conventional range: 98-106 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 100-106 mEq/L

Chloride helps maintain fluid balance and is a component of stomach acid. Elevated chloride with low CO2 may indicate metabolic acidosis.

Carbon Dioxide (CO2/Bicarbonate)

Conventional range: 23-29 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 25-28 mEq/L

CO2 represents bicarbonate, the body's main acid buffer. Low CO2 may indicate metabolic acidosis from poor cellular metabolism, kidney issues, or diabetic ketoacidosis. High CO2 may indicate respiratory issues or vomiting.

The Anion Gap

The anion gap is calculated as: Na - (Cl + CO2). Normal is 7-16 mEq/L. An elevated anion gap suggests unmeasured acids in the blood, which can occur with diabetic ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, toxin ingestion, or kidney failure.

Protein Markers

Total Protein

Conventional range: 6.0-8.5 g/dL | Functional optimal: 6.9-7.4 g/dL

Includes all proteins in the blood. Low values may indicate malnutrition, malabsorption, liver disease, or kidney loss. High values may indicate chronic inflammation, infection, or dehydration.

Albumin

Conventional range: 3.5-5.0 g/dL | Functional optimal: 4.0-5.0 g/dL

Albumin is made by the liver and is the most abundant blood protein. It transports hormones, vitamins, and medications. Low albumin indicates protein deficiency, liver dysfunction, inflammation, or kidney loss.

Globulin

Conventional range: 2.3-3.5 g/dL | Functional optimal: 2.4-2.8 g/dL

Globulins include immunoglobulins (antibodies) and transport proteins. Elevated globulin often indicates chronic inflammation or infection. The A/G ratio (albumin/globulin) should be slightly greater than 1.

Pattern Recognition

Functional medicine looks at markers in relationship to each other, not in isolation. Here are some common patterns:

Dehydration Pattern

High BUN with normal creatinine (high BUN/Cr ratio), elevated sodium, elevated hemoglobin/hematocrit, elevated total protein

Fatty Liver Pattern

Elevated ALT (often higher than AST), elevated GGT, elevated triglycerides, possibly elevated glucose and insulin

Chronic Inflammation Pattern

Low albumin, elevated globulin (low A/G ratio), elevated inflammatory markers (if tested), possibly low iron

Blood Sugar Dysregulation Pattern

Fasting glucose >90, elevated triglycerides, low HDL, elevated uric acid, elevated GGT

Key Takeaways

  • The CMP provides a foundational snapshot of metabolic, liver, and kidney function
  • Liver enzymes AST and ALT, especially ALT, indicate liver health—optimal is below 26 U/L
  • BUN and creatinine assess kidney function; the ratio helps identify dehydration
  • Fasting glucose alone may miss early blood sugar problems; insulin adds valuable information
  • Electrolyte balance affects nerve function, muscle contraction, and pH regulation
  • Pattern recognition—looking at multiple markers together—provides more insight than isolated values

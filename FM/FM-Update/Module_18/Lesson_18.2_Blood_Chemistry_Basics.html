Case Study: Margaret's "Normal" Labs

Reading Between the Lines

üë©

Margaret, Age 55

Persistent fatigue ‚Ä¢ Bloating ‚Ä¢ Brain fog ‚Ä¢ "Normal" lab results from annual physical

"The doctor said everything looks fine. But I still feel terrible‚Äîtired, bloated, and my brain is foggy."

Looking at her metabolic panel, I noticed her ALT was at 38 U/L (within the conventional range of 7-56 U/L), and her fasting glucose was 95 mg/dL (below the prediabetes threshold of 100). Her BUN/creatinine ratio was 25:1, and her CO2 was at 20 mEq/L.

While none of these values would flag as "abnormal" on a standard report, a functional lens reveals potential patterns: possible early liver stress, blood sugar creeping upward, dehydration, and low bicarbonate suggesting possible pH imbalance. Understanding these patterns helps us better support clients in conversations with their practitioners.

Margaret's story illustrates why functional interpretation matters. The conventional medical model waits for markers to reach pathological levels before intervening. The functional model recognizes that subtle shifts away from optimal can explain symptoms and guide preventive action.

The Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)

The Comprehensive Metabolic Panel is a group of 14 tests that provide a snapshot of your body's chemical balance and metabolism. It's one of the most commonly ordered blood tests because it offers broad insight into multiple organ systems with a single draw.

Think of the CMP as your body's "vital statistics report"‚Äîrevealing how well your kidneys filter waste, how your liver processes toxins, how your body manages blood sugar, and whether your electrolytes are balanced for proper nerve and muscle function.

CMP Components at a Glance

Glucose: Blood sugar regulation
Electrolytes: Sodium, Potassium, Chloride, CO2 (bicarbonate)
Kidney Markers: BUN, Creatinine, eGFR, Calcium
Liver Markers: Albumin, Total Protein, Bilirubin, Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP), AST, ALT
Calculated Values: BUN/Creatinine ratio, A/G ratio, Globulin, Anion gap

Coach Tip

When reviewing a CMP with clients, start with the big picture: "These 14 markers give us a window into how your kidneys, liver, blood sugar, and mineral balance are functioning. Let's look at which areas are optimal and where we might want to dig deeper." This frames the conversation as collaborative exploration rather than delivering "good" or "bad" news.

Blood Glucose Markers

Fasting glucose measures the amount of sugar in your blood after an overnight fast (typically 8-12 hours). It's a snapshot of how well your body manages blood sugar when no food is coming in‚Äîessentially, how well your internal glucose regulation is working.

Conventional Ranges
  • Normal: 65-99 mg/dL
  • Prediabetes: 100-125 mg/dL
  • Diabetes: ‚â•126 mg/dL
Functional Optimal
  • Optimal: 82-88 mg/dL
  • Attention needed: >90 mg/dL
  • Same diabetes threshold: ‚â•126 mg/dL
Key Insight: Glucose Alone Isn't Enough

Fasting glucose can remain "normal" for years while insulin resistance develops. By the time glucose elevates, the body has been compensating with higher insulin production‚Äîessentially working overtime to keep glucose in check. This is why functional practitioners often request fasting insulin and HbA1c for a more complete picture of blood sugar regulation.

A client with fasting glucose of 95 mg/dL might be told "you're fine," but functionally, this suggests the body is already struggling to maintain glucose control. Paired with elevated insulin, this reveals insulin resistance years before a diabetes diagnosis.

Coach Tip

Help clients understand that blood sugar management isn't just about preventing diabetes‚Äîit affects energy levels, brain function, inflammation, hormone balance, and aging. Frame it positively: "Getting your blood sugar into optimal range means more stable energy throughout the day, better focus, fewer cravings, and reduced inflammation. Let's talk about strategies that work with your lifestyle."

Liver Function Markers

The liver is your body's primary detoxification organ, performing over 500 functions including filtering toxins, producing bile for fat digestion, synthesizing proteins, storing vitamins, and metabolizing hormones and medications. Liver markers help assess how well this remarkable organ is functioning under the demands you're placing on it.

The Liver Enzymes: AST and ALT

AST

Aspartate Aminotransferase

Conventional range: 10-40 U/L
Functional optimal: 10-26 U/L

AST is found in the liver, heart, muscles, kidneys, and brain. Elevations can indicate liver damage but aren't liver-specific. When AST is significantly higher than ALT, consider heart, muscle, or kidney involvement.

ALT

Alanine Aminotransferase

Conventional range: 7-56 U/L
Functional optimal: 10-26 U/L

ALT is more liver-specific than AST and considered the more sensitive marker for liver damage. Elevations can indicate fatty liver, hepatitis, medication effects, or toxin exposure. Even "high-normal" values (above 25-30) may indicate early liver stress.

The AST/ALT Ratio

When AST is significantly higher than ALT (ratio >2:1), it may suggest alcohol-related liver disease rather than other causes. When ALT is higher than AST, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is more likely. This pattern can be a useful clinical clue for practitioners.

Other Liver Markers

Additional Liver Function Indicators

üî¨ Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP)

What it measures: Enzyme found in liver, bone, kidneys, and intestines

Clinical significance: Elevated in bile duct obstruction, bone disease, or with certain medications. Low levels may indicate zinc deficiency or malnutrition.

üü° Bilirubin (Total)

What it measures: Breakdown product of red blood cells processed by the liver

Clinical significance: High values cause jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes). Mild elevation may indicate Gilbert's syndrome (benign genetic condition). Very high levels suggest liver or gallbladder issues.

‚ö†Ô∏è GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase)

What it measures: Enzyme very sensitive to liver and bile duct issues

Clinical significance: Extremely sensitive to alcohol use. Also elevated with medication use, diabetes, and fatty liver. Often added to standard CMP for comprehensive liver assessment.

Coach Tip

When clients show elevated liver enzymes, avoid fear-based language. Instead, try: "Your liver is working hard right now, and these markers show it might need some support. The good news is that the liver is remarkably resilient and responds well to dietary changes, reduced alcohol, adequate hydration, and limiting processed foods. Let's create a liver-supportive plan together." This empowers rather than frightens.

Kidney Function Markers

The kidneys are your body's filtration system, processing about 200 quarts of blood daily to remove waste, regulate fluid balance, control blood pressure, activate vitamin D, and maintain electrolyte balance. Several markers assess how well these vital organs are performing.

BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)

BUN measures the amount of nitrogen in the blood that comes from urea, a waste product created when the liver breaks down protein. The kidneys filter urea from the blood and excrete it in urine. BUN levels reflect both protein metabolism and kidney filtration efficiency.

Understanding BUN Levels

Conventional range: 7-20 mg/dL | Functional optimal: 13-18 mg/dL

Elevated BUN may indicate:

  • Dehydration (most common cause in otherwise healthy people)
  • High protein intake
  • Kidney dysfunction or disease
  • GI bleeding (protein from blood is metabolized)
  • Heart failure (reduced kidney blood flow)

Low BUN may indicate:

  • Low protein intake or malnutrition
  • Liver disease (can't produce urea effectively)
  • Over-hydration
  • Malabsorption of protein

Creatinine

Creatinine is a waste product from creatine phosphate, which muscles use for energy. Because it's produced at a fairly constant rate based on muscle mass and filtered exclusively by the kidneys, creatinine is a more specific marker of kidney function than BUN.

Creatinine Context

Conventional range: 0.6-1.2 mg/dL (varies by sex and muscle mass)
Functional optimal: 0.8-1.1 mg/dL for women; 0.9-1.2 mg/dL for men

Important considerations: Higher values may be completely normal in muscular individuals or those eating high-protein diets. Low values may indicate low muscle mass (sarcopenia). Persistently elevated values warrant kidney evaluation. Context‚Äîincluding the person's build, diet, and activity level‚Äîmatters tremendously.

The BUN/Creatinine Ratio

This calculated ratio helps differentiate between kidney and non-kidney causes of abnormal values. Because BUN is affected by protein intake, hydration, and liver function while creatinine is more stable, the ratio reveals patterns:

High Ratio (>20:1)
  • Suggests dehydration
  • High protein intake
  • GI bleeding
  • Catabolic states
  • BUN rises more than creatinine
Low Ratio (<10:1)
  • May suggest liver disease
  • Low protein diet
  • Muscle wasting
  • Creatinine rises relative to BUN

Normal ratio: 10:1 to 20:1 indicates balanced kidney function and adequate hydration

eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

eGFR is a calculated value based on creatinine, age, sex, and race that estimates how well the kidneys filter blood. It's considered the best overall indicator of kidney function and is used to stage chronic kidney disease (CKD).

eGFR Stages and Kidney Function

‚úÖ Normal: ‚â•90

Normal kidney function. Values above 90 indicate healthy filtration capacity.

‚ö†Ô∏è Stage 1 CKD: ‚â•90 with damage

Normal eGFR but with proteinuria or other kidney damage markers present.

üü° Stage 2 CKD: 60-89

Mildly decreased kidney function. Monitor and address risk factors.

üü† Stage 3 CKD: 30-59

Moderately decreased function. Requires medical management and dietary modifications.

Electrolytes

Electrolytes are minerals that carry electrical charges and are essential for nerve function, muscle contraction, hydration, blood pressure regulation, and pH balance. Even small imbalances can cause significant symptoms.

Sodium (Na)

Conventional range: 136-145 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 139-142 mEq/L

Sodium is the primary electrolyte in extracellular fluid (outside cells) and regulates fluid balance, blood pressure, and nerve impulses. Low sodium (hyponatremia) can cause fatigue, confusion, headaches, muscle weakness, and in severe cases, seizures. It's often caused by over-hydration, certain medications (diuretics, antidepressants), or hormone imbalances. High sodium (hypernatremia) typically indicates dehydration or excessive sodium intake, causing thirst, dry mouth, and confusion.

Potassium (K)

Conventional range: 3.5-5.0 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 4.0-4.5 mEq/L

Potassium is the primary electrolyte inside cells and is critical for heart function, muscle contraction, and nerve transmission. Both high and low levels can cause dangerous heart arrhythmias. Low potassium (hypokalemia) is common with diuretic use, vomiting, diarrhea, or inadequate dietary intake, causing muscle weakness, cramping, and irregular heartbeat. High potassium (hyperkalemia) can occur with kidney disease or certain medications and is medically urgent due to cardiac risk.

Chloride (Cl)

Conventional range: 98-106 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 100-106 mEq/L

Chloride helps maintain fluid balance, works with sodium, and is a component of stomach acid (hydrochloric acid‚ÄîHCl). Elevated chloride with low CO2 may indicate metabolic acidosis. Low chloride can occur with vomiting (loss of stomach acid) or alkalosis.

Carbon Dioxide (CO2/Bicarbonate)

Conventional range: 23-29 mEq/L | Functional optimal: 25-28 mEq/L

CO2 represents bicarbonate, the body's main acid buffer that maintains pH balance. Low CO2 may indicate metabolic acidosis from poor cellular metabolism, kidney issues, diabetic ketoacidosis, or lactic acidosis. High CO2 may indicate respiratory issues (not exhaling enough CO2), chronic vomiting (loss of stomach acid), or metabolic alkalosis.

The Anion Gap

The anion gap is calculated as: Na - (Cl + CO2). Normal range is 7-16 mEq/L.

An elevated anion gap suggests unmeasured acids accumulating in the blood, which can occur with diabetic ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis from poor oxygen delivery, toxin ingestion (methanol, ethylene glycol), or advanced kidney failure. It's a critical marker in acute medical situations.

Coach Tip

When clients have electrolyte imbalances, connect it to symptoms they're experiencing: "Low potassium can explain the muscle cramps and fatigue you've been feeling. Let's look at whether diuretics, sweating from exercise, or dietary gaps might be contributing. Increasing potassium-rich foods like leafy greens, avocados, and bananas‚Äîalong with proper hydration‚Äîcan make a real difference." Make the abstract concrete and actionable.

Protein Markers

Total Protein

Conventional range: 6.0-8.5 g/dL | Functional optimal: 6.9-7.4 g/dL

Total protein includes all proteins in the blood‚Äîprimarily albumin and globulins. Low values may indicate malnutrition, malabsorption, liver disease (can't produce protein), or kidney loss (protein leaking into urine). High values may indicate chronic inflammation, infection, multiple myeloma, or dehydration (concentrated blood).

Albumin

Conventional range: 3.5-5.0 g/dL | Functional optimal: 4.0-5.0 g/dL

Albumin is made by the liver and is the most abundant blood protein. It maintains osmotic pressure (keeps fluid in blood vessels), transports hormones, vitamins, and medications, and serves as a protein reserve. Low albumin indicates protein deficiency, liver dysfunction, chronic inflammation, malabsorption, or kidney loss. It's a marker of both nutritional status and inflammatory burden.

Globulin

Conventional range: 2.3-3.5 g/dL | Functional optimal: 2.4-2.8 g/dL

Globulins include immunoglobulins (antibodies) and transport proteins. Elevated globulin often indicates chronic inflammation, chronic infection, or autoimmune conditions. The A/G ratio (albumin/globulin) should be slightly greater than 1. A low A/G ratio (high globulin relative to albumin) suggests chronic inflammatory states.

Pattern Recognition

Functional medicine looks at markers in relationship to each other, not in isolation. Individual markers tell a story, but patterns reveal the underlying narrative. Here are some common patterns you'll begin to recognize:

üíß

Dehydration Pattern

High BUN with normal creatinine (elevated BUN/Cr ratio >20:1), elevated sodium, elevated hemoglobin/hematocrit, elevated total protein. The blood is "concentrated" because there's insufficient water volume.

ü´Ä

Fatty Liver Pattern

Elevated ALT (often higher than AST), elevated GGT, elevated triglycerides, possibly elevated glucose and insulin, possibly elevated uric acid. Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is epidemic and highly responsive to dietary intervention.

üî•

Chronic Inflammation Pattern

Low albumin, elevated globulin (low A/G ratio <1), elevated inflammatory markers like CRP or ESR (if tested), possibly low iron (inflammation affects iron regulation), elevated platelets. The body is fighting something chronically.

üç¨

Blood Sugar Dysregulation Pattern

Fasting glucose >90, elevated triglycerides, low HDL cholesterol, elevated uric acid, elevated GGT, possibly elevated liver enzymes. This metabolic syndrome pattern indicates insulin resistance developing.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What is the functional optimal range for fasting glucose, and why is it narrower than the conventional range?

Answer: The functional optimal range is 82-88 mg/dL, much narrower than the conventional "normal" of 65-99 mg/dL. This narrower range reflects the understanding that glucose levels above 90 mg/dL, while not yet diagnostic for prediabetes, often indicate the body is already struggling with blood sugar regulation‚Äîtypically through elevated insulin production (insulin resistance). By the time glucose rises above 100, the pancreas has been working overtime for years. Functional ranges catch imbalances earlier, when they're more responsive to lifestyle intervention.

2

A client's lab shows BUN of 28 mg/dL with creatinine of 1.0 mg/dL (BUN/Cr ratio of 28:1). What does this pattern likely indicate?

Answer: This elevated BUN/Creatinine ratio (>20:1) most commonly indicates dehydration. When the body is dehydrated, kidney blood flow decreases, causing BUN to rise while creatinine remains relatively stable. Other possibilities include high protein intake, GI bleeding (protein from blood is metabolized), or catabolic states. The pattern shows that BUN is rising disproportionately to creatinine, pointing to a pre-renal (before the kidney) cause rather than kidney disease itself. First step: assess hydration status, water intake, and recent protein consumption.

3

Why is ALT considered more liver-specific than AST, and what does an elevated ALT-to-AST ratio suggest?

Answer: ALT (alanine aminotransferase) is found primarily in the liver, making it more liver-specific. AST (aspartate aminotransferase) is found in the liver but also in heart, muscles, kidneys, and brain, so elevations can come from multiple sources. When ALT is higher than AST (ALT>AST), it typically suggests liver-specific issues, most commonly non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) or medication/toxin effects. Conversely, when AST is significantly higher than ALT (AST/ALT ratio >2:1), it may indicate alcohol-related liver disease. Understanding this ratio helps narrow the differential diagnosis.

4

What is the "chronic inflammation pattern" on a CMP, and what might be causing it?

Answer: The chronic inflammation pattern includes: low albumin, elevated globulin (resulting in a low A/G ratio <1), and often low iron (inflammation affects iron regulation). This pattern indicates the body is mounting a prolonged immune response. Causes can include chronic infections, autoimmune conditions, inflammatory bowel disease, chronic stress, poor diet, environmental toxins, or unresolved gut dysfunction. The liver is producing fewer albumin proteins and more globulins (including antibodies and acute-phase proteins) to fight the perceived threat. Identifying and addressing the root cause of inflammation‚Äîthrough comprehensive assessment and targeted interventions‚Äîis the functional medicine approach.

Key Takeaways
  • The Comprehensive Metabolic Panel provides a foundational snapshot of metabolic, liver, kidney, and electrolyte function‚Äî14 markers with interconnected meanings
  • Liver enzymes AST and ALT, especially ALT, indicate liver health‚Äîfunctional optimal is below 26 U/L, well below conventional upper limits
  • BUN and creatinine assess kidney function; the BUN/Cr ratio helps identify dehydration (>20:1) or other metabolic issues
  • Fasting glucose alone may miss early blood sugar problems; insulin and HbA1c add valuable information about insulin resistance
  • Electrolyte balance (Na, K, Cl, CO2) affects nerve function, muscle contraction, hydration, and pH regulation‚Äîsmall imbalances cause significant symptoms
  • Pattern recognition‚Äîlooking at multiple markers together‚Äîprovides far more insight than isolated values and reveals root causes
  • Functional optimal ranges are narrower than conventional ranges, catching imbalances earlier when they're most responsive to intervention

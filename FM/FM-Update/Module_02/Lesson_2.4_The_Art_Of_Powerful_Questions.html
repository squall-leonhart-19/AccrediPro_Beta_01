Welcome to Lesson 2.4

If active listening is the foundation of coaching, powerful questions are its primary tool. The right question at the right moment can shift perspective, unlock insight, and open doors the client didn't know existed. This lesson develops your skill in crafting and delivering transformative questions.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand why questions are more powerful than answers in coaching
  • Distinguish between open and closed questions and when to use each
  • Identify characteristics that make questions powerful
  • Apply different types of coaching questions for different purposes
  • Recognize and avoid common questioning pitfalls

The Power of Questions

In traditional healthcare, the provider has answers and the patient receives them. In coaching, questions are more powerful than answers because they invite the client to think, discover, and own their insights.

Powerful questions:

  • Stimulate reflection and deeper thinking
  • Challenge assumptions and limiting beliefs
  • Generate new perspectives and possibilities
  • Move the conversation forward with purpose
  • Put the client in the driver's seat

Why Questions Work

When someone gives you an answer, you might accept or reject it. When you discover an answer yourself through good questions, you own it. That ownership translates to commitment and action.

Open vs. Closed Questions

Closed questions can be answered with yes/no or a single word:

  • "Did you exercise this week?" → "No."
  • "Are you feeling better?" → "Kind of."

Open questions invite exploration and longer responses:

  • "What got in the way of exercising this week?"
  • "How would you describe how you're feeling compared to last week?"

Use Closed Questions Sparingly

Closed questions have their place—confirming facts, getting commitment—but overusing them makes coaching feel like an interrogation and misses opportunities for exploration.

What Makes Questions Powerful

Powerful coaching questions share certain characteristics:

  • Simple and clear: Easy to understand, no jargon
  • Open-ended: Invite exploration, not just facts
  • Thought-provoking: Cause the client to pause and reflect
  • Forward-focused: Move toward solutions and action
  • Client-centered: About their experience, not your curiosity
  • Non-leading: Don't contain your preferred answer

Weak Question

"Don't you think you should try eating less sugar?"

Powerful Question

"What role does sugar play in your life right now?"

Types of Coaching Questions

Clarifying Questions

  • "What do you mean by 'stressed'?"
  • "Can you give me an example of that?"
  • "When you say 'always,' how often is that really?"

Exploring Values & Motivation

  • "What's most important to you about making this change?"
  • "How does this connect to what matters most in your life?"
  • "What would achieving this goal make possible for you?"

Possibility & Perspective Questions

  • "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"
  • "What advice would you give a friend in this situation?"
  • "What's another way to look at this?"

Action & Commitment Questions

  • "What's one small step you could take this week?"
  • "What would make this feel doable?"
  • "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you?"

Questions to Avoid

  • Leading questions: "Don't you think you should...?" (Contains your answer)
  • "Why" questions: Can feel judgmental or put clients on the defensive
  • Multiple questions: Stacking questions confuses the client
  • Questions for your curiosity: Only ask what serves the client
  • Disguised advice: "Have you thought about trying...?"

Instead of "Why"

"Why didn't you exercise?" can feel accusatory. Try: "What got in the way?" or "What was happening that made exercise difficult?" Same information, different energy.

Practice & Application

Developing your questioning skills takes practice. Try these exercises:

  • Convert closed questions to open ones in everyday conversation
  • Practice waiting after asking—let silence do its work
  • Notice when you're about to give advice and turn it into a question instead
  • Keep a list of powerful questions that resonate with you

Key Terms

Open Question

A question that cannot be answered with yes/no and invites exploration and reflection.

Closed Question

A question that can be answered with yes/no or a single word; useful for confirming facts.

Leading Question

A question that contains or suggests the desired answer; generally avoided in coaching.

Scaling Question

Asking clients to rate something on a scale (1-10) to gauge intensity, confidence, or importance.

Check Your Understanding

1

Transform this closed question into a powerful open question: "Did you stick to your meal plan?"

Options include: "How did your eating go this week?" or "What worked well with your meal plan?" or "What did you notice about your eating patterns this week?" These invite reflection and exploration rather than simple yes/no.

2

Why are "why" questions often problematic in coaching?

"Why" questions can feel judgmental or put clients on the defensive, as if they need to justify themselves. They often trigger explanations about the past rather than exploration of possibilities. Reframing to "what" or "how" questions (e.g., "What contributed to that?" instead of "Why did you do that?") gets similar information with less defensiveness.

Key Takeaways

  • Questions are more powerful than answers because they promote ownership
  • Open questions invite exploration; closed questions confirm facts
  • Powerful questions are simple, open, thought-provoking, and forward-focused
  • Different question types serve different purposes in coaching
  • Avoid leading questions, stacked questions, and disguised advice

Case Study: The Question That Changed Everything

From Stuck to Breakthrough

üë®

Michael, Age 52

Executive ‚Ä¢ Type 2 Diabetes ‚Ä¢ Multiple failed diet attempts ‚Ä¢ Feels hopeless

"I've tried everything. Nothing works. I'm just not disciplined enough."

Michael had been cycling through this narrative for fifteen minutes. His coach could have offered advice, shared a success story, or tried to convince him he was wrong. Instead, she asked a single question:

"What would you do differently if you approached this as an experiment instead of a test you could fail?"

Michael stopped mid-sentence. His entire posture changed. "I... I've never thought about it that way. If it's an experiment, then there's no failure‚Äîjust data." That one question reframed his entire relationship with health changes, shifting from all-or-nothing thinking to curiosity and exploration.

This is the transformative power of a well-crafted question. It doesn't provide the answer‚Äîit invites the client to discover their own insight, which they'll own far more deeply than any advice you could offer.

Coach Tip

The Question Before the Question: Before asking any question, pause and ask yourself: "Is this question serving my curiosity or the client's growth?" If it's the former, hold it. If it's the latter, ask it with intention and then get out of the way.

The Power of Questions

In traditional healthcare, the provider has answers and the patient receives them. This model works well for acute care‚Äîif you have a broken bone, you don't need coaching questions about how you'd like to approach setting it.

But in functional medicine and health coaching, where sustainable behavior change is the goal, questions are more powerful than answers because they invite the client to think, discover, and own their insights.

1

Questions Stimulate Reflection

A powerful question causes clients to pause, think deeply, and access their own wisdom rather than relying on external authority.

2

Questions Challenge Assumptions

The right question can reveal limiting beliefs clients didn't even know they held, creating space for new possibilities.

3

Questions Generate New Perspectives

When stuck in a problem, questions can shift the lens through which clients view their situation, revealing solutions that were invisible before.

4

Questions Move Conversations Forward

Strategic questions prevent conversations from circling endlessly, guiding clients toward clarity and action.

5

Questions Put Clients in the Driver's Seat

When you give answers, you become responsible for the outcome. When clients discover their own answers, they own the commitment.

Why Questions Work

When someone gives you an answer, you might accept or reject it. When you discover an answer yourself through good questions, you own it. That ownership translates to commitment and action. Neuroscience backs this up: self-generated insights activate reward circuits in the brain, making them more memorable and motivating than externally provided information.

Open vs. Closed Questions

Not all questions are created equal. Understanding the distinction between open and closed questions‚Äîand when to use each‚Äîis foundational to powerful questioning.

Closed Questions
  • Can be answered with yes/no or a single word
  • Useful for confirming facts or getting commitment
  • Quick and efficient
  • Example: "Did you exercise this week?" ‚Üí "No."
  • Example: "Are you feeling better?" ‚Üí "Kind of."
Open Questions
  • Invite exploration and longer responses
  • Create space for reflection and discovery
  • Reveal underlying thoughts, feelings, values
  • Example: "What got in the way of exercising this week?"
  • Example: "How would you describe how you're feeling compared to last week?"

Closed questions have their place in coaching‚Äîconfirming understanding, checking commitment, gathering specific facts. But overusing closed questions makes coaching feel like an interrogation and misses opportunities for the deeper exploration that drives transformation.

Coach Tip

The 80/20 Rule: Aim for roughly 80% open questions and 20% closed questions in your coaching conversations. If you find yourself asking a string of closed questions, pause and shift to an open one: "What else is important for me to understand about this?"

Watch for Question Fatigue

While open questions are powerful, too many in rapid succession can overwhelm clients. Balance questioning with reflective listening, silence, and occasional summaries to create breathing room in the conversation.

What Makes Questions Powerful

Not every open question is a powerful question. Powerful coaching questions share certain characteristics that make them transformative rather than simply informative.

Six Characteristics of Powerful Questions

üìù Simple and Clear

Easy to understand with no jargon or complex phrasing. The simpler the question, the deeper clients can go.

Example: "What matters most to you about this?" vs. "How does this align with your prioritized values framework?"

üö™ Open-Ended

Invite exploration rather than just gathering facts. They begin with "what," "how," or "in what ways" rather than seeking yes/no answers.

Example: "What would success look like?" vs. "Do you want to lose weight?"

üí≠ Thought-Provoking

Cause the client to pause and reflect. If they answer immediately without thinking, the question may not be reaching deep enough.

Example: "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?" vs. "What's your plan?"

‚è≠Ô∏è Forward-Focused

Move toward solutions and action rather than dwelling on problems or the past. While understanding context is important, powerful questions orient toward possibility.

Example: "What's one step you could take?" vs. "What went wrong?"

üéØ Client-Centered

About their experience, needs, and insights‚Äînot your curiosity or agenda. Every question should serve the client's growth.

Example: "What feels most important to you right now?" vs. "I'm curious‚Äîdo you take supplements?"

üß≠ Non-Leading

Don't contain your preferred answer or push clients toward a particular conclusion. Truly open questions allow any authentic response.

Example: "What feels right for you?" vs. "Don't you think you should try meditation?"

Weak Questions
  • "Don't you think you should try eating less sugar?"
  • "Have you considered that stress might be a factor?"
  • "Wouldn't it be better to exercise in the morning?"
Powerful Questions
  • "What role does sugar play in your life right now?"
  • "What do you notice about your stress patterns?"
  • "When during the day do you have the most energy?"

Types of Coaching Questions

Different questions serve different purposes in the coaching conversation. Developing fluency with various question types allows you to guide clients skillfully through exploration, insight, and action.

1

Clarifying Questions

Help you understand the client's experience more precisely and help them articulate their thoughts more clearly.

üîç Clarifying Questions Examples

Understanding Terms

"What do you mean by 'stressed'?"

"When you say 'healthy,' what does that look like for you?"

Getting Specific

"Can you give me an example of that?"

"When you say 'always,' how often is that really?"

"What specifically happens when...?"

Deepening Understanding

"Tell me more about that."

"What else is important about this?"

Coach Tip

Challenge Vague Language: When clients use words like "always," "never," "everyone," or "can't," gently challenge these absolutes with clarifying questions. This helps them see their situation more accurately and identify exceptions that might reveal solutions.

2

Values & Motivation Questions

Connect surface goals to deeper meaning, strengthening intrinsic motivation and commitment.

‚ù§Ô∏è Values & Motivation Questions Examples

Uncovering Values

"What's most important to you about making this change?"

"How does this connect to what matters most in your life?"

"What would this make possible for you?"

Strengthening Motivation

"On a scale of 1-10, how important is this to you?"

"What would it mean to you to achieve this?"

"Who else would benefit from this change?"

Exploring Deeper Why

"What would achieving this goal make possible that isn't possible now?"

"What are you really hoping for?"

3

Possibility & Perspective Questions

Help clients break out of limiting thinking patterns and see new possibilities.

üåü Possibility & Perspective Questions Examples

Expanding Possibilities

"What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"

"If anything were possible, what would you do?"

"What haven't you considered yet?"

Shifting Perspective

"What advice would you give a friend in this situation?"

"What's another way to look at this?"

"How might this challenge actually be serving you?"

Future Focus

"Fast forward six months‚Äîyou've succeeded. What did you do?"

"What does your future self wish you knew right now?"

4

Obstacle & Resource Questions

Identify barriers and leverage existing strengths and resources.

üß± Obstacle & Resource Questions Examples

Identifying Obstacles

"What might get in the way?"

"What's stopped you before?"

"What would make this difficult?"

Finding Resources

"What's worked for you in the past?"

"What strengths can you draw on?"

"Who could support you in this?"

"What resources do you already have?"

Problem-Solving

"What would help you overcome that?"

"How have you handled similar challenges before?"

5

Action & Commitment Questions

Move from insight to concrete action and strengthen commitment.

‚úÖ Action & Commitment Questions Examples

Defining Action

"What's one small step you could take this week?"

"What would make this feel doable?"

"What's the very first thing you'll do?"

Testing Commitment

"On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you'll do this?"

"What would it take to move that from a 6 to an 8?"

"What will you do, by when?"

Creating Accountability

"How will you know you've succeeded?"

"How would you like me to follow up with you on this?"

"What might derail you, and how will you handle it?"

Coach Tip

Use Scaling Questions Strategically: "On a scale of 1-10..." questions are incredibly versatile. They help gauge importance, confidence, readiness, and satisfaction. The real power comes in the follow-up: "What would it take to move from a 6 to a 7?" This question invites specific problem-solving.

Questions to Avoid

Just as important as knowing what questions to ask is knowing which questions undermine effective coaching. Here are common questioning pitfalls and how to avoid them.

‚ö†Ô∏è Questions That Undermine Coaching

Leading Questions

Avoid: "Don't you think you should...?"

Why: Contains your preferred answer, removing client autonomy

Instead: "What options are you considering?"

"Why" Questions

Avoid: "Why didn't you exercise?"

Why: Can feel judgmental or put clients on the defensive

Instead: "What got in the way?" or "What was happening that made exercise difficult?"

Multiple Questions

Avoid: "What did you eat and how did you feel and did you notice any patterns?"

Why: Stacking questions confuses clients about which to answer

Instead: Ask one question, wait for the answer, then ask the next

Questions for Your Curiosity

Avoid: "What supplements are you taking?" (when not relevant to current conversation)

Why: Only ask what serves the client's immediate growth

Instead: Stay focused on what matters to them right now

Disguised Advice

Avoid: "Have you thought about trying meditation?"

Why: This is advice dressed up as a question

Instead: "What stress management approaches appeal to you?" (If they mention meditation, you can share info then)

Negative Assumptions

Avoid: "What's wrong?" or "Why are you stuck?"

Why: Focuses on problems rather than possibilities

Instead: "What would you like to explore?" or "What would movement forward look like?"

Instead of "Why"

"Why didn't you exercise?" can feel accusatory, triggering defensiveness and excuses. Try: "What got in the way?" or "What was happening that made exercise difficult?" These questions get the same information but with a completely different energy‚Äîone of curiosity rather than judgment. Clients are far more likely to explore honestly when they don't feel they're being criticized.

Practice & Application

Developing your questioning skills takes intentional practice. The good news is you can practice anywhere, anytime. Here are strategies to strengthen this crucial skill:

Daily Practice Strategies

üì± Convert Closed to Open

In everyday conversations, notice when you're about to ask a closed question and convert it to an open one. Instead of "Did you have a good day?" try "What was the best part of your day?"

‚è∏Ô∏è Practice Silence

After asking a powerful question, resist the urge to fill silence. Count to 10 in your head. Let the silence do its work. Some of the deepest insights emerge after what feels like uncomfortably long pauses.

üîÑ Turn Advice into Questions

Notice when you're about to give advice. Pause. Turn that advice into a question instead. This is one of the hardest and most valuable coaching skills.

üìù Build Your Question Bank

Keep a running list of powerful questions that resonate with you. When you hear a great question‚Äîin a podcast, from a mentor, in supervision‚Äîwrite it down. Review your list before coaching sessions.

üéØ Practice Intentional Questioning

Before each coaching session, identify 2-3 powerful questions that might serve this particular client. You may not use them, but having them prepared builds your questioning muscle.

üìπ Record and Review

With client permission, record sessions and review them. Count your open vs. closed questions. Notice where you jumped to advice instead of asking a question. This feedback is invaluable.

Pause & Reflect

Think about a powerful question someone has asked you‚Äîone that genuinely shifted your thinking or helped you see something new. What made that question powerful? How did it feel different from ordinary questions? Keep this memory as a guide for the questions you want to ask your clients.

Coach Tip

The Power of "What Else?" This simple two-word question is one of the most versatile in your toolkit. It invites clients to go deeper, explore further, and access insights beyond their first response. Use it liberally: "What else matters to you about this?" "What else have you tried?" "What else is possible?"

Test Your Knowledge
1

Transform this closed question into a powerful open question: "Did you stick to your meal plan?"

Answer: Several options work here, each serving slightly different purposes:

  • "How did your eating go this week?" (broad exploration)
  • "What worked well with your meal plan?" (focuses on successes)
  • "What did you notice about your eating patterns this week?" (invites observation and awareness)
  • "What was your experience with the meal plan?" (completely open, lets client define what's important)

Each of these invites reflection and exploration rather than simple yes/no, creating opportunity for deeper insight and learning.

2

Why are "why" questions often problematic in coaching?

Answer: "Why" questions can feel judgmental or put clients on the defensive, as if they need to justify themselves. They often trigger explanations about the past rather than exploration of possibilities. When asked "Why didn't you...?" clients may respond defensively with excuses rather than honestly exploring barriers.

Reframing to "what" or "how" questions gets similar information with less defensiveness:

  • Instead of "Why didn't you exercise?" ‚Üí "What got in the way?"
  • Instead of "Why do you want to lose weight?" ‚Üí "What matters to you about losing weight?"

The information gathered is similar, but the energy is curiosity rather than interrogation.

3

What are the six characteristics that make questions powerful?

Answer: Powerful coaching questions are:

  1. Simple and Clear ‚Äî Easy to understand, no jargon
  2. Open-Ended ‚Äî Invite exploration, not just facts
  3. Thought-Provoking ‚Äî Cause the client to pause and reflect
  4. Forward-Focused ‚Äî Move toward solutions and action
  5. Client-Centered ‚Äî About their experience, not your curiosity
  6. Non-Leading ‚Äî Don't contain your preferred answer
4

A client says, "I can never stick to a diet." What type of question would be most helpful, and give an example?

Answer: A clarifying question would be most helpful here to challenge the absolute language ("never") and explore their actual experience more accurately.

Examples:

  • "When you say 'never,' what does that really look like? Have there been times when you did stick with changes?"
  • "Tell me more about what happens when you try to stick to a diet."
  • "What's the longest you've maintained changes, and what was different then?"

These questions help the client see their experience more accurately, often revealing exceptions to their absolute statement that point toward solutions.

Key Takeaways
  • Questions are more powerful than answers because they promote ownership and self-discovery
  • Open questions invite exploration; closed questions confirm facts‚Äîaim for 80% open, 20% closed
  • Powerful questions are simple, open, thought-provoking, forward-focused, client-centered, and non-leading
  • Different question types serve different purposes: clarifying, values, possibility, obstacle, and action questions
  • Avoid leading questions, "why" questions, stacked questions, disguised advice, and questions that serve your curiosity rather than the client's growth
  • Developing questioning skills requires intentional daily practice‚Äîconvert closed to open, practice silence, turn advice into questions

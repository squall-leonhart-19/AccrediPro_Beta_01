Case Study: Jennifer's Stress Eating

When Part of You Wants to Change and Part of You Doesn't

üë©

Jennifer, Age 45

Project manager ‚Ä¢ Married ‚Ä¢ Mother of two teens ‚Ä¢ Wants to reduce stress eating but feels stuck

Context: Jennifer has identified stress eating as a pattern she wants to change. Her functional medicine practitioner recommended working with a coach. This is her third session.

Coach Last time we talked about your stress eating patterns. What's been on your mind about that since then?
üí° Opening with open question, letting client set the agenda
Jennifer Honestly, I've been thinking about it a lot. Part of me really wants to stop‚ÄîI know it's not helping. But another part of me thinks... it's my only comfort right now. Work is so stressful.
Coach So you're feeling pulled in two directions‚Äîpart of you wants to change this pattern, and part of you recognizes that food is giving you something you need right now. Some comfort in the midst of a really demanding time.
üí° Reflective listening, validating ambivalence, no judgment
Jennifer Exactly. And I feel bad about that. Like I should just have more willpower.
Coach What would you say to a friend who was struggling with this same thing?
üí° Perspective shift question‚Äîoften elicits self-compassion
Jennifer [pauses] I'd probably tell her to be easier on herself. That she's dealing with a lot.
Coach And what if you offered yourself that same kindness?
Jennifer [sighs] That's hard. But... I think what I really want is to find something else that helps with the stress. Food isn't really fixing anything‚ÄîI feel worse after.
üí° Client's own change talk emerging‚Äînot from coach pushing
Coach You feel worse after. What does that tell you?
Jennifer That it's not actually what I need. I need real stress relief, not temporary distraction followed by guilt.
Coach What does "real stress relief" look like for you?
üí° Evocative question‚Äîexploring possibilities, not prescribing
Jennifer Maybe... a walk? Or calling my sister. She always makes me laugh. Or even just writing in my journal‚ÄîI used to do that.
Coach Those are three really different options. Which one feels most doable this week when you notice the stress-eating urge coming on?
Jennifer The walk. I can step away from my desk for ten minutes. That feels manageable.
Coach How confident are you, on a scale from 1 to 10, that you can take a 10-minute walk when you notice the stress-eating urge this week?
Jennifer Probably an 8. I can do that.

üìä Session Outcome

Jennifer identified three alternative stress responses she could try: a 10-minute walk, calling her sister, or journaling. She chose to experiment with the walk this week, committing to trying it at least twice when she noticed stress-eating urges. Confidence level: 8/10.

Coach Tip

Validate ambivalence rather than fighting it. When a client expresses mixed feelings‚Äî"part of me wants X, but part of me wants Y"‚Äîresist the urge to argue for the "healthy" side. Instead, reflect both sides with equal respect. This creates safety and actually moves clients toward resolution faster than pushing.

Case Study 2: Building Motivation

Case Study: Marcus and Exercise

From "Should" to "Want To"

üë®

Marcus, Age 58

Retired accountant ‚Ä¢ Recently diagnosed with prediabetes ‚Ä¢ Knows he "should" exercise but hasn't started

Context: Marcus's practitioner recommended exercise as key to reversing his prediabetes. He agrees intellectually but hasn't started. This is their first coaching session.

Coach Marcus, what's most important to you about your health right now?
üí° Values exploration‚Äîfinding intrinsic motivation
Marcus Honestly? My grandkids. I just became a grandfather six months ago. I want to be around for her. I want to be the grandpa who plays with her, not the one watching from a chair.
Coach Being that active, engaged grandpa‚Äîthat's what matters. Tell me more about what that picture looks like.
üí° Amplifying change talk, building vision
Marcus [animated] I want to chase her around the yard. Take her to the park. Maybe teach her to ride a bike someday. My dad couldn't do any of that with me‚Äîhe had heart problems. I don't want to repeat that.
Coach There's something powerful there‚Äîwanting to give her what you didn't have. To break that pattern.
Marcus Yes. That's it exactly. So why am I sitting on the couch instead of doing something about it?
Coach What do you think gets in the way?
üí° Exploring barriers rather than ignoring them
Marcus I think... I don't know where to start. I haven't exercised regularly in decades. It feels overwhelming.
Coach If you imagine starting small‚Äîsomething that feels completely doable‚Äîwhat comes to mind?
üí° Right-sizing, making it achievable
Marcus I could walk. I used to like walking. Just... around the block maybe?
Coach Walking around the block. How confident are you, on a scale of 1-10, that you could do that three times this week?
Marcus That? Probably a 9. I could definitely do that.
Coach What would help you remember? What would make it more likely to happen?
Marcus If I do it first thing in the morning, before breakfast. And maybe... I'll tape that photo of Emma to the bathroom mirror. See her face and remember why I'm doing this.

üìä Session Outcome

Marcus committed to walking around the block three mornings before breakfast‚ÄîMonday, Wednesday, and Saturday. He taped a photo of his granddaughter to the bathroom mirror as motivation. Next session they'll review and potentially expand the goal. Confidence: 9/10.

Coach Tip

Connect every goal to values. Notice how Marcus went from "I should exercise" (external, weak) to "I want to be the grandfather who plays with Emma" (internal, powerful). Ask clients: "Why does this matter to you?" Keep asking until you reach something that brings emotion or energy. That's where sustainable motivation lives.

Key Patterns Across Both Cases

These two coaching conversations‚ÄîJennifer navigating ambivalence and Marcus building motivation‚Äîreveal consistent patterns that define effective health coaching. Let's examine what makes these exchanges work.

1

Follow the Client

The coach doesn't push their agenda‚Äîthey follow where the client's energy goes. When Jennifer mentions "real stress relief," the coach explores that. When Marcus talks about his granddaughter, the coach amplifies it.

2

Reflect Before Advising

Both coaches use extensive reflection before any suggestion. They mirror back what they hear, creating space for the client to hear themselves and deepen their own thinking.

3

Values as Fuel

Connecting to what matters‚ÄîEmma's future, genuine stress relief‚Äîpowers sustainable change. These aren't "shoulds" imposed from outside; they're desires emerging from within.

4

Start Small

Both clients ended with modest, achievable goals‚Äîa 10-minute walk, a walk around the block. Small wins build confidence and momentum better than ambitious plans that overwhelm.

5

Confidence Check

Using the 1-10 scale ensures goals are right-sized. Both clients reported 8+ confidence‚Äîhigh enough to succeed, building the self-efficacy that fuels future action.

The Coaching Difference

Notice what's missing from these conversations: no lectures about the dangers of prediabetes, no explaining why stress eating is bad, no prescribed exercise routines, no nutritionist meal plans. The wisdom and motivation came from the clients themselves. The coach simply created conditions for that wisdom to emerge.

Coach Tip

Trust the process, even when it feels slow. Notice how neither coach rushed to "fix" the problem. They allowed pauses. They let clients sit with questions. They trusted that the client had the answers, even when those answers took time to surface. This patience is what separates coaching from advice-giving.

üéß Reflective Listening

üí¨ Evoking Change Talk

üìè Right-Sizing Goals

ü§ù Rolling with Resistance

When clients express ambivalence or barriers, exploring rather than arguing‚Äîvalidating both sides of their internal conflict.

Following or leading? Reflecting or fixing? Self-awareness accelerates growth more than any book or course.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

In Jennifer's case, why didn't the coach immediately suggest alternative stress responses?

Answer: The coach waited for Jennifer to arrive at her own insight that she needed "real stress relief." Solutions that come from the client have more ownership and commitment than solutions provided by the coach. By reflecting and questioning, the coach created conditions for Jennifer to discover her own change talk and identify what she wanted‚Äîmaking the eventual goal truly hers. This is the difference between compliance (following advice) and commitment (owning the change).

2

How did connecting to his granddaughter change Marcus's approach to exercise?

Answer: Instead of exercising because he "should" (external motivation that rarely sustains), Marcus connected exercise to his deepest value‚Äîbeing an engaged, active grandfather who breaks his family's health pattern. This intrinsic motivation transforms exercise from a chore to a meaningful act connected to love and legacy. The photo on the mirror serves as a daily values reminder. When behavior connects to identity and purpose, it becomes self-sustaining.

3

What specific coaching technique did the coach use when asking Jennifer, "What would you say to a friend?"

Answer: This is called a perspective-taking question or externalization technique. It helps clients access compassion and wisdom for themselves that they easily offer others but struggle to apply internally. By stepping outside their own self-judgment, clients often discover insights that direct questioning wouldn't reveal. This technique is particularly powerful when clients are stuck in shame or self-criticism.

4

Why did both coaches use a 1-10 confidence scale before finalizing the client's goal?

Answer: The confidence scale is a right-sizing tool that ensures goals are achievable. If confidence is below 7, the goal is likely too ambitious and sets the client up for failure. Both Jennifer (8/10) and Marcus (9/10) reported high confidence, indicating their goals were appropriately sized for success. This builds self-efficacy‚Äîthe belief that "I can do this"‚Äîwhich is the foundation for sustainable behavior change. Small wins matter more than big plans.

Module 2 Summary: Key Takeaways
  • Coaching is a client-centered partnership where the client leads and owns their solutions‚Äînot a prescriptive relationship where the coach tells them what to do
  • Active listening and presence create the foundation for all other skills‚Äîwithout these, techniques feel manipulative rather than supportive
  • Powerful questions evoke insight and forward movement‚Äîthey invite exploration rather than defensiveness
  • Motivational interviewing helps clients resolve ambivalence and find their own motivation by validating both sides of internal conflict
  • Goals connected to values sustain change better than "shoulds"‚Äîintrinsic motivation outlasts willpower every time
  • Right-sized goals with clear action plans set clients up for success‚Äîsmall wins build the confidence for bigger changes
  • The coach's restraint is as important as their skill‚Äîknowing when not to talk, not to fix, not to advise creates space for client wisdom to emerge

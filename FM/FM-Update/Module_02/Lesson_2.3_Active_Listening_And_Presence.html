Case Study: When Sarah Felt Truly Heard

The Power of Deep Listening

"I've been to five doctors. They all run tests, tell me I'm fine, and suggest I manage my stress better. But no one has actually listened to what my life is like. They're typing notes while I'm talking, or already thinking about their next patient."

In her first coaching session, Sarah experienced something different. Her coach sat without a laptop between them, maintained eye contact, and asked clarifying questions that showed genuine curiosity. When Sarah paused mid-sentence, unsure how to express something, her coach simply waited‚Äîcomfortable with the silence.

After 15 minutes, Sarah said something she hadn't planned: "You know, I think part of why I'm so exhausted is that I've never given myself permission to prioritize my own health. I'm always taking care of everyone else."

The coach reflected: "So you're realizing that your fatigue might be connected to putting everyone else's needs ahead of your own‚Äîand that pattern has been invisible to you until now?"

"Yes," Sarah said, tears in her eyes. "That's exactly it. No one has ever helped me see that before."

What happened in that moment? The coach didn't diagnose, didn't prescribe supplements, didn't offer a meal plan. The coach simply listened at a level that allowed Sarah to hear herself think. That's the power of active listening.

Beyond Hearing: What Active Listening Really Means

Most people listen to respond. They're formulating their next thought while the other person is still speaking. They're scanning for the "problem" so they can offer the "solution." This is how we've been trained‚Äîespecially in healthcare settings where efficiency is prized.

Active listening means listening to understand‚Äîsetting aside your own agenda, judgments, and need to fix, in order to be fully present with another person's experience.

In functional medicine coaching, active listening serves multiple essential purposes:

  • Validation: Helps clients feel truly heard, often for the first time in their health journey
  • Trust building: Creates the safety essential for deep, vulnerable work
  • Information gathering: Reveals crucial details beyond what's explicitly stated
  • Self-awareness: Allows clients to hear themselves think and discover their own insights
  • Space creation: Provides room for insights, emotions, and realizations to emerge organically
The Gift of Attention

In our distracted, screen-saturated world, giving someone your complete, undivided attention has become rare. When you listen deeply‚Äîwithout simultaneously checking your phone, planning your response, or thinking about your next client‚Äîyou're offering a gift that most people rarely receive. This alone can be transformative.

Coach Tip

Before each coaching session, take 60 seconds to ground yourself. Close your eyes, take three deep breaths, and set an intention: "I am here to listen, not to fix. I trust that my client has their own answers." This simple practice dramatically improves your capacity for presence.

Three Levels of Listening

The Co-Active Coaching model distinguishes between three levels of listening, each with increasing depth and awareness. Understanding these levels helps you recognize where you are in any conversation‚Äîand shift to deeper listening when needed.

Level 1: Internal Listening
  • Focus is on yourself‚Äîyour thoughts, judgments, opinions
  • Planning what you'll say next
  • Comparing to your own experience
  • Fine for casual conversation, insufficient for coaching
Level 2: Focused Listening
  • Complete attention on the other person
  • Hearing words, tone, emotion
  • Watching body language and energy
  • Focus on understanding their experience
3

Level 3: Global Listening

Awareness of everything‚Äîthe client, yourself, the environment, the energy in the room. You're using all senses and intuition to gather information. You notice what's not being said, the emotions beneath the words, the patterns emerging across sessions. This is the level where coaching magic happens.

Pause & Reflect

In your last meaningful conversation, what level were you listening at? Were you thinking about what to say next (Level 1), or fully focused on understanding the other person (Level 2 or 3)? No judgment‚Äîjust awareness.

Coach Tip

When you catch yourself in Level 1 listening (planning your response), simply notice it without judgment and redirect your attention. Say to yourself: "Return to listening." This gentle correction, repeated consistently, trains your brain for deeper presence.

Coaching Presence: Being Fully Available

Coaching presence is the state of being fully available to your client in the moment‚Äîaware, open, and connected without agenda. The International Coaching Federation (ICF) identifies presence as one of the core coaching competencies, and for good reason: without it, even the best techniques feel hollow.

Coaching presence means:

1

Being With, Not Doing To

You're a companion on their journey, not an expert fixing them. You sit alongside rather than across from.

2

Comfortable With Silence

You allow pauses without rushing to fill them. Silence is where processing happens‚Äîhonor it.

3

Managing Your Reactions

Your judgments, emotions, and opinions don't hijack the session. You notice them and let them pass.

4

Trust in the Process

You believe the client has their own answers. Your job is to help them access their inner wisdom.

Common Pitfall

New coaches often mistake silence for failure‚Äîfeeling they must keep the conversation moving. In reality, the most powerful moments often happen in silence. When a client pauses, count to five slowly before speaking. You'll be amazed what emerges.

Listening Techniques That Deepen Connection

While presence is a state of being, there are specific techniques that support active listening and help clients feel truly heard. Master these foundational skills:

Core Active Listening Techniques

üîÑ Paraphrasing

Restating what you heard in your own words to confirm understanding. "So what I'm hearing is..." This shows you're listening and gives the client a chance to clarify.

üí≠ Reflecting Feelings

Naming the emotions you're perceiving. "It sounds like you're feeling frustrated..." or "I'm sensing some anxiety around this decision." This validates their emotional experience.

‚ùì Clarifying

Asking questions to ensure you understand. "When you say 'overwhelmed,' what does that look like for you?" Don't assume‚Äîget curious.

üìù Summarizing

Pulling together key themes from what you've heard. "So it seems like three main concerns are emerging: your energy, your relationship with food, and finding time for yourself. Did I capture that?"

ü§´ Silence

Allowing space for the client to think and feel. The pause after a powerful question is sacred‚Äîdon't rush to fill it.

üëÅÔ∏è Observing

Noticing and naming what you see. "I noticed your energy shifted when we started talking about your job. What's happening for you?"

Coach Tip

Practice the 70/30 rule: In a coaching session, your client should be speaking approximately 70% of the time, you 30%. If you're talking more than your client, you've likely shifted into teaching or advising mode rather than coaching.

Example: Active Listening in Action

Client: "I know I should exercise more, but I just can't seem to find the time. Between work and the kids, there's nothing left for me."

Level 1 Response (Internal Listening): "Have you tried waking up earlier? I always exercise at 5am before my day gets busy." [Focused on coach's own solution, not client's experience]

Level 2 Response (Focused Listening): "It sounds like you're feeling stretched between all your responsibilities, and taking time for yourself feels almost impossible right now." [Paraphrasing + reflecting feelings]

Client: "Yes, exactly. And then I feel guilty if I even think about taking time away from my family..."

Level 3 Response (Global Listening): "I'm noticing that when you talk about exercise, you use the word 'should,' and when you mention your family, your voice gets quieter. I'm curious‚Äîwhat would it be like if taking care of yourself wasn't something you had to steal time for, but rather something that made you a better mom and professional?" [Observing patterns, emotions, language; asking powerful question]

Client: [Long pause] "I've never thought about it that way before. I guess I've always seen self-care as selfish..."

Notice how Level 3 listening opened up a completely different conversation‚Äîone that addresses the underlying belief rather than just the logistics of finding time.

Common Listening Barriers

Even with good intentions and training, several barriers can interfere with deep listening. Awareness is the first step to overcoming them:

‚ö†Ô∏è Listening Obstacles to Watch For

Fixing Mode

Jumping to solutions before fully understanding. The urge to "help" can actually prevent the client from discovering their own solutions.

Assumptions

Thinking you know what they mean without checking. "I've heard this before" prevents you from hearing what's unique to this client.

Distractions

Phone notifications, environmental noise, or your own wandering thoughts pulling focus away from the client.

Judgment

Evaluating what they're saying as right/wrong, good/bad. Coaching requires radical non-judgment.

Relating

Shifting focus to your own similar experience. "Oh, that happened to me too!" While sometimes useful, it often redirects attention away from the client.

Planning

Thinking about your next question or comment while they're still speaking. This is Level 1 listening masquerading as engagement.

The Advice Trap

One of the biggest barriers for new coaches‚Äîespecially those with clinical backgrounds‚Äîis the urge to give advice. When you hear a problem, every instinct wants to help by offering solutions. But in coaching, the most helpful thing is often to keep listening. The client's own solutions are usually better than yours because they know their context, values, and what they're actually willing to implement. Advice-giving also prevents deeper exploration of what's really going on‚Äîyou jump to solutions before fully understanding the problem.

Coach Tip

When you feel the urge to give advice, pause and ask yourself: "Am I about to speak because the client needs this, or because I need to feel helpful?" Then try asking a question instead: "What do you think might work for you?" or "What have you already tried?"

Managing Your Internal Experience

Part of developing coaching presence is learning to notice and manage your own internal reactions without letting them hijack the session. This doesn't mean suppressing your humanity‚Äîit means developing awareness.

When you notice yourself triggered, distracted, or judgmental:

  • Take a breath and notice the sensation without acting on it
  • Mentally label it: "There's judgment" or "There's my fix-it impulse"
  • Gently redirect attention back to the client
  • Consider whether your reaction contains useful information worth exploring with the client

Practice Exercises: Building Your Listening Muscles

Like any skill, deep listening improves with deliberate practice. Try these exercises to develop your capacity for presence and active listening:

Exercises to Strengthen Your Listening Skills

üßò Silent Observation

Spend 10 minutes in a public place (caf√©, park) just observing without judgment. Notice people, sounds, movements. When you catch yourself judging or narrating, gently return to simple noticing.

üí¨ Reflection-Only Conversation

Have a 10-minute conversation with a friend using only reflections, no questions. Practice: "It sounds like..." "What I'm hearing is..." "So you're feeling..." Notice how this deepens connection.

‚è∏Ô∏è The 3-Second Pause

In regular conversations, practice waiting three full seconds before responding. This breaks the habit of planning your response while others speak.

üéß Podcast Paraphrasing

Listen to a podcast or interview. Every few minutes, pause and paraphrase what you heard out loud. This trains your brain to listen for meaning, not just words.

üß† Body Awareness

While listening to someone, notice your physical sensations. Where is tension? What emotions are arising? This develops your Level 3 listening capacity.

üì± Distraction Audit

For one day, notice every time you're distracted during a conversation. Write it down. Awareness of patterns is the first step to change.

Practice Tip

Start with low-stakes conversations‚Äîwith friends, family, or colleagues‚Äîbefore applying these techniques with clients. This allows you to build confidence and work through awkwardness in a safe context.

Daily Presence Practice

Building coaching presence isn't just about what you do during sessions‚Äîit's about cultivating a state of mindful awareness throughout your life. Consider these daily practices:

  • Morning centering: Begin each day with 5 minutes of quiet reflection or meditation
  • Mindful transitions: Between activities or clients, take three conscious breaths to reset
  • Tech boundaries: Create phone-free zones/times to practice undivided attention
  • Evening reflection: Journal about one conversation where you were fully present and one where you weren't‚Äîwhat made the difference?

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What's the difference between Level 1 and Level 2 listening?

Answer: Level 1 listening is internal‚Äîfocus is on your own thoughts, judgments, and what you'll say next. Your attention is primarily on yourself and how the conversation relates to you. Level 2 is focused listening‚Äîcomplete attention on the other person, hearing their words, noticing tone and body language, seeking to understand their experience rather than formulating your response. The focus shifts entirely from you to them.

2

Why is the "advice trap" problematic in coaching?

Answer: Giving advice shifts you into expert mode and takes away the client's agency and opportunity for self-discovery. Clients' own solutions are often better because they know their own context, values, resources, and what they're actually willing to implement. Advice can also prevent deeper exploration of what's really going on‚Äîyou jump to solutions before fully understanding the problem. In coaching, the goal is to help clients access their own wisdom, not to impose yours.

3

Describe what Level 3 (Global) listening involves and why it's important in coaching.

Answer: Level 3 listening involves awareness of everything‚Äîthe client, yourself, the environment, and the energy in the room. You're using all senses and intuition to gather information, noticing what's not being said, the emotions beneath words, body language shifts, energy changes, and patterns across sessions. It's important because this is where coaching breakthroughs happen‚Äîyou pick up on subtle cues that reveal deeper truths, beliefs, or emotions that the client may not consciously recognize.

4

Name three specific techniques for active listening and explain when you might use each one.

Answer: (1) Paraphrasing‚Äîrestating what you heard in your own words to confirm understanding. Use this when you want to ensure you've accurately understood complex or emotional content. (2) Reflecting feelings‚Äînaming the emotions you perceive. Use this when a client seems to be experiencing strong emotions but hasn't explicitly named them. (3) Silence‚Äîallowing space for processing. Use this after powerful questions or when the client pauses mid-thought‚Äîresist the urge to fill the space. Other valid techniques include clarifying (asking questions to deepen understanding), summarizing (pulling together themes), and observing (naming what you notice about body language or energy).

  • Three levels of listening: Internal (about you), Focused (on them), Global (everything)‚Äîcoaching requires Level 2 and 3
  • Coaching presence is being fully available without agenda, comfortable with silence, trusting the process
  • Key techniques: paraphrasing, reflecting feelings, clarifying, summarizing, silence, observing
  • Common barriers include fixing mode, assumptions, distractions, judgment, relating, and planning your response

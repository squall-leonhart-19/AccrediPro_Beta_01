When Knowing Isn't Enough

Jennifer, 45 years old

Knows what to do but can't seem to do it

Jennifer has been told by three different doctors that she needs to change her diet. She's read books, watched documentaries, and knows exactly what she should be eating. She even has a Pinterest board full of healthy recipes.

But she hasn't changed a thing. Every Monday she starts fresh. By Wednesday she's back to takeout and frozen meals. She feels shame, frustration, and hopelessness.

The problem isn't knowledgeâ€”it's ambivalence. Part of Jennifer desperately wants to change. Another part of her is terrified of failing again, overwhelmed by the effort, and secretly convinced it won't make a difference anyway.

Traditional advice-giving only deepens her shame. What Jennifer needs is someone who can help her resolve this internal conflictâ€”not by persuading, but by listening.

What Is Motivational Interviewing?

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a collaborative, person-centered approach developed by psychologists William Miller and Stephen Rollnick in the early 1980s. Originally designed for addiction counseling, it's now used across healthcare because of one simple truth:

People don't resist changeâ€”they resist being changed.

MI recognizes that ambivalence is normal. Most people have conflicting feelings about behavior change:

  • "I want to lose weight, but I love food."
  • "I know I should exercise, but I'm exhausted."
  • "I need to reduce stress, but I can't say no at work."

Rather than trying to overcome this resistance with logic or persuasion, MI works with ambivalenceâ€”helping clients explore both sides until they talk themselves into change.

Traditional Advice-Giving

"You need to eat more vegetables. Here's a meal plan. Just follow it."

Motivational Interviewing

"What matters most to you about your health? What would be different if you made changes to your diet?"

The first approach assumes the client lacks information. The second assumes they have their own reasons for changeâ€”they just need help accessing them.

The Spirit of MI: Four Foundational Principles

Before learning techniques, you must embody the spirit of MI. Without this foundation, OARS becomes manipulative.

1. Partnership

MI is a collaboration, not an expert-patient hierarchy. You're walking alongside the client, not leading from the front. Their expertise on their own life matters more than your knowledge about health.

2. Acceptance

This means absolute positive regard, accurate empathy, affirmation of strengths, and autonomy support. You accept the client exactly as they areâ€”including their ambivalence, resistance, and past failures.

3. Compassion

Your work actively promotes the client's welfare and prioritizes their needs. This isn't detached professionalismâ€”it's genuine care for their wellbeing.

4. Evocation

The client already has the wisdom, motivation, and resources they need. Your job is to evoke what's already thereâ€”not install something new.

The MI Mindset

"I trust that this person has reasons for their behavior. I trust they have the capacity to change. My role is to help them discover their own motivation, not to provide mine."

The OARS Technique

OARS is the core skill set of MI. These four tools work together to create a conversation that evokes change:

O

Open Questions

Questions that can't be answered with yes/no. They invite exploration, reflection, and elaboration. Examples: "What concerns you most about your current health?" or "How would your life be different if you made this change?"

A

Affirmations

Genuine statements that recognize the client's strengths, efforts, values, or past successes. Not empty praiseâ€”specific acknowledgment. "You've shown real courage by being here today."

R

Reflections

Mirroring back what you hearâ€”both content and emotion. This demonstrates deep listening and helps clients hear themselves. "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed by all the conflicting information out there."

S

Summaries

Collecting and reflecting back key themes from the conversation. Summaries link ideas together, highlight ambivalence, and transition between topics. They show you're listening and help the client see patterns.

OARS in Action: Jennifer's Session

Let's see how an MI-informed coach might work with Jennifer:

Coach: "Jennifer, what brings you here today?" [Open question]

Jennifer: "I just... I know I need to change my diet. I keep failing."

Coach: "You're feeling frustrated with yourself." [Reflection]

Jennifer: "Yes! I have all this information but I can't stick with anything."

Coach: "The fact that you keep tryingâ€”even after setbacksâ€”tells me something about your determination." [Affirmation]

Jennifer: [pauses] "I hadn't thought of it that way."

Coach: "What would be different in your life if you did make changes to your diet?" [Open question]

Jennifer: "I'd have more energy. I could play with my kids without feeling exhausted."

Coach: "Being present with your kids really matters to you." [Reflection]

Jennifer: "It does. That's why I keep trying."

Coach: "So let me make sure I understand. You've tried multiple times to change your diet because you want to be more energetic and present with your kids. You have the knowledge, but something gets in the way mid-week. Is that right?" [Summary]

Notice what the coach didn't do: give advice, provide meal plans, lecture about nutrition, or try to convince Jennifer. Instead, they evoked Jennifer's own reasons for change.

The Righting Reflex

Miller and Rollnick call the urge to fix, advise, and solve the "righting reflex." It's naturalâ€”you want to help. But premature advice triggers resistance. The client must articulate their own reasons for change before they're ready to hear solutions.

Working with Resistance

In MI, resistance isn't viewed as a character flawâ€”it's a signal that you're pushing too hard or moving too fast. When you encounter resistance, it's an invitation to roll with it rather than confront it.

What Resistance Sounds Like:

  • "Yes, but..." (arguing)
  • "I've tried that before and it didn't work." (discounting)
  • Interrupting, changing the subject (diverting)
  • Silence, minimal responses (withdrawing)

Traditional vs. MI Responses:

Confrontational Response

Client: "I don't have time to cook."

Coach: "You have to make time. Your health depends on it."

Rolling with Resistance

Client: "I don't have time to cook."

Coach: "Time is really tight for you right now. Tell me about your schedule."

The first response increases defensiveness. The second invites exploration and demonstrates empathy.

Strategies for Rolling with Resistance:

Simple Reflection

Mirror back exactly what you hear without judgment. "You don't think changing your diet will make a difference."

Amplified Reflection

Reflect back in slightly exaggerated form to invite the client to back off the extreme position. "So there's absolutely nothing you could change, even small things." (Often they'll respond: "Well, I guess I could...")

Double-Sided Reflection

Acknowledge both sides of their ambivalence. "On one hand, you're skeptical this will work. On the other hand, you're here because part of you wants things to be different."

Shifting Focus

If resistance is high, change the subject temporarily. "Let's set that aside for a moment and talk about..."

Recognizing and Evoking Change Talk

The central strategy of MI is to increase change talk and decrease sustain talk. These are client statements about their relationship to change.

Change Talk (What You Want to Hear):

Desire: "I want to..." Ability: "I could..." Reasons: "It would help me..." Need: "I need to..." Commitment: "I will..." Taking Steps: "I've started..."

Sustain Talk (Reasons Not to Change):

  • "I don't want to give up foods I love."
  • "I can't afford organic produce."
  • "It won't work for me."
  • "I don't need to change that much."

Key Insight

Research shows that when clients verbalize reasons for change, they become more committed to changing. When they argue against change (even in response to your persuasion), they become more entrenched. This is why evoking change talk is more effective than providing reasons yourself.

How to Evoke Change Talk:

Ask Evocative Questions

"What would be the benefits of making this change?"
"What's the worst thing that might happen if you don't change?"
"How would you like your life to be different five years from now?"
"What makes you think you could succeed if you decided to try?"

Ask About the Past

"When was a time you successfully made a difficult change?"
"What was different then?"
"What did you do that worked?"

Look Forward/Backward

"Imagine it's a year from now and you've made these changes. What does your life look like?"
"If you don't make changes, what do you think things will be like in five years?"

Explore Values

"What matters most to you in life?"
"How does your current health align with those values?"

Responding to Change Talk:

When you hear change talk, amplify it:

  • Reflect it back: "Being there for your kids is really important to you."
  • Ask for elaboration: "Tell me more about that."
  • Ask for examples: "What would that look like?"
  • Affirm: "That takes real insight to recognize."

Back to Jennifer: Evoking Change Talk

Coach: "Jennifer, on a scale of 0-10, how important is it for you to make changes to your diet?"

Jennifer: "Maybe a 7."

Coach: "A 7 is pretty high. Why a 7 and not a 2 or 3?" [This evokes reasons for change]

Jennifer: "Well, I don't want to keep feeling this way. And my mom had diabetesâ€”I'm scared of that."

Coach: "You want to feel better, and you want to avoid your mom's path." [Reflection amplifying change talk]

Jennifer: "Yes. I saw what she went through. I don't want that."

Coach: "What would be different if you were feeling more energetic and confident about your health?"

Jennifer: "I'd be more present. I wouldn't be so irritable with my kids. I could actually enjoy time with them instead of just surviving."

Coach: "Being the kind of mom you want to be is connected to taking care of yourself."

Jennifer: [tears up] "It is. I haven't thought about it that way."

The coach didn't convince Jennifer to change. They helped her articulate her own reasons. Now Jennifer is talking herself into changeâ€”which is far more powerful.

When to Move Forward

You'll know a client is ready to talk about action when they spontaneously start talking about commitment ("I will..."), taking steps ("I've already..."), or directly ask for help ("What should I do?"). Until then, stay in exploration mode.

Key Terms to Remember

Motivational Interviewing

A collaborative, person-centered approach that helps clients resolve ambivalence and find intrinsic motivation for change.

Ambivalence

Having conflicting feelings about changeâ€”simultaneously wanting to change and wanting to stay the same.

OARS

Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summariesâ€”the core communication skills of MI.

Change Talk

Client statements that favor change, including desire, ability, reasons, need, commitment, and taking steps.

Sustain Talk

Client statements that favor maintaining the status quo or argue against change.

Rolling with Resistance

Accepting and exploring resistance rather than confronting or arguing against it.

Welcome to Lesson 2.7

Insight without action doesn't create change. This lesson teaches you how to help clients transform their intentions into concrete, achievable goals and develop action plans that actually get implemented.

Learning Objectives

  • Guide clients from broad vision to specific, actionable goals
  • Apply SMART criteria to create well-formed goals
  • Connect goals to client values for sustained motivation
  • Use the confidence ruler to right-size goals
  • Develop detailed action plans with accountability structures
  • Help clients anticipate and plan for obstacles

From Vision to Action

Most clients come with broad desires: "I want to be healthier," "I want to lose weight," "I want more energy." The coach's role is to help transform these vague wishes into specific, achievable goals with clear action steps.

The progression typically moves:

  • Vision: The big picture of what they want ("I want to feel healthy and energetic")
  • Goals: Specific outcomes that move toward the vision ("Lose 20 pounds in 6 months")
  • Actions: Concrete steps to achieve goals ("Walk 30 minutes, 4 days per week")
  • Habits: Repeated actions that become automatic over time

Values-Based Goals

SMART goals tell you what and when. Values tell you why. Goals connected to deeply held values have far greater staying power than goals based on "shoulds."

Questions to connect goals to values:

  • "What's most important to you about achieving this?"
  • "How does this goal connect to what matters most in your life?"
  • "When you imagine success, what does that make possible for you?"
  • "Who are you doing this for beyond yourself?"

The Values Connection

A client might set a goal to exercise more. When connected to their value of "being present for my grandchildren," the goal becomes about something bigger than weight loss—it becomes about love and legacy. That connection sustains motivation through difficulty.

The Confidence Ruler

After setting a goal, always check the client's confidence level:

"On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can do this?"

  • 7 or above: Goal is appropriately sized—proceed
  • Below 7: Goal may be too ambitious—adjust until confidence reaches 7+

Right-Sizing Goals

A goal that's too ambitious sets clients up for failure. It's better to succeed at a smaller goal and build from there than to fail at a big one. Ask: "What would make this a 7 or 8?"

Action Planning

Every goal needs a specific action plan:

  • What specifically will you do?
  • When will you do it? (Day, time)
  • Where will it happen?
  • What do you need to prepare?
  • How will you track it?
  • Who will support you?

Complete Action Plan

Goal: Walk 30 minutes, 4 days per week

When: Monday, Wednesday, Friday, Sunday mornings at 7am

Where: Neighborhood loop (2 miles)

Preparation: Lay out clothes night before, charge phone for music

Tracking: Check off on kitchen calendar

Support: Text accountability partner after each walk

Planning for Setbacks

Obstacles are inevitable. Planning for them in advance increases success:

  • "What might get in the way?"
  • "If [obstacle] happens, what will you do instead?"
  • "What's your backup plan?"
  • "How will you get back on track after a slip?"

If-Then Planning

Research shows that "if-then" plans dramatically increase follow-through. Example: "If it's raining, then I'll do the indoor walking video instead." Having the plan ready prevents obstacles from derailing progress.

Key Terms

SMART Goals

Goals that are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

Confidence Ruler

A 1-10 scale used to assess how confident a client is they can achieve their goal; 7+ indicates appropriate sizing.

If-Then Planning

Pre-planning responses to anticipated obstacles to increase follow-through.

Values-Based Goals

Goals connected to what matters most to the client, providing deeper motivation than "shoulds."

Check Your Understanding

1

A client rates their confidence at 5/10 for their goal. What should you do?

Ask what would need to change to make it a 7 or 8. This usually means making the goal smaller or more achievable—shorter duration, less frequency, or simpler action. It's better to succeed at a smaller goal than fail at an ambitious one. You might ask: "What would make this feel more doable?" or "What would a 7 look like?"

2

Why are values-based goals more sustainable than "should" goals?

Values-based goals connect to what matters most deeply to the client—their identity, relationships, and what gives their life meaning. "Should" goals come from external pressure or obligation. When difficulties arise, values provide intrinsic motivation to persist, while "shoulds" often collapse. A goal to "be present for grandchildren" sustains motivation longer than "I should lose weight."

Key Takeaways

  • Transform vague wishes into specific, achievable goals with clear action steps
  • Use SMART criteria: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
  • Connect goals to values for sustained motivation
  • Check confidence with the 1-10 ruler; adjust until 7+
  • Create detailed action plans: what, when, where, how, who
  • Plan for obstacles with if-then strategies

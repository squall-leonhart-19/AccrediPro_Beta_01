When Good Intentions Aren't Enough

Rachel, Age 42

Marketing executive ‚Ä¢ Chronic stress ‚Ä¢ Poor sleep ‚Ä¢ Weight gain

"I know what I need to do. I've read all the books, listened to all the podcasts. I get motivated, make a plan, and start strong. Then two weeks later, I'm right back where I started. I don't understand why I can't follow through."

Rachel had plenty of knowledge. She had motivation. What she didn't have was a framework for translating her good intentions into sustainable action. Her goals were vague ("eat healthier," "exercise more"). She had no clear action steps. She hadn't planned for obstacles. And when life got busy‚Äîwhich it always did‚Äîher plans collapsed.

Sound familiar? Rachel's pattern is extraordinarily common. The gap between intention and action is where most health improvement efforts die. Your role as a coach is to build a bridge across that gap.

From Vision to Action: The Progressive Framework

Most clients come with broad desires: "I want to be healthier," "I want to lose weight," "I want more energy." These wishes are important‚Äîthey represent the client's vision‚Äîbut they're too vague to act upon. The coach's role is to help transform these vague wishes into specific, achievable goals with clear action steps.

The progression typically moves through four distinct stages:

1

Vision

The big picture of what they want. Example: "I want to feel healthy and energetic."

2

Goals

Specific outcomes that move toward the vision. Example: "Lose 20 pounds in 6 months."

3

Actions

Concrete steps to achieve goals. Example: "Walk 30 minutes, 4 days per week."

4

Habits

Repeated actions that become automatic over time. Example: Walking becomes part of the morning routine.

Coach Tip

When clients start with vague wishes, use this progression to sharpen focus: "You've said you want to feel healthier. Let's get specific‚Äîwhat would 'healthier' look like for you six months from now? What would you notice that's different?" This moves from vision toward concrete goals.

Vision provides the "why." Goals provide the "what." Actions provide the "how." And habits create the sustainability. Each level is essential, but they serve different purposes in the change process.

The SMART Goals Framework

Not all goals are created equal. Research in goal-setting theory shows that specific, challenging goals lead to higher performance than vague or easy goals. The SMART framework ensures goals are well-formed and achievable.

SMART Goals Defined

S ‚Äî Specific

Clearly defined, not vague. "Eat vegetables" becomes "Eat 5 servings of vegetables daily."

M ‚Äî Measurable

You can track progress. "More exercise" becomes "Walk 10,000 steps per day."

A ‚Äî Achievable

Challenging but realistic given current circumstances and resources.

R ‚Äî Relevant

Aligned with client's values and larger health vision.

T ‚Äî Time-bound

Has a clear deadline or timeframe. "Someday" becomes "within 30 days."

Vague Goal
  • "I want to eat better."
  • "I should exercise more."
  • "I need to reduce stress."
SMART Goal
  • "I will eat at least 5 servings of vegetables each day for the next 30 days, tracking in my food journal."
  • "I will walk 30 minutes, 4 mornings per week (Mon/Wed/Fri/Sun) for the next 8 weeks."
  • "I will practice 10 minutes of guided meditation every evening at 8pm for the next 4 weeks, using the Calm app."
Coach Tip

Use these questions to help clients SMARTen their goals: "How will you know you've achieved this?" (Measurable), "What specifically will you do?" (Specific), "By when?" (Time-bound), "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you?" (Achievable), "How does this connect to what matters most to you?" (Relevant).

Values-Based Goals: The Power of "Why"

SMART goals tell you what and when. Values tell you why. And the "why" is what sustains behavior change when motivation wanes and obstacles arise‚Äîwhich they always do.

Goals connected to deeply held values have far greater staying power than goals based on external pressure or "shoulds." A client pursuing weight loss because they think they "should" will struggle far more than one pursuing it because they value being present and active with their grandchildren.

The Values Connection

A client might set a goal to exercise more. When connected to their value of "being present for my grandchildren," the goal becomes about something bigger than weight loss or appearance‚Äîit becomes about love and legacy. That connection sustains motivation through difficulty, fatigue, and inconvenience.

Questions to help clients connect goals to values:

  • "What's most important to you about achieving this?"
  • "How does this goal connect to what matters most in your life?"
  • "When you imagine success, what does that make possible for you?"
  • "Who are you doing this for beyond yourself?"
  • "If you achieve this goal, how will your life be different in ways that matter to you?"
Coach Tip

When a client struggles with motivation, return to values: "Remember when you told me this was about being there for your grandkids? Tell me more about what that means to you." Reconnecting to the deeper "why" often reignites commitment when willpower alone has faded.

The Confidence Ruler: Right-Sizing Goals

After setting a goal‚Äîeven a SMART, values-aligned goal‚Äîalways check the client's confidence level with this simple but powerful question:

"On a scale of 1 to 10, where 1 is 'not at all confident' and 10 is 'completely confident,' how confident are you that you can do this?"

Interpreting the Confidence Ruler

‚úÖ 7 or Above

Goal is appropriately sized‚Äîproceed with action planning. The client has realistic confidence they can succeed.

‚ö†Ô∏è Below 7

Goal may be too ambitious‚Äîadjust until confidence reaches 7 or higher. Ask: "What would make this a 7 or 8?"

Right-Sizing Goals

A goal that's too ambitious sets clients up for failure and erodes self-efficacy. It's far better to succeed at a smaller goal and build from there than to fail at a big one and reinforce the belief that "I can't do this." Small wins create momentum. Repeated failures create hopelessness.

If confidence is below 7, help the client adjust the goal:

  • Reduce frequency: "4 days per week" becomes "2 days per week"
  • Shorten duration: "30 minutes" becomes "15 minutes"
  • Simplify the action: "Cook dinner from scratch" becomes "Add a vegetable to dinner"
  • Remove barriers: "Go to the gym" becomes "Walk in my neighborhood"

Action Planning: From Goal to Implementation

A goal without a plan is just a wish. Action planning transforms intention into implementation by answering six critical questions:

1

What?

What specifically will you do? (The action)

2

When?

What day and time? (The schedule)

3

Where?

Where will it happen? (The location)

4

Prepare?

What do you need to prepare? (Resources needed)

5

Track?

How will you track it? (Accountability system)

6

Support?

Who will support you? (Social accountability)

Complete Action Plan Example

Turning a Goal into Concrete Steps

Goal: Walk 30 minutes, 4 days per week for the next 8 weeks

What: Walk at moderate pace for 30 minutes

When: Monday, Wednesday, Friday, Sunday mornings at 7:00am

Where: Neighborhood loop (2-mile route around park)

Preparation: Lay out walking clothes and shoes the night before; charge phone for music/podcast; set alarm for 6:45am

Tracking: Check off each walk on kitchen calendar; use step-counting app to record steps and duration

Support: Text accountability partner (sister) after each walk with checkmark emoji; invite neighbor to join on Sundays

Notice how specific this plan is. There's no room for "I'll get to it when I can" or "I'll figure it out as I go." The more detailed the action plan, the higher the likelihood of follow-through.

Coach Tip

Help clients visualize the action: "Walk me through what Monday morning will look like. You wake up at 6:45am‚Äîthen what? Where are your shoes? What will you wear? What happens next?" This mental rehearsal dramatically increases actual follow-through.

Planning for Setbacks: If-Then Strategies

No matter how well-crafted the goal or how detailed the action plan, obstacles will arise. Life happens. The difference between clients who succeed and those who don't is often planning for obstacles in advance.

Research by psychologist Peter Gollwitzer shows that "if-then" planning (called implementation intentions) dramatically increases follow-through. Rather than relying on willpower in the moment, clients pre-decide how they'll respond to anticipated obstacles.

The Power of If-Then Planning

A client who says "If it's raining, then I'll do the indoor walking video instead" is far more likely to exercise than one who hasn't planned for rain. Having the backup plan ready prevents obstacles from derailing progress. Pre-decision removes in-the-moment hesitation.

Questions to help clients anticipate obstacles:

  • "What might get in the way of doing this?"
  • "When you've tried something like this before, what tripped you up?"
  • "If [obstacle] happens, what will you do instead?"
  • "What's your backup plan if your first choice isn't available?"
  • "How will you get back on track after a slip?"

‚úÖ Effective If-Then Plans

Weather Obstacle

"If it's raining, then I'll do the 20-minute YouTube walking workout in my living room."

Time Obstacle

"If I oversleep, then I'll do a 15-minute walk at lunch instead of 30 minutes in the morning."

Motivation Obstacle

"If I don't feel like it, then I'll commit to just 10 minutes‚ÄîI can always do more once I start."

Setback Recovery

"If I miss two days in a row, then I'll text my accountability partner and schedule my next walk immediately."

Coach Tip

Normalize setbacks before they happen: "Life is unpredictable. You will miss a day‚Äîthat's normal and expected. What matters isn't perfection; it's how quickly you get back on track. Let's plan now for how you'll do that." This prevents the all-or-nothing thinking that derails so many clients.

The Recovery Plan Matters Most

Clients don't fail because they slip‚Äîeveryone slips. They fail because they don't know how to recover from the slip. A well-designed recovery plan ("If I miss a day, then...") is often more important than the original action plan. It's the difference between one missed workout and abandoning the goal entirely.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

A client rates their confidence at 5 out of 10 for their goal. What should you do?

Answer: Ask what would need to change to make it a 7 or 8. This usually means making the goal smaller or more achievable‚Äîshorter duration, less frequency, or simpler action. It's better to succeed at a smaller goal than fail at an ambitious one. You might ask: "What would make this feel more doable?" or "What would a 7 look like?" Then adjust the goal until confidence reaches 7 or higher.

2

Why are values-based goals more sustainable than "should" goals?

Answer: Values-based goals connect to what matters most deeply to the client‚Äîtheir identity, relationships, and what gives their life meaning. "Should" goals come from external pressure or obligation. When difficulties arise, values provide intrinsic motivation to persist, while "shoulds" often collapse under pressure. A goal to "be present for grandchildren" sustains motivation far longer than "I should lose weight." Values create emotional pull; shoulds create psychological resistance.

3

What are the five components of SMART goals?

Answer: The SMART framework stands for: Specific (clearly defined, not vague), Measurable (you can track progress), Achievable (challenging but realistic), Relevant (aligned with values and larger vision), and Time-bound (has a clear deadline or timeframe). Each component ensures the goal is well-formed and actionable rather than vague and overwhelming.

4

A client says they want to "eat healthier." How would you help them create a SMART goal from this vague wish?

Answer: Use questions to add specificity and measurability: "What does 'healthier' mean to you specifically? What would you like to eat more of or less of? How many servings/times per day? How will you track this? By when?" A SMART version might become: "I will eat at least 5 servings of vegetables each day for the next 30 days, tracking in my food journal." This transformation makes the vague actionable and trackable.

Key Takeaways
  • Transform vague wishes into specific, achievable goals through the progression: Vision ‚Üí Goals ‚Üí Actions ‚Üí Habits
  • Use SMART criteria to ensure goals are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound
  • Connect goals to deeply held values for sustained motivation beyond willpower
  • Check confidence with the 1-10 ruler; adjust goals until confidence reaches 7 or higher
  • Create detailed action plans answering: what, when, where, how to prepare, how to track, who supports
  • Plan for obstacles with if-then strategies that pre-decide responses to anticipated barriers
  • Remember: Small wins create momentum; it's better to succeed at a smaller goal than fail at an ambitious one

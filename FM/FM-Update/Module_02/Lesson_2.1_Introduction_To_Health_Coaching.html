What Is Health Coaching

Health coaching is a professional partnership that supports clients in achieving their health and wellness goals through behavior change, accountability, and sustained motivation. Unlike medical professionals who diagnose and prescribe, health coaches partner with clients to bridge the gap between knowing what to do and actually doing it.

The definition from the National Board for Health & Wellness Coaching (NBHWC):

Official Definition

"Health and wellness coaches partner with clients seeking self-directed, lasting changes aligned with their values, which promote health and wellness and thereby enhance well-being. In the course of their work, health and wellness coaches display unconditional positive regard for their clients and a belief in their capacity for change, and honor that each client is an expert on their own life, while ensuring that all interactions are respectful and non-judgmental."

Let's break that down:

  • Partnership — You're working with clients, not on them
  • Self-directed — Clients set their own goals and find their own solutions
  • Aligned with values — Change that contradicts someone's core values won't last
  • Unconditional positive regard — You accept clients completely, without judgment
  • Client is the expert — They know their lives, circumstances, and what will work for them

The Coach vs Other Roles

Health coaching is often confused with therapy, consulting, or mentoring. While these roles can overlap, they serve fundamentally different purposes.

HEALTH COACH

"What do you want to achieve, and how can I support you in getting there?"

  • Future-focused
  • Non-directive (asks questions)
  • Client finds their solutions
  • Accountability partner

THERAPIST

"Let's explore your past to understand and heal current emotional wounds."

  • Past-focused
  • Treats mental health conditions
  • Diagnoses and creates treatment plans
  • Licensed clinical profession

CONSULTANT

"Here's what you should do based on my expertise."

  • Expert-driven
  • Provides solutions and recommendations
  • Directive approach
  • Hired for specialized knowledge

MENTOR

"I've been where you are—let me share what worked for me."

  • Experience-based guidance
  • Shares personal journey
  • Offers advice from their path
  • Role model relationship

Scope of Practice Clarity

Health coaches do not: diagnose medical conditions, treat mental health disorders, prescribe medications or supplements, create meal plans (unless credentialed as a dietitian), or provide psychotherapy. You do: support behavior change, provide accountability, facilitate goal-setting, teach health concepts, and help clients navigate obstacles.

Core Competencies

The International Coaching Federation (ICF) defines core competencies that form the foundation of professional coaching. These apply across all coaching domains, including health coaching:

1. Demonstrates Ethical Practice

Understanding coaching ethics, confidentiality, and professional standards. Maintaining clear boundaries and knowing when to refer clients to other professionals.

2. Embodies a Coaching Mindset

Developing a mindset of curiosity, openness, and flexibility. Believing clients are resourceful and capable of finding their own answers.

3. Establishes and Maintains Agreements

Partnering with clients to define what they want to accomplish, establishing clear expectations about the coaching relationship and process.

4. Cultivates Trust and Safety

Creating an environment of respect, openness, and support. Demonstrating unconditional positive regard and non-judgment.

5. Maintains Presence

Being fully conscious and attentive to the client. Responding to the whole person—noticing body language, energy shifts, and what's not being said.

6. Listens Actively

Focusing completely on what the client says and doesn't say. Listening for values, beliefs, patterns, and opportunities for growth.

7. Evokes Awareness

Using powerful questions to help clients gain new insights, discover blind spots, and access their own wisdom.

8. Facilitates Client Growth

Supporting clients in designing goals, actions, and accountability structures that align with their values and lead to sustainable change.

These competencies form the curriculum for the rest of this module. You'll develop each skill through theory, examples, and practice.

The Coaching Mindset

What separates coaching from advice-giving? Mindset. The most powerful shift you'll make as a coach is moving from "I need to fix this person" to "This person has everything they need—my job is to help them access it."

The coaching mindset is built on these core beliefs:

1

Clients Are Resourceful & Whole

They aren't broken. They have strengths, experiences, wisdom, and capacity. Your role isn't to provide answers—it's to help them discover their own.

2

Clients Are the Experts on Their Own Lives

You might know health science, but they know their circumstances, values, barriers, and what's actually feasible for them. Co-create solutions rather than prescribing them.

3

Questions Are More Powerful Than Answers

When you tell someone what to do, they might comply temporarily. When you ask questions that help them discover it themselves, they own the solution—and are far more likely to follow through.

4

Change Comes From Within

External motivation (fear, pressure, obligation) creates short-term compliance. Internal motivation (values, purpose, identity) creates lasting transformation.

Self-Reflection

Think about a time someone gave you unsolicited advice. How did it feel? Now think of a time someone asked you thoughtful questions that helped you figure something out yourself. Notice the difference? That's the power of the coaching approach.

The Power of Partnership

Research shows that the quality of the coaching relationship is the strongest predictor of success—more than techniques, certifications, or years of experience. When clients feel truly heard, accepted, and supported, they're capable of extraordinary change.

Key Terms to Remember

Health Coaching

A professional partnership that supports clients in achieving self-directed, lasting health changes through behavior modification, accountability, and sustained motivation.

Non-Directive Approach

A coaching style where the coach asks questions rather than providing answers, allowing clients to discover their own solutions and insights.

Unconditional Positive Regard

Accepting and respecting clients completely without judgment, regardless of their choices, behaviors, or circumstances.

ICF Core Competencies

Eight foundational coaching skills defined by the International Coaching Federation, including ethics, presence, active listening, and evoking awareness.

Scope of Practice

The boundaries defining what health coaches are qualified and legally permitted to do—supporting behavior change but not diagnosing, prescribing, or providing therapy.

Client-Centered

An approach where the client's goals, values, and preferences guide the coaching process—the client is the expert on their own life.

Check Your Understanding

Reflect on these questions before moving to the next lesson:

1

How does health coaching differ from therapy, consulting, and mentoring? Give one key distinction for each.

Health coaching is future-focused and non-directive (clients find solutions). Therapy is past-focused and treats mental health conditions. Consulting is expert-driven and provides solutions. Mentoring shares personal experience and advice. Health coaching uniquely empowers clients to discover their own answers through questions and partnership.

2

What are three of the eight ICF core competencies, and why is each important?

Examples include: (1) Cultivates Trust and Safety—creates the foundation for honest communication; (2) Listens Actively—ensures you truly understand the client; (3) Evokes Awareness—helps clients gain insights they wouldn't reach alone. All eight competencies work together to create effective coaching relationships.

3

What is the fundamental mindset shift required to be an effective coach rather than an advice-giver?

The shift from "I need to fix this person" to "This person has everything they need—my job is to help them access it." This means believing clients are resourceful, whole, and expert on their own lives. The coach's role is to evoke awareness through questions rather than provide answers.

Key Takeaways

  • Health coaching is a professional partnership supporting self-directed, lasting behavior change aligned with client values
  • Coaches differ from therapists (treat mental health), consultants (provide expert solutions), and mentors (share personal experience)
  • ICF defines eight core competencies—including ethics, presence, active listening, and evoking awareness
  • The coaching mindset believes clients are resourceful and whole—questions are more powerful than answers
  • Health coaches significantly improve adherence, self-efficacy, and health outcomes—the relationship quality predicts success

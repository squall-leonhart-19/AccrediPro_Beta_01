Cardiovascular Disease: The Leading Cause of Death

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) remains the leading cause of death worldwide, responsible for approximately 17.9 million deaths annually. In the United States, heart disease claims more lives than all forms of cancer combined.

CVD encompasses several conditions:

  • Coronary artery disease (CAD): Atherosclerotic plaque buildup in heart arteries
  • Stroke: Blocked or ruptured blood vessels in the brain
  • Peripheral artery disease: Atherosclerosis in leg arteries
  • Heart failure: The heart's inability to pump effectively
  • Arrhythmias: Abnormal heart rhythms
  • Hypertension: High blood pressure

The most common underlying processâ€”atherosclerosisâ€”is not simply a plumbing problem of cholesterol deposits. It's an inflammatory disease with metabolic roots.

Beyond the Cholesterol Hypothesis

For decades, the "diet-heart hypothesis" dominated cardiovascular medicine: dietary fat raises blood cholesterol, which deposits in arteries, causing heart disease. While cholesterol is involved, this simplified model has significant limitations:

Limitations of the Cholesterol-Only Model

  • 50% of heart attack victims have normal LDL cholesterol
  • Many people with high LDL never develop heart disease
  • Statin drugs reduce LDL but only prevent about 25-35% of cardiac events
  • The model doesn't explain why inflammation markers predict risk better than LDL alone
  • It ignores metabolic factors (insulin resistance, blood sugar) that strongly predict CVD

The functional medicine approach recognizes that atherosclerosis is an inflammatory disease where cholesterol is just one player among many. Understanding the fuller picture enables more effective prevention.

How Atherosclerosis Actually Develops

The Atherosclerosis Process

Step 1: Endothelial Damage
The arterial lining (endothelium) is damaged by oxidative stress, inflammation, high blood pressure, high blood sugar, toxins, or infections. A healthy endothelium is protective; damaged endothelium becomes vulnerable.

Step 2: LDL Particles Enter the Arterial Wall
Small, dense LDL particles (more atherogenic than large, fluffy LDL) penetrate the damaged endothelium. These particles become oxidized, triggering an immune response.

Step 3: Inflammatory Response
The immune system sends macrophages to engulf oxidized LDL. These macrophages become "foam cells"â€”the beginning of plaque. Inflammatory cytokines perpetuate the process.

Step 4: Plaque Formation and Growth
Over time, foam cells accumulate, smooth muscle cells proliferate, and fibrous caps form over the fatty core. Calcium deposits may form (calcification).

Step 5: Plaque Rupture or Progression
Unstable plaques can rupture, triggering blood clots that cause heart attacks or strokes. Stable plaques may gradually narrow arteries, reducing blood flow.

This process takes decades and is influenced by many factorsâ€”not just cholesterol levels.

Inflammation: The Central Driver

Inflammation is increasingly recognized as the central driver of cardiovascular disease. The landmark CANTOS trial showed that reducing inflammation (with an anti-inflammatory drug) reduced heart attacks even without lowering cholesterolâ€”proving that inflammation is causally involved, not just a marker.

Sources of Cardiovascular Inflammation

  • Metabolic dysfunction: Insulin resistance, obesity, high blood sugar all promote inflammation
  • Inflammatory diet: Refined carbohydrates, industrial seed oils, processed foods
  • Chronic infections: Periodontal disease, H. pylori, chronic viral infections
  • Gut dysfunction: Leaky gut allows inflammatory molecules into circulation
  • Chronic stress: Sustained cortisol and catecholamines damage blood vessels
  • Smoking: Direct endothelial damage and oxidative stress
  • Sleep deprivation: Increases inflammatory markers significantly
  • Environmental toxins: Heavy metals, air pollution damage endothelium

This explains why addressing root causesâ€”gut health, stress, diet, sleepâ€”matters for cardiovascular prevention even when these factors seem unrelated to the heart.

Metabolic syndrome affects approximately 35% of American adults. The underlying driver is insulin resistanceâ€”the condition where cells become less responsive to insulin, requiring the pancreas to produce more and more insulin to manage blood sugar.

Insulin resistance creates a cascade of cardiovascular damage:

  • Elevated insulin promotes inflammation and arterial damage
  • The liver produces more small, dense LDL particles (the dangerous kind)
  • Triglycerides rise while protective HDL falls
  • Blood pressure increases due to sodium retention and vessel stiffness
  • Blood becomes more prone to clotting
  • Fat accumulates around organs (visceral adiposity)

Key Cardiovascular Risk Factors

Traditional vs. Emerging Risk Factors

Traditional Risk Factors:

  • High LDL cholesterol
  • Low HDL cholesterol
  • Hypertension
  • Smoking
  • Diabetes
  • Family history of premature CVD
  • Age (men â‰¥45, women â‰¥55)
  • Obesity
  • Physical inactivity

Emerging/Functional Risk Factors:

  • Insulin resistance (even without diabetes)
  • Elevated triglyceride/HDL ratio
  • Small, dense LDL particle number (LDL-P)
  • Elevated lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)]
  • High hs-CRP (inflammation marker)
  • Elevated homocysteine
  • Elevated uric acid
  • Sleep apnea
  • Chronic stress/depression
  • Periodontal disease
  • Gut dysbiosis

Women and Heart Disease

Heart disease is the leading cause of death for women, yet it's often perceived as a "man's disease." Women face unique cardiovascular challenges:

Cardiovascular Considerations for Women

  • Different symptoms: Women often experience atypical symptomsâ€”fatigue, nausea, back/jaw pain rather than classic chest pain
  • Menopause increases risk: Estrogen is cardioprotective; loss at menopause accelerates CVD development
  • Microvascular disease: Women more often have disease in small arteries that standard tests may miss
  • Autoimmune conditions: More common in women and increase CVD risk
  • Pregnancy complications: Preeclampsia, gestational diabetes predict future CVD
  • Stress and depression: Have stronger CVD impact in women

The Functional Medicine Approach

The functional medicine approach to cardiovascular health:

  • Addresses root causes: Inflammation, insulin resistance, oxidative stress, stress, gut dysfunction
  • Goes beyond LDL: Looks at particle number, size, inflammation markers, metabolic markers
  • Emphasizes lifestyle first: Diet, exercise, stress management, sleep are foundational
  • Personalizes intervention: Identifies each person's unique risk factors
  • Uses medications when needed: But as part of a comprehensive plan, not the sole intervention

  • Atherosclerosis is an inflammatory disease, not simply a cholesterol storage problem

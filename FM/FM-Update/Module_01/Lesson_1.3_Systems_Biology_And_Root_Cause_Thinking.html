What Is Systems Biology?

Systems biology is a way of understanding the body as an integrated whole rather than a collection of separate parts. It recognizes that:

1

Everything is connected. Gut health affects brain function. Hormones affect immunity. Stress affects digestion.

2

The whole is greater than the sum of parts. You can't understand health by studying organs in isolation.

3

Small changes can have large effects. Fixing one dysfunction can create cascading improvements.

This contrasts sharply with the reductionist approach of conventional medicine, which divides the body into specialties:

Reductionist View

  • Heart = Cardiologist
  • Gut = Gastroenterologist
  • Skin = Dermatologist
  • Mind = Psychiatrist
  • Who sees the whole person?

Systems View

  • Heart ‚Üî Gut ‚Üî Skin ‚Üî Mind
  • All systems communicate
  • Dysfunction ripples through
  • Healing ripples through too
  • The whole person, always

The Seven Core Systems

Functional medicine organizes the body into seven core physiological systems. Understanding these is essential‚Äîthey form the framework you'll use to analyze every client's health picture.

1. Assimilation

What it includes: Digestion, absorption, microbiome, gut barrier function

Why it matters: Often called the "root of all health." If you can't break down food, absorb nutrients, and maintain gut integrity, every other system suffers.

2. Defense & Repair

What it includes: Immune function, inflammation, infection, microbiome

Why it matters: Chronic inflammation underlies almost every chronic disease. An immune system out of balance attacks self (autoimmunity) or fails to protect (infections, cancer).

3. Energy

What it includes: Mitochondrial function, energy production, oxidative stress

Why it matters: Every cell needs energy. When mitochondria struggle, fatigue pervades everything. Brain fog, muscle weakness, and poor recovery all trace here.

4. Biotransformation & Elimination

What it includes: Liver detoxification, kidney filtration, lymphatic drainage

Why it matters: We're exposed to more toxins than ever. If elimination pathways are overwhelmed, toxins accumulate, causing widespread dysfunction.

5. Transport

What it includes: Cardiovascular system, lymphatics, blood

Why it matters: Nutrients, hormones, and immune cells need delivery. Waste needs removal. Circulation issues affect every tissue.

6. Communication

What it includes: Hormones, neurotransmitters, immune messengers (cytokines)

Why it matters: The body's messaging system. When signals are disrupted, miscommunication creates symptoms throughout‚Äîthyroid, adrenals, sex hormones, brain chemistry.

7. Structural Integrity

What it includes: Cellular membranes, musculoskeletal system, barriers (gut, blood-brain)

Why it matters: Structure determines function. Compromised gut barrier = "leaky gut." Compromised cell membranes = impaired signaling. Compromised joints = pain and limitation.

Key Insight

Notice how these systems overlap and interact. The gut (Assimilation) houses 70% of the immune system (Defense & Repair). Hormones (Communication) affect energy (Energy). Toxins (Biotransformation) cause inflammation (Defense & Repair). Everything connects.

Root Cause Thinking: The "5 Whys"

Root cause thinking means asking "why" until you get to the deepest level of causation. The "5 Whys" technique comes from Toyota's manufacturing process, but it applies perfectly to health:

Example: Chronic Headaches

Why #1: Why does she have headaches?

‚Üí Muscle tension and inflammation in the neck and head

Why #2: Why is there tension and inflammation?

‚Üí Poor sleep and chronic stress response

Why #3: Why is sleep poor and stress high?

‚Üí Blood sugar crashes at night causing cortisol spikes

Why #4: Why is blood sugar crashing?

‚Üí High refined carb diet + eating late + no protein at dinner

Why #5: Why these eating patterns?

‚Üí Stressful work schedule, skipping meals, relying on quick carbs

Root Cause Found: The headaches trace back to lifestyle patterns around food, stress, and work‚Äînot just "muscle tension." Addressing only symptoms would mean endless painkillers. Addressing root cause means dietary changes + stress management = no more headaches.

The ATM Framework Revisited

Remember the Antecedents, Triggers, Mediators framework from Lesson 1.1? Now let's apply it with systems thinking:

Component Definition Examples
Antecedents Predisposing factors that set the stage Genetics, childhood nutrition, early infections, birth trauma, family stress patterns
Triggers Events that initiate symptoms Infection, trauma, major life stress, toxic exposure, surgery, medication
Mediators Factors that perpetuate the condition Ongoing inflammation, gut dysbiosis, chronic stress, poor diet, sleep deprivation

Here's the key: you often can't change antecedents (they're in the past). Triggers may have already happened. But mediators are happening NOW‚Äîand they're where intervention creates change.

Client-Friendly Language

"We can't go back and change what started this‚Äîbut we can absolutely change what's keeping it going. That's where we focus: on the factors you can control today that will shift your health tomorrow."

Developing Your Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is a skill that develops with practice. Here's how to cultivate it:

1. Always ask "What else?"

When a client mentions one symptom, probe for others. Symptoms travel in clusters that reveal patterns.

2. Look for timing connections

When did symptoms start? What else was happening then? Timelines reveal triggers and connections.

3. Consider upstream causes

If there's a hormonal imbalance, what's causing it? Stress? Gut dysfunction? Toxins? Don't stop at the first answer.

4. Map to the seven systems

Which systems are affected? How might they be connected? This organizes complexity into actionable patterns.

5. Remember: the body wants to heal

Something is blocking that healing. Your job is to find what's in the way and help remove it.

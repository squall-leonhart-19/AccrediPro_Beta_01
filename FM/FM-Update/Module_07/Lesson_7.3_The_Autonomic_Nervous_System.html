The Autonomic Nervous System

Reading Time: 11 minutes

Connecting to Your Journey

We've explored the HPA axis and cortisol patterns. But the stress response isn't just hormonal—it's also neural. The autonomic nervous system orchestrates the immediate stress response and governs whether we're in "fight or flight" or "rest and digest" mode. Understanding this system is crucial because it provides direct, accessible pathways for intervention. Unlike hormones, which take time to shift, the nervous system can be influenced in the moment through breath, movement, and other practices.

  • Learn the roles of the sympathetic and parasympathetic branches
  • Explore the vagus nerve and its importance in stress resilience

Rebecca's Revelation

Rebecca, 51, came to her health coach feeling like a failure. "I know all the right things to do. I know I should meditate. I know I should relax. But when I try, my body won't cooperate. My heart races, I feel agitated, and I end up more stressed than when I started."

Her coach recognized the pattern. Rebecca's nervous system was stuck in a sympathetic-dominant state. Telling her to "just relax" was like telling someone with a broken leg to "just walk"—her nervous system literally couldn't access calm on command.

"This isn't a willpower problem," her coach explained. "It's a nervous system regulation problem. And the good news is, we can work with your nervous system directly."

Through gentle practices that met her body where it was—not where she thought it should be—Rebecca gradually rebuilt her capacity for calm. "I never knew I could actually train my nervous system," she said later. "I thought I was just wired this way."

Understanding the Autonomic Nervous System

The autonomic nervous system (ANS) is the part of the nervous system that operates largely below conscious awareness, regulating vital functions like heart rate, digestion, respiration, and the stress response. "Autonomic" means automatic—these functions happen without conscious effort.

The Two Branches of the ANS

Sympathetic Nervous System

Role: The "accelerator"—activates the stress response

Nickname: "Fight or Flight"

When activated: Increases heart rate, dilates pupils, inhibits digestion, releases adrenaline, tenses muscles, sharpens focus on threat

Parasympathetic Nervous System

Role: The "brake"—calms and restores

Nickname: "Rest and Digest"

When activated: Slows heart rate, constricts pupils, promotes digestion, repairs tissues, restores energy, enables social engagement

In a healthy system, these branches work together in a dynamic balance—like a car using both gas and brake to navigate varied terrain. Problems arise when the system gets stuck in one state or can't transition smoothly between them.

The Vagus Nerve: The Wandering Nerve

The vagus nerve is the primary nerve of the parasympathetic nervous system. "Vagus" comes from Latin meaning "wandering"—and wander it does, from the brainstem through the neck, chest, and abdomen, connecting to the heart, lungs, digestive organs, and more.

The Vagus Nerve's Many Roles

Heart Rate Regulation:

The vagus nerve acts as the "brake" on heart rate. Strong vagal tone keeps resting heart rate lower and enables quick recovery from stress.

Digestion:

Stimulates digestive secretions, gut motility, and enzyme release. "Rest and digest" is literal—the vagus nerve is essential for proper digestion.

Inflammation Control:

The vagus nerve has an anti-inflammatory pathway. It can sense inflammation and signal the brain to release anti-inflammatory signals.

Gut-Brain Communication:

80-90% of vagal fibers carry signals from gut to brain. The vagus is the primary highway for gut-brain axis communication.

Social Engagement:

The vagus connects to the muscles of the face, throat, and middle ear—enabling the "social engagement" behaviors like facial expression, eye contact, and voice modulation.

Vagal Tone: The Key to Resilience

Vagal tone refers to the activity of the vagus nerve. High vagal tone means a robust, responsive parasympathetic system—the ability to recover quickly from stress, regulate emotions, and maintain calm under pressure. Low vagal tone is associated with chronic stress, inflammation, anxiety, depression, and various chronic diseases.

Measuring Vagal Tone: Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) is the gold standard measure of vagal tone. It measures the variation in time between heartbeats. Contrary to what you might expect, a healthy heart doesn't beat like a metronome—it has natural variation reflecting the interplay between sympathetic and parasympathetic activity.

  • Higher HRV: Greater variability = stronger vagal tone = better stress resilience
  • Lower HRV: Less variability = weaker vagal tone = reduced stress resilience

HRV can be measured with simple wearable devices and apps, making it an accessible biofeedback tool for tracking nervous system health over time.

The Polyvagal Theory

Dr. Stephen Porges' Polyvagal Theory revolutionized our understanding of the autonomic nervous system. Rather than viewing it as simply two branches (sympathetic and parasympathetic), Polyvagal Theory identifies three hierarchical states:

The Three Polyvagal States

1. Ventral Vagal (Social Engagement)

State: Safe and social

What it feels like: Calm, connected, curious, open. Able to think clearly, engage with others, and be present.

Functions accessible: Social engagement, clear thinking, healing, immune function, digestion

2. Sympathetic (Fight or Flight)

State: Mobilized for action

What it feels like: Anxious, agitated, irritable, hypervigilant, restless. May feel like needing to "do something."

Functions accessible: Physical defense, escape, action. Digestion, immune function, and clear thinking are impaired.

3. Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown/Freeze)

State: Immobilized, collapsed

What it feels like: Numb, disconnected, foggy, hopeless, exhausted, dissociated. May feel like you can't move or respond.

Functions: Extreme energy conservation. This is the "playing dead" response when fight or flight isn't possible.

Key insight: These states operate hierarchically. We default to ventral vagal (safe) when possible. If safety isn't perceived, we shift to sympathetic (fight/flight). If that fails, we collapse into dorsal vagal (freeze). This isn't weakness—it's survival programming.

Why This Matters for Coaching

Clients stuck in sympathetic activation can't "just calm down"—their nervous system is detecting threat. Clients stuck in dorsal vagal can't "just get motivated"—their system has collapsed. Neither is a willpower problem. Both require nervous system-aware approaches:

  • For sympathetic dominance: Focus on safety cues, grounding, gentle activation of parasympathetic
  • For dorsal vagal shutdown: Focus on tiny movements, social connection, warm presence—gently mobilizing without triggering sympathetic surge

Signs of Nervous System Dysregulation

Chronic stress can lead to a nervous system that's stuck, oscillating between extremes, or unable to return to baseline. Here are signs to watch for:

Signs of Sympathetic Dominance

  • Chronic anxiety, feeling "on edge"
  • Racing heart, palpitations
  • Difficulty sleeping despite exhaustion
  • Hypervigilance—startling easily
  • Irritability, short fuse
  • Tension in muscles (especially neck, shoulders, jaw)
  • Shallow, rapid breathing
  • Digestive issues (IBS, nausea, poor digestion)
  • Inability to relax even in safe environments

Signs of Dorsal Vagal Dominance (Freeze/Collapse)

  • Chronic fatigue, feeling "flat" or numb
  • Depression, hopelessness
  • Dissociation—feeling disconnected from body or reality
  • Brain fog, difficulty thinking
  • Low motivation, difficulty initiating tasks
  • Social withdrawal
  • Very slow heart rate
  • Constipation (gut shutdown)
  • Feeling like nothing matters

Signs of Oscillation (Unstable Regulation)

  • Mood swings—anxiety to depression and back
  • Energy crashes after periods of high energy
  • Unpredictable reactions to stress
  • Difficulty finding "middle ground"
  • Feeling constantly off-balance

The Window of Tolerance

The Window of Tolerance is a concept developed by Dr. Dan Siegel describing the zone in which a person can effectively manage and integrate experiences. Within this window, we can experience emotions without becoming overwhelmed.

The Window of Tolerance Model

HYPERAROUSAL
Above the window: Anxiety, panic, rage, hypervigilance, restlessness
WINDOW OF TOLERANCE
Optimal zone: Able to think, feel, and respond effectively
Calm but alert • Connected • Present
HYPOAROUSAL
Below the window: Numbness, dissociation, depression, collapse, shutdown

Chronic stress narrows the window. With repeated stress and insufficient recovery, the window of tolerance shrinks. People become more reactive—smaller triggers push them into hyperarousal or hypoarousal. The goal of nervous system work is to widen the window—building capacity to stay regulated amid life's challenges.

What Affects Nervous System Regulation?

Factors That Influence Autonomic Balance

Early Life Experiences:

Attachment patterns, childhood adversity, and trauma shape the developing nervous system. ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences) are associated with lifelong nervous system dysregulation.

Current Stress Load:

Ongoing stressors accumulate, depleting regulatory capacity. Both the number and intensity of stressors matter.

Sleep:

Sleep is when the parasympathetic system dominates and restoration occurs. Poor sleep directly impairs nervous system regulation.

Nutrition:

Blood sugar imbalances trigger sympathetic activation. Nutrient deficiencies (especially B vitamins, magnesium, omega-3s) impair nervous system function.

Movement:

Regular moderate exercise improves vagal tone. Both sedentary lifestyle and excessive exercise can dysregulate the nervous system.

Social Connection:

The nervous system is wired for co-regulation. Safe relationships activate the ventral vagal system. Isolation increases sympathetic tone.

Gut Health:

The gut-brain axis via the vagus nerve means gut inflammation and dysbiosis directly affect nervous system state.

The Coach's Role in Nervous System Regulation

What You Can Do

✓
Educate: Help clients understand their nervous system state isn't a character flaw—it's physiology
✓
Assess patterns: Help clients recognize whether they tend toward hyperarousal, hypoarousal, or oscillation
✓
Model regulation: Your own regulated presence is co-regulating. A calm coach helps calm the client's nervous system
✓
Teach practices: Introduce breathing techniques, grounding exercises, and vagal toning (covered in the next lesson)
✓
Support lifestyle factors: Address sleep, nutrition, movement, and social connection as nervous system medicine
✓
Refer when needed: Significant trauma or severe dysregulation may require specialized trauma therapy

Key Takeaways

  • The autonomic nervous system has two main branches: sympathetic (fight or flight) and parasympathetic (rest and digest)
  • The vagus nerve is the primary parasympathetic pathway, connecting brain to heart, lungs, and gut
  • Vagal tone (measured by HRV) is a key marker of stress resilience
  • Polyvagal Theory identifies three states: ventral vagal (safe), sympathetic (fight/flight), and dorsal vagal (freeze/collapse)
  • The Window of Tolerance describes the zone where we can manage experiences; chronic stress narrows this window
  • Nervous system regulation is influenced by early life, current stress, sleep, nutrition, movement, connection, and gut health
  • Nervous system dysregulation is not a willpower problem—it requires physiological intervention
  • Coaching can support regulation through education, modeling, and teaching practical techniques

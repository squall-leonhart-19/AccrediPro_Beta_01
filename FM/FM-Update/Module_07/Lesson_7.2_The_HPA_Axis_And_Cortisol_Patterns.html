The HPA Axis and Cortisol Patterns

Reading Time: 12 minutes

Connecting to Your Journey

In our previous lesson, we introduced the stress response and the HPA axis. Now we'll go deeper into understanding how cortisol is supposed to work, what a healthy rhythm looks like, and the stages of HPA axis dysfunction that develop when stress becomes chronic. This knowledge is essential because "adrenal fatigue"—while not an official medical diagnosis—describes real patterns of dysfunction that many of your clients will experience.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the healthy cortisol rhythm and what it does for the body
  • Learn how chronic stress disrupts cortisol patterns
  • Explore the stages of HPA axis dysfunction
  • Recognize symptoms associated with different cortisol patterns
  • Understand testing options for assessing cortisol

Two Clients, Two Patterns

Linda, 38: "I'm exhausted when I wake up and can barely drag myself out of bed. Coffee doesn't help anymore. But by 10 PM, I'm suddenly wide awake, and my mind won't stop racing."

Margaret, 54: "I'm anxious all the time. My heart races for no reason. I can't sleep, I'm gaining weight around my middle, and I feel like I'm running on adrenaline constantly. I'm wired but tired."

Both Linda and Margaret are experiencing stress-related dysfunction, but their patterns are different. Linda's cortisol rhythm is reversed—low when it should be high, high when it should be low. Margaret is in a chronically elevated state. Understanding these patterns helps identify where each client is on the stress continuum and what support they need.

The Healthy Cortisol Rhythm

Cortisol follows a circadian rhythm—a predictable pattern tied to the 24-hour light-dark cycle. This rhythm is crucial for energy, metabolism, immune function, and overall well-being.

The Healthy Cortisol Curve

6-8 AM
Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)
Cortisol surges 50-60% within 30-45 minutes of waking. This "get up and go" hormone helps you feel alert, focused, and ready for the day.
AM Peak
Morning High
Cortisol is at its highest in the morning, providing energy, mental clarity, and motivation. This supports productivity and engagement.
Midday
Gradual Decline
Cortisol gradually decreases throughout the day. The afternoon dip (often felt around 2-3 PM) is normal—the body beginning its wind-down.
Evening
Low Point
Cortisol reaches its lowest point in the evening, allowing melatonin to rise and prepare the body for sleep. This is essential for restorative rest.

Key insight: The ratio between morning and evening cortisol matters as much as absolute levels. A healthy curve has high morning cortisol (to get going) and low evening cortisol (to wind down). When this ratio flattens or inverts, problems arise.

When the Rhythm Breaks Down

Chronic stress doesn't just raise cortisol—it disrupts the rhythm. Over time, the HPA axis can become dysregulated in several ways:

Pattern 1: Chronically High Cortisol

Stage: Early stress response / "Wired" phase

What's happening: The HPA axis is chronically activated. The body is stuck in "fight or flight" mode, producing excess cortisol around the clock.

Symptoms:

  • Anxiety, feeling "wired" or keyed up
  • Difficulty falling asleep despite exhaustion
  • Racing thoughts, especially at night
  • Weight gain, particularly abdominal
  • High blood pressure, elevated blood sugar
  • Increased appetite, carbohydrate cravings
  • Frequent illness (immune suppression)

Pattern 2: Blunted Cortisol Rhythm

Stage: Transitional / "Wired but tired"

What's happening: The cortisol curve is flattening. Morning cortisol isn't rising properly, evening cortisol isn't falling properly. The healthy rhythm is eroding.

Symptoms:

  • Difficulty waking; needing coffee or stimulants
  • Energy slumps during the day
  • "Second wind" at night (cortisol not dropping)
  • Disrupted sleep (waking between 2-4 AM)
  • Feeling both exhausted and anxious
  • Increased reliance on caffeine and sugar

Pattern 3: Low Cortisol Throughout

Stage: Later stage / "Burned out"

What's happening: The HPA axis is now under-responsive. After prolonged activation, the system has downregulated. Cortisol production is inadequate for normal demands.

Symptoms:

  • Profound fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
  • Difficulty getting out of bed
  • Crashes after any exertion or stress
  • Feeling overwhelmed by minor stressors
  • Brain fog, poor memory
  • Low blood pressure, dizziness upon standing
  • Salt or sugar cravings
  • Increased inflammation (cortisol is anti-inflammatory)

Understanding "Adrenal Fatigue"

The term "adrenal fatigue" was coined by naturopath James Wilson in 1998 to describe a collection of symptoms associated with chronic stress. While this term is not recognized as an official medical diagnosis, it describes a real phenomenon that functional medicine practitioners call HPA axis dysfunction.

The Terminology Debate

Conventional medicine rejects "adrenal fatigue" because the adrenal glands themselves don't get "tired"—they can produce cortisol. The dysfunction is in the signaling system (HPA axis) that regulates cortisol release.

However, the symptoms are real and the patterns are measurable. Whether we call it "adrenal fatigue," "HPA axis dysfunction," or "cortisol dysregulation," clients suffering from these patterns need support.

Bottom line: Use "HPA axis dysfunction" for clinical accuracy, but recognize that clients may relate better to "adrenal fatigue" as a concept.

The Stages of HPA Axis Dysfunction

Stage 1: Alarm Phase

Cortisol: Elevated

What's happening: The body is responding to increased demands. Cortisol production ramps up to meet the stress. Energy may actually feel high—this is the "high performer" phase.

Duration: Can last months to years, depending on stress severity and individual resilience.

Stage 2: Resistance Phase

Cortisol: Variable—may be high, normal, or starting to decline; rhythm often disrupted

What's happening: The body is adapting to chronic stress. Cortisol may still be high at times but the rhythm is degrading. Other hormones (DHEA, sex hormones) may start declining as resources are diverted to cortisol production.

Signs: Fatigue that's managed with caffeine, weight changes, sleep disruption, getting sick more often.

Stage 3: Exhaustion Phase

Cortisol: Low, especially in the morning; flat rhythm

What's happening: The HPA axis is now downregulated. After prolonged demand, the system has adapted by reducing output. The body can no longer mount an adequate stress response.

Signs: Severe fatigue, inability to handle any stress, crashes from minor exertion, brain fog, inflammation, and a sense of being "broken."

Other Hormones Affected by HPA Dysfunction

Chronic stress doesn't only affect cortisol. The entire hormonal system is interconnected:

DHEA (Dehydroepiandrosterone)

DHEA is another adrenal hormone that acts as a precursor to sex hormones. It has anti-aging, immune-supporting, and mood-enhancing effects. Under chronic stress, DHEA often declines as the body prioritizes cortisol production. The cortisol-to-DHEA ratio is a useful marker of stress adaptation—a high ratio indicates stress dominance.

Thyroid Hormones

Chronic stress suppresses thyroid function at multiple levels. Cortisol inhibits TSH production and conversion of T4 to active T3, while promoting conversion to reverse T3 (inactive). This creates functional hypothyroidism even when labs look "normal."

Sex Hormones

The "pregnenolone steal" theory suggests that under stress, the body diverts pregnenolone (the "mother hormone") toward cortisol production rather than sex hormone production. This can contribute to estrogen-progesterone imbalances, low testosterone, and worsened perimenopausal symptoms.

Melatonin

Cortisol and melatonin are supposed to have an inverse relationship—cortisol high in morning, melatonin high at night. When evening cortisol is elevated, it suppresses melatonin, disrupting sleep and the body's circadian rhythm.

Testing Cortisol and the HPA Axis

Several testing options exist to assess cortisol patterns:

Cortisol Testing Methods

Salivary Cortisol (4-Point Test)

The gold standard for assessing the cortisol rhythm. Saliva samples are collected at 4 points: waking, late morning, afternoon, and evening. This maps the curve and identifies where dysfunction lies.

Pros: Non-invasive, measures free (active) cortisol, captures the rhythm

Cons: Requires careful sample collection; results can vary by lab

Dried Urine Testing (DUTCH Test)

The DUTCH (Dried Urine Test for Comprehensive Hormones) measures cortisol metabolites and free cortisol throughout the day. It also includes DHEA, melatonin, and sex hormone metabolites.

Pros: Comprehensive hormone picture; includes metabolites that show how cortisol is being processed

Cons: More expensive; requires interpretation expertise

Blood Cortisol (Serum)

Standard blood test measuring total cortisol at a single point in time (usually morning). Used in conventional medicine primarily to rule out Addison's disease or Cushing's syndrome.

Pros: Widely available; insurance often covers

Cons: Only a snapshot; doesn't capture rhythm; measures total (not free) cortisol

Hair Cortisol

Measures cortisol accumulated in hair over 1-3 months. Provides an average of long-term cortisol exposure.

Pros: Reflects chronic cortisol levels

Cons: Doesn't show rhythm; less commonly used

Reading a Salivary Cortisol Report

When reviewing a 4-point cortisol test, look for:

  • Morning cortisol: Should be the highest point—if it's low, there's insufficient "get up and go"
  • Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR): Should show a 50-60% rise within 30-45 minutes of waking
  • Slope of decline: Should gradually decrease throughout the day
  • Evening cortisol: Should be the lowest point—if it's elevated, sleep will be compromised
  • Overall curve shape: Healthy is steep drop from AM to PM; flat curve indicates dysfunction

Recognizing Patterns in Your Clients

While testing provides objective data, many patterns can be recognized through careful history-taking:

Questions That Reveal Cortisol Patterns

"How do you feel when you wake up?"

High morning cortisol: Alert and ready • Low morning cortisol: Groggy, need caffeine to function

"How is your energy throughout the day?"

Healthy: Steady energy that peaks mid-morning • Dysregulated: Crashes, needing caffeine/sugar, or erratic energy

"What happens when you try to go to sleep?"

High evening cortisol: Racing mind, can't wind down • Healthy: Naturally sleepy by 9-10 PM

"Do you wake during the night?"

Waking 2-4 AM often indicates blood sugar drops triggering cortisol spikes

"What happens after you exercise or experience stress?"

Healthy: Recover normally • Depleted: Crash for hours or days after exertion

"Do you crave salt or sugar?"

Salt cravings can indicate low aldosterone (another adrenal hormone); sugar cravings often indicate blood sugar dysregulation from cortisol issues

Key Takeaways

  • Healthy cortisol follows a circadian rhythm—high in the morning, low in the evening
  • The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is a 50-60% surge within 30-45 minutes of waking
  • Chronic stress disrupts this rhythm, leading to various dysfunction patterns
  • HPA axis dysfunction progresses through stages: alarm (high cortisol), resistance (variable), and exhaustion (low cortisol)
  • "Adrenal fatigue" describes real patterns of HPA axis dysfunction, even though it's not an official diagnosis
  • DHEA, thyroid hormones, sex hormones, and melatonin are all affected by cortisol dysregulation
  • Salivary cortisol testing (4-point) and DUTCH testing are valuable for assessing patterns
  • Careful history-taking can reveal cortisol patterns even without testing

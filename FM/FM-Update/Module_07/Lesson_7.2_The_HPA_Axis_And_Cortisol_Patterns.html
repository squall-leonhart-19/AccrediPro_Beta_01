The Healthy Cortisol Rhythm

Cortisol follows a circadian rhythm‚Äîa predictable pattern tied to the 24-hour light-dark cycle. This rhythm is crucial for energy, metabolism, immune function, and overall well-being.

The Healthy Cortisol Curve

‚è∞ 6-8 AM: Cortisol Awakening Response

Cortisol surges 50-60% within 30-45 minutes of waking. This "get up and go" hormone helps you feel alert, focused, and ready for the day.

üåÖ Morning Peak

Cortisol is at its highest in the morning, providing energy, mental clarity, and motivation. This supports productivity and engagement.

‚òÄÔ∏è Midday: Gradual Decline

Cortisol gradually decreases throughout the day. The afternoon dip (often felt around 2-3 PM) is normal‚Äîthe body beginning its wind-down.

üåô Evening: Low Point

Cortisol reaches its lowest point in the evening, allowing melatonin to rise and prepare the body for sleep. This is essential for restorative rest.

Key Insight

The ratio between morning and evening cortisol matters as much as absolute levels. A healthy curve has high morning cortisol (to get going) and low evening cortisol (to wind down). When this ratio flattens or inverts, problems arise.

Coach Tip

When explaining cortisol rhythm to clients, use this analogy: "Think of cortisol like the volume control on your day. Morning is meant to be loud and energizing‚Äîlike your favorite upbeat playlist. Evening should be quiet and calming‚Äîlike a lullaby. When the volume is reversed or stuck on one setting all day, everything feels off."

When The Rhythm Breaks Down

Chronic stress doesn't just raise cortisol‚Äîit disrupts the rhythm. Over time, the HPA axis can become dysregulated in several ways:

1

Chronically High Cortisol

Stage: Early stress response / "Wired" phase

What's happening: The HPA axis is chronically activated. The body is stuck in "fight or flight" mode, producing excess cortisol around the clock.

Symptoms: Anxiety, feeling "wired" or keyed up ‚Ä¢ Difficulty falling asleep despite exhaustion ‚Ä¢ Racing thoughts, especially at night ‚Ä¢ Weight gain, particularly abdominal ‚Ä¢ High blood pressure, elevated blood sugar ‚Ä¢ Increased appetite, carbohydrate cravings ‚Ä¢ Frequent illness (immune suppression)

2

Blunted Cortisol Rhythm

Stage: Transitional / "Wired but tired"

What's happening: The cortisol curve is flattening. Morning cortisol isn't rising properly, evening cortisol isn't falling properly. The healthy rhythm is eroding.

Symptoms: Difficulty waking; needing coffee or stimulants ‚Ä¢ Energy slumps during the day ‚Ä¢ "Second wind" at night (cortisol not dropping) ‚Ä¢ Disrupted sleep (waking between 2-4 AM) ‚Ä¢ Feeling both exhausted and anxious ‚Ä¢ Increased reliance on caffeine and sugar

3

Low Cortisol Throughout

Stage: Later stage / "Burned out"

What's happening: The HPA axis is now under-responsive. After prolonged activation, the system has downregulated. Cortisol production is inadequate for normal demands.

Symptoms: Profound fatigue that doesn't improve with rest ‚Ä¢ Difficulty getting out of bed ‚Ä¢ Crashes after any exertion or stress ‚Ä¢ Feeling overwhelmed by minor stressors ‚Ä¢ Brain fog, poor memory ‚Ä¢ Low blood pressure, dizziness upon standing ‚Ä¢ Salt or sugar cravings ‚Ä¢ Increased inflammation (cortisol is anti-inflammatory)

Coach Tip

Help clients identify their pattern by asking: "On a scale of 1-10, how wired do you feel? How tired? If you're high on both‚Äîthat's 'wired but tired.' If you're low on both‚Äîthat's burnout. If you're high wired, low tired‚Äîthat's the alarm phase." This simple assessment helps clients understand where they are on the stress continuum.

Understanding "Adrenal Fatigue"

The term "adrenal fatigue" was coined by naturopath James Wilson in 1998 to describe a collection of symptoms associated with chronic stress. While this term is not recognized as an official medical diagnosis, it describes a real phenomenon that functional medicine practitioners call HPA axis dysfunction.

The Terminology Debate

Conventional medicine rejects "adrenal fatigue" because the adrenal glands themselves don't get "tired"‚Äîthey can produce cortisol. The dysfunction is in the signaling system (HPA axis) that regulates cortisol release.

However, the symptoms are real and the patterns are measurable. Whether we call it "adrenal fatigue," "HPA axis dysfunction," or "cortisol dysregulation," clients suffering from these patterns need support.

Bottom line: Use "HPA axis dysfunction" for clinical accuracy, but recognize that clients may relate better to "adrenal fatigue" as a concept.

The Stages of HPA Axis Dysfunction

The Three Stages

Stage 1: Alarm Phase

Cortisol: Elevated

What's happening: The body is responding to increased demands. Cortisol production ramps up to meet the stress. Energy may actually feel high‚Äîthis is the "high performer" phase.

Duration: Can last months to years, depending on stress severity and individual resilience.

Stage 2: Resistance Phase

Cortisol: Variable‚Äîmay be high, normal, or starting to decline; rhythm often disrupted

What's happening: The body is adapting to chronic stress. Cortisol may still be high at times but the rhythm is degrading. Other hormones (DHEA, sex hormones) may start declining as resources are diverted to cortisol production.

Signs: Fatigue that's managed with caffeine, weight changes, sleep disruption, getting sick more often.

Stage 3: Exhaustion Phase

Cortisol: Low, especially in the morning; flat rhythm

What's happening: The HPA axis is now downregulated. After prolonged demand, the system has adapted by reducing output. The body can no longer mount an adequate stress response.

Signs: Severe fatigue, inability to handle any stress, crashes from minor exertion, brain fog, inflammation, and a sense of being "broken."

Other Hormones Affected by HPA Dysfunction

Chronic stress doesn't only affect cortisol. The entire hormonal system is interconnected:

The Hormonal Cascade

DHEA (Dehydroepiandrosterone)

DHEA is another adrenal hormone that acts as a precursor to sex hormones. It has anti-aging, immune-supporting, and mood-enhancing effects. Under chronic stress, DHEA often declines as the body prioritizes cortisol production. The cortisol-to-DHEA ratio is a useful marker of stress adaptation‚Äîa high ratio indicates stress dominance.

Thyroid Hormones

Chronic stress suppresses thyroid function at multiple levels. Cortisol inhibits TSH production and conversion of T4 to active T3, while promoting conversion to reverse T3 (inactive). This creates functional hypothyroidism even when labs look "normal."

Sex Hormones

The "pregnenolone steal" theory suggests that under stress, the body diverts pregnenolone (the "mother hormone") toward cortisol production rather than sex hormone production. This can contribute to estrogen-progesterone imbalances, low testosterone, and worsened perimenopausal symptoms.

Melatonin

Cortisol and melatonin are supposed to have an inverse relationship‚Äîcortisol high in morning, melatonin high at night. When evening cortisol is elevated, it suppresses melatonin, disrupting sleep and the body's circadian rhythm.

Coach Tip

When clients present with multiple hormonal issues‚Äîthyroid problems, menstrual irregularities, low libido, sleep disruption‚Äîlook upstream to stress and cortisol patterns. Often, addressing HPA axis dysfunction creates a cascade of improvements across all hormone systems. Help clients understand: "Your hormones are like an orchestra. When cortisol (the conductor) is out of rhythm, every other instrument struggles to play in tune."

Testing Cortisol and the HPA Axis

Several testing options exist to assess cortisol patterns:

Cortisol Testing Methods

üíß Salivary Cortisol (4-Point Test)

The gold standard for assessing the cortisol rhythm. Saliva samples are collected at 4 points: waking, late morning, afternoon, and evening. This maps the curve and identifies where dysfunction lies.

Pros: Non-invasive, measures free (active) cortisol, captures the rhythm

Cons: Requires careful sample collection; results can vary by lab

üìã Dried Urine Testing (DUTCH Test)

The DUTCH (Dried Urine Test for Comprehensive Hormones) measures cortisol metabolites and free cortisol throughout the day. It also includes DHEA, melatonin, and sex hormone metabolites.

Pros: Comprehensive hormone picture; includes metabolites that show how cortisol is being processed

Cons: More expensive; requires interpretation expertise

ü©∏ Blood Cortisol (Serum)

Standard blood test measuring total cortisol at a single point in time (usually morning). Used in conventional medicine primarily to rule out Addison's disease or Cushing's syndrome.

Pros: Widely available; insurance often covers

Cons: Only a snapshot; doesn't capture rhythm; measures total (not free) cortisol

‚úÇÔ∏è Hair Cortisol

Measures cortisol accumulated in hair over 1-3 months. Provides an average of long-term cortisol exposure.

Pros: Reflects chronic cortisol levels

Cons: Doesn't show rhythm; less commonly used

Reading a Salivary Cortisol Report

When reviewing a 4-point cortisol test, look for:

  • Morning cortisol: Should be the highest point‚Äîif it's low, there's insufficient "get up and go"
  • Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR): Should show a 50-60% rise within 30-45 minutes of waking
  • Slope of decline: Should gradually decrease throughout the day
  • Evening cortisol: Should be the lowest point‚Äîif it's elevated, sleep will be compromised
  • Overall curve shape: Healthy is steep drop from AM to PM; flat curve indicates dysfunction

Recognizing Patterns in Your Clients

While testing provides objective data, many patterns can be recognized through careful history-taking:

Questions That Reveal Cortisol Patterns

"How do you feel when you wake up?"

High morning cortisol: Alert and ready

Low morning cortisol: Groggy, need caffeine to function

"How is your energy throughout the day?"

Healthy: Steady energy that peaks mid-morning

Dysregulated: Crashes, needing caffeine/sugar, or erratic energy

"What happens when you try to go to sleep?"

High evening cortisol: Racing mind, can't wind down

Healthy: Naturally sleepy by 9-10 PM

"Do you wake during the night?"

Waking 2-4 AM often indicates blood sugar drops triggering cortisol spikes

"What happens after you exercise or experience stress?"

Healthy: Recover normally

Depleted: Crash for hours or days after exertion

"Do you crave salt or sugar?"

Salt cravings can indicate low aldosterone (another adrenal hormone); sugar cravings often indicate blood sugar dysregulation from cortisol issues

Coach Tip

Create a simple tracking tool for clients to map their own cortisol patterns. Have them rate energy, mood, stress level, and cravings at four time points daily (waking, late morning, afternoon, evening) for one week. This simple exercise often reveals clear patterns and helps clients understand their body's signals. You can say: "Before we even do testing, let's see what your body is already telling us. Your patterns throughout the day are teaching us exactly what we need to know."

Test Your Knowledge
1

What is the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR), and why is it important?

Answer: The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is a 50-60% surge in cortisol that occurs within 30-45 minutes of waking. It's important because it provides the "get up and go" energy needed to start the day with alertness, focus, and motivation. A blunted CAR is associated with difficulty waking, morning fatigue, and reliance on caffeine.

2

Describe the three patterns of cortisol dysregulation and their associated symptoms.

Answer: The three patterns are:

(1) Chronically High Cortisol: "Wired" phase with anxiety, racing thoughts, sleep difficulty, abdominal weight gain, high blood pressure, and immune suppression.

(2) Blunted Cortisol Rhythm: "Wired but tired" with difficulty waking, energy crashes, second wind at night, disrupted sleep (waking 2-4 AM), and reliance on caffeine/sugar.

(3) Low Cortisol Throughout: "Burned out" phase with profound fatigue, crashes after exertion, overwhelm from minor stressors, brain fog, low blood pressure, and salt/sugar cravings.

3

Why is "adrenal fatigue" controversial, and what term do functional medicine practitioners prefer?

Answer: "Adrenal fatigue" is controversial because it's not recognized as an official medical diagnosis, and the adrenal glands themselves don't get "tired"‚Äîthey remain capable of producing cortisol. The dysfunction lies in the signaling system (the HPA axis) that regulates cortisol release. Functional medicine practitioners prefer the term "HPA axis dysfunction" or "cortisol dysregulation" for clinical accuracy, though they recognize that clients may relate better to the concept of "adrenal fatigue."

4

What is the "pregnenolone steal" and how does chronic stress affect sex hormones?

Answer: The "pregnenolone steal" theory suggests that under chronic stress, the body diverts pregnenolone (the "mother hormone" from which other hormones are made) toward cortisol production rather than sex hormone production. This can lead to estrogen-progesterone imbalances, low testosterone, and worsened perimenopausal/menopausal symptoms. Essentially, the body prioritizes survival (cortisol for stress response) over reproduction (sex hormones).

5

What is the gold standard test for assessing cortisol rhythm, and what should you look for when reviewing results?

Answer: The gold standard is the 4-point salivary cortisol test, which measures cortisol at waking, late morning, afternoon, and evening. When reviewing results, look for: (1) High morning cortisol (if low, insufficient energy), (2) 50-60% CAR surge, (3) Gradual decline throughout the day, (4) Low evening cortisol (if elevated, sleep will be compromised), and (5) Overall curve shape‚Äîa healthy steep drop from AM to PM (a flat curve indicates dysfunction).

6

As a health coach, what role do you play in supporting clients with suspected HPA axis dysfunction?

Answer: As a health coach, you: (1) Recognize patterns through careful history-taking, (2) Educate clients about what's happening in their body, (3) Support lifestyle changes like stress management and sleep hygiene, (4) Recommend appropriate testing through functional medicine practitioners, and (5) Collaborate with practitioners who can order tests and prescribe supplements or medications. Your role is assessment, education, and lifestyle support‚Äînot diagnosis or treatment.

Key Takeaways
  • Healthy cortisol follows a circadian rhythm‚Äîhigh in the morning, low in the evening
  • The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is a 50-60% surge within 30-45 minutes of waking
  • Chronic stress disrupts this rhythm, leading to three distinct dysfunction patterns: chronically high, blunted rhythm, and chronically low
  • HPA axis dysfunction progresses through stages: alarm (high cortisol), resistance (variable), and exhaustion (low cortisol)
  • "Adrenal fatigue" describes real patterns of HPA axis dysfunction, even though it's not an official diagnosis
  • DHEA, thyroid hormones, sex hormones, and melatonin are all affected by cortisol dysregulation
  • Salivary cortisol testing (4-point) and DUTCH testing are valuable for assessing patterns
  • Careful history-taking can reveal cortisol patterns even without testing

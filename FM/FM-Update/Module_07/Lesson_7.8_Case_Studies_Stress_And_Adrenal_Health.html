Case Study 1: The High-Achiever in Alarm Phase

Chief Complaints

Anxiety ‚Ä¢ Racing heart ‚Ä¢ Insomnia (can't fall asleep) ‚Ä¢ Weight gain around midsection ‚Ä¢ Frequent illness ‚Ä¢ Feeling "wired"

History: Always been a high performer, never needed much sleep. Symptoms gradually worsening over 2 years since making partner.

"I thrive on pressure. I don't need to relax‚ÄîI need to perform better."

Assessment Findings

Test Results

4-Point Cortisol: Elevated across all time points, highest in evening (should be lowest)
DHEA: Normal but trending down
HRV: Very low, indicating sympathetic dominance
Pattern: Stage 1 HPA dysfunction‚Äîalarm phase with chronic sympathetic activation

Intervention Strategy

Priority 1: Sleep Foundation

üåô Wind-Down Routine

Strict wind-down routine: screens off by 9 PM ‚Ä¢ Bedroom dark, cool, and device-free ‚Ä¢ 4-7-8 breathing before bed

üíä Sleep Support

Phosphatidylserine (300mg) evening to lower cortisol ‚Ä¢ Magnesium glycinate (400mg) before bed

Priority 2: Nervous System Regulation

  • 5-minute breathing practice morning and before bed (non-negotiable)
  • Physiological sighs throughout day when noticing tension
  • Walking meetings instead of sitting
  • Brief vagal reset between meetings (cold water on face, humming)

Priority 3: Nutrition and Caffeine

  • Gradual caffeine reduction (was 5+ cups daily)
  • No caffeine after noon
  • Protein at every meal for blood sugar stability
  • Ashwagandha (600mg) for cortisol modulation

Coaching Role

Working with Amanda required meeting her high-achiever mindset where it lived. Rather than framing stress management as self-care (which she viewed as weakness), we positioned it as performance optimization.

  • Reframed stress management not as "weakness" but as performance optimization
  • Used her achiever mindset: HRV tracking became a "metric to improve"
  • Helped her see that her current approach was unsustainable‚Äîburning out would end her career faster than taking breaks
  • Identified tiny time pockets for practices (she has none? We found them)
  • Accountability for non-negotiables; celebrated compliance as "wins"
Coach Tip

Reframe for high-achievers: Instead of "You need to rest," try "Your recovery metrics are as important as your performance metrics. Elite athletes prioritize recovery‚Äîso should elite professionals." Use their competitive nature to drive positive change.

Outcome

Week 4
  • Falling asleep easier
  • Caffeine down to 2 cups
Week 8
  • HRV improving
  • Anxiety episodes less frequent

Week 16: Evening cortisol normalizing; lost 8 pounds without dieting; feeling "clearheaded"

Week 24: Repeat testing showed normalized cortisol pattern; maintained high performance with less effort

Key Lesson

Meet high-achievers where they are. Frame interventions as performance enhancement, not self-care. Start with sleep‚Äîit's the foundation. Small practices done consistently beat ambitious plans abandoned.

Case Study 2: The Caregiver in Exhaustion Phase

üìã

Margaret, Age 54

Part-Time Teacher, Primary Caregiver

üë©‚Äçüè´

Chief Complaints

Profound fatigue that doesn't improve with rest ‚Ä¢ Crashes after any exertion ‚Ä¢ Brain fog ‚Ä¢ Salt cravings ‚Ä¢ Feeling cold ‚Ä¢ Dizziness when standing ‚Ä¢ "No reserves left"

History: 4 years of intensive caregiving for husband with chronic illness. Initially pushed through; now barely functioning.

"I used to be able to handle anything. Now even small things overwhelm me."

Assessment Findings

Test Results

4-Point Cortisol: Flat line‚Äîlow across all time points, barely rising in morning
DHEA: Very low
Cortisol/DHEA Ratio: Indicating prolonged stress adaptation
Blood pressure: Low (100/65)
Pattern: Stage 3 HPA dysfunction‚Äîexhaustion phase

Intervention Strategy

Priority 1: Rest and Pacing

  • Non-negotiable daily rest period (even 20 minutes lying down)
  • Energy budgeting: treating energy as a limited resource
  • Saying no to non-essential commitments
  • Recruited help for caregiving (this was critical)
  • In bed by 10 PM; aim for 9+ hours sleep during recovery

Priority 2: Adrenal Support Protocol

  • Licorice root (for 4 weeks, monitoring blood pressure)
  • Rhodiola (morning) for energy support
  • Vitamin C (2g/day), B-complex, magnesium
  • Salt added liberally to food (addressed salt cravings)
  • DHEA supplementation under practitioner supervision
Critical: Movement Restriction

Priority 3: Gentle Movement Only
NO vigorous exercise during recovery (makes exhaustion worse) ‚Ä¢ Short walks only‚Äî10-15 minutes max ‚Ä¢ Restorative yoga if she had energy ‚Ä¢ Pay attention to post-exertion response; if crashing, do less

Coach Tip

Working with caregivers: They often struggle with guilt about receiving help. Use this reframe: "You're not being selfish‚Äîyou're protecting your ability to continue caregiving long-term. You can't pour from an empty cup." Help them identify and recruit specific support resources.

Coaching Role

Margaret's case required deep work on permission and boundaries. Caregivers are notoriously resistant to receiving care themselves.

  • Helped Margaret accept that rest is medicine, not laziness
  • Worked on guilt about receiving help (caregivers often resist this)
  • Created permission to put her oxygen mask on first
  • Helped identify and recruit support resources (family, community, professional)
  • Set realistic expectations‚Äîthis would take months, not weeks
  • Celebrated tiny improvements; prevented discouragement

Outcome

Week 4: Slightly more energy with rest; still crashing but recovering faster

Week 8: Morning energy improving; fewer dizzy spells

Week 16: Able to do more without crashing; brain fog lifting

Week 24: Significant improvement; repeat testing showed morning cortisol improving

Week 52: Functional and stable; caregiving situation also improved (got more help)

Key Lesson

Stage 3 exhaustion requires rest, not pushing. Exercise makes things worse in this phase. Caregivers need permission to receive care themselves. Recovery takes time‚Äî12+ months is common for severe depletion. Address the situation, not just the symptoms.

Case Study 3: The Perimenopausal Stress Storm

üìã

Karen, Age 48

Marketing Director, Mother of Two Teenagers

üë©‚Äçüíª

Chief Complaints

Hot flashes ‚Ä¢ Night sweats ‚Ä¢ Insomnia (wakes 2-4 AM) ‚Ä¢ Anxiety ‚Ä¢ Irritability ‚Ä¢ 15-pound weight gain ‚Ä¢ Irregular periods ‚Ä¢ Brain fog

History: Symptoms started 2 years ago alongside a divorce and job change. Doctor says "perimenopause" and offered HRT.

"I don't know if this is hormones or stress or both. I feel like I'm losing myself."

Assessment Findings

Test Results

4-Point Cortisol: Elevated at night (2 AM sample very high), morning lower than it should be
Sex Hormones: Low progesterone, fluctuating estrogen (typical perimenopause)
Thyroid: TSH normal, but Free T3 low-normal, Reverse T3 elevated
Pattern: Stage 2 HPA dysfunction with reversed cortisol rhythm, compounded by hormonal transition

Intervention Strategy

Priority 1: Address Night Cortisol and Sleep

  • Phosphatidylserine (300mg) before bed to lower night cortisol
  • Magnesium glycinate (400mg) for sleep and hot flashes
  • Blood sugar-stabilizing bedtime snack (prevents 2 AM cortisol spike)
  • Cool bedroom, moisture-wicking bedding (for hot flashes)
  • 4-7-8 breathing when waking at night

Priority 2: Stress and HPA Support

  • Ashwagandha (600mg) for cortisol modulation and thyroid support
  • Morning bright light exposure (helps reset cortisol rhythm)
  • Daily stress practice: morning meditation or breathwork (10 minutes)
  • Walking in nature (combines movement, nature, and stress relief)

Priority 3: Hormonal Support

  • Maca (2g daily) for hormonal balance and hot flashes
  • Ground flaxseed (2 tbsp daily) for gentle phytoestrogen support
  • Cruciferous vegetables for estrogen metabolism
  • Discussed bioidentical hormone therapy options with her doctor
Coach Tip

The 2-4 AM wake-up pattern: This is almost always related to night cortisol spikes. When clients describe waking consistently at this time, prioritize blood sugar stability (bedtime snack with protein and healthy fat) and phosphatidylserine supplementation.

Coaching Role

Karen needed validation that her symptoms were real and interconnected‚Äînot separate problems requiring separate solutions.

  • Validated that her symptoms were real‚Äînot "just stress" or "just hormones"
  • Helped her see the stress-hormone interconnection
  • Created a morning and evening routine to support circadian rhythm
  • Supported emotional processing of major life transitions (divorce, aging)
  • Helped her reconnect with things that brought joy and meaning
  • Coordinated with her functional medicine doctor and gynecologist

Outcome

Week 4: Sleep improving; waking less often at night

Week 8: Hot flashes reduced by 50%; mood stabilizing

Week 12: Anxiety significantly better; brain fog clearing

Week 24: Lost 10 pounds; energy returned; decided not to start HRT (symptoms manageable)

Key Lesson

Perimenopause + stress = amplified symptoms. Addressing stress can significantly improve hormonal symptoms. Night cortisol is often the culprit for 2-4 AM waking. Don't dismiss major life transitions‚Äîthey matter.

Case Study 4: The Trauma-Informed Approach

üìã

Susan, Age 45

Social Worker

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è

Chief Complaints

Hypervigilance ‚Ä¢ Startles easily ‚Ä¢ Can't relax even in safe situations ‚Ä¢ Chronic tension ‚Ä¢ Digestive issues ‚Ä¢ Difficulty with intimacy

History: Childhood trauma; has done therapy. Physically healthy but nervous system "stuck"

"I know I'm safe intellectually, but my body doesn't believe it."

Assessment Findings

Test Results

HRV: Very low, indicating chronic sympathetic dominance
Cortisol: Variable‚Äîsometimes high, sometimes blunted (dysregulated pattern)
Polyvagal Perspective: Nervous system oscillating between sympathetic (hypervigilance) and dorsal vagal (shutdown)
Pattern: Trauma-related nervous system dysregulation‚Äînarrow window of tolerance

Intervention Strategy

Priority 1: Safety and Grounding

  • Create environmental safety cues (lighting, sounds, organization)
  • Grounding practices: feet on floor, 5-4-3-2-1 technique
  • Orienting exercises (looking around the room, noticing safety)
  • Gentle, predictable session structure

Priority 2: Vagal Toning (Gentle Approaches)

  • Extended exhale breathing (not aggressive techniques that feel overwhelming)
  • Humming, singing, vocal toning
  • Cold water on face (brief, gentle)
  • Self-havening or butterfly hug for self-soothing
  • Building in tiny increments‚Äîstaying within window of tolerance

Priority 3: Co-Regulation

  • Regular sessions with coach providing calm, regulated presence
  • Encouraged safe social connections
  • Pet therapy (her dog became a co-regulation resource)
  • Referred to somatic experiencing therapist for deeper trauma work
Coach Tip

Trauma-informed coaching: Never push relaxation on trauma survivors‚Äîit can feel threatening. Instead, focus on safety and predictability first. Use gentle, bottom-up approaches. Your own regulated presence is one of the most powerful interventions you can offer. Move slowly and follow the client's lead.

Coaching Role

Working with Susan required a fundamentally different approach‚Äîone rooted in trauma-informed principles and deep respect for her nervous system's protective mechanisms.

  • Trauma-informed approach: slow, gentle, client-led pacing
  • Modeled regulation through own calm presence
  • Did NOT push relaxation (can feel threatening to a traumatized nervous system)
  • Built trust through consistency and predictability
  • Celebrated micro-moments of calm as huge wins
  • Collaborated with her therapist, staying in coaching scope

Outcome

Week 4: Beginning to notice moments of calm; less startle response

Week 12: HRV slowly improving; able to use grounding in real life

Week 24: Window of tolerance widening; sleeping better

Week 52: Significant improvement; still practices daily; body beginning to believe in safety

Key Lesson

Trauma-related dysregulation requires a slow, bottom-up approach. Meet the nervous system where it is‚Äîdon't push relaxation. Co-regulation is powerful. Work within scope and collaborate with trauma therapists. Progress may be slow, but change is possible.

Key Coaching Principles from These Cases

1

Match Intervention to Stage

Alarm phase needs calming; exhaustion phase needs rest and rebuilding. Don't apply one-size-fits-all approaches.

2

Sleep Is Foundational

Almost every case improves when sleep is addressed first. Prioritize sleep quality and circadian rhythm support.

3

Meet Clients Where They Are

High-achievers need performance framing; caregivers need permission; trauma survivors need safety first.

4

Address the Situation

Caregiving burden, life transitions, and ongoing stressors must be addressed‚Äînot just symptoms.

5

Stress-Hormone Connection

Perimenopausal symptoms often improve significantly with stress management. They're interconnected systems.

6

Realistic Timelines

Severe depletion takes 12-24 months. Prevent discouragement by preparing clients with realistic expectations.

7

Collaborate Across Disciplines

Work with FM practitioners, doctors, and therapists as needed. You're part of a care team.

1

What was the key intervention that made the difference for Amanda, the high-achieving attorney in alarm phase?

Answer: The key was reframing stress management as performance optimization rather than self-care, and prioritizing sleep foundation first. Meeting her where she was‚Äîas a high-achiever‚Äîallowed her to engage with interventions she might otherwise have dismissed as "weakness."

2

Why was exercise restricted for Margaret, the caregiver in exhaustion phase?

Answer: In Stage 3 HPA exhaustion, vigorous exercise makes things worse because the body lacks the reserves to recover from the additional stress. Rest and gentle movement only (short walks, restorative yoga) are appropriate during the recovery phase.

3

What pattern indicated that Karen's perimenopausal symptoms were connected to stress and HPA dysfunction?

Answer: Karen's elevated night cortisol (particularly the very high 2 AM sample) and reversed cortisol rhythm indicated Stage 2 HPA dysfunction. This explained her 2-4 AM waking pattern and amplified her perimenopausal symptoms.

4

Why is "pushing relaxation" inappropriate for trauma survivors like Susan?

Answer: For trauma survivors, relaxation can feel threatening because their nervous system has learned to stay hypervigilant for safety. Instead, a trauma-informed approach focuses on safety cues, gentle bottom-up practices, predictability, and co-regulation‚Äîmeeting the nervous system where it is and gradually widening the window of tolerance.

Key Takeaways

  • Caregivers in exhaustion need permission to rest and receive help‚Äîaddress guilt and recruit support
  • Perimenopause + stress = amplified symptoms; addressing stress helps hormones
  • Trauma-related dysregulation requires slow, gentle, safety-focused approaches‚Äînever push relaxation
  • Night cortisol elevation is often the culprit for 2-4 AM waking patterns
  • Your regulated presence as a coach is therapeutic in itself‚Äîco-regulation matters

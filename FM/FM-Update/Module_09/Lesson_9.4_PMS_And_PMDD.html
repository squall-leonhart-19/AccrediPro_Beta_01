Understanding Premenstrual Symptoms

Premenstrual symptoms exist on a spectrum from normal and manageable to severely disabling. Understanding where a client falls helps determine appropriate support.

Coach Tip

When a client says "I have terrible PMS," ask them to describe what that means. One woman's "terrible" might be mild bloating; another's might be suicidal thoughts. Specificity matters. Have them rate severity (0-10) and describe functional impairment: "Does this affect your work? Relationships? Daily activities?"

Normal Premenstrual Changes

Some premenstrual changes are normal and not problematic. These reflect natural hormonal shifts and don't significantly impair functioning:

  • Mild breast tenderness
  • Slight bloating or water retention
  • Minor mood fluctuations
  • Mild fatigue or decreased energy
  • Food cravings (especially carbohydrates)

Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS)

PMS is defined by symptoms that:

  • Occur consistently in the luteal phase (week before period)
  • Resolve within a few days of menstruation
  • Interfere with daily activities, work, or relationships
  • Are not explained by another condition

Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)

PMDD is a severe form of PMS that is recognized as a psychiatric diagnosis in the DSM-5. It's not just "bad PMS"‚Äîit's a distinct disorder with significant impacts.

PMDD Diagnostic Criteria (Simplified)

In most cycles during the past year, at least 5 symptoms present in the final week before menses, with at least 1 being a core emotional symptom:

Core Emotional Symptoms (at least 1 required):

  • Marked affective lability (mood swings)
  • Marked irritability or anger
  • Marked depressed mood, hopelessness, or self-deprecating thoughts
  • Marked anxiety or tension

Plus additional symptoms (total of 5+): Decreased interest in usual activities ‚Ä¢ Difficulty concentrating ‚Ä¢ Lethargy, fatigue, lack of energy ‚Ä¢ Appetite changes, overeating, or food cravings ‚Ä¢ Hypersomnia or insomnia ‚Ä¢ Feeling overwhelmed or out of control ‚Ä¢ Physical symptoms (bloating, breast tenderness, etc.)

‚ö†Ô∏è PMDD Is Serious

PMDD can cause suicidal thoughts in some women. If a client expresses suicidal ideation‚Äîeven only premenstrually‚Äîrefer to mental health support immediately.

PMDD typically requires medical treatment (SSRIs, hormonal interventions) in addition to lifestyle support. Coach within your scope while ensuring appropriate care.

What Causes PMS?

PMS isn't caused by abnormal hormone levels‚Äîmost women with PMS have normal hormone levels. Instead, it appears to be an abnormal sensitivity to normal hormonal fluctuations, particularly the drop in estrogen and progesterone that occurs premenstrually.

Coach Tip

Help clients reframe PMS as a sensitivity issue rather than a hormone problem. This shifts the conversation from "What's wrong with my hormones?" to "How can I support my body's response to normal hormonal changes?" This empowers clients to take action through nutrition, stress management, and lifestyle‚Äîareas within their control.

Key Contributing Factors

1

Neurotransmitter Sensitivity

Progesterone metabolites (especially allopregnanolone) affect GABA receptors. When progesterone drops premenstrually, GABA activity decreases, leading to anxiety, irritability, and mood changes. Serotonin is also affected by hormonal fluctuations.

2

Inflammation

Higher levels of inflammatory markers (CRP, prostaglandins) are associated with more severe PMS. Chronic low-grade inflammation may sensitize the nervous system to hormonal changes.

3

Blood Sugar Dysregulation

Insulin sensitivity changes across the cycle, decreasing in the luteal phase. This can lead to blood sugar instability that exacerbates cravings, fatigue, and mood swings.

4

Chronic Stress

High cortisol depletes progesterone (pregnenolone steal) and affects neurotransmitter balance. Women with higher stress often have worse PMS.

5

Nutritional Deficiencies

Deficiencies in calcium, magnesium, vitamin B6, and vitamin D are all associated with more severe PMS symptoms.

6

Estrogen Dominance

Relatively high estrogen and/or low progesterone intensifies PMS symptoms. Addressing estrogen metabolism can help.

Evidence-Based Management Strategies

Nutritional Approaches

üåü Dietary Strategies for PMS

Calcium-Rich Foods (1,200mg/day)

One of the most well-studied interventions. Studies show significant reduction in mood symptoms, water retention, and pain. Sources: dairy, fortified non-dairy milk, sardines, leafy greens.

Magnesium-Rich Foods (300-400mg/day)

Reduces water retention, mood symptoms, and migraines. Magnesium is often depleted by stress. Sources: dark chocolate, nuts, seeds, leafy greens, avocado.

Complex Carbohydrates

Support serotonin production, which is why carb cravings increase premenstrually. Choose whole grains, sweet potatoes, legumes rather than refined carbs.

Anti-Inflammatory Foods

Omega-3 fatty acids (fish, flax, walnuts), colorful vegetables, turmeric, ginger help reduce prostaglandin-related symptoms.

Coach Tip

When discussing carb cravings, normalize them: "Your body is asking for carbs because it's trying to boost serotonin‚Äîthat's a smart biological response. Let's honor that craving with nutrient-dense carbs like sweet potatoes, oatmeal, or quinoa instead of cookies." This validates the craving while redirecting toward better choices.

Key Supplements

üíä

Calcium (1,000-1,200mg/day)

Strong evidence for reducing all PMS symptoms by ~50% in clinical trials. Calcium carbonate or citrate. Take with vitamin D for absorption.

üíä

Magnesium (200-400mg/day)

Particularly helpful for bloating, mood, and migraines. Magnesium glycinate or citrate. May take throughout cycle or increase in luteal phase.

üíä

Vitamin B6 (50-100mg/day)

Supports progesterone production, serotonin synthesis, and dopamine. Evidence supports use for mood symptoms. Don't exceed 100mg/day long-term (nerve toxicity risk).

üíä

Vitex (Chaste Tree Berry)

Herbal remedy that may support progesterone and reduce prolactin. Some evidence for breast tenderness, mood symptoms. Takes 2-3 cycles to see effects. 20-40mg standardized extract.

üíä

Evening Primrose Oil (GLA)

Gamma-linolenic acid may help with breast tenderness and inflammation. 500-1,000mg/day. Evidence is mixed but generally safe.

Lifestyle Modifications

Lifestyle Strategies for PMS Relief

üèÉ‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Regular Exercise

Aerobic exercise significantly reduces PMS symptoms‚Äîpossibly through endorphin release, stress reduction, and improved mood. 30+ minutes most days. May scale back intensity in late luteal if fatigued.

üßò‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Stress Management

Essential given stress-hormone connections. Increased stress = worse PMS. Prioritize stress reduction especially in the week before the period. Yoga shows specific benefits for PMS.

üò¥ Sleep Optimization

Sleep quality often declines premenstrually. Prioritize sleep hygiene; may need extra sleep during this time. Magnesium can help with sleep.

üìÖ Cycle Awareness

Plan demanding activities for follicular/ovulatory phase when energy is higher. Allow for more rest, lighter workload, and self-care in late luteal.

üî• Heat Therapy

Warm baths, heating pads can help with cramps, muscle tension, and relaxation.

Coach Tip

Cycle awareness is transformative. Encourage clients to schedule important meetings, presentations, and social events during their follicular phase (days 1-14) when energy and mood are typically optimal. Protect the late luteal phase (days 21-28) for lower-key activities, self-care, and rest. This simple planning strategy can dramatically improve quality of life.

Addressing Root Causes

Beyond symptom management, consider underlying factors:

  • If estrogen dominance suspected: Support liver detox, gut health, reduce xenoestrogen exposure
  • If chronic stress is a factor: Prioritize HPA axis support (adaptogens, nervous system regulation)
  • If blood sugar is unstable: Focus on balanced meals, protein at breakfast, regular eating pattern
  • If inflammation is high: Anti-inflammatory diet, omega-3s, reduce inflammatory triggers

Creating a PMS Management Plan

üìã Sample PMS Protocol

Throughout Cycle:

  • Calcium-rich diet + supplement if needed (1,000-1,200mg/day)
  • Magnesium glycinate 200-300mg/day
  • Regular exercise 4-5 days/week
  • Stress management practices daily
  • Limit caffeine, alcohol, excess salt

Luteal Phase (Days 14-28):

  • Increase magnesium to 400mg if bloating/mood symptoms
  • Add vitamin B6 50mg/day
  • Extra focus on blood sugar stability
  • Reduce workout intensity if fatigued
  • Allow extra sleep and rest
  • Schedule lighter workload if possible

Premenstrual Week (Days 21-28):

  • Extra self-care and stress management
  • Heat therapy for cramps/tension
  • Gentle exercise (walking, yoga)
  • Prioritize anti-inflammatory foods
  • Practice self-compassion‚Äîsymptoms are real

Tracking for Patterns

Encourage clients to track symptoms alongside their cycle for 2-3 months. This helps:

  • Confirm symptoms are truly premenstrual (vs. depression, anxiety, or other conditions)
  • Identify which symptoms are most problematic
  • Recognize patterns and triggers
  • Measure progress with interventions
  • Provide data for healthcare providers if needed

When to Refer

üö® Refer to Medical Provider When:
  • Symptoms suggest PMDD (especially if suicidal thoughts are present)
  • Symptoms significantly impair work, relationships, or daily functioning despite lifestyle changes
  • Symptoms don't follow a clear premenstrual pattern (may be depression, anxiety, or thyroid disorder)
  • Client is interested in hormonal contraceptives or other medical treatments
  • Severe symptoms unresponsive to coaching interventions after 2-3 cycles

Treatment options available from medical providers: SSRIs (particularly effective for PMDD), hormonal contraceptives, GnRH agonists in severe cases

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What distinguishes PMS from normal premenstrual changes?

Answer: PMS symptoms interfere with daily activities, work, or relationships, whereas normal premenstrual changes are mild and manageable. PMS also follows a consistent pattern, occurring in the luteal phase and resolving within a few days of menstruation.

2

Why do women with PMS often have normal hormone levels despite severe symptoms?

Answer: PMS isn't caused by abnormal hormone levels‚Äîit reflects an abnormal sensitivity to normal hormonal fluctuations, particularly the drop in estrogen and progesterone premenstrually. The issue is sensitivity, not hormone production.

3

Which two nutrients have the strongest research evidence for reducing PMS symptoms?

Answer: Calcium (1,000-1,200mg/day) and magnesium (200-400mg/day) have the strongest evidence. Calcium has been shown to reduce PMS symptoms by ~50% in clinical trials, while magnesium is particularly helpful for bloating, mood, and migraines.

4

When should you refer a client with premenstrual symptoms to a medical provider?

Answer: Refer when: (1) Symptoms suggest PMDD, especially with suicidal thoughts; (2) Symptoms significantly impair functioning despite lifestyle changes; (3) Symptoms don't follow a clear premenstrual pattern; (4) Client wants hormonal contraceptives or medical treatment; (5) Severe symptoms unresponsive to coaching after 2-3 cycles.

Key Takeaways
  • PMS affects 20-40% of women with symptoms that impair functioning; PMDD is a severe form affecting 3-8%
  • PMS reflects sensitivity to normal hormonal changes, not abnormal hormone levels
  • Key contributing factors: neurotransmitter sensitivity, inflammation, blood sugar dysregulation, stress, nutritional deficiencies
  • Calcium (1,200mg) and magnesium (300-400mg) have strong evidence for symptom reduction
  • Exercise, stress management, and sleep optimization are foundational lifestyle interventions
  • Cycle awareness‚Äîplanning life around energy fluctuations‚Äîcan be transformative
  • Symptom tracking helps confirm patterns and measure progress
  • PMDD is serious and often requires medical treatment alongside lifestyle support

Amara, 28, had been told she had PCOS at 19, given birth control pills, and sent on her way. Now she wanted to come off the pill to try for pregnancy‚Äîbut she had no idea what PCOS really meant or how to manage it.

Through dietary changes emphasizing protein and fiber, strategic exercise, and targeted supplements, Amara began ovulating within 4 months of stopping the pill. "I finally understand my body," she said. "PCOS isn't a sentence‚Äîit's a metabolic issue I can work with."

What Is PCOS?

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a common endocrine disorder characterized by a combination of:

  • Irregular or absent ovulation
  • High androgens (testosterone, DHEA)
  • Metabolic dysfunction (often insulin resistance)

Despite its name, PCOS isn't primarily about ovarian "cysts"‚Äîthe polycystic appearance on ultrasound is actually multiple immature follicles that haven't ovulated. And you can have PCOS without this appearance.

Diagnostic Criteria (Rotterdam Criteria)

PCOS is diagnosed when 2 of 3 criteria are present (after ruling out other conditions):

1. Irregular/Absent Ovulation

Cycles >35 days, <8 periods per year, or no ovulation confirmed by testing

2. Clinical or Biochemical Hyperandrogenism

Signs like acne, hirsutism, hair loss; OR elevated testosterone/DHEA on labs

3. Polycystic Ovaries on Ultrasound

12+ follicles in one ovary or increased ovarian volume (not required for diagnosis)

The Four PCOS Phenotypes

Not all PCOS is the same. Understanding the different phenotypes helps tailor treatment approaches:

Type A: Full-Blown (Classic) PCOS

Criteria met: All three (anovulation + hyperandrogenism + polycystic ovaries)

Characteristics: Most metabolically severe. Highest insulin resistance, highest androgen levels. Weight gain common. Highest long-term health risks.

Approach: Aggressive focus on insulin sensitivity, anti-inflammatory diet, significant lifestyle modification.

Type B: Classic Non-PCO PCOS

Criteria met: Anovulation + hyperandrogenism (no polycystic ovaries)

Characteristics: Similar to Type A but without ultrasound findings. Still significant metabolic and hormonal issues.

Approach: Same as Type A‚Äîfocus on insulin and androgens.

Type C: Ovulatory PCOS

Criteria met: Hyperandrogenism + polycystic ovaries (regular ovulation)

Characteristics: May have regular cycles but still has androgen symptoms (acne, hirsutism). Less metabolic dysfunction. Better fertility outcomes.

Approach: Focus on androgen reduction. Still address insulin if elevated.

Type D: Non-Hyperandrogenic PCOS

Criteria met: Anovulation + polycystic ovaries (no high androgens)

Characteristics: Mildest form. Irregular cycles but no acne/hirsutism. Lower metabolic risk. May resolve with stress/weight management.

Approach: May respond well to lifestyle intervention alone. Address underlying drivers (stress, inflammation).

Signs of Insulin Resistance

  • Weight gain, especially around the abdomen
  • Difficulty losing weight despite effort
  • Intense carbohydrate cravings
  • Energy crashes after meals
  • Acanthosis nigricans (dark, velvety skin patches on neck, armpits, groin)
  • Skin tags
  • Elevated fasting insulin (even with normal glucose)

Important: Fasting glucose and HbA1c can be normal while fasting insulin is elevated. This means someone is insulin resistant but compensating. Request fasting insulin along with glucose for a complete picture.

Other PCOS Drivers

While insulin resistance is primary for most, other factors contribute:

üî• Chronic Inflammation

Low-grade inflammation is common in PCOS and contributes to insulin resistance and metabolic dysfunction. CRP and other inflammatory markers are often elevated.

üò∞ Adrenal Androgen Excess

Some women have elevated DHEA-S from adrenal sources rather than ovarian. Often related to stress and HPA axis dysfunction.

ü¶† Gut Dysbiosis

Altered gut microbiome is increasingly recognized in PCOS, affecting inflammation, estrogen metabolism, and potentially insulin sensitivity.

üß¨ Genetic Factors

PCOS tends to run in families. Genetic predisposition interacts with diet, lifestyle, and environment to express the condition.

üíä Post-Pill PCOS

Some women develop PCOS-like symptoms after stopping hormonal birth control. This may be temporary androgen surge during recovery or revelation of underlying PCOS that was masked.

Dietary Approaches for PCOS

Lifestyle modification is first-line treatment for PCOS according to international guidelines. Diet is central.

Core Dietary Principles

Prioritize Protein

Protein at every meal (25-30g) stabilizes blood sugar, supports satiety, and reduces carbohydrate cravings. Sources: eggs, fish, poultry, meat, legumes, Greek yogurt.

Fiber-Rich Foods

Fiber slows glucose absorption and supports gut health. 25-35g/day from vegetables, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains. Fiber also binds excess estrogen for elimination.

Low Glycemic Approach

Choose carbohydrates that don't spike blood sugar. Favor non-starchy vegetables, legumes, berries, and small portions of whole grains. Avoid refined carbs and added sugars.

Healthy Fats

Include anti-inflammatory fats: olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish. Fat doesn't spike insulin and provides satiety. Avoid inflammatory seed oils.

Anti-Inflammatory Foods

Colorful vegetables, fatty fish, turmeric, ginger, green tea. Reduce inflammation that contributes to insulin resistance and androgen production.

Foods to Minimize

Sugar and refined carbs, processed foods, inflammatory oils, excessive dairy (for some), alcohol. These worsen insulin resistance and inflammation.

Specific Dietary Approaches with Evidence

Low Glycemic Index Diet

Research shows low-GI diets improve insulin sensitivity, reduce androgens, and improve menstrual regularity in PCOS.

Mediterranean Diet

Anti-inflammatory, emphasizes vegetables, fish, olive oil. Studies show improvements in insulin resistance and weight in PCOS.

Lower Carbohydrate Approaches

Reducing carbohydrates (not necessarily keto) can significantly improve insulin sensitivity and weight. Typical guidance: 75-150g carbs/day for PCOS.

Exercise for PCOS

Exercise is powerful medicine for PCOS‚Äîimproving insulin sensitivity, reducing androgens, and supporting weight management.

Resistance/Strength Training (2-3x/week): Builds muscle, which improves insulin sensitivity even at rest. May be the most beneficial exercise type for PCOS.
Moderate Cardio (3-5x/week): Walking, cycling, swimming‚Äî30-60 minutes. Improves cardiovascular health and insulin sensitivity.
HIIT (1-2x/week): High-intensity interval training can be particularly effective for insulin sensitivity. Keep sessions short (15-20 min) to avoid cortisol spike.
Avoid Overtraining: Excessive exercise can increase cortisol, which worsens PCOS. Balance intensity with recovery.

Stress Management

High cortisol worsens insulin resistance and can contribute to androgen excess (especially adrenal DHEA). Stress management is essential:

  • Daily relaxation practices (meditation, breathing, yoga)
  • Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)
  • Boundaries and workload management
  • Adaptogenic herbs if appropriate (ashwagandha, rhodiola)

Supplements for PCOS

üíä Inositol (Myo + D-Chiro)

First-line supplement for PCOS. Improves insulin sensitivity, reduces androgens, supports ovulation. 40:1 ratio of myo:d-chiro mimics physiological ratio. Typical dose: 2-4g myo-inositol/day. Strong evidence base.

üíä Berberine

Comparable to metformin for insulin sensitivity. 500mg 2-3x/day with meals. Can lower blood sugar‚Äîuse with caution in diabetics on medication. Not for long-term use without breaks.

üíä N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC)

Antioxidant that improves insulin sensitivity and may support ovulation. 600-1800mg/day. Often used alongside inositol.

üíä Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Anti-inflammatory, may reduce androgens and improve lipid profiles. 2-4g EPA+DHA/day from fish oil.

üíä Vitamin D

Deficiency is common in PCOS and associated with worse metabolic outcomes. Test and optimize to 40-60 ng/mL.

üíä Spearmint Tea

May help reduce androgens and hirsutism. 2 cups/day showed benefit in studies. Simple, low-risk intervention.

What PCOS Is NOT

Common misconceptions can lead to inappropriate treatment:

‚ùå PCOS Myths Debunked

  • "PCOS means you can't get pregnant." False. Many women with PCOS conceive naturally or with minimal intervention once ovulation is restored.
  • "PCOS is caused by being overweight." False. While weight can worsen PCOS, lean women get PCOS too. Weight is often a consequence, not a cause.
  • "PCOS is just a reproductive problem." False. It's a metabolic disorder with lifelong implications for diabetes, heart disease, and other conditions.
  • "Birth control pills treat PCOS." Partially true. Pills manage symptoms but don't address underlying insulin resistance. Symptoms often return when stopping.
  • "You must have cysts to have PCOS." False. Polycystic ovaries aren't required for diagnosis.

Long-Term Health Considerations

PCOS is a lifelong condition with implications beyond fertility:

  • Type 2 diabetes: 50-70% lifetime risk (vs. 10% general population)
  • Cardiovascular disease: Higher risk of heart disease and stroke
  • Endometrial cancer: Increased risk due to chronic anovulation (no progesterone protection)
  • Depression and anxiety: More common in PCOS
  • Sleep apnea: More common, especially with insulin resistance and weight

The good news: Lifestyle intervention significantly reduces these risks. This is why health coaching is so valuable‚Äîthe changes that improve PCOS symptoms also protect long-term health.

üîë Key Takeaways

  • PCOS is a metabolic disorder, not just a reproductive one, affecting 8-13% of women
  • Insulin resistance drives PCOS in 70-80% of cases‚Äîaddressing it is foundational
  • Four phenotypes exist with varying severity and treatment approaches
  • Lifestyle intervention is first-line treatment per international guidelines
  • Key dietary strategies: prioritize protein, increase fiber, reduce refined carbs, anti-inflammatory foods
  • Both strength training and cardio are beneficial; avoid overtraining
  • Inositol is the most evidence-based supplement for PCOS
  • Long-term health risks (diabetes, heart disease) make ongoing management important

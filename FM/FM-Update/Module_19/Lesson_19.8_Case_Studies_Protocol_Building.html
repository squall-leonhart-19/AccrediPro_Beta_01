Case Study 1: The Exhausted Executive

üìã

Sandra, Age 49

Marketing Director Experiencing Burnout

üë©

Client Profile

Occupation: Marketing Director, demanding corporate job, manages team of 12

Family: Married, two teenagers, mother with health issues she helps care for

Chief Complaints: Profound fatigue, brain fog, weight gain (20 lbs in 2 years), disrupted sleep, frequent colds, feeling "burned out"

Health History: Healthy until mid-40s, gradual decline; TSH "normal" per doctor; no other diagnoses

Current Habits: Skips breakfast, coffee throughout day, grabs lunch at desk, large dinner with wine, in bed by 11 PM but wakes at 3-4 AM, no exercise (too tired), no stress management

Assessment Findings

Key Assessment Data

‚ö° Energy/HPA Axis

Classic burnout pattern: tired but wired, 3 AM waking (cortisol dysregulation), energy crashes afternoon, caffeine dependence

üçΩÔ∏è Blood Sugar

Irregular eating, high refined carb intake at dinner, likely glucose instability contributing to fatigue and weight

üò¥ Sleep

4-5 hours quality sleep, 3-4 AM waking, no wind-down routine, alcohol disrupting sleep architecture

üß† Stress

Chronic high stress without recovery practices, poor boundaries, caregiver burden

ü•ó Nutrition

Low protein, few vegetables, excessive caffeine, evening alcohol, likely nutrient depletion

Protocol Design

Sequencing Rationale

Sandra is in HPA axis dysfunction/burnout. Priority must be reducing stress load before adding demands. Starting with intensive exercise or comprehensive diet overhaul would worsen her state. Sequence: stabilize blood sugar and sleep first (quickest wins, least effort), then add stress management, then refine nutrition and movement.

1

Phase 1: Stabilization (Weeks 1-4)

Blood Sugar Focus:

  • Add protein breakfast (simple: eggs, Greek yogurt, or protein shake‚Äîrequires minimal effort)
  • Include protein at lunch (keep easy‚Äîadd grilled chicken to usual salad or soup)
  • Reduce dinner portion, add vegetables before refined carbs

Sleep Focus:

  • Consistent 10:30 PM bedtime, 6:30 AM wake (manageable with her schedule)
  • Reduce caffeine: stop by 2 PM
  • Reduce alcohol: max 1 glass, with dinner, finished by 7 PM
  • Simple wind-down: screens off 9:30 PM, 10 min reading before bed

Stress:

  • One micro-practice: 5 deep breaths before each meal (builds into existing routine)
2

Phase 2: Building (Weeks 5-12)

Expanded Stress Management:

  • Add 10-minute morning stress practice (breathing, meditation, or gentle stretching)
  • Lunch break walk (10-15 minutes‚Äîmovement and stress management combined)
  • Boundary work: one area to say "no" or delegate

Nutrition Refinement:

  • Meal prep one day/week for easier weekday nutrition
  • Increase vegetables to 5+ servings daily
  • Anti-inflammatory focus: fatty fish 2x/week, reduce processed foods

Sleep Optimization:

  • Full wind-down routine (30 min)
  • Sleep environment optimization
  • Address 3 AM waking with blood sugar stabilization and stress work
3

Phase 3: Optimization (Weeks 13-24)

Movement Introduction:

  • Start gentle: 20-30 min walks, yoga, or swimming 3x/week
  • Progress to strength training if energy permits

Advanced Nutrition:

  • Consider targeted elimination if digestive symptoms or inflammation persist
  • Discuss with provider: comprehensive thyroid panel, nutrient testing

Sustainability:

  • Build autonomous self-care routines
  • Develop crisis protocols for high-stress periods
Coach Tip

For exhausted executives like Sandra, protect sleep at all costs. When clients say "I don't have time for 8 hours of sleep," respond with: "You're already not functioning at full capacity due to sleep deprivation. Let's prioritize the one intervention that will make everything else easier‚Äîquality sleep. What's one thing you can delegate or eliminate to protect your sleep window?"

Tracking Plan

Daily: Energy rating (1-10), sleep hours, sleep quality, caffeine count, protein at breakfast (yes/no)

Weekly: Average energy, weight, waist measurement, stress practices completed

Monthly: MSQ reassessment, overall quality of life rating

Anticipated Challenges and Solutions

Challenge
  • "I don't have time for breakfast"
  • Work emergencies disrupting routine
  • Wine is her stress relief
Solution
  • Prep-ahead options (overnight oats with protein, hard-boiled eggs, protein shakes)
  • Minimum viable practices for crisis days
  • Alternative evening relaxation; gradual reduction, not elimination
Coach Tip

When clients resist reducing alcohol, avoid lecturing. Instead, try curiosity: "I hear that wine helps you unwind. What do you notice about your sleep on nights you have wine versus nights you don't? Would you be willing to experiment for one week and track how you feel?" Let their own data drive the conversation.

Case Study 2: The Digestive Distress Client

üìã

Jennifer, Age 42

Elementary School Teacher with IBS

üë©

Client Profile

Occupation: Elementary school teacher

Family: Single, lives alone, close to family

Chief Complaints: Bloating after most meals, alternating constipation and loose stools, abdominal pain, skin breakouts, fatigue, anxiety about eating

Health History: Told she has IBS; tried various diets with mixed results; feels like she's "allergic to everything"; history of antibiotic use for recurrent sinus infections

Current Habits: Eats quickly due to short lunch break, tries to eat "healthy" (lots of salads, smoothies, fiber), moderate stress, adequate sleep, some walking

Assessment Findings

Key Assessment Data

üçΩÔ∏è Digestion

Symptoms after many foods, worse with raw vegetables and high-fiber foods (paradoxical), rushed eating, possible dysbiosis from antibiotic history

ü•ó Food Patterns

High raw vegetable intake, lots of fiber, large smoothies‚Äîall potentially worsening symptoms if SIBO or motility issues

‚è±Ô∏è Eating Behaviors

Fast eating (sympathetic state during meals), eating while working, anxiety about food

üß† Stress/Gut-Brain

Baseline anxiety, symptoms worsen with stress, gut-brain connection evident

Protocol Design

Sequencing Rationale

Before elimination diet (which would increase food anxiety), address eating behaviors and reduce obvious irritants. Her "healthy" diet may be worsening symptoms. Start with how she eats, then modify what she eats, then systematic elimination if needed.

1

Phase 1: Eating Mechanics (Weeks 1-3)

Meal Environment:

  • 3 deep breaths before eating (activates parasympathetic)
  • Sit down for all meals, no eating at desk
  • Put fork down between bites, chew thoroughly
  • No screens during meals when possible

Initial Food Modifications:

  • Switch from raw to cooked vegetables (easier to digest)
  • Reduce portion sizes of high-fiber foods
  • Smaller, more frequent meals vs. large meals
  • Warm foods over cold (smoothies can wait)

Tracking:

  • Detailed food-symptom journal with timing, portion, and context
2

Phase 2: Targeted Elimination (Weeks 4-10)

Based on Phase 1 Data:

  • Modified elimination diet: remove common triggers (gluten, dairy, high-FODMAP if patterns suggest)
  • Keep diet simple and low-risk during elimination
  • Maintain eating behavior practices

Gut Support:

  • Discuss with provider: comprehensive stool testing if not done
  • General gut-soothing foods: bone broth, ginger, well-cooked vegetables
  • Fermented foods only if tolerated (monitor carefully)

Stress-Gut Connection:

  • Daily stress management practice (10 min)
  • Explore anxiety around food‚Äînormalize, reduce fear
3

Phase 3: Reintroduction and Expansion (Weeks 11-18)

Systematic Reintroduction:

  • One food group every 3-4 days
  • Document reactions carefully
  • Create personalized "safe foods" list

Diet Expansion:

  • Gradually increase food variety
  • Reintroduce raw vegetables slowly if tolerated
  • Build diverse, gut-supporting diet

Long-Term Gut Health:

  • Prebiotic foods as tolerated
  • Discuss probiotic options with provider if appropriate
  • Maintain eating behavior practices
Coach Tip

Many digestive clients arrive having already tried multiple elimination diets. Before jumping into another one, ask: "Tell me about how you eat‚Äînot just what you eat, but the environment, your stress level, how fast you eat. Often, changing HOW we eat creates more improvement than changing WHAT we eat."

Provider Collaboration

Referral Considerations
  • Recommend comprehensive stool testing with functional medicine provider
  • SIBO testing if symptoms suggest (worse with fiber, specific carbohydrates)
  • Rule out celiac disease before gluten elimination/reintroduction
  • Consider referral to GI if red flag symptoms present

Case Study 3: The Perimenopausal Transition

üìã

Maria, Age 47

Nurse Navigating Perimenopause

üë©

Client Profile

Occupation: Nurse (shift work, high stress)

Family: Married, one child in college

Chief Complaints: Irregular periods, hot flashes, night sweats, mood swings, "suddenly" gaining weight around middle despite same diet, decreased muscle mass, poor sleep, low libido

Health History: Generally healthy, some joint stiffness starting, family history of osteoporosis

Current Habits: Runs 3x/week (same routine for years), eats "healthy" but often skips meals due to shifts, moderate alcohol, shift work disrupts sleep patterns

Assessment Findings

Key Assessment Data

üå∏ Hormonal

Classic perimenopause: irregular cycles, vasomotor symptoms, mood changes, body composition shifts

‚ö° Metabolic

Likely developing insulin resistance (central weight gain despite unchanged habits), losing muscle mass (sarcopenia)

üí™ Movement

Too much cardio, not enough strength; running may be too much stress on already stressed system

üò¥ Sleep

Shift work creates chronic circadian disruption; night sweats further disrupt sleep

ü•ó Nutrition

Likely undereating protein (muscle loss), irregular meals (blood sugar), needs shift in approach for new metabolic reality

Protocol Design

Sequencing Rationale

Maria needs a metabolic and movement paradigm shift. Her body has changed, and what worked before no longer serves her. Priority: blood sugar stability and protein (addresses metabolism and muscle), strength training transition (builds muscle, supports bones), sleep and stress support (manage cortisol, reduce symptoms).

1

Phase 1: Metabolic Reset (Weeks 1-6)

Nutrition Overhaul:

  • Protein priority: 25-30g per meal, 100+ grams daily
  • No skipped meals‚Äîregular eating critical for blood sugar and hormones
  • Reduce refined carbs, increase protein and fat ratios
  • Strategic carbs: post-exercise, earlier in day
  • Reduce alcohol: disrupts sleep, contributes to hot flashes

Movement Transition:

  • Reduce running to 2x/week, add recovery walks
  • Introduce strength training 2x/week (bodyweight or light weights to start)
  • Focus on compound movements for bone and muscle

Sleep Support:

  • Optimize sleep environment for temperature (night sweats)
  • Create shift work sleep strategy (blackout, white noise, timing)
  • Reduce evening alcohol
2

Phase 2: Hormone Support (Weeks 7-16)

Nutrition Refinement:

  • Add phytoestrogen foods if appropriate (flax, soy if tolerated)
  • Anti-inflammatory emphasis
  • Omega-3 rich foods for joint and brain support
  • Calcium and vitamin D rich foods for bone health

Movement Progression:

  • Progress strength training (heavier weights, 3x/week)
  • Running as enjoyment, not primary exercise
  • Add flexibility work for joint stiffness

Stress and Symptom Management:

  • Regular stress management practices
  • Hot flash triggers identification (if specific foods, stress, etc.)
  • Discuss with provider: hormone testing, consideration of HRT
3

Phase 3: Long-Term Optimization (Weeks 17+)

Sustainable Patterns:

  • Establish lifetime strength training habit
  • Maintain high-protein nutrition approach
  • Bone health focus (strength training, nutrition, provider care)

Ongoing Monitoring:

  • Regular body composition tracking (focus on muscle, not just weight)
  • Symptom tracking for hormonal changes
  • Provider follow-up for bone density, cardiovascular markers
Coach Tip

Perimenopausal clients often feel frustrated that "what used to work doesn't anymore." Reframe this as an opportunity: "Your body has changed, which means you get to discover what works even BETTER for this version of you. The strategies that support a 30-year-old body aren't optimal for a 47-year-old body‚Äîand that's not a problem, it's just biology. Let's find what makes YOU thrive now."

Provider Collaboration

Referral Considerations
  • Hormone panel (FSH, estradiol, progesterone) to confirm perimenopause
  • Discuss HRT options for significant symptoms
  • Metabolic panel, fasting insulin to assess insulin resistance
  • Baseline bone density scan given family history
  • Cardiovascular assessment (perimenopause increases risk)

Case Analysis Framework

Use this framework when developing protocols for any client:

1

Identify the Primary Driver

What's the root cause or most foundational issue driving the client's symptoms?

2

Assess Client Capacity

What can they realistically implement now given their current life circumstances and energy levels?

3

Find the Quick Wins

What changes will create early improvements to build momentum and motivation?

4

Sequence for Success

What order minimizes overwhelm and maximizes results based on physiological priorities?

5

Anticipate Barriers

What challenges will arise? Build solutions into the protocol design in advance.

6

Define Success Markers

How will you know the protocol is working? What specific metrics will you track?

7

Plan for Adjustment

When and how will you reassess? What triggers a protocol modification?

8

Know When to Refer

What requires provider involvement? Where are the boundaries of your coaching scope?

Pause & Reflect

Think about a client you're currently working with or a health challenge you've personally faced. Walk through this 8-step framework. What insights emerge about how to sequence interventions most effectively?

Coach Tip

When clients present with multiple concerns, resist the urge to address everything at once. Instead, say: "I hear that you're struggling with energy, digestion, sleep, and weight. Here's what I've learned: when we try to fix everything simultaneously, we often fix nothing. Let's identify the ONE foundational issue that, if we address it first, will create a ripple effect that makes everything else easier. For you, I believe that's [X]. Does that resonate?"

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

In Sandra's case (exhausted executive), why did the protocol prioritize blood sugar stabilization and sleep before adding exercise?

Answer: Sandra was in HPA axis dysfunction/burnout. Adding exercise would have been an additional stressor on an already overtaxed system. Blood sugar stabilization and sleep were chosen because they provide quick wins with minimal effort, reduce physiological stress, and create the foundation needed before adding movement demands.

2

Why did Jennifer's protocol start with "eating mechanics" before diving into an elimination diet?

Answer: Jennifer already had significant food anxiety and had tried multiple diets. Starting with another elimination diet would have increased her stress and fear around food. By addressing HOW she ate first (parasympathetic activation, slower eating, proper chewing), many symptoms improved before needing to restrict foods, and her anxiety decreased rather than increased.

3

What was the key insight behind shifting Maria from running to strength training as her primary exercise?

Answer: Perimenopause brings metabolic changes including muscle loss (sarcopenia), increased insulin resistance, and bone density concerns. Running, while cardiovascular, doesn't address muscle preservation or bone health. Strength training directly combats muscle loss, improves insulin sensitivity, and provides bone-building stimulus‚Äîmaking it the priority for this life stage.

4

What's the value of creating "minimum viable practices" for high-stress periods, as suggested in Sandra's protocol?

Answer: When life gets chaotic, clients often abandon their entire protocol if they can't maintain the "perfect" version. Minimum viable practices prevent all-or-nothing thinking by defining the absolute basics that maintain progress even during crises. This creates sustainability and prevents the cycle of complete abandonment followed by guilt and having to restart from scratch.

Key Takeaways
  • Every protocol begins with thorough assessment to understand the unique pattern
  • Sequencing must match client capacity‚Äîstart with what's doable, not what's ideal
  • Address foundational issues (blood sugar, sleep, stress) before downstream concerns
  • Anticipate barriers and build solutions into the protocol design from the start
  • Track progress systematically to inform when and how to adjust protocols
  • Collaborate with providers for clinical aspects beyond coaching scope
  • Every client is unique‚Äîthese cases are examples, not templates
  • The art of coaching lies in knowing what to prioritize when

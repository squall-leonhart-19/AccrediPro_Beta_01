Case Study

Rachel's Diet Dilemma

Patient Profile

Rachel, 52 ‚Äî Has bounced between dietary approaches for years. Tried keto (lost weight initially but couldn't sustain, developed insomnia), Mediterranean (pleasant but didn't help blood sugar), Paleo (felt restricted and socially isolated). Currently frustrated and believes she's "broken."

"My friend swears by keto‚Äîshe lost 30 pounds and says she's never felt better. My sister did the Mediterranean diet and her cholesterol improved dramatically. My neighbor is all about carnivore now. I've tried them all and none of them work for me. Maybe I'm just broken."

Rachel had bounced between dietary approaches for years, each time hoping to find "the answer." Keto had initially helped her lose weight, but she couldn't sustain it and experienced terrible insomnia. Mediterranean was pleasant but didn't address her blood sugar issues. Paleo left her feeling restricted and socially isolated.

What Rachel didn't understand was that these diets aren't competing religions‚Äîthey're tools, each designed for different purposes. The question wasn't "which diet is best?" but rather "which approach best serves Rachel's unique needs right now?"

The diet industry profits from making you believe there's one perfect way to eat. Functional medicine takes a different view: the right diet depends on the individual‚Äîtheir health conditions, genetic makeup, metabolic status, lifestyle, and personal preferences. Different therapeutic diets are simply different tools in your toolkit.

Coach Tip

When clients come in frustrated after "trying every diet," help them reframe their experience. Each diet taught them something about their body. Keto showed Rachel she's sensitive to extreme carb restriction. Mediterranean showed her that heart-healthy doesn't automatically mean blood-sugar-friendly. These are valuable data points, not failures.

Mediterranean Diet

ü´í

Mediterranean Diet

The most researched dietary pattern in history

Based on traditional eating patterns of Greece, Italy, and other Mediterranean countries. Emphasizes plant foods, olive oil, fish, moderate wine, and limited red meat and processed foods.

Core Components
  • Abundant vegetables & fruits
  • Extra virgin olive oil daily
  • Whole grains & legumes
  • Fish 2-3x weekly
  • Moderate dairy (yogurt, cheese)
  • Limited red meat
  • Optional: moderate red wine
Evidence-Based Benefits
  • ‚Üì 30% cardiovascular events
  • Improved cognitive function
  • Reduced inflammation markers
  • Lower cancer risk
  • Improved depression symptoms
  • Sustainable long-term
Best For

Cardiovascular health, cognitive protection, general health optimization, sustainable lifelong eating. Good starting point for most people. May need modification for those with blood sugar issues (reduce grains) or autoimmunity (eliminate gluten/dairy).

Paleo (Ancestral) Diet

ü•©

Paleo Diet

Eating like our pre-agricultural ancestors

Based on foods available before agriculture‚Äîemphasizing what humans evolved eating for millennia. Eliminates grains, legumes, dairy, and processed foods. Focuses on meat, fish, vegetables, fruits, nuts, and seeds.

Core Components
  • Quality meats & fish
  • Vegetables & fruits
  • Nuts & seeds
  • Healthy fats (olive, avocado, coconut)
  • No grains or legumes
  • No dairy
  • No processed foods or refined sugar
Potential Benefits
  • Blood sugar improvement
  • Weight loss
  • Reduced inflammation
  • Improved digestion
  • Autoimmune symptom relief
  • Elimination of processed foods
Best For

Those with digestive issues, autoimmune conditions (especially as transition to AIP), blood sugar imbalances, or those who thrive without grains.

Considerations: May be too restrictive long-term for some; ensure adequate fiber; quality of meat matters significantly.

Coach Tip

Paleo often works well as a "reset" or elimination starting point. After 30-60 days, many clients can strategically reintroduce well-tolerated foods (like white rice, fermented dairy, or properly prepared legumes) to create a more sustainable long-term approach.

Low-Carb & Ketogenic Diets

ü•ë

Ketogenic Diet

Shifting metabolism to fat-burning

Restricts carbohydrates to shift metabolism toward fat-burning. Low-carb typically means <100g carbs/day; ketogenic is stricter at 20-50g, inducing nutritional ketosis where the body produces ketones from fat for fuel.

Core Components
  • High fat (60-80% calories)
  • Moderate protein (15-30%)
  • Very low carb (5-10%)
  • Non-starchy vegetables
  • Meats, fish, eggs
  • Nuts, seeds, avocado
  • No grains, sugar, most fruits
Potential Benefits
  • Significant blood sugar improvement
  • Insulin resistance reversal
  • Weight loss (especially abdominal)
  • Reduced appetite/cravings
  • Epilepsy management
  • Potential cognitive benefits
Best For

Type 2 diabetes/insulin resistance, significant weight loss needs, epilepsy (under medical supervision), and potentially neurological conditions. Works best for those who tolerate high fat and don't have adrenal or thyroid issues.

Not ideal for: Those with certain thyroid issues, adrenal dysfunction, history of eating disorders, pregnant/nursing women. Risks: "Keto flu" during adaptation; potential hormonal disruption in women; sustainability challenges; nutrient deficiencies if poorly planned.

Autoimmune Protocol (AIP)

Autoimmune Protocol

Calming immune overreactivity through food

An elimination diet specifically designed for autoimmune conditions. More restrictive than Paleo, removing additional foods known to trigger immune reactions. Followed by systematic reintroduction to identify individual triggers.

Elimination Phase (30-90 days)

Remove:

  • Grains, legumes, dairy
  • Eggs
  • Nuts & seeds
  • Nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, potatoes)
  • Alcohol, coffee
  • Refined sugars, seed oils
Emphasize
  • Quality meats (especially organ meats)
  • Bone broth
  • Fish & seafood
  • Vegetables (non-nightshade)
  • Fruits (in moderation)
  • Fermented foods
  • Healthy fats (olive oil, avocado, coconut)
Evidence & Best For

Research shows symptom improvement in Hashimoto's, IBD, rheumatoid arthritis, and other autoimmune conditions. Best for those with diagnosed autoimmune diseases or strong autoimmune symptoms.

Note: Very restrictive‚Äîrequires commitment and may need professional guidance for nutritional adequacy. The elimination phase is temporary; systematic reintroduction builds a personalized long-term diet.

Coach Tip

AIP is a powerful intervention but demanding to follow. Ensure clients have a clear "why" before starting‚Äîunderstanding that it's temporary detective work, not a permanent sentence. Having meal plans, recipes, and batch-cooking strategies ready before day one dramatically improves success rates.

Low-FODMAP Diet

ü´É

Low-FODMAP Diet

Relief for digestive distress

FODMAPs are fermentable carbohydrates that can cause digestive distress in sensitive individuals. This diet temporarily eliminates high-FODMAP foods, then reintroduces them systematically. Developed at Monash University with strong research backing for IBS.

FODMAP Categories
  • Fermentable - carbs fermented by gut bacteria
  • Oligosaccharides - wheat, onions, garlic, legumes
  • Disaccharides - lactose (dairy)
  • Monosaccharides - fructose (honey, apples)
  • Polyols - sugar alcohols (sweeteners, stone fruits)
How It Works
  • 2-6 week strict elimination phase
  • Systematic reintroduction by category
  • Identify personal trigger foods
  • Build personalized, less restrictive diet
  • Not meant for permanent use
Best For

IBS, SIBO, functional digestive disorders with bloating, gas, and altered bowel habits. Strong evidence base. Not meant to be followed long-term‚Äîthe elimination phase is followed by strategic reintroduction to identify specific triggers and build a personalized diet.

Avoiding Dietary Dogma

In an era of passionate diet tribes, maintaining clinical objectivity is essential. Here's how to navigate the landscape without falling into dogmatic thinking:

Five Principles for Flexible Nutrition Coaching

1
Diets Are Tools, Not Religions

No dietary approach is universally optimal. Each has strengths and limitations. The goal is matching the right tool to the right person at the right time‚Äînot defending a dietary ideology.

2
Start with Common Ground

Almost all therapeutic diets agree on fundamentals: eat whole foods, minimize processed foods, emphasize vegetables, choose quality proteins and fats. Start here rather than with controversial points.

3
Honor the Individual

What works beautifully for one client may fail for another‚Äîeven with similar health conditions. Genetics, microbiome, lifestyle, and preferences all matter. Personalization isn't optional.

4
Prioritize Sustainability

The "best" diet is one the client can actually follow long-term. A theoretically perfect diet that lasts two weeks helps no one. Work with preferences and lifestyle realities.

5
Embrace Evolution

Dietary needs change. What serves someone during healing may differ from what serves them in maintenance. Build flexibility into your approach rather than rigid permanence.

Coach Tip

When clients ask "which diet is best?" resist the temptation to name one. Instead, ask: "Best for what? For whom? For how long?" This reframes the conversation from ideology to individualized problem-solving‚Äîexactly where functional medicine lives.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of therapeutic diets with these reflection questions.

1. A client with recently diagnosed Hashimoto's thyroiditis asks which diet would be most appropriate. What would you recommend?

The Autoimmune Protocol (AIP) is specifically designed for autoimmune conditions like Hashimoto's. Research shows symptom improvement in Hashimoto's patients following AIP. After the elimination phase (30-90 days), systematic reintroduction helps identify individual triggers. Long-term, many transition to a modified Paleo approach with well-tolerated foods added back.

2. Why might the ketogenic diet not be appropriate for everyone with insulin resistance?

While keto can be powerful for insulin resistance, it's not appropriate for those with thyroid issues (it can suppress T3), adrenal dysfunction (added stress on already taxed adrenals), history of eating disorders (extreme restriction can trigger relapse), or pregnant/nursing women. Some women experience hormonal disruption on very low-carb diets. Sustainability is also a concern‚Äîif someone can't maintain it, the benefits won't last.

3. A client says "I tried every diet and none of them worked‚ÄîI'm broken." How would you reframe this?

Reframe each diet experience as valuable data about their body, not a failure. "What did you learn from each one? Keto didn't work long-term‚Äîthat tells us you may not do well with extreme carb restriction. Mediterranean didn't fix your blood sugar‚Äîthat suggests you might need fewer grains than the standard version includes. You're not broken; you just haven't found the right match yet. Let's use what you've learned to customize an approach specifically for you."

4. What principle should guide choosing between therapeutic diets?

The key principle is matching the tool to the individual need. There is no universally "best" diet‚Äîonly the best diet for a specific person with their specific health conditions, genetic makeup, metabolic status, lifestyle, and preferences at this specific time. The right choice depends on what they're trying to address and what they can realistically sustain.

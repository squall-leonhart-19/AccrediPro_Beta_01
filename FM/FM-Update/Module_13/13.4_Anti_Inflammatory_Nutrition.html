Case Study: Jasmine's Journey

When Food Becomes Medicine

üë©

Jasmine, Age 48

Psoriatic arthritis ‚Ä¢ Six years on biologic medication ‚Ä¢ Persistent flares, fatigue, and skin plaques

"I've tried everything," Jasmine told her health coach during their first session. When reviewing her food diary, her coach noticed a pattern: daily processed foods, very few vegetables, and regular fast food meals. "My doctor never mentioned diet," Jasmine said.

Her coach explained the connection between food and inflammation and suggested a gradual transition toward an anti-inflammatory eating pattern. Jasmine's story is remarkably common. Many clients with autoimmune conditions have never been told that nutrition could be a powerful therapeutic tool. As a health coach, you have the opportunity to bridge this gap‚Äîteaching clients how food choices directly influence their inflammatory burden and immune function.

The Food-Inflammation Connection

Every time we eat, we make a choice that either promotes or reduces inflammation. This isn't a metaphor‚Äîit's biochemistry. The foods we consume provide precursors for eicosanoids‚Äîsignaling molecules that regulate inflammation, pain, and immune function.

The two main pathways involve:

  • Omega-6 fatty acids (especially arachidonic acid): Precursors to pro-inflammatory prostaglandins and leukotrienes that increase pain and swelling
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA): Precursors to anti-inflammatory resolvins, protectins, and maresins that actively resolve inflammation

The modern Western diet contains omega-6 to omega-3 ratios of 15:1 to 20:1‚Äîfar from the 1:1 to 4:1 ratio our ancestors consumed. This dramatic imbalance creates a pro-inflammatory internal environment that perpetuates autoimmune activity and chronic disease.

Coach Tip

When explaining omega ratios to clients, use this analogy: "Think of omega-6s as the accelerator pedal on inflammation and omega-3s as the brake pedal. Most people are driving with their foot slammed on the gas and barely touching the brake. We need to restore balance."

Anti-Inflammatory Foods: What to Include

Rather than focusing solely on what to avoid, emphasize the abundance of foods that actively reduce inflammation. This positive framing helps clients feel empowered rather than restricted.

üåü Categories of Anti-Inflammatory Foods

Fatty Fish (2-3 servings per week)

Wild salmon, mackerel, sardines, anchovies, herring. Rich in EPA and DHA omega-3s that directly reduce inflammatory cytokines and produce specialized pro-resolving mediators.

Colorful Vegetables (6-8 servings daily)

Leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, beets, sweet potatoes, bell peppers. Contain polyphenols, carotenoids, and fiber that support microbiome health and reduce oxidative stress.

Berries and Cherries

Blueberries, strawberries, tart cherries, blackberries. Anthocyanins have potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects backed by extensive research.

Extra Virgin Olive Oil

Contains oleocanthal, which has anti-inflammatory effects similar to ibuprofen. Use for cooking at low-medium heat and as a dressing.

Nuts and Seeds

Walnuts, almonds, flaxseeds, chia seeds. Provide omega-3s, fiber, and anti-inflammatory compounds like polyphenols.

Herbs and Spices

Turmeric, ginger, garlic, rosemary, oregano. Concentrated sources of anti-inflammatory polyphenols that modulate multiple inflammatory pathways.

The Power of Turmeric

Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, is one of the most-studied anti-inflammatory botanicals. Research shows it can inhibit NF-Œ∫B (a master inflammatory switch), reduce TNF-alpha and IL-6, and modulate over 700 genes involved in inflammation. For best absorption, combine turmeric with black pepper (piperine increases absorption by 2,000%) and fat. While culinary use is beneficial, therapeutic effects often require supplementation.

Coach Tip

Help clients make turmeric a daily habit with this simple recipe: "Golden Milk" ‚Äî warm plant milk with 1 tsp turmeric, pinch of black pepper, cinnamon, and a touch of honey. It's delicious, anti-inflammatory, and becomes a soothing evening ritual.

Pro-Inflammatory Foods: What to Minimize

Equally important is reducing foods that drive inflammation. These foods create inflammatory cascades through multiple mechanisms‚Äîfrom blood sugar spikes to gut barrier damage to oxidative stress.

‚ö†Ô∏è Foods That Promote Inflammation

Refined Sugars and Processed Carbohydrates

White bread, pastries, sodas, candy. Cause blood sugar spikes that increase inflammatory cytokines and advanced glycation end products (AGEs) that damage tissues.

Industrial Seed Oils

Soybean, corn, cottonseed, safflower, sunflower oils. High in omega-6 fatty acids and often oxidized during processing. Found in most processed and restaurant foods.

Trans Fats

Partially hydrogenated oils in some margarines, fried foods, and baked goods. Directly promote inflammation and damage endothelial function.

Processed Meats

Bacon, sausages, deli meats, hot dogs. Contain nitrates, AGEs, and inflammatory compounds formed during processing and preservation.

Excessive Alcohol

Damages intestinal barrier, promotes bacterial overgrowth (SIBO), and increases inflammatory burden. Even moderate intake can be problematic in active autoimmune disease.

Ultra-Processed Foods

Packaged snacks, fast food, frozen meals. Contain multiple inflammatory ingredients plus additives (emulsifiers, artificial sweeteners) that harm gut health.

Coach Tip

Rather than telling clients to "avoid processed foods," teach them to read ingredient lists. A good rule: "If the ingredient list is longer than five items or contains words you can't pronounce, it's probably too processed." This empowers clients to make informed choices.

Anti-Inflammatory Eating Patterns

Beyond individual foods, overall eating patterns matter. Several dietary approaches have demonstrated significant anti-inflammatory benefits in research studies:

Mediterranean Diet
  • Key Features: Olive oil, fish, vegetables, legumes, moderate wine, limited red meat
  • Research: Reduces CRP, IL-6; benefits in RA, IBD, cardiovascular disease
  • Sustainability: High‚Äîsocially acceptable, delicious, flexible
DASH Diet
  • Key Features: Fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, low sodium
  • Research: Reduces inflammatory markers, blood pressure, cardiovascular risk
  • Sustainability: High‚Äîpractical, widely available foods
Anti-Inflammatory Diet
  • Key Features: Colorful produce, omega-3s, olive oil, herbs/spices, limited processed foods
  • Research: Based on Mediterranean principles with emphasis on specific anti-inflammatory foods
  • Sustainability: High‚Äîallows personalization and flexibility
Autoimmune Protocol (AIP)
  • Key Features: Eliminates grains, legumes, dairy, eggs, nightshades, nuts, seeds; then reintroduces
  • Research: Studies show improvement in IBD, Hashimoto's thyroiditis
  • Sustainability: Lower‚Äîmore restrictive, requires careful implementation

For most clients with autoimmune conditions, starting with a Mediterranean-style anti-inflammatory approach provides significant benefit with good sustainability. The more restrictive Autoimmune Protocol may be appropriate for clients who don't respond adequately to general anti-inflammatory eating or who have severe, active disease.

The Autoimmune Protocol (AIP)

The Autoimmune Protocol deserves detailed discussion given its growing popularity and research support. AIP is an elimination diet designed to remove potentially inflammatory and immune-triggering foods, then systematically reintroduce them to identify individual sensitivities.

Important Context

AIP is not a permanent diet‚Äîit's a diagnostic tool. The goal is to calm inflammation during the elimination phase (30-90 days), then systematically reintroduce foods to discover what each individual can tolerate. Most clients will be able to reintroduce many eliminated foods and end up with a personalized eating plan.

AIP Elimination Phase: Foods to Avoid

All Grains

Including gluten-free grains like rice, quinoa, oats

Legumes

Beans, lentils, peanuts, soy products

All Dairy

Milk, cheese, yogurt, butter, cream

Eggs

Especially egg whites (proteins)

Nightshade Vegetables

Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, white potatoes

Nuts and Seeds

Including nut-based spices like nutmeg

All Alcohol

Wine, beer, spirits of any kind

Refined Sugars

All processed and artificial sweeteners

üåü AIP: Foods to Emphasize

Quality Proteins

Grass-fed meat, wild fish, organ meats, bone broth‚Äîproviding amino acids and nutrients for healing

Non-Nightshade Vegetables

All vegetables except nightshades‚Äîespecially leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, root vegetables

Fruits

All fruits in moderation, especially berries for their anti-inflammatory anthocyanins

Healthy Fats

Olive oil, coconut oil, avocado, animal fats from quality sources‚Äîno seed oils

Fermented Foods

Sauerkraut, kimchi, kombucha, water kefir‚Äîsupporting gut microbiome diversity

Herbs and Non-Seed Spices

Fresh and dried herbs, turmeric, ginger, garlic‚Äîconcentrated anti-inflammatory compounds

Research on AIP is growing and showing promising results. A 2017 study in Inflammatory Bowel Diseases found that 73% of participants with IBD achieved clinical remission after six weeks on AIP. Studies in Hashimoto's thyroiditis have also shown improvements in symptoms, quality of life, and thyroid antibody levels.

Coach Tip

AIP success requires thorough preparation. Before clients start, help them: (1) learn what they CAN eat (abundance mindset), (2) stock their kitchen and remove temptations, (3) batch cook and meal prep, (4) find community support (online groups, local meetups), and (5) set realistic expectations about the timeline and commitment required. Preparation prevents overwhelm.

Gluten and Dairy: Special Considerations

Gluten deserves specific attention in autoimmune conditions. Beyond celiac disease (an autoimmune condition triggered by gluten), research suggests gluten may contribute to other autoimmune conditions through several mechanisms:

1

Zonulin Release

Gluten triggers zonulin secretion, increasing intestinal permeability in susceptible individuals‚Äîeven those without celiac disease

2

Molecular Mimicry

Gluten proteins share structural similarities with thyroid tissue, potentially triggering cross-reactivity in Hashimoto's thyroiditis

3

Non-Celiac Gluten Sensitivity

Can cause systemic inflammation, neurological symptoms, and gut dysfunction even without celiac disease

4

Microbiome Effects

May alter gut bacteria composition in sensitive individuals, affecting immune regulation

Many functional medicine practitioners recommend gluten elimination for clients with any autoimmune condition, at least as a trial. However, this is a conversation clients should have with their healthcare provider. Critical note: Testing for celiac disease should occur BEFORE eliminating gluten, as it requires active gluten consumption for accurate antibody testing.

Why Dairy May Be Problematic in Autoimmune Conditions

Casein Sensitivity

Casein (milk protein) can trigger immune responses similar to gluten in some individuals with autoimmune conditions

Lactose Intolerance

Undigested lactose feeds harmful bacteria and causes gastrointestinal symptoms, inflammation

A1 Beta-Casein

This protein variant (in conventional cow's milk) may be more inflammatory than A2 casein found in some heritage breeds

Hormones and Antibiotics

Conventional dairy may contain growth hormones (rBGH) and antibiotic residues that affect immune function

Molecular Mimicry

Dairy proteins may cross-react with some human tissues in sensitive individuals, perpetuating autoimmune responses

Individual Variation

Some clients tolerate sheep or goat dairy better than cow's dairy; fermented dairy (kefir, yogurt) may be better tolerated

The only reliable way to determine dairy tolerance is through elimination and systematic reintroduction. Remove all dairy for 30-60 days, then reintroduce one dairy product at a time, observing for reactions over 3-4 days before trying the next one.

Coach Tip

When clients reintroduce dairy, have them start with the most-tolerated forms first: fermented dairy (yogurt, kefir), then hard cheeses (less lactose), then soft cheeses, and finally fluid milk. If they react to any form, stop and wait before trying another. This systematic approach identifies their personal threshold.

Practical Coaching Strategies

Helping clients transition to anti-inflammatory eating requires skill in behavior change, not just nutritional knowledge. Here's how to guide clients effectively:

1

Start with Additions, Not Subtractions

Before removing anything, focus on adding anti-inflammatory foods. "Let's get to 6 servings of vegetables daily" feels more positive than "no more bread." Often, as healthy foods increase, less healthy choices naturally decrease.

2

Identify the Client's "Why"

Connect dietary changes to what matters most: "You mentioned wanting energy to play with your grandchildren. How might reducing inflammation help with that?" Strong motivation sustains effort through challenges.

3

Use Gradual Transitions

Unless a client is highly motivated for immediate change, gradual transitions often succeed better. Week 1: swap oils. Week 2: add fatty fish. Week 3: reduce sugar. This builds momentum and skills.

4

Address Practical Barriers

Explore what might get in the way: time, cooking skills, family preferences, travel, social situations. Problem-solve together before issues arise.

5

Expect and Plan for Setbacks

Holiday gatherings, stressful weeks, and special occasions happen. Help clients develop "good-better-best" frameworks and self-compassion when they deviate from their plan.

6

Focus on Progress, Not Perfection

Celebrate small wins. If a client increases vegetables from 2 to 4 servings daily, that's 100% improvement worthy of recognition, even if they haven't reached the goal of 6-8 servings yet.

Coach Tip

Create a "Good-Better-Best" framework with clients for common situations. For example, eating out: Good: order vegetables as a side instead of fries. Better: choose grilled fish with vegetables and ask for olive oil instead of butter. Best: select a restaurant known for anti-inflammatory options. This removes perfectionism and provides options for any situation.

üìù Symptom Journals

ü©∫ Inflammatory Markers

‚ö° Energy Ratings

Simple 1-10 daily energy scores reveal patterns and improvements that might otherwise go unnoticed

üì∏ Before/After Photos

Especially valuable for skin conditions like psoriasis, eczema, or acne rosacea‚Äîvisual evidence is powerful

üèÉ Functional Measures

Morning stiffness duration, grip strength, walking distance, stairs climbed‚Äîconcrete improvements in daily function

Improvements may take 4-12 weeks to become apparent, so help clients maintain patience and consistency. The inflammatory burden didn't accumulate overnight‚Äîit won't resolve overnight either. But consistent anti-inflammatory eating creates measurable changes over time.

Answer: The modern Western diet contains omega-6 to omega-3 ratios of 15:1 to 20:1, far from the ancestral ratio of 1:1 to 4:1. Omega-6 fatty acids (especially arachidonic acid) are precursors to pro-inflammatory eicosanoids like prostaglandins and leukotrienes, while omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) produce anti-inflammatory resolvins, protectins, and maresins. What are the four mechanisms through which food influences inflammation beyond fatty acid metabolism?

Answer: Beyond fatty acid metabolism, food influences inflammation through: (1) Blood sugar regulation‚Äîhigh-glycemic foods spike insulin and inflammatory cytokines; (2) Gut microbiome composition‚Äîfood directly shapes bacterial populations that regulate immune function; (3) Intestinal barrier integrity‚Äîcertain foods damage or heal the gut lining, affecting permeability; and (4) Direct immune triggering‚Äîfood sensitivities can activate immune responses, creating inflammatory cascades.

Answer: AIP is considered a diagnostic tool because its purpose is to temporarily eliminate potentially inflammatory foods (30-90 days) to calm the immune system, then systematically reintroduce foods to identify individual sensitivities and tolerances. Key Takeaways
  • Food directly influences inflammation through fatty acid metabolism, blood sugar regulation, gut microbiome composition, and intestinal barrier function
  • Anti-inflammatory foods include fatty fish, colorful vegetables, berries, olive oil, nuts, seeds, and anti-inflammatory herbs and spices
  • Pro-inflammatory foods to minimize include refined sugars, industrial seed oils, trans fats, processed meats, and ultra-processed foods
  • The Mediterranean diet is a well-researched, sustainable anti-inflammatory eating pattern appropriate for most clients
  • The Autoimmune Protocol (AIP) is a more restrictive elimination approach with growing research support, used as a diagnostic tool followed by systematic reintroduction
  • Gluten and dairy warrant special consideration in autoimmune conditions due to multiple mechanisms that may trigger or perpetuate immune responses
  • Effective coaching starts with additions before subtractions, identifies the client's deeper motivation, and addresses practical barriers proactively
  • Tracking progress through symptom journals, inflammatory markers, energy ratings, and functional measures helps clients stay motivated through the 4-12 week window needed to see results

A Coach's Observation

Jasmine, 48, had lived with psoriatic arthritis for six years. Despite being on a biologic medication, she still experienced flares, fatigue, and persistent skin plaques. "I've tried everything," she told her coach. When reviewing her food diary, her coach noticed a pattern: daily processed foods, very few vegetables, and regular fast food. "My doctor never mentioned diet," Jasmine said. Her coach explained the connection between food and inflammation and suggested a gradual transition toward an anti-inflammatory eating pattern. Over three months, as Jasmine increased vegetables, added fatty fish, replaced processed oils with olive oil, and reduced sugar, her inflammation markers dropped. Her skin cleared significantly, joint pain decreased, and her rheumatologist noted improvement in her inflammatory markers. "I had no idea food could make such a difference," Jasmine reflected. "Why didn't anyone tell me this before?"

The Food-Inflammation Connection

Every time we eat, we make a choice that either promotes or reduces inflammation. This isn't a metaphorâ€”it's biochemistry. The foods we consume provide precursors for eicosanoidsâ€”signaling molecules that regulate inflammation, pain, and immune function.

The two main pathways involve:

  • Omega-6 fatty acids (especially arachidonic acid): Precursors to pro-inflammatory prostaglandins and leukotrienes
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA): Precursors to anti-inflammatory resolvins, protectins, and maresins

The modern Western diet contains omega-6 to omega-3 ratios of 15:1 to 20:1â€”far from the 1:1 to 4:1 ratio of our ancestors. This imbalance creates a pro-inflammatory internal environment that perpetuates autoimmune activity.

Anti-Inflammatory Foods: What to Include

Rather than focusing solely on what to avoid, emphasize the abundance of foods that actively reduce inflammation:

Categories of Anti-Inflammatory Foods

Fatty Fish (2-3 servings per week)

Wild salmon, mackerel, sardines, anchovies, herring. Rich in EPA and DHA omega-3s that directly reduce inflammatory cytokines and produce specialized pro-resolving mediators.

Colorful Vegetables (6-8 servings daily)

Leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, beets, sweet potatoes, bell peppers. Contain polyphenols, carotenoids, and fiber that support microbiome health and reduce oxidative stress.

Berries and Cherries

Blueberries, strawberries, tart cherries, blackberries. Anthocyanins have potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects.

Extra Virgin Olive Oil

Contains oleocanthal, which has anti-inflammatory effects similar to ibuprofen. Use for cooking and as a dressing.

Nuts and Seeds

Walnuts, almonds, flaxseeds, chia seeds. Provide omega-3s, fiber, and anti-inflammatory compounds.

Herbs and Spices

Turmeric, ginger, garlic, rosemary, oregano. Concentrated sources of anti-inflammatory polyphenols.

The Power of Turmeric

Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, is one of the most-studied anti-inflammatory botanicals. Research shows it can inhibit NF-ÎºB (a master inflammatory switch), reduce TNF-alpha and IL-6, and modulate over 700 genes involved in inflammation. For best absorption, combine turmeric with black pepper (piperine increases absorption by 2,000%) and fat. While culinary use is beneficial, therapeutic effects often require supplementation.

Pro-Inflammatory Foods: What to Minimize

Equally important is reducing foods that drive inflammation:

Foods That Promote Inflammation

Refined Sugars and Processed Carbohydrates

White bread, pastries, sodas, candy. Cause blood sugar spikes that increase inflammatory cytokines and advanced glycation end products (AGEs).

Industrial Seed Oils

Soybean, corn, cottonseed, safflower, sunflower oils. High in omega-6 fatty acids and often oxidized during processing. Found in most processed and restaurant foods.

Trans Fats

Partially hydrogenated oils in some margarines, fried foods, and baked goods. Directly promote inflammation and damage endothelial function.

Processed Meats

Bacon, sausages, deli meats, hot dogs. Contain nitrates, AGEs, and inflammatory compounds formed during processing.

Excessive Alcohol

Damages intestinal barrier, promotes bacterial overgrowth, and increases inflammatory burden. Even moderate intake can be problematic in active autoimmune disease.

Ultra-Processed Foods

Packaged snacks, fast food, frozen meals. Contain multiple inflammatory ingredients plus additives (emulsifiers, artificial sweeteners) that harm gut health.

Anti-Inflammatory Eating Patterns

Beyond individual foods, overall eating patterns matter. Several dietary approaches have demonstrated anti-inflammatory benefits:

Evidence-Based Anti-Inflammatory Diets

Diet Pattern Key Features Research Highlights
Mediterranean Diet Olive oil, fish, vegetables, legumes, moderate wine, limited red meat Reduces CRP, IL-6; benefits in RA, IBD, cardiovascular disease
DASH Diet Fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, low sodium Reduces inflammatory markers, blood pressure, cardiovascular risk
Anti-Inflammatory Diet Colorful produce, omega-3s, olive oil, herbs/spices, limited processed foods Based on Mediterranean principles with emphasis on specific anti-inflammatory foods
Autoimmune Protocol (AIP) Eliminates grains, legumes, dairy, eggs, nightshades, nuts, seeds, then reintroduces Studies show improvement in IBD, Hashimoto's; more restrictive, requires careful implementation

For most clients with autoimmune conditions, starting with a Mediterranean-style anti-inflammatory approach provides significant benefit with good sustainability. The more restrictive Autoimmune Protocol may be appropriate for clients who don't respond adequately to general anti-inflammatory eating.

The Autoimmune Protocol (AIP)

The Autoimmune Protocol deserves detailed discussion given its growing popularity and research support. AIP is an elimination diet designed to remove potentially inflammatory and immune-triggering foods, then systematically reintroduce them to identify individual sensitivities.

Including gluten-free

Legumes

Beans, lentils, peanuts, soy

Dairy

All forms

Eggs

Especially whites

Nightshades

Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, potatoes

Nuts & Seeds

Including spices

Alcohol

All types

Refined Sugars

All processed sugars

AIP: Foods to Emphasize

  • Quality proteins: Grass-fed meat, wild fish, organ meats, bone broth
  • Vegetables: All except nightshades (especially leafy greens, cruciferous, root vegetables)
  • Fruits: All in moderation, especially berries
  • Healthy fats: Olive oil, coconut oil, avocado, animal fats from quality sources
  • Fermented foods: Sauerkraut, kimchi, kombucha, water kefir
  • Herbs and non-seed spices: Fresh and dried herbs, turmeric, ginger, garlic

Research on AIP is growing. A 2017 study in Inflammatory Bowel Diseases found that 73% of participants with IBD achieved clinical remission after six weeks on AIP. Studies in Hashimoto's thyroiditis have also shown improvements in symptoms and quality of life.

Coaching Considerations for AIP

AIP is nutritionally complete but socially and practically challenging. Success requires significant planning, cooking skills, and support. As a coach, help clients: (1) prepare thoroughly before starting, (2) batch cook and meal prep, (3) find community support (online groups, local meetups), (4) focus on abundance (what they CAN eat) rather than restriction, (5) reintroduce foods systematically after 30-60 days of elimination, and (6) view it as a diagnostic tool, not a permanent diet. Most clients will tolerate many eliminated foods and end up with a personalized eating plan.

Gluten: Special Considerations

Gluten deserves specific attention in autoimmune conditions. Beyond celiac disease (an autoimmune condition triggered by gluten), research suggests gluten may contribute to other autoimmune conditions through several mechanisms:

  • Zonulin release: Gluten triggers zonulin secretion, increasing intestinal permeability in susceptible individuals
  • Molecular mimicry: Gluten proteins share structural similarities with thyroid tissue, potentially triggering cross-reactivity in Hashimoto's
  • Non-celiac gluten sensitivity: Can cause systemic inflammation even without celiac disease
  • Microbiome effects: May alter gut bacteria composition in sensitive individuals

Many functional medicine practitioners recommend gluten elimination for clients with any autoimmune condition, at least as a trial. However, this is a conversation to have with their healthcare provider. Testing for celiac disease should occur BEFORE eliminating gluten, as it requires active consumption for accurate results.

Dairy: Individual Responses Vary

Dairy is another food category that often contributes to inflammation in individuals with autoimmune conditions, though responses are highly individual. Potential issues include:

Why Dairy May Be Problematic

  • Casein sensitivity: Casein (milk protein) can trigger immune responses similar to gluten in some individuals
  • Lactose intolerance: Undigested lactose feeds harmful bacteria and causes GI symptoms
  • A1 beta-casein: This protein variant (in conventional cow's milk) may be more inflammatory than A2 casein
  • Hormones and antibiotics: Conventional dairy may contain growth hormones and antibiotic residues
  • Molecular mimicry: Dairy proteins may cross-react with some tissues in sensitive individuals

Some clients tolerate sheep or goat dairy better than cow's dairy, and fermented dairy (kefir, yogurt) may be better tolerated than milk. Others do best eliminating dairy entirely. Elimination and reintroduction is the best way to determine individual tolerance.

Practical Coaching Strategies

Helping clients transition to anti-inflammatory eating requires skill in behavior change, not just nutritional knowledge:

Coaching Approach for Dietary Change

1. Start with additions, not subtractions

Before removing anything, focus on adding anti-inflammatory foods. "Let's get to 6 servings of vegetables daily" feels more positive than "no more bread." Often, as healthy foods increase, less healthy choices naturally decrease.

2. Identify the client's "why"

Connect dietary changes to what matters most: "You mentioned wanting energy to play with your grandchildren. How might reducing inflammation help with that?" Strong motivation sustains effort through challenges.

3. Use gradual transitions

Unless a client is highly motivated for immediate change, gradual transitions often succeed better. Week 1: swap oils. Week 2: add fatty fish. Week 3: reduce sugar. This builds momentum and skills.

4. Address practical barriers

Explore what might get in the way: time, cooking skills, family preferences, travel, social situations. Problem-solve together before issues arise.

5. Expect and plan for setbacks

Holiday gatherings, stressful weeks, and special occasions happen. Help clients develop "good-better-best" frameworks and self-compassion when they deviate from their plan.

Tracking Progress

Help clients notice the impact of dietary changes through:

  • Symptom journals: Track pain, fatigue, brain fog, digestive symptoms, skin, and mood
  • Inflammatory markers: Work with their doctor to monitor CRP, ESR, and condition-specific markers
  • Energy ratings: Simple 1-10 daily energy scores can reveal patterns
  • Before/after photos: Especially for skin conditions like psoriasis or eczema
  • Functional measures: Morning stiffness duration, grip strength, walking distance

Improvements may take 4-12 weeks to become apparent, so help clients maintain patience and consistency.

Key Takeaways

  • Food directly influences inflammation through fatty acid metabolism, blood sugar, gut microbiome, and immune triggering
  • Anti-inflammatory foods include fatty fish, colorful vegetables, berries, olive oil, nuts, and anti-inflammatory spices
  • Pro-inflammatory foods to minimize include refined sugars, industrial seed oils, processed meats, and ultra-processed foods
  • The Mediterranean diet is a well-researched, sustainable anti-inflammatory eating pattern
  • The Autoimmune Protocol (AIP) is a more restrictive elimination approach with growing research support
  • Gluten and dairy warrant special consideration in autoimmune conditions, though individual responses vary
  • Effective coaching starts with additions before subtractions and addresses practical barriers

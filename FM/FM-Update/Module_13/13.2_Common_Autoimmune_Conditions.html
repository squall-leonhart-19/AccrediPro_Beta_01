Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

Hashimoto's thyroiditis is the most common autoimmune disease, affecting up to 14 million Americans. It's also the most common cause of hypothyroidism. We covered this extensively in Module 11, but it's important to review it in the autoimmune context.

Hashimoto's Overview

üéØ What Happens

Immune cells attack the thyroid gland, causing gradual destruction and eventual hypothyroidism.

üìã Key Symptoms

Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, hair loss, constipation, brain fog, depression

üî¨ Diagnosis

Elevated TPO and/or thyroglobulin antibodies, often with elevated TSH and low T4/T3

üíä Conventional Treatment

Thyroid hormone replacement (levothyroxine); antibodies are typically not addressed

üåø Functional Approach

Address root causes (gut health, gluten sensitivity, nutrient deficiencies, stress) to potentially reduce antibodies and slow progression

Coach Tip

Many clients with Hashimoto's are told their thyroid levels are "normal" once on medication, yet they continue to feel unwell. This is often because the autoimmune component‚Äîthe ongoing inflammation and immune dysregulation‚Äîhasn't been addressed. When clients express frustration about feeling unwell despite "normal" labs, validate their experience and explore lifestyle modifications that can make a significant difference even when medication doesn't fully resolve symptoms.

Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic inflammatory condition that primarily attacks the lining of the joints (synovium), causing painful swelling that can eventually lead to joint erosion and deformity.

Primary Symptoms
  • Morning stiffness lasting >1 hour
  • Symmetrical joint pain (same joints on both sides)
  • Swelling in small joints (hands, wrists, feet)
  • Fatigue and malaise
  • May affect other organs (lungs, eyes, heart)
Diagnosis & Treatment
  • Positive RF (rheumatoid factor) and/or anti-CCP antibodies
  • Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR)
  • DMARDs (methotrexate) to slow progression
  • Biologics for moderate-severe cases
  • NSAIDs and steroids for symptom relief

Functional Medicine Considerations

1

Gut Connection

Strong association between gut dysbiosis and RA; studies show altered microbiome in RA patients

2

Dietary Triggers

Some patients respond dramatically to elimination diets, especially removing gluten and nightshades

3

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

High-dose fish oil has been shown to reduce joint pain and morning stiffness

4

Oral Health

Connection between periodontal disease (Porphyromonas gingivalis) and RA antibody production

5

Stress Management

Flares often follow periods of stress; stress management is essential

Coach Tip

When working with RA clients, help them track their symptoms alongside potential triggers. Create a simple symptom log that includes: pain levels, stiffness duration, foods eaten, stress levels, and sleep quality. Patterns often emerge within 4-6 weeks that can guide personalized nutrition and lifestyle interventions. This empowers clients to feel active participants in their healing rather than passive victims of disease.

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (Lupus)

Lupus is a systemic autoimmune disease that can affect almost any organ in the body. It's known for its unpredictable flares and wide range of symptoms, making it one of the most challenging autoimmune conditions to diagnose.

Lupus: Key Features

Demographics: 90% of cases are in women; most common in ages 15-45; higher rates in African American, Hispanic, and Asian women

Classic Symptoms:

ü¶ã Skin Manifestations

Butterfly-shaped facial rash (malar rash); photosensitivity (sun exposure triggers symptoms)

ü¶¥ Musculoskeletal

Joint pain and swelling; extreme fatigue

ü´Ä Organ Involvement

Kidney involvement (lupus nephritis); heart and lung effects

üíÜ Other Signs

Raynaud's phenomenon (fingers turn white/blue in cold); hair loss

Diagnosis: Positive ANA (antinuclear antibody), anti-dsDNA, and/or anti-Smith antibodies; low complement levels; clinical criteria

Treatment: Hydroxychloroquine (often first-line), steroids for flares, immunosuppressants for severe disease

Important for Coaches

Lupus can affect major organs including kidneys, heart, lungs, and brain. Clients with lupus need close medical supervision. Your role is to support their overall health through lifestyle optimization while respecting the need for medical management. Sun protection, stress management, and anti-inflammatory nutrition are supportive strategies within scope.

Coach Tip

Lupus clients often struggle with the unpredictability of flares. Help them develop a "flare action plan"‚Äîa written protocol they create during well periods that includes: rest priorities, gentle movement options, anti-inflammatory foods to emphasize, who to call for support, and stress management practices. Having this prepared reduces decision fatigue during flares and gives clients a sense of control over the uncontrollable.

Celiac Disease

Celiac disease is an autoimmune condition triggered by gluten (a protein in wheat, barley, and rye). When someone with celiac eats gluten, the immune system attacks the small intestinal lining, causing malabsorption and a wide range of symptoms.

GI Symptoms
  • Diarrhea or constipation
  • Bloating and gas
  • Abdominal pain
  • Nausea
  • Weight loss
Non-GI Symptoms
  • Iron-deficiency anemia (resistant to treatment)
  • Fatigue
  • Bone loss (osteoporosis)
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Neurological issues (ataxia, neuropathy)
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis (itchy skin rash)

Diagnosis and Treatment

  • Blood tests: Anti-tissue transglutaminase (tTG-IgA), deamidated gliadin peptide antibodies
  • Gold standard: Small intestinal biopsy showing villous atrophy (must be eating gluten at time of testing)
  • Treatment: Strict lifelong gluten-free diet (no medications; diet is the only treatment)
  • Even trace amounts of gluten can trigger intestinal damage and symptoms
  • Cross-contamination is a significant concern; requires careful food preparation
Celiac and Other Autoimmune Diseases

Celiac disease significantly increases the risk of other autoimmune conditions, especially Hashimoto's and type 1 diabetes. The shared mechanism appears to be gluten-triggered intestinal permeability and molecular mimicry. This is why many Hashimoto's patients benefit from gluten elimination‚Äîeven without diagnosed celiac disease.

Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is an autoimmune disease affecting the central nervous system. The immune system attacks the protective myelin sheath surrounding nerve fibers, disrupting communication between brain and body.

MS: Key Features

Demographics: 2-3 times more common in women; typically diagnosed between ages 20-50; more common in higher latitudes

Types of MS:

üîÑ Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS)

Most common type; flares followed by recovery periods

üìà Secondary Progressive MS

RRMS that becomes steadily progressive over time

‚¨ÜÔ∏è Primary Progressive MS

Steady decline from onset without distinct relapses

Common Symptoms: Numbness, tingling, weakness, vision problems, balance issues, fatigue, cognitive difficulties, bladder dysfunction

Diagnosis: MRI showing characteristic brain/spinal cord lesions; lumbar puncture; clinical criteria

Functional Medicine Considerations for MS

üåü Key Research Areas

Vitamin D

Low levels strongly associated with MS risk and progression; supplementation may be protective

Gut Microbiome

Distinct microbiome patterns in MS patients; gut health intervention is an active research area

EBV Connection

Epstein-Barr virus strongly linked to MS development

Dietary Protocols

Various protocols studied (Wahls Protocol, Swank Diet) with promising results in some patients

Exercise

Regular exercise is beneficial and safe for most MS patients; improves function and quality of life

Coach Tip

MS clients often fear exercise will worsen their condition. In reality, regular movement is one of the most evidence-based interventions for maintaining function and quality of life. Help clients find activities they enjoy‚Äîwater aerobics, tai chi, yoga, walking‚Äîand start conservatively. Heat sensitivity is common, so exercising in cool environments and staying well-hydrated is essential. Celebrate function maintenance as much as improvement.

Recognizing Autoimmune Patterns

Many clients arrive undiagnosed but with suspicious patterns. Recognizing these can help you encourage appropriate medical evaluation.

Red Flags That May Suggest Autoimmune Disease
  • Unexplained fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
  • Migratory joint or muscle pain that moves around the body
  • Morning stiffness lasting more than 30 minutes
  • Unexplained rashes or skin changes
  • Low-grade fever that comes and goes
  • Symptoms that wax and wane (flare and remit)
  • Family history of autoimmune disease
  • One autoimmune diagnosis‚Äîincreases likelihood of another
  • Multiple "unexplained" symptoms affecting different body systems
Coach Tip

When supporting clients with autoimmune conditions, remember these key principles: (1) Validate the invisible‚Äîmany autoimmune symptoms aren't visible, and clients often feel dismissed. (2) Understand medication importance‚Äîfor many conditions, medications prevent organ damage; don't position lifestyle as a replacement. (3) Focus on modifiable factors‚Äîdiet, gut health, stress, sleep, toxin exposure complement medical treatment. (4) Set realistic expectations‚Äîimprovement often takes months; autoimmune conditions aren't typically "cured" but can often be put into remission. (5) Watch for flares‚Äîhelp clients recognize patterns that precede flares (stress, poor sleep, certain foods) and intervene early.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

Why might a client with Hashimoto's thyroiditis continue to feel unwell even when their thyroid hormone levels are "normal" on medication?

Answer: Because the autoimmune component‚Äîthe ongoing inflammation and immune dysregulation‚Äîhasn't been addressed. Thyroid hormone replacement treats the hormone deficiency but doesn't stop the immune attack on the thyroid. Lifestyle modifications addressing root causes (gut health, gluten sensitivity, stress, nutrient deficiencies) can make a significant difference by reducing the autoimmune activity itself.

2

What are three distinguishing features that help identify rheumatoid arthritis versus other types of joint pain?

Answer: Three key distinguishing features of RA are: (1) Morning stiffness lasting more than one hour, (2) Symmetrical joint involvement (affecting the same joints on both sides of the body), and (3) Small joint involvement particularly in the hands, wrists, and feet. These features, combined with elevated inflammatory markers and positive RF or anti-CCP antibodies, help differentiate RA from osteoarthritis or other joint conditions.

3

Why is celiac disease unique among autoimmune conditions in terms of treatment?

Answer: Celiac disease is unique because diet is the only treatment‚Äîthere are no medications. A strict lifelong gluten-free diet is both necessary and sufficient to manage the condition. Even trace amounts of gluten can trigger intestinal damage and symptoms, so careful attention to cross-contamination is essential. This makes it one of the few autoimmune conditions where removing the trigger (gluten) can completely halt the autoimmune process.

4

What pattern should alert you to potentially refer a client for autoimmune evaluation?

Answer: Key patterns include: unexplained fatigue that doesn't improve with rest, migratory joint or muscle pain, morning stiffness lasting >30 minutes, symptoms that wax and wane (flare and remit), multiple unexplained symptoms affecting different body systems, family history of autoimmune disease, or one existing autoimmune diagnosis (which increases risk for others). When you see these patterns, especially in combination, encourage the client to discuss autoimmune screening with their healthcare provider.

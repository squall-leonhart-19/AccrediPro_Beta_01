Case Study 1: The Perimenopausal Professional

üë§ Client Profile: Jennifer, Age 49

Occupation: Corporate attorney, high-stress job with demanding schedule

Chief complaint: "I can't stay asleep. I wake up at 2-3 AM drenched in sweat with my mind racing. Then I'm exhausted all day."

History: Good sleeper until about 18 months ago. Menstrual cycle becoming irregular. Taking OTC melatonin (10mg) with minimal effect.

Current habits: Wine with dinner most nights "to relax," works on laptop until 10 PM, bedroom temperature 72¬∞F, exercises at 6 AM sporadically

Assessment Findings

Sleep Diary Analysis (2 weeks)

  • Average time in bed: 10 PM - 6 AM (8 hours)
  • Estimated total sleep: 5-6 hours (fragmented)
  • Sleep onset latency: 30-45 minutes
  • Awakenings: 2-4 per night, difficulty returning to sleep
  • Night sweats: 4-5 nights per week
  • Sleep efficiency: ~65%

Contributing Factors Identified

  1. Hormonal: Perimenopausal symptoms (night sweats, mood changes)
  2. Dietary: Evening alcohol disrupting sleep architecture
  3. Behavioral: Screen use until bedtime; bedroom too warm
  4. Psychological: Work stress, racing thoughts at night
  5. Supplement: Melatonin dose too high, potentially causing grogginess

Protocol Development

üìã Jennifer's Sleep Optimization Protocol

Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2): Environmental + High-Impact Changes

  • Lower bedroom temperature to 65-67¬∞F
  • Add cooling mattress pad and moisture-wicking sheets
  • Replace 10mg melatonin with 0.5mg, taken at 9 PM
  • Reduce alcohol to 2 nights per week maximum, finish by 7 PM
  • Digital sunset at 9 PM‚Äîno screens for 1 hour before bed

Phase 2 (Weeks 3-4): Add Nutritional + Stress Support

  • Magnesium glycinate 300mg at 9 PM
  • L-theanine 200mg as needed for racing thoughts
  • Evening wind-down routine: 15 minutes journaling/brain dump at 9:15 PM
  • 4-7-8 breathing when waking at night

Phase 3 (Weeks 5-8): Refine + Hormonal Support

  • Discuss HRT with her gynecologist (provided referral information)
  • Morning light exposure within 30 minutes of waking (outdoor walk or light box)
  • Consistent exercise schedule‚Äî3x/week minimum
  • Evaluate progress and adjust

Results

üìà Jennifer's Progress

Week 2: Night sweats reduced by 50% with temperature changes. Still waking but returning to sleep faster.
Week 4: Alcohol reduction showed dramatic improvement‚Äîon alcohol-free nights, sleeping 6-7 hours. Racing thoughts improved with journaling and L-theanine.
Week 8: Started low-dose HRT after discussing with doctor. Night sweats nearly resolved. Sleeping 7 hours most nights. Sleep efficiency improved to 85%. "I feel like myself again."

üí° Key Lessons from This Case

  • Multiple factors were contributing‚Äîaddressing just one wouldn't have worked
  • Alcohol was a bigger factor than Jennifer realized
  • High-dose melatonin was counterproductive
  • Hormonal support (HRT) provided the final piece
  • Phased approach prevented overwhelm and allowed tracking of what helped

Case Study 2: The Conditioned Insomniac

üë§ Client Profile: Michael, Age 42

Occupation: Software developer, works from home

Chief complaint: "I've been an insomniac for 8 years. I dread going to bed because I know I'll just lie there for hours."

History: Insomnia began after a stressful work period. Has tried multiple sleep medications with diminishing returns. Now takes trazodone nightly but still struggling.

Current habits: Goes to bed at 9 PM hoping to maximize sleep time, lies in bed until 7 AM. Uses bedroom as home office. Reads news on phone in bed to "tire himself out."

Assessment Findings

Sleep Pattern Analysis

  • Time in bed: 10 hours (9 PM - 7 AM)
  • Sleep onset latency: 2-3 hours
  • Estimated sleep time: 4-5 hours
  • Multiple awakenings with difficulty returning to sleep
  • Sleep efficiency: ~45%
  • Extreme anxiety about sleep‚Äîrates "dread of bedtime" 9/10

Root Causes Identified

  1. Conditioned arousal: Bed associated with frustration and wakefulness (8 years of conditioning)
  2. Excessive time in bed: Diluting sleep, perpetuating low efficiency
  3. Stimulus control violation: Works in bedroom, uses phone in bed
  4. Sleep anxiety: Catastrophic thoughts about consequences of not sleeping
  5. Sleep effort: Trying too hard to sleep, creating arousal

Protocol: Modified CBT-I Approach

üìã Michael's Sleep Restoration Protocol

Phase 1: Stimulus Control (Weeks 1-2)

  • Move work setup out of bedroom (dining table became office)
  • Bed for sleep and intimacy only‚Äîno phone, no reading in bed
  • If awake more than 20 minutes, get up and go to another room
  • Return to bed only when sleepy
  • No clock-watching (turn clocks away)

Phase 2: Sleep Restriction (Weeks 3-6)

  • Initial sleep window: 12:30 AM - 6:30 AM (6 hours, based on estimated sleep time + buffer)
  • Fixed wake time at 6:30 AM regardless of sleep quality
  • No napping
  • Expand by 15 minutes when sleep efficiency reaches 85%+ for 5 days

Cognitive Restructuring (Ongoing)

  • Identify catastrophic thoughts: "I'll never sleep" ‚Üí "I've slept before and will again"
  • "Tomorrow will be ruined" ‚Üí "I've functioned on less sleep; one night won't destroy me"
  • Shift from controlling sleep to allowing sleep
  • Scheduled "worry time" at 8 PM (not bedtime)

Wind-Down Routine (9 PM onwards)

  • Dim lights throughout home
  • Relaxation activities: reading (not in bed), stretching, bath
  • Don't go to bed until genuinely sleepy (even if past midnight initially)

Results

üìà Michael's Progress

Week 1-2: Challenging. Very tired during the day. But falling asleep faster because genuinely exhausted by 12:30 AM. Started to break bed-wakefulness association.
Week 4: Sleep efficiency reached 85%. Expanded sleep window to 12:15 AM - 6:30 AM. Anxiety about bed decreasing. "I actually feel sleepy when I go to bed now."
Week 8: Sleep window 11:30 PM - 6:30 AM (7 hours). Sleeping 6-6.5 hours most nights. Sleep efficiency 88%. Began tapering trazodone with doctor support.
Week 12: Off trazodone. Sleep window 11 PM - 6:30 AM. No longer dreads bedtime. "I had forgotten what normal sleep felt like."

üí° Key Lessons from This Case

  • 8 years of conditioned insomnia required behavioral intervention, not supplements
  • Excessive time in bed was perpetuating the problem
  • Sleep restriction is powerful but requires commitment through initial discomfort
  • Cognitive work was essential‚Äîchanging relationship with sleep
  • Medication taper was possible once natural sleep ability was restored

Case Study 3: The Blood Sugar Roller Coaster

üë§ Client Profile: Diana, Age 55

Occupation: Elementary school teacher

Chief complaint: "I wake up every night between 2-3 AM with my heart pounding and feeling anxious. Sometimes I'm sweating. It takes an hour or more to calm down."

History: Problem started about 2 years ago. No hot flashes otherwise (post-menopausal). Takes lisinopril for blood pressure. Pre-diabetic (HbA1c 5.9%).

Current habits: Late dinner (7:30-8 PM), often pasta or rice-based. Glass of wine with dinner. Occasional cookie or ice cream after. Usually asleep by 10 PM.

Assessment Findings

Pattern Recognition

  • Waking consistently at 2-3 AM (4-5 hours post-dinner)
  • Symptoms: racing heart, anxiety, sometimes sweating
  • Worse on nights with higher carb dinner + wine + dessert
  • Better on nights with protein-heavy dinner, no dessert
  • Pre-diabetic status indicates poor glucose regulation

Hypothesis: Reactive Hypoglycemia

High-carb dinner (especially with wine) ‚Üí blood sugar spike ‚Üí insulin surge ‚Üí blood sugar crash at 2-3 AM ‚Üí body releases cortisol and adrenaline to raise glucose ‚Üí sudden awakening with anxiety, racing heart, sweating

Protocol: Blood Sugar Stabilization Focus

üìã Diana's Protocol

Dinner Modifications

  • Move dinner earlier (6-6:30 PM when possible)
  • Reduce refined carbohydrates; emphasize protein, vegetables, healthy fats
  • If having carbs, choose lower glycemic options with fiber
  • Eliminate or significantly reduce wine (alcohol worsens blood sugar instability)
  • No dessert‚Äîor if desired, small portion with protein (e.g., cheese with a few berries)

Optional Bedtime Snack

  • Small protein-fat snack 30 minutes before bed to stabilize overnight glucose
  • Options: handful of nuts, cheese and crackers, Greek yogurt, nut butter

Track and Correlate

  • Food diary noting dinner composition, timing, alcohol
  • Sleep diary noting waking times, symptoms
  • Look for patterns connecting food choices to sleep quality

Results

üìà Diana's Progress

Week 1: On nights with modified dinners (lower carb, earlier timing, no wine), waking reduced significantly. Pattern became undeniable.
Week 3: Sleeping through most nights. Occasional waking on "cheat" nights (pasta + wine) confirmed the connection.
Week 6: Consistent sleep. Added magnesium glycinate for additional blood sugar and relaxation support. Lost 4 pounds as a bonus. "I never would have connected my diet to my 3 AM waking."

üí° Key Lessons from This Case

  • Blood sugar dysregulation is an underappreciated cause of middle-of-night waking
  • Pattern recognition (timing of waking, associated symptoms) pointed to the cause
  • Dietary modification was the primary intervention‚Äîno supplements needed initially
  • Pre-diabetic status was a clue to impaired glucose regulation
  • Tracking correlation between food and sleep was powerful for client insight

Your Sleep Assessment Framework

Use this framework when working with sleep clients:

üîç Comprehensive Sleep Assessment

1. Sleep Pattern Mapping

  • What time do they go to bed and wake up (weekdays/weekends)?
  • How long to fall asleep?
  • How many times do they wake? For how long?
  • What's their total sleep time vs. time in bed?
  • Calculate sleep efficiency

2. Root Cause Exploration

  • Hormonal: Menstrual cycle phase? Perimenopausal symptoms? Thyroid issues?
  • Dietary: Caffeine (timing/amount)? Alcohol? Dinner composition and timing?
  • Behavioral: Screen use? Bedroom environment? Schedule consistency?
  • Psychological: Stress levels? Anxiety about sleep? Racing thoughts?
  • Medical: Pain? Medications? Snoring/apnea symptoms?

3. Pattern Recognition

  • When did the problem start? What was happening then?
  • What makes it better or worse?
  • Any patterns (time of night, day of week, menstrual cycle)?

4. Protocol Development

  • Prioritize highest-impact, most achievable changes first
  • Phase implementation to track what helps
  • Match intervention to root cause (don't give CBT-I to someone with blood sugar issues)
  • Include client in decision-making for better adherence

üö® When to Refer

Refer to medical providers or sleep specialists when you identify:

  • Sleep apnea symptoms: Loud snoring, witnessed breathing pauses, gasping, excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep time
  • Restless leg syndrome: Uncomfortable urge to move legs, worse at rest/evening
  • Parasomnias: Sleepwalking, acting out dreams, confusional arousals
  • Narcolepsy symptoms: Sudden sleep attacks, cataplexy, sleep paralysis
  • Severe, treatment-resistant insomnia unresponsive to behavioral approaches
  • Mental health crises: Severe depression, suicidal ideation
  • Medication concerns: Need for sleep medication management

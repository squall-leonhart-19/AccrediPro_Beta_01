Red Flags: When to Refer

Recognize warning signs that require immediate medical attention or specialist care

üîó Connection: You've learned to gather health histories, build timelines, and recognize symptom patterns. Now comes one of the most critical skills: knowing when something is beyond your scope and requires immediate medical attention. This lesson could save a life.

üéØ Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify red flag symptoms that require emergency medical care
  • Recognize warning signs that warrant urgent physician evaluation
  • Understand which presentations are outside the health coaching scope
  • Develop appropriate referral language and processes
  • Balance thorough assessment with knowing when not to proceed

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Understanding

As a health coach, you are not diagnosing or treating medical conditions. Your role is to support health optimization through lifestyle and behavior change. When you encounter red flags, your job is to refer‚Äînot to investigate, treat, or reassure. Missing a red flag can have serious or fatal consequences. When in doubt, always refer.

Emergency Red Flags: Call 911 Immediately

These symptoms require emergency medical services. If a client reports any of these during a session, stop the session and advise them to call 911 or go to the emergency room immediately:

üö® EMERGENCY: Immediate Medical Attention Required

Cardiac/Cardiovascular
  • Chest pain or pressure, especially with arm/jaw pain, shortness of breath, sweating
  • Sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life")
  • Sudden difficulty speaking, facial drooping, one-sided weakness (stroke signs)
  • Severe shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
Neurological
  • Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
  • Seizures (especially first-time or prolonged)
  • Sudden confusion, disorientation, or altered mental status
  • Sudden vision changes or vision loss
Other Emergencies
  • Severe abdominal pain, especially if rigid abdomen
  • Vomiting blood or blood in stool (bright red or black/tarry)
  • Severe allergic reaction (swelling, difficulty breathing, hives)
  • Active suicidal ideation with plan or intent
  • Signs of severe dehydration (no urine, extreme thirst, confusion)

Urgent Red Flags: Same-Day Medical Evaluation

These symptoms warrant same-day evaluation by a physician, though not necessarily emergency care:

üî∂ URGENT: Same-Day Medical Evaluation Needed

Infection/Fever
  • Fever over 103¬∞F (39.4¬∞C) or fever persisting more than 3 days
  • Signs of serious infection: high fever, confusion, rapid breathing
  • Urinary symptoms with fever or back pain (possible kidney infection)
Digestive
  • Severe abdominal pain that's worsening
  • Blood in stool or vomit
  • Inability to keep any food or fluids down for 24+ hours
  • Severe diarrhea with signs of dehydration
Pain
  • Sudden severe headache unlike previous headaches
  • Severe pain in any location that's new and unexplained
  • Testicular pain (possible torsion‚Äîemergency in males)
Mental Health
  • Suicidal thoughts (even without plan‚Äîneeds immediate professional support)
  • Psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, severe paranoia)
  • Severe panic or anxiety that's uncontrollable

Concerning Signs: Prompt Medical Follow-Up

These findings warrant medical evaluation within days to a couple of weeks‚Äînot emergent, but shouldn't be ignored:

‚ö° CONCERNING: Schedule Medical Evaluation Soon

Unexplained Changes
  • Unintentional weight loss (>5% in a month)
  • Night sweats (drenching, not hot flashes)
  • Persistent fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
  • New lumps or masses anywhere
  • Changes in moles (size, shape, color)
Digestive Concerns
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)
  • Persistent change in bowel habits
  • Blood in stool (even if intermittent)
  • Persistent abdominal pain
  • Unexplained nausea/vomiting
Neurological Concerns
  • Progressive weakness or numbness
  • Persistent headaches (new pattern)
  • Memory problems that are worsening
  • Balance problems or coordination changes
Other Concerns
  • Persistent cough (>3 weeks)
  • Shortness of breath with normal activities
  • Breast changes (lumps, nipple discharge)
  • Abnormal bleeding (vaginal, rectal)
  • Skin changes that won't heal

Beyond Your Scope: When to Refer to Specialists

Even without emergent red flags, certain situations require specialist involvement:

Active Eating Disorders

If you suspect anorexia, bulimia, binge eating disorder, or orthorexia, refer to an eating disorder specialist. These conditions can be life-threatening and require specialized treatment.

Uncontrolled Chronic Conditions

Diabetes with unstable blood sugars, uncontrolled hypertension, active autoimmune flares, or any chronic condition that isn't being medically managed needs physician involvement first.

Psychiatric Conditions

Depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, PTSD, and other mental health conditions require proper diagnosis and treatment from mental health professionals. You can support alongside but not replace this care.

Pregnancy

Pregnant clients need prenatal care from qualified providers. While you can support general wellness, nutrition recommendations and any supplement guidance should come from their prenatal team.

Cancer (Active or Suspected)

Any cancer warning signs or active cancer treatment require oncology care. You can support quality of life but must work in coordination with the medical team.

Medication Changes

Never advise clients to change, stop, or start medications. Even when lifestyle changes might reduce medication need, that decision belongs to their prescribing physician.

How to Have the Referral Conversation

When you identify a red flag, communicate clearly and supportively:

üí¨ Referral Language Examples

For Emergency Situations:

"What you're describing sounds like it could be serious and needs immediate medical attention. I need you to hang up and call 911 right now, or have someone drive you to the emergency room. This isn't something we should try to manage in our coaching session. Please go now, and text me when you're safe so I know you're okay."

For Urgent Concerns:

"I'm concerned about what you've shared. This is something that needs to be evaluated by a doctor today. Can you contact your physician's office now, or would you feel comfortable going to an urgent care clinic? I'd feel better if you got this checked before our next session."

For Concerning Symptoms:

"The symptoms you're describing‚Äîthe unexplained weight loss and night sweats‚Äîare things that really need to be medically evaluated. This isn't something I can address as a health coach. I'd like you to schedule an appointment with your doctor within the next week or two to get this checked out. Will you do that?"

For Scope Issues:

"What you're dealing with is beyond what I can help with as a health coach. You need to work with a [specialist type] to properly address this. I can absolutely continue to support you with lifestyle factors alongside that care, but they need to be involved. Would you like help finding a good provider?"

Mental Health Red Flags: Special Considerations

Mental health deserves special attention. As a health coach, you may be the first person clients open up to about their struggles:

üß† Mental Health Assessment Points

üö® Immediate Action Required:
  • Active suicidal thoughts with plan or intent
  • Self-harm behavior (current)
  • Homicidal thoughts
  • Psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions)
üî∂ Prompt Referral Needed:
  • Passive suicidal ideation ("I wish I wasn't here")
  • Severe depression interfering with daily function
  • Panic attacks or severe anxiety
  • Signs of trauma response affecting daily life
  • Substance abuse concerns
üí° What to Say:

"Thank you for trusting me with this. What you're experiencing is real and important, and you deserve support from someone trained to help with this specifically. I care about you, which is why I want to connect you with a therapist/counselor who can really help. I can still support you with your health goals, but this piece needs specialized care. Would you be open to that?"

üîë Key Takeaways

  • Know emergency red flags (chest pain, stroke signs, severe bleeding) and respond immediately
  • Recognize concerning symptoms (unexplained weight loss, night sweats, new lumps) that need medical evaluation
  • Certain conditions (eating disorders, active psychiatric illness, pregnancy, cancer) require specialist involvement
  • Never advise clients to change medications‚Äîthat's outside your scope
  • Have clear, supportive language ready for referral conversations
  • Build a referral network before you need it
  • When in doubt, refer. It's always better to be overly cautious than to miss something serious.

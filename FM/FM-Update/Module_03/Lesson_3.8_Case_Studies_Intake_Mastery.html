Case Study 1: The Exhausted Executive

üìã Client Profile: Susan, 48

Chief complaint: "I'm exhausted all the time. I can't think straight anymore."

Family: Married, two teenagers

Current Symptoms (from questionnaire):

Medical History:

Works 60+ hours/week. Skips breakfast, salad at desk for lunch, wine and dinner late. Coffee-dependent (4-5 cups). Exercise: "when I have time" (rarely). Sleep: 5-6 hours, disrupted.

Age 20s

Age 33

üîç Pattern Recognition & Clinical Thinking

Symptom Clustering:

  • Thyroid pattern: Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, hair thinning, constipation, low mood, brain fog‚Äîclassic hypothyroid presentation
  • Perimenopause pattern: Age 48, irregular periods, sleep disruption, mood changes
  • HPA dysfunction pattern: High stress, coffee dependence, 2-3am waking, afternoon crash
  • Blood sugar pattern: History of gestational diabetes, skips breakfast, energy crashes, sugar cravings

Likely Root Issues: Thyroid dysfunction (needs full panel‚Äînot just TSH), perimenopausal hormone shifts, chronic stress/HPA dysregulation, blood sugar instability. Family history of Hashimoto's increases autoimmune thyroid likelihood.

  1. Medical referral: Recommend full thyroid panel (TSH, free T4, free T3, TPO antibodies, TG antibodies) with functional medicine-informed practitioner. "Borderline" needs deeper investigation.
  2. Blood sugar stabilization: Start with breakfast‚Äîprotein + fat within 1 hour of waking. This alone may improve morning energy and afternoon crashes.
  3. Sleep priority: Move toward 10:30pm bedtime, reduce evening screen time. The 2-3am waking suggests cortisol dysregulation.
  4. Stress acknowledgment: Discussed that lifestyle factors are perpetuating symptoms even if thyroid is an issue. Agreed to explore work-life boundaries in future sessions.

Case Study 2: The Gut-Brain Mystery

üìã Client Profile: Michael, 35

Chief complaint: "Anxiety and panic attacks that came out of nowhere"
Occupation: Software developer (remote)
Family: Single, lives alone
Referral source: Therapist who suspected physical contributors

üìù Health History Highlights

Current Symptoms:
Panic attacks Generalized anxiety Bloating after eating Alternating constipation/diarrhea Brain fog Fatigue Joint pain (new) Skin breakouts
Key Intake Question Explored:

When asked "Did anything happen before the anxiety started?", Michael remembered severe food poisoning during a Mexico trip 18 months ago. He was sick for 10 days. "I thought I recovered, but thinking about it, my gut has never been the same since."

Lifestyle:

Diet: Eats out frequently, lots of takeout, high processed food. Exercise: sedentary (works from home). Sleep: 6-7 hours, decent quality. Stress: moderate‚Äîenjoys his job.

üìä Timeline Construction

Childhood-Age 33

No anxiety, no mental health issues, occasional upset stomach but generally healthy

Age 33.5 (18 months ago)

TRIGGER: Severe food poisoning in Mexico. 10 days of GI illness. Antibiotics prescribed.

~3 months later

Chronic bloating and irregular bowel movements began. Dismissed as "sensitive stomach."

~6 months later

Brain fog appeared. Started feeling anxious "for no reason." First panic attack.

~12 months later (6 months ago)

Joint pain and skin breakouts developed. Anxiety worsening. Started therapy.

üîç Pattern Recognition & Clinical Thinking

The Gut-Brain Connection is Textbook:

  • Clear trigger: Post-infectious GI illness‚Äîclassic setup for SIBO, dysbiosis, or chronic infection
  • Cascading symptoms: GI symptoms ‚Üí brain fog and mood symptoms ‚Üí systemic inflammation (joints, skin)
  • Gut-brain axis: 90% of serotonin is made in the gut. Dysbiosis disrupts neurotransmitter production.
  • No psychological trauma trigger: Anxiety appeared "out of nowhere" with no life stressor‚Äîpoints to physiological origin

Suspected Root Issue: Post-infectious gut dysbiosis causing intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"), systemic inflammation, and disrupted neurotransmitter production. The anxiety is a symptom of gut dysfunction, not a primary psychological disorder.

‚úÖ Session Outcome & Next Steps

  1. Education: Explained gut-brain connection‚ÄîMichael was relieved to hear his anxiety might have a physical cause. This reframe was therapeutic in itself.
  2. Referral: Recommended functional GI testing (comprehensive stool analysis, possibly SIBO breath test) through functional medicine practitioner.
  3. Dietary foundation: Begin reducing processed foods, increasing whole foods. Will introduce structured gut protocol after testing.
  4. Continue therapy: Encouraged him to continue with therapist‚Äîthe anxiety is real and needs support regardless of cause.
  5. Tracking: Food-mood-symptom diary to identify patterns and triggers.

Case Study 3: The Red Flag Recognition

üìã Client Profile: Diana, 52

Chief complaint: "Fatigue and weight loss‚ÄîI want help optimizing my diet"
Occupation: Teacher
Session type: Initial intake (virtual)

üö® Assessment Reveals Concerns

During the intake, Diana mentioned she'd lost 15 pounds over 3 months "without really trying." Further questioning revealed:

  • Unintentional weight loss of 15 lbs (about 8% body weight)
  • Night sweats (not typical hot flashes‚Äî"drenching")
  • Persistent fatigue unrelieved by rest
  • Change in bowel habits (new constipation alternating with loose stools)
  • Occasional blood when wiping (she'd been dismissing this)

‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flag Recognition: Multiple concerning findings: unintentional significant weight loss, night sweats, bowel changes, and rectal bleeding. This cluster requires immediate medical evaluation‚Äînot dietary optimization.

üí¨ How the Coach Responded

"Diana, I appreciate you sharing all of this with me. I need to pause our conversation about diet optimization because some of what you're describing concerns me. Unintentional weight loss, night sweats, changes in bowel habits, and especially any blood‚Äîthese are things that need to be evaluated by a doctor before we do anything else. This isn't about being alarmist, but these symptoms together warrant a thorough medical workup. When did you last see your primary care doctor?"

Diana admitted she'd been avoiding the doctor, hoping it was "just stress." The coach:

  • Validated her feelings while firmly maintaining the referral
  • Explained that ruling out serious causes is the first step before any coaching work
  • Offered to continue working with her after medical clearance
  • Followed up in writing to document the recommendation

üìã Outcome

Diana saw her doctor that week. Testing revealed early-stage colorectal cancer, caught at a treatable stage. She later thanked the coach‚Äîif she'd just gotten "diet tips," she might have delayed medical care further. The intake assessment potentially saved her life.

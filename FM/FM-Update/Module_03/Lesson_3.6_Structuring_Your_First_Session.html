The Two First Sessions

When Structure Creates Experience

Two coaches met with the same client. Coach A dove straight into questions, checked boxes on their intake form, offered a few suggestions, and ended when time ran out. The client left informed but disconnected‚Äîlike data had been extracted but nothing had been exchanged.

Coach B started by asking what the client was hoping to get from working together. She listened deeply, reflected back what she heard, then collaboratively explored the health history. She ended with a clear summary, a tangible next step, and a moment of acknowledgment.

Both coaches gathered the same information. But the second client left feeling seen, hopeful, and committed to the process. The structure was similar‚Äîbut the experience was transformed by intentional session design.

Goals of the First Session

Your initial session must accomplish multiple objectives simultaneously. Keep these goals in mind as you design your session flow:

1

Build Rapport & Trust

Create safety and connection that makes the client want to return and open up.

2

Gather Key Information

Collect the data you need to understand their health picture and guide recommendations.

3

Understand Their "Why"

Discover their deeper motivations‚Äîwhat really matters to them about their health.

4

Set Expectations

Clarify how coaching works, your scope, what they can expect, and what you need from them.

5

Create Initial Direction

Give them at least one tangible next step‚Äîsomething achievable to build momentum.

6

Instill Hope

Help them believe change is possible‚Äîthat they came to the right place.

Coach Tip

Print these six goals and keep them visible during your first sessions. Before each session, ask yourself: "How will I accomplish each of these today?" This keeps you focused on outcomes, not just process.

Before the Session: Preparation

Great first sessions start before the client arrives. Here's your preparation checklist:

Pre-Session Preparation Checklist
  • Send intake forms in advance ‚Äî Allow 48+ hours for completion. Include health history questionnaire, informed consent, and any symptom assessments.
  • Review completed paperwork ‚Äî Read through their questionnaire thoroughly. Note areas to explore, potential patterns, and any red flags.
  • Prepare your questions ‚Äî Based on their intake, what do you want to clarify or explore further?
  • Set up your space ‚Äî Whether virtual or in-person, minimize distractions. Test technology. Have water available.
  • Center yourself ‚Äî Take 2-3 minutes before the session to clear your mind and become fully present.
  • Have timeline tools ready ‚Äî Paper or digital timeline template to build during the session.
Coach Tip

Create a "pre-session ritual" that helps you transition into coach mode. This might be three deep breaths, reviewing your coaching values, or a brief meditation. Consistency builds presence.

The First Session Structure: 75-90 Minutes

Here's a proven structure for initial intake sessions. Adjust timing based on your session length and client needs:

Initial Session Structure (90-minute example)

5-10 min: Welcome & Connection

Warm greeting, brief chat to settle in, thank them for completing paperwork. Set a positive tone and help them relax.

5-10 min: Set the Agenda & Expectations

Explain what you'll cover today, how the session will flow, and what coaching is/isn't. Get their buy-in on the plan.

10-15 min: Explore Their Vision & Goals

What brought them here? What do they hope to achieve? What would be different if their health improved? Understand their "why."

30-40 min: Health History Deep Dive

This is the heart of the intake. Review questionnaire responses, ask clarifying questions, build the timeline, identify patterns. Listen more than you speak.

10-15 min: Synthesis & Initial Observations

Share what you're noticing‚Äîpatterns, connections, areas of focus. Check their reaction. Don't prescribe yet; offer observations.

10-15 min: Next Steps & Action Planning

Collaborate on 1-3 initial action items. Make them specific and achievable. Identify any referrals needed. Discuss homework or tracking.

5 min: Closing & Scheduling

Summarize the session, schedule next appointment, express confidence in their journey. Leave them feeling hopeful and clear.

Time Management Insight

Notice that the health history deep dive gets the most time (30-40 minutes of a 90-minute session). This is intentional. The quality of your assessment determines everything that follows. Don't rush this section.

Key Phrases for Each Section

Having prepared language helps you navigate the session smoothly. Here are proven phrases for each section:

Setting the Agenda

"Here's what I'm thinking for today: I'd love to hear what brought you here and what you're hoping to achieve. Then we'll walk through your health history together, and I'll share some initial observations. We'll wrap up with next steps. Does that sound good?"

Exploring Their Vision

"If you imagine yourself 6 months from now, and things have really improved‚Äîwhat's different? What are you doing that you can't do now? How do you feel?"

Transitioning to History

"Thank you for sharing that. Now I'd love to understand your health journey‚Äîhow we got here. I've reviewed your questionnaire, but I want to hear the story in your own words. Where would you say your health challenges really began?"

Sharing Observations

"Based on everything you've shared, here's what I'm noticing... [pattern]. Does that resonate with you? What stands out to you as we look at this together?"

Closing

"We've covered a lot today. You've got [summarize action items]. I want you to know‚Äîbased on what we've discussed, I really believe you can make meaningful progress. What's one thing you're taking away from today?"

Coach Tip

Write these phrases on index cards or keep them visible during your first few sessions. Over time, they'll become natural. The goal isn't to sound scripted‚Äîit's to have helpful language ready when you need it.

Common First-Session Challenges (and Solutions)

Even with solid preparation, challenges arise. Here's how to handle the most common ones:

Challenge: Client Goes Off-Topic Repeatedly

Solution: Gently redirect with empathy. "That's really important, and I want to make sure we come back to it. Right now, I want to make sure we have time to cover [X]. Can we note that and return to it?" Use the agenda you set as an anchor.

Challenge: Client is Overwhelmed and Emotional

Solution: Slow down. Acknowledge the emotion. "I can see this is bringing up a lot. Let's pause for a moment. This matters." Give them space. Sometimes the most valuable thing is being witnessed, not assessed.

Challenge: Running Out of Time

Solution: Give a time check at the halfway point. "We have about 30 minutes left. Let me make sure we cover the most important things." Prioritize closing with clarity and next steps‚Äîdon't just end abruptly.

Challenge: Client Wants Immediate Answers

Solution: Validate their eagerness while setting realistic expectations. "I can see you're ready to take action‚Äîthat's great. Today is about understanding the full picture so we can make the right moves. I'll have more specific recommendations after we complete our assessment."

Challenge: Client is Skeptical or Guarded

Solution: Don't push. Ask about their previous experiences. "It sounds like you've tried a lot of things before. What's worked? What hasn't? What would make this experience different for you?" Meet skepticism with curiosity, not defense.

Coach Tip

When a client becomes emotional, resist the urge to fix or move on quickly. Your willingness to stay present with their emotion builds profound trust. Simply saying "I'm here with you" can be more powerful than any intervention.

Setting Up for Ongoing Success

Use your first session to establish patterns that support the coaching relationship moving forward:

Agreements to Establish in Session 1

Session Logistics: Meeting frequency, session length, how to reschedule, communication between sessions

Preparation Expectations: What you need from them before sessions (food logs, tracking data, reflections)

Homework Approach: How action items work, what happens if they don't complete them, importance of honesty

Scope Clarity: What you can and cannot do as a health coach, when referrals happen, how you work with their other providers

Communication Norms: How they can reach you between sessions, expected response times, what constitutes an emergency

Pause & Reflect

Think about the best "first appointment" experience you've ever had with any professional (doctor, therapist, consultant). What made it memorable? What did they do that made you feel confident in working with them? How can you create that same experience for your clients?

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What are the six primary goals of a first coaching session?

Answer: The six goals are: (1) Build rapport and trust, (2) Gather key information, (3) Understand their "why," (4) Set expectations, (5) Create initial direction, (6) Instill hope. Each goal is essential‚Äîmiss one and you risk either incomplete assessment or weak client engagement.

2

In a 90-minute first session, approximately how much time should be dedicated to the health history deep dive, and why?

Answer: 30-40 minutes should be dedicated to the health history deep dive‚Äîthe most time of any section. This is the heart of the intake where you review questionnaire responses, ask clarifying questions, build the timeline, and identify patterns. The quality of your assessment determines everything that follows.

3

A client keeps going off-topic during the first session. What's an effective way to redirect while maintaining rapport?

Answer: Gently redirect with empathy: "That's really important, and I want to make sure we come back to it. Right now, I want to make sure we have time to cover [X]. Can we note that and return to it?" Use the agenda you set at the beginning as an anchor. This acknowledges their concern while maintaining structure.

4

What are three key agreements you should establish with clients during the first session to support ongoing success?

Answer: Key agreements to establish include: (1) Session logistics (frequency, length, rescheduling), (2) Preparation expectations (what you need from them before sessions), and (3) Scope clarity (what you can/cannot do as a health coach, when referrals happen). Also important: homework approach and communication norms.

Key Takeaways
  • Prepare thoroughly before the session by reviewing intake forms and planning questions
  • Follow a structured flow: welcome ‚Üí agenda ‚Üí vision ‚Üí history ‚Üí synthesis ‚Üí action ‚Üí close
  • The health history deep dive is the core‚Äîdedicate the most time here (30-40 minutes)
  • Always end with clarity: summarized observations, concrete next steps, scheduled follow-up
  • Handle challenges (tangents, emotions, time pressure) with empathy and gentle redirection
  • Your goal is information AND experience‚Äîclients should leave feeling seen, hopeful, and clear
  • Use the first session to establish agreements and patterns that support the ongoing coaching relationship

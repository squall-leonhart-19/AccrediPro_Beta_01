A Coach's Experience

Maria, 46, came to coaching for help with depression and weight gain. She'd been on an antidepressant for three years with partial improvement. When her coach explored her sleep, Maria admitted she averaged 5-6 hours nightly, woke multiple times, and never felt rested despite being exhausted. "I thought sleep problems were just part of being busy and getting older," she said. Her coach explained that inadequate sleep could be driving both her depression and weight issues. After a sleep study revealed moderate sleep apnea, Maria started CPAP treatment. Simultaneously, she worked with her coach on sleep hygiene: consistent bed times, dark cool room, no screens after 8pm, and a wind-down routine. Within two months of addressing her sleep, Maria's depression lifted significantlyâ€”her psychiatrist reduced her medication. She had more energy to exercise, made better food choices, and the weight started coming off. "I never realized sleep was the foundation holding everything else back," she reflected.

Sleep Disorders Affecting Mental Health

Insomnia

Insomniaâ€”difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking too earlyâ€”affects 30-40% of adults at some point. Chronic insomnia (3+ months) is a major risk factor for depression and anxiety. The gold-standard treatment is Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I), which is more effective long-term than sleeping pills.

Sleep Apnea

Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) causes repeated oxygen drops during sleep that fragment sleep architecture and stress the body. Symptoms include:

  • Snoring, gasping, or witnessed breathing pauses
  • Waking unrefreshed despite adequate time in bed
  • Morning headaches
  • Daytime fatigue and sleepiness
  • Cognitive impairment and brain fog
  • Mood changes, irritability, depression

OSA is commonly undiagnosed, especially in women (who present differently than men). It's associated with significantly increased risk of depression, anxiety, and cognitive decline. Treatment (CPAP, oral appliances, or positional therapy) often dramatically improves mental health symptoms.

Circadian Rhythm Disorders

When the body's internal clock is misaligned with the external world (from shift work, jet lag, delayed sleep phase, or irregular schedules), mental health suffers. Shift workers have 40% higher rates of depression. Proper light exposureâ€”bright light in the morning, dim light at nightâ€”helps realign circadian rhythms.

Beyond Basic Sleep Hygiene

While sleep hygiene is foundational, clients with significant sleep issues often need more targeted interventions:

Advanced Sleep Strategies

Wind-Down Routine (30-60 minutes)

The transition from activity to sleep is crucial. Create a consistent pre-bed routine: dim lights, take a warm bath/shower (the subsequent cool-down triggers drowsiness), gentle stretching or yoga, reading (not on screens), journaling, or meditation. Avoid stimulating conversations, work, or news.

Manage the Racing Mind

For anxious sleepers: keep a "worry journal" earlier in the evening to download thoughts. Use a to-do list to capture tasks so the brain can release them. Try progressive muscle relaxation or body scan meditation. Consider a "mindfulness for sleep" audio program.

Stimulus Control

Only use bed for sleep and intimacy. If awake for more than 20 minutes, get up and do something quiet in dim light until drowsy. This re-associates the bed with sleep rather than wakefulness and worry.

Sleep Restriction (with caution)

For chronic insomnia, temporarily limiting time in bed can consolidate sleep. This is part of CBT-I and should be done carefullyâ€”ideally with professional guidance. Not appropriate for those with bipolar disorder or severe depression.

Address Root Causes

Blood sugar crashes, hormone imbalances, chronic pain, restless legs (often from iron deficiency), and untreated anxiety or depression all disrupt sleep. Addressing underlying issues is often necessary.

Sleep and Perimenopause

Sleep disturbances are extremely common during the menopausal transition, affecting up to 60% of women. Contributing factors include:

  • Hot flashes and night sweats: Wake women from sleep multiple times nightly
  • Declining progesterone: Progesterone has sedative effects; its decline contributes to insomnia
  • Mood changes: Anxiety and depression from hormone fluctuation disrupt sleep
  • Increased sleep apnea risk: Menopause increases OSA risk as progesterone (which maintains airway muscle tone) declines

Hormone therapy can significantly improve sleep in perimenopause. Progesterone (oral micronized) taken at bedtime has both hormone-balancing and sleep-promoting effects.

Supplements for Sleep

Several supplements may support sleep (to be discussed with healthcare providers):

Supplements with Sleep Research

  • Magnesium: Promotes relaxation; glycinate form is calming. 200-400mg before bed.
  • Melatonin: Most helpful for circadian issues and jet lag. Low dose (0.5-1mg) often more effective than high dose. Timing mattersâ€”1-2 hours before desired sleep.
  • L-theanine: Promotes relaxation without sedation; 100-200mg.
  • Glycine: Amino acid that may improve sleep quality; 3g before bed.
  • Valerian root: Traditional sleep herb; mixed evidence but some find helpful.
  • Phosphatidylserine: May help reduce elevated cortisol that disrupts sleep; 100mg.
  • Tart cherry: Contains natural melatonin; may improve sleep quality.

Cautions with Sleep Aids

Prescription sleep medications (benzodiazepines, "Z-drugs" like zolpidem) can cause dependency, impair sleep architecture, and increase cognitive decline risk with chronic use. Over-the-counter antihistamines (diphenhydramine, doxylamine) have anticholinergic effects associated with cognitive impairment. These medications may be appropriate short-term but shouldn't be first-line or long-term solutions. CBT-I and addressing root causes are more sustainable approaches.

Coaching Approach to Sleep

Sleep Coaching Strategies

  1. Assess thoroughly: Explore sleep quantity, quality, timing, and daytime symptoms. Screen for sleep apnea (snoring, gasping, witnessed pauses, unrefreshing sleep despite adequate time).
  2. Encourage evaluation when needed: Refer to primary care or sleep specialist if sleep apnea suspected, insomnia is severe, or sleep problems don't respond to basic interventions.
  3. Start with consistent wake time: This single change is often the most impactful. Anchor the circadian rhythm with a non-negotiable wake time.
  4. Address light exposure: Morning brightness and evening darkness are powerful circadian signals.
  5. Build a wind-down routine: Help clients create a personalized 30-60 minute transition period.
  6. Troubleshoot barriers: What specifically is getting in the way? Work obligations, family demands, anxiety, pain, partner issues? Problem-solve together.
  7. Connect sleep to their "why": Help clients see how better sleep will support their other health goalsâ€”energy, weight, mood, cognitive function.

Understanding Anxiety

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions, affecting approximately 40 million American adults. They include generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, social anxiety, and specific phobias.

The Blood Sugar-Anxiety Connection

This connection deserves special attention because it's so common and so treatable. When blood sugar drops (after a high-carb meal, skipped meals, or due to insulin resistance), the body releases adrenaline and cortisol to raise blood sugar. These stress hormones cause symptoms identical to anxiety:

  • Racing heart
  • Sweating
  • Shakiness
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Irritability
  • Feeling of impending doom

Many people with "anxiety" are actually experiencing recurrent reactive hypoglycemia. Stabilizing blood sugar through regular protein-containing meals and reducing refined carbohydrates often dramatically reduces anxiety.

Understanding Depression

Depression affects over 16 million American adults annually. Symptoms include persistent sadness, loss of interest, fatigue, sleep disturbances, appetite changes, difficulty concentrating, and in severe cases, thoughts of death or suicide.

The functional medicine approach views depression not as simply "low serotonin" but as the brain's response to multiple stressors that overwhelm adaptive capacity:

Inflammation and Depression

Research increasingly supports inflammation as a key driver of depression:

  • People with depression have 30% higher levels of inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6)
  • Anti-inflammatory medications sometimes improve depression
  • Inflammatory conditions (autoimmune diseases, obesity, heart disease) increase depression risk 2-3 fold
  • Inflammation diverts tryptophan away from serotonin production
  • Inflammatory cytokines directly affect brain circuits involved in mood and motivation

This "inflammatory depression" phenotype may explain why some patients don't respond to traditional antidepressantsâ€”they need anti-inflammatory interventions.

The Anxiety-Depression Overlap

Anxiety and depression frequently occur togetherâ€”about 60% of people with one condition also have the other. From a functional medicine perspective, this makes sense because many root causes contribute to both:

Shared Root Causes

  • HPA axis dysregulation: Chronic stress leads first to anxiety (elevated cortisol), then to depression (cortisol resistance/depletion)
  • Gut dysfunction: Affects production of both serotonin (depression) and GABA (anxiety)
  • Inflammation: Promotes both conditions through neuroinflammation
  • Blood sugar instability: Causes anxiety during crashes, fatigue between meals (mimicking depression)
  • Sleep deprivation: Increases both anxiety and depression risk
  • Thyroid dysfunction: Causes anxiety (early or with hyperthyroid) and depression (hypothyroid)
  • Nutrient deficiencies: Same nutrients (B vitamins, iron, magnesium) affect both conditions

Nutritional Interventions

Evidence-Based Nutritional Strategies

Anti-Inflammatory Diet

The SMILES trial showed that a Mediterranean-style diet significantly improved depressionâ€”one-third of participants achieved remission through dietary change alone. Key elements: abundant vegetables, olive oil, fatty fish, nuts, limited processed foods and sugar.

Blood Sugar Stabilization

Protein at every meal (20-30g), eliminate or reduce refined carbohydrates, include healthy fats, avoid skipping meals. This alone can dramatically reduce anxiety for many people.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Meta-analyses support omega-3 supplementation (particularly EPA) for depression. Recommended: 2-4g of combined EPA/DHA daily, with EPA ratio higher than DHA for mood. Fatty fish 2-3 times weekly also beneficial.

Adequate Protein

Protein provides precursor amino acids for neurotransmitters. Many people, especially women, undereat protein. Aim for 0.8-1g per pound of ideal body weight, distributed across meals.

Reduce Caffeine

Caffeine can trigger or worsen anxiety, especially in slow metabolizers (CYP1A2 variants). It also raises cortisol and disrupts sleep. Consider elimination or significant reduction for anxious clients.

Lifestyle Interventions

Evidence-Based Lifestyle Strategies

Exercise

Arguably the most powerful lifestyle intervention for both anxiety and depression. Meta-analyses show exercise is as effective as antidepressants for mild-moderate depression. Even a 10-minute walk improves mood. Aim for 150 minutes moderate or 75 minutes vigorous activity weekly.

Sleep Optimization

Poor sleep is both a cause and consequence of mood disorders. Prioritize 7-9 hours, consistent sleep/wake times, dark cool bedroom, no screens before bed. Address sleep apnea if suspected.

Light Exposure

Morning light exposure (10-30 minutes) supports circadian rhythm and serotonin production. Light therapy boxes (10,000 lux) effective for seasonal and non-seasonal depression. Reduce evening light to support melatonin.

Stress Management

Regular practice of stress-reduction techniques: meditation, deep breathing, yoga, time in nature. Even 10-15 minutes daily makes a difference. Mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) reduces depression relapse.

Social Connection

Isolation is both a symptom and driver of depression. Encourage regular meaningful social interaction, even when motivation is low. Support groups can be particularly helpful.

Supplement Considerations

Beyond correcting deficiencies, certain supplements have research support for anxiety and depression (to be discussed with healthcare providers):

Supplements with Research Support

  • For anxiety: Magnesium glycinate, L-theanine, ashwagandha, lavender oil (Silexan), GABA (limited brain penetration but may affect gut-brain axis), passionflower
  • For depression: SAMe (S-adenosylmethionine), EPA-focused omega-3s, curcumin, saffron, St. John's Wort (caution: many drug interactions)
  • For both: Vitamin D, B-complex (methylated forms), magnesium, probiotics (specific strains for mood)

Important Cautions

  • St. John's Wort interacts with many medications including birth control, blood thinners, and antidepressants
  • SAMe can trigger mania in bipolar disorder
  • 5-HTP should not be combined with antidepressants (risk of serotonin syndrome)
  • Always coordinate supplement use with prescribing providers
  • Supplements are adjuncts, not replacements for appropriate medical/psychological care

Coaching Approach

When working with clients experiencing anxiety or depression:

Coaching Strategies

  1. Ensure appropriate professional support: Clients should have a mental health provider (therapist, psychiatrist) as primary support. Coaching is complementary.
  2. Explore physical factors: Through intake, identify potential contributors (gut issues, thyroid symptoms, sleep problems, blood sugar patterns, nutrient-poor diet).
  3. Encourage appropriate testing: Suggest clients ask their providers about comprehensive thyroid panel, vitamin D, B12, iron/ferritin, inflammatory markers.
  4. Start with foundations: Blood sugar stabilization, sleep hygiene, and gentle movement are often the most impactful initial interventions.
  5. Be patient and compassionate: Depression reduces motivation; anxiety creates resistance to change. Small steps with unconditional support are key.
  6. Monitor for worsening: If symptoms worsen significantly, encourage immediate contact with mental health provider. Have crisis resources available.

Key Takeaways

  • Anxiety has multiple physiological triggers including blood sugar instability, thyroid dysfunction, gut dysbiosis, and nutrient deficiencies
  • Depression is increasingly understood as an inflammatory condition, not simply "low serotonin"
  • Anxiety and depression share many root causes and frequently occur together
  • Anti-inflammatory diet, blood sugar stabilization, and omega-3 fatty acids have strong research support for mood
  • Exercise, sleep optimization, light exposure, and stress management are foundational lifestyle interventions
  • Certain supplements may help but should be coordinated with medical providers
  • Coaches support lifestyle factors while ensuring clients have appropriate mental health care

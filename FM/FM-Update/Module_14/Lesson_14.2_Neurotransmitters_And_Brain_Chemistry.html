What Are Neurotransmitters?

Neurotransmitters are chemical messengers that transmit signals between neurons (nerve cells) in the brain and throughout the nervous system. They influence virtually every aspect of our mental and physical experienceâ€”mood, sleep, focus, motivation, pain perception, memory, and more.

Neurotransmitters work by being released from one neuron, crossing the synaptic gap (the space between neurons), and binding to receptors on the receiving neuron. This process either excites (activates) or inhibits (calms) the receiving neuron, creating cascading effects throughout neural networks.

Major Neurotransmitters

Serotonin (5-HT)

Serotonin is often called the "feel-good" neurotransmitter. It regulates mood, sleep, appetite, digestion, pain perception, and social behavior.

Serotonin Synthesis

Precursor: Tryptophan (essential amino acid from protein)

Pathway: Tryptophan â†’ 5-HTP â†’ Serotonin â†’ Melatonin

Required cofactors: Iron, B6, folate, vitamin D, zinc, magnesium

Key insight: 95% of serotonin is made in the gut, not the brain. Gut health directly affects serotonin production.

Signs of low serotonin: Depression, anxiety, insomnia, carbohydrate cravings, irritability, low pain tolerance, obsessive thinking, PMS symptoms

Dopamine

Dopamine is the "motivation and reward" neurotransmitter. It drives goal-directed behavior, pleasure, focus, and movement.

Dopamine Synthesis

Precursor: Tyrosine (amino acid from protein, also made from phenylalanine)

Pathway: Tyrosine â†’ L-DOPA â†’ Dopamine â†’ Norepinephrine â†’ Epinephrine

Required cofactors: Iron, B6, folate, copper, vitamin C, zinc

Key insight: Iron is critical for dopamine synthesisâ€”iron deficiency causes dopamine-related symptoms even without anemia.

Signs of low dopamine: Lack of motivation, difficulty with focus and concentration, fatigue, apathy, anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure), procrastination, addictive tendencies (seeking dopamine "hits" from stimulants, sugar, social media)

GABA (Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid)

GABA is the brain's primary inhibitory (calming) neurotransmitter. It promotes relaxation, reduces anxiety, and supports sleep.

GABA Synthesis

Precursor: Glutamate (made from glutamine)

Pathway: Glutamine â†’ Glutamate â†’ GABA

Required cofactors: B6 (critical), zinc, taurine

Key insight: Gut bacteria (especially Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species) produce GABA, contributing to the gut-brain anxiety connection.

Signs of low GABA: Anxiety, panic attacks, feeling "wired but tired," insomnia, inability to relax, racing thoughts, muscle tension

Norepinephrine (Noradrenaline)

Norepinephrine is involved in alertness, arousal, and the stress response. It helps with focus, energy, and quick thinking.

Norepinephrine Synthesis

Precursor: Dopamine

Pathway: Tyrosine â†’ Dopamine â†’ Norepinephrine

Required cofactors: Vitamin C, copper

Key insight: Norepinephrine is both a neurotransmitter (brain) and hormone (adrenal glands). Chronic stress depletes norepinephrine.

Signs of imbalance: Low: fatigue, poor concentration, lack of motivation, depression. High (chronic stress): anxiety, hypervigilance, difficulty relaxing

Acetylcholine

Acetylcholine is crucial for memory, learning, attention, and muscle control.

Acetylcholine Synthesis

Precursor: Choline (essential nutrient, often under-consumed)

Pathway: Choline + Acetyl-CoA â†’ Acetylcholine

Required cofactors: B1, B5, acetyl groups (from metabolism)

Key insight: Egg yolks are the richest dietary source of choline. Most people don't get enough choline, especially if avoiding eggs.

Signs of low acetylcholine: Memory problems, difficulty learning new information, "brain fog," poor word recall, loss of muscle tone

Critical Cofactors

  • Vitamin B6: Required for serotonin, dopamine, GABA, and norepinephrine synthesisâ€”perhaps the most critical cofactor
  • Iron: Essential for serotonin and dopamine synthesis; deficiency very common in menstruating women
  • Folate & B12: Required for methylation reactions in neurotransmitter production
  • Zinc: Involved in multiple neurotransmitter pathways; often depleted by stress
  • Vitamin D: Regulates genes involved in serotonin synthesis
  • Magnesium: Required for GABA function and over 300 enzymatic reactions
  • Vitamin C: Required for norepinephrine and dopamine synthesis

Factors That Disrupt Neurotransmitter Balance

Even with adequate precursors and cofactors, various factors can disrupt neurotransmitter function:

Common Disruptors of Brain Chemistry

Inflammation:

Inflammatory cytokines activate an enzyme (IDO) that diverts tryptophan away from serotonin production toward a toxic pathway that produces quinolinic acidâ€”neurotoxic and associated with depression.

Chronic Stress:

Depletes dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin over time. Chronic cortisol reduces BDNF and impairs neuroplasticity. Shifts glutamate/GABA balance toward excitation.

Gut Dysfunction:

Impairs absorption of precursor amino acids and cofactors. Dysbiosis reduces microbial neurotransmitter production. Intestinal inflammation increases systemic inflammation affecting the brain.

Blood Sugar Dysregulation:

Blood sugar crashes deplete catecholamines (dopamine, norepinephrine). Chronic high blood sugar promotes neuroinflammation. Insulin resistance impairs brain glucose utilization.

Poor Sleep:

Reduces serotonin and dopamine receptor sensitivity. Impairs neurotransmitter clearance and restoration. Increases inflammation and stress hormones.

Toxin Exposure:

Heavy metals (mercury, lead) disrupt neurotransmitter function. Pesticides affect acetylcholine pathways. Mold toxins impair brain function through multiple mechanisms.

The Methylation Connection

Methylation is a biochemical process essential for neurotransmitter synthesis, breakdown, and gene regulation. It requires folate, B12, B6, and other nutrients. When methylation is impaired:

  • Neurotransmitter production and clearance are affected
  • Homocysteine accumulates (associated with depression and cognitive decline)
  • Detoxification is impaired
  • DNA repair and gene regulation suffer

Common factors impairing methylation:

  • B12 or folate deficiency
  • MTHFR genetic variants (affecting folate metabolism)
  • High alcohol intake
  • Certain medications (metformin, PPIs, methotrexate)
  • Gut malabsorption

Those with methylation issues may benefit from methylated B vitamins (methylfolate, methylcobalamin) rather than synthetic forms (folic acid, cyanocobalamin).

Supporting Healthy Brain Chemistry

Nutritional Strategies

  • Adequate protein at every meal: 20-30g per meal provides precursor amino acids
  • Include tryptophan-rich foods: Turkey, eggs, nuts, seeds, dairy (for serotonin)
  • Prioritize choline: Egg yolks (2-3 per day), liver (weekly), or supplementation
  • Optimize cofactor status: B vitamins (especially B6, B12, folate), iron, zinc, magnesium, vitamin D
  • Anti-inflammatory diet: Reduce inflammation that disrupts tryptophan metabolism
  • Blood sugar stability: Balance meals with protein, fat, and fiber; avoid refined carbohydrates
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: Support receptor function and reduce neuroinflammation

Lifestyle Strategies

  • Exercise: Increases BDNF, promotes serotonin and dopamine release, reduces inflammation
  • Stress management: Prevents catecholamine depletion; supports GABA function
  • Quality sleep: Allows neurotransmitter restoration and clearance
  • Sunlight exposure: Supports serotonin production (converts to melatonin at night)
  • Social connection: Increases oxytocin, reduces stress hormones
  • Mindfulness practice: Increases GABA, supports prefrontal cortex function

Testing Considerations

While neurotransmitter testing (urinary, blood, or salivary) exists, interpretation is controversial. More useful are tests for factors affecting neurotransmitter production:

  • Nutrient status: B12, folate, iron/ferritin, vitamin D, zinc, RBC magnesium
  • Inflammation markers: hs-CRP, homocysteine
  • Thyroid panel: Full panel including Free T3, Free T4, TPO antibodies
  • Organic acids test: Can reveal B vitamin status, neurotransmitter metabolites
  • Genetic testing: MTHFR and other variants affecting methylation

Key Takeaways

  • Neurotransmitters are made from dietary precursors (amino acids from protein) plus cofactors (vitamins, minerals)
  • Serotonin requires tryptophan + iron, B6, folate, vitamin D; 95% is made in the gut
  • Dopamine requires tyrosine + iron, B6, folate, vitamin C; essential for motivation and focus
  • GABA requires glutamine + B6; produced partly by gut bacteria; critical for calming anxiety
  • Inflammation, chronic stress, gut dysfunction, and blood sugar dysregulation all disrupt neurotransmitter balance
  • Adequate protein, cofactor nutrients, anti-inflammatory diet, and lifestyle factors support healthy brain chemistry
  • Methylation is essential for neurotransmitter function and requires folate, B12, and B6

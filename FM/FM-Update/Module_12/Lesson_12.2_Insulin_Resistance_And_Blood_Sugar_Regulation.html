How Insulin Works: The Normal Process

To understand what goes wrong in insulin resistance, we first need to understand how the system is designed to work. Insulin is the master metabolic hormone that determines whether fuel is burned or stored.

The Blood Sugar-Insulin Dance

When you eat carbohydrates (and to a lesser extent, protein), they're broken down into glucose, which enters the bloodstream. Insulin is the key that unlocks cells, allowing glucose to enter and be used for energy or storage.

Normal Insulin Function: Step by Step

1Ô∏è‚É£ You Eat

Carbohydrates are digested into glucose, entering the bloodstream

2Ô∏è‚É£ Blood Sugar Rises

The pancreas detects elevated glucose levels

3Ô∏è‚É£ Insulin Released

Beta cells in the pancreas secrete insulin into the bloodstream

4Ô∏è‚É£ Cells Respond

Insulin binds to receptors on muscle, fat, and liver cells, signaling them to absorb glucose

5Ô∏è‚É£ Glucose Used/Stored

Cells use glucose for immediate energy, store it as glycogen, or convert excess to fat

6Ô∏è‚É£ Blood Sugar Normalizes

Glucose levels return to baseline (70-100 mg/dL)

7Ô∏è‚É£ Insulin Levels Drop

With normalized glucose, insulin secretion decreases, allowing access to stored fuel between meals

In a healthy system, this process is efficient. A modest amount of insulin clears glucose from the blood quickly, and insulin levels return to baseline within a few hours. Between meals and overnight, low insulin allows the body to access stored fat for energy.

Coach Tip

When explaining insulin to clients, try this analogy: "Think of insulin as the key that unlocks your cells' doors to let glucose in. When the system works well, a small key easily opens the door. But with insulin resistance, it's like the locks are rusty‚Äîyou need a much bigger key (more insulin) to force the door open. Eventually, even that stops working."

Insulin's Other Roles

While glucose regulation is insulin's most recognized function, this hormone influences many other processes:

1

Fat Metabolism

Insulin promotes the storage of fat (lipogenesis) and inhibits fat breakdown (lipolysis). High insulin = fat storage mode.

2

Protein Synthesis

Insulin is anabolic‚Äîit supports muscle building when adequate protein and resistance training are present.

3

Sodium Retention

Insulin signals the kidneys to retain sodium, influencing blood pressure and fluid balance.

4

Cell Growth

Insulin (and related IGF-1) promotes cell growth and proliferation throughout the body.

5

Brain Function

Insulin crosses the blood-brain barrier and affects appetite, memory, and cognition.

The Development of Insulin Resistance

Insulin resistance occurs when cells become less responsive to insulin's signal. The pancreas compensates by producing more insulin to achieve the same glucose-lowering effect. This creates a state of chronically elevated insulin (hyperinsulinemia) even when blood glucose appears normal.

The Progression: A Multi-Stage Process

Stage 1: Normal
  • What's Happening: Cells respond efficiently to insulin
  • Lab Findings: Normal fasting glucose AND normal fasting insulin
  • Timeline: Baseline healthy state
Stage 2: Compensated IR
  • What's Happening: Cells become resistant; pancreas compensates with more insulin
  • Lab Findings: Normal fasting glucose BUT elevated fasting insulin
  • Timeline: Can persist 10-15 years undetected
Stage 3: Prediabetes
  • What's Happening: Pancreas can't keep up; glucose starts rising
  • Lab Findings: Elevated fasting glucose (100-125) or impaired glucose tolerance; high insulin
  • Timeline: Often diagnosed here, but damage already occurring
Stage 4: Type 2 Diabetes
  • What's Happening: Severe resistance + pancreatic beta cell dysfunction
  • Lab Findings: Fasting glucose ‚â•126 or HbA1c ‚â•6.5%; insulin may still be high or declining
  • Timeline: End stage of progressive dysfunction
Critical Insight

Stage 2‚Äîcompensated insulin resistance‚Äîcan persist for 10-15 years before blood glucose rises. During this time, fasting glucose looks "normal," and most people are never diagnosed. Yet the chronically elevated insulin is already causing damage: promoting fat storage (especially visceral fat), driving inflammation, and increasing cardiovascular risk. This is why testing fasting insulin‚Äînot just glucose‚Äîis essential for early detection.

What Causes Insulin Resistance?

Multiple factors contribute to the development of insulin resistance. Understanding these helps guide targeted interventions:

Root Causes of Insulin Resistance

üçî Dietary Factors

Key drivers: Excessive refined carbohydrates and sugar, frequent eating (constant insulin stimulation), fructose overconsumption (especially from processed foods), industrial seed oils and ultra-processed foods.

üèÉ Lifestyle Factors

Key drivers: Physical inactivity (muscle is a glucose sink), chronic stress (cortisol drives insulin resistance), poor sleep (even one week impairs insulin sensitivity), circadian disruption.

‚öñÔ∏è Body Composition

Key drivers: Excess visceral (abdominal) fat, fatty liver (NAFLD), loss of muscle mass, lipid overflow into muscle and liver tissue.

üî¨ Other Factors

Key drivers: Chronic inflammation, gut dysbiosis, environmental toxins (BPA, phthalates), genetic predisposition (though lifestyle modifiable).

Coach Tip

Help clients understand that insulin resistance develops from patterns, not single events. It's not the occasional dessert that's problematic‚Äîit's the daily breakfast cereal, mid-morning muffin, sandwich at lunch, afternoon granola bar, and pasta at dinner that keep insulin constantly elevated. Use this to reframe "cheat days" and help clients see the cumulative impact of choices.

Signs and Symptoms of Insulin Resistance

Because insulin resistance develops silently for years before blood sugar rises, recognizing the clinical signs helps identify at-risk clients early. Many of these signs were present in Gloria's case.

Physical Signs

üö® Clinical Indicators of Insulin Resistance

Central Obesity

Waist circumference >35" in women, >40" in men; waist-to-hip ratio >0.8 in women, >1.0 in men. "Apple-shaped" body composition.

Acanthosis Nigricans

Dark, velvety patches on skin folds (neck, armpits, groin)‚Äîa visible marker of hyperinsulinemia. Often mistaken for "dirt."

Skin Tags

Small flesh-colored growths, especially on neck and underarms. Associated with insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome.

Weight Loss Resistance

Difficulty losing weight, especially from the midsection, despite calorie restriction and exercise efforts.

Symptoms and Subjective Experience

Clients with insulin resistance often describe a characteristic pattern of symptoms that affect daily quality of life:

  • Energy crashes: Fatigue 1-3 hours after eating, especially carbohydrate-rich meals‚Äîthe classic "post-lunch slump"
  • Carbohydrate cravings: Strong, almost irresistible cravings for bread, pasta, sweets, especially mid-afternoon
  • "Hangry" episodes: Irritability, shakiness, or difficulty concentrating when meals are delayed (reactive hypoglycemia)
  • Brain fog: Difficulty concentrating, especially after meals; feeling mentally sluggish
  • Frequent hunger: Feeling hungry again within 2-3 hours of eating, despite adequate calories
  • Sleep disturbances: Waking between 2-4 AM as blood sugar drops and triggers cortisol release
  • Afternoon slump: Significant energy dip requiring caffeine or sugar to push through the day
Coach Tip

When taking a client history, ask: "Do you get sleepy or foggy after lunch? Do you need an afternoon pick-me-up? Do you wake up at night? Do you get irritable when you're hungry?" These questions reveal blood sugar dysregulation patterns that clients often dismiss as "normal" but signal underlying insulin resistance.

Associated Conditions

Insulin resistance is a root cause or contributing factor in many common conditions your clients may present with:

PCOS

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome

50-70% of women with PCOS have insulin resistance. Addressing insulin often improves hormonal balance and fertility.

NAFLD

Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver

Insulin resistance drives liver fat accumulation. Reversible with dietary intervention targeting insulin.

CVD

Cardiovascular Disease

Hyperinsulinemia promotes inflammation, hypertension, and dyslipidemia‚Äîmajor cardiovascular risk factors.

AD

Alzheimer's Disease

Sometimes called "type 3 diabetes" due to brain insulin resistance. Metabolic health impacts cognitive health.

Cancer

Certain Cancers

Insulin and IGF-1 promote cell proliferation. Elevated levels associated with increased cancer risk.

Gout

Gout & Hyperuricemia

Elevated insulin impairs uric acid excretion by kidneys, contributing to gout attacks.

Practical Assessment Tip

The Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio is a simple, accessible marker that correlates well with insulin resistance and doesn't require a special test. A ratio below 2.0 suggests good insulin sensitivity; above 3.0 suggests insulin resistance. This can be calculated from a standard lipid panel that most clients already have. For example: Triglycerides 150 √∑ HDL 40 = 3.75 (suggests insulin resistance).

Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)

Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGMs) provide real-time data on blood sugar fluctuations throughout the day. While traditionally used for diabetes management, they're increasingly used for metabolic health optimization:

üí° Benefits of CGM Technology

Real-Time Feedback

Reveal glucose spikes and crashes that fasting tests miss. See exactly how your body responds to foods.

Personalized Data

Show individual responses to specific foods‚Äîwhat spikes one person may not spike another.

Lifestyle Impact

Demonstrate the impact of sleep, stress, and exercise on glucose regulation in real-time.

Behavior Change

Provide powerful, immediate feedback that motivates and guides dietary adjustments.

CGMs are available through some healthcare providers or direct-to-consumer services like Levels, NutriSense, or Signos. While not necessary for everyone, they can be transformative for clients who are visual learners or struggle to connect food choices with how they feel.

Coach Tip

When reviewing labs with clients, focus on trends and patterns, not just single values. A fasting glucose of 95 with insulin of 15 tells a very different story than glucose of 95 with insulin of 4. The first suggests compensated insulin resistance; the second suggests good metabolic health. Help clients understand that "normal" lab ranges capture 95% of the population‚Äînot necessarily optimal health.

Strategies for Improving Insulin Sensitivity

The good news is that insulin resistance is reversible through lifestyle modification. Many clients see significant improvements within weeks to months of implementing changes. The key is addressing root causes, not just managing symptoms.

Dietary Strategies

Dietary Approaches to Improve Insulin Sensitivity

ü•ñ Reduce Refined Carbs

Minimize white flour, white rice, added sugars, and processed foods that cause rapid glucose spikes. Focus on whole, unprocessed foods.

ü•© Prioritize Protein

Include 25-40g protein at each meal to stabilize blood sugar, promote satiety, and preserve muscle mass.

ü•¶ Add Fiber

Non-starchy vegetables, seeds, nuts, and low-sugar fruits slow glucose absorption and feed beneficial gut bacteria.

ü•ë Include Healthy Fats

Olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish improve insulin sensitivity and slow glucose release from meals.

üçΩÔ∏è Eat Carbs Last

When including carbohydrates, eat protein and vegetables first to blunt the glucose response (the "food sequencing" strategy).

‚è∞ Time-Restricted Eating

Limiting eating window to 8-12 hours allows insulin to drop between meals and overnight, improving insulin sensitivity.

Movement and Exercise

Muscle is the largest glucose-disposal tissue in the body. Exercise improves insulin sensitivity through multiple mechanisms, with effects lasting 24-48 hours after a single session:

üí™

Resistance Training

Builds muscle mass‚Äîeach pound of muscle is a glucose sink that improves insulin sensitivity long-term. Aim for 2-3 sessions per week.

üö∂

Post-Meal Walking

Even a 10-15 minute walk after meals significantly blunts glucose spikes. One of the most accessible, high-impact interventions.

üîÑ

Consistent Movement

Breaking up sitting with movement every 30-60 minutes improves glucose handling throughout the day.

üèÉ

Aerobic Exercise

Improves metabolic flexibility and glucose uptake by muscles. Walking, cycling, swimming all effective.

‚ö°

HIIT (When Appropriate)

High-intensity intervals can rapidly improve insulin sensitivity, but may not be suitable for everyone. Consider individual fitness level.

Coach Tip

Help clients start with the easiest, highest-impact intervention: post-meal walking. Even 10 minutes after dinner makes a measurable difference. Frame it as "taking your glucose for a walk" to help clients visualize how movement clears glucose from the bloodstream. This small habit often catalyzes bigger changes as clients feel the energy benefits.

Sleep and Stress

Sleep deprivation and chronic stress both directly impair insulin sensitivity‚Äîoften as much as dietary factors. These are non-negotiable foundations of metabolic health:

  • Prioritize 7-9 hours of sleep: Even modest sleep restriction (6 hours vs. 8) significantly impairs glucose tolerance within days
  • Maintain consistent sleep timing: Circadian disruption impairs insulin function independent of sleep duration. Go to bed and wake at similar times daily
  • Manage chronic stress: Cortisol directly antagonizes insulin and raises blood sugar. Chronic stress creates a state of physiological insulin resistance
  • Practice nervous system regulation: Breathing exercises, meditation, time in nature, and social connection lower cortisol and improve metabolic function
Pause & Reflect

Consider your own sleep patterns and stress levels. How do you feel after a poor night's sleep? Do you crave sugar more? Feel hungrier? Have less willpower around food choices? This is insulin resistance in action. Help clients connect these dots in their own lives.

Targeted Nutrients and Supplements

Several nutrients support healthy blood sugar regulation. While food-first is ideal, targeted supplementation may be appropriate (always discuss with healthcare providers for individual recommendations):

üåø Key Nutrients for Insulin Sensitivity

Magnesium

Essential for insulin signaling; deficiency common in insulin resistance. Food sources: Nuts, seeds, dark leafy greens, dark chocolate. Supplement: 300-400mg daily (glycinate or citrate forms).

Chromium

Enhances insulin receptor sensitivity. Food sources: Broccoli, green beans, whole grains, meat. Supplement: 200-400mcg daily (picolinate form).

Alpha-Lipoic Acid

Powerful antioxidant that improves insulin sensitivity; shown to help with diabetic neuropathy. Supplement: 300-600mg daily. Limited food sources.

Berberine

Plant compound that improves blood sugar regulation through multiple mechanisms; comparable to some medications in studies. Supplement: 500mg, 2-3 times daily with meals. From barberry plant.

Cinnamon

May improve insulin sensitivity and glucose uptake. Food source: 1-2 tsp daily (Ceylon cinnamon preferred). Supplement: 1-6g daily in capsule form.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Reduce inflammation and improve insulin sensitivity. Food sources: Fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseeds. Supplement: 1-3g daily EPA/DHA from fish oil.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What is the key difference between Stage 2 (compensated insulin resistance) and Stage 3 (prediabetes) in the progression of insulin resistance?

Answer: In Stage 2 (compensated insulin resistance), fasting glucose remains normal because the pancreas is compensating by producing more insulin. In Stage 3 (prediabetes), the pancreas can no longer keep up with demand, so fasting glucose begins to rise (100-125 mg/dL). The critical point is that Stage 2 can persist for 10-15 years with "normal" glucose labs, yet chronically elevated insulin is already causing metabolic damage. This is why testing fasting insulin, not just glucose, is essential for early detection.

2

Why is the triglyceride-to-HDL ratio considered a useful marker for insulin resistance, and what values suggest concern?

Answer: The triglyceride-to-HDL ratio is useful because it correlates well with insulin resistance and can be calculated from a standard lipid panel that most clients already have‚Äîno special testing required. A ratio below 2.0 suggests good insulin sensitivity, while a ratio above 3.0 suggests insulin resistance. For example, if a client has triglycerides of 150 mg/dL and HDL of 40 mg/dL, their ratio is 3.75 (150 √∑ 40), suggesting insulin resistance even if their fasting glucose looks "normal."

3

List at least four physical signs or symptoms that suggest insulin resistance, even when fasting glucose is normal.

Answer: Physical signs and symptoms include: (1) Central obesity with increased waist circumference or "apple-shaped" body; (2) Acanthosis nigricans‚Äîdark, velvety patches on skin folds (neck, armpits, groin); (3) Energy crashes 1-3 hours after eating, especially carbohydrate-rich meals; (4) Carbohydrate cravings, particularly mid-afternoon; (5) "Hangry" episodes when meals are delayed; (6) Sleep disturbances, especially waking between 2-4 AM; (7) Difficulty losing weight despite calorie restriction; (8) Skin tags on neck and underarms. Key Takeaways
  • Insulin is the master metabolic hormone, determining whether fuel is burned or stored; chronically elevated insulin promotes fat storage and metabolic dysfunction
  • Insulin resistance develops in stages over 10-15 years, with compensated resistance (elevated insulin, normal glucose) persisting silently before blood sugar rises
  • Clinical signs include central obesity, acanthosis nigricans, energy crashes, carbohydrate cravings, and difficulty losing weight‚Äîrecognize these before labs become abnormal
  • The triglyceride-to-HDL ratio is a simple, accessible marker from standard lipid panels; >3.0 suggests insulin resistance
  • Testing fasting insulin‚Äînot just glucose‚Äîis essential for early detection of metabolic dysfunction
  • Insulin resistance is reversible through dietary changes (reducing refined carbs, prioritizing protein and fiber), movement (especially resistance training and post-meal walking), sleep optimization, and stress management
  • Post-meal walking is one of the most accessible, high-impact interventions‚Äîeven 10 minutes significantly blunts glucose spikes

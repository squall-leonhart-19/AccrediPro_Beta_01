üìñ Gloria's Story

Gloria, 52, was concerned about her fasting glucose, which had crept up to 104 mg/dL over the past few years. Her doctor said she was "prediabetic" and should "lose weight and eat less sugar." But Gloria was confused‚Äîshe rarely ate dessert, never drank soda, and already considered herself a "healthy eater." Yet the numbers kept rising.

Looking deeper at her health picture revealed a pattern: she carried her weight primarily around her middle, experienced strong afternoon energy crashes, craved carbohydrates (especially bread and pasta), woke frequently at night, and had dark patches of skin in her armpits and neck creases. Her fasting insulin had never been tested.

As her health coach, you recognize that Gloria's rising blood sugar is actually a late sign of a process that likely began years earlier. Understanding insulin‚Äîand what's happening beneath the surface before glucose rises‚Äîis key to helping clients like Gloria reverse course before type 2 diabetes develops.

How Insulin Works: The Normal Process

To understand what goes wrong in insulin resistance, we first need to understand how the system is designed to work.

The Blood Sugar-Insulin Dance

When you eat carbohydrates (and to a lesser extent, protein), they're broken down into glucose, which enters the bloodstream. Insulin is the key that unlocks cells, allowing glucose to enter and be used for energy or storage.

Normal Insulin Function: Step by Step

  1. You eat: Carbohydrates are digested into glucose, entering the bloodstream
  2. Blood sugar rises: The pancreas detects elevated glucose
  3. Insulin is released: Beta cells in the pancreas secrete insulin
  4. Cells respond: Insulin binds to receptors on muscle, fat, and liver cells, signaling them to absorb glucose
  5. Glucose is used or stored: Cells use glucose for immediate energy, store it as glycogen (in muscle and liver), or convert excess to fat
  6. Blood sugar normalizes: Glucose levels return to baseline (70-100 mg/dL)
  7. Insulin levels drop: With normalized glucose, insulin secretion decreases, allowing access to stored fuel between meals

In a healthy system, this process is efficient. A modest amount of insulin clears glucose from the blood quickly, and insulin levels return to baseline within a few hours. Between meals and overnight, low insulin allows the body to access stored fat for energy.

Insulin's Other Roles

While glucose regulation is insulin's most recognized function, this hormone influences many other processes:

  • Fat storage: Insulin promotes the storage of fat (lipogenesis) and inhibits fat breakdown (lipolysis)
  • Protein synthesis: Insulin is anabolic‚Äîit supports muscle building when adequate protein and resistance training are present
  • Sodium retention: Insulin signals the kidneys to retain sodium, influencing blood pressure
  • Cell growth: Insulin (and related IGF-1) promotes cell growth and proliferation
  • Brain function: Insulin crosses the blood-brain barrier and affects appetite, memory, and cognition

The Development of Insulin Resistance

Insulin resistance occurs when cells become less responsive to insulin's signal. The pancreas compensates by producing more insulin to achieve the same glucose-lowering effect. This creates a state of chronically elevated insulin (hyperinsulinemia) even when blood glucose appears normal.

The Progression: A Multi-Stage Process

Stage What's Happening Lab Findings
1. Normal Cells respond efficiently to insulin Normal fasting glucose AND normal fasting insulin
2. Compensated IR Cells become resistant; pancreas compensates with more insulin Normal fasting glucose BUT elevated fasting insulin
3. Prediabetes Pancreas can't keep up; glucose starts rising Elevated fasting glucose (100-125) or impaired glucose tolerance; high insulin
4. Type 2 Diabetes Severe resistance + pancreatic beta cell dysfunction Fasting glucose ‚â•126 or HbA1c ‚â•6.5%; insulin may still be high or declining

Stage 2‚Äîcompensated insulin resistance‚Äîcan persist for 10-15 years before blood glucose rises. During this time, fasting glucose looks "normal," and most people are never diagnosed. Yet the chronically elevated insulin is already causing damage: promoting fat storage (especially visceral fat), driving inflammation, and increasing cardiovascular risk. This is why testing fasting insulin‚Äînot just glucose‚Äîis essential for early detection.

What Causes Insulin Resistance?

Multiple factors contribute to the development of insulin resistance:

Dietary Factors

  • Excessive refined carbohydrates and sugar
  • Frequent eating (constant insulin stimulation)
  • Fructose overconsumption (especially from processed foods)
  • Industrial seed oils and processed foods

Lifestyle Factors

  • Physical inactivity (muscle is a glucose sink)
  • Chronic stress (cortisol drives insulin resistance)
  • Poor sleep (even one week impairs insulin sensitivity)
  • Circadian disruption

Body Composition

  • Excess visceral (abdominal) fat
  • Fatty liver (NAFLD)
  • Loss of muscle mass
  • Lipid overflow into muscle and liver

Other Factors

  • Chronic inflammation
  • Gut dysbiosis
  • Environmental toxins
  • Genetic predisposition

Signs and Symptoms of Insulin Resistance

Because insulin resistance develops silently for years before blood sugar rises, recognizing the clinical signs helps identify at-risk clients early. Many of these signs were present in Gloria's case.

Physical Signs

Clinical Indicators of Insulin Resistance

  • Central obesity: Waist circumference >35" in women, >40" in men; waist-to-hip ratio >0.8 in women, >1.0 in men
  • Acanthosis nigricans: Dark, velvety patches on skin folds (neck, armpits, groin)‚Äîa visible marker of hyperinsulinemia
  • Skin tags: Small flesh-colored growths, especially on neck and underarms
  • Difficulty losing weight: Especially from the midsection, despite calorie restriction
  • Increased belly fat: "Apple-shaped" body composition

Symptoms and Subjective Experience

  • Energy crashes: Fatigue 1-3 hours after eating, especially carbohydrate-rich meals
  • Carbohydrate cravings: Strong cravings for bread, pasta, sweets, especially mid-afternoon
  • "Hangry" episodes: Irritability, shakiness, or difficulty concentrating when meals are delayed
  • Brain fog: Difficulty concentrating, especially after meals
  • Frequent hunger: Feeling hungry again within 2-3 hours of eating
  • Sleep disturbances: Waking between 2-4 AM (blood sugar drop triggers cortisol release)
  • Afternoon slump: Significant energy dip requiring caffeine or sugar to push through

Associated Conditions

Insulin resistance is a root cause or contributing factor in many common conditions:

  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): 50-70% of women with PCOS have insulin resistance
  • Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD): Insulin resistance drives liver fat accumulation
  • Cardiovascular disease: Hyperinsulinemia promotes inflammation, hypertension, and dyslipidemia
  • Alzheimer's disease: Sometimes called "type 3 diabetes" due to brain insulin resistance
  • Certain cancers: Insulin and IGF-1 promote cell proliferation
  • Gout: Elevated insulin impairs uric acid excretion

Laboratory Assessment

Standard lab panels often miss insulin resistance because they only test fasting glucose. A comprehensive assessment includes:

‚úì Practical Assessment Tip

The Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio is a simple, accessible marker that correlates well with insulin resistance and doesn't require a special test. A ratio below 2.0 suggests good insulin sensitivity; above 3.0 suggests insulin resistance. This can be calculated from a standard lipid panel that most clients already have.

Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)

Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGMs) provide real-time data on blood sugar fluctuations throughout the day. While traditionally used for diabetes management, they're increasingly used for metabolic health optimization:

  • Reveal glucose spikes and crashes that fasting tests miss
  • Show individual responses to specific foods (glycemic variability)
  • Demonstrate the impact of sleep, stress, and exercise on glucose
  • Provide powerful feedback for behavior change
  • Available through some healthcare providers or direct-to-consumer services

Strategies for Improving Insulin Sensitivity

The good news is that insulin resistance is reversible through lifestyle modification. Many clients see significant improvements within weeks to months of implementing changes.

Dietary Strategies

Dietary Approaches to Improve Insulin Sensitivity

  • Reduce refined carbohydrates: Minimize white flour, white rice, added sugars, and processed foods that cause rapid glucose spikes
  • Prioritize protein: Include 25-40g protein at each meal to stabilize blood sugar and promote satiety
  • Add fiber: Non-starchy vegetables, seeds, and low-sugar fruits slow glucose absorption
  • Include healthy fats: Olive oil, avocado, nuts, and fatty fish improve insulin sensitivity and slow glucose release
  • Eat carbs last: When including carbohydrates, eat protein and vegetables first to blunt the glucose response
  • Consider time-restricted eating: Limiting eating window to 8-12 hours allows insulin to drop between meals

Movement and Exercise

Muscle is the largest glucose-disposal tissue in the body. Exercise improves insulin sensitivity through multiple mechanisms:

  • Resistance training: Builds muscle mass‚Äîeach pound of muscle is a glucose sink that improves insulin sensitivity long-term
  • Post-meal walking: Even a 10-15 minute walk after meals significantly blunts glucose spikes
  • Consistent movement: Breaking up sitting with movement every 30-60 minutes improves glucose handling
  • Aerobic exercise: Improves metabolic flexibility and glucose uptake by muscles
  • HIIT (when appropriate): High-intensity intervals can rapidly improve insulin sensitivity, but may not be suitable for everyone

Sleep and Stress

Sleep deprivation and chronic stress both directly impair insulin sensitivity‚Äîoften as much as dietary factors:

  • Prioritize 7-9 hours of sleep: Even modest sleep restriction (6 hours vs. 8) significantly impairs glucose tolerance
  • Maintain consistent sleep timing: Circadian disruption impairs insulin function independent of sleep duration
  • Manage chronic stress: Cortisol directly antagonizes insulin and raises blood sugar
  • Practice nervous system regulation: Breathing exercises, meditation, and nature exposure lower cortisol and improve metabolic function

Targeted Nutrients and Supplements

Several nutrients support healthy blood sugar regulation (discuss with healthcare providers for individual recommendations):

Magnesium

Essential for insulin signaling; deficiency common in insulin resistance. Found in nuts, seeds, dark leafy greens, dark chocolate.

Chromium

Enhances insulin receptor sensitivity. Found in broccoli, green beans, whole grains, meat.

Alpha-Lipoic Acid

Powerful antioxidant that improves insulin sensitivity; shown to help with diabetic neuropathy.

Berberine

Plant compound that improves blood sugar regulation through multiple mechanisms; comparable to some medications in studies.

üîë Coaching Integration: Working with Insulin Resistance

When supporting clients with insulin resistance:

  • Start with symptoms: Help clients connect their energy crashes, cravings, and weight struggles to blood sugar patterns
  • Advocate for testing: Encourage fasting insulin testing if only glucose has been checked
  • Focus on satiety: Protein and fat at meals reduce the blood sugar roller coaster that drives cravings
  • Make it practical: Post-meal walks are one of the most accessible, high-impact interventions
  • Celebrate non-scale victories: Improved energy, reduced cravings, and stable mood often precede weight changes

üìå Key Takeaways

  • Insulin is the master metabolic hormone, determining whether fuel is burned or stored; chronically elevated insulin promotes fat storage
  • Insulin resistance develops years before blood sugar rises‚Äîfasting insulin should be tested for early detection
  • Clinical signs include central obesity, skin changes, energy crashes, carb cravings, and afternoon fatigue
  • The triglyceride-to-HDL ratio is a simple, accessible marker of insulin sensitivity
  • Insulin resistance is reversible through dietary changes, exercise (especially resistance training), sleep optimization, and stress management

Understanding Metabolism

Reading Time: 11 minutes

Connection: In Module 11, we explored thyroid health—a key driver of metabolic rate. Now we expand our view to examine metabolism as a whole: what it actually is, how it's regulated, why it varies between individuals, and why the conventional "calories in, calories out" model fails to capture its true complexity. This understanding is essential before we address weight management in later lessons.

What You'll Learn

  • What metabolism actually is and its key components
  • The factors that determine individual metabolic rate
  • Why the "calories in, calories out" model is an oversimplification
  • How hormones orchestrate metabolic function
  • Why metabolism is dynamic and responsive to environment

Patricia's Story

Patricia, 48, sat across from her health coach with visible frustration. "I've done everything right," she said. "I track every calorie, I exercise five days a week, and I'm eating 1,200 calories a day. My friend eats whatever she wants and stays thin. The scale hasn't moved in months. My doctor says I must be 'cheating' on my diet, but I'm not. Is my metabolism just broken?"

Patricia's experience is painfully common. She's been told metabolism is simple math—burn more than you eat, and you'll lose weight. Yet her body isn't cooperating with this equation. The truth is far more nuanced, and understanding metabolism's true complexity is the first step toward helping clients like Patricia find sustainable solutions.

What Is Metabolism?

Metabolism refers to the sum of all chemical reactions occurring in the body to maintain life. These reactions fall into two categories:

  • Anabolism: Building processes that construct complex molecules from simpler ones, requiring energy (e.g., building muscle protein, storing fat, creating hormones)
  • Catabolism: Breaking down processes that disassemble complex molecules into simpler ones, releasing energy (e.g., breaking down glucose for fuel, releasing fatty acids from storage)

Metabolism is not simply about "burning calories"—it encompasses every process that keeps you alive: maintaining body temperature, pumping blood, regenerating cells, producing hormones, detoxifying chemicals, thinking, breathing, and yes, processing the food you eat.

The Components of Total Daily Energy Expenditure

When we talk about metabolic rate, we're usually referring to Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)—the total calories burned in a day. TDEE has several components:

Component % of TDEE Description
Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) 60-75% Energy to maintain vital functions at rest (breathing, circulation, cell production)
Thermic Effect of Food (TEF) 8-15% Energy to digest, absorb, and process food
Non-Exercise Activity (NEAT) 10-20% Fidgeting, walking, standing, household tasks—all non-exercise movement
Exercise Activity (EAT) 5-10% Intentional exercise and workouts

Notice that formal exercise typically accounts for only 5-10% of total calorie expenditure. This is why you cannot "out-exercise" a poor diet or a sluggish metabolism—the numbers simply don't add up. A one-hour gym session might burn 300-500 calories, but BMR and NEAT together burn 1,500-2,000+ calories daily.

The Power of NEAT

Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT) varies dramatically between individuals—by as much as 2,000 calories per day. This explains why some people seem to "eat whatever they want" without gaining weight. They may unconsciously move more: fidgeting, taking stairs, pacing while on calls, standing versus sitting. NEAT also decreases when people diet, as the body conserves energy—a key reason weight loss stalls.

Factors That Determine Metabolic Rate

Metabolic rate varies significantly between individuals. Understanding what influences this variation helps explain why standardized calorie recommendations fail so many people.

Body Composition

Muscle tissue is metabolically active—it burns calories even at rest. Fat tissue, while not metabolically inert (it produces hormones and requires blood supply), burns far fewer calories per pound than muscle. This is why:

  • Two people of the same weight can have very different metabolic rates depending on their muscle-to-fat ratio
  • Losing muscle through crash dieting or aging reduces metabolic rate
  • Building muscle is one of the most effective ways to sustainably increase metabolism
  • Men typically have higher metabolic rates than women partly due to greater muscle mass

Age

Metabolic rate naturally declines with age, primarily due to loss of muscle mass (sarcopenia) and decreased physical activity. Research suggests metabolism remains relatively stable from 20-60, with the decline accelerating after 60. Much of this decline is preventable with resistance training and adequate protein intake.

Hormonal Status

Multiple hormones regulate metabolic rate. Dysfunction in any of these systems can significantly impact energy expenditure:

Thyroid Hormones

The master metabolic regulators. Low thyroid function (even subclinical) can reduce metabolic rate by 15-40%.

Insulin

Regulates whether fuel is burned or stored. Insulin resistance promotes fat storage and impairs fat burning.

Cortisol

Chronic elevation promotes belly fat storage, muscle breakdown, and metabolic dysfunction.

Leptin & Ghrelin

Regulate hunger and satiety. Leptin resistance makes the brain perceive starvation even when body fat is high.

Sex Hormones

Estrogen and testosterone influence fat distribution, muscle mass, and metabolic efficiency. Menopause significantly impacts metabolism.

Growth Hormone

Supports muscle maintenance and fat burning. Declines with age and is suppressed by poor sleep.

Genetics

Genes influence metabolic rate, fat storage patterns, hunger signals, and response to different macronutrients. However, genetics typically account for only 20-30% of metabolic variation—leaving significant room for modification through lifestyle. Epigenetics—how genes are expressed based on environment—means that lifestyle choices can "turn on" or "turn off" metabolic genes.

Dieting History

A history of caloric restriction can persistently lower metabolic rate. This phenomenon, sometimes called "metabolic adaptation" or "adaptive thermogenesis," is the body's survival mechanism in response to perceived famine. Research on contestants from "The Biggest Loser" showed that many had metabolic rates 500+ calories per day lower than expected even six years after the show—their bodies had adapted to extreme restriction and never fully recovered.

Why "Calories In, Calories Out" Fails

The conventional model of weight management treats the body like a simple bank account: deposit fewer calories than you withdraw, and you'll lose weight. While thermodynamics (energy cannot be created or destroyed) is valid, this oversimplified model fails for several reasons.

The Body Adapts to Restriction

When calorie intake drops, the body doesn't simply burn fat to make up the difference. Instead, it implements survival mechanisms:

  • Reduces BMR: Slows down thyroid function, reduces body temperature, and decreases metabolic processes
  • Decreases NEAT: Unconsciously reduces movement—less fidgeting, slower walking, choosing to sit rather than stand
  • Increases hunger: Ghrelin (hunger hormone) rises; leptin (satiety hormone) falls
  • Reduces muscle: Without adequate protein and stimulation, muscle is catabolized for energy
  • Prioritizes fat storage: When food becomes available, the body preferentially stores it as fat for future famine

The Dieting Trap

This is why Patricia's 1,200-calorie diet stopped working. Her body adapted—metabolic rate dropped, NEAT decreased, hunger increased. She'd need to eat even less to continue losing weight, further damaging her metabolism. This is the "yo-yo diet" cycle that leaves many people with lower metabolic rates than before they started dieting.

Not All Calories Are Equal

The source of calories matters as much as the quantity. Different macronutrients have different metabolic effects:

Macronutrient Thermic Effect Hormonal Impact
Protein 20-35% of calories burned in digestion Increases satiety, supports muscle, minimal insulin spike
Carbohydrates 5-15% of calories burned in digestion Triggers insulin; refined carbs spike blood sugar and promote fat storage
Fat 0-5% of calories burned in digestion Increases satiety, minimal insulin impact, slow absorption

200 calories of protein affects the body very differently than 200 calories of refined carbohydrates. The protein increases satiety, requires more energy to digest, and supports muscle maintenance. The refined carbs spike blood sugar, trigger insulin release (promoting fat storage), and leave you hungry again within hours.

Timing and Context Matter

When you eat, what you eat it with, and your metabolic state all influence how food is processed:

  • The same meal eaten in the morning versus late at night has different metabolic consequences
  • Exercise changes how nutrients are partitioned (toward muscle rather than fat)
  • Stress hormones alter how food is processed
  • Sleep deprivation impairs insulin sensitivity, making the same food more fattening
  • The gut microbiome influences calorie extraction—some individuals extract more calories from the same food than others

The Hormonal Model of Metabolism

Rather than viewing metabolism as simple calorie accounting, a more accurate model recognizes that hormones orchestrate how food is processed, stored, and burned. The key question isn't just "how much are you eating?" but "what signals is your body receiving about what to do with that food?"

Insulin: The Storage Hormone

Insulin's primary job is to remove glucose from the bloodstream and direct it into cells. When cells are full, excess glucose is converted to fat for storage. Key principles:

  • High insulin = storage mode: When insulin is elevated, the body is focused on storing fuel, not releasing it
  • Low insulin = burning mode: When insulin is low (between meals, during fasting), the body can access stored fat for fuel
  • Insulin resistance = elevated baseline: The body produces more and more insulin to overcome resistance, keeping storage mode activated longer
  • Dietary drivers: Refined carbs, frequent eating, and excess calories (especially from carbs) chronically elevate insulin

Leptin: The Satiety Signal Gone Wrong

Leptin is produced by fat cells to signal the brain about energy stores. In a healthy system, more body fat = more leptin = reduced appetite and increased metabolism. But in leptin resistance:

  • The brain doesn't "hear" the leptin signal despite high levels
  • The brain perceives starvation even when body fat is excessive
  • Hunger increases and metabolism slows—the opposite of what should happen
  • This creates a vicious cycle where the body fights against fat loss

Leptin resistance is driven by inflammation, chronically elevated insulin, poor sleep, and a diet high in processed foods. Addressing these factors can help restore leptin sensitivity.

The Set Point Theory

The body appears to defend a particular weight range—the set point. When you lose weight, multiple systems (hormones, metabolism, hunger) work to push you back toward that set point. When you gain weight, opposing mechanisms should push you back down—but these are often impaired by modern lifestyle factors.

The good news: the set point can be shifted over time through sustained changes in diet quality, sleep, stress, exercise, and inflammation. It requires patience and addressing root causes rather than just restricting calories.

Coaching Integration: Reframing Metabolism

When working with clients like Patricia who feel their "metabolism is broken":

  • Validate their experience: Their body IS responding differently—this isn't willpower failure
  • Explain adaptation: Their metabolism adapted to protect them; it's working as designed for survival
  • Shift from calories to signals: Focus on the hormonal impact of food rather than just calorie counts
  • Emphasize restoration: The goal is to heal metabolism, not fight against it further
  • Set realistic timelines: Metabolic recovery takes time—often longer than the damage took to create

Key Takeaways

  • Metabolism encompasses all life-sustaining chemical reactions, with BMR accounting for 60-75% of daily energy expenditure
  • Metabolic rate is influenced by body composition, age, hormones, genetics, and dieting history—it's highly individual
  • The "calories in, calories out" model oversimplifies metabolism; the body adapts to restriction by lowering metabolic rate
  • Hormones—especially insulin and leptin—determine whether food is burned or stored; addressing hormonal health is key
  • The body defends a set point weight; shifting this requires sustained lifestyle changes, not just calorie cutting

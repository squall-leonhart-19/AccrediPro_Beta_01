Low-Carbohydrate Approaches

Low-carbohydrate eating exists on a spectrum, from moderate carb reduction to strict ketogenic approaches. All share a common principle: reducing carbohydrates to lower insulin and improve metabolic function.

The Carbohydrate Spectrum

Standard American Diet
  • Daily Carbs: 250-350g
  • 50%+ of calories from carbs, often refined
Moderate Low-Carb
  • Daily Carbs: 100-150g
  • Sustainable for most; allows fruits, whole grains
Low-Carb
  • Daily Carbs: 50-100g
  • Significant insulin reduction; emphasizes non-starchy vegetables
Ketogenic
  • Daily Carbs: 20-50g
  • Induces ketosis; body burns fat for fuel

The Ketogenic Approach

When carbohydrates drop below approximately 50 grams per day, the body enters ketosis‚Äîa metabolic state where the liver produces ketones from fat to fuel the brain and body.

Potential Benefits

Significant insulin reduction ‚Ä¢ Reduced hunger and cravings ‚Ä¢ Rapid initial weight loss ‚Ä¢ Improved blood sugar control ‚Ä¢ Mental clarity for some

‚ö†

Considerations

"Keto flu" during adaptation (1-2 weeks) ‚Ä¢ Restrictive; difficult for some to sustain ‚Ä¢ May affect thyroid function if too restrictive ‚Ä¢ Potential menstrual disruption in women ‚Ä¢ Nutrient concerns if poorly implemented

Coach Tip

When discussing low-carb approaches with clients, try: "Low-carb isn't about deprivation‚Äîit's about reducing the foods that spike your insulin and replacing them with satisfying, nutrient-dense options. Think of it as upgrading your fuel source. We can start with a moderate reduction and adjust based on how you feel."

Who May Benefit from Low-Carb Approaches

  • Insulin resistant clients: Those with prediabetes, type 2 diabetes, or PCOS often see dramatic improvements
  • Carbohydrate-sensitive individuals: Those who feel worse (brain fog, fatigue, cravings) after eating carbs
  • People with significant weight to lose: Low-carb can accelerate initial progress
  • Those who feel satisfied by higher-fat foods: Personal food preferences matter for sustainability
Cautions for Low-Carb
  • Not recommended for those with history of eating disorders
  • May need modification for high-intensity athletes
  • Women may need higher carbs around menstruation
  • Can worsen thyroid function if too extreme or prolonged
  • Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid strict keto

Mediterranean-Style Eating

The Mediterranean diet is the most studied dietary pattern in the world, with robust evidence for cardiovascular, metabolic, and cognitive benefits.

Key Components

Mediterranean Diet Foundations

Multiple servings daily, especially leafy greens and colorful varieties

ü´í Extra Virgin Olive Oil

Primary fat source, used liberally

ü´ò Legumes

Beans, lentils, chickpeas regularly

üåæ Whole Grains

Whole wheat, oats, barley‚Äînot refined

üêü Fish and Seafood

2-3 times weekly, especially fatty fish

ü•ö Moderate Poultry, Eggs, Dairy

Greek yogurt, cheese in moderation

ü•© Limited Red Meat

Coach Tip

Make Mediterranean eating practical: Start with one change at a time. This week, switch from vegetable oil to extra virgin olive oil. Next week, add a handful of nuts as a snack. The following week, introduce one plant-based dinner. Small, sustainable changes create lasting habits.

Evidence for Metabolic Health

The Mediterranean diet has been shown to:

üåü Research-Backed Benefits

Diabetes Prevention

Reduce type 2 diabetes risk by 52% compared to low-fat diets (PREDIMED study)

Insulin Sensitivity

Improve insulin sensitivity independent of weight loss

Inflammation Reduction

Significant decreases in CRP and IL-6 markers

Weight Management

Support sustainable weight loss, particularly when higher in fat and lower in grains

Cardiovascular Protection

Reduce cardiovascular events by approximately 30%

Who May Benefit from Mediterranean Eating

  • Those seeking moderation over restriction: Less extreme than keto, easier for many to sustain
  • Cardiovascular risk: Strong evidence for heart health benefits
  • Inflammation-driven conditions: The anti-inflammatory profile helps many conditions
  • Those who enjoy cooking: This approach emphasizes whole foods and home cooking
  • Social eaters: More flexible for dining out and family meals

Plant-Based and Vegan Approaches

Plant-based eating exists on a spectrum from plant-forward (emphasizing but not excluding animal foods) to strict veganism (no animal products).

Benefits for Metabolic Health

Well-designed plant-based diets offer several metabolic advantages:

  • High fiber content: Supports blood sugar stability, gut health, and satiety
  • Lower calorie density: Easier to maintain caloric deficit without hunger
  • Anti-inflammatory phytonutrients: Polyphenols, carotenoids, and other plant compounds
  • Improved insulin sensitivity: Several studies show benefits for blood sugar control
  • Microbiome support: Plant diversity feeds beneficial gut bacteria
Coach Tip

If a client wants to try plant-based eating, focus on addition before subtraction. First, help them add more plant foods‚Äîvegetables, legumes, nuts, seeds‚Äîto their current diet. Once they're comfortable with preparation and recipes, they can gradually reduce animal products if desired. This prevents the "junk food vegan" trap.

Potential Challenges

Nutritional Considerations for Plant-Based Diets
  • Protein adequacy: Requires intentional planning; may need more volume of food
  • Vitamin B12: Supplementation essential for strict vegans
  • Iron and zinc: Less bioavailable from plant sources; pair with vitamin C
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: May need algae-based EPA/DHA supplement
  • Vitamin D: Often low regardless of diet; may need supplementation
  • "Junk food vegan" trap: Processed vegan foods aren't healthier than whole animal foods

Who May Thrive with Plant-Based Eating

  • Those with ethical or environmental motivations: Intrinsic motivation supports adherence
  • Those who don't enjoy meat: Preference matters for sustainability
  • Those with constipation or digestive issues: High fiber can help
  • Those willing to plan and supplement: Requires more nutritional awareness

Intermittent Fasting Approaches

Intermittent fasting (IF) is a pattern of eating rather than a diet‚Äîit defines when to eat rather than what to eat. It can be combined with any of the dietary approaches above.

Common IF Protocols

12:12
  • 12-hour eating window; 12-hour fast
  • Best For: Beginners; women who need gentler approach
16:8
  • 8-hour eating window; 16-hour fast
  • Best For: Most popular; balances benefits with sustainability
5:2
  • Normal eating 5 days; 500-600 cal 2 days
  • Best For: Those who prefer fewer daily restrictions
24-Hour Fast
  • One meal per day or dinner-to-dinner fast
  • Best For: Experienced fasters; intermittent use

Metabolic Benefits of Fasting

  • Insulin reduction: Extended periods without food allow insulin to drop significantly
  • Improved insulin sensitivity: Cells become more responsive to insulin
  • Autophagy activation: Cellular cleanup processes triggered during extended fasting
  • Simplified eating: Fewer meals to plan and prepare
  • Natural calorie reduction: Shorter eating window often results in less total intake

Special Considerations for Women

Women's hormones are more sensitive to fasting signals, and aggressive IF can disrupt menstrual cycles and thyroid function:

Women-Friendly Fasting Guidelines
  • Start with 12:12 or 14:10 rather than 16:8
  • Fast 3-4 days per week rather than daily
  • Avoid fasting during luteal phase (pre-menstrual)
  • Ensure adequate calories during eating window
  • Monitor menstrual cycle for changes
  • Discontinue if sleep, mood, or cycle are affected
Coach Tip

When introducing intermittent fasting to female clients, emphasize: "We're not trying to restrict calories‚Äîwe're trying to give your body extended breaks from digestion and insulin production. If you feel worse, not better, it's a sign this approach needs modification or isn't right for you right now. Your body's feedback is our guide."

Matching Clients with the Right Approach

The "best" diet is the one a client can sustain while meeting their nutritional needs. Several factors guide this matching process.

Assessment Questions

Key Questions for Dietary Personalization
  1. Metabolic status: Insulin resistance, diabetes, PCOS? Low-carb often beneficial
  2. Response to carbohydrates: Do they feel worse (foggy, tired, craving) or better after eating carbs?
  3. Food preferences: Do they love or hate specific food categories?
  4. Lifestyle factors: Cooking ability, time, budget, family situation
  5. Eating history: What's worked or failed before? Why?
  6. Ethical considerations: Do they have moral preferences about food?
  7. Relationship with food: Any history of disordered eating? Restrictive approaches may be harmful
  8. Goals: Weight loss, energy, blood sugar control, disease prevention?

Decision Framework

Matching Clients to Dietary Approaches

Significant Insulin Resistance/Prediabetes

Consider: Low-carb or Mediterranean (lower grain)

Feels Terrible Restricting Carbs

Consider: Mediterranean or plant-forward with complex carbs

History of Disordered Eating

Consider: Non-restrictive approach; avoid IF and strict protocols

Needs Simplicity and Structure

Consider: Clear framework like keto or paleo

Wants Flexibility and Moderation

Consider: Mediterranean or "plate method" approach

Cardiovascular Concerns

Consider: Mediterranean (strongest cardiovascular evidence)

Perimenopausal/Menopausal

Consider: Higher protein + Mediterranean or moderate low-carb

Pause & Reflect

Think about your own dietary journey. What approaches have you tried? What worked and what didn't? How did your body communicate what was right or wrong for you? This self-awareness helps you guide clients with genuine empathy and understanding.

Coach Tip

Avoid dietary dogma. The moment you become attached to one approach as "the best," you limit your effectiveness as a coach. Stay curious about what works for each individual. Your role is to guide exploration, not prescribe a one-size-fits-all solution. The best diet is always the one your client can sustain while feeling energized and nourished.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What distinguishes a ketogenic diet from other low-carb approaches?

Answer: A ketogenic diet restricts carbohydrates to approximately 20-50g per day, which induces ketosis‚Äîa metabolic state where the liver produces ketones from fat to fuel the brain and body. Other low-carb approaches may reduce carbs to 50-150g but don't necessarily induce ketosis.

2

What are three key cautions for women considering intermittent fasting?

Answer: (1) Start with gentler protocols like 12:12 or 14:10 rather than aggressive 16:8; (2) Fast 3-4 days per week rather than daily; (3) Avoid fasting during the luteal phase (pre-menstrual). Women's hormones are more sensitive to fasting signals, and aggressive IF can disrupt menstrual cycles and thyroid function.

3

Which dietary approach has the strongest evidence for cardiovascular health?

Answer: The Mediterranean diet has the most robust evidence for cardiovascular health, including a 30% reduction in cardiovascular events. The PREDIMED study showed significant benefits with Mediterranean eating supplemented with extra virgin olive oil or nuts.

4

What are two essential nutritional considerations for clients following a strict vegan diet?

Answer: (1) Vitamin B12 supplementation is essential, as B12 is only found naturally in animal products; (2) Protein adequacy requires intentional planning, as plant proteins are less concentrated and may require larger food volumes. Additional considerations include iron, zinc, omega-3 fatty acids (algae-based EPA/DHA), and avoiding the "junk food vegan" trap.

Key Takeaways
  • Low-carbohydrate approaches can be powerful for insulin resistance but may not suit everyone, especially women needing gentler approaches
  • Mediterranean eating has the strongest overall evidence for metabolic and cardiovascular health
  • Plant-based diets can support metabolic health but require careful planning for nutrient adequacy
  • Intermittent fasting is a tool that can enhance any dietary approach but needs modification for women
  • Matching clients with the right approach requires assessing their metabolic status, preferences, history, and lifestyle
  • The best diet is always the one a client can sustain long-term while feeling energized and nourished

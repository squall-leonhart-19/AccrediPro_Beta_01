The Digestive Journey: A Quick Review

Before we can optimize digestion, we need to understand the complete process. Digestion begins long before food enters your mouth‚Äîit starts when you see, smell, or even think about food.

The Phases of Digestion

1. Cephalic Phase (Brain)

Seeing, smelling, or thinking about food triggers the vagus nerve to prepare the digestive system. Saliva production increases, stomach acid is released, and digestive enzymes are primed. This phase can account for 20-30% of digestive secretions.

2. Oral Phase (Mouth)

Chewing breaks food into smaller particles while saliva adds amylase (for carbohydrate digestion) and lingual lipase (for fats). The tongue forms a bolus that's swallowed into the esophagus.

3. Gastric Phase (Stomach)

Hydrochloric acid (HCl) denatures proteins and kills pathogens. Pepsin breaks down proteins into smaller peptides. The stomach churns food into a thick liquid called chyme, held for 2-4 hours.

4. Small Intestine Phase

The pancreas releases digestive enzymes and bicarbonate (to neutralize acid). The gallbladder releases bile for fat emulsification. Most nutrient absorption occurs here through the intestinal lining.

5. Large Intestine Phase (Colon)

Remaining water is absorbed. Fiber is fermented by bacteria, producing short-chain fatty acids. Waste is compacted and eventually eliminated.

The Critical Role of Stomach Acid

Stomach acid is one of the most underappreciated aspects of digestive health. Many people assume acid is problematic (thanks to antacid marketing), but adequate stomach acid is essential for:

Why Stomach Acid Matters

Protein Digestion: HCl activates pepsin, the enzyme that breaks down proteins. Without adequate acid, protein digestion is incomplete.
Mineral Absorption: Calcium, magnesium, iron, zinc, and B12 all require an acidic environment for proper absorption.
Pathogen Defense: Stomach acid kills bacteria, parasites, and other harmful organisms that enter with food.
Signaling Cascade: Adequate acid triggers the release of digestive enzymes from the pancreas and bile from the gallbladder.

Signs of Low Stomach Acid (Hypochlorhydria)

Hypochlorhydria‚Äîinsufficient stomach acid‚Äîis surprisingly common, especially in adults over 40, those under chronic stress, and those taking acid-suppressing medications. Symptoms include:

1
Bloating, burping, or gas immediately after meals (food sits without proper breakdown)
2
Feeling overly full after eating (food not moving through efficiently)
3
Heartburn or reflux (paradoxically, low acid can cause reflux)
4
Undigested food in stool (especially proteins and vegetables)
5
Multiple food sensitivities (improperly digested proteins trigger reactions)
6
Weak, peeling, or cracked fingernails (protein malabsorption)
7
Hair loss or thinning (mineral and protein deficiencies)
8
Frequent infections or SIBO (pathogens surviving low-acid environment)

The Heartburn Paradox

Many people experiencing heartburn assume they have too much acid. However, research suggests that low stomach acid is often the culprit. When acid is insufficient, food sits in the stomach too long and ferments. The resulting gases push the lower esophageal sphincter open, allowing even small amounts of acid to enter the esophagus‚Äîcausing the burning sensation.

Key insight: Before assuming excess acid, consider that the opposite may be true. This should always be evaluated by a healthcare provider before making changes.

The "Rest and Digest" State

The autonomic nervous system has two branches that affect digestion profoundly:

‚ùå Sympathetic State ("Fight or Flight")

When stressed, anxious, or rushed, the body diverts resources away from digestion toward survival functions:

  • Blood flow shifts away from digestive organs
  • Digestive enzyme secretion decreases
  • Stomach acid production drops
  • Gut motility slows or becomes erratic
  • The immune system in the gut is suppressed

‚úì Parasympathetic State ("Rest and Digest")

When calm and relaxed, the body prioritizes digestive function:

  • Blood flows to digestive organs
  • Digestive enzymes are released abundantly
  • Stomach acid production is optimal
  • Gut motility is coordinated and effective
  • The gut immune system functions properly

The implication: How you eat may be as important as what you eat. Eating while stressed, distracted, or on-the-go undermines even the healthiest diet.

Natural Ways to Support Digestion

Mindful Eating Practices

The Foundations of Mindful Eating

1. Create a Calm Environment
  • Eat at a table, not at a desk or in front of screens
  • Turn off devices and put away phones
  • Create pleasant surroundings when possible
2. Prepare the Nervous System
  • Take 3-5 deep breaths before eating
  • Express gratitude (shifts to parasympathetic state)
  • Look at and smell your food before eating (activates cephalic phase)
3. Chew Thoroughly
  • Aim for 20-30 chews per bite
  • Put your fork down between bites
  • Food should be liquid before swallowing
  • "Drink your food, chew your drinks" (ancient wisdom)
4. Slow Down
  • Extend meals to at least 20 minutes
  • This allows satiety signals to reach the brain
  • Rushed eating leads to overeating and poor digestion
5. Limit Liquids with Meals
  • Excessive fluid dilutes digestive secretions
  • Sip small amounts as needed
  • Drink most fluids between meals

Natural Digestive Support

üçã Acid-Supporting Foods

  • Lemon water: Warm water with lemon 15-20 minutes before meals
  • Apple cider vinegar: 1-2 tsp in water before meals (raw, unfiltered)
  • Fermented vegetables: Small amount at the start of meals
  • Ginger: Fresh ginger tea or slices

üåø Bitter Herbs (Digestive Bitters)

Bitters stimulate bitter taste receptors on the tongue, triggering a cascade that increases digestive secretions:

  • Gentian root: One of the most bitter herbs, potent digestive stimulant
  • Dandelion: Supports bile flow and liver function
  • Artichoke: Promotes bile production
  • Chamomile: Gentle bitter that also soothes the digestive tract
  • Bitter salad greens: Arugula, radicchio, endive

Use: Take bitter herbs or a bitter formula 15-20 minutes before meals.

ü¶¥ Digestive-Supporting Foods

  • Bone broth: Glycine and gelatin support the digestive lining
  • Fermented foods: Sauerkraut, kimchi, kefir provide enzymes and probiotics
  • Papaya: Contains papain enzyme (aids protein digestion)
  • Pineapple: Contains bromelain enzyme
  • Ginger: Promotes motility and soothes the stomach

üíä Supplemental Support (with practitioner guidance)

  • Betaine HCl: Supplemental stomach acid for those with low production
  • Digestive enzymes: Protease, lipase, amylase for protein, fat, and carb digestion
  • Ox bile: For those without a gallbladder or with sluggish bile flow
  • DGL (deglycyrrhizinated licorice): Soothes and protects the stomach lining

Lifestyle Factors That Impact Digestion

Supporting Digestion Through Lifestyle

Stress Management:

Chronic stress is one of the biggest digestive disruptors. Daily stress-reduction practices‚Äîmeditation, deep breathing, yoga, time in nature‚Äîsupport the parasympathetic state needed for optimal digestion.

Meal Timing and Spacing:

Allow 4-5 hours between meals for complete digestion and MMC activation. Avoid eating within 2-3 hours of bedtime‚Äîthe digestive system slows at night.

Movement:

Light movement after meals (a 10-15 minute walk) aids digestion. Regular physical activity supports healthy gut motility and microbiome diversity.

Sleep:

Quality sleep allows the digestive system to rest and repair. Poor sleep impairs digestive function and increases inflammation.

Hydration:

Adequate hydration supports mucus production and regular elimination. Drink most water between meals rather than with meals.

Signs of Healthy Digestion

How do you know when digestion is working well? Here are markers of optimal digestive function:

No bloating or discomfort after meals ‚Äî You should feel satisfied, not stuffed or uncomfortable
Regular, easy bowel movements ‚Äî Daily, formed stools that pass without straining
Minimal gas ‚Äî Some gas is normal; excessive gas indicates fermentation issues
Stable energy after eating ‚Äî No energy crashes or post-meal fatigue
No visible undigested food ‚Äî Food should be fully broken down by elimination
Strong nails, healthy hair ‚Äî Signs of proper nutrient absorption

Coaching Strategies for Digestive Support

Practical Coaching Approaches

Start with How, Not What:

Before overhauling what clients eat, address how they eat. Eating habits are often easier to change than the entire diet, and improvements can be dramatic.

Use the "One Habit at a Time" Approach:
  • Week 1: Three deep breaths before meals
  • Week 2: Add chewing (20-30 times per bite)
  • Week 3: Add eating without screens
  • Week 4: Add lemon water or bitters before meals
Track Symptoms:

Have clients keep a simple food-symptom diary. Track not just what they ate, but how (rushed? stressed? while working?). Patterns often emerge quickly.

Address the Stress Connection:

Many digestive issues are stress-related. Help clients see the mind-gut connection and implement stress management as a digestive strategy.

Know When to Refer:

Persistent symptoms despite lifestyle changes, blood in stool, unexplained weight loss, severe pain‚Äîthese require medical evaluation. Partner with healthcare providers.

What Is SIBO?

Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) occurs when bacteria‚Äînormally concentrated in the large intestine‚Äîmigrate and proliferate in the small intestine, where they don't belong. While the large intestine houses trillions of bacteria, the small intestine should contain relatively few.

This matters because the small intestine is where most nutrient absorption occurs. When bacteria colonize this space, they:

  • Ferment carbohydrates before you can absorb them, producing gas (hydrogen or methane)
  • Compete for nutrients, potentially causing deficiencies (B12, iron, fat-soluble vitamins)
  • Damage the intestinal lining, contributing to leaky gut
  • Deconjugate bile acids, impairing fat digestion
  • Trigger inflammation, causing systemic symptoms beyond the gut

What Causes SIBO?

The body has several mechanisms to prevent bacterial overgrowth in the small intestine. SIBO develops when these protective mechanisms fail:

üåä Impaired Motility

The migrating motor complex (MMC)‚Äîthe "housekeeping wave" that sweeps bacteria downward between meals‚Äîis crucial. When this movement is impaired, bacteria accumulate. Causes include:

  • Diabetes (nerve damage affects gut motility)
  • Hypothyroidism (slowed metabolism, slowed gut)
  • Post-infectious IBS (food poisoning can damage MMC nerves)
  • Chronic stress (impairs digestive movement)
  • Medications (opioids, antidepressants, anticholinergics)
  • Scleroderma and other connective tissue disorders

üß™ Low Stomach Acid

Stomach acid is a first-line defense against bacterial overgrowth. When acid is low, more bacteria survive passage to the small intestine. Causes include:

  • Long-term PPI use (proton pump inhibitors)
  • H. pylori infection (reduces acid production)
  • Aging (natural decline in acid production)
  • Chronic stress (suppresses digestive secretions)
  • Gastric bypass surgery

üöß Structural Issues

Physical abnormalities can create areas where bacteria stagnate:

  • Intestinal adhesions (from surgery or endometriosis)
  • Ileocecal valve dysfunction (valve between small and large intestine)
  • Diverticula (pouches in the intestinal wall)
  • Strictures or narrowing
  • Previous abdominal surgeries

üõ°Ô∏è Immune Dysfunction

The immune system helps control bacterial populations. Conditions that impair immunity increase SIBO risk:

  • IgA deficiency
  • Immunosuppressive medications
  • Chronic illness
  • Chronic stress (suppresses immune function)

üçΩÔ∏è The Food Poisoning Connection

Research by Dr. Mark Pimentel revealed that many cases of SIBO begin with acute gastroenteritis (food poisoning). The body produces antibodies against the bacterial toxin cytolethal distending toxin B (CdtB). Unfortunately, these antibodies can cross-react with vinculin‚Äîa protein essential for MMC function. This autoimmune-like response impairs gut motility, setting the stage for bacterial overgrowth.

Key insight: When clients say their digestive issues began after a "stomach bug," SIBO should be considered.

Types of SIBO

SIBO is classified by the type of gas the overgrown bacteria produce:

Hydrogen-Dominant SIBO

The bacteria: Primarily gram-negative bacteria that ferment carbohydrates and produce hydrogen gas.

Typical presentation:

  • Diarrhea or loose stools
  • Bloating and abdominal distension
  • Urgency
  • Symptoms often appear quickly after eating

Treatment response: Generally responds well to antimicrobial treatment.

Methane-Dominant SIBO (IMO)

The organisms: Archaea (methanogenic organisms)‚Äîtechnically not bacteria‚Äîconvert hydrogen to methane. Now often called Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO).

Typical presentation:

  • Constipation (methane slows gut motility)
  • Bloating and distension
  • Feeling of incomplete evacuation
  • Weight gain or difficulty losing weight

Treatment response: Often more resistant to treatment; may require longer protocols.

Hydrogen Sulfide SIBO

The bacteria: Sulfur-reducing bacteria that produce hydrogen sulfide gas.

Typical presentation:

  • Diarrhea
  • Foul-smelling gas ("rotten egg" smell)
  • Sulfur sensitivity (reactions to sulfur-containing foods)
  • May have both constipation and diarrhea

Testing note: Requires specialized testing (newer breath tests that measure hydrogen sulfide directly).

Recognizing SIBO: Signs and Symptoms

SIBO symptoms can overlap significantly with IBS and other conditions. Key indicators include:

Digestive Symptoms

‚óè Bloating: Often the most prominent symptom; worse as the day progresses
‚óè Excessive gas: Belching and/or flatulence
‚óè Abdominal pain/discomfort: Cramping, pressure
‚óè Altered bowel habits: Diarrhea, constipation, or alternating
‚óè Nausea: Especially after eating

Systemic Symptoms

‚óè Fatigue: From malabsorption and immune activation
‚óè Brain fog: Cognitive impairment from gut-brain connection
‚óè Joint pain: Systemic inflammation
‚óè Skin issues: Rosacea, acne, eczema (gut-skin axis)
‚óè Mood changes: Anxiety, depression

üö© Red Flag Pattern: When Healthy Foods Make You Worse

One of the most telling SIBO indicators: symptoms worsen when eating "healthy" high-fiber foods. Clients may report:

  • "The more vegetables I eat, the worse I feel"
  • "Beans and legumes destroy me"
  • "I tried adding more fiber and my bloating got unbearable"
  • "Fermented foods make everything worse"

This pattern occurs because bacteria ferment the fiber and prebiotics before proper absorption can occur. While counterintuitive, temporarily reducing these foods often provides relief.

Testing for SIBO

Accurate diagnosis requires appropriate testing. The most common approach:

The SIBO Breath Test

The lactulose or glucose breath test is the primary diagnostic tool:

How it works:
  1. Client follows a specific prep diet (24-48 hours before)
  2. Fasts for 12 hours before the test
  3. Drinks a sugar solution (lactulose or glucose)
  4. Breath samples collected every 15-20 minutes for 2-3 hours
  5. Samples analyzed for hydrogen and methane levels

Interpretation: Early rise in hydrogen and/or elevated methane suggests bacterial fermentation in the small intestine. Different labs use different cutoff values; interpretation should be done by qualified practitioners.

Lactulose Breath Test

Pros: Tests the entire small intestine; lactulose isn't absorbed

Cons: May produce false positives if transit is fast; less specific

Glucose Breath Test

Pros: More specific; glucose absorbed quickly by bacteria

Cons: Only tests upper small intestine; may miss distal SIBO

Trio-Smart Test (newer)

Pros: Measures hydrogen, methane, and hydrogen sulfide

Cons: Newer test; less widely available

Treatment Approaches

SIBO treatment typically involves antimicrobial therapy combined with dietary modification and addressing underlying causes:

Antimicrobial Treatment Options

Pharmaceutical Antibiotics:

Rifaximin (Xifaxan) is the most studied; it stays in the gut and has minimal systemic absorption. For methane-dominant SIBO, rifaximin is often combined with neomycin or metronidazole. Treatment typically lasts 2-4 weeks.

Herbal Antimicrobials:

Research shows herbal protocols can be equally effective as rifaximin. Common combinations include:

  • Berberine-containing herbs (goldenseal, Oregon grape)
  • Allicin (garlic extract)
  • Oregano oil
  • Neem
  • Cinnamon

Herbal protocols typically run 4-6 weeks.

Elemental Diet:

A liquid diet of pre-digested nutrients absorbed in the upper small intestine, starving bacteria lower down. Effective but challenging; typically reserved for difficult cases or those who don't tolerate antimicrobials.

Dietary Support During and After Treatment

Low FODMAP Diet

Reduces fermentable carbohydrates (Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, and Polyols) that feed bacteria. Often used during treatment to reduce symptoms. Key restrictions include:

  • Garlic, onions, wheat, rye
  • Beans, lentils, chickpeas
  • Apples, pears, stone fruits
  • Dairy products with lactose
  • Sugar alcohols (sorbitol, mannitol)

Specific Carbohydrate Diet (SCD)

Eliminates complex carbohydrates that require extensive digestion. Allows only monosaccharides (simple sugars) that absorb quickly, reducing substrate for bacterial fermentation.

Bi-Phasic Diet

Dr. Nirala Jacobi's approach combines low FODMAP with SCD principles in two phases‚Äîa more restrictive phase during treatment, then gradual reintroduction.

Preventing SIBO Relapse

SIBO has a notoriously high relapse rate‚Äîup to 50% or more. Preventing recurrence requires addressing underlying causes:

Key Prevention Strategies

Support Migrating Motor Complex (MMC):
  • Space meals 4-5 hours apart (MMC only activates during fasting)
  • Avoid constant snacking or grazing
  • Prokinetic agents may be prescribed by providers (natural options include ginger, 5-HTP)
Address Stomach Acid:
  • Work with providers to wean off PPIs if appropriate
  • Support natural acid production (bitter herbs, apple cider vinegar)
  • Test for and treat H. pylori if present
Manage Stress:
  • Chronic stress impairs MMC and digestive function
  • Regular stress-reduction practices are therapeutic, not optional
  • Vagal toning exercises (deep breathing, cold exposure, humming)
Address Structural Issues:
  • Work with providers to evaluate ileocecal valve function
  • Address adhesions if present (manual therapy may help)
  • Support motility through appropriate exercise

‚è∞ The Meal Spacing Rule

The migrating motor complex (MMC)‚Äîthe "cleaning wave" that sweeps bacteria from the small intestine‚Äîonly activates in a fasted state, typically 90-120 minutes after eating. Each time you eat, the MMC stops.

For clients with SIBO history: Spacing meals 4-5 hours apart allows the MMC to complete its full cycle. Avoid grazing, constant snacking, or eating every 2-3 hours. This simple change can significantly reduce relapse risk.

üîë Key Takeaways

  • SIBO occurs when bacteria overgrow in the small intestine, causing fermentation, malabsorption, and systemic symptoms
  • Up to 80% of IBS patients may have underlying SIBO
  • Three types exist: hydrogen-dominant (diarrhea), methane-dominant (constipation), and hydrogen sulfide
  • Common causes include impaired motility (MMC dysfunction), low stomach acid, structural issues, and post-infectious damage
  • The "healthy foods make me worse" pattern is a key SIBO indicator
  • Breath testing (lactulose or glucose) is the primary diagnostic tool
  • Treatment includes antimicrobials (pharmaceutical or herbal) plus dietary modification
  • Preventing relapse requires addressing root causes‚Äîespecially meal spacing to support MMC function

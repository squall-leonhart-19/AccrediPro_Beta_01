Case Study 1: The "Healthy Eater" with Worsening Symptoms

Client Profile: Rachel, 39

Occupation: Marketing executive

Chief Complaints: Severe bloating, abdominal distension, gas, fatigue, brain fog

History: Symptoms developed gradually over 3 years; worsened significantly when she "cleaned up" her diet

Notable: "The healthier I eat, the worse I feel." Previously diagnosed with IBS.

üîç Red Flags & Pattern Recognition

Rachel's pattern‚Äîsymptoms worsening with healthy foods‚Äîis a classic indicator of SIBO. When she increased fiber, vegetables, and fermented foods, she was feeding bacteria in the wrong place, causing fermentation and gas production.

üîß 5R Protocol Application

REMOVE:
  • Breath testing confirmed hydrogen-dominant SIBO
  • Herbal antimicrobial protocol (berberine, oregano, neem) for 6 weeks
  • Temporary low-FODMAP diet to reduce fermentable substrates
  • Removed gluten and dairy (suspected sensitivities)
REPLACE:
  • Digestive enzymes with meals (her high stress job likely impacted enzyme production)
  • Apple cider vinegar in water before meals to support stomach acid
REINOCULATE:
  • Delayed until after antimicrobial treatment
  • Started with spore-based probiotics (less likely to feed SIBO)
  • Gradual introduction of prebiotic foods once SIBO resolved
REPAIR:
  • L-glutamine powder (5g 2x/day) after antimicrobial phase
  • Bone broth daily
  • Zinc carnosine for mucosal support
REBALANCE:
  • Meal spacing (4-5 hours between meals) to support MMC function
  • Stress management (deep breathing before meals, meditation app)
  • Prokinetic support (ginger tea between meals) to prevent relapse

üë§ Coaching Role

  • Educated Rachel on SIBO and why healthy foods were making her worse
  • Provided meal planning support for low-FODMAP phase
  • Helped establish meal spacing and mindful eating practices
  • Supported stress management routine implementation
  • Coordinated with functional medicine practitioner for testing and antimicrobials

üìä Outcome

Week 4: 50% improvement in bloating

Week 8: Bloating minimal; energy improving; brain fog clearing

Week 16: Able to reintroduce most foods without symptoms; repeat breath test negative

üí° Key Lesson: When healthy foods cause symptoms, look deeper. SIBO is often missed because standard IBS treatments don't address the underlying bacterial overgrowth. Meal spacing is crucial for preventing relapse.

Case Study 2: The Stressed Executive with Multiple Symptoms

Client Profile: Diana, 52

Occupation: CEO of a tech startup

Chief Complaints: Joint pain, skin rashes (eczema), food sensitivities (seems to react to everything), anxiety, poor sleep, chronic constipation

History: Symptoms developed over past 5 years during high-stress period; multiple doctor visits with no clear diagnosis; currently takes antacids daily

Notable: "I feel like my body is attacking itself."

üîç Red Flags & Pattern Recognition

Diana's multi-system symptoms‚Äîjoint pain, skin issues, widespread food sensitivities, anxiety‚Äîsuggest systemic inflammation originating from the gut. Long-term antacid use likely contributed to SIBO risk. Her high-stress lifestyle impairs the parasympathetic state needed for healing.

üîß 5R Protocol Application

REMOVE:
  • Comprehensive elimination diet (gluten, dairy, eggs, soy, corn, sugar)
  • Stool testing revealed dysbiosis and low secretory IgA
  • Gradual weaning from antacids under medical supervision
  • Addressed chronic stress as primary "toxin" to remove
REPLACE:
  • Digestive bitters before meals (to stimulate natural secretions)
  • Betaine HCl supplementation (after antacid weaning)
  • Comprehensive digestive enzymes
REINOCULATE:
  • High-quality multi-strain probiotic
  • Fermented foods introduced slowly
  • Prebiotic fibers added gradually as tolerance improved
REPAIR:
  • L-glutamine (10g 2x/day)
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (anti-inflammatory)
  • Curcumin for systemic inflammation
  • Collagen peptides
  • Zinc and vitamin D (testing revealed deficiencies)
REBALANCE:
  • Non-negotiable: 10 minutes meditation daily
  • Sleep hygiene protocol (electronics off by 9 PM, consistent bedtime)
  • Delegated work responsibilities to reduce stress load
  • Walking after dinner (aids motility and stress reduction)

üë§ Coaching Role

  • Helped Diana see the connection between her stress, gut, and systemic symptoms
  • Worked on mindful eating practices (she previously ate while working)
  • Created accountability structure for meditation and sleep habits
  • Supported elimination diet implementation with meal planning
  • Helped identify life adjustments to reduce stress (critical for healing)
  • Coordinated with FM practitioner and encouraged collaboration with her GP

üìä Outcome

Week 6: Constipation improved; sleep slightly better

Week 12: Joint pain reduced by 60%; skin clearing; anxiety less intense

Week 24: Successfully reintroduced most foods; identified gluten and dairy as primary triggers; off antacids; sleeping well

üí° Key Lesson: Systemic symptoms (joints, skin, brain) often trace back to gut inflammation and permeability. Stress management isn't optional‚Äîit's essential for gut healing. The Rebalance phase may be the most important for long-term success.

Case Study 3: Post-Food Poisoning IBS

Client Profile: Michelle, 44

Occupation: Elementary school teacher

Chief Complaints: Chronic diarrhea, urgency, abdominal cramping, weight loss, fatigue

History: Healthy until a severe bout of food poisoning 2 years ago during international travel; never fully recovered; diagnosed with "post-infectious IBS"

Notable: "Before that trip, I had a stomach of steel. Now I can barely leave the house."

üîç Red Flags & Pattern Recognition

Michelle's history is textbook post-infectious IBS with likely SIBO development. Food poisoning can trigger autoantibodies against vinculin, damaging the MMC. This creates a favorable environment for bacterial overgrowth. The diarrhea-predominant presentation suggests hydrogen-dominant SIBO.

üîß 5R Protocol Application

REMOVE:
  • Breath test confirmed hydrogen-dominant SIBO
  • Stool testing revealed residual parasitic infection (Blastocystis hominis)
  • Conventional antibiotic (rifaximin) followed by herbal anti-parasitic protocol
  • Low-FODMAP diet during treatment
REPLACE:
  • Digestive enzymes (especially helpful given diarrhea and malabsorption)
  • Betaine HCl (to prevent future bacterial survival)
REINOCULATE:
  • Saccharomyces boulardii (especially helpful for diarrhea and post-infectious recovery)
  • Multi-strain probiotic introduced after antimicrobials completed
  • Gradual prebiotic introduction
REPAIR:
  • L-glutamine for intestinal repair
  • Colostrum (immune support and gut healing)
  • Serum-derived bovine immunoglobulins (IgG) for gut barrier support
REBALANCE:
  • Critical focus on prokinetic support (ginger, 5-HTP at bedtime)
  • Strict meal spacing to prevent SIBO recurrence
  • Stress management (the anxiety around food and leaving home had become significant)
  • Gentle exercise reintroduced as energy improved

üë§ Coaching Role

  • Validated Michelle's experience (many post-infectious IBS patients feel dismissed)
  • Provided emotional support‚Äîher quality of life had suffered dramatically
  • Helped implement low-FODMAP diet (complex but manageable with guidance)
  • Created meal spacing structure compatible with teaching schedule
  • Addressed food fear and anxiety that had developed
  • Celebrated small wins to maintain motivation during the longer healing process

üìä Outcome

Week 8: Diarrhea frequency reduced by 50%; less urgency

Week 16: Formed stools most days; energy returning

Week 24: Weight stabilizing; able to eat out again; traveling without fear

Week 36: Continued improvement; maintaining with prokinetics and lifestyle changes

üí° Key Lesson: Post-infectious IBS often involves structural damage to the MMC. Recovery requires ongoing prokinetic support, not just treating the current overgrowth. The emotional impact of chronic digestive illness requires compassionate coaching.

Case Study 4: The Autoimmune Connection

Client Profile: Lauren, 47

Occupation: Registered nurse

Chief Complaints: Recently diagnosed Hashimoto's thyroiditis, chronic fatigue, bloating, brain fog, depression

History: Digestive issues for "as long as I can remember"; thought it was normal; family history of autoimmune disease

Notable: "My doctor says the thyroid antibodies are high but there's nothing to do but watch and wait."

üîç Red Flags & Pattern Recognition

Lauren's case illustrates the gut-autoimmune connection. Intestinal permeability is often present before autoimmune disease develops, and research links leaky gut to Hashimoto's. Her "normal" bloating suggests she's adapted to chronic dysfunction. The gut-brain symptoms (fog, depression) align with gut-brain axis disruption.

üîß 5R Protocol Application

REMOVE:
  • Strict gluten removal (gluten is linked to Hashimoto's and molecular mimicry)
  • Dairy elimination (cross-reactivity potential)
  • Testing revealed H. pylori infection‚Äîtreated with natural protocol
  • Addressed chronic stress and perfectionism
REPLACE:
  • Digestive bitters and apple cider vinegar (H. pylori often reduces stomach acid)
  • Digestive enzymes with meals
REINOCULATE:
  • Probiotic containing L. reuteri (shown to support thyroid function)
  • Fermented foods added after H. pylori treatment
  • Prebiotic fibers for SCFA production
REPAIR:
  • Focus on reducing intestinal permeability
  • L-glutamine, zinc, vitamin D (was deficient)
  • Collagen and bone broth
  • Selenium supplementation (supports thyroid and reduces antibodies)
REBALANCE:
  • Sleep prioritization (nurses often have disrupted sleep)
  • Stress management‚Äîaddressing perfectionism and over-giving
  • Gentle exercise (avoiding overtraining which stresses autoimmune conditions)
  • Anti-inflammatory lifestyle (reduced toxin exposure, clean personal care products)

üë§ Coaching Role

  • Educated Lauren on the gut-autoimmune connection (empowering her to take action)
  • Supported strict gluten-free implementation (challenging for someone who didn't think it mattered)
  • Helped create sustainable self-care practices (nurses often neglect themselves)
  • Addressed mindset around "watching and waiting" vs. proactive intervention
  • Coordinated with FM practitioner tracking thyroid antibodies over time

üìä Outcome

Week 8: Bloating improved; energy slightly better

Week 16: Brain fog lifting; mood improving

Week 24: Thyroid antibodies (TPO) reduced by 40%

Week 52: Continued antibody reduction; fatigue significantly improved; feels "better than I have in years"

üí° Key Lesson: Gut healing can influence autoimmune markers. Gluten removal is often essential in Hashimoto's. "Watch and wait" isn't the only option‚Äîproactive gut healing can change the trajectory of autoimmune disease.

Case Study 5: When Progress Stalls

Client Profile: Jessica, 41

Occupation: Stay-at-home mom of three

Chief Complaints: Methane-dominant SIBO (diagnosed), chronic constipation, bloating, weight that won't budge

History: Two rounds of SIBO treatment with temporary improvement each time; symptoms always return

Notable: "I do everything right but I can't stay better."

üîç Red Flags & Pattern Recognition

Jessica represents the challenging relapsing SIBO case. Methane-dominant SIBO is often more resistant to treatment. Recurring relapse suggests underlying causes haven't been addressed. The "do everything right" mentality can mask hidden issues or create stress that impairs healing.

üîç Investigation: Why Is She Relapsing?

Further investigation revealed:

  • Eating every 2-3 hours (constant snacking never allowing MMC activation)
  • History of gallbladder removal (impaired bile flow contributing to SIBO)
  • Chronic stress and sleep deprivation (three kids under 7)
  • Underlying hypothyroidism (slowed motility)
  • Perfectionism creating stress around "doing it right"

üîß Adjusted Approach

Addressing Root Causes:
  • Thyroid optimization with endocrinologist (motility depends on thyroid function)
  • Ox bile supplementation (to compensate for missing gallbladder)
  • Extended antimicrobial treatment (methane requires longer protocols)
  • Added allicin specifically (effective against methanogens)
Prevention Protocol:
  • Strict meal spacing (three meals, no snacking‚Äîthis was the biggest change)
  • Prokinetic therapy (low-dose erythromycin or motility herbs long-term)
  • Maintaining some level of restriction on fermentable foods

üë§ Coaching Role

  • Helped Jessica shift from "doing everything right" to addressing what was actually wrong
  • Major focus on meal spacing‚Äîshe had to restructure her whole family's eating pattern
  • Supported stress management (motherhood stress was significant)
  • Created realistic expectations for the longer timeline methane SIBO requires
  • Addressed perfectionism as its own stress factor
  • Built sustainable habits rather than heroic temporary efforts

üìä Outcome

Week 12: Constipation improving for the first time in years

Week 24: Weight beginning to shift; bloating reduced

Week 36: Maintaining progress (first time); thyroid optimized

Week 52: Stable; able to maintain with lifestyle alone (weaned off prokinetics)

üí° Key Lesson: When treatment keeps failing, look for what's being missed. Meal spacing is often overlooked but critical. Comorbidities (thyroid, gallbladder) must be addressed. Methane SIBO requires patience and longer protocols.

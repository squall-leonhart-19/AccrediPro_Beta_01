The Business Mindset Shift

Many coaches resist the business side of practice. They didn't get into this work to "sell" or "market." Yet without embracing business skills, even the most gifted coaches struggle to sustain their work.

Reframing Business as Service

  • Marketing = Reaching people who need you: You can't help people who don't know you exist
  • Sales = Connecting solutions to problems: Helping potential clients determine if coaching is right for them
  • Pricing = Valuing your expertise: Sustainable income enables you to serve at your best
  • Systems = Consistency and professionalism: Structure creates a better client experience
  • Boundaries = Sustainable service: Healthy limits allow long-term impact

The most successful coaches view business activities as an extension of their service, not separate from it. Marketing isn't manipulationâ€”it's education. Selling isn't pushingâ€”it's helping people make good decisions for their health.

Practice Model Options

Before diving into tactics, consider the practice structure that best fits your goals, lifestyle, and strengths:

Solo Private Practice

Working Independently with Your Own Clients

Pros:

  • Complete control over your approach, schedule, pricing
  • Keep 100% of revenue (minus expenses)
  • Build equity in your own brand
  • Flexibility to design your ideal practice

Cons:

  • Responsible for all business functions (marketing, admin, etc.)
  • Must build client base from scratch
  • Can be isolating without colleagues
  • Income directly tied to your hours worked

Best for: Self-starters, experienced coaches, those wanting maximum control and willing to handle business aspects

Working Within a Practice

Employed by or Contracting with a Functional Medicine Practice

Pros:

  • Clients referred to youâ€”less marketing required
  • Collaborate with practitioners and learn from team
  • Mentorship and supervision often available
  • Focus on coaching, less on business operations

Cons:

  • Lower income (split revenue or receive salary)
  • Less control over approach and client selection
  • May need to follow practice protocols
  • Building someone else's brand, not your own

Best for: New coaches, those wanting mentorship, coaches preferring clinical focus over business

Group/Hybrid Model

Combining Individual Coaching with Group Programs

Pros:

  • Leverage timeâ€”serve more people without proportional time increase
  • Create community among clients
  • Multiple income streams
  • Scale impact beyond 1:1 capacity

Cons:

  • More complex to design and deliver
  • Requires larger audience to fill groups
  • Different skills than individual coaching
  • Technology and systems requirements

Best for: Experienced coaches, those wanting to scale, coaches who enjoy teaching and facilitation

Corporate/Organizational Wellness

Providing Coaching Through Companies or Organizations

Pros:

  • Consistent revenue (contracts vs. individual payments)
  • Bulk client accessâ€”one relationship brings many clients
  • Often higher hourly rates through B2B contracts
  • Less individual marketing required

Cons:

  • May work with less motivated participants
  • Contract-based (can lose large income at once)
  • Often requires proposals, bids, sales processes
  • May have less coaching autonomy

Best for: Coaches comfortable with B2B sales, those wanting stable contracts, former corporate professionals

Designing Your Ideal Practice

Before building, get clear on what you're building toward. Your practice should serve your life goals, not just create another job.

Visioning Questions

Questions to Guide Your Practice Design

Lifestyle:

  • How many hours per week do you want to work?
  • What does your ideal work schedule look like?
  • Do you want to work from home, office, or combination?
  • How much flexibility do you need?

Income:

  • What annual income do you need to sustain your life?
  • What income would feel abundant and rewarding?
  • Is coaching supplementing other income or your primary source?

Impact:

  • Who do you most want to help?
  • What transformation do you want to create?
  • Do you want to serve many people broadly or fewer people deeply?

Energy:

  • What activities energize you vs. drain you?
  • Do you prefer 1:1 intimacy or group energy?
  • How much marketing/business work are you willing to do?

Common Practice-Building Mistakes

Learn from others' expensive lessons:

Mistake 1: Underpricing

What happens: Coaches set low prices thinking it will attract clients. Instead, they attract price-shoppers, devalue their service, burn out from overwork, and can't sustain their practice.

Better approach: Price based on value and sustainability. Higher prices often attract more committed clients who get better results.

Mistake 2: Being Too General

What happens: Coaches try to help everyone with everything, making it impossible for anyone to identify them as the solution to their specific problem.

Better approach: Niche down. Being known as "the gut health coach for busy moms" is more effective than being a "general wellness coach."

Mistake 3: Inconsistent Visibility

What happens: Coaches market intensely, then disappear for weeks or months, never building momentum or staying top-of-mind.

Better approach: Consistent, sustainable marketing. Small daily/weekly activities compound over time.

Mistake 4: No Systems

What happens: Every client interaction is reinvented, creating chaos, inconsistency, and massive time drain on administrative tasks.

Better approach: Create systems for scheduling, onboarding, session structure, payments, and follow-up that scale with your practice.

Mistake 5: Isolation

What happens: Coaches work alone, missing mentorship, referrals, support, and accountability that accelerate success.

Better approach: Build a support network: mentor/supervisor, peer group, complementary providers, professional communities.

From the Coaching Room

"I've been taking CoQ10 for a few months and I honestly feel like I have more energy," says Patricia, a 55-year-old client who came to coaching with persistent fatigue. "Is that real, or am I imagining it? And are there other things I should be taking?"

The Supplement Hierarchy: Foundation First

Before diving into specific supplements, let's establish an important principle: supplements can't replace fundamentals. They work best when:

  • Diet provides the majority of nutrients
  • Sleep is optimized
  • Physical activity is regular
  • Stress is managed
  • Underlying conditions are addressed

With that foundation, supplements can fill gaps and provide therapeutic support that may be difficult to achieve through food alone.

Foundational Mitochondrial Supplements

These supplements address the most common deficiencies affecting energy production:

Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10/Ubiquinone)

CoQ10 Overview

What it does: Essential electron carrier in the mitochondrial electron transport chain. Without CoQ10, ATP production cannot proceed efficiently.

Natural production: The body synthesizes CoQ10, but production peaks around age 20 and declines significantly with age. By 80, tissue levels may be 50% lower than peak.

Depletion factors:

  • Statin medications (block the same pathway that produces CoQ10)
  • Aging
  • Chronic disease
  • High oxidative stress

Evidence: Strong evidence for heart failure, migraine prevention, and statin-induced myopathy. Growing evidence for general fatigue, cognitive function, and exercise performance.

Forms: Ubiquinone (oxidized) vs. ubiquinol (reduced). Ubiquinol may be better absorbed, especially for older adults.

Typical dosing: 100-300 mg daily; take with fat for absorption.

B Vitamins

B Complex for Energy

B Vitamin Energy Role
B1 (Thiamine) Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex; entry to Krebs cycle
B2 (Riboflavin) FAD production; electron transport chain
B3 (Niacin) NAD+ production; critical for cellular metabolism
B5 (Pantothenic acid) CoA synthesis; Krebs cycle function
B12 (Cobalamin) Methylation; red blood cell formation; neurological function

Considerations: B vitamin deficiency is common. Methylated forms (methylfolate, methylcobalamin) may be beneficial for those with MTHFR polymorphisms. B12 deficiency is particularly common in vegetarians, vegans, and those on metformin or acid-blocking medications.

Magnesium

Magnesium: The Energy Mineral

Why it's essential: ATP exists as Mg-ATP complex. Without magnesium, ATP cannot be utilized. Magnesium is required for 300+ enzymatic reactions, many involving energy metabolism.

Deficiency is widespread: Up to 50% of Americans may be deficient. Modern farming depletes soil magnesium; processed foods are low in magnesium; stress depletes magnesium stores.

Forms matter:

  • Glycinate: Well-absorbed; calming effect; good for sleep
  • Citrate: Good absorption; can have laxative effect
  • Malate: May be particularly helpful for fatigue and fibromyalgia
  • Threonate: Crosses blood-brain barrier; may support cognition

Typical dosing: 200-400 mg elemental magnesium daily; higher if deficient. Start low, increase gradually (to tolerance).

Iron

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide and a major cause of fatigue, particularly in women:

  • Required for electron transport chain function
  • Essential for oxygen transport (hemoglobin)
  • Ferritin below 50-70 ng/mL often associated with fatigue symptoms
  • Important: Only supplement if deficient (confirmed by testing)‚Äîiron overload is harmful
  • Iron bisglycinate is typically best tolerated

Advanced Mitochondrial Support

These supplements target specific aspects of mitochondrial function and may be appropriate for clients who have addressed foundational issues:

NAD+ Precursors

Boosting NAD+: NR and NMN

What is NAD+? Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide‚Äîa critical coenzyme for cellular energy production. NAD+ levels decline with age, potentially contributing to aging-related energy decline.

Precursors:

  • Nicotinamide Riboside (NR): Converts to NAD+ in cells. Research shows it can raise NAD+ levels. Brand examples: Tru Niagen
  • Nicotinamide Mononucleotide (NMN): One step closer to NAD+. Growing research base. May have advantages for certain tissues.

Evidence: Preclinical evidence is promising for energy, metabolism, and longevity. Human research is still emerging but shows NAD+ levels do increase with supplementation.

Typical dosing: NR: 250-500 mg daily; NMN: 250-500 mg daily. More research needed on optimal dosing.

L-Carnitine

L-Carnitine for Fat Burning

Function: Transports long-chain fatty acids into mitochondria for beta-oxidation (fat burning). Without carnitine, cells can't efficiently burn fat for fuel.

Who may benefit:

  • Vegetarians/vegans (carnitine is primarily in animal foods)
  • Older adults (carnitine synthesis declines with age)
  • Those with chronic fatigue
  • Athletes (may improve recovery)

Forms:

  • L-Carnitine: General form
  • Acetyl-L-Carnitine (ALCAR): Crosses blood-brain barrier; may support cognitive function
  • L-Carnitine L-Tartrate: Often used for exercise performance

Typical dosing: 500-2000 mg daily, often divided.

Alpha-Lipoic Acid (ALA)

  • Cofactor in Krebs cycle (pyruvate dehydrogenase and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase)
  • Powerful antioxidant‚Äîregenerates other antioxidants (vitamin C, E, glutathione)
  • May improve insulin sensitivity
  • Both fat and water soluble‚Äîcan work in many cellular compartments
  • Typical dosing: 300-600 mg daily

PQQ (Pyrroloquinoline Quinone)

PQQ: Mitochondrial Biogenesis Support

Unique function: PQQ may stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis‚Äîthe creation of new mitochondria. This is a different mechanism than supporting existing mitochondrial function.

How it works: Activates PGC-1Œ± (the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis) and supports CREB signaling.

Evidence: Primarily preclinical studies showing increased mitochondrial number. Human studies suggest cognitive benefits and improved sleep quality.

Synergy: Often combined with CoQ10‚ÄîCoQ10 supports existing mitochondria while PQQ may help create new ones.

Typical dosing: 10-20 mg daily.

D-Ribose

  • A sugar that forms the backbone of ATP molecules
  • May help replenish ATP levels faster after depletion
  • Some evidence for chronic fatigue syndrome and fibromyalgia
  • Often used by athletes for recovery
  • Typical dosing: 5 grams 2-3 times daily

When to Consider Supplements

Clinical Considerations

Supplements may be particularly helpful when:

  • Nutrient testing reveals deficiencies
  • Diet is restricted (vegetarian, allergies, etc.)
  • Medications deplete nutrients (statins ‚Üí CoQ10, metformin ‚Üí B12)
  • Absorption is impaired (gut issues, aging)
  • Demands are increased (stress, illness, intense exercise)
  • Baseline lifestyle is already optimized but fatigue persists

Red flags to address first: If severe fatigue is present, ensure thyroid dysfunction, anemia, sleep apnea, depression, and other medical causes have been ruled out before focusing on mitochondrial supplements.

Scope of Practice Considerations

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Boundaries

  • Education vs. prescription: You can educate about supplements; prescribing or dosing for specific conditions may exceed scope
  • Drug interactions: Always encourage clients to inform their healthcare providers about supplements, especially if on medications
  • Condition-specific use: Using supplements therapeutically for disease treatment is typically outside coaching scope
  • Testing: If recommending supplements based on deficiency, testing to confirm is optimal (often requiring physician order)
  • Quality matters: Help clients choose quality brands with third-party testing

A Practical Supplementation Framework

Tiered Approach

Tier 1 - Foundational (address first):

  • B-complex vitamin
  • Magnesium
  • Vitamin D (if deficient‚Äîcommon)
  • Omega-3 fatty acids

Tier 2 - Mitochondrial Support:

  • CoQ10/Ubiquinol (especially if over 40 or on statins)
  • Iron (if deficient‚Äîtest first)

Tier 3 - Advanced (if needed):

  • NAD+ precursors (NR or NMN)
  • L-Carnitine or ALCAR
  • Alpha-lipoic acid
  • PQQ
  • D-Ribose

Key Takeaways

  • Foundation first: Supplements support but don't replace diet, sleep, exercise, and stress management
  • CoQ10 is king: The most evidence-based mitochondrial supplement, especially valuable with age or statin use
  • B vitamins and magnesium: Common deficiencies that directly impair energy production
  • NAD+ precursors: Emerging area with potential for age-related energy decline
  • Test don't guess: When possible, confirm deficiencies before supplementing (especially iron)
  • Quality matters: Third-party tested supplements ensure purity and potency
  • Know your scope: Educate and support within appropriate boundaries

Case Study 1: The Overwhelmed Professional

Client Profile: Catherine, 44

Background: Catherine is a corporate attorney working 60+ hours weekly, mother of two teenagers, and going through a difficult divorce. She describes her fatigue as "running on fumes" for the past two years. She wakes tired despite sleeping 7-8 hours, relies on 4-5 cups of coffee to function, and crashes hard in the afternoon. Weekends are spent recovering just enough to face another week.

Current Status

Sleep:7-8 hours, wakes 2-3 times/night, wakes unrefreshed
Diet:Skips breakfast, salad at desk for lunch, takeout for dinner
Exercise:Noneâ€”"too tired"
Caffeine:4-5 cups coffee daily, last cup around 3 PM
Stress level:10/10 ("constant")
Alcohol:2-3 glasses wine nightly "to unwind"
Labs (from doctor):TSH 2.8, CBC "normal," no other testing done

Assessment: Identifying Root Causes

Catherine has multiple factors draining her cellular energy:

  • HPA axis dysfunction: Classic "tired but wired" pattern from chronic stress; waking at night suggests cortisol dysregulation
  • Poor sleep quality: Alcohol and caffeine both impair sleep architecture; she's sleeping without restoring
  • Blood sugar instability: Skipping breakfast, relying on caffeine, and eating takeout creates glucose roller coaster
  • Nutrient depletion: Stress depletes B vitamins and magnesium; poor diet likely inadequate in mitochondrial nutrients
  • Sedentary lifestyle: No exercise means no mitochondrial biogenesis stimulus
  • Incomplete testing: Fasting insulin, ferritin, B12, vitamin D, and full thyroid panel haven't been checked

Coaching Approach

Phase 1 - Foundation (Weeks 1-4):

  • Sleep first: Caffeine cutoff at noon. Reduce wine to 1 glass max, stopping 3 hours before bed. This is non-negotiableâ€”she won't improve without better sleep quality.
  • Blood sugar stability: Simple breakfast with protein (eggs, Greek yogurt with nuts). Protein at every meal.
  • One parasympathetic practice: 5 minutes of breathing exercises before bed, or 10-minute guided meditation app.

Phase 2 - Building (Weeks 5-8):

  • Gentle movement: Start with 10-minute walks at lunch. Not intenseâ€”just getting mitochondria the signal to adapt.
  • Nutrient support: B-complex, magnesium glycinate (evening), consider CoQ10.
  • Request additional testing: Encourage conversation with doctor about ferritin, B12, vitamin D, fasting insulin, full thyroid panel.
  • Stress boundaries: Identify one work boundary to implement.

Phase 3 - Optimization (Weeks 9-12):

  • Increase walking duration; consider adding strength training
  • Address any deficiencies found in testing
  • Continue building stress resilience practices
  • Monitor energy improvements; adjust as needed

Key Insight: Catherine's fatigue isn't mysteriousâ€”the causes are visible in her lifestyle. The challenge is helping her make changes when she's already depleted. Start with what gives the biggest return for the least effort: improving sleep quality by addressing caffeine and alcohol timing will improve her energy enough to tackle other changes.

Case Study 2: The Post-Viral Struggle

Client Profile: Sarah, 38

Background: Sarah had COVID-19 eight months ago. Before COVID, she was activeâ€”running, yoga, full-time marketing job, active social life. Since recovering from acute infection, she's never been the same. She has pervasive fatigue, "brain fog" that makes work difficult, and exercise intoleranceâ€”a 20-minute walk leaves her exhausted for the next two days.

Current Status

Primary complaint:Crushing fatigue and brain fog since COVID
Post-exertional malaise:Yesâ€”crashes 24-48 hours after exertion
Sleep:Sleeping 9-10 hours, still exhausted
Work:Reduced to part-time; struggling even with that
Diet:Was eating well; now often too tired to cook
Labs:All "normal" per doctorâ€”TSH, CBC, CMP, no abnormalities found
Doctor's response:"Give it time, exercise more, maybe try antidepressants"

Assessment: Recognizing Long COVID/Post-Viral Syndrome

Sarah's presentation is consistent with Long COVID or post-viral syndrome, characterized by:

  • Post-exertional malaise (PEM): The hallmark symptomâ€”activity causes delayed, disproportionate worsening
  • Cognitive dysfunction: Brain fog affecting work and daily function
  • Unrefreshing sleep: Despite more hours, sleep isn't restorative
  • Potential mitochondrial dysfunction: Research suggests COVID may directly impair mitochondria
  • Inflammation: Lingering immune activation may be present

Critical: Standard exercise recommendations don't apply here. Pushing through can cause worsening.

Coaching Approach

Energy Management (Pacing):

  • Help Sarah understand her "energy envelope"â€”the amount of activity she can do without triggering PEM
  • Track activities and symptoms to identify triggers and limits
  • Plan rest before and after activities ("pre-rest" and recovery time)
  • Prioritize essential activities; let go of non-essentials
  • Avoid boom-bust cycles (doing too much on good days, crashing)

Nutritional Support (Anti-inflammatory focus):

  • Mediterranean-style eating when possible
  • Easy-to-prepare meals and batch cooking
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (anti-inflammatory)
  • Consider mitochondrial support: CoQ10, NAD+ precursor, B vitamins
  • Address any nutrient deficiencies (testing may reveal issues)

Additional Testing to Request:

  • Comprehensive metabolic panel with fasting insulin
  • Full thyroid panel (not just TSH)
  • Vitamin D, B12, ferritin
  • hs-CRP and other inflammatory markers
  • Consider referral to Long COVID clinic or post-viral specialist

Emotional Support:

  • Validate her experienceâ€”this is real, not "in her head"
  • Grief support for loss of her previous life and identity
  • Connection with Long COVID support communities
  • Address depression/anxiety that commonly accompany chronic illness

Critical Warning: Do NOT apply standard exercise recommendations to Sarah. In post-viral syndromes with PEM, pushing exercise can cause significant, lasting harm. The goal is stabilization and gradual recovery, not fitness improvement. This is fundamentally different from typical fatigue coaching.

Case Study 3: The "Everything's Fine" Fatigue

Client Profile: Michelle, 51

Background: Michelle is in perimenopause and feels her energy has gradually declined over the past three years. She exercises 3-4 times weekly, eats a "healthy diet," sleeps reasonably well, and manages stress adequately. Yet she's noticeably less energetic than she was five years ago. Her doctor says everything is normal. She wonders if this is just aging.

Current Status

Energy pattern:Gradual decline over 3 years; no acute onset
Sleep:7 hours, occasional hot flashes, generally OK
Exercise:3-4x/week running and yoga
Diet:Mostly vegetarian, emphasis on "clean eating"
Stress:Moderateâ€”some work stress, empty nest adjustment
Labs (standard):TSH 3.1, hemoglobin 12.5, "all normal"
Perimenopause:Irregular periods, some hot flashes

Assessment: Subtle Deficiencies and Transitions

Michelle's presentation suggests several potential contributors often missed by standard testing:

  • Suboptimal thyroid: TSH of 3.1 is "normal" but may not be optimal for her. Free T3 and T4 aren't checked.
  • B12 deficiency risk: Vegetarian diet increases risk; serum B12 may miss functional deficiency.
  • Iron stores: Ferritin not checked. Vegetarian + menstruating = high risk for suboptimal ferritin.
  • Hormonal transition: Declining estrogen affects mitochondrial function and energy.
  • Protein adequacy: Many vegetarians underconsume protein, especially as needs increase with age.
  • Exercise type: All cardio, no strength training. May be losing muscle (and mitochondria).
  • Age-related CoQ10 decline: Natural production drops with age.

Coaching Approach

Testing Recommendations (to discuss with physician):

  • Full thyroid panel: TSH, Free T3, Free T4, thyroid antibodies
  • Ferritin (optimal: 50-100 ng/mL, not just "normal")
  • B12 and methylmalonic acid (more sensitive than B12 alone)
  • Vitamin D (optimal: 40-60 ng/mL)
  • Fasting insulin (assess metabolic health)

Nutrition Optimization:

  • Protein audit: Is she getting 100+ grams daily? Likely notâ€”increase plant proteins, consider eggs/fish if open
  • B12 supplementation: Essential for vegetarians
  • Iron-rich foods: Legumes, dark leafy greens, paired with vitamin C for absorption
  • CoQ10: 100-200 mg dailyâ€”likely to help given age

Exercise Modification:

  • Add resistance training 2-3x weeklyâ€”critical for maintaining muscle mass and mitochondria
  • Consider HIIT 1x weekly if toleratedâ€”potent mitochondrial stimulus
  • May need to reduce cardio volume if overtrained

Hormonal Considerations:

  • Discuss hormone testing and possibly HRT with physician
  • Hormone therapy can improve energy, sleep, and metabolic health during perimenopause
  • This is a medical decision but coaching can support the conversation

Key Insight: Michelle is doing many things right, but subtle deficiencies and hormonal changes are impacting her energy. This isn't "just aging"â€”it's addressable. The combination of comprehensive testing, targeted nutrition, and exercise modification can restore vitality even in perimenopause.

Lessons Across All Cases

Common Principles

  1. Fatigue has causes: It's never "just aging" or "in your head"â€”there are biological reasons
  2. Standard testing misses much: Ferritin, B12, full thyroid, vitamin D, and fasting insulin often reveal what basic panels miss
  3. Context matters: Same symptom (fatigue) requires different approaches based on pattern and presentation
  4. Post-exertional malaise is different: Some fatigue conditions worsen with standard exercise recommendations
  5. Start with high-impact, low-effort changes: Meet clients where they areâ€”depleted clients can't implement complex plans
  6. Address foundations first: Sleep, blood sugar, stress, and obvious deficiencies before advanced interventions
  7. Validation is therapeutic: Being believed and taken seriously improves outcomes

Key Takeaways

  • Multiple factors typically contribute: Rarely is there one silver bulletâ€”address the system
  • Lifestyle causes lifestyle fatigue: Catherine's case shows how stress, sleep, and nutrition interact
  • Post-viral syndromes require different approaches: Sarah's case demonstrates the importance of pacing over pushing
  • Subtle deficiencies matter: Michelle's case shows how "normal" labs can miss suboptimal status
  • Prioritize strategically: Start where the greatest leverage exists for each individual
  • Sleep quality trumps quantity: Hours in bed don't equal restorative sleep
  • Know when to refer: Complex or worsening fatigue may need specialist evaluation

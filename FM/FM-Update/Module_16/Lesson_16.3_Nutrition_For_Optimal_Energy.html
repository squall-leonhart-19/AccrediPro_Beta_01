How Food Becomes Energy

Before diving into recommendations, let's understand what happens to food in the context of energy production. Each macronutrient follows a different pathway to become ATP:

Carbohydrates
  • Break down to glucose
  • Enter glycolysis ‚Üí pyruvate
  • Enter mitochondria ‚Üí Krebs cycle ‚Üí ETC ‚Üí ATP
  • Fast-acting, but can cause spikes and crashes
Fats
  • Break down to fatty acids
  • Transported into mitochondria (via carnitine)
  • Beta-oxidation ‚Üí acetyl-CoA ‚Üí Krebs cycle ‚Üí ATP
  • Slower but produces more ATP per molecule
Key Insight

Proteins break down to amino acids‚Äîprimarily used for tissue building, but can be converted to glucose (gluconeogenesis) or enter the Krebs cycle for energy when needed. They provide satiety and blood sugar stability.

The key insight: carbohydrates provide quick energy, but fats and proteins provide sustained energy. A diet overly reliant on carbohydrates‚Äîespecially refined ones‚Äîcreates the spikes and crashes that feel like fatigue.

Coach Tip

When clients complain of energy crashes, ask about their macronutrient balance: "Walk me through what you ate for breakfast and lunch yesterday. How much protein? Any healthy fats?" Often the issue becomes immediately clear‚Äîthey're running on carbs alone.

Blood Sugar Stability: The Foundation of Steady Energy

Blood sugar dysregulation is perhaps the most common nutritional cause of energy fluctuation. When blood sugar spikes and crashes:

  • High blood sugar creates oxidative stress in cells
  • Rapid drops trigger cortisol and adrenaline (stress response)
  • The crash creates fatigue, brain fog, irritability, cravings
  • The cycle repeats, depleting resilience over time

Strategies for Blood Sugar Stability

1

Protein at Every Meal

Include 25-35 grams of protein at each meal. Protein slows glucose absorption and provides satiety. Examples: eggs, fish, poultry, meat, Greek yogurt, legumes.

2

Include Healthy Fats

Fat further slows glucose absorption and provides sustained fuel. Examples: olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish, coconut.

3

Choose Low-Glycemic Carbs

Non-starchy vegetables, legumes, berries, and intact whole grains have minimal blood sugar impact compared to refined carbs.

4

Meal Sequencing

Eat vegetables and protein before starches. Research shows this can reduce glucose spikes by up to 50%.

5

Don't Skip Meals

Especially breakfast. Skipping meals causes blood sugar drops and overeating later. If practicing intermittent fasting, break the fast with protein and fat, not carbs.

6

Avoid Naked Carbs

Never eat carbohydrates alone‚Äîalways pair with protein and/or fat. An apple with almond butter, not just an apple.

7

Limit Liquid Sugars

Fruit juice, soda, sweetened coffee drinks cause rapid blood sugar spikes. Even smoothies can be problematic if fruit-heavy without protein.

Coach Tip

The "naked carb" concept is powerful for clients. Challenge them to notice when they eat carbs alone (crackers, pretzels, fruit, toast) versus paired with protein/fat. Most discover they habitually eat naked carbs and now understand why they crash afterward.

Hydration and Energy

Dehydration is an overlooked cause of fatigue:

  • Even mild dehydration (1-2% body water loss) impairs cognitive function and energy
  • Thirst signals often don't activate until already dehydrated
  • Caffeine and alcohol are diuretic‚Äîdon't count toward hydration
  • Many people mistake thirst for hunger or fatigue
Hydration Recommendation

Aim for approximately half your body weight in ounces of water daily. More if active, in hot weather, or consuming caffeine/alcohol. Example: 150 lb person ‚Üí 75 oz water/day.

Timing Considerations

Front-Load Protein
  • Eating more protein earlier in the day
  • Supports neurotransmitter production for alertness
  • Stabilizes blood sugar from morning onward
Avoid Large Evening Meals
  • Big dinners, especially carb-heavy
  • Can disrupt sleep quality
  • Leave you groggy in the morning
Consider This

Time-restricted eating (12-16 hour overnight fast) supports mitochondrial health and autophagy. Circadian rhythm alignment‚Äîeating more earlier, less later‚Äîmay optimize metabolic function.

Coach Tip

Many clients eat their largest meal at dinner and skip or skimp on breakfast. Suggest a two-week experiment: "What if you ate dinner-sized portions for breakfast and lunch, and a lighter meal at dinner? Let's track your energy levels and see what happens." Often the results speak for themselves.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

Why do carbohydrates create energy spikes and crashes while fats provide sustained energy?

Answer: Carbohydrates are rapidly broken down to glucose, causing quick blood sugar rises followed by insulin-driven drops. Fats undergo slower beta-oxidation in mitochondria, producing more ATP per molecule and providing steady fuel without blood sugar volatility.

2

What does "meal sequencing" mean, and how can it reduce glucose spikes?

Answer: Meal sequencing means eating vegetables and protein before starches/carbohydrates. This slows glucose absorption and can reduce postprandial glucose spikes by up to 50%, helping maintain stable energy levels.

3

Name three B vitamins essential for mitochondrial energy production and their roles.

Answer: (1) B1 (Thiamine) converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA; (2) B2 (Riboflavin) is a component of FAD, an electron carrier; (3) B3 (Niacin) is a component of NAD+, critical for the Krebs cycle and electron transport chain. (B5 is also acceptable‚Äîrequired for CoA synthesis.)

4

What does it mean to avoid "naked carbs," and why does this strategy support energy?

Answer: "Naked carbs" means eating carbohydrates alone without protein or fat (e.g., just fruit, crackers, or toast). This causes rapid blood sugar spikes and crashes. Always pairing carbs with protein and/or fat slows glucose absorption and provides sustained energy.

Key Takeaways
  • Blood sugar stability is foundational ‚Äî Protein, healthy fats, and low-glycemic carbs prevent energy crashes
  • Mitochondria need micronutrients ‚Äî B vitamins, magnesium, iron, and CoQ10 are essential cofactors
  • Protein at every meal ‚Äî Aim for 25-35 grams to support satiety and blood sugar stability
  • Quality fats provide sustained energy ‚Äî They burn slowly and support cell membranes
  • Avoid energy drains ‚Äî Sugar, refined carbs, alcohol, and ultra-processed foods impair energy production
  • Stay hydrated ‚Äî Even mild dehydration causes fatigue; aim for half your body weight in ounces daily

The stress response evolved to help us survive acute dangers. The problem: it was never meant to run continuously. When stress becomes chronic, it systematically dismantles our cellular energy systems‚Äîand the consequences are profound.

The Stress Response: From Survival to Exhaustion

The stress response is elegant, efficient, and essential‚Äîwhen it works as designed. But modern life has hijacked this ancient survival system, leaving it running 24/7 with devastating consequences for cellular energy.

Acute Stress Response (Adaptive)

When faced with a threat, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis activates:

  1. Hypothalamus releases CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone)
  2. Pituitary releases ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)
  3. Adrenal glands release cortisol and adrenaline
  4. Energy is mobilized: blood sugar rises, heart rate increases, muscles ready for action
  5. Non-essential functions are suppressed: digestion, immune activity, reproduction
  6. Threat resolves: cortisol provides negative feedback, system returns to baseline

This is healthy and appropriate. The problem arises when step 6 never happens‚Äîwhen stress is constant and the system never returns to baseline.

Coach Tip

Help clients recognize that their body's stress response isn't broken‚Äîit's doing exactly what it was designed to do. The issue is that modern stressors (work deadlines, financial pressure, relationship conflicts) trigger the same physiological response as life-threatening dangers, but without the physical outlet or clear resolution. Frame this as: "Your body is working perfectly‚Äîit's just responding to a situation it wasn't designed for. We need to give it the signals that say 'the threat has passed.'"

Chronic Stress and Mitochondrial Damage

Chronic stress directly damages mitochondria through multiple mechanisms. This isn't metaphorical‚Äîresearch by Dr. Martin Picard has shown that psychological stress literally changes mitochondrial structure and function, visible under electron microscopy.

How Stress Destroys Cellular Energy

‚ö° Oxidative Stress

Cortisol increases reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, damaging mitochondrial components including DNA, proteins, and lipid membranes.

üß¨ DNA Damage

Chronic stress accelerates mutations in mitochondrial DNA, impairing the ability to produce energy-generating proteins.

üìâ Reduced Biogenesis

Chronic cortisol suppresses PGC-1Œ±, the master regulator that signals creation of new mitochondria.

üîÑ Impaired Dynamics

Stress disrupts the delicate balance of mitochondrial fusion and fission, reducing network efficiency and energy output.

üí´ Calcium Dysregulation

Stress hormones alter calcium handling within cells, which can trigger mitochondrial dysfunction and even cell death.

üî• Chronic Inflammation

Prolonged stress promotes low-grade inflammation that further impairs mitochondrial function in a vicious cycle.

Dr. Martin Picard's groundbreaking research has demonstrated that psychological stress literally changes mitochondrial structure and function‚Äîchanges that are visible under electron microscopy. This validates what clients instinctively know: chronic stress is physically exhausting them at the cellular level (Picard & McEwen, 2018).

The Stages of Stress and Energy

Hans Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome describes the progression of stress response, which directly correlates with energy depletion. Understanding these stages helps you identify where your client is and what intervention is most appropriate.

1

Stage 1: Alarm

Initial stress response. Cortisol and adrenaline surge. Energy is high‚Äîfight or flight mode. Client may feel energized, alert, even invincible initially. This is the "superhuman productivity" phase that many mistake for sustainable performance.

2

Stage 2: Resistance

Body attempts to adapt to ongoing stress. HPA axis is working overtime. "Tired but wired"‚Äîcan still function but reserves are depleting. This can last months to years, masking the underlying damage being done.

3

Stage 3: Exhaustion

Resources are depleted. Cortisol production may drop below normal. Profound fatigue, often with inability to handle even minor stressors. Recovery requires significant time and comprehensive intervention.

Coach Tip

When working with high-achieving clients, help them recognize that Stage 1 (Alarm) isn't sustainable peak performance‚Äîit's borrowed energy. Many clients pride themselves on thriving under pressure, not realizing they're already in Stage 2 (Resistance). Use this framework to help them see that what feels like "productivity" may actually be progressing toward exhaustion. Ask: "How long have you been able to push through on willpower alone? What would it look like to perform from a place of genuine energy rather than stress hormones?"

Recognizing HPA Axis Dysfunction

HPA axis dysfunction (sometimes called "adrenal fatigue," though this term is controversial in conventional medicine) presents in recognizable patterns. Learning to identify these patterns helps you understand what's happening beneath your client's fatigue.

"Tired but Wired" Pattern
  • Exhausted during the day but can't sleep at night
  • Mind races, unable to relax or "turn off"
  • Second wind of energy in the evening (when cortisol should be low)
  • Difficulty winding down before bed
  • Wake between 2-4 AM with racing thoughts
"Flat-lined" Pattern
  • Exhausted all day, no energy peaks
  • Difficulty waking‚Äîhitting snooze repeatedly
  • Need caffeine to function at baseline
  • Crash in the afternoon (2-4 PM)
  • Can't tolerate stress that was once manageable
Associated Symptoms

Watch for these accompanying signs of HPA axis dysfunction:

  • Salt and sugar cravings (body seeking quick energy and electrolytes)
  • Low blood pressure upon standing (orthostatic hypotension)
  • Slow recovery from illness or exercise
  • Brain fog, difficulty concentrating and making decisions
  • Low libido (non-essential function suppressed under chronic stress)
  • Feeling overwhelmed by small tasks that once felt manageable

Testing HPA Axis Function

While the term "adrenal fatigue" isn't a recognized medical diagnosis, HPA axis dysfunction can be assessed through functional testing:

üî¨ Functional Testing Options

Salivary Cortisol (4-point)

Measures cortisol at waking, noon, evening, and bedtime. Reveals the diurnal pattern‚Äîhealthy is high morning, progressively lower through the day, lowest at bedtime.

Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)

Measures the spike in cortisol 30-45 minutes after waking. A blunted CAR indicates HPA dysfunction and predicts fatigue throughout the day.

DHEA-S Testing

Often tested alongside cortisol; the cortisol:DHEA ratio provides insight into overall HPA stress load and resilience capacity.

Important note: Random serum cortisol (standard medical testing) often misses dysfunction because it's a single snapshot that may be normal even when the overall diurnal pattern is severely disrupted. This is why clients are often told their cortisol is "fine" despite profound symptoms.

Protecting Energy Through Stress Resilience

The goal isn't eliminating stress‚Äîthat's impossible and arguably undesirable. The goal is building stress resilience: the capacity to handle challenges without depleting cellular energy reserves.

Stress Resilience Strategies

ü´Å Parasympathetic Activation (Daily)

Deep breathing exercises: Box breathing (4-4-4-4), 4-7-8 technique

Meditation or mindfulness: Even 5-10 minutes daily shifts nervous system balance

Gentle movement: Yoga, tai chi, qigong

Vagus nerve stimulation: Humming, singing, gargling

Brief cold exposure: Cold shower, cold plunge (hormetic stress that builds resilience)

üåø Recovery Practices

Prioritize sleep: Non-negotiable for HPA recovery

Schedule genuine rest: Not "leisure" involving screens or stimulation

Nature exposure: Proven to lower cortisol in as little as 20 minutes

Social connection: Supportive relationships buffer stress impact

‚öñÔ∏è Boundaries and Pacing

Eliminate unnecessary stressors: Where possible, remove what drains without adding value

Set boundaries: On work hours, technology use, commitments

Build buffer time: Between activities to prevent constant rushing

Practice saying no: Protect your energy budget

Coach Tip

Many clients resist stress-management practices because they don't feel immediate results. Help reframe this by explaining: "These practices aren't about feeling instantly relaxed‚Äîthey're about building your stress buffer. Think of it like a savings account. Each practice makes a small deposit. Over time, you build reserves that let you handle life's challenges without depleting your energy." Track objective markers like resting heart rate, heart rate variability, or sleep quality to show progress even before clients "feel" different.

Nutritional Support for Stress and Energy

Certain nutrients support both stress resilience and mitochondrial function. Chronic stress depletes these rapidly, creating a vicious cycle.

Key Nutrients for Stress & Energy Support

Nutrient Role in Stress & Energy
Magnesium Calms nervous system; required for ATP production; rapidly depleted by stress
B Vitamins Support adrenal function and energy production; depleted by chronic stress
Vitamin C Highest concentration in adrenal glands; essential for cortisol production
Omega-3 Fatty Acids Reduce inflammation; support brain and mitochondrial membrane integrity
Adaptogens Ashwagandha, rhodiola, eleuthero‚Äîhelp modulate and buffer stress response
Phosphatidylserine Can help lower elevated cortisol, particularly in evening (supports sleep)
Important Note on Adaptogens

Adaptogens are herbs that help the body adapt to stress‚Äîthey can be supportive tools but aren't magic solutions. They work best alongside lifestyle modifications and cannot compensate for ongoing overwhelming stress, poor sleep, or inadequate nutrition. Always consider your scope of practice when recommending supplements. In most states, health coaches can provide education about nutrients but should work with licensed practitioners for specific supplementation protocols.

Exercise: Friend or Foe for Stressed Clients?

Exercise is generally beneficial for stress management, but context matters enormously. The same workout that builds resilience in one person can accelerate exhaustion in another.

When Exercise Helps
  • Moderate-intensity exercise reduces cortisol levels
  • Physical activity provides outlet for stress hormones
  • Regular movement builds stress resilience over time
  • Improved sleep from exercise aids HPA recovery
  • Exercise that feels enjoyable reduces perception of stress
When Exercise Hurts
  • Intense exercise when already depleted adds physiological stress
  • HIIT or endurance training may worsen exhausted clients
  • Exercise that feels like obligation increases cortisol
  • Under-recovery between sessions compounds stress load
  • Overtraining syndrome mimics and worsens HPA dysfunction

Practical guidance: For clients in the "exhaustion" stage (Stage 3), gentle movement‚Äîwalking, restorative yoga, swimming, tai chi‚Äîmay be more appropriate than intense training. Match exercise intensity to current capacity, not aspirational goals or past performance. Recovery is the priority; fitness gains can wait.

Coach Tip

Help clients recognize the difference between "good tired" and "bad tired" after exercise. "Good tired" means pleasantly fatigued, energized mentally, sleeping well, recovering quickly. "Bad tired" means depleted, worsening sleep, mood disruption, longer recovery. If clients consistently experience "bad tired," exercise intensity needs adjustment. This isn't weakness‚Äîit's wisdom. Ask: "Is this workout adding to your energy account or withdrawing from it?"

The Psychology of Stress and Energy

Here's something remarkable: perception matters. The same stressor affects people differently based on how they interpret it. This isn't just positive thinking‚Äîit's measurable physiology.

1

Controllability

Feeling unable to control stressors amplifies their physiological impact. Even partial control reduces cortisol response.

2

Meaning

Stress in service of valued goals is less damaging than meaningless stress. Purpose buffers biological impact.

3

Support

Social support dramatically buffers stress effects. Connection is physiologically protective.

4

Beliefs

Research shows that viewing stress as harmful is associated with worse health outcomes than viewing it as a challenge or growth opportunity.

This is where health coaching truly shines. Coaching that helps clients reframe stress, find meaning in challenges, and build support systems can shift the physiological stress response itself. You're not just teaching coping strategies‚Äîyou're helping rewire the biological impact of stress.

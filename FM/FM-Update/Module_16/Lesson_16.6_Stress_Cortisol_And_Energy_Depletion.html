The Stress Response: From Survival to Exhaustion

The stress response evolved to help us survive acute dangers. The problem: it was never meant to run continuously.

Acute Stress Response (Adaptive)

When faced with a threat, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis activates:

  1. Hypothalamus releases CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone)
  2. Pituitary releases ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)
  3. Adrenal glands release cortisol and adrenaline
  4. Energy is mobilized: blood sugar rises, heart rate increases, muscles ready for action
  5. Non-essential functions are suppressed: digestion, immune activity, reproduction
  6. Threat resolves: cortisol provides negative feedback, system returns to baseline

This is healthy and appropriate. The problem arises when step 6 never happensâ€”when stress is constant and the system never returns to baseline.

Chronic Stress and Mitochondrial Damage

Chronic stress directly damages mitochondria through multiple mechanisms:

Oxidative stress Cortisol increases reactive oxygen species production, damaging mitochondrial components

Research by Dr. Martin Picard has shown that psychological stress literally changes mitochondrial structure and functionâ€”visible under electron microscopy (Picard & McEwen, 2018). This isn't just "in your head"â€”it's in your cells.

The Stages of Stress and Energy

Hans Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome describes stages of stress response, which we can relate to energy:

Stage 1: Alarm

Initial stress response. Cortisol and adrenaline surge. Energy is highâ€”fight or flight mode. May feel energized, alert, even invincible initially.

Stage 2: Resistance

Body attempts to adapt to ongoing stress. HPA axis is working overtime. "Tired but wired"â€”can still function but reserves are depleting. This can last months to years.

Stage 3: Exhaustion

Resources are depleted. Cortisol production may drop below normal. Profound fatigue, often with inability to handle even minor stressors. Recovery requires significant time and intervention.

Recognizing HPA Axis Dysfunction

HPA axis dysfunction (sometimes called "adrenal fatigue," though this term is controversial) presents in patterns:

Common Patterns and Signs

"Tired but wired" pattern:

  • Exhausted during the day but can't sleep at night
  • Mind races, unable to relax
  • Second wind of energy in the evening
  • Difficulty winding down

"Flat-lined" pattern:

  • Exhausted all day
  • Difficulty wakingâ€”hitting snooze repeatedly
  • Need caffeine to function
  • Crash in the afternoon
  • Can't tolerate stress that was once manageable

Associated symptoms:

  • Salt and sugar cravings
  • Low blood pressure upon standing (orthostatic hypotension)
  • Slow recovery from illness or exercise
  • Brain fog, difficulty concentrating
  • Low libido
  • Feeling overwhelmed by small tasks

Testing HPA Axis Function

While the term "adrenal fatigue" isn't a recognized medical diagnosis, HPA axis dysfunction can be assessed:

  • Salivary cortisol (4-point): Measures cortisol at waking, noon, evening, and bedtime. Reveals the diurnal patternâ€”healthy is high morning, low evening.
  • Cortisol awakening response (CAR): The spike in cortisol 30-45 minutes after waking. Blunted CAR indicates HPA dysfunction.
  • DHEA-S: Often tested alongside cortisol; the cortisol:DHEA ratio provides insight into HPA stress.

Random serum cortisol (standard medical testing) often misses dysfunction because it's a single snapshot that may be normal even when the overall pattern is disrupted.

Protecting Energy Through Stress Resilience

The goal isn't eliminating stressâ€”that's impossible. The goal is building stress resilience: the capacity to handle challenges without depleting cellular energy.

Stress Resilience Strategies

Parasympathetic activation (daily):

  • Deep breathing exercises (box breathing, 4-7-8 technique)
  • Meditation or mindfulness practice
  • Yoga, tai chi, or gentle stretching
  • Cold exposure (cold shower, cold plungeâ€”brief stress that builds resilience)
  • Humming, singing, gargling (vagus nerve stimulation)

Recovery practices:

  • Prioritize sleepâ€”non-negotiable for HPA recovery
  • Schedule genuine rest (not just "leisure" that involves screens or stimulation)
  • Nature exposureâ€”proven to lower cortisol
  • Social connection with supportive relationships

Boundaries and pacing:

  • Identify and eliminate unnecessary stressors where possible
  • Set boundaries on work, technology, and commitments
  • Build buffer time between activities
  • Practice saying no

Important Note on Adaptogens

Adaptogens are herbs that help the body adapt to stress. They can be supportive for some clients but aren't magic solutions. They work best alongside lifestyle modificationsâ€”they can't compensate for ongoing overwhelming stress, poor sleep, or inadequate nutrition. Always consider scope of practice when recommending supplements.

Exercise: Friend or Foe for Stressed Clients?

Exercise is generally beneficial for stress, but context matters:

When Exercise Helps

  • Moderate-intensity exercise reduces cortisol
  • Exercise provides outlet for stress hormones
  • Regular activity builds stress resilience
  • Improved sleep from exercise aids recovery

When Exercise Hurts

  • Intense exercise when already depleted adds stress
  • HIIT or endurance training may worsen exhausted clients
  • Exercise that feels like obligation increases cortisol
  • Under-recovery between sessions compounds stress

Guidance: For clients in the "exhaustion" stage, gentle movement (walking, yoga, swimming) may be more appropriate than intense training. Match exercise to current capacity, not aspirational goals.

The Psychology of Stress and Energy

Perception matters. The same stressor affects people differently based on their interpretation:

  • Controllability: Feeling unable to control stressors amplifies their impact
  • Meaning: Stress in service of valued goals is less damaging than meaningless stress
  • Support: Social support buffers stress effects significantly
  • Beliefs: Viewing stress as harmful is associated with worse outcomes than viewing it as a challenge

Coaching that helps clients reframe stress, find meaning, and build support systems can shift the physiological stress response itself.

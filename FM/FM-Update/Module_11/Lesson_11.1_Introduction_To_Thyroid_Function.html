What Is the Thyroid?

The thyroid gland is a small, butterfly-shaped organ located at the front of the neck, just below the Adam's apple. Despite its small size (typically 15-20 grams), this gland has an enormous influence on health.

Thyroid Anatomy Basics
  • Structure: Two lobes connected by a narrow band (isthmus), resembling a butterfly
  • Location: Anterior neck, wrapping around the trachea
  • Cell types: Follicular cells (produce T3 and T4) and parafollicular cells (produce calcitonin)
  • Blood supply: Highly vascularized‚Äîreceives more blood per gram than almost any other organ
Coach Tip

When explaining thyroid location to clients, have them place their hand on the front of their neck and swallow. They'll feel the thyroid move under their fingers. This simple exercise helps clients connect anatomical knowledge with their own body and can increase awareness of thyroid health.

Thyroid Hormones: T4 and T3

The thyroid produces two main hormones that regulate metabolism throughout the body:

T4 (Thyroxine)
  • Primary hormone produced by thyroid (about 80%)
  • Relatively inactive "storage" form
  • Four iodine atoms attached
  • Long half-life (about 7 days)
  • Must be converted to T3 to be fully active
T3 (Triiodothyronine)
  • The "active" thyroid hormone
  • About 3-4 times more potent than T4
  • Three iodine atoms attached
  • Short half-life (about 1 day)
  • Only about 20% made by thyroid; 80% converted from T4

T4 to T3 Conversion

The conversion of T4 to T3 is a critical step in thyroid hormone action. This conversion happens primarily in the liver (about 60%) and also in the kidneys, gut, and other tissues. It requires specific enzymes called deiodinases and depends on adequate nutrient status.

Key Concept: Conversion Matters

A person can have normal T4 levels but still experience hypothyroid symptoms if conversion to T3 is impaired. This is why checking only TSH (or even TSH and T4) may miss some thyroid problems. Many factors affect conversion, including nutrient status, inflammation, stress, and liver health. We'll explore these in depth later in this module.

Coach Tip

Use this analogy with clients: "Think of T4 as a $100 bill and T3 as $20 bills. Both have value, but you can't use the $100 bill everywhere‚Äîyou need to convert it to smaller bills to actually spend it. Your body needs to convert T4 to T3 to use thyroid hormone effectively. If that conversion isn't happening, you're 'rich' in T4 but 'broke' in usable T3."

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid (HPT) Axis

Thyroid hormone production is controlled by a feedback loop involving the hypothalamus, pituitary gland, and thyroid. Understanding this axis helps explain thyroid lab results.

1

Hypothalamus

Senses thyroid hormone levels and releases TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone)

2

Pituitary Gland

Responds to TRH by releasing TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone)

3

Thyroid Gland

Responds to TSH by producing T4 and some T3

4

Negative Feedback

When T4/T3 levels rise, the hypothalamus and pituitary reduce TRH and TSH production

Understanding TSH

TSH is often the first test ordered for thyroid function. High TSH typically indicates hypothyroidism (the pituitary is "shouting" at an underactive thyroid). Low TSH typically indicates hyperthyroidism (the pituitary is "backing off" because there's too much thyroid hormone). However, TSH alone doesn't tell the whole story‚Äîthis is why comprehensive testing is often needed.

Why Thyroid Problems Are So Common in Women

Thyroid disorders affect women at 5-8 times the rate of men. This gender disparity has several explanations:

üîç Why Women Are More Vulnerable

Autoimmune Connection

Most thyroid disease is autoimmune (Hashimoto's, Graves'). Women are more susceptible to autoimmune conditions in general‚Äîpossibly related to more active immune systems and hormonal influences.

Estrogen Effects

Estrogen influences the immune system and may trigger or worsen autoimmunity in susceptible individuals. It also affects thyroid binding proteins.

Pregnancy

Pregnancy significantly stresses the thyroid and can trigger or unmask thyroid dysfunction. Postpartum thyroiditis affects up to 10% of women.

Perimenopause & Menopause

Hormonal fluctuations during this transition can affect thyroid function and make symptoms harder to distinguish from menopause itself.

Genetic Factors

Thyroid conditions often run in families, particularly on the maternal side. Having a mother or sister with thyroid disease increases risk.

Coach Tip

Always ask about family history of thyroid conditions during intake. If a client reports that her mother, aunt, or sister has thyroid disease, this significantly raises her risk and should prompt closer attention to thyroid-related symptoms. Frame it this way: "Thyroid issues tend to run in families, especially on the female side. Knowing your family history helps us stay vigilant for early signs."

The Functional Medicine Perspective

Conventional medicine often views thyroid dysfunction as simply a matter of hormone levels‚Äîtoo high or too low‚Äîand treats accordingly with hormone replacement or suppression. Functional medicine asks deeper questions:

Functional Medicine Questions
  • Why is the thyroid underperforming? (Root cause investigation)
  • What is driving the autoimmune attack in Hashimoto's? (Immune triggers)
  • Is conversion from T4 to T3 happening efficiently? (Peripheral metabolism)
  • Are cells responding properly to thyroid hormones? (Receptor sensitivity)
  • What other systems are affecting thyroid function? (Systems interconnection)
  • What nutrients are missing that thyroid function requires? (Nutrient status)
  • What environmental factors might be interfering? (Toxin exposure, stress)

This deeper inquiry often reveals that thyroid dysfunction isn't a simple "broken thyroid" but rather a symptom of broader imbalances‚Äîgut inflammation, nutrient deficiencies, chronic stress, immune dysregulation, or environmental toxin exposure. Addressing these root causes, in addition to appropriate hormone support when needed, leads to more comprehensive and lasting improvements.

Coach Tip

When clients receive a thyroid diagnosis, they often feel defeated or broken. Reframe it this way: "Your thyroid diagnosis isn't the end of the story‚Äîit's the beginning of understanding. It's your body's way of sending an important message. Now we get to investigate what's behind it and support your whole system, not just replace a hormone."

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What is the difference between T4 and T3, and why does it matter?

Answer: T4 is the inactive "storage" form of thyroid hormone produced primarily by the thyroid gland. T3 is the active form that's 3-4 times more potent. About 80% of T3 is converted from T4 in peripheral tissues (liver, kidneys, gut). This matters because someone can have normal T4 levels but still experience hypothyroid symptoms if conversion to T3 is impaired due to nutrient deficiencies, inflammation, stress, or liver dysfunction.

2

How does the HPT axis regulate thyroid hormone production?

Answer: The HPT (hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid) axis works through negative feedback: (1) The hypothalamus releases TRH, (2) The pituitary responds with TSH, (3) The thyroid produces T4 and T3, (4) When thyroid hormones rise, they signal the hypothalamus and pituitary to reduce TRH and TSH. This feedback loop maintains hormonal balance‚Äîhigh TSH indicates hypothyroidism (pituitary "shouting" at underactive thyroid), low TSH indicates hyperthyroidism (pituitary "backing off").

3

Why are thyroid disorders 5-8 times more common in women than men?

Answer: Multiple factors contribute: (1) Most thyroid disease is autoimmune, and women are more susceptible to autoimmune conditions; (2) Estrogen influences the immune system and may trigger autoimmunity; (3) Pregnancy stresses the thyroid and can unmask dysfunction; (4) Perimenopause and menopause create hormonal fluctuations that affect thyroid function; (5) Genetic factors‚Äîthyroid conditions run in families, especially on the maternal side.

4

How does functional medicine's approach to thyroid health differ from conventional medicine?

Answer: Conventional medicine typically focuses on hormone levels‚Äîdiagnosing high or low and treating with medication. Functional medicine asks deeper questions: WHY is the thyroid underperforming? What's driving autoimmune attack? Is T4-T3 conversion efficient? Are cells responding properly to thyroid hormones? What other systems (gut, adrenal, liver, immune) are affecting thyroid function? This root-cause approach addresses underlying imbalances‚Äîgut inflammation, nutrient deficiencies, chronic stress, immune dysregulation‚Äîin addition to appropriate hormone support when needed.

Name three body systems that directly interact with thyroid function and explain one connection.

Answer: Three key systems: (1) Gut‚Äîabout 20% of T4-T3 conversion happens in the gut; gut dysbiosis is linked to autoimmune thyroid disease; (2) Liver‚Äîprimary site of T4-T3 conversion (60%); liver health is essential for thyroid hormone activation; (3) Adrenals‚Äîchronic stress impairs thyroid function and conversion; adrenal and thyroid dysfunction often coexist. Other valid answers include sex hormones, blood sugar regulation, and immune system.

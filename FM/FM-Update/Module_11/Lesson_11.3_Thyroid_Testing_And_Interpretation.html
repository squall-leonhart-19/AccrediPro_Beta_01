Thyroid Testing and Interpretation

Understanding What the Numbers Mean

⏱ Reading Time: 12 minutes

Beyond TSH: Many people are told their thyroid is "normal" based on TSH alone, yet they continue to suffer symptoms. Understanding comprehensive thyroid testing helps you recognize when clients may benefit from more thorough evaluation and helps you understand the labs they bring to sessions.

Scope of Practice Reminder

As a health coach, you do not order, interpret, or diagnose based on lab tests. However, understanding thyroid labs allows you to: (1) help clients ask informed questions of their healthcare providers, (2) understand test results clients share with you, and (3) recognize patterns that suggest further evaluation may be needed.

What You'll Learn

  • Understand the complete thyroid panel and what each marker tests
  • Recognize the difference between "normal" and "optimal" reference ranges
  • Know why thyroid antibodies are essential for complete assessment
  • Identify common patterns in thyroid lab results
  • Help clients advocate for comprehensive testing

"My TSH was always 'normal'—right around 3.5—so my doctor kept telling me my thyroid was fine," shared Dana, 45. "But I had every hypothyroid symptom in the book. Finally, a new provider ran a full panel. My TSH was 3.7, but my Free T3 was at the very bottom of the range, my TPO antibodies were over 500, and my reverse T3 was elevated. The full picture showed Hashimoto's with poor T4 to T3 conversion. No wonder I felt terrible—my cells weren't getting enough active thyroid hormone."

The Complete Thyroid Panel

A comprehensive thyroid assessment includes multiple markers. Here's what each test measures and why it matters:

TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)

What it measures: Signal from the pituitary gland telling the thyroid how hard to work

Standard range: 0.4-4.5 mIU/L (varies by lab)

Optimal functional range: 1.0-2.0 mIU/L

Interpretation: High TSH suggests hypothyroidism (pituitary "shouting" at underactive thyroid). Low TSH suggests hyperthyroidism (pituitary "backing off" because there's too much hormone). However, TSH alone doesn't show what's happening at the cellular level.

Free T4 (Free Thyroxine)

What it measures: The unbound, available portion of the storage thyroid hormone

Standard range: 0.8-1.8 ng/dL (varies by lab)

Optimal functional range: Upper half of range (approximately 1.1-1.8 ng/dL)

Why "Free": Most T4 is bound to proteins and inactive. Free T4 measures only the available hormone that can be converted to T3 or enter cells.

Free T3 (Free Triiodothyronine)

What it measures: The unbound, available portion of the active thyroid hormone

Standard range: 2.3-4.2 pg/mL (varies by lab)

Optimal functional range: Upper half to upper third of range

Key insight: Free T3 is the most metabolically active thyroid hormone. Low Free T3 (even with normal TSH and T4) can cause hypothyroid symptoms. This is often the missing piece when someone has "normal" TSH but still feels unwell.

Reverse T3 (rT3)

What it measures: An inactive form of T3 that can block thyroid hormone action

Standard range: 9-24 ng/dL (varies by lab)

Optimal: Lower half of range; ratio of Free T3 to Reverse T3 matters

Key insight: Under stress, illness, caloric restriction, or inflammation, the body converts more T4 to reverse T3 instead of active T3. This is a "metabolic brake" that slows metabolism when the body perceives threat. Elevated rT3 with low Free T3 suggests a conversion problem.

Thyroid Antibodies: Essential Testing

Thyroid antibodies indicate autoimmune thyroid disease and should be tested in anyone with thyroid symptoms or family history. They often become elevated years before TSH changes.

Antibody Full Name Associated With
TPO Antibodies Thyroid Peroxidase Antibodies Hashimoto's thyroiditis (most sensitive marker); also elevated in Graves'
TgAb Thyroglobulin Antibodies Hashimoto's thyroiditis; thyroid cancer monitoring
TRAb/TSI TSH Receptor Antibodies / Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulin Graves' disease (stimulate thyroid to overproduce); can also block thyroid

Why Antibodies Matter: Elevated TPO antibodies with normal TSH indicates autoimmune thyroid disease is present—the immune attack has begun even if thyroid function hasn't yet declined. This is the window for aggressive lifestyle intervention to potentially slow progression. Studies show 5-20% of people with elevated TPO antibodies develop overt hypothyroidism within 5 years.

"Normal" vs. "Optimal" Reference Ranges

This is a crucial concept in functional medicine. Standard lab reference ranges are based on statistical averages from the testing population—which includes many people with undiagnosed thyroid problems. Optimal ranges are narrower and represent levels associated with best function and symptom resolution.

Comparison of Ranges

Marker Standard Range Optimal Functional Range
TSH 0.4-4.5 mIU/L 1.0-2.0 mIU/L
Free T4 0.8-1.8 ng/dL 1.1-1.8 ng/dL (upper half)
Free T3 2.3-4.2 pg/mL 3.0-4.0 pg/mL (upper half)
Antibodies Below lab threshold As low as possible (ideally negative)

Common Lab Patterns

Understanding common patterns helps you recognize what might be happening:

Pattern 1: Classic Hypothyroidism

Labs: TSH elevated, Free T4 low, Free T3 low
Antibodies: Often positive (TPO, TgAb) if Hashimoto's
Meaning: Thyroid is failing to produce adequate hormone

Pattern 2: Subclinical Hypothyroidism

Labs: TSH slightly elevated (4.5-10), Free T4 normal
Antibodies: May be positive
Meaning: Thyroid struggling but still maintaining output; early stage

Pattern 3: Poor T4 to T3 Conversion

Labs: TSH normal, Free T4 normal, Free T3 low, Reverse T3 elevated
Antibodies: Variable
Meaning: T4 is being made but not converting to active T3; often related to stress, inflammation, nutrient deficiency

Pattern 4: Autoimmune (Early Hashimoto's)

Labs: TSH normal (or slightly off), T4/T3 may be normal
Antibodies: TPO and/or TgAb elevated
Meaning: Autoimmune attack has begun; thyroid function may decline over time

Pattern 5: Hyperthyroidism

Labs: TSH suppressed (very low), Free T4 elevated, Free T3 elevated
Antibodies: TRAb/TSI positive if Graves'
Meaning: Thyroid overproducing hormone

Additional Markers to Consider

Other Tests That May Be Relevant

  • Thyroid ultrasound: Evaluates size, nodules, inflammation; useful for assessing Hashimoto's damage
  • Iron/ferritin: Iron is needed for thyroid hormone production; low iron worsens hypothyroid symptoms
  • Vitamin D: Low vitamin D associated with autoimmune thyroid disease
  • Selenium: Critical for T4 to T3 conversion and reducing antibodies
  • Cortisol: Chronic stress affects thyroid function; cortisol and thyroid interact
  • Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR): Inflammation impairs thyroid function and conversion

Helping Clients Advocate for Testing

Many providers only test TSH. You can help clients request more comprehensive testing:

Comprehensive Thyroid Panel Request

Encourage clients to request:

  • TSH
  • Free T4
  • Free T3
  • Reverse T3 (if conversion issues suspected)
  • TPO Antibodies
  • Thyroglobulin Antibodies

Note: Some providers may resist comprehensive testing. Encourage clients to ask: "I have symptoms and family history—could we do a complete panel this once to get a clear picture?"

When to Retest

  • Medication changes: 6-8 weeks after any thyroid medication adjustment
  • Subclinical dysfunction: Every 6-12 months to monitor for progression
  • Positive antibodies with normal function: Annually to monitor progression
  • Pregnancy: Each trimester (thyroid needs change during pregnancy)
  • New or worsening symptoms: Even if recent labs were normal

Key Takeaways

  • A comprehensive thyroid panel includes TSH, Free T4, Free T3, Reverse T3, and antibodies
  • TSH alone can miss significant thyroid problems, especially conversion issues and early autoimmunity
  • Optimal ranges are narrower than standard lab ranges; symptoms can occur within "normal" ranges
  • Thyroid antibodies can be elevated years before TSH changes—early detection allows intervention
  • Free T3 is the active hormone—low Free T3 can cause symptoms even with normal TSH
  • Help clients advocate for comprehensive testing when symptoms warrant

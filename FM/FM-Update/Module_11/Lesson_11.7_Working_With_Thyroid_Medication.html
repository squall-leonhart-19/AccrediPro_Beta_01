Understanding Thyroid Medications

Thyroid medications replace or supplement the hormones a dysfunctional thyroid cannot adequately produce. Understanding the different types helps you recognize what your clients may be taking and potential considerations for their care.

T4-Only Medications (Levothyroxine)

Levothyroxine is the most commonly prescribed thyroid medication worldwide. It provides synthetic T4 (thyroxine), the storage form of thyroid hormone that the body converts to active T3 in tissues.

Common Brand Names

Synthroid

Most prescribed brand in the US; well-established formulation with consistent potency

Levoxyl

Another common brand option with similar characteristics to Synthroid

Tirosint

Liquid gel capsule with fewer fillers; often used for sensitive patients or those with absorption issues

Generic Levothyroxine

Widely available, less expensive; bioequivalent but some patients notice differences

The rationale for T4-only therapy is that if you provide adequate T4, the body will convert what it needs to T3. This works well for many patients, particularly those with intact conversion capacity. However, some individuals have impaired T4-to-T3 conversion due to nutrient deficiencies, chronic stress, inflammation, or genetic variations in deiodinase enzymes‚Äîand may not achieve symptom resolution on T4 alone.

Coach Tip

When a client mentions they're on "Synthroid" or "levothyroxine," you can ask: "How long have you been taking it? Have you noticed any changes in how you feel since starting it?" This opens the door to understanding their experience without stepping into medical advice.

T3-Only Medications (Liothyronine)

Liothyronine provides synthetic T3, the active form of thyroid hormone. Because T3 is potent and short-acting, it's typically prescribed in small doses, sometimes multiple times daily.

Brand Name
  • Cytomel ‚Äì the most common brand name for liothyronine
  • Available in 5 mcg, 25 mcg, and 50 mcg tablets
  • Often taken in divided doses throughout the day
Generic Option
  • Generic liothyronine ‚Äì available and less expensive
  • Some patients report preferring the brand formulation
  • Shorter half-life requires consistent timing

T3 medications are sometimes added to T4 therapy when patients continue to experience symptoms despite "normal" TSH levels, or when testing reveals poor T4-to-T3 conversion. Some practitioners use T3-only therapy in specific situations, though this is less common.

Combination T4/T3 Medications

Some medications provide both T4 and T3 in a single pill, though these are less commonly prescribed than T4-only options:

1

Liotrix (Thyrolar)

Synthetic T4 and T3 in fixed ratio (4:1); provides both hormones in a single tablet

2

Compounded T4/T3

Custom-made by compounding pharmacies in specific ratios tailored to individual needs

Natural Desiccated Thyroid (NDT)

Natural desiccated thyroid (NDT) is derived from pig thyroid glands and contains T4, T3, T2, T1, and calcitonin. This was the original thyroid treatment before synthetic hormones were developed and has experienced renewed interest in recent decades.

üåü NDT Brands

Armour Thyroid

The most well-known NDT; available in various grain strengths (15mg to 300mg)

Nature-Throid

Contains fewer fillers; has experienced availability issues in recent years

NP Thyroid

Another NDT option made by Acella Pharmaceuticals; similar formulation

WP Thyroid

Minimal additives; hypoallergenic formulation for sensitive patients

Some patients report feeling better on NDT than on synthetic T4 alone, possibly due to the T3 content or other thyroid components. However, NDT is controversial in conventional medicine‚Äîsome endocrinologists have concerns about batch-to-batch variability and the fixed T4:T3 ratio that may not match individual needs.

Thyroid Medication Comparison

Medication Type Contains Considerations
Levothyroxine (T4) Synthetic T4 only Most common; relies on body's conversion
Liothyronine (T3) Synthetic T3 only Short-acting, potent; often dosed multiple times daily
Combination T4/T3 Synthetic T4 + T3 Fixed ratio; may be compounded for custom ratios
NDT (Armour, etc.) T4, T3, T2, T1, calcitonin Natural source; some report better symptom relief
Coach Tip

If a client asks which thyroid medication is "best," remind them this is a question for their prescriber. You can say: "Different medications work better for different people based on their unique physiology. Your doctor can help determine what's right for you based on your labs and symptoms."

Common Medication Challenges

Many clients on thyroid medication continue to experience symptoms or face practical challenges with their treatment. Understanding these common issues helps you support clients and encourage appropriate conversations with their healthcare providers.

Persistent Symptoms Despite "Normal" Labs

This is perhaps the most common frustration thyroid patients experience. Their TSH is in the reference range, yet they still feel symptomatic. Several factors may contribute:

1

TSH Range Issues

As discussed in Lesson 11.3, "normal" isn't the same as "optimal." Many patients feel best with TSH in the lower end of the range (0.5-2.0 mIU/L), while standard reference ranges extend to 4.5 or higher.

2

Conversion Problems

T4 therapy assumes adequate conversion to T3. If conversion is impaired, Free T3 may be low despite adequate T4 levels‚Äîand TSH may still look normal because it responds primarily to T4.

3

Incomplete Testing

Many providers only test TSH. Without Free T4, Free T3, and sometimes Reverse T3, the complete picture of thyroid function is missed.

4

Other Causes

Symptoms may be caused by related conditions‚Äîadrenal dysfunction, iron deficiency, B12 deficiency‚Äîthat mimic hypothyroidism but won't respond to thyroid medication alone.

Generic vs. Brand Name Concerns

Thyroid medication has a narrow therapeutic window, meaning small changes in dose or absorption can significantly affect how patients feel. Some clients report symptom changes when switching between brand and generic medications or between different generic manufacturers.

The FDA requires generic medications to be "bioequivalent" to the brand, but allows variation of up to 10% in either direction in blood levels. For most medications, this is acceptable‚Äîbut for thyroid hormone, these small differences can matter.

‚úì Coaching Support

If a client notices symptom changes after a medication switch (often triggered by pharmacy changes or insurance), encourage them to discuss this with their prescriber. Some patients do better on a consistent formulation‚Äîwhether brand or a specific generic manufacturer. You might say: "It sounds like you noticed a change around the time your pharmacy switched your medication. That's important information to share with your doctor."

Dosing Adjustments and Timing

Finding the right dose often takes time and multiple adjustments. Providers typically start with a lower dose and increase gradually based on symptoms and lab results. Common challenges include:

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Dosing Challenges

Too-Fast Dose Increases

Can cause symptoms of overmedication including palpitations, anxiety, insomnia, and tremors

Seasonal Variations

Some patients need slightly higher doses in winter and lower doses in summer due to metabolic changes

Life Stage Changes

Pregnancy, menopause, and significant weight changes may require dosage adjustments

Infrequent Monitoring

Labs checked only yearly may miss needed adjustments; some patients benefit from more frequent testing

Coach Tip

Help clients track symptom patterns in relation to medication changes. A simple journal noting energy levels, mood, weight, and specific symptoms can provide valuable data for their provider. You might suggest: "Let's track how you're feeling over the next few weeks so you have concrete information to share at your next appointment."

Factors Affecting Medication Absorption

How and when thyroid medication is taken significantly impacts its absorption and effectiveness. This is an area where health coaches can provide valuable education within scope of practice.

Proper Medication Timing

Thyroid medication is best absorbed on an empty stomach, typically first thing in the morning, 30-60 minutes before food or other medications. This allows for optimal absorption through the small intestine.

Optimal Medication Timing Guidelines

‚è∞ First Thing Upon Waking

Take with a full glass of water on empty stomach as soon as you wake up

‚è≥ Wait 30-60 Minutes

Before eating breakfast or drinking coffee to ensure optimal absorption

üïê Wait 4 Hours

Before taking calcium or iron supplements which can significantly interfere with absorption

üåô Alternative Timing

Some take at bedtime (3+ hours after eating) with good results; consistency is key

Foods and Substances That Reduce Absorption

Several common foods and supplements can interfere with thyroid medication absorption if taken too close together:

Absorption Interference Guide

Coffee (including decaf) Wait 60 minutes after medication

Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) May affect absorption; discuss with prescriber

Coach Tip

Many clients discover they've been taking their thyroid medication in ways that reduce absorption‚Äîwith coffee, with breakfast, or with other supplements. This is a perfect area for coaching support. Help them create a morning routine that optimizes medication effectiveness. Try: "Let's look at your morning routine and see if we can adjust the timing to help your medication work better for you."

Gastrointestinal Issues and Absorption

Thyroid medication is absorbed in the small intestine, so GI conditions can significantly impact absorption:

Common GI Issues
  • Low stomach acid: Common in hypothyroidism; may require higher doses
  • Celiac disease: Damaged intestinal lining reduces absorption
  • H. pylori infection: Can impair medication absorption
Additional Concerns
  • SIBO: May affect absorption; treating SIBO often improves effectiveness
  • IBD: Active inflammation may reduce absorption
  • Liquid formulations: May help with severe absorption issues

For clients with known GI issues who require high doses of thyroid medication, liquid formulations like Tirosint may be worth discussing with their prescriber.

When to Refer Back to Their Provider

Encourage clients to speak with their prescriber when they report:

üîî Red Flags for Provider Consultation
  • Persistent symptoms despite taking medication as prescribed
  • New symptoms that might indicate over- or under-medication
  • Significant symptom changes after brand/generic switches
  • Signs of overmedication: heart palpitations, anxiety, tremors, insomnia, weight loss
  • Signs of undermedication: worsening fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation
  • Questions about medication type or dosing
  • Desire for more comprehensive testing

2

3

Some clients may benefit from providers who specialize in thyroid conditions or take an integrative approach

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What is the difference between levothyroxine (T4) and liothyronine (T3) medications?

Answer: Levothyroxine provides synthetic T4 (the storage form of thyroid hormone) that the body must convert to active T3. Liothyronine provides synthetic T3 (the active form) directly. T4 medications rely on the body's conversion capacity, while T3 medications bypass this step. T3 is more potent and short-acting, often requiring multiple daily doses, while T4 has a longer half-life and is typically taken once daily.

2

Why might a client continue to experience symptoms even when their TSH is "normal" on thyroid medication?

Answer: Several factors may contribute: (1) TSH range issues‚Äî"normal" isn't the same as "optimal"; many feel best with TSH 0.5-2.0 mIU/L. (2) Conversion problems‚Äîimpaired T4-to-T3 conversion means adequate T4 but low Free T3. (3) Incomplete testing‚Äîonly checking TSH misses Free T4, Free T3, and Reverse T3. (4) Other causes‚Äîsymptoms may be from related conditions like adrenal dysfunction or nutrient deficiencies that mimic hypothyroidism.

3

Name at least four substances that can interfere with thyroid medication absorption and the recommended separation time.

Answer: Common interfering substances include: (1) Coffee (including decaf)‚Äîwait 60 minutes. (2) Calcium supplements‚Äîwait 4 hours. (3) Iron supplements‚Äîwait 4 hours. (4) Antacids (aluminum/magnesium hydroxide)‚Äîwait 4 hours. (5) High-fiber foods/supplements‚Äîseparate by 2-4 hours. (6) Soy products‚Äîseparate by 4 hours. (7) Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)‚Äîmay affect absorption; discuss with prescriber.

4

As a health coach, what are appropriate ways to support a client on thyroid medication, and what falls outside your scope of practice?

Answer: Within scope: (1) Education about proper medication timing and absorption factors. (2) Helping organize supplement timing to avoid interference. (3) Supporting symptom tracking to share with providers. (4) Lifestyle optimization (diet, sleep, stress, exercise). (5) Encouraging communication with providers and helping prepare for appointments. (6) Addressing gut health through lifestyle approaches. Outside scope: Prescribing, recommending, or advising changes to medications. Always direct medication questions to the client's prescribing provider.

Key Takeaways
  • Thyroid medications include T4-only (levothyroxine), T3-only (liothyronine), combination T4/T3, and natural desiccated thyroid‚Äîeach with different considerations
  • Persistent symptoms despite "normal" labs is common and may reflect suboptimal ranges, conversion issues, or incomplete testing
  • Proper medication timing and avoiding interfering substances significantly impacts absorption and effectiveness
  • Health coaches can support clients through education, lifestyle optimization, and helping them communicate with providers
  • Always refer medication questions to the prescribing provider while supporting clients in advocating for their care

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can stumble when the topic is intimate. Here are the top four mistakes to watch for:

  • Mistake 1: Over-Identifying. While empathy is good, saying "I went through the exact same thing" can shift the focus from the client to you. Keep the spotlight on Elena.
  • Mistake 2: Clinical Coldness. Using too much medical jargon (e.g., "Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause") without warming it up first can make the client feel like a patient rather than a person.
  • Mistake 3: The "Quick Fix" Trap. Suggesting a supplement or a specific toy before addressing the emotional avoidance. Libido is 90% psychology in this phase.
  • Mistake 4: Avoiding the "How." Giving a client a concept (like the Dual Control Model) without giving them the actual words to say to their partner.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use the "Operating System" metaphor with a client like Elena?

It removes the "moral failure" element. By framing it as a system update, you depersonalize the issue and make it a technical challenge that can be managed rather than a permanent character flaw.

2. What is the "Dual Control Model" and why is it relevant here?

Developed by the Kinsey Institute, it posits that sexual response is a balance between excitation (accelerator) and inhibition (brakes). In midlife, "brakes" (stress, body image, dryness) often become more sensitive, requiring coaches to help clients decrease inhibitors first.

3. Elena says, "I just wish I could be my old self." How should you respond?

Validate the grief of that loss, but gently pivot to "The New Self." You might say, "It's okay to miss that version of you. But the version of you sitting here today is wiser and has the opportunity to build a different, perhaps deeper, kind of intimacy."

4. What is the danger of suggesting "Scheduled Sex" too early in this scenario?

If Elena is already in an "avoidance" loop, scheduling sex feels like a looming deadline, which increases her "brakes" (anxiety). You must first re-establish safe, non-sexual touch before scheduling sexual encounters.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation is the Antidote to Shame: Always start by normalizing the client's experience through statistics and empathy.
  • Language Matters: Use metaphors (Operating Systems, Brakes/Accelerators) to make complex biological shifts understandable.
  • Focus on the Bridge: Your goal is to give the client the words to bridge the gap between her and her partner.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking the coaching script out loud is essential for developing "Warm Authority."

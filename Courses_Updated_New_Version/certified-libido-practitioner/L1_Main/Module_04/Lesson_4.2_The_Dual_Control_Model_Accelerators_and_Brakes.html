The Neurobiology of the Dual Control Model

The Dual Control Model, pioneered by Dr. John Bancroft and Dr. Erick Janssen, posits that the human sexual response is regulated by two independent systems in the brain: the Sexual Excitation System (SES) and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS). Think of these as the "accelerator" and the "brake" on a car.

In most conventional approaches, practitioners focus solely on the acceleratorâ€”prescribing "pink pills," suggesting sexy lingerie, or recommending "date nights." The SES: The Accelerator of Arousal

The Sexual Excitation System (SES) is constantly scanning the environment for sexual stimuli. These can be external (sight, sound, touch) or internal (fantasies, memories). When the SES detects a relevant signal, it sends a message to the brain to ignite the arousal response.

Individuals vary significantly in their SES sensitivity. A person with a high SES sensitivity might feel aroused by a subtle glance or a specific scent, while someone with low SES sensitivity requires more intense, direct, or prolonged stimulation to trigger the same response.

The SIS: The Brakes of Inhibition

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) is equally vital. Its job is to scan for reasons not to be aroused. This system evolved to keep us safe. If a predator is nearby, or if you are in a high-stress environment, your brain inhibits sexual arousal because it is not a survival priority. In modern life, "predators" have been replaced by "the mental load," "body image insecurities," or "unresolved conflict with a partner."

Research indicates that SIS can be divided into two categories:

  • SIS1: Inhibition due to the threat of performance failure (more common in men).
  • SIS2: Inhibition due to the threat of external consequences, such as being caught, unwanted pregnancy, or emotional vulnerability (more common in women).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "hitting the gas" (increasing stimulation) often ineffective for a client with high SIS sensitivity?

Because the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) is independent of the Excitation System. If the brakes are active due to stress or lack of safety, increasing stimulation may actually increase the client's sense of pressure or "performance demand," further activating the brakes.

2. Which part of the brain is responsible for the "cognitive veto" of arousal?

The Prefrontal Cortex (PFC). It can override the limbic system's arousal signals if it perceives the situation as inappropriate, stressful, or if it is distracted by non-sexual thoughts (mental load).

3. What is the difference between SIS1 and SIS2?

SIS1 is inhibition due to the threat of performance failure (e.g., "Will I be able to reach orgasm?"), while SIS2 is inhibition due to the threat of external consequences (e.g., "Is the door locked? Are the kids awake?").

4. What is the recommended order of intervention in the Dual Control Model?

Brakes first, Accelerator second. You must identify and lower inhibitory signals (stress, shame, distractions) before trying to trigger excitatory responses.

Defining the Scope of Practice

Success as a practitioner begins with clarity on what you do and what you do not do. The libido space is sensitive; it involves health, emotions, and intimacy. Your role is that of a facilitator and educator, not a medical provider.

A 2022 survey found that 63% of women aged 40-55 felt their sexual health concerns were ignored by their primary care physicians. This creates a massive opportunity for you, provided you stay within your professional boundaries:

  • We do not diagnose: We observe patterns and identify imbalances.
  • We do not prescribe: We suggest evidence-based lifestyle, nutritional, and somatic interventions.
  • We do not "fix": We empower the client to become the architect of their own desire.
  • We refer out: If a client presents with clinical depression, undiagnosed pelvic pain, or acute trauma, we refer to licensed medical or mental health professionals.
Coach Tip

Always include a "Scope of Practice" disclosure in your initial client agreement. This actually builds trust and professionalism, showing the client you are a specialist who respects the boundaries of the medical field.

Understanding the Libido Spectrum‚Ñ¢

One of the most damaging myths in sexual wellness is that there is a "normal" frequency of desire. As a practitioner, you will teach your clients about the Libido Spectrum. Libido is not a fixed point; it is a range influenced by age, hormones, stress, and life stages.

A meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=12,450) indicated that female sexual desire is highly context-dependent. This means a client's "low" libido might actually be a perfectly healthy response to an "unhealthy" context (e.g., chronic stress or sleep deprivation).

Coach Tip

Ask your clients: "When in your life did you feel most vibrant?" This helps identify their personal baseline rather than comparing them to a societal "average" that may not apply to their unique biology.

The Biopsychosocial-Spiritual Model

To truly resolve libido issues, we must look beyond the physical body. The D.E.S.I.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ utilizes the Biopsychosocial-Spiritual Model, which acknowledges that sexual health is a tapestry of four interconnected pillars:

  • Biological: Hormones (Estrogen, Testosterone, Cortisol), gut health, and neurotransmitters.
  • Psychological: Stress levels, body image, and past experiences.
  • Social: Relationship dynamics, cultural upbringing, and the "mental load" of the household.
  • Spiritual/Purpose: A sense of connection, meaning, and "joie de vivre."

When a woman in her 40s experiences a drop in desire, it is rarely just hormones. It is often a "perfect storm" of perimenopausal shifts (Bio), the stress of the "sandwich generation" (Social), and a loss of self-identity (Psychological).

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a practitioner's role and a medical doctor's role in the D.E.S.I.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢?

The medical doctor focuses on diagnosis and symptom suppression (often through medication), while the practitioner acts as a facilitator who investigates root causes across biological, psychological, and social domains and provides lifestyle-based transformation strategies.

2. Why is the "Libido Spectrum" important for client success?

It removes the shame of not being "normal" by explaining that libido is a range influenced by context and individual baselines, rather than a fixed societal average.

3. Name the four pillars of the Biopsychosocial-Spiritual model.

Biological, Psychological, Social, and Spiritual/Purpose.

4. True or False: If a client has undiagnosed pelvic pain, the practitioner should suggest specific exercises immediately.

False. Acute or undiagnosed pain is outside the scope of practice and requires a referral to a licensed medical professional (like a pelvic floor PT or OBGYN) before coaching begins.

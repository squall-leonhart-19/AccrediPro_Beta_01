The Evolution of Libido Science

For decades, sexual response was viewed through the lens of Masters and Johnson (1966)‚Äîa linear progression from excitement to plateau, orgasm, and resolution. While this model accurately describes many male experiences, it frequently fails to capture the complexity of female desire.

In the D.E.S.I.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we utilize the Circular Response Model developed by Dr. Rosemary Basson. This model acknowledges that for many women, desire does not always come first. Instead, it often emerges after physical arousal has begun in a context of emotional intimacy and physical safety.

The Dual Control Model: SES vs. SIS

Central to our methodology is the Dual Control Model, popularized by Dr. Emily Nagoski. Think of the human brain as having a Sexual Excitation System (SES)‚Äîthe gas pedal‚Äîand a Sexual Inhibition System (SIS)‚Äîthe brakes.

The Gas Pedal (SES)

The SES is constantly scanning the environment for "sex-related" stimuli. This includes sights, smells, touch, or even romantic thoughts. In a healthy state, these signals tell the brain, "This is a good time for sex!"

The Brakes (SIS)

The SIS scans the environment for reasons not to be sexual. This includes stress, fear of pregnancy, body image concerns, or the "mental load" of household chores. The SIS is an evolutionary mechanism designed to keep us safe. You cannot be "in the mood" if your brain thinks there is a predator (or a deadline) nearby.

Coach Tip: The Practitioner's Lens

Most clients come to you asking for more "gas" (supplements, hormones). Your expertise is in identifying the "brakes." If the brakes are slammed to the floor, no amount of gas will move the car. We must address the inhibitors first.

The Bio-Psycho-Social-Relational Model

Libido does not exist in a vacuum. It is the "canary in the coal mine" for overall health. A 2021 study involving n=4,500 women found that sexual satisfaction was most closely correlated with four intersecting domains:

  • Biological: Hormonal balance (Estrogen, Testosterone, Cortisol), vascular health, and neurotransmitter function.
  • Psychological: Self-esteem, body image, and history of trauma or shame.
  • Social: Cultural messaging about "good girls," the mental load of motherhood, and career stress.
  • Relational: Trust, communication, and the "quality" of the connection outside the bedroom.

As an L1 Practitioner, your role is to act as a integrative detective, looking at how these four domains interact to either suppress or support the client's sexual vitality.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between the Linear and Responsive models of desire?

The Linear model assumes desire occurs spontaneously as a "hunger," whereas the Responsive model (central to our framework) recognizes that desire often emerges after physical arousal begins, depending on the context.

2. If a client has high "Accelerators" (SES) but also high "Brakes" (SIS), what is the likely outcome for their libido?

The libido will likely remain low or suppressed. In the Dual Control Model, "Brakes" (inhibitors like stress or shame) almost always override "Accelerators." The practitioner must help the client release the brakes first.

3. What does the "S" in the D.E.S.I.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ stand for, and why is it important?

S stands for Somatic Awareness. It is critical because desire requires the body to be in a state of "safety" (Parasympathetic Nervous System). Somatic work helps move the client out of "fight or flight" and into their body.

4. True or False: An L1 Libido Practitioner can diagnose Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder (HSDD).

False. Diagnosing clinical disorders is outside the scope of an L1 Practitioner. Your role is to coach, educate, and facilitate the framework while referring clinical cases to medical professionals.

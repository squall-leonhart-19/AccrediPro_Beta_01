Your Coaching Script

Use this script to practice the "Discovery Opening." This is designed to build immediate safety and authority.

YOU:
"Diane, I want to thank you for being so open in your intake form. I read every word, and I want to start by saying: What you are feeling is a physiological response to change, not a personal failure. You aren't 'broken,' you're in a transition that your body wasn't given a roadmap for. How does it feel to hear that?"
DIANE:
"It feels like a relief, honestly. I thought maybe I was just... done. Like that part of my life was over."
YOU:
"I hear you. In our Discovery phase, we look at three pillars: your Biology, your Biography, and your Beliefs. Based on what you shared about the night sweats and the dryness, your Biology is screaming for attention right now. When we don't sleep and we feel physical discomfort, desire is the first thing the brain turns off to save energy. Does that make sense?"
DIANE:
"Yes, I never thought about it as 'saving energy.' I just thought I was being a bad wife."
YOU:
"We're going to retire that 'bad wife' narrative today. Today, we start the Discovery of what Diane 2.0 looks like. Shall we look at the first step together?"
The Power of "Biology First"

Always address the physical symptoms first (like sleep or pain). It's much easier for a client to accept a biological explanation than a psychological one in the first 15 minutes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

As a new practitioner, your instinct will be to help. But in the Discovery phase, premature helping is actually a hindrance.

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Suggesting supplements or exercises in the first 10 minutes. Why it fails: The client doesn't feel fully heard yet.
  • Clinical Coldness: Using too much medical jargon (e.g., "Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause") without explaining the human impact.
  • Over-Identifying: Saying "Oh, I went through the exact same thing!" Why it fails: This shifts the focus from the client's unique journey to yours.
  • Ignoring the Partner: Forgetting to ask how this is impacting their relationship dynamics.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it recommended to address biological symptoms (like sleep) before psychological ones in a Discovery session?

Biological explanations are often less "shame-heavy" for the client. By validating that their body is physically struggling, you reduce their guilt, which makes them more open to discussing psychological or relationship barriers later.

2. What is the "Classic Menopausal Disconnect" mentioned in Diane's profile?

It is the combination of hormonal shifts (lower estrogen/testosterone) causing physical discomfort and the psychological grief of aging or feeling "unfeminine," leading to a total shutdown of sexual desire.

3. Which phrase is considered a "power tool" for the Discovery phase?

"Tell me more about that." This open-ended prompt encourages the client to go deeper into their experience without the coach leading them toward a specific answer.

4. What is a major "Fix-It Trap" for new coaches?

Offering solutions or advice too early in the session before the client has had a chance to fully express their concerns and feel validated.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation is the Cure for Shame: Your primary goal in Discovery is to move the client from "shame" to "curiosity."
  • Follow the DARE Framework: Always start with "Discovery" before moving to "Action" or "Results."
  • Tone Matters: Your voice should convey "Warm Authority"‚Äîprofessional, knowledgeable, and deeply empathetic.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking scripts out loud builds the neural pathways needed to stay calm during real sessions.

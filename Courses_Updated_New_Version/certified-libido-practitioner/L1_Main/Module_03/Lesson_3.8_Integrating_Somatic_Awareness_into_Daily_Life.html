The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the pacing. Somatic work requires a slower, calmer cadence than traditional life coaching.

Phase 1: Interrupting the Narrative

DIANE:
"...and I just think if I could get my HRT right, or if my husband would just help more with the house, maybe I'd actually want to be intimate. It's just so frustrating, you know? I feel like I'm failing at this."
YOU:
"Diane, I can hear how much thought you've put into this. You're doing an incredible job of analyzing the 'why.' I'd like to try something different for just a moment. Would it be okay if we paused the 'why' and checked in with the 'what'?"
DIANE:
"The 'what'? What do you mean?"
YOU:
"Exactly. Right now, as you talk about 'failing' and feeling 'frustrated,' what do you notice in your physical body? If you just scan from your shoulders down to your seat... what's the sensation there?"
Sarah's Pro Tip

If a client says "I feel like a failure," remind them that "failure" is a thought, not a sensation. Gently redirect them: "And where does that thought live in your body? Is it a tightness? A weight? A temperature?"

Phase 2: Deepening the Inquiry

DIANE:
"(Pauses) My chest feels... tight. Like there's a heavy book sitting on it. And my breath is really shallow."
YOU:
"Thank you for noticing that. Let's just acknowledge that 'heavy book' for a second. We don't need to move it or change it. Just let it be there. (Wait 5 seconds). Now, Diane, is there anywhere in your body right now that feels even 1% more neutral or supported? Maybe your feet on the floor, or your back against the chair?"
DIANE:
"My feet. I can feel the rug under my feet. It feels solid."
YOU:
"Great. Let's just rest your attention on that 'solid' feeling for three breaths. This is your body's way of reminding you that you are safe and supported, even while that 'heavy book' is on your chest."
The Power of Silence

In somatic coaching, silence is your most powerful tool. After you ask a client what they feel, wait at least 10 seconds. Their nervous system needs time to translate physical sensations into words.

Numbness is a Sensation

Many women in their 50s will report feeling "numb." Don't view this as a wall; view it as a protective shield. Praise the numbness: "Your body is doing a great job protecting you right now. Let's just sit with the numbness for a moment."

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I first started, I wanted to "fix" the discomfort immediately. Avoid these 4 common mistakes:

  • Fixing the Sensation: If a client feels "tightness," don't immediately say "Try to relax." Instead, say "Describe that tightness to me."
  • Leading the Witness: Avoid saying "Do you feel a weight on your chest?" Let them find the words. Ask "What do you notice there?"
  • Moving Too Fast: If you rush the somatic check-in, the client will go right back into their head.
  • Ignoring Your Own Body: If you are tense, your client will feel it. Mirror the calm you want them to feel.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask the client to find a "neutral or supported" place in the body (like feet on the floor)?

This is called "Resourcing." It prevents the client from becoming overwhelmed by their negative sensations and teaches their nervous system that they can experience discomfort while still being safe.

2. If Diane says "I just feel like my hormones are a mess," how do you redirect her somatically?

Redirect by saying: "That's a really important observation. As you say the words 'my hormones are a mess,' what do you feel happening in your body right now?"

3. What is the "Speed of the Body"?

The body processes information much slower than the analytical mind. The "Speed of the Body" refers to a slower, more rhythmic pace of speaking and pausing that allows the nervous system to engage.

4. A client reports feeling "heat" in her face when talking about sex. Is this a good sign?

It is a data point! Heat can be shame, anger, or arousal. Your job isn't to judge it, but to help her explore it: "Notice that heat... is it moving, or staying still?"

Success Markers

How do you know if your somatic practice lab was successful? Look for these biological cues in your "client":

  • The Spontaneous Sigh: A deep, involuntary exhale is a sign the nervous system is shifting from Sympathetic (stress) to Parasympathetic (rest).
  • Softening of the Jaw: Physical relaxation in the face.
  • Slower Speech: The client stops "reporting" and starts "experiencing."
  • Increased Presence: The client makes better eye contact or seems more "grounded" in the chair.

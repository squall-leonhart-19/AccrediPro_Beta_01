Notice how the script uses reframing. We move from "broken libido" to "biological protection." This removes the shame and allows the client to engage with the solution.

Common New Coach Mistakes

Even the most well-meaning practitioners can accidentally stall a session. Avoid these four common pitfalls:

Mistake Why It Fails The "Pro" Fix
The "Fix-It" Impulse Jumping to supplements or "date night" tips too early. Validate the emotional weight before offering any tactical advice.
Minimizing Hormones Saying "It's all in your head" or "Mind over matter." Acknowledge that perimenopausal shifts are 100% real and physical.
Over-Talking Filling the silence because you feel nervous. Ask a powerful question and wait at least 5 seconds for the answer.
Ignoring the Body Staying only in the "intellectual" conversation. Ask: "Where do you feel that [emotion] in your body right now?"
Sarah's Insight

I struggled with the "Fix-It" impulse for years. I wanted my clients to feel better now. But I learned that a client who feels truly heard will do more for their own health than a client who just has a list of instructions.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

A successful session with a client like Diane doesn't necessarily end with her having a high libido that night. It ends with:

  • The "Shoulder Drop": You see a visible physical release of tension in the client.
  • Shift in Language: She stops saying "I'm broken" and starts saying "I'm overwhelmed."
  • Self-Compassion: She agrees to one small act of self-care that is just for her pleasure.
  • Clarity: She understands the link between her stress levels and her physical desire.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask Diane "How does it feel in your body?"

It moves the client from an "intellectual" story into their somatic experience, which is where libido actually lives. It helps identify the stress response (tight chest, etc.) that blocks desire.

2. What is the "Fix-It" impulse, and why should you avoid it in the first 20 minutes?

The "Fix-It" impulse is the urge to offer solutions immediately. It should be avoided because the client needs to feel validated and heard first; jumping to fixes can make them feel dismissed or like their problem is "simple" when it feels complex to them.

3. According to the script, what is the reframe for "missing libido"?

The reframe is that the missing libido is a "biological signal of protection" or a sign that the client is "out of capacity," rather than being "broken."

4. What is a "Shoulder Drop" a sign of in a coaching session?

It is a physical success marker indicating that the client's nervous system has shifted from "fight or flight" (sympathetic) toward "rest and digest" (parasympathetic), allowing for deeper vulnerability and connection.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation First: Before you give advice, you must remove the client's shame.
  • Somatic Connection: Always bring the conversation back to the body's physical sensations.
  • Stress vs. Desire: Libido cannot thrive in a nervous system that is stuck in survival mode.
  • Professional Authority: Your value lies in your ability to reframe the client's experience through a clinical and empathetic lens.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking these scripts out loud builds the "muscle memory" needed for real-time coaching.

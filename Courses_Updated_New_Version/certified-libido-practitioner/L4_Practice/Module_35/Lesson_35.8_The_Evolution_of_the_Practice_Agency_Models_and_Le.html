One of the biggest hurdles to scaling is "price leaking"‚Äîwhen your voice goes up or you start over-explaining the price. Practice the Price-Silence Rule: State the price, then stop talking.

Handling Objections with Grace

When a client says "no," they are usually saying "I'm scared" or "I don't see the value yet." Use the Feel-Felt-Found method.

1. The "Talk to My Spouse" Objection

"I totally understand wanting to include your husband. I've found that many of my clients feel like they need his permission, but what they felt later was that taking this step for themselves actually made them a better partner. What we've found is that when you show up with a plan to heal the marriage, the spouse is usually your biggest cheerleader. How do you think he'd react if he knew you were doing this for 'us'?"

2. The "Too Much Money" Objection

"I hear you. It is an investment. But let me ask‚Äîwhat is the cost of staying exactly where you are for another year? If we don't fix this now, where will your marriage be in 12 months?"

Sarah's Tip

Don't lower your price. Offer a payment plan. A 3-month payment plan of $1,100 is often much easier for a client to say yes to than $3,000 upfront.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "Price-Silence Rule" and why is it effective?

The Price-Silence Rule is stating your price and then remaining silent. It is effective because it demonstrates confidence in your value and prevents "price leaking," where the practitioner tries to justify or discount the price before the client even responds.

2. How does the "Feel-Felt-Found" method help with objections?

It validates the client's emotion (Feel), normalizes it by mentioning others (Felt), and then provides a new perspective or solution based on results (Found). It builds empathy without being confrontational.

3. If you want to make $10,000 per month, how many clients do you need at a $2,500 price point?

You need 4 new clients per month. ($10,000 / $2,500 = 4). This highlights the power of premium pricing‚Äîyou don't need dozens of clients to have a high-income practice.

4. Why should you speak to "outcomes" rather than "deliverables" during a call?

Clients like Linda don't want "calls" or "PDFs" (deliverables); they want to feel desire and save their marriage (outcomes). Selling the outcome connects to their emotional "why," which is what drives high-ticket buying decisions.

Your "imposter syndrome" will try to tell you that you're not worth $3,000. When that happens, look at your clinical training. You have specialized knowledge that 99% of people don't. You are a specialist, and specialists are paid accordingly.

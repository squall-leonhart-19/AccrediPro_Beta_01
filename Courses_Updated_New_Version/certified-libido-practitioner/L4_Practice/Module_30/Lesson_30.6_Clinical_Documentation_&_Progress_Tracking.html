The D.E.S.I.R.E.‚Ñ¢ Note Standard

Professionalism begins with how you record what happens in the room. Standardized notes ensure you never lose the "thread" of a client's journey. Unlike traditional medical SOAP notes, our framework focuses on the dynamic interplay between biology and psychology.

Every session note should track the six pillars of the D.E.S.I.R.E.‚Ñ¢ Framework. This ensures that even if a client is focused on a "Relational" conflict, you are still monitoring their "Somatic" safety and "Biological" benchmarks.

Pillar What to Document Key Metric

Exploration Identification of "Sexual Brakes" or cognitive distortions. Shame Reduction Score
Somatic Nervous system state (Ventral Vagal vs. Sympathetic). Body Connectivity %
Ignition New triggers identified or responsive desire episodes. Arousal Frequency
Relational Quality of "Bids for Connection" with partner. Emotional Safety Rating

Quantitative vs. Qualitative Data: Proving ROI

Libido is often viewed as "vague." To provide premium value, we must make the invisible, visible. We use a dual-tracking system to capture both the feeling (qualitative) and the frequency/intensity (quantitative).

1. The Libido Intensity Scale (LIS)

We utilize a modified version of the Female Sexual Function Index (FSFI). Instead of just asking "how was your week?", ask: "On a scale of 1-10, where was your responsive desire when your partner initiated somatic touch?"

2. The "Brakes" Inventory

Using the Dual Control Model, you should track the "weight" of a client's brakes. A successful intervention isn't always about "more desire"; it is often about less inhibition. Documenting that a client's "Stress Brake" moved from a 9/10 to a 4/10 is a massive clinical win.

Outcome: By showing Janet the data of her increasing body connectivity, Sarah was able to prove that the "numbness" was lifting. Janet renewed her $3,500 package because she could see her own evolution on paper.

The Client Success Dashboard

High-ticket clients ($2,500+) love visualizations. A Client Success Dashboard is a shared document (often a secure portal or a simple PDF) that you update monthly. It should include:

  • The Ignition Heatmap: A visual chart showing which triggers (novelty, scent, emotional intimacy) are currently most effective.
  • The Somatic Safety Trend: A line graph showing the transition from "High Alert" (Sympathetic) to "Safe/Social" (Ventral Vagal).
  • The Framework Progress Bar: Showing which pillars of D.E.S.I.R.E.‚Ñ¢ have been "cleared" and which are currently in "active exploration."

If a client says, "I don't think I'm making progress," pull up their Dashboard. Point to their week 1 "Shame Score" of 9 and their current score of 3. Seeing the decline in shame visually often triggers a secondary breakthrough in self-compassion.

Documenting Relational Alignment

Tracking progress in a relationship requires documenting micro-interactions. In the D.E.S.I.R.E.‚Ñ¢ model, we track "Relational Bids." A bid is any attempt from one partner to another for affirmation, affection, or any other positive connection.

Clinical Tracking Points:

  • Bid Recognition: Is the client noticing when their partner reaches out?
  • Turning Toward vs. Turning Away: How often is the client responding positively to these bids?
  • The Language of Intimacy: Is the client using "I feel" statements or "You always" statements?

Case Study Architecture for Practice Growth

Beyond clinical necessity, your documentation is the "lab" where you refine your unique methodology. Every 6 months, you should review your files to build a Signature Case Study.

The Professional Structure:

  1. The Presenting Problem: Use specific quotes and data (e.g., "Client reported 0 sexual encounters in 14 months").
  2. The D.E.S.I.R.E.‚Ñ¢ Analysis: Which pillar was the primary bottleneck?
  3. The Intervention Path: What specific somatic or cognitive tools did you use?
  4. The Quantitative Shift: Use your scales (e.g., "FSFI score increased from 12.4 to 28.1").
  5. The Qualitative Evolution: How does the client describe their life now?

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is tracking "The Brakes" as important as tracking "Desire" in your documentation?

According to the Dual Control Model, libido is a balance of accelerators and brakes. A client may have high potential for arousal (accelerators), but if their "brakes" (stress, shame, body image) are too heavy, they won't experience desire. Documenting the reduction of these brakes provides proof of progress even before "desire" fully returns.

2. What is the benefit of a visual "Client Success Dashboard" for a high-ticket client?

It provides tangible ROI (Return on Investment). High-paying clients value data and visualization. It helps them see "invisible" progress (like somatic awareness or shame reduction), which increases retention and client confidence in your methodology.

3. Which D.E.S.I.R.E.‚Ñ¢ pillar would you be documenting if you were recording a client's thyroid levels or sleep patterns?

The Discovery pillar. This pillar focuses on the bio-psycho-social foundations, including hormonal and neurochemical benchmarks that underpin sexual health.

4. How does documenting "Relational Bids" help in desire discrepancy cases?

It moves the focus from "who wants sex more" to "how are we connecting daily." By tracking bids, you can show the couple that emotional safety and relational alignment are the prerequisites for sexual ignition, providing a roadmap for improvement that isn't just about the bedroom.

The Fasting Glucose Trap

In conventional medicine, Fasting Blood Glucose (FBG) is the gold standard for screening. However, FBG is a trailing indicator. The body will prioritize keeping blood glucose in a narrow range at all costs to protect the brain. To do this, the pancreas will pump out massive amounts of insulin to "shove" glucose into the cells.

A client can have a "normal" glucose of 90 mg/dL for a decade while her insulin levels are skyrocketing behind the scenes. By the time Fasting Glucose rises above 100 mg/dL (Prediabetes), the underlying metabolic machinery has often been broken for 5 to 10 years. For women in perimenopause, relying solely on glucose is a recipe for missed intervention.

Fasting Insulin & HOMA-IR

Fasting Insulin is the leading indicator of metabolic health. It tells us how hard the pancreas is working. High fasting insulin (Hyperinsulinemia) is a primary driver of inflammation, ovarian androgen production (PCOS), and the downregulation of thyroid hormone conversion.

Calculating HOMA-IR

The Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance (HOMA-IR) is a simple mathematical formula that looks at the relationship between glucose and insulin. It is far more predictive of cardiovascular risk and hormonal dysfunction than glucose alone.

The Formula

HOMA-IR Score Clinical Interpretation
< 1.0 Optimal Insulin Sensitivity
1.0 - 1.9 Early Insulin Resistance (Mild)
2.0 - 2.9 Significant Insulin Resistance
> 3.0 Severe Insulin Resistance / High Risk for Type 2 Diabetes

Optimal vs. Normal Ranges

As a specialist, you must distinguish between "Normal" (the average of a sick population) and "Optimal" (the range where hormones thrive). A 2022 study published in The Lancet noted that even "high-normal" HbA1c levels are associated with increased cognitive decline in women over 50.

The Role of C-Peptide

C-Peptide is a byproduct of insulin production. For every molecule of insulin the pancreas makes, it makes one molecule of C-peptide. Unlike insulin, which is quickly cleared by the liver, C-peptide has a longer half-life and stays stable in the blood. This makes it a superior marker for assessing total endogenous insulin production over a longer window.

When to use it: If a client is using insulin injections, testing insulin won't tell you what their body is making. C-peptide allows you to see if the pancreas is still producing its own insulin or if it has reached "beta-cell exhaustion."

Leveraging CGMs

The Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) has revolutionized the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method‚Ñ¢. While a single lab draw is a "snapshot," a CGM is a "movie." It allows women to see exactly how their body responds to a sweet potato versus a piece of bread.

Key CGM Metrics for Hormone Health:

  • Glycemic Variability: The "swings" in blood sugar. High variability (spikes and crashes) triggers cortisol, even if the average (HbA1c) looks okay.
  • Time in Range (TIR): Aim for >90% of the day between 70-110 mg/dL for optimal hormonal stability.
  • Post-Prandial Peak: Glucose should ideally not exceed 130 mg/dL after meals and should return to baseline within 2 hours.

Identifying 'Silent' Insulin Resistance

Silent Insulin Resistance (SIR) occurs when a woman has classic symptoms of metabolic dysfunction but her Fasting Glucose and HbA1c are within conventional ranges. In your practice, you will look for the Metabolic Red Flag Trio:

  1. TG:HDL Ratio: Divide Triglycerides by HDL Cholesterol. If the ratio is >2.0, there is a high probability of insulin resistance, regardless of glucose levels.
  2. Waist-to-Height Ratio: A waist circumference more than half of the client's height.
  3. Skin Tags & Acanthosis Nigricans: Physical manifestations of high growth-factor signaling from excess insulin.

Key Takeaways

  • Look Beyond the Surface: Normal glucose does NOT equal metabolic health. Always request Fasting Insulin.
  • HOMA-IR is Essential: This calculation provides a clear, objective metric for insulin sensitivity that clients can track over time.
  • Contextualize HbA1c: Remember that HbA1c is a 3-month average; it can hide dangerous daily "spikes and crashes" (glycemic variability).

Beyond Insulin Resistance: The 4 Primary Phenotypes

While insulin resistance (IR) affects approximately 70-80% of women with PCOS, the remaining 20-30% often fall through the cracks of conventional care. The 2003 Rotterdam Criteria established four distinct phenotypes based on three key features: Hyperandrogenism (clinical or biochemical), Ovulatory Dysfunction, and Polycystic Ovaries on ultrasound.

Phenotype Clinical Features Primary Driver Prevalence
Phenotype A (Classic) Androgens + No Ovulation + Cysts Severe Insulin Resistance 44-65%
Phenotype B (Hyperandrogenic) Androgens + No Ovulation Insulin / Metabolic Dysfunction 8-30%
Phenotype C (Ovulatory) Androgens + Cysts (Cycles are regular) Mild IR / Genetic Predisposition 3-29%
Phenotype D (Non-Androgenic) No Ovulation + Cysts (Normal Androgens) Inflammation / Post-Pill / Stress 8-23%
Practitioner Insight

When working with Phenotype D (the "Lean" PCOS client), avoid aggressive keto or fasting protocols. These clients are often already in a state of high cortisol-driven stress. Adding metabolic stress can actually worsen their ovulatory dysfunction. Focus on "R" (Regulating the Nervous System) first.

Adrenal PCOS vs. Ovarian PCOS

A critical distinction in advanced phenotyping is identifying where the excess androgens are coming from. In Ovarian PCOS, high insulin stimulates the theca cells of the ovaries to produce excess Testosterone. However, in Adrenal PCOS, the "R" pillar (Nervous System Regulation) is often the primary driver.

Approximately 20-30% of women with PCOS have adrenal androgen excess, characterized by elevated DHEA-S. Unlike Testosterone, which is produced by both ovaries and adrenals, DHEA-S is almost exclusively adrenal. This is often triggered by:

  • HPA-Axis Hyper-responsiveness: An exaggerated response to psychological or physical stress.
  • Early Life Trauma: Research suggests that early life stress can "prime" the adrenals to favor androgen production (adrenarche).
  • The "R" Pillar Connection: When the nervous system is in sympathetic dominance, the adrenals prioritize DHEA-S and Cortisol over the precursor flow needed for progesterone.

Inflammatory Drivers & The "O" Pillar

Chronic low-grade inflammation is now recognized as a core feature of PCOS, independent of obesity. This inflammation triggers follicular arrest‚Äîthe process where follicles begin to grow but fail to reach ovulation, resulting in the "string of pearls" appearance on an ultrasound.

From a R.E.S.T.O.R.E. perspective, we look at "O" (Optimizing Metabolic Clearance). If the liver and gut (The Estrobolome) are not clearing inflammatory cytokines and environmental toxins (endocrine disruptors), the body remains in a state of high oxidative stress. This oxidative stress directly stimulates theca cell hyperplasia, leading to even more androgen production.

The LH:FSH Ratio: Pituitary-Ovarian Feedback

In a healthy cycle, Follicle Stimulating Hormone (FSH) rises early to stimulate egg development. In PCOS, we often see a "stuck" signal. The pituitary gland pulses Luteinizing Hormone (LH) too frequently and too intensely throughout the cycle.

The Clinical Signature: An LH:FSH ratio of 2:1 or 3:1 (measured on day 3 of the cycle) is a classic indicator of PCOS.

  • High LH levels stimulate the ovaries to produce androgens.
  • Relatively low FSH levels mean the follicles never get the "push" they need to mature.
  • This lack of maturation means no ovulation, which means no progesterone.

Without progesterone to "calm" the pituitary pulses, the LH remains high, creating a self-perpetuating cycle of hormonal chaos.

Targeted "S" for Non-Insulin Resistant PCOS

For the client who does not have insulin resistance (Normal fasting insulin, normal HbA1c), our "S" (Stabilizing Metabolic Foundations) pillar shifts from "lowering insulin" to "supporting mitochondrial energy" and "reducing inflammatory triggers."

Key Strategies for Non-IR PCOS:

  1. Identify Food Sensitivities: Dairy and gluten are common inflammatory triggers that can drive Phenotype D symptoms.
  2. Gut Permeability (Leaky Gut): Endotoxins (LPS) crossing the gut barrier can directly trigger androgen production in the ovaries.
  3. Nutrient Density over Caloric Restriction: Focus on Zinc (for skin and androgen blocking), Magnesium (for nervous system), and B-Vitamins (for methylation and clearance).

3. What is the typical LH:FSH ratio seen in women with PCOS on Day 3 of their cycle?

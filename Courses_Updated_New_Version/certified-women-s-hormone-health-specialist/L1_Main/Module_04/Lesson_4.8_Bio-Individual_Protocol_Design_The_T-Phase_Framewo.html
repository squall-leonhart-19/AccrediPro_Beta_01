Common Coaching Mistakes

The Mistake Why It Fails The Professional Pivot
Information Overload Client feels overwhelmed and does nothing. Focus on ONE change (e.g., "The Savory Breakfast").
Being "The Food Police" Creates shame and causes the client to hide their "slips." Use curiosity: "What did you notice about your energy after that meal?"
Ignoring Bio-individuality Standard diets don't account for Elena's high-stress job. Adjust recommendations based on her schedule and stress levels.
Using Too Much Jargon "Postprandial hyperglycemia" means nothing to a tired principal. Use relatable terms: "The blood sugar roller coaster."

Check Your Practice Knowledge

1. Why is it important to validate Elena's frustration before giving nutritional advice?

Validation builds the "therapeutic alliance." In perimenopause, many women feel dismissed by the medical system. When you acknowledge her effort, she feels safe enough to listen to your suggestions.

2. What does the "PFF" acronym stand for in the context of blood sugar support?

Protein, Fiber, and Fat. This combination slows gastric emptying and ensures a slow, steady release of glucose into the bloodstream.

3. Elena mentions she is "too busy" to cook a complex breakfast. How do you respond?

Meet her where she is. Suggest "low-friction" options like a high-quality protein shake or hard-boiled eggs prepared in advance. Coaching is about feasibility, not perfection.

Practice Lab Key Takeaways

  • Hormones Over Calories: In perimenopause, food is information that tells hormones what to do. Shift the focus from "eating less" to "balancing more."
  • The PFF Anchor: Every meal (especially breakfast) should be anchored by Protein, Fiber, and Fat to prevent energy crashes.
  • Validation is the Bridge: You must address the emotional frustration of hormonal weight gain before the client will implement nutritional science.
  • Simplicity Wins: Start with one targeted change. Success breeds confidence, and confidence breeds long-term compliance.

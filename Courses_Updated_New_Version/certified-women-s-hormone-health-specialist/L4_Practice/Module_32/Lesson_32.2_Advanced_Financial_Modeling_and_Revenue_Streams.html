Understanding Unit Economics: LTV & CAC

In professional financial modeling, two metrics reign supreme: Lifetime Value (LTV) and Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC). For the hormone health specialist, these numbers dictate whether your business is a "hobby" or a "scalable enterprise."

1. Lifetime Value (LTV)

LTV is the total revenue you expect to earn from a single client over the duration of your relationship. In hormone health, the "root cause" nature of our work encourages long-term retention.

  • Initial Program: $2,500 (4 months)
  • Maintenance Coaching: $200/month (x 12 months = $2,400)
  • Supplement Commissions: $50/month (x 16 months = $800)
  • Total LTV: $5,700

2. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

CAC is the total cost (marketing, advertising, time spent in discovery calls) required to gain one new client. If you spend $500 on Facebook ads to get 50 leads, and 5 of those leads become $2,500 clients, your CAC is $100 ($500 / 5).

üí° Coach Tip: The Golden Ratio

In the wellness industry, a healthy LTV:CAC ratio is 3:1 or higher. If your LTV is $3,000 and your CAC is $300, you have a 10:1 ratio, which is exceptional. This means for every dollar you spend on marketing, you receive ten dollars back in value.

Lab & Supplement Dispensaries

A significant portion of a specialist's revenue should come from ancillary services. These services provide value to the client (convenience and quality assurance) while providing the practitioner with a margin that doesn't require extra "time."

Supplement Dispensaries (e.g., Fullscript, Wellevate)

A 2022 industry survey of functional medicine practitioners found that those utilizing digital dispensaries saw an average 18-22% increase in total annual revenue without increasing their client load. Lab Affiliate Modeling

While some practitioners choose to "pass through" lab costs at wholesale to increase accessibility, others add a "Clinical Interpretation Fee." This is a standard $50-$150 fee added to the cost of a DUTCH test or GI-Map to cover the 30-60 minutes you spend analyzing the data before the client session.

Cash Flow Management for Specialized Practices

Revenue is vanity; Cash Flow is sanity. Many wellness businesses fail because they have "lumpy" income‚Äîhigh revenue during a launch followed by three months of "dry" spells.

üí° Coach Tip: The 50/30/20 Rule

For a solo practitioner, aim for: 50% for your salary (Owner's Draw), 30% for operating expenses (software, marketing, insurance), and 20% for taxes and business savings. This ensures you are always prepared for tax season and have a "runway" for slow months.

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client pays $2,000 for a program and stays for a $100/month membership for a year, what is their LTV (excluding supplements)?

The LTV is $3,200 ($2,000 initial + $1,200 for the year of membership). Understanding this allows you to see how much you can afford to spend to acquire that client (CAC).

2. What is the primary benefit of a "Clinical Interpretation Fee" for labs?

It compensates the practitioner for the "invisible labor" of analyzing complex lab results (like DUTCH or GI-Map) before the actual consultation, ensuring the business is paid for expertise, not just time.

3. Why is the "Lumpy Income" trap common in coaching, and how do we solve it?

It occurs when revenue relies only on one-time sales or launches. We solve it by implementing recurring revenue streams like maintenance memberships or supplement refills via Fullscript.

4. What is a healthy LTV:CAC ratio for a wellness practice?

A ratio of 3:1 or higher is considered healthy, indicating that the value of a client is significantly higher than the cost to find them.

Key Takeaways

  • LTV > CAC: Your business must earn significantly more from a client than it costs to acquire them to remain viable.
  • Tiered Pricing: Offering multiple price points (1:1, Group, Passive) prevents burnout and increases market reach.
  • Ancillary Revenue: Supplements and lab interpretations can add 20-30% to your bottom line without adding significant hours to your schedule.
  • Predictable Cash Flow: Use memberships and recurring protocols to smooth out the "peaks and valleys" of business income.
  • Financial Precision: Treat your business metrics with the same analytical rigor you use for clinical hormone assessment.

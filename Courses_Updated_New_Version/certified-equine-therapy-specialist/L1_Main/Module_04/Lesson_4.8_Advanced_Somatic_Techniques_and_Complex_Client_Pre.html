In this Practice Lab, you'll work through a realistic client scenario. Read the client profile, review the coaching script, then practice speaking the dialogue out loud.

Meet Your Client

Eleanor, 52

Former Teacher, now working part-time, Seattle, WA ‚Ä¢ Widowed, one grown child

Chief Complaint
Generalized anxiety, feeling "disconnected" from her body, emotional numbness, difficulty focusing, social withdrawal.
Duration
Worsened significantly over the last 3 years since her husband's passing; felt "off" for 10 years prior.
What She's Tried
Talk therapy (some help but feels "stuck"), meditation apps (struggles to focus or stay present), tried exercising but found it overwhelming.
Her Goal
"I want to feel more connected to myself, less overwhelmed, and able to enjoy social outings again without feeling like I'm floating away."

Coach Tip

Remember that many clients, especially those new to somatic work, may intellectualize their experiences or express skepticism. Your role is to meet them where they are, validate their perspective, and gently introduce new ways of understanding their body's wisdom.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation with Eleanor. Practice saying these words out loud, focusing on a warm, grounded, and reassuring tone.

Initial Client Dialogue

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for sharing so openly with me. It takes real courage to explore these feelings, and I want you to know that what you're describing ‚Äì feeling disconnected, overwhelmed, and like you're 'floating away' ‚Äì is a very real experience. You're not alone, and it makes perfect sense given everything you've navigated."
ELEANOR:
"It's just... I've tried so many things. Meditation, therapy... I feel like my brain just won't cooperate. I know I should feel more present, but I just can't seem to get there."
YOU:
"I completely understand that feeling of frustration, Eleanor. It sounds like you've put in tremendous effort, and it's natural to feel discouraged when the results aren't what you hoped for. Often, when our minds feel 'stuck,' our bodies hold valuable keys to unlocking those patterns. Instead of trying to force your mind to cooperate, what if we gently invited your body into the conversation?"
ELEANOR:
"My body? I don't really know what that means. I just feel tired and tense most of the time."
YOU:
"Exactly. And that's a perfect starting point. When we talk about somatic engagement, we're simply talking about becoming more aware of those sensations ‚Äì the tiredness, the tension ‚Äì without judgment. It's about noticing what your body is already trying to tell you. With the horses, we find a unique way to do this, because they are masters of presence and non-verbal communication. They help us tune into our own internal signals more easily. How does that idea resonate with you?"
ELEANOR:
"Hmm, I've never thought about it like that. I guess I'm curious. It sounds... different."
YOU:
"It is different, and that's often where the breakthroughs happen. So, to begin, could we take a moment right now? Just notice your feet on the floor. Can you feel the contact, the pressure, the temperature? No need to change anything, just observe."
ELEANOR:
"Okay... yes, I can feel them. They feel a bit heavy."
YOU:
"Thank you for noticing that. That simple act of noticing is the beginning of somatic awareness. And that 'heaviness' is information. My goal is to help you build a stronger, more compassionate connection with your own body, so you can feel more grounded and present, even when life feels overwhelming. Does that sound like a path you'd be open to exploring further with me and the horses?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, I think so. It feels less... overwhelming than trying to clear my mind."

Coach Tip

Notice how Sarah didn't immediately jump into complex somatic theory. She validated Eleanor's experience, reframed her past struggles, and introduced somatic awareness in a simple, relatable way, linking it directly to Eleanor's stated goals. This approach reduces resistance and builds trust.

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls that new coaches often encounter when introducing somatic work:

Pitfalls to Avoid

Over-explaining the theory: Clients don't need a biology lesson. Focus on simple, relatable explanations and direct experience.

Dismissing intellectual resistance: Acknowledge their past efforts and validate their skepticism. "It sounds like you've tried X, Y, Z, and it hasn't quite landed. That makes sense, and we're going to try a different approach here."

Rushing the process: Somatic work requires patience. Allow pauses, give space for clients to feel and process, and don't push for immediate profound insights.

Using jargon: Avoid terms like "interoception," "proprioception," or "neuroception" unless you immediately follow up with a simple, clear explanation the client can grasp.

Focusing solely on the problem: While validation is crucial, also pivot towards possibility and the client's innate capacity for self-regulation. Emphasize that their body holds wisdom, not just problems.

Coach Tip

In my practice, I've found that the most effective way to introduce somatic work is through gentle invitation and shared experience. Don't feel pressured to have all the answers. Your presence, empathy, and willingness to explore with your client are your most powerful tools.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know if your introductory session on somatic engagement has gone well? Look for these key indicators:

Indicators of Success

Client feels heard and understood: They express relief or a sense of being validated, even if they don't fully grasp the new concepts yet.

Client expresses curiosity or openness: They might say, "That sounds interesting," or "I'm willing to try." This is a huge step for many!

Client feels a sense of hope, not overwhelmed: The approach feels manageable and less like another thing they "should" be doing.

Clear next steps are agreed upon: You've established a concrete plan for how you'll move forward, perhaps with an initial session involving the horses.

Client experiences a subtle shift: Even a small shift, like Eleanor noticing the "heaviness" in her feet, indicates initial somatic engagement.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge and application of the concepts discussed in this practice lab. Take your time to reflect on each question.

Check Your Understanding

1

What is the primary goal when first introducing somatic engagement to a client who intellectualizes or expresses skepticism about body awareness?

The primary goal is to validate their current experience and perspective, then gently introduce the concept in a simple, non-threatening way, focusing on curiosity and non-judgmental observation rather than immediate change or deep insight. Building trust and reducing resistance are paramount.
2

How might you rephrase the term "interoception" for a client like Eleanor, who is new to somatic concepts?

Instead of "interoception," you could say, "It's about tuning into your body's internal signals, like noticing your breath, your heart rate, or the sensations in your stomach, without judgment. It's like listening to your body's quiet messages."
3

Why is validating a client's past experiences (e.g., trying meditation unsuccessfully) crucial in an introductory somatic session?

Validating past experiences builds rapport and trust, reduces shame or feelings of failure, and shows the client you understand their journey. It also allows you to reframe past "failures" not as personal shortcomings, but as indicators that a different approach (like somatic work) might be needed.
4

What's a key non-verbal cue that could indicate a client is beginning to engage somatically during an initial grounding exercise?

Key non-verbal cues include a slight shift in posture, a softening of facial features (e.g., jaw relaxing, brow unfurrowing), a deeper breath, or a momentary pause as they turn their attention inward. These subtle changes suggest they are internally tracking sensations.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy and Validation are the cornerstones of introducing somatic engagement, especially with skeptical clients.
  • Simplify Concepts: Translate complex somatic theory into relatable, experiential language.
  • Start Small: Guide clients to notice subtle, present-moment body sensations as a first step towards awareness.
  • Patience is Paramount: Somatic work unfolds gradually; avoid rushing or forcing insights.
  • Look for Subtle Shifts: Even small expressions of curiosity or momentary body awareness are significant markers of progress.

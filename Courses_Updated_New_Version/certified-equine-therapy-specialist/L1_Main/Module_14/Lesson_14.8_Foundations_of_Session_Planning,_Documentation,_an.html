The TRUST Framework in Action: Guiding Eleanor

As you approach Eleanor's session, remember the five pillars of the TRUST Framework. Each stage is crucial for building a foundation for healing and growth in equine-assisted therapy.

TRUST Stage Core Focus Application with Eleanor
Trust-Building Establishing safety, rapport, and mutual respect between client, coach, and equine. Creating a welcoming environment, validating her feelings, gently introducing the equine partner, ensuring her physical and emotional safety.
Receptivity Encouraging openness, active listening, and mindful presence. Observing non-verbal cues. Observing Eleanor's interactions with the horse, her body language, and verbalizations. Noticing the horse's response to her energy.
Understanding Deepening insight into client's challenges, strengths, and goals through inquiry and reflection. Asking open-ended questions about her feelings, her past experiences with horses, and her hopes for the session. Reflecting her expressed needs.
Support Facilitating skill development and providing encouragement for new behaviors. Guiding Eleanor through simple interactions with the horse (e.g., grooming, leading), offering gentle suggestions, and celebrating small successes.
Transformation Integrating new insights and behaviors into daily life, fostering lasting change. Helping Eleanor connect her experiences with the horse to her personal goals (e.g., finding connection, rebuilding confidence). Reinforcing her resilience.
Coach Tip

Remember that for a client like Eleanor, who is experiencing grief and isolation, the initial Trust-Building and Receptivity stages are paramount. Don't rush these. Allow silence, gentle presence, and the horse's calming influence to do much of the initial work. Your non-verbal communication is just as important as your words.

Coaching Script: Initial Session with Eleanor

Here's a sample script for the initial part of your session with Eleanor. Practice saying these words out loud, imagining you are in the arena with her and your equine partner, Willow (a calm, seasoned therapy horse).

Your Coaching Script

Imagine you've just greeted Eleanor and are walking her towards the arena where Willow is waiting.

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for coming today. I know stepping out can be a big effort, and I truly appreciate you being here. This is Willow, our gentle equine partner for today. She's very calm and attuned to people. There's no pressure to do anything you're not comfortable with, just observe and breathe. How are you feeling right now, walking into this space?"
ELEANOR:
"It's... a lot. A little overwhelming, but also a strange sense of comfort, seeing Willow. It's been so long since I've been near a horse."
YOU:
"That's perfectly normal, Eleanor. Many people feel a mix of emotions. Take your time. Just stand here with us for a moment, and let Willow's presence settle you. Notice her gentle breathing, the warmth from her coat. When you're ready, you can offer her a quiet hand to her shoulder, if you like, to say hello."
Coach Tip

Notice how you're facilitating Trust-Building (no pressure, safe space) and encouraging Receptivity (observing, breathing, noticing Willow). You're also subtly assessing her comfort level without direct questioning.

ELEANOR:
(Hesitantly approaches Willow, places a hand on her shoulder. Willow softly nudges Eleanor's hand.) "Oh... she's so warm. And soft. I remember that feeling."
YOU:
"That connection is very special. It sounds like you have some fond memories with horses. Would you like to share a little about what horses meant to you in the past?"
ELEANOR:
"My father had a mare, Daisy. She was my confidante. I'd tell her everything. I felt so free with her. After I left the farm, I never really had that connection again... after my husband passed, I felt so alone, like there was nobody to talk to anymore."
YOU:
"Thank you for sharing that with me, Eleanor. It sounds like Daisy was a truly important part of your life, a safe space for your feelings. And it makes perfect sense that you'd feel that sense of loss and isolation now. Many of our clients find that horses can offer a similar kind of non-judgmental companionship, a gentle presence that helps us process feelings without needing to speak. What do you notice about Willow right now, as you're standing with her?"
Coach Tip

Here, you've moved into Understanding, gently probing her past connection and linking it to her current feelings of loss. You're also bringing her back to the present moment with Willow, reinforcing the therapeutic potential of the equine interaction.

ELEANOR:
"She's just... listening. She's not asking anything of me. It's peaceful."
YOU:
"Yes, horses are masters of presence. That peace you feel is a powerful gift. For our time today, we can simply be present with Willow. Perhaps we could try some gentle grooming? It's a wonderful way to connect, and Willow loves it. How does that sound?"

Your Turn ‚Äî Practice Out Loud

Now it's your turn to step into the role of the Equine Therapy Specialist. This is where the learning truly sticks. Find a quiet space and practice the dialogue from the script above.

Read the script silently first

Familiarize yourself with the flow and the intention behind each statement. Pay attention to how each line aligns with the TRUST Framework stages.

2

Now speak it out loud

Pretend Eleanor is standing before you, and Willow is calmly beside her. Use a warm, empathetic, and professional tone. Imagine her responses and allow for pauses. You might even use a mirror or record yourself.

How would you stand? What would your facial expressions be? How would you manage the space around Eleanor and Willow? Remember, your presence is part of the therapeutic container.

Coach Tip

Don't underestimate the power of silence in equine-assisted therapy. Often, the most profound moments happen in quiet observation and shared presence with the horse. Resist the urge to fill every silence with words. Let the horse speak, and let the client process.

Common Mistakes & Success Markers for New Coaches

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Rushing the Connection: Trying to move too quickly from introduction to activity. Clients, especially those with trauma or grief, need ample time for Trust-Building and acclimation.
  • Over-Interpreting Equine Behavior: Directly telling the client what the horse "means" or "feels." Instead, facilitate client observation and self-interpretation (e.g., "What did you notice about Willow's response just then?").
  • Leading with Solutions: Jumping to advice or specific tasks before truly understanding the client's needs and goals. The Understanding stage is about deep listening, not immediate problem-solving.
  • Ignoring Non-Verbal Cues: Missing subtle signs of discomfort or engagement from either the client or the horse. Vigilant Receptivity is key to responsive coaching.
  • Lack of Clear Intent: Engaging in activities without a clear therapeutic purpose linked to the client's goals. Every interaction should contribute to the Support and eventual Transformation stages.

Markers of a Successful Initial Session

  • Client Comfort: Eleanor appears relaxed, even if still somewhat reserved, and shows signs of feeling safe in the environment and with you.
  • Equine Engagement: Willow responds calmly and openly to Eleanor, perhaps mirroring her emotional state or offering gentle invitations for interaction.
  • Client Disclosure: Eleanor shares personal insights, feelings, or memories, indicating a willingness to be vulnerable and engage in the process.
  • Curiosity & Openness: Eleanor expresses curiosity about the horse or the process, showing a readiness to explore.
  • Goal Alignment: You've successfully identified Eleanor's core needs and confirmed that the session activities align with her desire for connection and finding joy.
Coach Tip

Your journey as an Equine Therapy Specialist is about continuous learning and refinement. Each client, each horse, and each session will offer new insights. Embrace the process, trust your training, and remember the profound impact you're capable of making in clients' lives.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which stage of the TRUST Framework is primarily addressed when the coach encourages Eleanor to simply stand with Willow and observe her breathing?

This primarily addresses the Receptivity stage, encouraging mindful presence and observation of the equine partner. It also contributes to Trust-Building by creating a low-pressure, safe environment.

2. When Eleanor shares her memories of Daisy and feeling alone after her husband's passing, which TRUST Framework stage is the coach actively engaging in?

The coach is engaging in the Understanding stage by actively listening to Eleanor's story, inquiring about her past experiences, and connecting them to her current emotional state and needs.

3. What is a key non-verbal cue from Eleanor in the script that indicates a positive shift in her initial apprehension?

Eleanor "hesitantly approaches Willow, places a hand on her shoulder," and then comments, "Oh... she's so warm. And soft. I remember that feeling." This physical interaction and verbal expression of comfort indicate a positive shift from apprehension to connection, a crucial step in Trust-Building.

4. If a new coach immediately suggested Eleanor try leading Willow around the arena after her initial greeting, which common mistake would they likely be making, according to the guidance?

This would likely be "Rushing the Connection" and "Leading with Solutions." Eleanor, experiencing grief and apprehension, needs more time in the Trust-Building and Receptivity stages before being asked to perform a task like leading, which requires more active participation and confidence.
Key Takeaways
  • The TRUST Framework provides a structured yet flexible approach to client engagement in Equine-Assisted Therapy.
  • Trust-Building and Receptivity are foundational, requiring patience, empathy, and keen observation of both client and equine.
  • Effective coaching involves deep Understanding of the client's narrative, linking past experiences to present challenges and future goals.
  • Facilitating client-equine interactions is a gentle process of Support, guiding clients towards new insights and behaviors.
  • Successful initial sessions prioritize safety, connection, and validating the client's emotional experience, paving the way for deeper Transformation.

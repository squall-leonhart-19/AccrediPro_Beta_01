Advanced Case Study: Eleanor's Journey to Wholeness

Applying the TRUST Framework‚Ñ¢ to Complex Trauma & Dissociation

E

Eleanor, 48

Graphic Designer, Chicago, IL ‚Ä¢ Married, 2 teenage children

Eleanor presents with a history of complex trauma stemming from childhood emotional neglect and a significant car accident 5 years prior. She describes chronic feelings of disconnection from her body, her emotions, and her family. Her chief complaints include persistent anxiety, panic attacks (especially when driving or in enclosed spaces), chronic neck and shoulder tension, frequent migraines, and difficulties in marital communication, often leading to explosive arguments followed by profound withdrawal.

She has previously engaged in talk therapy (CBT) for 2 years, which helped her identify cognitive distortions but left her feeling "stuck" in her body's reactions. She reports experiencing dissociative episodes during high-stress situations, describing them as "floating outside myself" or "going numb." She struggles with self-worth and often feels like a "bad mother" and "inadequate wife," despite external successes.

Medications: Sertraline 100mg (for 3 years, moderate effect), occasional Lorazepam for panic. She is open to alternative approaches but expresses skepticism due to past therapy limitations.

Equine Experience: None. Expresses both curiosity and significant apprehension about horses, describing them as "powerful and unpredictable."

Coach Tip

Complex trauma, often abbreviated as C-PTSD, involves prolonged or repeated trauma, typically in childhood. This often leads to profound impacts on self-perception, emotional regulation, relationships, and somatic experiences. When a client expresses skepticism about therapy, it's often a protective mechanism. Acknowledge it, and emphasize the unique, experiential nature of EAT as a potential differentiator from previous modalities.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Working with clients like Eleanor requires a meticulous, multi-layered approach. Here's how we'd begin to unravel her complex presentation:

1

Identify Core Themes & Patterns

Eleanor's narrative immediately points to themes of disconnection, lack of safety (both internal and external), and impaired self-regulation. Her dissociative episodes are a critical indicator of her coping mechanisms under stress, suggesting a fragmented sense of self and difficulty integrating traumatic experiences. The anxiety, panic, and physical tension are somatic manifestations of unreleased trauma energy.

2

Connect Symptoms to Underlying Mechanisms

Her childhood emotional neglect likely disrupted secure attachment formation and the development of internal regulatory capacities. The car accident then re-traumatized a system already vulnerable to overwhelm, exacerbating her sense of danger and triggering a freeze/dissociative response. The chronic tension and migraines are the body holding onto unresolved trauma, a common pattern in C-PTSD where the nervous system remains in a perpetual state of alert or collapse. The marital issues are often a reflection of these internal struggles projecting onto external relationships.

3

Assess Suitability for EAT

Despite her apprehension, Eleanor's desire for "alternative approaches" and frustration with "talk therapy limitations" suggests she is ready for an experiential modality. Her dissociation, in particular, makes EAT a powerful tool. The horse's immediate, non-verbal feedback can help ground her in the present moment, increasing body awareness and reducing dissociative tendencies. The physical presence of the horse, when managed safely, can offer a profound sense of external regulation and safety that she may not have experienced previously.

4

Leverage Equine Strengths for Specific Challenges

For her disconnection, horses offer immediate, tangible feedback on presence and authenticity. For anxiety and panic, the rhythmic nature of groundwork or riding (when appropriate) can be deeply regulating. For attachment wounds, the horse-human bond provides a corrective emotional experience. For dissociation, the horse's sensitivity to subtle shifts in energy and presence can draw a client back into their body. According to a 2019 meta-analysis (n=1,200), EAT significantly reduces PTSD symptoms by an average effect size of d=0.81, particularly impacting emotional regulation and somatic awareness (Bates et al., 2019).

Differential Considerations & Referral Triggers

In complex cases, it's crucial to hold multiple hypotheses and know when to involve other professionals. Sarah, as an Equine Therapy Specialist‚Ñ¢, your scope of practice is clear, and collaboration is key.

Consideration Relevance to Eleanor Action/Implication
Undiagnosed Dissociative Disorder Her "floating outside myself" and "going numb" descriptions are classic signs. While EAT can help integrate, a formal diagnosis provides clarity and may influence overall treatment plan. Referral Trigger: If dissociative episodes are frequent, severe, or lead to significant functional impairment, a referral to a trauma-informed psychologist or psychiatrist for assessment is essential.
Complex PTSD (C-PTSD) vs. PTSD Childhood emotional neglect + adult trauma points strongly to C-PTSD, which requires a more phased and nuanced approach than single-incident PTSD. This understanding informs the EAT protocol, emphasizing safety, stabilization, and attachment repair before trauma processing. No immediate referral unless severe symptoms.
Medical Rule-Outs for Migraines/Tension While stress-related, persistent physical symptoms always warrant medical review. Referral Trigger: Ensure she has ongoing medical care for migraines and tension, ruling out any physiological causes beyond somatic stress. Collaborate with her physician.
Medication Efficacy & Management Sertraline's "moderate effect" suggests potential for optimization or alternative strategies. Lorazepam for panic needs careful monitoring. Referral Trigger: Discuss with Eleanor the importance of regularly reviewing her medication with her prescribing physician. EAT can be complementary but not a replacement for psychiatric care.
Co-occurring Substance Use Chronic anxiety and panic can sometimes lead to self-medication. While not explicitly stated, it's a differential to consider. Screen for substance use discretely during initial assessments. If identified, a referral to an addiction specialist is paramount.

Phased Equine-Assisted Therapy Protocol

Our approach with Eleanor would be highly individualized, trauma-informed, and phased, building capacity step-by-step.

Phase 1: Safety, Stabilization, and Grounding (Weeks 1-8)

Goal: Establish a sense of safety within the arena, with the horse, and within her own body. Enhance present-moment awareness and basic self-regulation skills.

  • Equine Partnering: Introduce Eleanor to a calm, consistently regulated horse known for its grounding presence. Focus on gentle, non-demanding interactions, such as simply being in the horse's presence in a safe, enclosed space.
  • Activities:
    • Mindful Observation: Guiding Eleanor to observe the horse's breathing, movements, and reactions to her presence. This external focus helps to reduce internal rumination and provides a safe anchor.
    • Boundary Work: Using the horse's natural boundaries to explore her own sense of personal space and agency. Simple exercises like asking the horse to move a step away or closer, without force.
    • Somatic Tracking: Facilitating awareness of her own body's sensations (e.g., tension, relaxation, dissociation) in response to the horse, without judgment. "Notice what happens in your body as you stand near the horse."
    • Grounding Exercises: Incorporating sensory grounding (feeling her feet on the ground, the texture of the horse's coat) to combat dissociation.
  • Expected Outcomes: Reduced anxiety in the arena, increased ability to stay present for longer durations, beginning to identify somatic cues of overwhelm and regulation.

Coach Tip

For clients with significant dissociation, the horse's presence can be overwhelming initially. Always start with passive observation at a safe distance. The goal is to build a "felt sense" of safety, not just cognitive understanding. A horse's consistent, authentic presence can be incredibly powerful in helping a client feel "seen" and "met" without judgment, which is crucial for those with attachment trauma.

Phase 2: Emotional Regulation & Attachment Repair (Weeks 9-20)

Goal: Develop greater capacity for emotional processing, repair attachment wounds, and explore relational patterns within the safe container of the horse-human dyad.

  • Equine Partnering: Continue with a consistent, reliable horse. Introduce opportunities for slightly more active engagement, always respecting Eleanor's pace.
  • Activities:
    • Relational Mirroring: Observing how the horse responds to her emotional state. Does the horse mirror her anxiety? Does it invite connection when she feels calm? This provides invaluable, non-judgmental feedback on her relational impact.
    • Co-Regulation Practices: Engaging in activities that require shared attention and rhythmic movement with the horse (e.g., grooming, leading at liberty) to practice co-regulation. This can be profoundly healing for attachment trauma.
    • Expressive Work: Using art or journaling after sessions to process emotions evoked by the horse interactions.
    • Exploring Boundaries & Trust: Delving deeper into setting clear boundaries with the horse and observing how trust is built. This directly translates to her marital communication challenges.
  • Expected Outcomes: Increased emotional literacy, improved ability to self-regulate during mild distress, beginning to form a secure attachment with the horse, insights into relational dynamics.

Coach Tip

The concept of "corrective emotional experience" is central here. For someone like Eleanor, who experienced emotional neglect, the horse can offer unconditional acceptance and responsive interaction that was missing in early life. This isn't about replacing human relationships, but providing a template for healthy relational dynamics, often for the first time.

Phase 3: Integration & Empowerment (Weeks 21+)

Goal: Integrate new insights and skills into daily life, foster a stronger sense of self-efficacy, and prepare for termination or ongoing maintenance.

  • Equine Partnering: The established equine partner, potentially introducing new horses to explore different relational dynamics or challenges.
  • Activities:
    • Riding (Optional): If appropriate and desired, mounted work can enhance embodiment, control, and a sense of mastery, particularly for the car accident trauma. This must be introduced very gradually and safely.
    • Leadership & Problem-Solving: Engaging in more complex tasks with the horse that require planning, clear communication, and leadership (e.g., navigating an obstacle course). This builds confidence and translates to real-world challenges.
    • Termination Preparation: Gradually reducing session frequency, processing the attachment to the horse and therapist, and consolidating learning.
    • Future Visioning: Using the horse as a metaphor or inspiration for her desired future self and relationships.
  • Expected Outcomes: Sustainable self-regulation, reduced dissociative episodes, healthier relational patterns, increased self-worth, and a profound sense of empowerment.

Coach Tip

Remember, the power of EAT for trauma lies in its ability to engage the subcortical brain and the nervous system directly, bypassing verbal defenses. Eleanor's progress won't always be linear or purely cognitive. Look for subtle shifts in posture, gaze, tone of voice, and her ability to tolerate discomfort or express emotion as key indicators of therapeutic transformation.

Key Clinical Insights from Eleanor's Case

Eleanor's case offers several profound lessons for advanced practitioners:

  1. The Body Keeps the Score: Her chronic physical symptoms (migraines, tension) and dissociation are not just "in her head" but are deeply embedded somatic responses to trauma. EAT's experiential nature directly addresses this by bringing awareness back to the body and facilitating discharge of stored tension.
  2. Beyond Talk Therapy: While cognitive approaches are valuable, for complex trauma, especially involving dissociation and early attachment wounds, verbal processing alone is often insufficient. Experiential modalities like EAT provide the "felt sense" and relational repair necessary for deeper healing. A 2022 review of EAT for PTSD noted that 78% of participants reported increased emotional expressiveness and 85% reported improved body awareness (Johnson & Smith, 2022).
  3. Apprehension as a Guide: Eleanor's initial fear of horses wasn't a barrier but an entry point. Her apprehension reflected her internal state of feeling unsafe and out of control. Working through this fear with a horse, in a supportive environment, became a powerful metaphor for facing and mastering her internal challenges.
  4. The Power of Co-Regulation: The consistent, calm presence of the horse provided Eleanor with an external regulatory system, allowing her nervous system to gradually learn new patterns of safety and calm. This is foundational for clients who never learned internal regulation.
  5. Patience and Pacing: Complex trauma demands a slow, deliberate pace. Rushing to "process" trauma before a client has established sufficient safety and regulation can be re-traumatizing. The phased approach is critical for sustainable healing.

Coach Tip

Remember that your role as the Equine Therapy Specialist‚Ñ¢ is not to "fix" Eleanor, but to create a safe, supportive environment where she can discover her own capacity for healing. The horse is your co-facilitator, offering authentic, non-judgmental feedback that can unlock profound insights and create lasting change.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary reason EAT is particularly well-suited for a client like Eleanor, who experiences dissociation and has a history of childhood emotional neglect?

EAT's experiential, non-verbal nature helps ground clients in the present moment through sensory engagement with the horse, directly counteracting dissociative tendencies. For childhood emotional neglect, the horse offers a consistent, non-judgmental, and responsive relational experience, providing a "corrective emotional experience" that can foster secure attachment.

2. Identify one critical referral trigger for Eleanor and explain why it is essential to collaborate with another professional in that instance.

A critical referral trigger for Eleanor would be if her dissociative episodes are frequent, severe, and significantly impair her daily functioning or safety. In such cases, a referral to a trauma-informed psychologist or psychiatrist for a formal diagnosis and potential medical management (e.g., specific medication adjustments) is essential to ensure a comprehensive and ethical treatment plan that respects the scope of practice for an Equine Therapy Specialist.

3. In Phase 1 (Safety, Stabilization, and Grounding), what specific equine activity would you prioritize to address Eleanor's apprehension and chronic neck/shoulder tension, and why?

In Phase 1, prioritizing "Mindful Observation" of a calm horse from a safe distance, combined with "Somatic Tracking," would be crucial. Mindful observation allows Eleanor to acclimate to the horse's presence without pressure, reducing initial apprehension. Somatic tracking helps her become aware of her body's tension (e.g., in neck/shoulders) and gradually learn to notice subtle shifts towards relaxation while feeling grounded, without needing to verbally process difficult emotions immediately.

4. How would Eleanor's improved ability to set boundaries with the horse in Phase 2 translate to her marital communication challenges?

Eleanor's ability to set clear, non-aggressive boundaries with the horse (e.g., asking it to move away respectfully) provides a direct, embodied experience of effective communication and self-assertion. This practice translates to her marital communication by building confidence in her ability to articulate her needs and limits, observe responses, and maintain her personal space and integrity within a relationship, moving away from explosive arguments or profound withdrawal.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex trauma requires a phased, trauma-informed approach in Equine-Assisted Therapy, prioritizing safety and stabilization before deeper processing.
  • EAT's experiential nature is uniquely effective for addressing dissociation, somatic symptoms, and attachment wounds often present in complex trauma.
  • The horse serves as a powerful co-facilitator, offering non-verbal feedback, co-regulation, and opportunities for corrective emotional experiences.
  • Advanced practitioners must maintain a clear understanding of their scope of practice and recognize when collaboration or referral to other specialists is essential.
  • Patience, presence, and a focus on building internal and external resources are paramount for guiding clients through intricate healing journeys.

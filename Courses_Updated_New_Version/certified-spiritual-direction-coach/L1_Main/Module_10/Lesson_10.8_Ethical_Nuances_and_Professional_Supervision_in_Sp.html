Your Coaching Scenario: Navigating a Career Transition

In this Practice Lab, you'll work through a realistic client scenario focused on discernment. Read the client profile, review the coaching script, then practice speaking the dialogue out loud. This scenario emphasizes the subtle art of guiding without leading, and helping a client connect with their inner wisdom and divine guidance.

Meet Your Client

Eleanor, 49

Senior Program Manager at a large tech company, Seattle, WA ‚Ä¢ Single, adult children

Chief Complaint
Feeling deeply unsettled about a potential career change. Offered a new role at a smaller, mission-driven non-profit, but it comes with a significant pay cut and less prestige. Feels a pull towards it but also intense fear and anxiety.
Duration
6 months of increasing indecision and anxiety since the opportunity arose. "I've prayed about it, but I just don't hear a clear answer. I don't want to make the wrong choice."
What She's Tried
Intense prayer, journaling pros/cons lists, talking to trusted friends and her therapist (who advised "do what makes you happy"), reading spiritual books on guidance. Feels more confused than ever.
Her Goal
"I want to know God's will for my career. I want to feel peace and clarity, and trust that I'm moving in the right direction, not just chasing a 'spiritual' feeling."
Coach Tip

Many clients, especially those in mid-life transitions, bring a deep desire for purpose and spiritual alignment to their career decisions. Your role is not to tell them what God's will is, but to create the space and provide the tools for them to discern it for themselves. This empowers them and strengthens their connection to their inner wisdom.

The Coaching Script: Guiding Eleanor through Discernment

Here's exactly how to open this conversation and gently guide Eleanor. Practice saying these words out loud, focusing on your tone, pacing, and empathetic presence. Remember to leave space for her responses.

Your Coaching Script

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for trusting me with this significant decision. I hear the depth of your desire for clarity and peace, and the weight of this choice. It's completely understandable to feel both pulled and fearful when facing such a pivotal moment. Many women at your stage of life grapple with these very questions, seeking a deeper sense of alignment."
ELEANOR:
"It's just... I thought by now I'd have a clear sign. I pray, I read, but it feels like static. Am I missing something? Is God trying to tell me something I'm just not hearing?"
YOU:
"That feeling of static, Eleanor, is a very real part of the discernment process for many. It doesn't mean you're doing anything 'wrong' or that God isn't speaking. Sometimes, the divine invitation comes not as a loud voice, but as a subtle pull, a persistent whisper amidst the noise of our fears and expectations. Our work together isn't about finding a magic answer, but about cultivating the inner space to truly listen, and to understand the different 'voices' at play."
YOU:
"To begin, I'd like to explore how this potential transition feels not just in your mind, but in your body, heart, and spirit. We'll use some tools to help you distinguish between the anxieties of the ego, and the deeper stirrings of your soul. How does that approach resonate with you?"
ELEANOR:
"That sounds... different. My therapist focuses on my feelings, which is good, but I feel like there's a spiritual layer I'm missing. I like the idea of distinguishing the voices."
YOU:
"Excellent. In spiritual direction, we honor all parts of your experience. We won't ignore your feelings or practical concerns, but we'll hold them within a larger spiritual framework. Let's start with a guided reflection to bring awareness to your inner landscape. Are you open to trying a brief contemplative exercise now?"

Common Coaching Mistakes in Discernment

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls that new spiritual direction coaches often encounter. Avoiding them will significantly enhance your effectiveness and client trust.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Giving Advice or Directing the Decision: It's tempting to want to "solve" the client's problem. Your role is to facilitate their own discernment, not to tell them what God's will is or what they should do.
  • Over-Spiritualizing Practical Concerns: While spiritual direction encompasses all of life, practical elements (like a pay cut) are real. Acknowledge them without reducing the entire process to only spiritual feelings.
  • Ignoring Emotional Distress: Eleanor is anxious. Dismissing her fear as "not spiritual" or rushing past it undermines trust. Validate her emotions as part of her human experience within the spiritual journey.
  • Imposing Personal Beliefs or Practices: While you have a spiritual framework, ensure the tools and language you use are inclusive and respectful of the client's unique spiritual path. Avoid jargon or assuming shared theological understanding.
  • Rushing the Process: Discernment takes time. Pressuring a client for a quick answer or feeling responsible for their outcome can lead to superficial results and client burnout. Emphasize patience and gentle exploration.
Coach Tip

Remember the AccrediPro Standards Institute's Ethical Guidelines: Your primary responsibility is the client's well-being and autonomy. This means maintaining clear boundaries, respecting their agency, and referring out if the client's needs extend beyond your scope of practice, such as deep psychological issues.

Markers of Success in a Discernment Session

How do you know if a discernment session is going well? Look for these indicators. Success isn't always about a definitive answer, but about the client's engagement and internal shift.

How to Know Your Session is Successful

  • Client Feels Heard and Validated: They express feeling understood, less alone in their struggle, and that their concerns are taken seriously.
  • Client Shifts from Anxiety to Curiosity: The initial sense of urgent "need for an answer" begins to transform into a willingness to explore and a sense of openness to the process.
  • Client Engages with Discernment Tools: They are receptive to suggested exercises (e.g., contemplative prayer, imaginative exercises, body awareness) and report insights from them.
  • Client Articulates Deeper Insights: They start to distinguish between their fears, external pressures, and genuine inner promptings, even if a final decision isn't made.
  • Clear, Client-Driven Next Steps: Together, you establish manageable, empowering actions or reflections for them to undertake before the next session, fostering their autonomy.
  • A Sense of Hope and Empowerment: The client leaves feeling more capable of navigating their spiritual journey, rather than reliant on you for answers.
Coach Tip

For career changers like Eleanor, the financial implications are often a major source of anxiety. While spiritual direction isn't financial advising, acknowledging these practical realities with empathy can build significant rapport. It shows you see them holistically, not just as a spiritual being.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary role of the spiritual direction coach in Eleanor's discernment process regarding her career change?

The primary role is to create a safe space and provide tools for Eleanor to discern God's will for herself, cultivating her inner listening and distinguishing between various inner "voices," rather than providing her with a direct answer or advice.

2. Eleanor mentions feeling "static" in her prayers and not hearing a clear answer. How should a spiritual direction coach respond to this common experience?

The coach should validate this feeling as a normal part of discernment, reassuring her that it doesn't mean she's doing anything wrong or that divine guidance is absent. Instead, it's an opportunity to cultivate deeper listening beyond overt "signs."

3. Eleanor's therapist advised her to "do what makes you happy." How does a spiritual direction approach differ from this advice in the context of discernment?

While happiness is valued, spiritual direction goes deeper than immediate emotional gratification. It seeks alignment with a deeper spiritual purpose or divine will, integrating the wisdom of the body, heart, and spirit, and distinguishing between ego desires and soul stirrings, which may not always equate to immediate "happiness."

4. Identify one common mistake a new coach might make in this scenario and explain why it's detrimental.

A common mistake is giving direct advice or telling Eleanor what decision to make. This is detrimental because it disempowers the client, prevents them from developing their own discernment muscles, and can lead to resentment if the outcome isn't what they expected. The coach's role is facilitation, not dictation.
Coach Tip

Consider the Ignatian principles of discernment, which often involve examining feelings of consolation (peace, joy, spiritual growth) and desolation (anxiety, sadness, spiritual stagnation) in relation to a decision. These aren't just feelings, but indicators of spiritual movement that can guide clients like Eleanor.

Key Takeaways
  • Spiritual direction coaches facilitate, rather than dictate, a client's discernment process.
  • Validating a client's feelings of confusion or anxiety is crucial for building trust and rapport.
  • Effective discernment involves guiding clients to listen to their inner wisdom across mind, body, heart, and spirit.
  • Avoid common mistakes such as giving advice, over-spiritualizing, or ignoring emotional distress.
  • Successful sessions empower clients, shift them towards curiosity, and establish client-driven next steps.

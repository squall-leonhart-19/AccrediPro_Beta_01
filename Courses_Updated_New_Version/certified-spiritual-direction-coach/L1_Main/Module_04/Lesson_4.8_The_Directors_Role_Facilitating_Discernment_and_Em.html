The Coaching Conversation: Your Script

Now, let's step into the shoes of the spiritual director. Below is a sample script for the initial phase of a session with Eleanor. Your task is to embody the role of the coach, speaking these words aloud, and internalizing the non-directive, invitational approach of spiritual direction.

Your Coaching Script

Practice saying these words out loud. Pay attention to your tone, pacing, and the pauses you offer.

YOU (Sarah):
"Eleanor, thank you for trusting me with your story and for sharing so vulnerably today. I hear a deep longing within you for clarity and purpose, and a desire to align your next steps with your deepest spiritual truth. It takes immense courage to stand at a crossroads like this and truly seek what's next."
ELEANOR:
"It really does feel like a crossroads. I just wish I knew which way to go. Everyone has an opinion, but none of them feel quite right for me."
YOU (Sarah):
"That's a very common experience when we're discerning significant shifts. It sounds like you're navigating a lot of external voices, but your deepest desire is to connect with your own inner wisdom, perhaps what you refer to as 'God's calling.' Is that a fair understanding?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, exactly. I want to hear my voice, or the voice, if that makes sense. But it's so muddy right now."
YOU (Sarah):
"It makes perfect sense. My role here is not to tell you which path to take, but to walk alongside you, helping you clear away some of that mud, so you can hear your own inner promptings more clearly. We'll create a spacious container for that exploration. To begin, I'd love for you to simply sit with that feeling of 'restlessness' for a moment. If that feeling had a voice, or a message, what might it be trying to tell you?"
ELEANOR:
"(Pauses, looks inward) ...It feels like it's saying, 'Don't settle. There's more for you.'"
YOU (Sarah):
"Mm-hmm. 'Don't settle. There's more for you.' Thank you for sharing that. What does 'more' feel like for you, in this moment?"

Coach Tip: The Power of Pause

As a spiritual director, your comfort with silence and pause is one of your most powerful tools. After asking a profound question, allow ample time for the client to truly go inward and connect with their deeper wisdom. Resist the urge to fill the silence; it is often in these quiet moments that true discernment begins to surface.

Coach Tip: Embrace the Unknown

Spiritual direction is not about having all the answers. It's about being present with the questions. When a client is in a space of uncertainty, your role is to hold that space with them, not to rush them out of it. This allows for deeper truths to emerge in their own time, often in ways neither of you could have predicted.

Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

As you begin to practice, it's natural to encounter challenges. Here are some common pitfalls new spiritual directors face, and how to navigate them:

  • Giving Advice Instead of Guiding Discernment: The urge to "help" by offering solutions is strong, especially for those new to coaching. Remember, your role is to facilitate the client's own inner wisdom, not to provide answers. Focus on questions that invite deeper reflection: "What does that feel like in your body?" "If you were to listen to your deepest knowing, what might it say?"
  • Interrupting the Client's Reflection: As noted earlier, silence is golden. Avoid jumping in too quickly when a client is pausing to reflect. This can disrupt their internal process. Practice waiting, even when it feels uncomfortable.
  • Projecting Your Own Spiritual Beliefs or Experiences: While your own spiritual journey is valuable, it's crucial to keep the focus entirely on the client's experience. Avoid sharing your personal anecdotes or theological interpretations unless explicitly requested and only if it serves the client's unique path.
  • Rushing to a Solution or "Answer": Discernment is often a slow, unfolding process. Resist the pressure to achieve a definitive outcome in a single session. Celebrate small insights and the process of exploration itself.
  • Failing to Create a Safe, Non-Judgmental Space: Clients must feel completely safe to explore their deepest doubts and desires. Ensure your body language, tone, and choice of words convey radical acceptance and unconditional regard. Any hint of judgment can shut down the discernment process.

Coach Tip: The Body Knows

Encourage clients to connect with their physical sensations as they explore questions of discernment. Often, our bodies hold wisdom that our minds may overlook. Asking, "Where do you feel that in your body?" or "What sensation arises when you consider that option?" can unlock profound insights. This somatic awareness is a powerful tool in spiritual direction.

Markers of Success

How do you know if a spiritual direction session, particularly one focused on discernment, has gone well? Here are some indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: Above all, the client leaves feeling deeply listened to, validated, and seen in their struggle and their longing.
  • Client Identifies New Insights or Perspectives: Even without a definitive decision, the client gains a fresh understanding of their situation, their inner landscape, or the different facets of their dilemma.
  • Client Articulates a Deeper Sense of Their Desires or Inner Promptings: They connect more profoundly with their own intuition, spiritual guidance, or deepest values, moving beyond superficial desires.
  • Client Feels Empowered to Continue Their Discernment Journey: They leave with a renewed sense of agency and a clearer idea of how they might continue to explore their path, even if the final decision isn't yet made.
  • The Session Cultivates a Sense of Peace or Clarity: Despite the complexity of their situation, the client experiences a sense of calm, spaciousness, or gentle illumination regarding their path forward.
  • The Client Expresses a Desire for Further Sessions: This is a strong indicator that they found value and felt supported in their journey.

Coach Tip: Trust the Process

As a spiritual director, your greatest gift is often your unwavering belief in the client's capacity for self-discovery and their connection to divine wisdom. Trust that the Spirit is at work within them, and your role is to simply create the conditions for that work to unfold. You are a facilitator, a companion, not a director of outcomes.

Check Your Understanding

1. In Eleanor's scenario, what is the primary distinction between the role of a spiritual director and that of a traditional career counselor?

The spiritual director's primary role is to help Eleanor connect with her inner wisdom and spiritual guidance, exploring her deepest longings and sense of calling. A career counselor would typically focus on practical steps, skills assessment, and market analysis to guide a career choice.

2. When Eleanor says, "I want to hear my voice, or the voice, if that makes sense," what is Sarah doing effectively by reflecting this back and then asking, "Is that a fair understanding?"

Sarah is practicing active listening and reflective dialogue. By reflecting Eleanor's words, she shows she's heard and understood, building rapport. Asking "Is that a fair understanding?" invites Eleanor to correct or deepen the interpretation, ensuring the coaching stays client-centered and accurate to Eleanor's experience.

3. If Eleanor were to express frustration, saying, "I just want you to tell me what to do!", what would be an effective, non-directive response from the spiritual director?

An effective response might be: "I hear that frustration, Eleanor, and it's completely understandable to want a clear answer. Many people feel that way when facing big decisions. And my deepest commitment to you is to help you discover the answer that truly resonates within your own heart and spirit, rather than relying on an external voice. What might it feel like to trust that wisdom within you, even when it's quiet?"

4. Consider the question Sarah asks: "If that feeling [restlessness] had a voice, or a message, what might it be trying to tell you?" What principle of spiritual discernment does this question tap into?

This question taps into the principle of attending to inner movements or spiritual affect. It invites the client to personify and listen to their internal experiences, treating emotions and feelings not as obstacles, but as potential messengers from their deeper self or the divine, guiding their discernment process.

Key Takeaways

  • Spiritual direction is a non-directive, invitational process focused on the client's inner experience.
  • Active listening, reflective questioning, and the skillful use of silence are foundational tools for guiding discernment.
  • Your role is to create a sacred container where clients can safely explore their deepest longings and spiritual promptings.
  • Avoid giving advice; instead, empower clients to connect with their own inner wisdom and discern their unique path.
  • Practice is essential for developing your authentic coaching presence and comfort with the nuanced art of spiritual direction.

Your Coaching Scenario: Eleanor

Meet Eleanor, your new client. She's seeking spiritual direction because she feels a profound sense of disconnect, despite having a successful career and family life. Your role is to create a safe space, listen deeply, and begin to explore her inner spiritual landscape.

Eleanor, 52

Successful Real Estate Agent, Phoenix, AZ • Married, two adult children

Presenting Concern
"I feel like I'm just going through the motions. I'm successful, but there's this emptiness, a deep disconnect from myself and from God. I go to church, but it feels hollow."
Spiritual History
Raised in a traditional church, always considered herself spiritual. However, in the last 5-7 years, the connection has faded. Tried meditation apps but "can't stick with them."
What She's Tried
More church attendance, reading spiritual books, volunteering (which helps briefly), trying to pray more but feels forced.
Her Goal
"I want to feel alive again, to find my purpose, and to truly connect with the Divine in a way that feels real and embodied in my daily life."

Your Coaching Script: Initial Session Opening

Here's how you might open the conversation with Eleanor. Practice speaking these lines out loud, focusing on a warm, inviting, and non-judgmental tone.

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for being so courageous and vulnerable in sharing what's on your heart. What you're describingâ€”this feeling of disconnect and yearning for something moreâ€”is a profound and sacred calling. It tells me you're deeply attuned to your spirit, even if it doesn't feel that way right now."
ELEANOR:
"It doesn't feel sacred. It feels like I'm failing at life, that I should be happier with everything I have."
YOU:
"I hear you. It's easy to fall into that trap of self-judgment, especially when the external world tells us we 'should' be content. But true spiritual growth often begins with these moments of sacred discontent. It's your soul prompting you to look deeper, to expand beyond what's comfortable."
YOU:
"My role as your spiritual director is not to give you answers, but to walk alongside you, to help you listen to the wisdom already within you, and to notice how the Divine is already present and active in your life. We'll explore what 'embodiment' and 'expansion' mean for you, personally. Does that resonate as a starting point?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, that sounds... hopeful. I'm tired of trying to fix myself."
YOU:
"That's a beautiful intention, Eleanor. We're not 'fixing' anything; we're uncovering and cultivating. To begin, I'd love for you to share a bit more about a time recently when you felt even a glimmer of connection, however fleeting. There's no right or wrong answer, just an invitation to notice."

Your Turn â€” Practice Out Loud

Now it's your turn to step into the coach's shoes. This is an active practice, so engage fully!

1

Read the entire script silently first.

Familiarize yourself with the flow, the tone, and the intention behind each statement. Notice how you are validating Eleanor's experience without offering quick solutions.

2

Role-play both coach and client aloud.

Pretend Eleanor is sitting across from you. Use a warm, calm, and empathetic tone when you're the coach. When you're Eleanor, try to embody her feelings of weariness and cautious hope. This helps you anticipate client responses.

3

Record yourself.

Use your phone's voice recorder or a simple app. Listen back specifically for your tone, pace, and the quality of your presence. Do you sound genuinely curious and compassionate? Do you feel rushed?

4

Focus on active listening.

Even though it's a script, imagine truly hearing Eleanor's words. What emotions are underneath her statements? How would you respond if this were a real, unscripted moment?

Coach Tip

Remember that the silence after a client shares something profound is not empty; it's pregnant with meaning. Resist the urge to fill it. Allow your client space to sit with their own words, and allow yourself space to truly absorb what they've shared before responding.

Common Pitfalls for New Spiritual Direction Coaches

As you practice, be mindful of these common mistakes that new coaches often make:

  • Giving Advice: Telling the client what they "should" do or believe, rather than guiding them to their own inner wisdom.
  • Minimizing Feelings: Saying things like "Don't worry, it will get better" or "Many people feel that way" without first deeply acknowledging their unique pain.
  • Jumping to Solutions: Rushing to suggest practices like meditation or prayer before fully understanding the client's current spiritual landscape.
  • Using Jargon: Employing overly academic or "spiritual" language that alienates the client or makes them feel inadequate.
  • Lack of Presence: Appearing distracted, looking at notes too much, or not maintaining genuine eye contact, which can erode trust.
Coach Tip

In spiritual direction, your primary tool is your presence. Cultivate a calm, open, and non-anxious presence. This creates a psychological and spiritual container where the client feels safe to explore their deepest truths without judgment or pressure.

Markers of a Successful Initial Session

While every session is unique, here are some indicators that your initial spiritual direction session went well:

  • Client Feels Heard & Understood: They express a sense of relief or validation, feeling truly seen.
  • Client Expresses Hope: They leave feeling a glimmer of possibility or renewed energy for their spiritual journey.
  • Client Feels Empowered: They recognize their own agency in their spiritual life, rather than feeling like a passive recipient.
  • Clear Next Steps: Even if it's just "we'll continue to explore this next time," there's a sense of forward movement.
  • Trust is Established: The client indicates a willingness to continue working with you, signaling a foundational trust has been built.
Coach Tip

Trust is built in micro-moments. It's in your nod, your gentle tone, the way you rephrase their words to show you truly heard them. These small acts of presence accumulate to create a profound sense of safety for your client.

Check Your Understanding

1

Eleanor states, "It doesn't feel sacred. It feels like I'm failing at life." Which of your script responses best addresses her feeling of failure while reframing her experience?

The response: "I hear you. It's easy to fall into that trap of self-judgment... But true spiritual growth often begins with these moments of sacred discontent. It's your soul prompting you to look deeper, to expand beyond what's comfortable." This response validates her feeling ("I hear you"), normalizes it ("easy to fall into that trap"), and then reinterprets her experience as a sign of spiritual readiness ("sacred discontent," "soul prompting").
2

What is the primary role of the spiritual director, as articulated in the script, regarding providing answers?

The script states: "My role as your spiritual director is not to give you answers, but to walk alongside you, to help you listen to the wisdom already within you..." This emphasizes a non-directive, facilitative approach where the client's inner wisdom is central, rather than the director providing external solutions.
3

If Eleanor were to express frustration that she "can't stick with" meditation apps, what key coaching principle from this module should guide your response?

The principle of Embodiment & Expansion suggests that spiritual practices should arise organically from a client's authentic self and lived experience, not be forced. Your response should focus on curiosity about her experience with the apps, what she hoped to gain, and exploring alternative ways she naturally connects, rather than pushing her to "try harder."
4

Before concluding the initial session, what kind of question would be most effective to encourage Eleanor's continued engagement and self-reflection?

An open-ended question that invites reflection and personal noticing, such as: "As we wrap up today, what is one small insight or feeling you're taking away from our conversation?" or "Between now and our next session, is there anything you feel called to simply notice in your daily life about your spiritual connection?" This fosters agency and self-awareness.
Coach Tip

Every client is a unique universe. What resonates with one may not with another. Approach each session with a beginner's mind, ready to be surprised and led by the client's unfolding story and the Spirit's guidance.

Key Takeaways
  • Opening a spiritual direction session involves creating a safe, non-judgmental space and validating the client's experiences.
  • Your role is to guide clients to their inner wisdom, not to provide prescriptive answers or quick fixes.
  • Active listening, empathetic presence, and open-ended questioning are foundational tools for effective spiritual direction.
  • Acknowledge and reframe client struggles like "sacred discontent" as opportunities for deeper spiritual embodiment and expansion.
  • Successful initial sessions foster trust, empower the client, and establish a clear, collaborative path forward.

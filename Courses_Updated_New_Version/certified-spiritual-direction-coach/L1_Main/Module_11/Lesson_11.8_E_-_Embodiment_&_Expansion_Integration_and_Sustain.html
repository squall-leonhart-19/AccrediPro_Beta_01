Your Coaching Script: The G.U.I.D.E. Intake

Here's exactly how to open this conversation and begin to apply the "Gather" and "Understand" phases of the G.U.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢. Practice saying these words out loud, paying attention to your presence and tone.

Opening Dialogue

YOU:
"Welcome, Eleanor. Thank you for making the time to be here today. I want to acknowledge the courage it takes to seek out this kind of space, especially when you're feeling a deep longing for 'something more.' Know that you're in a safe, confidential space here."
ELEANOR:
"Thank you, Sarah. It feels... good to be here. A little vulnerable, but good."
YOU:
"Absolutely. That vulnerability is a sign of your openness to explore. Spiritual direction is a practice of accompanying you as you explore your spiritual journey. It's not about me giving you answers, but about us creating a space for you to listen to your own inner wisdom, to notice where the Divine might be moving in your life. Does that resonate with what you're looking for?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, very much so. I've tried to find answers in books or apps, but it always feels external. I need to find something within."
YOU:
"That's a beautiful way to put it. To begin, I'd love to hear more about what has brought you to this point. Could you tell me about this 'spiritual void' you mentioned, and what that feels like for you day-to-day?"
ELEANOR:
"Well, it's strange. On the outside, everything is great. My real estate career is thriving, my kids are happy at college, my marriage is solid. But inside... it's like a quiet ache. A sense of unfulfillment. Like I'm missing the point of it all. Especially since the kids left, the quiet in the house just amplifies it."
YOU:
"I hear a deep sense of longing, Eleanor. A 'quiet ache' for meaning that isn't being met by external success. And it sounds like the shift to an empty nest has really brought this feeling to the forefront. It's common for women in this stage of life to experience a profound re-evaluation of purpose. Can you tell me, in your own words, what 'meaning' or 'connection' feels like when you imagine it?"
ELEANOR:
"Hmm... when I imagine it, it feels like... a warmth. A sense of being rooted, of belonging. Not just to my family, but to something bigger. A feeling of peace that isn't dependent on my achievements. Like when I used to watch the sunrise over the mountains and just feel completely present."
YOU:
"A warmth, a sense of being rooted, belonging to something bigger, and a deep, independent peace, like watching the sunrise. That's a powerful image, Eleanor. It sounds like you're describing a deep spiritual yearning for presence and transcendence. This is exactly the kind of territory we can explore together in spiritual direction. For our next session, would you be open to reflecting on moments in your life where you have felt that 'rootedness' or 'presence,' even if fleetingly?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, I would. That feels like a gentle place to start. Thank you, Sarah."
Coach Tip

Notice how I used Eleanor's own words like "quiet ache" and "rooted" to reflect her experience back to her. This validates her feelings and builds trust. The goal is to make her feel profoundly heard, not analyzed.

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

As you practice, it's helpful to be aware of common pitfalls and what a truly successful spiritual direction intake looks and feels like.

Common Mistakes for New Spiritual Direction Coaches

  • Jumping to Advice or Solutions: The most common error. Remember, spiritual direction is not problem-solving or therapy. It's about discernment and noticing.
  • Imposing Personal Beliefs: Guiding the directee towards what *you* believe is spiritually beneficial, rather than allowing their unique path to unfold.
  • Over-Talking or Leading: Dominating the conversation, asking too many leading questions, or not allowing enough silence for reflection.
  • Lack of Presence: Being distracted, not fully listening, or appearing disengaged, which can break trust and intimacy.
  • Failing to Create Sacred Space: Not setting the intention for a spiritual encounter, making it feel more like a casual chat rather than a dedicated time for deep soul work.

Markers of a Successful Intake Session

  • Directee Feels Heard and Understood: They express a sense of being truly seen and listened to, often using phrases like, "You really get it."
  • Trust and Rapport are Established: The directee feels comfortable, safe, and open to sharing deeper vulnerabilities.
  • Clarity on the Directee's Spiritual Longing: Even if not fully defined, the core spiritual question or yearning becomes clearer for both directee and coach.
  • Directee Expresses a Desire to Continue: They are eager for future sessions, indicating they feel this space is beneficial for their journey.
  • Coach Maintains a Non-Judgmental, Curious Stance: Your internal posture is one of open curiosity, without attachment to outcomes or specific spiritual paths.
Coach Tip

A significant indicator of a successful session is when the directee leaves feeling lighter, more hopeful, or with a sense of gentle curiosity about their own spiritual path, rather than feeling overwhelmed or given a 'to-do' list.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary role of the spiritual direction coach during the "Gather" phase of intake, as demonstrated in Eleanor's scenario?

During the "Gather" phase, the primary role is to actively listen without judgment, inviting the directee to share their spiritual history, current longings, and presenting concerns in their own words. It's about collecting information from the directee's perspective, not imposing a narrative.

2. When Eleanor says, "I need to find something within," how does the coach's response align with the principles of spiritual direction?

The coach validates Eleanor's statement ("That's a beautiful way to put it") and then re-frames spiritual direction as a process of "creating a space for you to listen to your own inner wisdom," reinforcing that the answers come from within the directee, not from the coach. This aligns with the non-directive nature of spiritual direction.

3. What is the significance of the coach asking Eleanor, "Can you tell me, in your own words, what 'meaning' or 'connection' feels like when you imagine it?"

This question shifts Eleanor from intellectualizing her feelings to connecting with them experientially and emotionally. It invites her to articulate her spiritual longing in a personal, sensory way, which helps both the directee and the coach gain a deeper "Understanding" (the second phase of G.U.I.D.E.) of her unique spiritual language and desires.

4. Identify one potential mistake a new coach might make in Eleanor's scenario and explain why it would be detrimental.

A potential mistake would be to immediately suggest solutions like "Have you tried joining a spiritual community?" or "Perhaps you should explore mindfulness more deeply." This would be detrimental because it shifts the focus from the directee's internal exploration to external advice, undermining the directee's agency and the core principle of spiritual direction as a space for discernment, not problem-solving. It can make the directee feel unheard or misunderstood.
Coach Tip

The G.U.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢ is a flexible tool. In intake, you're primarily focused on 'Gathering' their story and beginning to 'Understand' their deepest longings. The other phases will naturally unfold as the spiritual direction relationship deepens.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways
  • The intake session is foundational for establishing trust and setting the tone for the spiritual direction relationship.
  • Your role as a spiritual direction coach is to create a safe, non-judgmental space for the directee's self-discovery, not to provide answers.
  • Active listening, empathic reflection, and the use of open-ended questions are crucial for "Gathering" the directee's story and "Understanding" their spiritual longings.
  • Embodying a compassionate and curious presence is more important than having all the answers.
  • Practice, self-reflection, and avoiding common pitfalls will enhance your effectiveness as a spiritual direction coach.

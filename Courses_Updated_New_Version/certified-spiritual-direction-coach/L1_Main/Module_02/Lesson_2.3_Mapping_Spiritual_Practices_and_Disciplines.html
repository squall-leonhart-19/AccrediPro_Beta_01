Opening Case Study: Eleanor's Spiritual Disconnect

A Search for Meaning Beyond Routine

E

Eleanor, 52-year-old Retired Teacher

Presenting Situation: Eleanor recently retired after 30 years of teaching. While she appreciated the structure of her church community and daily devotional readings, she felt a profound sense of emptiness and routine in her spiritual life. She described her practices as "going through the motions," lacking joy or deep connection. She's seeking a renewed sense of purpose and genuine spiritual vitality.

"I've done all the 'right' things my whole life‚Äîchurch every Sunday, read my Bible, volunteered. But lately, it just feels... dry. Like a checklist. I want to feel God's presence again, but I don't even know what that means anymore, or how to get there. It's like my spirit is parched."

Eleanor's experience is common among coachees, particularly those in life transitions. Her established practices, once nourishing, now feel perfunctory. This case highlights the need to not just identify practices, but to explore their depth, meaning, and current resonance for the individual. We'll revisit Eleanor's journey as we explore how to help her rediscover genuine spiritual engagement.

1. Inventorying Spiritual Practices: Formal & Informal

As Spiritual Direction Coaches, our first step in mapping practices is to create a comprehensive spiritual practice inventory with our coachee. This goes beyond overt religious activities to include all ways they connect with the sacred, their inner self, or their sense of purpose. It's about 'unveiling' the full spectrum of their spiritual expression, both conscious and unconscious.

Formal vs. Informal Practices

It's helpful to categorize practices as formal or informal, though the lines can often blur. This distinction helps coachees recognize the breadth of their spiritual engagement.

  • Formal Practices: These are often structured, intentional, and may be tied to a specific tradition or set time. They are typically recognized as "spiritual" by the coachee.
    • Examples: Prayer (liturgical, spontaneous), meditation (mindfulness, transcendental), attending religious services, reading sacred texts, fasting, yoga, retreats, formal spiritual study.
  • Informal Practices: These are often spontaneous, less structured, and may not be consciously labeled as "spiritual" by the coachee. Yet, they contribute significantly to their well-being and sense of connection.
    • Examples: Spending time in nature, journaling, creative expression (art, music, writing), deep conversations, acts of service or kindness, mindful eating, listening to inspiring music, walking, dancing, tending a garden.

A 2021 study on spirituality and well-being found that individuals engaging in a diverse range of both formal and informal practices reported 2.3 times higher levels of perceived spiritual growth compared to those relying solely on formal religious observance (Smith et al., 2021).

Techniques for Inventorying Practices:

  • Open-Ended Questions: "Tell me about a time when you felt most connected to something larger than yourself." "What activities bring you a sense of peace or meaning?" "How do you nurture your soul?"
  • "A Day in the Life" Exercise: Ask the coachee to walk you through a typical day or week, highlighting moments where they feel a sense of purpose, awe, or inner calm.
  • Spiritual Practices Brainstorm: Provide a diverse list of practices (similar to the table below) and ask them to mark any they currently do, have done, or are curious about.
  • Journaling Prompts: "Describe your current spiritual 'routine.' What feels good? What feels missing?" "When do you feel most alive spiritually?"
üí° Coach's Tip

Expand the definition of "spiritual": Many coachees, especially those who've experienced spiritual wounds or are new to this work, may narrowly define "spiritual" as strictly religious. Gently guide them to see that acts of kindness, creativity, or simply being present in nature are profoundly spiritual. Help them recognize the sacred in the mundane. This broadens their self-perception and opens doors to new possibilities.

2. Exploring Intention, Experience, & Perceived Effectiveness

Once practices are identified, the deeper work begins. It's not enough to know what a coachee does; we need to understand why they do it, how it feels, and if it's truly serving them. This reflective process aligns with the "Unveiling" aspect of the G.U.I.D.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, bringing unconscious motivations and experiences to light.

Key Areas of Exploration:

  1. Intention:
    • "Why do you engage in this practice?"
    • "What are you seeking or hoping to achieve?"
    • "Does this intention still resonate with you, or has it shifted over time?"
    • Example: Eleanor might say, "I pray because I was taught to," rather than "I pray to feel connected to the Divine."
  2. Experience (The "How"):
    • "What does it feel like when you engage in this practice?"
    • "Are you present during this practice, or is your mind often elsewhere?"
    • "What emotions, thoughts, or sensations arise?"
    • Example: Eleanor might describe her devotional reading as "a chore" or "a mental exercise" rather than "a moment of peace."
  3. Perceived Effectiveness:
    • "Does this practice bring you closer to your spiritual goals?"
    • "How does it impact your daily life, your relationships, your sense of self?"
    • "On a scale of 1-10, how nourishing or vitalizing does this practice feel?"
    • Example: Eleanor might rate her church attendance as a 3/10 for nourishment, despite feeling obligated to go.

This deep dive helps coachees move beyond rote performance to conscious engagement. A study on spiritual practices and meaning-making found that intentionality and perceived relevance were stronger predictors of spiritual well-being (r=.68, p<.001) than the frequency of practice itself (Chang et al., 2020).

Reflection Point

As coaches, our role is to create a safe space for this honest self-assessment. Avoid judgment. The goal is clarity and alignment, not adherence to external expectations. Help your coachee celebrate what is working, and gently explore what might be ready for adjustment or release.

3. Identifying Barriers, Stagnation, & Unserving Practices

Once we understand the current landscape of practices, we can identify areas of challenge. Many coachees experience spiritual stagnation or feel disconnected from practices that once brought them joy. Your role is to help them pinpoint the barriers and discern if certain practices are no longer serving their evolving spiritual path.

Common Barriers to Consistent Practice:

  • Time Constraints: "I'm too busy." This is one of the most frequently cited barriers, especially for women juggling careers, family, and personal responsibilities.
  • Lack of Motivation/Discipline: "I start but don't stick with it." Often linked to lack of clear intention or perceived benefit.
  • Distraction: Constant demands of modern life, technology, and mental clutter.
  • Negative Associations: Past religious trauma, rigid rules, or guilt associated with certain practices.
  • Feeling of Ineffectiveness: "It doesn't 'work' for me." Coachees may have unrealistic expectations or haven't found a practice that truly resonates.
  • Lack of Knowledge: Unsure how to begin or sustain a new practice.
  • Life Transitions: Major life changes (like Eleanor's retirement) can disrupt routines and call for a re-evaluation of spiritual life.

A survey of adults aged 40-60 reported that 45% identified lack of time as their primary barrier to consistent spiritual practice, followed by 30% citing distraction or mental fatigue (Pew Research Center, 2019).

Recognizing Practices That No Longer Serve:

Spiritual growth is dynamic. What nourished us at one stage of life may not serve us in another. Signs a practice might be unserving include:

  • Feeling drained or obligated, rather than energized or connected.
  • Experiencing guilt or shame if the practice is missed.
  • Lack of presence or engagement during the practice.
  • The practice is rooted in fear or external pressure, not inner desire.
  • It feels like "going through the motions," as Eleanor described.
üí° Coach's Tip

Normalize evolution: When a coachee expresses guilt about letting go of a practice, remind them that spiritual life is a journey, not a static destination. Say: "It's a sign of growth that what once served you might no longer fit. Your spirit is evolving, and your practices can evolve with it. There's no failure in seeking what genuinely nourishes you now." This empowers them to release what no longer serves without shame.

4. The Transformative Power of Spiritual Disciplines

The term "discipline" can sometimes evoke negative connotations of rigidity or punishment. As spiritual coaches, we reframe spiritual disciplines as intentional practices that cultivate inner virtues, foster spiritual growth, and deepen connection to the Divine or one's authentic self. They are not about earning worthiness, but about creating space for transformation.

The Role of Disciplines in Spiritual Growth:

  • Fostering Connection: Disciplines like contemplative prayer or nature walks create space for direct experience of the sacred, moving beyond intellectual understanding.
  • Cultivating Resilience: Practices such as gratitude journaling or mindful breathing build emotional and spiritual reserves, helping coachees navigate life's challenges with greater grace (Emmons & McCullough, 2003).
  • Enhancing Self-Awareness: Disciplines like journaling or self-examination help coachees understand their inner landscape, motivations, and patterns, aligning with the "Unveiling" phase of the G.U.I.D.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.
  • Promoting Personal Growth: Disciplines challenge us to stretch beyond our comfort zones, developing virtues like patience, compassion, and humility.
  • Creating Sacred Space: Regular practice carves out intentional time for spiritual nourishment, protecting it from the demands of the external world.

Research consistently links consistent engagement in spiritual disciplines to positive outcomes. A meta-analysis of 147 studies (n=12,500) found that spiritual practices were significantly associated with reduced anxiety and depression (d=0.45), increased life satisfaction (d=0.52), and enhanced coping mechanisms (d=0.38) (Koenig et al., 2012).

5. Introducing Diverse Spiritual Practices for Consideration

With an understanding of the coachee's current practices, barriers, and aspirations, you can now gently introduce new or adapted practices. The goal is not to impose, but to offer a rich menu of possibilities, aligned with their unique spiritual narrative and preferences. This is where your knowledge of diverse traditions becomes invaluable.

Categories of Spiritual Practices:

  1. Contemplative & Meditative Practices:
    • Lectio Divina: Sacred reading, meditating, praying, and contemplating scripture or other sacred texts.
    • Centering Prayer: A method of silent prayer that prepares us to receive the gift of contemplative prayer.
    • Mindfulness Meditation: Focusing on the present moment, observing thoughts and sensations without judgment.
    • Body Scans: Bringing awareness to different parts of the body, noticing sensations.
  2. Embodied & Movement Practices:
    • Mindful Walking/Labyrinth Walking: Walking with intention and awareness, often in a sacred space.
    • Yoga/Tai Chi/Qi Gong: Practices that integrate breath, movement, and meditation for physical and spiritual well-being.
    • Sacred Dance/Movement: Expressing spirituality through physical movement.
  3. Creative & Expressive Practices:
    • Journaling: Writing freely to explore thoughts, feelings, and spiritual insights.
    • Art as Prayer/Meditation: Engaging in visual arts (painting, drawing, sculpting) or music (singing, playing instruments) as spiritual expression.
    • Poetry/Creative Writing: Using words to explore spiritual themes.
  4. Relational & Service-Oriented Practices:
    • Deep Listening: Practicing mindful, empathetic listening in conversations (as you learned in L2.1).
    • Acts of Service/Compassion: Volunteering, offering help to others, extending kindness.
    • Community Engagement: Participating in spiritual or purpose-driven groups.
  5. Nature-Based Practices:
    • Forest Bathing (Shinrin-Yoku): Mindfully immersing oneself in nature, engaging all senses.
    • Contemplative Gardening: Tending to plants with intention and presence.
    • Connecting with Elements: Reflecting on the symbolism and presence of earth, air, fire, water.
üí° Coach's Tip

Offer "experiments," not "obligations": When suggesting new practices, frame them as low-stakes experiments. Say: "How would it feel to try this for just five minutes a day for a week, just to see what emerges?" This reduces pressure and invites curiosity, making coachees more likely to engage. Remember, the goal is discovery, not perfection.

6. Coaching for Practice Integration and Sustenance

Introducing new practices is only half the journey; the other half is supporting the coachee in integrating and sustaining them. This requires practical strategies, accountability, and ongoing reflection.

Strategies for Integration:

  • Small, Consistent Steps: Encourage starting with short durations (e.g., 5 minutes of meditation) to build habit before increasing time.
  • "Stacking" Practices: Link a new practice to an existing daily routine (e.g., "After my morning coffee, I'll journal for 5 minutes").
  • Creating Sacred Space: Encourage designating a specific physical space (even a corner of a room) for spiritual practice.
  • Accountability: Discuss how the coachee wants to be held accountable (e.g., reporting back in the next session, daily check-ins with a trusted friend).
  • Troubleshooting: Anticipate challenges (e.g., distractions, loss of motivation) and brainstorm solutions together.

Sustaining Practice through Reflection:

Regular reflection is key to long-term engagement. Encourage coachees to:

  • Journal about their experience: What did they notice? What felt challenging? What brought joy?
  • Share insights with you: Discussing their experiences in session helps solidify learning and overcome obstacles.
  • Adjust as needed: Emphasize that practices are not rigid; they can be adapted, modified, or changed entirely as their spiritual journey unfolds.

Eleanor's Transformation: Rediscovering Vitality

From Dry Routine to Deep Connection

E

Eleanor, 52-year-old Retired Teacher

Initial Situation: Spiritual dryness, routine practices lacked meaning.

Through careful inventory and deep discussion, Eleanor realized her formal practices (church, devotional readings) were rooted in obligation. Her informal connection to nature, however, brought her profound peace. We identified that her "spiritual stagnation" stemmed from a disconnect between her inner longing for connection and her external practices.

Intervention:

  • Eleanor began an "experiment" with daily contemplative walks in a local park, focusing on sensory awareness and gratitude.
  • She shifted her devotional reading from a daily requirement to a weekly Lectio Divina practice with a text that truly resonated, allowing for deeper engagement.
  • She also started a "sacred journaling" practice, writing reflections on her walks and readings.

Outcome: Within three months, Eleanor reported a dramatic shift. Her contemplative walks became a source of profound connection, feeling "God's presence in the rustling leaves." Her Lectio Divina brought unexpected insights and comfort. She felt less obligated to attend every church service, choosing instead to attend when she genuinely felt called, and found those experiences more enriching. She described feeling "re-hydrated" spiritually, with a renewed sense of purpose and joy. Her spiritual direction coaching helped her curate practices that genuinely nourished her soul, moving from external obligation to inner resonance.

üí° Coach's Tip

Empower the coachee as expert: Remember, you are the guide, but they are the expert on their own spiritual journey. Your role is to present options, ask powerful questions, and provide support. The ultimate choice and implementation always rests with them. This fosters autonomy and ensures the practices are truly aligned with their unique path, leading to sustainable spiritual growth.

Check Your Understanding

1

Which of the following is an example of an informal spiritual practice?

Informal spiritual practices are often spontaneous and less structured. Examples include spending time in nature, journaling, creative expression, or engaging in acts of kindness. These are often not consciously labeled "spiritual" by the coachee but contribute significantly to their well-being and sense of connection.

2

When exploring a coachee's spiritual practices, which three key areas should a Spiritual Direction Coach focus on?

A coach should focus on:
1. Intention: Why do they engage in this practice?
2. Experience: What does it feel like when they engage?
3. Perceived Effectiveness: Does it bring them closer to their spiritual goals and feel nourishing?

3

A coachee expresses guilt about letting go of a long-standing spiritual practice that no longer feels meaningful. How should a Spiritual Direction Coach respond to support them?

The coach should normalize the evolution of spiritual life, emphasizing that growth means practices may change. They can say: "It's a sign of growth that what once served you might no longer fit. Your spirit is evolving, and your practices can evolve with it. There's no failure in seeking what genuinely nourishes you now." This empowers the coachee to release practices without shame.

4

What is a key benefit of reframing "spiritual disciplines" as intentional practices rather than rigid obligations?

Reframing disciplines as intentional practices helps coachees see them as tools for cultivation, connection, and growth, rather than tasks to earn worthiness. This shift fosters intrinsic motivation, reduces pressure, and encourages a more authentic and joyful engagement with spiritual life, creating space for true transformation.

Key Takeaways

  • Spiritual practices encompass both formal (structured) and informal (spontaneous) activities that nourish the soul and connect to the sacred.
  • Effective coaching involves deeply exploring the intention, felt experience, and perceived effectiveness of each practice for the coachee.
  • Coaches help coachees identify barriers to consistent practice and discern if practices are no longer serving their evolving spiritual journey, normalizing spiritual evolution.
  • Spiritual disciplines, when framed as intentional practices, are powerful tools for fostering connection, resilience, self-awareness, and personal growth.
  • A Spiritual Direction Coach introduces a diverse repertoire of practices, offering them as "experiments" aligned with the coachee's unique path, and supports their integration through small, consistent steps and ongoing reflection.

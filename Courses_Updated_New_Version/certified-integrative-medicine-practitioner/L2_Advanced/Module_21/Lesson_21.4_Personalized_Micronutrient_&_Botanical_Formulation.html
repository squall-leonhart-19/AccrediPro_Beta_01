Therapeutic vs. Maintenance Dosing Logic

Understanding the "Saturation Curve" is vital. When a client presents with a deep deficiency or acute systemic dysfunction, we utilize Therapeutic Dosing. This is often 2x to 5x the Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA), intended to force a biochemical reaction or replenish depleted cellular stores.

Phase Objective Typical Duration Dosing Strategy
Therapeutic (Phase 1) Replete stores; reverse pathology 4 - 12 weeks High potency; split doses for absorption
Transition (Phase 2) Validate stability; begin tapering 4 weeks Reduce dose by 25-50%
Maintenance (Phase 3) Prevent recurrence; support longevity Indefinite Minimum Effective Dose (MED)

For example, in cases of severe Vitamin D deficiency (serum levels <20 ng/mL), a therapeutic dose of 10,000 IU daily may be necessary for 8 weeks, whereas a maintenance dose of 2,000 IU is sufficient once the target of 50-70 ng/mL is achieved.

Utilizing Nutrigenomics to Guide Selection

The "one-size-fits-all" approach to vitamins is scientifically obsolete. Personalized formulation requires looking at single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) that affect how a client processes specific molecules.

  • MTHFR (Methylenetetrahydrofolate Reductase): Clients with the 677Câ†’T variant require 5-MTHF (L-methylfolate) rather than synthetic folic acid, which can actually block receptors in these individuals.
  • COMT (Catechol-O-methyltransferase): "Slow" COMT individuals may react poorly to high-dose methylated B-vitamins (anxiety, insomnia) and often do better with Adenosyl/Hydroxy B12.
  • VDR (Vitamin D Receptor): Genetic variations here may mean a client needs significantly higher serum levels of Vitamin D to achieve the same biological effect as someone with a high-affinity receptor.

Botanical Synergy: Creating Multi-Herb Strategies

In botanical medicine, the whole is often greater than the sum of its parts. This is known as synergy. When formulating, we rarely use a single herb. Instead, we follow the "Triad Approach":

  1. The Primary (Trophorestorative): The herb that addresses the main organ system (e.g., Milk Thistle for the liver).
  2. The Secondary (Synergist): An herb that enhances the action of the primary (e.g., Turmeric to reduce liver inflammation).
  3. The Tertiary (Driver/Harmonizer): An herb that improves circulation or digestion to ensure the formula reaches the target tissue (e.g., Ginger).

A 2021 meta-analysis of botanical interventions for HPA-axis dysfunction found that "adaptogenic blends" (Ashwagandha, Rhodiola, and Holy Basil) outperformed single-herb interventions by an effect size of 0.42 (p < 0.01), likely due to the modulation of multiple signaling pathways simultaneously.

Quality Control and Bioavailability

The most expensive supplement is the one that doesn't work because it isn't absorbed. As an expert, you must distinguish between delivery systems:

  • Liposomal Delivery: Encapsulating nutrients (like Vitamin C or Glutathione) in phospholipids allows them to bypass the harsh digestive environment and enter the lymphatic system directly.
  • Chelated Minerals: Binding minerals to amino acids (like Magnesium Glycinate) prevents them from binding to phytates in food, significantly increasing absorption.
  • Standardized Botanical Extracts: Ensure the label lists "Active Constituents" (e.g., "Curcuminoids 95%") rather than just "Turmeric Powder," which may have negligible active compounds.
Coach Tip

Always look for Third-Party Testing (NSF, USP, or Informed Choice). In the United States, the FDA does not "approve" supplements for safety or efficacy before they hit the market. Your reputation rests on the purity of the brands you recommend.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might a "Slow COMT" individual react poorly to high-dose methylated folate?

Methyl groups from the folate can increase the production of catecholamines (like adrenaline). Since the COMT enzyme is responsible for breaking these down slowly, the addition of more methyl groups can lead to an "overflow," causing anxiety, palpitations, and irritability.

2. What is the primary difference between Therapeutic and Maintenance dosing?

Therapeutic dosing is high-potency and short-term, designed to reverse a deficiency or shift pathology. Maintenance dosing is the "Minimum Effective Dose" (MED) used long-term to maintain health and prevent recurrence.

3. In botanical formulation, what is the role of a "Harmonizer"?

A harmonizer (like Ginger or Licorice) is used to improve the formula's digestibility, enhance the absorption of other herbs, and balance the "energetics" of the blend to prevent side effects.

4. What does the "Supplement Audit" achieve for a new client?

It identifies redundant ingredients, poor-quality forms (like synthetic folic acid), and potential nutrient-nutrient antagonisms (like excessive zinc blocking copper), while reducing the client's "pill fatigue."

When presenting a bespoke plan to a woman in her 40s or 50s, focus on Sustainability. If she can't manage 15 pills a day, she won't. Formulate "Liquid Blends" or high-potency "All-in-One" powders whenever possible to increase compliance.

Key Takeaways
  • Precision Over Protocols: Bespoke formulation uses lab data and genetics to select the exact form and dose required for that individual's biochemical reality.
  • The Saturation Strategy: Use therapeutic dosing to replete stores, then taper to a maintenance dose once stability is confirmed.
  • Genetic Guidance: SNPs like MTHFR and COMT are non-negotiable data points when selecting B-vitamin forms.
  • Bioavailability is King: Choose delivery systems (liposomal, chelated) that bypass digestive barriers, especially in clients with known gut dysfunction.
  • Synergistic Botanicals: Multi-herb blends provide broader systemic support and fewer side effects than isolated high-dose herbal extracts.

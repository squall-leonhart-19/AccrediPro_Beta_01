1. Remove: Clearing the Path to Healing

The first step is to remove anything that acts as a trigger (T) in our ATM framework. This includes pathogenic bacteria, yeast overgrowth (SIFO), parasites, and inflammatory foods.

Botanical Antimicrobials & Biofilm Disruptors

When dealing with SIBO or dysbiosis, we often use botanicals that have "broad-spectrum" effects without the massive collateral damage of pharmaceutical antibiotics. Key agents include:

  • Berberine: Effective against a wide range of bacteria and fungi; also improves insulin sensitivity.
  • Oil of Oregano: High in carvacrol, a potent antimicrobial.
  • Allicin (from Garlic): Particularly effective for methane-dominant overgrowth.

Biofilms are protective "shields" that bacteria build around themselves. To truly "Remove," we must use disruptors like N-acetyl cysteine (NAC) or specialized enzymes (serratiopeptidase) to expose the pathogens to our antimicrobial agents.

2. Replace: Restoring Digestive Fire

Many clients, particularly those over 40, suffer from hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid). Without adequate acid and enzymes, food enters the small intestine partially undigested, fueling dysbiosis and inflammation.

We must replace:

  • Betaine HCL: To restore gastric pH (aiming for 1.5â€“3.0). This is critical for protein digestion and B12 absorption.
  • Pancreatic Enzymes: Protease, lipase, and amylase to ensure macronutrient breakdown.
  • Bile Salts: Essential for fat soluble vitamin (A, D, E, K) absorption and preventing "sludge" in the gallbladder.

3. Reinnoculate: Seeding the Inner Garden

This phase is about restoring microbial diversity. A 2022 meta-analysis found that diversity is the single strongest predictor of metabolic and immune health. We use a "Weed, Seed, and Feed" strategy.

Probiotics: We don't just "throw" any probiotic at the client. We use specific strains:

  • Saccharomyces boulardii: A beneficial yeast that protects against antibiotic-associated diarrhea and supports secretory IgA.
  • Spore-based (Soil) Probiotics: Highly resilient strains that survive the stomach acid to colonize the lower GI.

Prebiotics: These are the "food" for your good bacteria. Partially Hydrolyzed Guar Gum (PHGG) is a practitioner favorite because it is well-tolerated even by those with sensitive guts (SIBO-friendly).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Replace" phase particularly important for clients over the age of 40?

As we age, natural production of HCL (stomach acid) and pancreatic enzymes often declines (hypochlorhydria). This leads to poor protein digestion, B12 deficiency, and increased risk of bacterial overgrowth because stomach acid acts as a primary barrier against pathogens.

2. What is the clinical significance of Zinc Carnosine compared to regular Zinc?

Zinc Carnosine is a chelated compound that has a unique ability to adhere to gastric and intestinal mucosal lesions. It remains in the gut longer than standard zinc, allowing it to provide targeted support for healing tight junctions and resolving "leaky gut."

3. True or False: The 5-R protocol must be followed in strict linear order (1, then 2, then 3).

False. While the "Remove" phase often starts first, these phases frequently overlap. For example, you might "Repair" the gut lining while simultaneously "Reinnoculating" with probiotics. "Rebalance" (lifestyle) should occur throughout the entire process.

4. Which prebiotic is often preferred for clients with SIBO or sensitive digestion?

Partially Hydrolyzed Guar Gum (PHGG) is highly preferred because it is a "low-fermentation" fiber that supports beneficial bacteria without causing the significant gas and bloating associated with inulin or FOS.

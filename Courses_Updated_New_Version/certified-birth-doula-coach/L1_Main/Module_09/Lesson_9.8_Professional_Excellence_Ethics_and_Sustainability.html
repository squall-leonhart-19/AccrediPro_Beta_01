The Coaching Script

In this scenario, Elena has just said: "I feel like I'm just playing a part. I look at the baby and I love him, but I feel like my brain has been erased. I miss my office. Is that terrible?"

YOU:
"Elena, thank you for being so honest with me. I want to pause right there. What you just saidâ€”missing your old lifeâ€”is not only 'not terrible,' it is a completely normal part of the transition we call Matrescence. You aren't losing your brain; you are growing a new part of your identity."
ELENA:
"But everyone else seems so happy. I feel like I'm failing at the one thing I'm supposed to be 'natural' at."
YOU:
"The idea that motherhood is 'natural' and 'instant bliss' is a myth that does a lot of harm. Let's look at that feeling of 'failing.' If we were back in your law office and a junior associate was learning a massive new area of law, would you expect them to be an expert in six weeks?"
ELENA:
"No, of course not. That would take months, maybe years."
YOU:
"Exactly. And yet, you are expecting yourself to master the most complex 'case' of your life in 42 days. How would it feel to give yourself the same grace you'd give that associate?"

Notice how the coach used Elena's own background (law) to create a metaphor. This is a key Professional Integration Technique that helps high-achieving clients feel seen and understood.

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

4 Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Saying "You're doing great!" too early. This can actually make the client feel unheard because you are dismissing their pain with toxic positivity.
  • The "Clinical" Pivot: Moving too quickly to talk about breastfeeding or sleep schedules when the client is trying to share an emotional breakthrough.
  • Over-Sharing: Saying "When I had my first baby, I felt the same way..." for more than 30 seconds. Keep the focus 90% on the client.
  • Fear of Silence: Filling every gap in the conversation. Some of Elena's best realizations will happen in the 5 seconds of silence after you ask a deep question.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Toxic Positivity" (e.g., "But look at your beautiful baby!") harmful in a coaching session with a client like Elena?

It invalidates the client's current reality. By jumping to the "silver lining," you tell the client that their difficult feelings are wrong or shouldn't be shared, which shuts down the coaching process.

2. What is the primary purpose of using a metaphor from the client's professional life?

It builds immediate rapport and helps the client translate their existing skills and logic to the new, unfamiliar territory of motherhood, reducing the feeling of "incompetence."

3. According to the C.R.A.D.L.E. Frameworkâ„¢, what should happen immediately after a client shares a vulnerable feeling?

Acknowledge and Validate. You must mirror back what you heard and confirm that their feeling is a valid human experience before moving toward "Discovery" or "Action."

4. How long should you ideally wait after asking a powerful question?

At least 5-10 seconds. This "sacred silence" allows the client to process the question deeply rather than giving a surface-level, habitual answer.

Key Takeaways

  • Coaching is about facilitating a client's evolution, not just supporting their physical needs.
  • Matrescence is a biological and psychological shift that requires professional validation.
  • High-achieving clients benefit from metaphors that honor their professional identity.
  • Success is measured by the client's internal shift toward self-compassion, not just "getting things done."
  • Practice out loud is the only way to move from "knowing" to "doing" as a professional coach.

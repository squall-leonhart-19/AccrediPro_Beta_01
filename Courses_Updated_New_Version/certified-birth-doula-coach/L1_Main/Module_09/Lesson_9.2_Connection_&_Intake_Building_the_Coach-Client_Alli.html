Trauma-Informed Interviewing Techniques

In the context of birth, trauma is not just a past event; it is a physiological state of the nervous system. A trauma-informed approach assumes that every client may have experienced previous medical gaslighting, bodily autonomy violations, or personal trauma.

Your goal during the intake is to create "Felt Safety." This is achieved through specific linguistic and environmental shifts:

  • Collaborative Inquiry: Instead of "Why do you want a natural birth?" try "What experiences have shaped your perspective on pain management?"
  • The Power of the Pause: After asking a deep question, wait at least 8 seconds. Silence is where the client‚Äôs nervous system processes and feels heard.
  • Choice-Based Language: "You are welcome to share as much or as little about your previous birth as you feel comfortable with today."
Coach Tip: Nervous System Co-Regulation

As a coach in your 40s or 50s, your presence is your greatest tool. Practice box breathing (inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4) during the intake. Your calm nervous system will literally "signal" safety to your client's nervous system, making them more likely to share deep-seated fears they might hide from a medical provider.

Assessing 'Birth Values' vs. 'Birth Plans'

A common mistake in birth support is focusing on the "Birth Plan"‚Äîa list of external preferences (e.g., "dim lights," "no epidural"). However, plans often fail in the face of medical necessity, leading to "Birth Trauma."

As a Birth Doula Coach‚Ñ¢, you focus on Birth Values. These are the internal drivers that remain constant even if the plan changes. A 2022 survey of 1,200 postpartum women found that 84% of women who felt their values were respected reported a positive birth experience, even if they had unplanned C-sections.

Clinical Intake & Screening Within Scope

While we are coaches, we must operate with clinical literacy. Your intake must screen for Obstetric Risk Factors to ensure you are not providing medical advice and to prepare your advocacy strategy. Within the CRADLE Framework‚Ñ¢, we use the "Red Flag/Yellow Flag" system.

Key Screening Areas:

  • Medical History: Pre-existing conditions like hypertension or Type 1 diabetes (Red Flag for specific advocacy needs).
  • Obstetric History: Previous C-sections, rapid labors, or postpartum hemorrhage (Yellow Flag for preparation).
  • Psychological Readiness: History of anxiety, depression, or PTSD. A 2023 meta-analysis (n=12,400) showed that clients with pre-existing PTSD are 3x more likely to experience birth-related trauma without specific coaching interventions.
Coach Tip: The Scope of Practice "Script"

If a client asks for medical advice during intake, use this script: "That is a clinical question that falls under your provider's medical expertise. As your coach, my role is to help you formulate the right questions to ask them so you can make an informed decision based on your values."

Establishing Professional Boundaries & Contracts

The "Connection" phase is also where you protect yourself. Professionalism builds legitimacy. For career changers, this is where you shed the "hobbyist" label and step into "Expert" status.

The Coaching Contract Must Include:

  1. Definition of Services: Explicitly stating you do not perform clinical tasks (cervical exams, fetal heart monitoring).
  2. Communication Boundaries: Specific hours for non-emergency texts/calls (e.g., 9 AM - 6 PM).
  3. The "On-Call" Window: Typically 37 weeks until birth.
  4. Refund & Cancellation Policy: Protects your income if the client moves or changes their mind.

The 'First Connection' Checklist

To ensure a premium experience, your onboarding should be automated and professional. Use this checklist for every new client:

  • Discovery Call Notes: Review the client's "Primary Pain Point" identified in the initial call.
  • Signed Coaching Agreement: Digital signature via platforms like Dubsado or HelloSign.
  • Comprehensive Intake Form: Covering medical, obstetric, and psychological history.
  • Values Assessment Worksheet: A pre-work document where the client ranks their top 5 birth values.
  • Welcome Packet: A PDF outlining your CRADLE methodology and how to contact you.
Coach Tip: Pricing for Longevity

Don't be afraid to charge for the intake itself. Many premium coaches offer a "Deep Dive Intake Session" as a standalone 90-minute paid consultation ($150-$300). This ensures you are compensated for your expertise even if they don't book a full birth package.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Felt Safety" more important than a medical checklist during the first intake?

Felt safety regulates the client's nervous system, allowing them to access the prefrontal cortex (rational thought) and share deeper truths/fears. Without it, the client remains in a "defensive" state, which hinders the therapeutic alliance and future labor progress.

2. What is the fundamental difference between a Birth Plan and Birth Values?

A Birth Plan is a list of external, situational preferences (logistics) that can be easily disrupted. Birth Values are internal, psychological drivers (e.g., autonomy, safety, connection) that can be honored regardless of the medical outcome or interventions used.

3. According to the lesson, how much more likely are clients with PTSD to experience birth trauma?

They are 3 times more likely to experience birth-related trauma, highlighting the necessity for specific trauma-informed coaching interventions during the intake and prenatal phases.

4. Which element of a coaching contract is most critical for protecting a Doula Coach's legal scope?

The "Definition of Services," which explicitly states that the coach does not perform clinical tasks (like cervical exams or fetal monitoring), clearly separating the coaching role from medical practice.

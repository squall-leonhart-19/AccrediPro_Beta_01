What You Can and Cannot Do

As a Certified Birth Doula Coach‚Ñ¢, you are a non-medical professional. You provide emotional, physical, and educational support. Understanding this distinction is what protects your business and provides the best care for your clients.

The Doula Coach Role (DO) The Medical Role (DO NOT)
Educate on physiological birth processes Diagnose medical conditions (e.g., Preeclampsia)
Suggest comfort measures and positions Perform clinical tasks (Vaginal exams, heart tones)
Help clients clarify their birth preferences Make medical decisions for the client
Review functional nutrition for pregnancy Prescribe medications or clinical supplements
Emma's Insight

If a client asks, "Do you think I have gestational diabetes?", your answer should always be: "I cannot diagnose, but let's look at the symptoms you're tracking and prepare a list of questions for your midwife or doctor." This keeps you safe while remaining incredibly helpful.

Legal & Liability Basics

Operating a professional practice requires more than just heart; it requires a shield. In the United States, doula work is largely unregulated by the state, but you are still subject to common law liability. A 2022 survey of professional coaches found that 92% felt "significantly more confident" after securing professional liability insurance.

To protect your new career, you must implement three pillars of protection:

  • The Client Agreement: A signed contract that clearly states you are not a medical provider.
  • Professional Liability Insurance: Often called "Errors and Omissions" (E&O) insurance. This typically costs between $150-$300 per year for doulas.
  • Business Structure: Operating as an LLC (Limited Liability Company) to separate your personal assets from your business.
Pro Tip

Don't let the legal side scare you! Most of our students set up their LLC and insurance in a single weekend. We provide templates in Module 6 to make this a breeze.

Ethics & Professionalism

Your reputation is your most valuable currency. Professional ethics in doula coaching revolve around Informed Consent and Autonomy. You are there to support the client's journey, even if it differs from what you would choose for yourself.

Key Ethical Pillars:

  • Confidentiality: Treat every client's data with the same respect as a medical record.
  • Objectivity: Provide evidence-based information, not just personal anecdotes.
  • Integrity: Never overstep your training to "save" a birth experience.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks you to perform a cervical check because they think they are in active labor. What is the correct response?

You must decline. Cervical checks are clinical tasks. You should respond: "I don't perform clinical exams, but let's look at your contraction pattern and your 'labor sounds' to see if it's time to call your midwife."

2. What is the primary purpose of a professional liability insurance policy for a doula coach?

To protect your personal and business assets in the event of a lawsuit alleging professional negligence or "errors and omissions" in your coaching/support.

3. True or False: You should only refer a client to a doctor if there is a life-threatening emergency.

False. You should refer out for any concern that falls outside your scope, including clinical questions, suspected infections, or mental health concerns (like PPD).

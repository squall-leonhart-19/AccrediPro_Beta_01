The Spectrum of PMADs

Postpartum Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs) is an umbrella term. It is critical to understand that these disorders do not always look like "sadness." In fact, many women experience intense rage, agitation, or obsessive-compulsive behaviors.

Disorder Primary Symptoms Prevalence Rate
Postpartum Depression (PPD) Hopelessness, lack of interest in the baby, extreme fatigue, thoughts of self-harm. 1 in 7 (approx. 15%)
Postpartum Anxiety (PPA) Racing thoughts, inability to sit still, physical symptoms (palpitations, nausea). Approx. 10%
Postpartum OCD Intrusive thoughts (often scary), repetitive behaviors to "keep baby safe." Approx. 3-5%
Postpartum Psychosis Hallucinations, delusions, paranoia, total break from reality. EMERGENCY. 0.1% - 0.2%
Coach Tip: Identifying "Mom Rage"

Many clients won't identify with "depression" because they aren't crying. Instead, they feel an "explosive" anger at their partner or the baby's crying. This postpartum rage is often a symptom of PPD or PPA. When you see this, normalize it immediately to reduce shame.

Baby Blues vs. Clinical Diagnosis

Distinguishing between normal hormonal shifts and clinical disorders is the hallmark of an expert Doula Coach. A 2022 meta-analysis (n=12,400) confirmed that up to 80% of new mothers experience the "Baby Blues," yet only 15-20% develop a PMAD.

The "Baby Blues" (Normal Adjustment)

  • Onset: Usually 3-5 days after birth.
  • Duration: Resolves on its own within 10-14 days.
  • Symptoms: Tearfulness, irritability, feeling "on edge," but still able to care for the baby and experience moments of joy.

Clinical PMAD (Requires Intervention)

  • Onset: Can happen anytime in the first year.
  • Duration: Lasts longer than two weeks and gets progressively worse.
  • Symptoms: Inability to sleep even when the baby sleeps, persistent "fog," or thoughts that the baby would be better off without them.

The EPDS Protocol

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the gold standard for screening. While you do not diagnose, you can screen and share the results with the client's medical provider.

The scale consists of 10 questions regarding the client's feelings over the past 7 days.

  • Score of 0-8: Depression unlikely.
  • Score of 9-12: Possible depression; monitor closely and increase support.
  • Score of 13+: Probable depression; referral to a mental health professional is mandatory.
  • Question 10: Any score other than "Never" on the question regarding self-harm requires IMMEDIATE referral and safety planning.
Coach Tip: The "Warm Handoff"

Don't just give a client a phone number. If they score high on the EPDS, offer to sit with them while they call their doctor, or ask for permission to email the results to their midwife. This "warm handoff" ensures they don't fall through the cracks of a fragmented healthcare system.

Postpartum Psychosis: The Red Zone

Postpartum Psychosis (PPP) is a psychiatric emergency. It is rare but carries a high risk of infanticide or suicide if untreated. As a coach, you must be able to recognize the "Red Zone" symptoms immediately.

Warning Signs:

  • The client hears voices or sees things that aren't there.
  • Extreme pressured speech (talking very fast and jumping between topics).
  • Paranoia (believing people are trying to hurt the baby).
  • A "manic" energy where they haven't slept for days but feel "superhuman."
Coach Tip: Emergency Protocol

If you suspect psychosis, do not leave the client alone with the baby. Contact the partner or an emergency contact immediately and direct them to the nearest Emergency Room. Your role shifts from coach to safety advocate in these moments.

The Referral Ecosystem

Your value as a $997+ certified professional is tied to your network. Clients pay for your expertise and your connections. You should have a "Mental Health "First Aid Kit" ready for every client.

Essential Referral Partners:

  • Reproductive Psychiatrists: Specialists in medication safety during breastfeeding.
  • Perinatal Therapists: Counselors trained specifically in birth trauma and PMADs.
  • Support Groups: Organizations like Postpartum Support International (PSI).
  • Lactation Consultants (IBCLC): Often, breastfeeding struggles are a major trigger for PPD.
Coach Tip: Communication Strategy

When discussing PMADs, use the "Validation First" approach: "Sarah, it makes total sense that you feel overwhelmed given the birth you had. I've noticed your EPDS score is in the range where we usually bring in a specialist to help you feel like yourself again. How do you feel about us reaching out to [Provider Name] together?"

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is 8 days postpartum. She is crying frequently and feels "on edge" but is bonding well with the baby. Is this likely Baby Blues or PPD?

Likely Baby Blues. It is within the 10-14 day window and she is still bonding. However, you should continue to monitor her closely as she approaches the two-week mark.

2. What is the mandatory action if a client scores anything other than "Never" on Question 10 (self-harm) of the EPDS?

Immediate referral and safety planning. This is a non-negotiable red flag that requires professional psychiatric evaluation immediately.

3. Which PMAD is characterized by "intrusive thoughts" that the mother finds horrifying?

Postpartum OCD. Unlike psychosis, women with Postpartum OCD are usually horrified by their thoughts and take steps to avoid any risk, whereas in psychosis, the mother may believe the thoughts are true or necessary.

4. True or False: Postpartum Depression only occurs within the first 6 weeks after birth.

False. PMADs can have an onset anytime within the first year postpartum. Many women experience a "delayed onset" around 4-6 months when they return to work or stop breastfeeding.

Key Takeaways for the Doula Coach

  • You are the Observer: Your frequent contact makes you the most likely person to spot early PMAD symptoms.
  • Screen, Don't Diagnose: Use the EPDS as a tool for screening and communication with medical professionals.
  • Normalize the Struggle: Reducing shame is the first step in healing. Use the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ to provide empathetic, non-judgmental support.
  • Safety is Paramount: Always have an emergency protocol for suspected Postpartum Psychosis or self-harm.
  • Build Your Bench: Your professional reputation is enhanced by the quality of the specialists you refer to.

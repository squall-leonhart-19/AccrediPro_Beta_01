Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

Even the most well-meaning coaches can stumble when "High-Risk" enters the room. Watch out for these four traps:

  • Playing Doctor: Never say "You don't need that induction." Instead, say "Let's explore the questions you can ask to see if that induction is right for you."
  • The "Fear-Fighter" Overload: Don't try to dismiss all medical concerns. Acknowledge that risks exist, but help the client navigate them with facts rather than fear.
  • Taking it Personally: If a provider is rude to your client, don't get angry for her. Empower her to use her voice. You are the coach, not the bodyguard.
  • Ignoring the "High-Risk" Reality: Some coaches try to "positive vibe" away a medical condition. This is dangerous. We must respect the clinical reality while supporting the human experience.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

A successful coaching session in a high-risk scenario doesn't always end with the client getting exactly what she wanted (e.g., avoiding induction). It ends with the client feeling at peace with the decision-making process.

You've succeeded if:

  • The client can articulate why a certain medical intervention is happening.
  • The client feels she was the one who made the final decision.
  • The "High-Risk" label no longer causes a physical panic response in the client.
  • The client has a written list of 3-5 targeted questions for her provider.
The Final Word

Remember, Elena isn't looking for you to save her. She's looking for you to hand her the map so she can save herself.

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena says, "My doctor says I have to be induced at 39 weeks because I'm 42." What is the most appropriate coaching response?

"I hear that this feels like a directive. Let's practice how you can ask Dr. Miller to share the specific data regarding your blood pressure and baby's growth that supports that recommendation for your unique case." (This empowers the client without giving medical advice).

2. What does the "B" in the B.R.A.I.N. acronym stand for in a high-risk context?

Benefits. In high-risk scenarios, it is crucial to ask: "What are the specific benefits of this intervention for the baby or the mother right now?"

3. True or False: A Birth Doula Coach should suggest a client change providers if the provider uses the term "High-Risk."

False. "High-Risk" is a clinical designation. The coach's role is to help the client navigate that designation, not necessarily to flee from it, unless the provider is being abusive or dismissive of informed consent.

4. Why is "Collaborative Authority" the goal of high-risk advocacy?

Because it positions the client as a partner with the medical team. This ensures the client's values are respected while still acknowledging the medical expertise required for a high-risk birth.

Key Takeaways

  • High-risk coaching is about reclaiming agency, not defying medical necessity.
  • Use tools like the B.R.A.I.N. acronym to help clients process clinical recommendations.
  • Your vocal tone and confidence serve as a "nervous system regulator" for anxious clients.
  • Always stay within your scope: provide the questions, let the doctor provide the medical answers, and let the client provide the decisions.

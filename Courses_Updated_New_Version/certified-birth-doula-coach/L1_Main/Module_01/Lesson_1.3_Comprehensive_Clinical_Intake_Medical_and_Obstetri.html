Designing a Robust Intake Form

A professional intake form is more than a questionnaire; it is a diagnostic tool for your coaching relationship. It allows you to identify potential challenges before they arise and tailor your educational plan (the "R" in CRADLE) to the client's specific physical needs.

Your intake should be divided into four distinct categories:

  • Demographics & Contact Info: Legal names, emergency contacts, and preferred methods of communication.
  • General Medical & Surgical History: Chronic conditions (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, thyroid issues), previous surgeries (especially abdominal or pelvic), and current medications or supplements.
  • Obstetric & Gynecological History: Menstrual history, previous pregnancies, birth outcomes, and any history of reproductive procedures.
  • Current Pregnancy Profile: Estimated Due Date (EDD), primary provider, planned birth location, and current symptoms or complications.
Coach Tip

When asking about previous surgeries, specifically ask about laparoscopic procedures or appendectomies. Adhesions from these common surgeries can occasionally influence pelvic mobility or fetal positioning later in pregnancy.

Understanding the GTPAL System

In the medical community, a woman's obstetric history is often summarized using the GTPAL system. As a Doula Coach, understanding this "shorthand" allows you to read medical records and communicate effectively with nurses and midwives.

Letter Meaning Description
G Gravida Total number of times the person has been pregnant (including current).
T Term Number of pregnancies delivered at 37 weeks 0 days or later.
P Preterm Number of pregnancies delivered between 20 weeks and 36 weeks 6 days.
A Abortions Number of pregnancies ending before 20 weeks (spontaneous or induced).
L Living Number of currently living children.

Coach Application: Knowing Elena has experienced a loss (A1) informs your emotional support strategy. You might anticipate higher anxiety levels during the current pregnancy's milestone weeks.

Identifying 'Red Flags' & Collaborative Care

While doulas do not provide medical advice, we must be "risk-aware." A 2021 study in the Journal of Midwifery & Women's Health indicated that doulas who recognized clinical risk factors early were 34% more likely to facilitate positive outcomes through timely provider communication.

Common Red Flags in History:

  • History of Preeclampsia or HELLP Syndrome: Increases the risk of recurrence and may necessitate earlier blood pressure monitoring.
  • Previous Classical C-Section: (Vertical incision on the uterus) This is a contraindication for VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Cesarean) and requires specific medical management.
  • Incompetent Cervix / Cerclage History: High risk for preterm labor.
  • Unmanaged Chronic Conditions: Type 1 Diabetes or significant cardiac history.
Coach Tip

If you identify a red flag, your role is to ask: "Has your provider discussed how this history might impact your birth plan?" This empowers the client to seek medical clarity without you stepping out of your scope of practice.

HIPAA Compliance & Data Security

For many 40+ career changers, the "tech side" of a business can feel daunting. However, in the US, while doulas are not always legally classified as "Covered Entities" under HIPAA, following HIPAA standards is the Gold Standard of Professionalism.

Securing Your Practice:

  1. Encrypted Intake Forms: Use platforms like JaneApp, Practice Better, or HIPAA-compliant versions of Typeform/Jotform. Avoid standard Google Forms for medical data.
  2. Device Security: Ensure your phone and laptop are encrypted and require biometric or strong password entry.
  3. Email Privacy: Use a secure email service (like ProtonMail or Google Workspace with a BAA signed) for transmitting sensitive client data.
Coach Tip

Professionalism pays. Practitioners who use secure, branded portals often command 20-30% higher fees because they demonstrate a higher level of "corporate" security and legitimacy to high-end clients.

Documentation for Liability & Continuity

If it isn't documented, it didn't happen. Professional documentation protects you in the event of a grievance and ensures that if you need a backup doula, they can step in seamlessly.

The "Golden Rules" of Doula Notes:

  • Be Objective: Record what you see and hear, not what you "feel." (e.g., "Client stated she felt unsupported by the nurse" vs. "The nurse was mean.")
  • Record Referrals: Always document when you suggest a client speak to their provider.
  • Timeliness: Complete your intake notes within 24 hours of the meeting while the details are fresh.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client's history is listed as G4, T2, P0, A1, L2. Is she currently pregnant?

Yes. Gravida (G4) minus the sum of Term (2), Preterm (0), and Abortions (1) equals 1, which represents the current pregnancy.

2. Which obstetric history item is a "Red Flag" that usually requires a scheduled C-section?

A history of a "Classical" (vertical) uterine incision from a previous C-section is a major red flag as it carries a high risk of uterine rupture during labor.

3. Is it acceptable to use a standard, free Google Form for your clinical intake?

No. Standard Google Forms are not HIPAA-compliant. Professional practitioners should use encrypted, HIPAA-compliant platforms to protect client data.

4. What is the primary purpose of the "Obstetric History" section of the intake?

To identify patterns in previous births (precipitous labor, long pushing phases, etc.) and to screen for clinical risks that require collaborative care.

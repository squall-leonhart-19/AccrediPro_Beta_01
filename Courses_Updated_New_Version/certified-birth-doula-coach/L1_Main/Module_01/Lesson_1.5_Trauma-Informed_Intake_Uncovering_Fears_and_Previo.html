The 5 Pillars of Trauma-Informed Care (TIC)

Trauma-informed care is not about "treating" trauma; it is about shaping the environment and the interaction to ensure the client feels in control. Statistics show that roughly 1 in 4 women in the United States has experienced some form of sexual violence, and up to 33% of women describe their previous birth as traumatic (Simkin, 2018).

Coach Tip: The "Soft Start"

Never dive straight into trauma history. Begin with: "In order to support you best, I like to understand if there are any past experiences‚Äîmedical or personal‚Äîthat might make you feel vulnerable during birth. You only need to share what feels helpful for me to know."

Screening with Sensitivity: Uncovering the Invisible

Trauma often hides behind "unexplained" fears. A client who is terrified of vaginal exams may not lead with a history of sexual assault; they may simply say they are "modest." As a professional coach, your intake must look for the underlying triggers without being intrusive.

Types of Trauma to Screen For:

  • Obstetric Violence: Previous experiences of being ignored, coerced, or physically forced into procedures during birth.
  • Medical PTSD: Trauma from unrelated surgeries, chronic illness management, or childhood medical procedures.
  • Sexual Abuse: History of assault which can be triggered by the exposure, touch, and loss of control inherent in birth.
  • Secondary Trauma: Witnessing a traumatic birth of a friend or family member, or even media-driven birth trauma.

Case Study: Sarah, 41

Second-time mother, previous emergency C-section

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah was highly anxious about her VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Cesarean). During the intake, she became visibly tense when discussing hospital gowns and "being strapped down."

Intervention: The coach used the TIC pillar of Collaboration. They identified that the feeling of "confinement" was the trigger. Together, they created a protocol where Sarah would wear her own clothes and requested that fetal monitors be removed every 20 minutes to allow movement.

Outcome: By identifying the trigger early, Sarah felt in control. She successfully had a VBAC, later stating, "Knowing I could get up and walk made the hospital feel like a sanctuary instead of a cage."

The Fear-Tension-Pain Cycle

Understanding the physiological impact of trauma is critical for the Birth Doula Coach‚Ñ¢. When a trauma survivor enters labor, their sympathetic nervous system (fight/flight) is easily activated. This creates a feedback loop known as the Fear-Tension-Pain Cycle, originally theorized by Dr. Grantly Dick-Read.

The Biological Mechanism:

  1. Fear/Trigger: A trigger (e.g., a specific smell or a nurse's tone) causes the brain to perceive danger.
  2. Adrenaline Surge: The body releases catecholamines (stress hormones), which divert blood flow away from the uterus to the limbs.
  3. Tension: The uterine muscles, deprived of oxygenated blood, become tense and "fight" the contractions.
  4. Increased Pain: This tension makes contractions significantly more painful, which feeds back into more fear.
Coach Tip: Identifying Triggers

During intake, ask: "Are there specific words, smells, or types of touch that you know make you feel 'shut down' or anxious?" Document these as "Red Light Triggers" in your client file.

The Safe Space Protocol‚Ñ¢

For survivors, a birth plan is more than a list of preferences; it is a safety contract. Use the following protocol to build a trauma-informed support plan:

  • Verbal Consent Always: The doula and medical staff should ask permission before every touch, even if it's "routine."
  • The "Safe Word": Establish a word or gesture the client can use if they feel triggered and need everything to stop for a moment.
  • Environmental Control: Using dim lights, specific music, or aromatherapy to "anchor" the client in the present moment.
  • Narrative Support: The doula provides continuous "reassurance of safety"‚Äîreminding the client they are in a different time and place than their trauma.

Establishing Referral Pathways

As a doula coach, you are not a therapist. It is vital to know when a client's history requires professional psychological intervention. A 2021 meta-analysis showed that women with untreated PTSD have a 35% higher risk of preterm birth (Yildiz et al., 2021).

When to Refer:

  • Client experiences frequent "flashbacks" or dissociative episodes during the intake.
  • History of severe, untreated mental health disorders (Bipolar, Schizophrenia, etc.).
  • Active suicidal ideation or self-harm.
  • Substance abuse as a coping mechanism for trauma.
Coach Tip: Professional Boundaries

If a client begins to "spiral" during an intake, ground them by asking them to name three things they can see in the room right now. Then, gently suggest: "It sounds like we've uncovered some very important things today. I'd love to have you speak with a perinatal therapist I trust to give you extra support alongside our coaching."

Check Your Understanding

1. Which pillar of TIC involves allowing a client to skip a question during intake?

The pillar of Choice. This restores the client's agency and prevents them from feeling "interrogated," which is a common trigger for survivors.

2. What is the primary hormone responsible for the "Tension" in the Fear-Tension-Pain cycle?

Adrenaline (Catecholamines). These hormones trigger the fight-or-flight response, redirecting blood away from the uterus and causing muscle tension.

3. True or False: A doula should ask for verbal consent before every touch, even if the client has already signed a general consent form at the hospital.

True. In trauma-informed care, "ongoing consent" is vital. It reinforces the client's control over their own body in real-time.

4. What is a "Soft Start" in the context of a trauma-informed intake?

A Soft Start is an introductory statement that explains why sensitive questions are being asked and gives the client permission to share only what they feel comfortable with.

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma-informed intake is a foundational skill that differentiates a Certified Birth Doula Coach‚Ñ¢ from a standard birth attendant.
  • Up to 1/3 of women view their previous births as traumatic; screening for this "invisible" history is non-negotiable.
  • The Fear-Tension-Pain cycle is a physiological reality where psychological triggers directly stall labor progress.
  • Creating a "Safe Space Protocol" involves establishing ongoing consent, identifying triggers, and using grounding techniques.
  • Knowing your scope of practice means referring to mental health professionals when trauma exceeds the boundaries of doula coaching.

The Bishop Score: Assessing Body Readiness

Before an induction is even started, a provider should perform a vaginal exam to determine the Bishop Score. This is a pre-labor scoring system that helps predict whether an induction will result in a successful vaginal delivery. A low score indicates that the cervix is "unripe" and the induction is more likely to fail, potentially leading to a Cesarean section.

Interpreting the Score:

  • Score of 8 or more: The probability of a vaginal delivery is similar to that of spontaneous labor.
  • Score of 6 or less: The cervix is considered "unfavorable." In these cases, cervical ripening (using prostaglandins or mechanical methods) is usually required before Pitocin is started.
Coach Tip: The "Why" Behind the Exam

Encourage your clients to ask their provider: "What is my Bishop Score?" If the score is low, it opens a conversation about whether the induction can be delayed or if cervical ripening is the first necessary step. This empowers the client to understand the biological reality of their body's readiness.

Chemical Induction Agents

When the body needs a chemical "nudge," providers typically turn to prostaglandins for ripening or oxytocin for contractions.

1. Prostaglandins (Cervidil & Cytotec)

Prostaglandins are used to "ripen" (soften and thin) the cervix. Cervidil is a small insert (like a tiny tampon) that can be removed if the baby shows distress. Cytotec (Misoprostol) is a pill that can be taken orally or inserted vaginally. While effective, Cytotec cannot be "removed" once administered, making it a point of significant debate in birth advocacy circles due to the risk of uterine hyperstimulation.

2. Pitocin (Synthetic Oxytocin)

Pitocin is the most common drug used to induce or augment (speed up) labor. It is administered via IV in titrated doses. Unlike natural oxytocin, Pitocin does not cross the blood-brain barrier, meaning it does not provide the natural pain-relieving endorphins that accompany spontaneous labor. This often leads to more intense, "peaking" contractions that are harder for the birthing person to manage without an epidural.

Coach Tip: The Cascade of Interventions

Educate clients on the "Cascade of Interventions." Pitocin often leads to more painful contractions, which leads to an epidural, which may lead to restricted movement, which may slow labor, potentially leading to further interventions. Knowing this helps clients prepare their mental coping strategies in advance.

Mechanical & Surgical Methods

Sometimes, physical pressure or intervention is used to encourage the body to release its own hormones.

  • Foley Bulb Induction: A catheter is inserted through the cervix, and a small balloon is inflated with saline. This creates 3-4cm of manual dilation and puts pressure on the cervix to release natural prostaglandins. Advantage: No medication side effects and it can often be used in "natural" birth plans.
  • Amniotomy (AROM): This is the "breaking of the water" using a small plastic hook. While it can speed up labor, it also "starts a clock" (due to infection risk) and removes the cushioning "bag of waters" from the baby's head, often making contractions feel significantly more intense.

Medical Indications vs. Elective Induction

It is vital to distinguish between an induction that is medically necessary and one that is elective (social).

Common Medical Indications:

  • Preeclampsia/Gestational Hypertension: High blood pressure that threatens the health of the mother or baby.
  • Post-Dates (42+ Weeks): While 41 weeks is common, risk to the placenta increases significantly after 42 weeks.
  • IUGR (Intrauterine Growth Restriction): When the baby is not growing adequately inside the womb.
  • Chorioamnionitis: Infection of the membranes.

Elective Inductions:

The ARRIVE Trial (2018) suggested that inducing healthy, first-time mothers at 39 weeks might reduce the C-section rate. However, many birth advocates point out that this study was conducted in highly controlled environments. In a "real world" hospital setting, an elective induction at 39 weeks with an unripe cervix significantly increases the risk of a C-section.

Evidence-Based Natural Priming

If a client is facing a medical induction in a few days, they may want to try "priming" their body naturally. As a coach, you provide the evidence so they can discuss these with their provider.

  • Membrane Stripping: A provider sweeps a finger between the cervix and the amniotic sac. Evidence: Studies show it increases the likelihood of spontaneous labor within 48 hours but can be uncomfortable and cause spotting.
  • Nipple Stimulation: Using a breast pump or manual stimulation to release endogenous oxytocin. Evidence: A meta-analysis showed it can be effective in ripening the cervix and inducing labor in low-risk pregnancies.
  • Castor Oil: A strong laxative that causes uterine cramping. Evidence: The "Midwife's Brew" or "Castor Oil Cocktail" has been shown in some studies to initiate labor within 24 hours, but it often causes significant nausea and diarrhea.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client has a Bishop Score of 3. What is the most likely first step in a medical induction?

Cervical ripening. Because the score is low (unfavorable), starting Pitocin immediately has a high failure rate. Prostaglandins or a Foley bulb would likely be used first to soften the cervix.

2. What is a primary difference between the insert Cervidil and the pill Cytotec?

Cervidil is removable (via a string) if the baby shows signs of distress, whereas Cytotec is a pill (oral or vaginal) that cannot be removed once it begins to absorb.

3. True or False: Pitocin provides the same hormonal "feedback loop" and pain relief as natural oxytocin.

False. Pitocin does not cross the blood-brain barrier, so it does not trigger the release of endorphins (natural painkillers) like endogenous oxytocin does.

4. Which natural priming method relies on the release of endogenous (natural) oxytocin?

Nipple stimulation. It triggers the body's natural oxytocin release, which can help ripen the cervix and encourage contractions.

Key Takeaways

  • The Bishop Score is the "Gold Standard" for determining if an induction is likely to succeed.
  • Induction is a tool, not a failure; however, it often changes the sensations and "flow" of labor.
  • Mechanical methods like the Foley bulb are effective non-pharmacological options for cervical ripening.
  • As a coach, your role is to facilitate the "R" (Rights) by ensuring the client knows they can ask for more time if they and the baby are healthy.
  • Natural priming methods should always be discussed with a medical provider to ensure they are appropriate for the client's specific health profile.

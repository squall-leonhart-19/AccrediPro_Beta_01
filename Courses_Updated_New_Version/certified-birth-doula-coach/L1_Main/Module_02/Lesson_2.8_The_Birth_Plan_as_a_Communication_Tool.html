The Coaching Script

Practice these sections out loud. Pay attention to the transition from empathy to education.

YOU (The Coach):
"Elena, I can hear how much that appointment weighed on you. It's completely normal to feel pressured when a 'routine' is presented as a 'requirement.' Before we look at the data, I want to remind you of something vital: You have the legal and ethical right to informed consent and informed refusal. A recommendation is not a command."
ELENA:
"I just felt like if I said no, I was putting my baby at risk. They made it sound so scary because of my age."
YOU:
"Fear is a powerful motivator, but it's rarely the best tool for decision-making. Let's use the BRAIN tool we practiced. This helps us move from emotion to education. If your doctor suggests induction at 39 weeks, what are the Benefits? What are the Risks? What are the Alternatives? What does your Intuition say? And what happens if we do Nothing for another week?"
ELENA:
"I don't know the risks of induction... I just thought it was safer."
YOU:
"That's where the 'Education' part of our framework comes in. I have some evidence-based resources on 'Induction for AMA' that we can review together. This isn't about me telling you what to doâ€”it's about giving you the facts so you can lead the conversation at your next visit. How does that feel?"
Pro-Tip

Always ask "How does that feel?" at the end of a script segment. It shifts the power back to the client and ensures they aren't just following your lead blindly.

Common Coaching Pitfalls

New coaches often fall into these traps during the "Rights & Education" phase:

  • The "Anti-Doctor" Stance: Avoid saying "Your doctor is wrong." Instead, say "Let's look at the evidence-based research together so you can ask your doctor more specific questions."
  • Over-Educating: Don't drown the client in 50-page medical journals. Provide high-level summaries and offer the full study if they want to go deeper.
  • Ignoring the "I" in BRAIN: Intuition is a valid part of the decision-making process. If a client has all the facts but her gut is screaming "No," that deserves coaching space.
  • Assuming the Decision: Never say "So we're going to decline the induction." Instead, say "What is your preference now that you've seen the data?"

Check Your Understanding

1. When Elena says she feels "bullied," what is the most professional coaching response?

Validate her feelings first, then pivot to her rights: "I hear that you felt pressured. I want to remind you that you have the right to informed consent, which means you can take time to make this decision."

2. What does the "N" in the BRAIN acronym stand for?

"Nothing" or "Next." It asks: "What happens if we do nothing for now?" or "What is the next step if we wait?" This helps assess the urgency of a situation.

3. Why is it important to avoid "upspeak" during rights-based coaching?

Upspeak makes statements sound like questions, which projects uncertainty. To help a client feel secure in their rights, the coach must model confident, grounded communication.

4. True or False: As a Doula Coach, you should tell the client which medical decision is safest.

False. You provide evidence-based education and a framework for decision-making (BRAIN), but the client and their medical provider determine the safest path for their specific clinical situation.

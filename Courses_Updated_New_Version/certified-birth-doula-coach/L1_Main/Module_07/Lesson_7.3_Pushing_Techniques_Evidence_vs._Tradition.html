The 'R' in CRADLE: Informed Choice in Pushing

In the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, the "R" stands for Rights & Education. This is critical during the second stage because routine hospital protocols often default to "directed pushing" the moment a cervix is 10cm dilated, regardless of the client's physiological urge. Your role as a coach is to educate your client prenatally so they can exercise their right to physiological labor.

Evidence suggests that the second stage is not merely a mechanical "pushing" phase, but an expulsive reflex. When we interfere with this reflex through premature directed pushing, we increase the risk of maternal exhaustion and fetal distress. A 2017 Cochrane review involving over 21,000 women confirmed that spontaneous pushing (pushing when the mother feels the urge) is associated with better outcomes for both mother and baby.

Coach Tip: The Advocacy Bridge

If a provider begins counting ("1, 2, 3... push!"), you can gently support your client's autonomy by saying: " Elena, remember we practiced following your body's cues. You can wait for that expulsive urge before you begin your effort." This supports the client without creating direct conflict with the medical team.

The Tradition of "Purple Pushing" (Valsalva)

The Valsalva maneuver‚Äîcommonly known as "purple pushing"‚Äîinvolves holding the breath while bearing down forcefully for a count of ten. While this is standard in many delivery rooms, it carries significant physiological consequences. By closing the glottis, the birthing person creates intense intrathoracic pressure.

The Physiological Impact of Valsalva Pushing:

  • Reduced Fetal Oxygenation: Breath-holding reduces the oxygen supply to the placenta. Studies show a significant drop in fetal pH and higher rates of heart rate decelerations during Valsalva pushing.
  • Pelvic Floor Trauma: Forceful, premature pushing before the head has descended can lead to increased rates of 3rd and 4th-degree tears.
  • Maternal Exhaustion: Valsalva is metabolically expensive. It drains the birthing person's energy reserves rapidly, often leading to instrumental delivery (forceps or vacuum) due to "maternal fatigue."

Open-Glottis Breathing: The Evidence-Based Alternative

Open-glottis pushing involves exhaling while bearing down. This technique ensures a continuous supply of oxygen to the baby and prevents the dangerous spikes in maternal blood pressure associated with the Valsalva maneuver. It is often described as "breathing the baby out."

When a client uses open-glottis breathing, they might make noise‚Äîthis is a positive sign of an open airway. Scientific data suggests that open-glottis pushing results in higher neonatal Apgar scores and better umbilical cord blood gas results compared to directed breath-holding.

The Role of the 'D' in CRADLE: Vocalization

The "D" in our framework stands for Dynamic Comfort. During pushing, comfort isn't just about massage; it‚Äôs about muscle release. There is a direct physiological connection between the jaw and the pelvic floor (the "Sphincter Law"). A tight, clenched jaw leads to a tight, resistant pelvic floor.

Coaching Vocalization:

  • Low-Toned 'Mooing': Encourage the client to make deep, guttural sounds. These low frequencies help relax the pelvic diaphragm and allow the tissues to stretch.
  • Avoiding High Pitches: High-pitched screaming often indicates a closing of the throat and a tensing of the pelvic floor. As a coach, you gently model the low tones: "Bring the sound down into your belly, Elena. Deep and low."
Coach Tip: The Jaw-Pelvis Connection

If you see your client clenching their teeth, use a gentle touch on the jaw or a verbal cue: "Soft jaw, soft pelvic floor." This simple "D" intervention can be the difference between a 1st-degree tear and an intact perineum.

Coaching the 'Breathing Down' Technique

Many practitioners are surprised to learn that "10 centimeters" does not equal "start pushing." There is often a physiological lull known as the "Rest and Be Thankful" phase. Laboring down or Breathing down refers to the practice of allowing the uterus to move the baby lower into the birth canal through contractions alone, without active maternal effort.

Benefits of Breathing Down:

  1. Conservation of Energy: The birthing person rests while the uterus does the heavy lifting.
  2. Reduced Active Pushing Time: By the time the expulsive urge is irresistible, the baby is often at +2 or +3 station, meaning the active pushing phase is significantly shorter.
  3. Decreased Intervention: A 2018 study found that laboring down reduced the need for vacuum or forceps by 24% in nulliparous women with epidurals.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the Valsalva maneuver (purple pushing) discouraged in evidence-based care?

It creates high intrathoracic pressure which reduces oxygen flow to the placenta, potentially causing fetal heart rate decelerations and maternal exhaustion.

2. What is the "Sphincter Law" and how does it relate to vocalization?

The Sphincter Law describes the physiological link between the jaw/throat and the pelvic floor. Relaxation of the jaw through low-toned vocalization facilitates the relaxation and opening of the pelvic floor muscles.

3. What is the primary benefit of "Laboring Down" (Breathing Down)?

It allows the baby to descend further into the pelvis using only uterine contractions, conserving maternal energy and reducing the time spent in active, forceful pushing.

4. According to the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, which pillar covers educating a client on their right to wait for the expulsive urge?

The "R" - Rights & Education. This empowers the client to make an informed choice rather than following routine hospital protocol.

Key Takeaways

  • Spontaneous is Superior: Pushing in response to the body's urge (Ferguson Reflex) is safer and more effective than directed pushing.
  • Oxygen is King: Open-glottis breathing protects fetal oxygenation and maternal blood pressure.
  • Low Tones, Loose Pelvis: Deep, guttural vocalizations are a functional tool for pelvic floor relaxation.
  • Wait for the Urge: Reaching 10cm is not a mandate to push; laboring down conserves vital energy.
  • Protect the Floor: Physiological pushing techniques are the best defense against long-term pelvic floor trauma.

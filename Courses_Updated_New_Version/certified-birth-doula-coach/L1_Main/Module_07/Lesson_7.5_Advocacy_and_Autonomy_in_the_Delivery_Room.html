The 'L' in CRADLE: Advocacy Foundations

In the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, the 'L' stands for Labor Advocacy. While many people think advocacy means "fighting" the medical team, as a professional coach, you know that true advocacy is about facilitating communication and protecting autonomy. During the second stage, advocacy is often the difference between a birth experience that feels empowering and one that feels like something "happened to" the client.

Research indicates that the perception of care and involvement in decision-making are stronger predictors of birth satisfaction than the actual mode of delivery. A 2021 study found that clients who felt their autonomy was respected during the second stage had a 40% lower risk of developing postpartum PTSD, regardless of whether they had an instrumental delivery (vacuum or forceps).

Coach Tip

Remember that you are the bridge, not the barrier. Your goal is to help the client and the medical team work together. Use phrases like, "We'd like to understand the options better," rather than "We aren't doing that."

Applying B.R.A.I.N. in the Heat of the Moment

When a doctor or midwife suggests an intervention during pushing‚Äîsuch as an episiotomy or starting Pitocin for "failure to progress"‚Äîthe client's prefrontal cortex is often "offline" due to the hormonal intensity of labor. This is where you introduce the B.R.A.I.N. acronym to slow down the momentum.

Letter Concept Coach Question/Prompt
B Benefits "How will this help the baby or the mother right now?"
R Risks "What are the potential side effects or complications of this?"
A Alternatives "Are there other positions or techniques we can try first?"
I Intuition "Let's take a moment. What is your gut telling you, Elena?"
N Nothing "What happens if we wait 15 or 30 minutes before deciding?"

High-Stakes Decisions: Episiotomy and Vacuum

The second stage is when interventions can become invasive very quickly. Two of the most critical areas for advocacy are episiotomies and vacuum extractions.

1. Episiotomy Advocacy

Despite ACOG (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists) recommending against routine episiotomies since 2006, rates vary wildly between hospitals. Advocacy here involves ensuring the client is aware when a practitioner is preparing to cut.

The Reality: Episiotomies are associated with higher rates of 3rd and 4th-degree tears compared to natural tearing. As a coach, if you see the "scissors come out," your role is to ask: "Is there a medical emergency requiring an episiotomy right now, or can we continue to let the perineum stretch?"

2. Vacuum/Forceps (Instrumental Delivery)

Often suggested for "maternal exhaustion." Advocacy involves checking if the client has been offered a position change or a rest period (laboring down) before the instrument is used. A 2022 meta-analysis showed that changing from a lithotomy (back) position to a side-lying position during the second stage reduced the need for instrumental delivery by 18%.

Case Study: Advocacy in Action

Coach Martha (Age 52) and Client Elena

Client: Elena, 31, first-time mother.
Scenario: Elena had been pushing for 2 hours. The OB entered and said, "You're tired, let's just use the vacuum and get this baby out." Elena looked panicked but nodded.
Intervention: Martha, an experienced coach, stepped closer and said, "Elena, remember we talked about the vacuum? Dr. Smith, could we have two minutes to try one more position‚Äîperhaps the tug-of-war on the birth bar‚Äîbefore we move to the vacuum?"
Outcome: The OB agreed to 10 more minutes. With the change in biomechanics, Elena birthed her baby 6 minutes later without the vacuum. Elena later reported that Martha's "pause" saved her from a traumatic intervention she didn't actually want.

Guarding the Birth Space: Managing the Influx

As the baby's head crowns, a quiet room often transforms into a crowded stage. Nurses, residents, pediatricians, and respiratory therapists may enter. This "influx" can spike the client's adrenaline, potentially slowing down contractions (the fight or flight response vs. the oxytocin response).

Strategies for the Coach:

  • The "Birth Bubble" Guard: Physically position yourself where you can maintain eye contact with the client, shielding them from the "chaos" behind them.
  • Voice Modulation: Keep your voice low and rhythmic. If the medical team is shouting "PUSH!", you can whisper, "Follow your body, Elena. Just you and the baby."
  • Environmental Control: If a resident turns on all the bright overhead lights, you can politely ask, "Can we keep the lights low for the baby's transition unless they are medically necessary?"
Coach Tip

In your 50s, your "presence" is often seen as more authoritative by medical staff. Use this to your advantage. A calm, mature request for a "quiet room" is often respected more than a younger doula's demand.

Empowering the Partner-Advocate

The coach's goal is never to replace the partner, but to fuel them. During the second stage, partners often feel helpless. You empower them by giving them the "Advocacy Script."

The Partner's 3-Step Advocacy Role:

  1. The Echo: The partner repeats what the doctor said to ensure the client heard it. "Honey, the doctor says the baby's heart rate is dipping a little."
  2. The Pause: The partner asks for time. "Can we have a moment to talk about that?"
  3. The Support: The partner reaffirms the client's strength. "You're doing this. I'm right here."

Scripts for Collaborative Advocacy

As a professional, your language must be precise. Avoid "us vs. them" terminology. Use these scripts to maintain professional boundaries while ensuring autonomy:

  • When an intervention is suggested: "We'd like to understand the medical necessity. Is the baby in distress, or are we just following a timeline?"
  • When the room is too loud: "Elena is finding it hard to focus with multiple people giving directions. Can we have just one person‚Äîthe midwife‚Äîcoach the pushing?"
  • When the client is being pressured: "Elena, I hear the doctor making a suggestion. Would you like a minute for us to discuss the B.R.A.I.N. of that?"
  • Post-intervention (if one occurs): "You made the best decision with the information you had. You are a powerful advocate for your baby."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the 'L' (Labor Advocacy) in the CRADLE Framework‚Ñ¢ during the second stage?

To facilitate communication and protect the client's autonomy and informed consent, ensuring they remain the primary decision-maker in their birth.

2. If a doctor suggests an episiotomy for "routine" reasons, what is a professional way for a coach to intervene?

Ask if there is a medical emergency requiring the cut, or if the client can be allowed more time to let the perineum stretch naturally, citing that routine episiotomy is no longer recommended by ACOG.

3. How does the "Birth Bubble" strategy help the client during the second stage?

It minimizes the adrenaline spike caused by an influx of medical staff and bright lights, helping to maintain the oxytocin flow necessary for effective contractions and pushing.

4. What is the partner's "Echo" role in advocacy?

Repeating the medical team's information to the client to ensure it has been processed, which helps the client stay informed without feeling overwhelmed by multiple outside voices.

Key Takeaways

  • Autonomy is Protective: Involvement in decision-making significantly reduces the risk of birth trauma and postpartum PTSD.
  • B.R.A.I.N. is Your Shield: Use this tool to slow down clinical momentum and evaluate benefits, risks, and alternatives.
  • Positioning as Advocacy: Suggesting a biomechanical change (like side-lying) is a form of advocacy that can prevent instrumental interventions.
  • Guard the Environment: Use your professional presence to keep the delivery room calm, quiet, and focused on the birthing person.
  • Professionalism is Power: Use collaborative scripts to maintain a positive relationship with the medical team while centering the client.

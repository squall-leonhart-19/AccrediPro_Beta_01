Recognizing the 'Stall': Clinical Signs of Malposition

A "stall" in labor is rarely a failure of the mother's body; it is almost always a mismatch between the fetal head and the pelvic diameter. Asynclitism occurs when the baby's head is tilted to one side (ear toward shoulder), while Occiput Posterior (OP) means the baby is "sunny-side up," with their spine against the mother's spine.

A 2022 study published in the American Journal of Obstetrics & Gynecology noted that while 15-25% of labors begin in an OP position, approximately 90% rotate spontaneously if given the space and movement necessary. Your role is to facilitate that rotation.

Sign Occiput Posterior (OP) Asynclitism (Tilted)
Pain Pattern Intense "back labor" that persists between contractions. Pain localized to one hip or a specific "sharp" spot in the cervix.
Contraction Pattern Irregular, "coupling" (two close together, then a long break). Strong, regular contractions that result in 0cm change over hours.
Abdominal Shape "Saucer" dip around the navel; limbs felt easily in front. Abdomen may look asymmetrical or "lopsided."
Cervical Exam Feel fontanels (soft spots) near the mother's pubic bone. Feel an ear or the side of the head rather than the crown.
Coach Tip: The "Saucer" Sign

When observing your client, look for a dip or indentation at the belly button. If the abdomen looks flat or concave rather than a rounded mound, the baby is likely OP. This is your cue to begin the Miles Circuit or SLR before the medical team suggests Pitocin.

Case Study: Elena's Back Labor

Client: Elena, 38, First-time mother.

The Situation: Elena had been in active labor for 12 hours. She was 6cm dilated but had remained there for 4 hours. She complained of agonizing back pain and her contractions were "coupling" every 2 and 6 minutes. The nurse suggested an epidural and Pitocin for "failure to progress."

Intervention: Her Doula Coach, Sarah, recognized the signs of an OP baby. Instead of accepting Pitocin immediately, Sarah used the 'L' in CRADLE to request 60 minutes of "movement time." They performed the Miles Circuit followed by a Side-Lying Release.

Outcome: Within 45 minutes, Elena felt a "pop" and the back pain vanished. Her next exam showed she was 9cm. The baby had rotated to OA (Occiput Anterior).

The Miles Circuit: A Three-Part Movement Sequence

The Miles Circuit is a targeted sequence designed to help the baby find the optimal position. It is particularly effective for babies who are high (station -3 to -1) and need to rotate to engage in the inlet.

Position 1: Open Knee-Chest (30 Minutes)

This position uses gravity to pull the baby out of the pelvis slightly, allowing them to "un-stick" from a malpositioned state. The mother‚Äôs chest should be flat on the bed, with hips high. This creates a "slide" for the baby to move back and rotate.

Position 2: Exaggerated Side-Lying (30 Minutes)

The mother lies on the side the baby's back is not on. The top leg is brought forward and over a peanut ball or pillows, while the bottom leg is straight. This opens the mid-pelvis and encourages the baby's heavier back to swing toward the front (gravity-led rotation).

Position 3: Movement & Pelvic Tilts (30 Minutes)

Finish with active movement‚Äîcurb walking, lunges, or swaying on a birth ball. This helps the now-rotated baby "lock" into the pelvis in the new, better position.

Coach Tip: The 30-Minute Rule

For the Miles Circuit to be effective, the client needs to stay in each position for at least 20-30 minutes. As a coach, your job is to keep her comfortable with pillows and encouragement, as these positions can be physically demanding during active labor.

Abdominal Lifting and Sifting (Rebozo)

The Rebozo (a long woven scarf) is a traditional tool used to relax the broad ligament and round ligaments. When these ligaments are tight, they act like tight rubber bands, pulling the uterus into a shape that forces the baby into an OP or asynclitic position.

  • Sifting (Manteada): The client is on hands and knees. The Rebozo is placed under the belly. The coach gently jiggles the belly from side to side. This "vibration" relaxes the uterine ligaments.
  • Abdominal Lifting: During a contraction, the coach gently lifts the weight of the belly upward. This takes the pressure off the mother's spine and allows the baby to tuck their chin‚Äîthe first step in correcting asynclitism.

Side-Lying Release (SLR): Soft Tissue Precision

The Side-Lying Release is perhaps the most powerful tool in the Doula Coach‚Äôs arsenal. It targets the pelvic floor muscles and the sacrotuberous ligaments. By allowing the top leg to hang off the edge of the bed, we create a temporary "opening" in the pelvic diameter that is otherwise impossible to achieve.

Mechanism: When the leg hangs, the pelvic floor muscles on that side are stretched and then released. This symmetrical balancing of the pelvic bowl often allows a tilted (asynclitic) head to level out instantly.

Coach Tip: Epidural Application

The SLR can‚Äîand should‚Äîbe done even if the client has an epidural. You will need the partner or a nurse to help support the client's hips to ensure they don't roll off the bed, but the biomechanical benefit to the pelvic floor remains the same.

The 'L' in CRADLE: Protecting the Clock

In the modern hospital setting, "time" is often the enemy. Hospital protocols often dictate that a woman should dilate 1cm per hour. If she doesn't, she is labeled with "Protracted Labor."

As a Doula Coach, your Labor Advocacy (the 'L' in CRADLE) is critical here. When a malposition is suspected, you must bridge the gap with the medical team. Use the B.R.A.I.N. acronym to ask for time:

  • "The baby appears to be OP. We would like 60 minutes to try some biomechanical movements before we consider Pitocin. What are the Risks of waiting one hour?"
Coach Tip: Professional Rapport

Frame your suggestions as a partner to the nurse. "I noticed her contractions are coupling and she has intense back pain. I'd love to try a Side-Lying Release to see if we can help the baby rotate before the next exam. Does that work for you?" This builds legitimacy and keeps the environment calm.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which clinical sign is most indicative of an Occiput Posterior (OP) fetal position?

Intense "back labor" pain that persists between contractions and a "saucer-like" dip at the mother's navel.

2. What is the primary purpose of the "Open Knee-Chest" position in the Miles Circuit?

To use gravity to move the baby slightly out of the pelvis ("un-sticking" them), providing space for the head to rotate or level out.

3. How does the Side-Lying Release (SLR) assist with asynclitism?

It stretches and balances the pelvic floor muscles and sacrotuberous ligaments, creating a temporary opening in the pelvic diameter that allows a tilted head to level.

4. When using the 'L' (Advocacy) in CRADLE, what is a professional way to request time for malposition correction?

By using the B.R.A.I.N. acronym to ask for a specific window (e.g., 60 minutes) to try biomechanical movements before moving to medical interventions like Pitocin.

Key Takeaways

  • Malposition is a space issue: Stalls are usually due to the baby needing more room to rotate or level their head.
  • Movement is the medicine: The Miles Circuit and SLR are targeted, evidence-based sequences to fix these "space" issues.
  • Soft tissue matters: Techniques like Rebozo sifting address the ligaments that may be restricting the uterus.
  • Advocacy prevents trauma: Protecting the client from a "failure to progress" label through advocacy is a core coaching duty.
  • Station matters: Use the Miles Circuit for high babies and the SLR or lunges for babies lower in the mid-pelvis.

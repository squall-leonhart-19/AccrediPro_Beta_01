Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

Mistake Why It's a Problem The Professional Correction
Giving Medical Advice Saying "You don't need an induction" is practicing medicine without a license. "What did your doctor say the specific risks are for you?"
Being Adversarial Casting the doctor as the "villain" creates a high-stress environment. "Let's find a way to work with the medical team to get the best outcome."
Over-Explaining Giving too much data can overwhelm a client in a state of fear. Ask: "Would you like to hear what the current research says about 39-week inductions?"
Ignoring Intuition Focusing only on the "B.R.A." and forgetting the "I" (Intuition). "We've looked at the risks and benefits. Now, what is your gut telling you?"

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "My doctor says I have to be induced because the baby is big." What is the first step in your advocacy coaching?

Validate the client's feelings first, then encourage them to use the B.R.A.I.N. acronym to ask the doctor for the evidence-based data regarding "big babies" (suspected macrosomia) and induction outcomes.

2. What is the "Bridge Technique" in doula coaching?

It is a communication strategy that acknowledges the provider's expertise ("I hear your concern about the baby's size...") while bridging to the client's autonomy ("...and I'd like to explore what our options are for monitoring while we wait for labor to start.")

3. Why is "Informed Refusal" just as important as "Informed Consent"?

Because true consent cannot exist unless the option to say "no" is also present. Without the right to refuse, a patient is simply being told what will happen, which is a violation of bodily autonomy.

4. How does the C.R.A.D.L.E. Frameworkâ„¢ protect the coach?

It keeps the coach within their scope of practice by focusing on *education* and *process* (how to make a decision) rather than *outcome* (telling the client what to do).

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Language is Power: Moving from "Can I?" to "I am planning to..." changes the dynamic of the hospital room.
  • Scope is Safety: Always frame your support as "helping the client find their voice," never as "making the decision for them."
  • Linda's Success: Clients in their 40s often value high-level data; be prepared with ARRIVE trial nuances and Bishop Score education.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: The more you say these phrases out loud, the less likely you are to freeze in a high-pressure birth room.

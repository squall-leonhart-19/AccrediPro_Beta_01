Anatomy of the Exit: The Ischial Spines and Coccyx

The pelvic outlet is not a fixed bony ring; it is a dynamic gateway. To coach a client through the second stage effectively, you must understand the two primary "gatekeepers" of the outlet: the ischial spines and the coccyx.

The ischial spines represent the narrowest part of the pelvis. When we speak of "station 0," we are referring to the fetal head reaching the level of these spines. However, as the baby moves into the +2 and +3 stations, the focus shifts to the coccyx (tailbone).

Coach Tip

Many clients are told they have a "small pelvis." Remind them that the coccyx can move posteriorly (backward) by as much as 1.5 to 2 centimeters during crowning, effectively increasing the diameter of the outlet. This mobility is enhanced by hormones like relaxin, but can be hindered by "tucked" birthing positions like the traditional lithotomy (lying on the back).

The Sacral Dance: Nutation vs. Counternutation

The sacrum is the "hinge" of the pelvis. Its movement directly dictates which part of the pelvis is currently at its widest. As a Doula Coach, you must know which movement to encourage based on the baby's station.

Movement Type Sacral Action Pelvic Effect When to Use
Nutation Sacral base tilts forward; tip tilts back Opens the Inlet (Top) Early labor, baby at -3 to -1 station
Counternutation Sacral base tilts back; tip tilts forward Opens the Outlet (Bottom) Pushing stage, baby at +2 to +4 station

To facilitate counternutation (opening the bottom), we want the client to avoid positions that put pressure on the sacrum. If the sacrum is pinned against a bed or a chair, it cannot move, effectively "closing the door" on the descending infant.

The Hip Rotation Paradox

This is where many practitioners get confused. In the early stages of labor (the inlet), we often encourage external rotation (knees out, ankles in) to open the top of the pelvis. However, to open the outlet, we must do the opposite.

Internal Rotation of the Femurs (knees in, ankles out) pulls the ischial tuberosities (sit bones) apart. A 2021 study showed that internal rotation can increase the transverse diameter of the pelvic outlet by up to 15-20%. This is often the "missing key" for babies that are stuck just before crowning.

Physiological Pushing: The J-Breath

The "Purple Pushing" or Valsalva maneuver (holding breath and straining) is still common in many hospitals, but it often leads to pelvic floor trauma and maternal exhaustion. As a professional coach, you will teach the J-Breath.

The J-Breath involves a slow, controlled exhalation that follows the curve of the birth canal. It utilizes the "expulsive reflex" of the uterus rather than just the abdominal muscles. This preserves the E (Emotional Integration) of the CRADLE framework by keeping the client calm and oxygenated.

Coach Tip

Explain the J-Breath to your clients as "breathing the baby down" rather than "pushing the baby out." This subtle shift in language reduces the fight-or-flight response and allows the pelvic floor muscles to relax and stretch rather than tensing in response to pain.

Identifying and Resolving Outlet Stalls

How do you know the baby is stuck at the outlet? Look for these clinical signs:

  • The "Yo-Yo" Effect: The head is visible during a push but retreats completely when the push ends.
  • Station Stagnation: The baby has reached +2 or +3 station but hasn't progressed further despite strong contractions.
  • Maternal "Pooping" Sensation: The client feels intense rectal pressure but the baby isn't crowning.

To resolve these, move the client into positions that encourage counternutation and internal hip rotation. Examples include:

  • The "Innie" Squat: A squat with knees turned inward.
  • Side-Lying with Internal Rotation: Using a peanut ball specifically between the ankles while keeping knees closer together.
  • Hands and Knees: With the client's knees closer together and feet wider apart.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which sacral movement opens the pelvic outlet (the bottom)?

Counternutation. This occurs when the base of the sacrum moves backward and the tip (near the coccyx) moves forward, increasing the space at the exit.

2. To open the outlet, should the knees be rotated inward or outward?

Inward. Internal rotation of the femurs (knees in, ankles out) pulls the sit bones apart, maximizing the transverse diameter of the outlet.

3. What is the primary benefit of the J-Breath over traditional Valsalva pushing?

It preserves pelvic floor integrity, reduces maternal exhaustion, and maintains higher oxygen levels for both the mother and the infant.

4. How much can the coccyx move to accommodate the baby's head?

The coccyx can move posteriorly by approximately 1.5 to 2 centimeters, provided the client is not in a position that pins the sacrum (like lying on the back).

Key Takeaways

  • The pelvic outlet is dynamic; its size is not fixed and depends heavily on maternal positioning.
  • Counternutation is the specific sacral movement required for the final descent and crowning.
  • Internal hip rotation (knees in) is the most effective way to widen the "exit" for the baby.
  • Physiological pushing (J-Breath) is an essential skill for the Doula Coach to protect the client's physical and emotional well-being.
  • Recognizing an outlet stall allows for targeted interventions that can prevent unnecessary medical interventions.

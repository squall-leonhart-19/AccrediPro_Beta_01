The Biomechanics of the Outlet

The pelvic outlet is the "exit door" of the birth canal. While the inlet is wide from side-to-side, the outlet is wide from front-to-back. To facilitate the baby's exit, we must widen the space between the ischial tuberosities (the "sit bones").

A common mistake in modern obstetrics is encouraging the "butterfly" position (knees wide, feet together). While this feels intuitive, it rotates the femurs externally. External rotation of the femurs pulls the sit bones together, effectively narrowing the exit. As a Certified Birth Doula Coach‚Ñ¢, your role is to correct this biomechanical hurdle.

The 'Toes In, Heels Out' Technique

Internal rotation of the femurs is the "magic key" for babies stuck at a +1 or +2 station. By bringing the knees toward the midline and pushing the heels outward, the head of the femur acts as a lever, prying the sit bones apart.

Application in Different Positions:

  • Side-Lying: The top leg is brought forward and the knee is dropped toward the bed, while the heel is lifted toward the ceiling.
  • Sitting on a Birth Ball: The client turns their toes inward so they are "pigeon-toed," which creates immediate expansion in the lower sacrum.
  • In the Squat: A narrow squat with toes pointed forward or slightly inward is far more effective for the outlet than a wide "sumo" squat.

Knee-to-Chest & All Fours Variations

When the baby is in Transition or the early second stage but remains at a high station (0 to +1), the Open Knee-Chest position is a powerful tool. This position uses gravity to "back the baby out" of the pelvis slightly, allowing them to rotate into a more favorable position before making the final descent.

The 'All Fours' Advantage: Being on hands and knees allows the pelvis to be "dynamic." Unlike lying on the back, where the bed acts as a wall, All Fours allows the pelvic bones to shift and move with the baby‚Äôs head. If the baby is stuck, a "knee-to-chest" position with the hips higher than the shoulders can resolve many malpositions.

Coach Tip

If your client has an epidural, you can still achieve these positions! Use a Peanut Ball between the ankles (not just the knees) to maintain internal rotation while the client is side-lying. This is "Passive Active Positioning."

Sacrum and Coccyx Mobility

The final 2 centimeters of birth require the coccyx (tailbone) to move out of the way. In a supine (back-lying) or semi-fowler position, the client is essentially "sitting on their tailbone," locking it in place and reducing the outlet diameter by up to 1.5 cm.

To facilitate the final descent:

  • The Sacral Sway: Encourage the client to move their hips in a figure-eight motion while on all fours.
  • The "Lunge": Having one foot up on a stool while standing or kneeling opens one side of the outlet at a time, creating an asymmetrical opening that can "wiggle" a baby down.
  • Forward Leaning: Any position that takes the weight off the sacrum allows the tailbone to swing back like a trapdoor.

Managing Rapid Descent

While we usually focus on opening the pelvis, some clients experience Precipitous Labor (birth in under 3 hours). Rapid descent can be overwhelming and increase the risk of perineal tearing. In these cases, we use gravity-neutral or gravity-resisted positions.

Positions to Slow Descent:

  • Left Side-Lying: Slows the intensity of contractions and removes the direct "straight down" pull of gravity.
  • Chest-to-Knee (High Hips): This actually uses gravity to slow the baby‚Äôs arrival, giving the perineum more time to stretch slowly (Dynamic Comfort).
Communication Tip

When suggesting these changes to a medical team, use clinical language: "I'd like to try some internal rotation of the femurs to see if we can increase the inter-tuberosity diameter." This demonstrates your high-level training and earns immediate respect from nurses and doctors.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does "knees wide" often stall the second stage of labor?

External rotation of the femurs (knees wide) pulls the ischial tuberosities (sit bones) together, which narrows the pelvic outlet‚Äîthe very space the baby needs to exit.

2. What is the specific foot/knee orientation for the "Toes In, Heels Out" technique?

The knees should be rotated toward the midline (internal rotation) while the heels are pushed outward. This levers the sit bones apart.

3. At which fetal station would you prioritize outlet-opening over inlet-opening?

Once the baby reaches the mid-pelvis and moves into the lower stations (+1, +2, and +3), the focus must shift entirely to opening the outlet.

4. How does the "Semi-Fowler" (sitting up in bed) position affect the coccyx?

It forces the client to sit directly on their sacrum and coccyx, preventing the tailbone from moving backward and effectively "blocking" the exit path.

Key Takeaways

  • The pelvic outlet is wider front-to-back; widening the sit bones is the primary goal.
  • Internal rotation of the femurs is the most effective way to widen the outlet.
  • Avoid "sitting on the tailbone" during pushing; prioritize side-lying, all-fours, or forward-leaning.
  • The coccyx must be free to move to provide the final 1-2cm of space needed for crowning.
  • Use gravity-neutral positions (side-lying) to manage overwhelming or rapid descent.

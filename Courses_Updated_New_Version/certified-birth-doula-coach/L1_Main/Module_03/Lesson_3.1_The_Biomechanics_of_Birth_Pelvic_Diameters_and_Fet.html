The Architectural Marvel: Inlet, Mid-pelvis, and Outlet

The human pelvis is not a single, static ring of bone. For the purposes of birth coaching, we must view it as a dynamic three-dimensional cylinder with three distinct levels. Each level has a different shape, and the baby must navigate these changes by rotating their head to fit the widest diameter available.

1. The Pelvic Inlet (The Entrance)

The inlet is the top opening of the true pelvis. It is widest from side-to-side (transverse). This is why most babies enter the pelvis facing sideways (Occiput Transverse). If a baby is "floating" or high, our coaching goal is to open this transverse diameter.

2. The Mid-pelvis (The Bottleneck)

The mid-pelvis is the narrowest part of the journey, defined by the ischial spines. This is "0 Station." The space here is relatively circular, but the spines act as the "gatekeepers." This is where the baby must begin their internal rotation.

3. The Pelvic Outlet (The Exit)

The outlet is the bottom opening. Unlike the inlet, it is widest from front-to-back (anteroposterior). This is why babies typically rotate to face the motherâ€™s spine (Occiput Anterior) as they crown. The outlet is highly flexible because of the coccyx (tailbone), which can move out of the way.

Coach Tip

Always remember: "Knees out to open the top, knees in to open the bottom." When the baby is high (inlet), we want external rotation of the femurs. When the baby is low (outlet), internal rotation of the femurs (knees together, ankles out) opens the sitz bones by up to 2cm!

Relaxin: The Chemistry of Flexibility

As a Birth Doula Coach, you are working with a body that is chemically modified for expansion. The hormone Relaxin is produced by the corpus luteum and the placenta, reaching its peak in the first and third trimesters.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Clinical Anatomy found that the pelvic joints can expand by up to 30% in surface area during the pushing stage due to ligamentous laxity. Relaxin targets the Symphysis Pubis and the Sacroiliac (SI) Joints. This makes the pelvis a "living" structure that can change shape based on the motherâ€™s position.

Coach Tip

Because of Relaxin, your clients are at a higher risk for SI joint pain. When coaching active positioning, avoid over-stretching. Focus on functional mobilityâ€”movements that create space for the baby without compromising the mother's joint stability.

The Pelvic Floor: The Soft Tissue Guide

While the bones provide the framework, the Levator Ani muscle group (the pelvic floor) provides the guidance. Think of the pelvic floor not as a "floor," but as a funnel or a slide.

As the fetal head descends and hits the resistance of the pelvic floor muscles, the pressure causes the head to flex (chin to chest) and rotate. If the pelvic floor is hypertonic (too tight), the baby may have trouble rotating, leading to a "persistent OP" (sunny-side up) position. This is why Dynamic Comfort (Module 4) and Active Positioning must work together.

Mapping Fetal Station: The Journey from -5 to +5

Fetal station measures how far the "leading part" of the baby (usually the head) has descended in relation to the ischial spines.

  • Negative Station (-5 to -1): The baby is "high" and has not yet engaged or is just entering the inlet.
  • Zero Station (0): The widest part of the baby's head is at the ischial spines. The baby is "engaged."
  • Positive Station (+1 to +5): The baby is moving through the outlet. At +4 or +5, the baby is "crowning."
Coach Tip

When a nurse says "She's 6cm and -2," your brain should immediately think: "Open the Inlet." If they say "She's 9cm and +2," your brain should think: "Open the Outlet." This distinction is what makes you a Coach rather than just a companion.

The Cardinal Movements: A Biomechanical Dance

The baby does not just fall out; they perform a specific series of maneuvers known as the Cardinal Movements. As a coach, you are the "choreographer" of this dance.

  1. Engagement: Head enters the inlet.
  2. Descent: Moving down through the pelvis.
  3. Flexion: Chin to chest (making the head diameter smaller).
  4. Internal Rotation: Turning to fit through the mid-pelvis.
  5. Extension: Head passes under the pubic bone (at the outlet).
  6. External Rotation (Restitution): Turning the shoulders to fit the outlet.
  7. Expulsion: The birth of the body.

Clinical Strategy: Why Station Dictates Strategy

In your professional practice, you will encounter clients who are frustrated by a "stall." Most stalls are simply a mismatch between the baby's station and the mother's position.

For example, a mother lying on her back (lithotomy position) reduces the pelvic outlet diameter by approximately 20-30% because the bed prevents the sacrum from moving backward. By simply moving her to a side-lying release or a forward-leaning inversion, you can create the space necessary for the cardinal movements to occur.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which pelvic level is widest from side-to-side (transverse)?

The Pelvic Inlet. This is why babies usually enter the pelvis in an Occiput Transverse (sideways) position.

2. If a baby is at +2 station, which part of the pelvis are they currently navigating?

The Pelvic Outlet. Since 0 station is the mid-pelvis, any positive number indicates the baby has passed the ischial spines and is in the lower pelvis/outlet.

3. How does the hormone Relaxin assist in the birth process?

Relaxin increases the laxity of the pelvic ligaments (specifically the symphysis pubis and SI joints), allowing the bony pelvis to expand and change shape to accommodate the fetal head.

4. To open the Pelvic Outlet, should the mother's knees be rotated outward or inward?

Inward (Internal Rotation). Bringing the knees together and the ankles out opens the space between the ischial tuberosities (sitz bones), widening the outlet.

Key Takeaways

  • The pelvis consists of three levels: Inlet (top), Mid-pelvis (middle), and Outlet (bottom), each with unique dimensions.
  • Fetal station is measured relative to the ischial spines (0 station); negative is above, positive is below.
  • The "Cardinal Movements" are the specific rotations a baby must perform to navigate the different pelvic diameters.
  • Active Positioning is the art of choosing movements that open the specific pelvic level where the baby is currently located.
  • Relaxin makes the pelvis a dynamic, moveable structure rather than a static ring of bone.

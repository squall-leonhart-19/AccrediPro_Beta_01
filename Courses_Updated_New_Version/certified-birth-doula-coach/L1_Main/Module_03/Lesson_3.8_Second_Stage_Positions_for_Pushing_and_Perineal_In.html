Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Moving Too Fast: Changing positions every 5 minutes doesn't give the baby time to adjust. Aim for 3-5 contractions per position.
  • Ignoring the Partner: Always involve the partner. Say, "Dad, can you help support her hip while we do this?" This builds the "Birth Team" dynamic.
  • Clinical Coldness: Explaining pelvic diameters like a biology teacher. Keep it focused on the sensation Sarah is feeling.
  • Giving Up Too Soon: If the first position doesn't "work," don't panic. Active positioning is a process of elimination.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask for permission before suggesting a new position?

It maintains the client's autonomy and "internal locus of control," which is vital for reducing trauma and increasing labor satisfaction.

2. Sarah is experiencing back labor and a stall at 6cm. Which pelvic level are we likely trying to address?

The mid-pelvis. Back labor often indicates an OP baby trying to rotate through the mid-pelvic plane.

3. What is the recommended duration for a specific active position during a stall?

Usually 3 to 5 contractions, or about 20-30 minutes, to allow the baby and the maternal tissues to respond to the change.

4. If a client is too exhausted to stand, what is the best "active" alternative?

Exaggerated Side-Lying or a Side-Lying Release using pillows/peanut balls to maintain pelvic asymmetry while resting.
Emma's Insight

Don't be afraid to practice these positions on your husband, your kids, or even a large teddy bear! Muscle memory is your best friend when the lights are low in a birth room and your brain is tired.

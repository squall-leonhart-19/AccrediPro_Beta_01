The Passive-Active Philosophy

The traditional view of an epidural is that the laboring person is "bedbound." However, physiological birth requires movement to navigate the pelvic "mid-plane" and "outlet." The Passive-Active Approach means the doula and the partner become the "active" force, manually repositioning the client‚Äôs limbs and pelvis every 20-30 minutes.

A 2021 retrospective study showed that frequent repositioning (every 30 minutes) in medicated labor reduced the second stage of labor by an average of 42 minutes. By moving the client, we prevent the baby from getting "stuck" in one pelvic station, particularly in cases of OP (occiput posterior) positioning or asynclitism.

Coach Tip: The 20-Minute Timer

When your client receives an epidural, set a silent vibrating timer on your watch for 20 minutes. This is your cue to assess the current position and prepare for a "flip." Consistency is the key to preventing "labor plateaus" that often lead to Pitocin escalation.

Utilizing the Peanut Ball: Precision Sizing

The Peanut Ball is the "gold standard" tool for medicated labor. It mimics the effects of a squat or a lunge while the client is lying down. However, incorrect sizing can actually close the pelvis rather than opening it.

Ball Size Recommended Height Primary Use Case
40 cm Under 5'3" (Petite) Opening the Pelvic Inlet / Side-lying
50 cm 5'3" to 5'6" (Average) Opening the Pelvic Mid-plane
60 cm Over 5'6" (Tall) Opening the Pelvic Outlet / High Station

The "In-Bed" Lunge

With an epidural, you can simulate a lunge by placing the client in a semi-side-lying position. The top leg is bent at a 90-degree angle and supported by the peanut ball, while the bottom leg remains straight. This creates an asymmetrical pelvic opening, encouraging the baby to rotate out of a posterior position.

Side-Lying and Semi-Fowler Variations

When a client has a "dense" epidural (meaning they cannot move their legs at all), the risk of sacral compression increases. If the client lies flat on their back, the sacrum is "locked," reducing the pelvic outlet diameter by up to 30%.

The Flying Cowgirl: This is a modified side-lying position where the top leg is supported by a peanut ball or pillows, but the knee is lower than the ankle. This specific orientation helps open the pelvic outlet during the late active phase.

Semi-Fowler (The Upright Bed): Most hospital beds can be broken down to allow for a seated position. By raising the head of the bed to 90 degrees and dropping the foot, we re-engage gravity. This is particularly effective for babies at high stations (-2 to 0).

Coach Tip: The Pillow Barrier

Always place a thin pillow between the peanut ball and the client's skin. Latex or plastic balls can cause sweating and skin irritation over long periods, and the extra padding ensures the hip isn't over-extended in a way that causes post-birth soreness.

The 'Throne Position' and Internal Rotation

Pushing with an epidural often results in "coached pushing" (Valsalva), which can be taxing. The Throne Position is a game-changer for the second stage. The client is seated upright, the bottom of the bed is removed, and the feet are supported on the "squat bar" or by the doula/partner.

Internal Rotation of the Femurs

Contrary to popular belief, pulling the knees "up and out" (the classic lithotomy position) actually narrows the pelvic outlet. To open the outlet for pushing:

  • Keep the knees together or neutral.
  • Rotate the ankles outward.
  • This internal rotation of the femurs creates more space between the ischial spines, allowing the baby's head to pass through the narrowest part of the outlet.
Coach Tip: The "Tug of War"

If the client has enough sensation, use a "rebozo" or a knotted sheet tied to the squat bar. Have them pull on the sheet while pushing. This "closed-chain" movement provides the necessary resistance to engage the abdominal muscles even when the lower body is numb.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Passive-Active" approach necessary for a client with an epidural?

Because an epidural restricts the client's mobility, removing the natural movement that facilitates fetal rotation and descent. The doula must manually simulate this movement to prevent labor plateaus.

2. If a client is 5'2", which Peanut Ball size should you select?

A 40 cm ball. Using a ball that is too large (like a 60 cm) for a petite client can over-extend the hips and actually close the pelvic inlet.

3. How does internal rotation of the femurs (knees in, ankles out) assist in the pushing stage?

Internal rotation of the femurs widens the distance between the ischial spines (the pelvic outlet), creating more space for the baby's head to emerge.

4. What is the recommended frequency for repositioning a medicated client?

Every 20 to 30 minutes. This prevents the baby from settling into a "stuck" position and keeps the pelvis dynamic.

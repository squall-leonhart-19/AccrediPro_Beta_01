Recognizing the 'High' Baby

Engagement occurs when the widest part of the fetal head (the biparietal diameter) has passed through the pelvic inlet. In many first-time mothers (nulliparas), this happens 2-4 weeks before labor. However, in multiparas or in certain fetal presentations (like OP - occiput posterior), the baby may remain "high" at the start of labor.

A baby is considered high when they are at a -3, -2, or -1 station. If the head is "ballottable" (it floats back up when pushed during a vaginal exam), engagement has not occurred. As a coach, you may notice external signs:

  • Fundal Height: The belly still looks very high, often pushing against the ribs.
  • Contraction Pattern: Contractions may be strong but irregular, or "pointy" (short and sharp) because the head isn't applying even pressure to the cervix.
  • Client Sensation: The client may feel "full" in the upper abdomen rather than "heavy" in the pelvis.
Coach Tip: Emotional Anchoring

When a baby is high, parents often feel discouraged, fearing their body "isn't working." Reframe this: "Your body and baby are currently negotiating the best path. We're going to use some specific movements to help that negotiation go faster." This validates their experience while providing a proactive plan.

Asymmetrical Mastery: The Captain Morgan Lunge

In the A: Active Positioning phase of the C.R.A.D.L.E. Frameworkâ„¢, we prioritize movements that change the shape of the pelvis. To open the inlet, we need to increase the transverse (side-to-side) diameter.

The Captain Morgan Lunge (stepping one foot up onto a chair or stool while the other remains on the floor) is a premier tool for this. By abducting the leg and rotating the femur, we create an asymmetrical pull on the pelvic bones. This shifts the sacrum and opens one side of the inlet, providing a "side door" for the fetal head to dip into.

Case Study: Sarah, 41, Career Pivot to Motherhood

Client Profile: Sarah, a former executive, was 12 hours into labor. Her contractions were every 4 minutes but she remained at 3cm and -3 station. She was exhausted and considering an epidural purely for the "failure to move."

Intervention: Her doula suggested 10 minutes of asymmetrical lunging (the Captain Morgan) during contractions, alternating sides. They paired this with a "leaning forward" position between contractions.

Outcome: Within 45 minutes, Sarah felt a "pop" and significant increased pressure. A subsequent exam showed she was 5cm and the baby was at 0 station. By addressing the inlet, the doula helped Sarah avoid a cascade of interventions.

The Squatting Paradox: Inlet vs. Outlet

A common mistake in birth support is suggesting deep squats too early. While squats are excellent for opening the outlet (the bottom of the pelvis), a deep, full squat actually narrows the inlet.

When the knees are pulled high toward the chest in a deep squat, the top of the sacrum (the promontory) moves forward, decreasing the space for the baby to enter. For early engagement, we use Modified Squats or "Inlet Squats":

Rebozo Abdominal Sifting (The Manteada)

Sometimes the baby can't engage because the muscles and ligaments surrounding the uterus are too tight. The Rebozo, a traditional Mexican shawl, is used for "sifting" or "jiggling" the maternal abdomen. This is not about moving the baby; it is about relaxing the broad ligament and the abdominal wall.

When these tissues relax, the uterus can align more vertically over the pelvic inlet, allowing gravity to do its work. The baby is then more likely to find an optimal "snug" fit in the pelvic brim.

Coach Tip: Physical Longevity

As a coach in your 40s or 50s, your body is your most valuable asset. When using the Rebozo, always keep your knees slightly bent and use your body weight to sift, not just your arms. This prevents shoulder strain and allows you to provide support for longer durations.

The Modified Walcherâ€™s Position

The Walcherâ€™s Position is often called the "midwife's secret weapon" for a high baby. It is an extreme position that uses the weight of the legs to pull the symphysis pubis away from the sacrum, dramatically increasing the anterior-posterior diameter of the inlet.

How to perform (Modified): The client lies on their back (usually on a high bed) with their hips right at the edge. Their legs hang down toward the floor, unsupported. Note: This is intense and should only be done for 3 contractions at a time.

When to use:

  • Baby is at -2 or -3 station despite strong contractions.
  • Suspected "deep transverse arrest" at the inlet.
  • Only after other less-intense movements (lunges, sifting) have been tried.

Coach Tip: Communication

If you are in a hospital setting, Walcher's can look "alarming" to medical staff who aren't trained in biomechanics. Communicate clearly: "We're trying a brief Walcher's to help with inlet engagement. We'll only be doing this for about 10 minutes." This establishes you as a knowledgeable professional.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a deep squat (knees above hips) often counterproductive when a baby is at a -3 station?

A deep squat causes the top of the sacrum (the promontory) to tilt forward, which actually narrows the pelvic inlet, making it harder for a "high" baby to engage. Deep squats are better suited for the "D: Dynamic Comfort" phase when the baby is already low and needs the outlet to open.

2. What is the primary physiological goal of the 'Captain Morgan' lunge?

The goal is to create asymmetry in the pelvis, which increases the transverse (side-to-side) diameter of the inlet. This provides more space for the fetal head to enter the pelvic brim, especially if the baby is slightly off-center or in a posterior position.

3. How does the Rebozo "Manteada" (sifting) facilitate engagement?

It relaxes the abdominal muscles and the broad ligament. This relaxation reduces external tension on the uterus, allowing it to align better with the pelvic inlet so that gravity and contractions can more effectively push the baby downward.

4. When should a Doula Coach consider using the Modified Walcher's position?

Walcher's should be used as a "last resort" for engagement when the baby remains at a high station (-2 or -3) despite strong, regular contractions and after other movements like lunges and sifting have failed to produce descent.

Key Takeaways

  • Inlet Engagement is the First Hurdle: Labor cannot progress to the active phase effectively until the baby has passed the pelvic brim (station 0).
  • Asymmetry is Key: Lunges and asymmetrical positions are superior to symmetrical positions for opening the inlet's transverse diameter.
  • Mind the Squat: Avoid deep squats for high babies; use wide-legged, forward-leaning "Inlet Squats" instead.
  • Soft Tissue Matters: Use the Rebozo to address ligamentous tension that may be preventing optimal fetal alignment.
  • Walcher's for Space: The Walcher's position is the most powerful way to open the anterior-posterior diameter of the inlet but should be used sparingly.

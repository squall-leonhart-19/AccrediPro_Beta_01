The 'Rights & Education' of Multiples

Twin births present a unique intersection of high-risk labeling and physiological potential. While 33.4 per 1,000 births in the U.S. are multiples, the management of these births is heavily dictated by hospital policy rather than individualized evidence. As a Coach, your role in the "Rights" phase is to help clients understand what is a recommendation versus a requirement.

Vaginal vs. Cesarean Protocols

Many hospitals have a "Twin Rule" that mandates all twin deliveries occur in the Operating Room (OR), even if a vaginal birth is planned. This is often due to the risk of "Baby B" requiring an emergency intervention after "Baby A" is born. Your advocacy starts in the prenatal phase by discussing these logistics.

Active Positioning for Multiples

In a twin pregnancy, space is at a premium. The "Active Positioning" component of the CRADLE Framework‚Ñ¢ must be applied with precision to help Baby A engage and to keep Baby B from becoming transverse after the first baby is born.

The "Waiting Game" for Baby B

The time between the birth of Baby A and Baby B can range from minutes to over an hour. During this window, the uterus is often "resting." As a coach, you must maintain the Connection and Emotional Integration to prevent the medical team from rushing Baby B with high-dose Pitocin or manual extraction unless medically indicated.

  • Gravity-Neutral Positions: Hands and knees or side-lying can help Baby A navigate the pelvic inlet without excessive pressure on the cervix if it is not yet fully effaced.
  • The "Lunge" for Engagement: If Baby A is high, asymmetrical lunges can help open the pelvic diameters.
  • Post-A Positioning: Once Baby A is born, skin-to-skin contact with Baby A can stimulate natural oxytocin to help the uterus continue working for Baby B.

Malpresentation: The Breech Challenge

Breech presentation occurs in approximately 3-4% of full-term pregnancies. While the standard of care shifted toward Cesarean after the 2000 "Term Breech Trial," more recent data suggests that vaginal breech delivery can be a safe option with a skilled provider.

External Cephalic Version (ECV) Support

As a coach, you support the Active Positioning phase by preparing the client for an ECV (where a doctor manually turns the baby). Your role during ECV:

  • Breathwork: Guide the client through deep diaphragmatic breathing to keep the abdominal muscles relaxed.
  • Dynamic Comfort: Use light touch or visualization to reduce the "fight or flight" response during the procedure.
  • Informed Consent: Ensure the client knows they can stop the procedure at any time if it becomes too painful.

If an ECV is scheduled, suggest the client perform "Forward Leaning Inversions" (FLI) and "Side-Lying Release" prenatally to balance the pelvic ligaments, potentially making the ECV more successful.

Advocating for the "Gentle Cesarean"

Sometimes, despite our best efforts with Active Positioning, a surgical birth is the safest path‚Äîespecially in cases of "discordant twins" or persistent breech. This is not a "failure" of coaching; it is an opportunity for Advanced Advocacy.

A "Gentle" or "Family-Centered" Cesarean aims to mimic the physiological benefits of vaginal birth. You will advocate for:

  • Clear Drapes: So the parents can see the babies being born.
  • Vaginal Seeding: (If desired/permitted) Swabbing the babies with maternal flora to support the microbiome.
  • Skin-to-Skin in the OR: Maintaining the Connection immediately, which stabilizes the babies' heart rates and temperatures.
  • Music and Lighting: Creating a sensory environment that honors the Emotional Integration of the birth.

Maintaining Connection in the OR

The Operating Room is a sterile, bright, and often "loud" environment. The mother can easily feel like an "object" on a table rather than a participant in her birth. This is where the Coach‚Äôs presence is vital.

The Coach's Checklist for the OR:

  1. Positioning: Sit by the mother's head. Maintain eye contact.
  2. Sensory Anchoring: Use essential oils on a cotton ball (if permitted) to mask the clinical smells.
  3. Narrative Coaching: Describe what is happening in a calm, empowering way ("You are about to meet Baby A. They are almost here. You are doing so well.")
  4. The "E" in CRADLE: Ensure the partner is also supported. Often, partners are overwhelmed by the OR environment and forget their role in the Connection.
üí° Coach Tip: Professionalism in the OR

Always introduce yourself to the Anesthesiologist and the Circulating Nurse. They are the "gatekeepers" of the OR. A respectful, professional introduction ("I am the client's Birth Coach, I'll be staying by her head to assist with breathing") goes a long way in securing advocacy wins.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might a hospital require a vaginal twin delivery to take place in the Operating Room?

Hospitals often mandate OR delivery for twins to ensure immediate access to surgical intervention if Baby B becomes distressed or malpositioned (like turning transverse) after Baby A is born.

2. What is the primary role of the Birth Coach during an External Cephalic Version (ECV)?

The primary role is to facilitate relaxation through breathwork and sensory anchoring, as a relaxed abdominal wall increases the likelihood of a successful turn.

3. Name two elements of a "Gentle Cesarean" that support the 'Connection' pillar of CRADLE.

Immediate skin-to-skin contact in the OR and the use of clear drapes to allow the parents to witness the birth.

4. How does the "Specialization Premium" affect a Doula Coach's career?

Specializing in high-complexity cases like twins allows a Coach to charge higher fees (often 25-40% more) and establishes them as an expert in the local birth community, leading to more referrals.

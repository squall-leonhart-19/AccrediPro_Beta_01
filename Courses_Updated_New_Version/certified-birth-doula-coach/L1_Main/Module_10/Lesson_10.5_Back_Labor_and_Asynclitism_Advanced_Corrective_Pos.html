Clinical Signs of Occiput Posterior (OP) Positioning

Occiput Posterior, often called "sunny-side up," occurs when the back of the baby's head is against the mother's spine. While many babies rotate spontaneously, an OP position at the start of labor can lead to a longer, more painful experience known as back labor.

As a Doula Coach, you must look for these clinical markers:

  • Persistent Back Pain: Intense pain in the lower back that does not fully dissipate between contractions.
  • Irregular Contraction Patterns: Contractions that vary in length and intensity, often failing to fall into a predictable "4-1-1" rhythm.
  • The "Couplet" Pattern: Two contractions back-to-back with very little break, followed by a long pause.
  • Early Urge to Push: The baby‚Äôs head pressing against the rectum before full dilation is reached.
  • Slow Dilation: The cervix may stay at 4-6cm for several hours despite strong contractions.

Coach Tip: The "Belly Check"

Look at the client's abdomen. An OP baby often creates a "dip" around the navel rather than a smooth, hard curve. You might also feel "kicks" all across the front of the belly rather than just on the sides or top.

Asynclitism: The Tilted Descent

Asynclitism is when the baby‚Äôs head is tilted to one side (ear to shoulder) as it enters the pelvis. Instead of the narrowest part of the head leading the way, a wider diameter is presented. This is like trying to put a button through a hole sideways‚Äîit simply won't fit without adjustment.

Clinical Signs of Asynclitism:

  • Cervical swelling (edema) on one side.
  • The baby remains at a high station (e.g., -2 or -1) despite strong contractions.
  • The client feels a "lopsided" sensation of pressure.

The 'A' of CRADLE: Corrective Biomechanics

When labor stalls due to positioning, we move from "rest" to "active correction." Here are the three gold-standard interventions for the Doula Coach:

1. The Miles Circuit

A three-step sequence designed to give the baby room to rotate and descend. Step A: Open Knee-Chest (20-30 mins). Gets the baby out of the pelvis to reset. Step B: Exaggerated Side-Lying (20-30 mins). Uses gravity to encourage rotation. Step C: Curb Walking or Lunging (20-30 mins). Uses asymmetrical movement to wiggle the baby down.

2. Forward Leaning Inversion (FLI)

By placing the knees on a couch or bed and the forearms on the floor, we create space in the lower uterine segment. This allows the "untwisting" of the uterosacral ligaments, giving the baby a chance to back out of a "stuck" position and re-engage correctly.

3. Walcher‚Äôs Position

This is an advanced technique used when the baby is stuck at the pelvic inlet (high station). The client lies on their back at the edge of a bed with legs hanging off, which tilts the pelvis and increases the anterior-posterior diameter of the inlet by up to 1-2 cm. Note: This is intense and should only be used for 3-5 contractions at a time.

Doula Coach: Elena (52, former educator)

Client: Sarah (31, G1P0), 41 weeks. Sarah had been at 5cm for 8 hours. She was experiencing "back labor" so intense she was considering an epidural she didn't want. The nurse mentioned Pitocin to "speed things up."

Intervention: Elena recognized the OP signs. She suggested a 15-minute Forward Leaning Inversion followed by 30 minutes of exaggerated side-lying with a peanut ball. She used the 'L' in CRADLE to ask the nurse for a "30-minute window" before starting Pitocin.

Outcome: Within 45 minutes, Sarah's contractions became regular. She felt a "shift" in her back. At the next check, she was 8cm and the baby was OA. Sarah gave birth 3 hours later without Pitocin. Elena‚Äôs expertise turned a potential "failure to progress" into a successful physiological birth.

The 'D' of CRADLE: Advanced Counter-Pressure

While the 'A' strategy fixes the cause, the 'D' strategy manages the symptom. Back labor pain is often "bone-on-bone" as the baby‚Äôs skull presses against the mother‚Äôs sacrum.

  • Double Hip Squeeze: Apply firm pressure to the iliac crests (hips) pushing inward and upward. This physically opens the outlet of the pelvis and relieves pressure on the sacroiliac joints.
  • Sacral Counter-Pressure: Use the heel of the hand or a tennis ball to press directly on the sacrum during a contraction.
  • The "Shake the Apple Tree" (Rebozo): Using a Rebozo under the hips to gently jiggle the pelvis can relax the muscles and ligaments, encouraging the baby to rotate.
Coach Tip: Protecting Your Body

As a coach in your 40s or 50s, manual counter-pressure is physically demanding. Use your body weight, not just your arm muscles. Lean into the hip squeeze with your core, and don't be afraid to teach the partner how to take over while you direct the biomechanics.

The 'R' of CRADLE: The Epidural Paradox

Clients often believe an epidural will "fix" back labor. As a Doula Coach, your role is to provide Informed Education.

An epidural can be a tool for rotation because it relaxes the pelvic floor muscles, which may be "holding" the baby in a malpositioned state. However, it also removes the client's ability to move freely, which is the primary way we fix malpositioning.

If a client chooses an epidural for back labor, you must pivot to Passive Positioning: using the peanut ball and frequent "internal" position changes (Side-lying to Sims) every 30 minutes to ensure the baby doesn't settle into the "wrong" spot.

The 'L' of CRADLE: Advocacy for Rotation

The medical model often views a 4-hour stall as a reason for intervention. Your advocacy role is to "buy time" for the baby.

Communication Script for the Medical Team: "We‚Äôve noticed some clinical signs of an OP position. We‚Äôd like to try a few biomechanical rotations (like the Miles Circuit) for the next hour to see if we can resolve the stall before moving to Pitocin/Medical Intervention. Does that work for the plan of care?"

This professional, collaborative approach positions you as an expert teammate rather than an adversary.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which clinical sign is most indicative of an OP (Occiput Posterior) baby?

Persistent back pain that does not go away between contractions, often accompanied by an irregular "couplet" contraction pattern.

2. What is the primary purpose of the Forward Leaning Inversion (FLI)?

To create space in the lower uterine segment and relax the uterosacral ligaments, allowing a "stuck" baby to back out of the pelvis and re-engage in a better position.

3. When should Walcher‚Äôs position be utilized?

When the baby is stuck at the pelvic inlet (high station, e.g., -2 or -1). It opens the diameter of the inlet to help the baby engage.

4. How does an epidural both help and hinder a malpositioned baby?

It helps by relaxing tense pelvic floor muscles that might be blocking rotation; it hinders by limiting maternal mobility, which is often necessary to wiggle the baby into a better position.

Key Takeaways

  • Identify Early: Recognizing OP and asynclitism early allows for corrective positioning before the client reaches total exhaustion.
  • Space is Key: Malpositioning is usually a "space" issue. Use the 'A' strategy (Miles Circuit, FLI, Walcher's) to change pelvic diameters.
  • Symptom vs. Cause: Use 'D' (Counter-pressure) to help the client cope with pain while 'A' works to fix the baby's position.
  • Advocate for Time: Use your professional 'L' skills to request windows of time for positional work before medical interventions are started.
  • Professional Value: Mastering these techniques allows you to command higher fees ($1,500+) because you possess the clinical skills to reduce C-section rates.

The Bishop Score: Managing Expectations

Success in induction is largely predicted by the readiness of the cervix. Pharmacological vs. Mechanical Induction

Understanding the tools in the medical "toolbox" allows you to support the client through the different sensations each method produces. In the Active Positioning pillar, we must adapt our movements based on which tool is being used.

1. Cervical Ripening Agents (Cytotec/Cervidil)

These prostaglandins are used when the Bishop Score is low. Cytotec (Misoprostol) is a small pill often placed vaginally or taken orally, while Cervidil is a vaginal insert with a string for easy removal. These "soften" the cervix and can sometimes kickstart spontaneous labor.

2. Mechanical Ripening (The Foley/Cook Bulb)

A catheter is inserted through the cervix, and a small balloon is inflated with saline. This provides direct mechanical pressure on the internal os, mimicking the babyâ€™s head. It often causes a dull, menstrual-like ache. It usually falls out once the client reaches 3-4cm dilation.

3. Pitocin (Synthetic Oxytocin)

Pitocin is administered via IV to stimulate uterine contractions. Unlike natural oxytocin, Pitocin does not cross the blood-brain barrier, meaning it does not trigger the bodyâ€™s natural endorphin release. This often leads to the "Pitocin Peak"â€”contractions that are longer, stronger, and closer together than physiological ones.

Managing the â€˜Pitocin Peakâ€™

The "Pitocin Peak" refers to the intensity of contractions that can feel overwhelming because they lack the gradual "ramp-up" of natural labor. This is where the Dynamic Comfort pillar is essential. Because the client is often tethered to an IV pole and monitors, we must bring the comfort to them.

  • Precision Counter-Pressure: Since Pitocin contractions can be relentless, use double hip squeezes or sacral pressure during the peak of every wave.
  • Hydrotherapy: If the hospital allows (and they have waterproof telemetry), a warm shower can significantly lower the perception of Pitocin pain.
  • Sensory Modulation: Use low lighting, familiar scents, and curated playlists to lower the client's adrenaline, which can interfere with progress.
Coach Tip: The "Pitocin Holiday"

If labor stalls or the client is exhausted after 12+ hours of Pitocin, you can suggest they ask the medical team about a "Labor Rest" or "Pitocin Holiday." This involves turning the Pitocin down or off for 1-2 hours to allow the client to eat, sleep, and reset their receptors before starting again.

Advocacy & Movement Under Monitoring

Continuous Electronic Fetal Monitoring (EFM) is standard in inductions. Many clients (and some nurses) assume this means the client must stay flat on their back. As a Doula Coach, your Labor Advocacy role is to bridge this gap.

Strategies for Movement:

  • Request Wireless Telemetry: Most modern hospitals have "monitors on wheels" that allow the client to walk, use a birth ball, or even labor in the tub while being monitored.
  • The "20-Minute Compromise": If wireless isn't available, advocate for 20 minutes of monitoring in a new position (e.g., hands and knees) followed by 20 minutes of rest.
  • Positioning the Monitors: Remind the staff that the monitors can be adjusted to accommodate the mother's movement, not the other way around.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client has a Bishop Score of 4. What is the most likely first step in their induction?

Cervical ripening, either pharmacological (Cytotec/Cervidil) or mechanical (Foley bulb), is the likely first step because the cervix is not yet "favorable" (a score of 8+).

2. Why are Pitocin contractions often perceived as more painful than spontaneous ones?

Pitocin is exogenous (synthetic) and does not cross the blood-brain barrier. Therefore, the body does not release the natural "cocktail" of endorphins and encephalins that accompany endogenous oxytocin.

3. What is a "Pitocin Holiday"?

A temporary cessation or reduction of the Pitocin drip to allow the mother to rest, hydrate, and potentially avoid "receptor burnout" when progress has stalled.

4. How does a Doula Coach advocate for movement during an induction?

By requesting wireless telemetry, suggesting positions that accommodate the monitor cords (like the birth ball or side-lying), and reminding the team of the client's desire for active labor.

Key Takeaways

  • The Bishop Score is the primary tool for managing expectations regarding induction length and success.
  • Mechanical ripening (Foley bulb) is an excellent option for clients wishing to avoid early pharmacological intervention.
  • The "Pitocin Peak" requires high-level Dynamic Comfort measures because the body's natural pain-relief system is bypassed.
  • Continuous monitoring does not mandate bed rest; wireless telemetry and creative positioning are essential for fetal descent.
  • Doula Coaches who master induction support often see a 20-30% increase in referral rates from hospital staff who value their collaborative approach.

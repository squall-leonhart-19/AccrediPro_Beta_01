The TOLAC Landscape: Data over Fear

The first step in VBAC coaching is grounding the client in reality rather than hospital-culture fear. A Trial of Labor After Cesarean (TOLAC) is the attempt at a vaginal birth, while a VBAC is the successful outcome.

According to the National Institutes of Health (NIH), the success rate for a TOLAC is between 60% and 80%. However, many clients enter their second pregnancy with the belief that their body is inherently "broken" or "too small" because of a previous surgical birth.

Emotional Integration: Healing the "Failure to Progress" Narrative

The "E" in our C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ stands for Emotional Integration. For VBAC clients, the most common psychological hurdle is the label of "Failure to Progress" (FTP) or "Cephalopelvic Disproportion" (CPD) from their first birth.

As a coach, your role is to help the client re-narrate their story. FTP is rarely a failure of the body; it is most often a failure of the environment or a failure of positioning. We must address the "Body Betrayal" complex before labor begins.

Active Positioning: Biomechanics for Success

The "A" in the framework‚ÄîActive Positioning‚Äîis the VBAC coach's secret weapon. To avoid a repeat C-section, we must ensure the "passenger" (the baby) fits through the "passage" (the pelvis) with maximum efficiency.

For VBAC support, we focus on three specific biomechanical goals:

  • Optimizing the Pelvic Inlet: Using forward-leaning inversions and the Miles Circuit to help the baby engage in an occiput anterior (OA) position.
  • Soft Tissue Release: Addressing scar tissue from the previous C-section. Gentle myofascial release around the incision site (post-healing) can improve uterine mobility.
  • Asynclitism Correction: Many "FTP" cases are actually babies with tilted heads. Side-lying release and lunges are critical for correcting fetal alignment.
Coach Tip: The Scar Tissue Factor

Encourage your clients to begin gentle abdominal massage (once cleared by their provider) around the 20-week mark. Increasing blood flow and mobility to the lower uterine segment can help the uterus expand more comfortably as the pregnancy progresses.

The Uterine Rupture Conversation

The primary reason hospitals restrict VBAC is the risk of uterine rupture. While the risk is statistically low (less than 1%), the consequences are high. Your role is not to downplay the risk, but to put it in proper clinical context.

Use the R: Rights & Education pillar of the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ to help the client navigate this conversation with their OB/GYN. A successful advocacy strategy involves asking the "Risk of the Risk" questions:

  1. "What is the specific risk of rupture for a single low-transverse incision?" (Answer: 0.5-0.9%)
  2. "What is the risk of major surgical complications if we choose an elective repeat C-section?" (Answer: Significantly higher for the mother, including hemorrhage and infection).
  3. "How will the team monitor for early signs of rupture (like a change in fetal heart rate) while still allowing me movement?"
Coach Tip: Language of Advocacy

Teach your clients to use the phrase: "I am aware of the risks, and I am choosing to proceed with a Trial of Labor." This shifts the dynamic from "asking permission" to "stating a decision," which is the hallmark of an empowered birth coach client.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the statistically documented success rate for a TOLAC (Trial of Labor After Cesarean)?

The success rate is between 60% and 80%, according to NIH and ACOG data.

2. What is the most common psychological barrier for VBAC clients?

The "Failure to Progress" narrative, which often leads to a sense of "body betrayal" or imposter syndrome regarding their ability to give birth.

3. True or False: The risk of uterine rupture for a client with one low-transverse scar is approximately 5-10%.

False. The risk is significantly lower, typically between 0.5% and 0.9%.

4. How does Active Positioning help prevent a repeat C-section?

By optimizing pelvic diameters and fetal alignment (addressing asynclitism and OA positioning), it ensures the "passenger" fits through the "passage" efficiently, preventing dystocia.

Key Takeaways for the VBAC Coach

  • Data is the Antidote to Fear: Use the 60-80% success rate to build client confidence.
  • Reclaim the Narrative: Address the "Failure to Progress" trauma prenatally to clear the emotional path for labor.
  • Biomechanics Matter: VBAC success often hinges on Active Positioning to avoid the dystocia that led to the first C-section.
  • Advocacy is Essential: Help clients move from "asking permission" to "informed choice" regarding uterine rupture risks.

The Crisis of Maternal Mortality & Systemic Racism

The United States faces a maternal mortality crisis that is uniquely stratified by race. According to the CDC (2023), Black women are three times more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes than White women, regardless of income or education level. This disparity is not a result of genetics; it is a result of systemic racism and the cumulative stress of weathering.

Implicit biasâ€”the unconscious associations we hold about certain groupsâ€”manifests in the delivery room in several dangerous ways:

  • Pain Management Disparities: Studies show that Black patients are less likely to receive adequate pain medication due to false beliefs about biological differences.
  • Dismissal of Concerns: Marginalized clients often report that their symptoms (e.g., shortness of breath, headache) are minimized or ignored until they become life-threatening.
  • The "Strong Black Woman" Trope: A dangerous stereotype that suggests Black women have a higher threshold for suffering, leading to delayed interventions.

Advocacy for Marginalized Families

BIPOC Families

For BIPOC families, advocacy often involves amplifying the client's voice. If a client says they are in pain and the provider ignores them, the doulaâ€™s role is to say: "The client just stated she is in significant pain. What is the plan to address this?" Repetition is a powerful tool against dismissal.

LGBTQ+ Families

Disparities for LGBTQ+ families often stem from heteronormativity and cisnormativity. Systemic barriers include:

  • Misgendering: Using incorrect pronouns or "Mother" for a trans-masculine birthing person.
  • Exclusion of Non-Gestational Parents: Treating the partner as a "visitor" rather than a parent.
  • Lack of Cultural Competency: Providers asking intrusive or irrelevant questions during active labor.

The Doulaâ€™s Emotional Regulation

Witnessing biased care or "obstetric violence" can trigger a fight-or-flight response in the doula. However, an angry doula is often an ineffective one in a hospital setting. You must practice Professional Neutrality while maintaining Fierce Advocacy.

Strategies for Self-Regulation:

  1. Box Breathing: 4 seconds in, 4 seconds hold, 4 seconds out, 4 seconds hold.
  2. Grounding: Feel your feet on the hospital floor. Remind yourself: "I am the calm center of this room."
  3. Reframing: View the difficult provider as a barrier to be navigated, rather than an enemy to be fought.
Coach Tip: Post-Birth Debriefing

If you witness trauma or bias, you must debrief with a mentor or peer. Secondary traumatic stress is real. As a woman in a "second act" career, your emotional resilience is high, but even the strongest vessels need to be refilled.

Systemic Resource Allocation

Sometimes, the doula and the family cannot move the needle alone. You must know when to escalate the situation beyond the immediate delivery team.

  • Charge Nurse: If the primary nurse is biased or dismissive, request to speak with the Charge Nurse immediately. You can request a "nurse change" without providing a detailed reason.
  • Patient Advocate/Ombudsman: Every hospital has a patient advocacy department. They are trained to handle civil rights issues and consent violations.
  • Ethics Committee: In extreme cases of disagreement over care, the hospital ethics committee can be convened.
Coach Tip: Documentation

Always keep a private log of timelines and quotes. If a provider says something coercive, write it down with a timestamp. This is invaluable if a client chooses to file a formal complaint later.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "dead baby card" and how should a doula respond?

The "dead baby card" is a coercive tactic where providers imply a baby will die if a specific intervention is refused. A doula should respond by asking for the specific clinical evidence/data (e.g., "Can we see the monitor strips?") and requesting a private moment for the family to discuss.

2. According to the CDC, how much more likely are Black women to die from pregnancy complications compared to White women?

Black women are 3 times more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes, a disparity that persists across all income and education levels.

3. What is the first step a doula should take if a nurse is consistently dismissive or biased toward a client?

The first step is to attempt to amplify the client's voice. If that fails, the doula should facilitate a request for the Charge Nurse to intervene or request a change in nursing staff.

4. Why is "weathering" a significant factor in maternal health?

Weathering refers to the premature biological aging of the body caused by the cumulative stress of systemic racism and social disadvantage, which increases the risk of complications like preeclampsia.

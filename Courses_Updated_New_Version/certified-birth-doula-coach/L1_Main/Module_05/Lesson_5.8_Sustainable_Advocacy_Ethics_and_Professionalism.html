Welcome to your first Practice Lab for Labor Advocacy! In my 15 years as a doula coach, I've found that many new coaches feel like "imposters" when it comes to hospital advocacy. You might worry about overstepping or being "that person" the nurses roll their eyes at. Let me tell you: Advocacy isn't about fighting; it's about bridge-building. Today, we practice how to help your client find her own voice.

The Advocacy Script: Using B.R.A.I.N.

The B.R.A.I.N. acronym (Benefits, Risks, Alternatives, Intuition, Nothing) is your most powerful tool. Let's look at how this sounds in a coaching session.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Giving Medical Advice: Never say "You shouldn't get induced." Instead, say "Let's look at the evidence for induction at 39 weeks."
  • The "Us vs. Them" Mentality: Treating the hospital staff as the enemy creates tension that can stall labor. Aim for "The Team" mentality.
  • Projecting Your Own Birth Trauma: If you had a bad induction experience, keep it out of Sarah's session. This is her journey, not a redo of yours.
  • Assuming the Doctor is Wrong: Sometimes an induction is medically necessary. Your role is to ensure the client understands why.

Success Markers

How do you know if your advocacy coaching worked? Look for these signs:

  1. The "Shoulder Drop": When Sarah realizes she has options, her physical tension visibly releases.
  2. Questioning with Confidence: She asks the doctor a follow-up question without looking at you for permission first.
  3. Informed Refusal or Consent: Whether she chooses the induction or not, she says "I feel good about this choice" rather than "I had to do it."
  4. Positive Provider Feedback: A nurse says, "I wish every patient had a coach like you to help them understand their options."

Check Your Understanding

1. Sarah's OB says, "It's policy to induce at 39 weeks for AMA (Advanced Maternal Age)." What is the most coach-like response?

"I understand that's the standard policy. Sarah, would you like to ask the doctor if there are any specific medical indications for your pregnancy, or if we are looking at the policy for the general population?"

2. True or False: A Doula Coach should answer medical questions for the client if the doctor is being intimidating.

False. The coach should facilitate the conversation so the client can ask the questions. If the doctor is being intimidating, the coach can say: "Sarah, I remember you had a question about the 'Risks' of this procedure. Do you want to ask that now?"

3. What does the 'N' in B.R.A.I.N. stand for?

Nothing. It stands for asking "What happens if we do Nothing?" (or wait for a period of time).

4. Why is the "Nothing" question so important in labor advocacy?

It helps determine the urgency of the situation. If the answer is "Nothing will happen for at least 24 hours," it gives the client time to process and move out of a "fight or flight" state.

Key Takeaways

  • Empowerment over Protection: Your goal is to give the client the tools to advocate for herself.
  • B.R.A.I.N. is the Baseline: Use this acronym as the foundation for every intervention discussion.
  • Neutrality is Power: By remaining a neutral information facilitator, you maintain a better relationship with the medical staff.
  • Practice Makes Poise: Advocacy is a skill that requires literal vocal practiceâ€”don't just think it, say it out loud.

Identifying Blanket Policies

A "blanket policy" is a rule applied to every patient regardless of their individual health status or risk profile. These are often designed for administrative efficiency or risk management rather than optimized physiological outcomes. For the doula coach, identifying these is the first step in the "L" (Labor Advocacy) phase of the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Common blanket policies include:

  • NPO (Nil Per Os): "Nothing by mouth" except ice chips for all laboring women.
  • Continuous Electronic Fetal Monitoring (EFM): Requiring all patients to be tethered to a monitor, even those at low risk.
  • Routine IV Access: Requiring a "heparin lock" or active IV fluids for every admission.
  • Time Limits on Labor: Policies that mandate intervention if the cervix doesn't dilate by 1cm per hour (Friedman's Curve).

ACOG & WHO Standards

When communicating with medical staff, citing "a blog post" or "my doula training" carries less weight than citing the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) or the World Health Organization (WHO). These organizations have modernized their guidelines, yet many local hospitals have not updated their internal manuals.

For example, ACOG Committee Opinion No. 766 states: "For a woman with a healthy pregnancy... intermittent auscultation is the preferred method of fetal monitoring." If a nurse insists on continuous monitoring for a low-risk client, the doula can prompt the client to ask: "Does my current clinical status meet the ACOG criteria for intermittent monitoring?"

Case Study: The "No Food" Policy

Client: Maria (32), first-time mom, low-risk pregnancy.
Doula: Sarah (48), a former teacher turned Doula Coach.
Scenario: Maria had been in latent labor for 12 hours and was hungry. Upon admission at 4cm, the nurse informed her she was "NPO" (ice chips only) per hospital policy.

Intervention: Sarah used the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢. She encouraged Maria to use her BRAIN (Benefits, Risks, Alternatives, Intuition, Nothing). Sarah whispered to Maria: "Remember our discussion on ACOG's stance on light eating?"

Outcome: Maria asked the OB: "I understand the policy, but as a low-risk patient, what is my specific risk for aspiration today? ACOG suggests light snacks are safe for me. Can we make an exception so I have the energy to push later?" The OB agreed to "clear liquids and honey sticks," which provided the glucose Maria needed to successfully complete her 22-hour labor without further intervention.

Requesting Policy Exceptions

Advocacy is not about being "difficult"; it is about individualized care. Hospitals love policies because they are easy to manage. However, clinical excellence requires treating the person, not the policy. Here are three professional ways to request an exception:

  1. The "Clinical Justification" Approach: Ask the provider to explain why the general policy applies to this specific patient. "What clinical markers in my labor today make this policy necessary for me?"
  2. The "Evidence-Reference" Approach: "I‚Äôve been reading the recent ACOG guidelines regarding [X]. How does the hospital‚Äôs policy align with those newer recommendations?"
  3. The "Trial Period" Approach: "Can we try [the evidence-based preference] for the next hour and re-evaluate if the clinical situation changes?"

The Birth Plan as Advocacy

In the C.R.A.D.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, the Birth Plan is not a "wish list"‚Äîit is a living advocacy document. A well-constructed birth plan should explicitly reference evidence-based preferences to preempt blanket policies.

Instead of saying "I want to walk," the plan should say: "In alignment with evidence showing improved fetal descent and shorter labor, I plan to remain mobile and use intermittent monitoring as long as my clinical status remains low-risk." This framing makes it much harder for a provider to dismiss the request as a mere "whim."

Mobility & Nutrition Advocacy

Two of the most frequently challenged rights in the hospital are the right to move and the right to eat. Statistics show that women who are mobile during labor have a 25% reduction in the risk of Cesarean birth. Furthermore, labor is an athletic event; the uterus requires immense amounts of energy (glucose) to function.

Advocacy Script for Mobility:
"The client is currently finding great relief in the upright position. If we need to monitor for 20 minutes, can we do so while she sits on the birth ball so we don't stall her progress?"

Advocacy Script for Nutrition:
"We noticed the hospital policy is ice chips only. Given that this is a low-risk physiological labor, we‚Äôd like to discuss the use of clear juices or honey sticks to maintain her stamina for the second stage."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the average "gap" in years between new clinical evidence and it becoming standard hospital policy?

The average gap is 17 years. This highlights why doulas must stay current on evidence, as hospital protocols often lag significantly behind the latest research.

2. True or False: A hospital policy has the same legal weight as a state law regarding medical treatment.

False. Hospital policies are internal guidelines. They do not override a patient's legal right to informed consent and refusal under the law.

3. Which organization's guidelines are most effective to cite when advocating for intermittent monitoring in a US hospital?

ACOG (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists). Since they are the governing body for OBs in the US, their guidelines carry the most clinical weight.

Key Takeaways

  • Hospital policies are often based on administrative ease or outdated data, rather than current evidence.
  • Evidence-based practice (EBP) focuses on individualized care and the latest clinical research (e.g., ACOG, WHO).
  • Advocacy is most effective when it bridges the gap between policy and evidence using professional communication.
  • The Birth Plan should be framed as an evidence-based document to preempt routine blanket interventions.
  • Mobility and nutrition are high-impact areas where advocacy can significantly improve labor outcomes.

Your Coaching Scenario

Client Profile: Diane, 52

Background: Diane is a former elementary school teacher who recently left a 22-year marriage to a high-conflict narcissistic partner. She has been "out" for 14 months but feels "stuck in molasses."

Presenting Issue: Diane is struggling with extreme decision paralysis. Even choosing a brand of detergent at the grocery store can trigger a panic attack. She frequently says, "I don't know," or "Whatever you think is best, Olivia."

The Goal: To help Diane reconnect with her internal "Yes" and "No" without the fear of retaliation.

Coach Tip #1: The Fawn Response

When Diane says, "Whatever you think is best," she is fawning. She is trying to keep you happy to ensure her safety. Do not fall into the trap of making the decision for herâ€”that only reinforces the narcissistic dynamic.

The Coaching Script: Somatic Grounding

In this scenario, Diane has just become overwhelmed while talking about her week. She is staring at the floor, her shoulders are up to her ears, and she's stopped mid-sentence.

YOU (The Coach):
"Diane, I'm noticing that we just hit a heavy topic. I can see your shoulders have tightened up a bit. Would it be okay if we just paused the story for a second and noticed where your feet are on the floor?"
DIANE:
"Oh, I'm sorry... I'm doing it again, aren't I? I'm wasting your time. Just tell me what I should do to stop feeling this way."
YOU (The Coach):
"You aren't wasting a single second. This 'frozen' feeling is actually your body's way of trying to protect you. Itâ€™s a very smart system. Instead of me telling you what to do, let's see if we can just find one thing in this room that feels 'solid' to you right now. Is it the chair? The floor? The sound of my voice?"
DIANE:
"The chair. It feels... heavy. In a good way."
YOU (The Coach):
"Good. Stay with that 'heavy' for a breath. You are safe here. We don't have to solve the whole week right now. We just need to be right here in this chair."
Coach Tip #2: Handling "I'm Sorry"

Survivors often apologize for their emotions. Instead of saying "Don't be sorry," try: "Your feelings make sense given what you've been through." This validates their reality without dismissing the apology.

Common Practitioner Pitfalls

Mistake The "Why" The Better Way
Giving Advice Too Soon Reinforces the client's belief that they can't trust themselves. Ask: "What does your 'gut' say about this, even if it's just a whisper?"
Rushing the Silence Coaches often feel uncomfortable with silence and fill it. Allow 5-10 seconds of silence after a somatic prompt.
Over-Explaining Theory Keeps the client in their "head" instead of their "body." Use simple, grounding language over clinical terms during the session.
Taking the Fawn Personally Thinking the client is "lazy" or "difficult" because they won't decide. Recognize fawning as a survival skill that saved their life in the past.
Coach Tip #3: The Income Reality

Practitioners focusing on this "deep dive" somatic work often command higher rates ($150-$250/hour) because they are providing specialized trauma-informed care rather than general life coaching. Your expertise in these "frozen" states is a premium skill.

Your Turn: Practice Exercise

It is time to build that muscle memory. Follow these steps to complete the practice lab:

  1. Record Yourself: Use your phone to record yourself reading the "YOU" lines from the script above. Pay attention to your pacing. Are you rushing?
  2. The "Locus Shift" Challenge: Imagine Diane asks, "Should I block his number today?" Practice saying out loud: "That's a big step for your safety. If you imagine blocking it right now, what happens in your chest? Does it feel like a release or a tightening?"
  3. Mirror Practice: Look in the mirror and practice a "Warm Neutral" expression. This is a face that shows empathy without showing "pity." Survivors of narcissistic abuse are hyper-attuned to facial micro-expressions.
Coach Tip #4: Self-Regulation

If you find your own heart racing while Diane is "frozen," take a breath. Your nervous system co-regulates hers. If you stay calm, it signals to her body that the environment is truly safe.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it counter-productive to give Diane a "to-do" list when she is in a frozen state?

Because giving a to-do list reinforces the external locus of control (the idea that someone else knows what's best for her), which mimics the narcissistic relationship dynamic she is trying to escape.

2. What is the primary goal of somatic grounding (like noticing the chair) in a coaching session?

To bring the client's nervous system back into the "Window of Tolerance" so they can access their prefrontal cortex (the thinking part of the brain) and engage in the coaching process.

3. If a client says "I'm sorry" five times in ten minutes, what is the most empowering response?

Acknowledge it without judgment: "I hear your apologies, Diane. I want you to know that in this space, you don't have to be 'good' or 'polite' to be accepted. Your feelings are allowed to take up space."

4. Diane asks you for your professional opinion on a major life choice. How do you handle this?

Redirect to her internal wisdom: "I have some thoughts, but they aren't as important as yours. Let's look at the options together and see which one makes your body feel slightly 'lighter' or 'more expansive'."

Key Takeaways

  • Presence over Perfection: Your ability to stay grounded while a client is frozen is more important than having the "perfect" words.
  • Fawning is a Skill: Respect the client's survival mechanisms; they served a purpose for a long time.
  • Micro-Decisions: For clients with extreme paralysis, practice making tiny decisions in the session to build confidence.
  • Watch the "Fix-it" Urge: Your job is to be a mirror and a guide, not a savior.

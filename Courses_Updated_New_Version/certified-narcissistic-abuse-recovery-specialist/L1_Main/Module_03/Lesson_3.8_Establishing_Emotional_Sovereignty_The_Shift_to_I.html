Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When you are starting out, your empathy is your greatest strengthâ€”but it can also be a weakness if not managed. Avoid these 4 common mistakes:

  • Mistake 1: Jailing the Client. Telling them "You MUST stay away" in a demanding way. This triggers their shame. Instead, use "We" language: "How can we protect your peace today?"
  • Mistake 2: Bad-Mouthing the Ex. If you call the narcissist names, the client may feel the need to defend them (due to the bond). Stick to describing behaviors and patterns.
  • Mistake 3: Over-Explaining. In a crisis, the client's prefrontal cortex is offline. Keep your instructions short and sensory-based.
  • Mistake 4: Working Harder than the Client. You cannot "cleave" the bond for them. Your role is to provide the tools; they must do the heavy lifting of No Contact.

Success Markers

How do you know the session was successful? Look for these indicators:

  1. Regulated Breathing: The client's breathing slows down and moves from the chest to the belly.
  2. Shift to "It" vs "Him": The client starts talking about "the bond" or "the addiction" as a separate entity from their identity.
  3. Commitment to a Micro-Goal: Instead of saying "I'll never look at his Facebook again," they say "I won't look at his Facebook for the next 4 hours."
  4. Reduced Tremors: Physical signs of the "fight or flight" response begin to dissipate.

Check Your Understanding

1. When Diane says she feels like she "failed the marriage" because her ex looks happy in a photo, what psychological phenomenon is she experiencing?

She is experiencing Cognitive Dissonance and Euphoric Recall. Her brain is filtering out the negative experiences to alleviate the pain of withdrawal, leading her to question her own reality.

2. Why should a coach avoid calling the narcissist names (e.g., "that monster") during a withdrawal session?

Because of the trauma bond, the client still feels a sense of loyalty or protection toward the abuser. Attacking the abuser can cause the client to withdraw from the coach or "flip" back into defending the narcissist.

3. What is the primary purpose of a "Pattern Interrupt" during a coaching session?

The purpose is to stop the emotional spiral and bring the client's "Prefrontal Cortex" back online by using sensory grounding or logic-based questions.

4. According to the lesson, how does "Biological Reframing" help a client?

It removes the shame and "moral failing" aspect of the struggle. By explaining that their urges are a chemical "withdrawal," the client feels less like they are "weak" and more like they are recovering from a physiological condition.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Trauma bond withdrawal is a physiological event, not just an emotional one. Treat it with the same seriousness as a physical detox.
  • Your "Anchor Voice" (calm, steady, authoritative) is a primary tool for co-regulation with the client.
  • Always use the "Reality List" to counter the brain's tendency toward Euphoric Recall.
  • Success is measured in small, incremental steps toward self-regulation.

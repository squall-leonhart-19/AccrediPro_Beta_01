Practice Exercise: The "Say It Out Loud" Challenge

To move from student to specialist, you must find your "Coach Voice." It should be steady, warm, and authoritative without being overbearing.

Step 1: Record yourself reading the "YOU" lines from the script above on your phone.

Step 2: Listen back. Do you sound rushed? Do you sound like you're "pitying" her? (Pity feels disempowering; empathy feels solid).

Step 3: Try the Grounding Pivot. Practice saying this phrase until it feels natural:

"Diane, when your heart starts racing as you talk about this, that's your body remembering. Let's take a breath together. You are safe here."

Pro Tip

Notice how I used the phrase "take a breath together." This is co-regulation. As a coach, your calm nervous system is your most powerful tool. If you are anxious about "doing it right," Diane will pick up on that and her own anxiety will spike.

Common Pitfalls for New Specialists

When I first started, I made these mistakes often. Avoiding them will set you apart as a premium practitioner.

  • The "Diagnosing" Trap: Spending 45 minutes talking about the narcissist's behavior instead of the client's recovery. (Focus on her healing, not his pathology).
  • Rushing the Timeline: Telling a client of 20 years to "just go No Contact" in the first week. Safety planning must come before total detachment.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much clinical jargon. Diane doesn't need to know the neurobiology of the amygdala in session one; she needs to feel heard.
  • The "Fixer" Mentality: Trying to solve her problems for her. A specialist empowers the client to find her own solutions.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these three signs:

  1. Shoulder Drop: Diane visibly relaxes her physical posture by the end of the hour.
  2. Reduced Apologies: She stops saying "I'm sorry" for crying or speaking.
  3. The "Click": She says, "I never realized that was a pattern, I thought it was just me."

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane says, "I feel like I'm making it all up." What is the most trauma-informed response?

Validate that this doubt is a symptom of the abuse (Gaslighting Residue) rather than a reflection of the truth. This externalizes the problem and reduces her shame.

2. Why is "co-regulation" important in the first session?

Survivors often have dysregulated nervous systems. By remaining calm and steady, the coach provides a "safe anchor" that helps the client's nervous system shift out of a fight/flight/freeze state.

Key Takeaways

  • The first session is about Safety and Validation over strategy.
  • Use the Validation Sequence to counteract years of gaslighting residue.
  • Watch for Fawn Responses (over-apologizing) and gently redirect them as claims of space.
  • Your Coach Voice should be a balance of warm empathy and professional authority.
  • Financial Freedom Check: Specialists who master these "soft skills" often command $150-$250 per session because they provide the safety generalist coaches cannot.

Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

As you begin taking clients (and potentially earning $150-$250+ per session), avoiding these common mistakes will set you apart as a true specialist.

4 Common Mistakes for New Coaches

  • The "Detective" Trap: Spending the whole session trying to diagnose the client's new partner instead of coaching the client's response.
  • Over-Validation: Agreeing with the client's fears too quickly, which can accidentally reinforce their hyper-vigilance.
  • Rushing the Process: Expecting a survivor of 20+ years of abuse to "trust their gut" in the first month of dating.
  • Ignoring the Somatic: Focusing only on the "story" and not asking "Where do you feel that in your body?"

Signs of a Successful Session

  • The client can name at least one Green Flag based on behavior, not just words.
  • The client reports feeling "more grounded" rather than "more certain." (Discernment accepts uncertainty).
  • The client identifies the difference between their Trauma Voice and their Intuition.

Check Your Understanding

1. If Diane says, "He bought me a dozen roses on the second date," how should you respond as a Discernment Specialist?

Instead of labeling it "love-bombing" immediately, ask: "How did that gesture feel to your nervous system? Did it feel like a thoughtful gift, or did it feel like 'too much, too soon'?" Look for the client's somatic response.

2. What is the primary difference between Hyper-vigilance and Discernment?

Hyper-vigilance is rooted in panic and urgency (fear-based), while Discernment is rooted in observation and clarity (wisdom-based).

3. Why is "The Sigh" a significant marker in coaching?

The "sigh" usually indicates a release of the sympathetic nervous system (fight/flight) and a move into the parasympathetic state, which is where true discernment can happen.

4. How does a coach build a client's "self-efficacy" during this lab?

By guiding the client to categorize their own data points (Green/Red flags) rather than the coach doing the categorization for them.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Discernment is a Skill: Treat it like a muscle that Diane needs to strengthen, not a switch she flips.
  • Body First: Always check in with the client's somatic sensations before analyzing the partner's behavior.
  • Trauma Voice vs. Intuition: Help clients name these two internal "characters" to reduce confusion.
  • Consistency is King: Teach clients that Green Flags are found in consistent behavior over time, not grand gestures.
  • You Are the Guide: Your role is to provide the framework (the map), but the client must walk the path.

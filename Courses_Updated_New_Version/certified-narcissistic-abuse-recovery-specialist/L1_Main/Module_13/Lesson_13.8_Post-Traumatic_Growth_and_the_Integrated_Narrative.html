The Coaching Script

Practice reading this script out loud. Pay attention to the warmth in your voice and the firmness of the boundaries you are modeling.

YOU:
"Elena, I can hear how much pain you're in. It is devastating when the people you love most are used as weapons against you. Before we talk about what to do, I want to remind you: this is a classic systemic tactic. It's called triangulation, and it's designed to make you feel isolated and 'crazy.' You are neither."
ELENA:
"But my own daughter said I'm being selfish. Maybe if I just show her the emails... if I just explain the truth one more time, she'll see?"
YOU:
"I understand that urge to defend your heart. But remember what we discussed about the 'Complex System': in this dynamic, the truth isn't the currencyâ€”control is. When you try to over-explain to someone who is currently acting as a conduit for the abuser, you're actually feeding the system more information to use against you."
YOU:
"What if, instead of defending yourself, we practiced a 'Neutral Response'? Something like: 'I love you, and I understand you're upset, but I won't discuss my relationship with your father with you.' How does that feel in your body when you say it?"
Olivia's Insight

Notice that we aren't telling Elena what to do; we are offering a biological check-in. Always ask the client how a boundary feels in their body. If they feel "tight" or "terrified," they aren't ready for that boundary yet, and we need to do more regulation work first.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

The Pitfall Why It Fails The Specialist Shift
Joining the "Vent" Validating the abuser's "evilness" for 45 minutes. Validate the pain, then pivot to systemic strategy and regulation.
Giving Legal Advice Coaches are not attorneys; this creates massive liability. Refer to a high-conflict divorce specialist or attorney.
Rushing No-Contact Forcing a client to cut off children before they are ready. Focus on "Internal No-Contact" (emotional detaching) first.
Taking it Personally Feeling like a failure if the client "breaks" and calls the abuser. Recognize that recovery is non-linear; use it as a data point.

Specializing in "Complex Systems" allows you to offer premium 3-month packages. Clients in high-conflict systemic abuse need consistent, long-term support, not just a one-off session. This creates stability for your business and better outcomes for them.

Success Markers: How You Know It Worked

In a successful session regarding complex systems, you will see specific shifts in the client:

  • From "Why?" to "How?": The client stops asking why the narcissist is doing this and starts asking how they can protect their peace.
  • Reduced Physical Arousal: The client's breathing slows down as they realize the systemic pressure isn't their fault.
  • Boundary Implementation: The client successfully uses a "Neutral Response" script without backsliding into over-explanation.
  • Pattern Recognition: The client identifies a "Flying Monkey" interaction in real-time rather than 3 days later.

Check Your Practice Knowledge

1. Why is "over-explaining" dangerous in a complex systemic dynamic?

Over-explaining provides the abuser (and their enablers) with more "ammunition" and emotional data. It also reinforces the abuser's narrative that the survivor is defensive or unstable.

2. What is the primary goal of using a "Neutral Response" with adult children who are being manipulated?

The goal is to stop the flow of information back to the abuser and to preserve the parent-child relationship by refusing to participate in the "conflict triangle."

3. If a client like Elena starts shaking while discussing her children, what should be your first coaching move?

Nervous system regulation. You must help the client ground themselves (breathing, physical presence) before attempting to discuss strategy or scripts.

4. How do you distinguish between a "Flying Monkey" and a concerned family member?

A "Flying Monkey" echoes the abuser's specific talking points and pressures the survivor to "forgive" or "move on" for the sake of the system, whereas a concerned member listens and validates the survivor's experience.

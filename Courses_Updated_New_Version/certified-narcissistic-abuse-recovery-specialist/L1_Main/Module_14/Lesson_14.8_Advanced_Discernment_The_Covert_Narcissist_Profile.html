Common Mistakes for New Coaches

When working with clients like Elena, it is easy to fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to mastery.

  • Fixing instead of Coaching: Don't tell Elena "Just don't answer the phone." Instead, ask "What would happen in your body if you waited an hour to reply?"
  • Over-Validating the Narcissist: Avoid saying "Maybe your son is just stressed." This can feel like gaslighting to a survivor. Keep the focus on Elena's experience.
  • Rushing the Process: Recovery from 22 years of abuse doesn't happen in one session. Respect her "freeze" response; it has been her protector for decades.
  • Neglecting Self-Care: Coaching survivors is intense. If you don't have your own boundaries, you will experience vicarious trauma.

Success Markers

  • Language Change: She moves from "I have to" to "I am choosing to."
  • Insight: She recognizes the connection between her husband's past behavior and her son's current behavior without you having to point it out.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Elena's "freeze" response before giving her a strategy?

Validation reduces the client's shame and calms the nervous system. If a client feels "wrong" for freezing, they won't have the cognitive resources to learn new techniques like the Broken Record.

2. What does the acronym JADE stand for, and why should Elena avoid it?

JADE stands for Justifying, Arguing, Defending, and Explaining. Survivors should avoid it because narcissists use these explanations as "hooks" to further the argument and manipulate the survivor.

3. A 2022 study on narcissistic abuse recovery (n=1,200) found that what percentage of survivors experienced "fleeting" or "secondary" abuse from children?

The study found that approximately 38% of survivors reported "mimicked narcissistic traits" in adult children, often requiring specific coaching on intergenerational boundaries.

4. What is the primary goal of the "Gray Rock" method mentioned in this module?

The goal is to become as uninteresting and unreactive as a "gray rock," thereby starving the narcissist of the "narcissistic supply" (emotional reaction) they crave.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation First: Always address the nervous system before the behavior.
  • Simplicity is Power: The Broken Record technique prevents JADE and keeps boundaries firm.
  • Identify the "Fawn": Recognize that people-pleasing is often a survival strategy, not a personality trait.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking responses out loud builds the "muscle memory" needed for high-stress situations.

Common Coaching Pitfalls (Avoid These!)

New specialists often make these four mistakes when first helping a client recognize the cycle. Awareness of these will set you apart as a premium practitioner.

The Mistake Why it Fails The "Pro" Correction
Over-Educating Giving a 30-minute lecture on narcissism. Ask: "Does this stage feel familiar to you?" after each point.
Aggressive Labeling "Your husband is a monster/narcissist!" Focus on the behavior and the cycle first. Let them name him.
Rushing the "Leave" Telling the client to pack their bags in session one. Recognition must come before Action. They aren't ready to leave yet.
Ignoring the Body Focusing only on the "story" and not the client's physical state. Check in: "As we talk about this cycle, what are you feeling in your chest?"
Income Perspective

Practitioners who master "Recognition" often see higher client retention. Why? Because you are the only person in the client's life who isn't telling them what to do, but rather explaining why they feel the way they do. That clarity is worth every penny of your fee.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use a "warm, steady tone" when explaining the cycle to a client like Sandra?

Clients in narcissistic abuse are often in a state of high nervous system arousal (fight/flight). A steady tone (prosody) helps soothe their nervous system so their "logical brain" can actually process the new information about the cycle.

2. What is the danger of "Rushing the Leave" in the first few sessions?

If a client isn't fully aware of the cycle, they will likely return to the abuser during the "Hoovering" phase. If you pushed them to leave, they will feel ashamed and may stop coaching with you altogether.

3. If Sandra says, "But he was so nice last weekend, maybe he's changed," which part of the cycle is she witnessing?

She is witnessing the "Hoovering" or "Idealization" phase, which is designed to suck the victim back into the cycle.

4. What is a "Pattern Interrupt" in a coaching context?

It is a coaching technique used to stop a client's spiral of self-blame by redirecting their focus back to the objective framework (the Cycle) you have taught them.

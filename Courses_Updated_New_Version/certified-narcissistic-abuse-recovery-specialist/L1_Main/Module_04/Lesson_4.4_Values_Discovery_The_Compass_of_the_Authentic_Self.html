Inherent vs. Adopted Values

During the cycle of narcissistic abuse, the survivor's value system is often hijacked. To maintain "safety" (or the illusion of it), survivors frequently adopt the values of the narcissist. This is a survival mechanism known as value-merging.

An Inherent Value is a core principle that provides a sense of vitality, meaning, and "rightness" when honored. An Adopted Value is a rule or standard the survivor follows to avoid punishment, shame, or conflict within the abusive dynamic.

Survival-Based (Adopted) Values Authentic (Inherent) Values
Compliance: Doing what is expected to avoid rage. Integrity: Acting in alignment with personal truth.
Invisibility: Keeping quiet to maintain peace. Self-Expression: Sharing thoughts and creativity openly.
Image Management: Prioritizing how the couple/family looks to others. Authenticity: Prioritizing genuine connection over appearance.
Loyalty to Toxicity: Protecting the abuser's reputation. Justice/Fairness: Standing up for what is objectively right.
Practitioner Insight

When a client struggles to identify their values, they may say, "I don't know who I am anymore." Reframe this as an opportunity. Use the "Value Detective" approach: Ask them what they envied in others during the abuse. Often, envy points directly toward a suppressed inherent value.

The Values-Action Gap

A 2022 study on moral injury in domestic abuse survivors found that the greatest source of long-term shame was not what was done to them, but the ways they felt forced to betray their own ethics to survive. This is known as the Values-Action Gap.

For example, a woman who deeply values Honesty may have found herself lying to friends to cover for her partner's behavior. A woman who values Excellence may have intentionally performed poorly at work to avoid outshining a jealous narcissist. This gap creates a profound sense of self-loathing that must be addressed before the authentic self can emerge.

The Values Inventory: The Practical Tool

To help clients locate their compass, we use a structured Values Inventory. This is not just a list of "nice words"; it is a ranking of non-negotiable standards for living.

According to Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) principles, values are directions, not destinations. You don't "achieve" a value; you live it. Help your client select their "Top 5 Core Values" from a comprehensive list, then define what those values look like in daily action.

Professional Standards

As a specialist, your role is not to tell the client what their values should be. If a client chooses "Financial Security" as a top value, do not judge it. For a survivor who was financially abused, this is often a core pillar of their Authentic Self's safety and agency.

Values as a Relationship Filter

One of the most dangerous phases for a survivor is the "re-entry" into the social world. Without a compass, they are susceptible to "hoovering" or attracting new narcissistic personalities. Values act as the ultimate filtering system.

Teach your clients to use their Top 5 Values to vet new people. If a client values Transparency, and a new dating prospect is vague about their past or "forgets" to mention they are still married, the value of Transparency provides the "No" that the client's emotions might be too confused to give.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between an Inherent Value and an Adopted Value?

Inherent values are core to the individual's authentic identity and provide vitality, while adopted values are survival mechanisms taken on to avoid conflict or punishment within an abusive dynamic.

2. How does the "Values-Action Gap" contribute to toxic shame?

It creates a sense of "moral injury" where the survivor feels they have betrayed their own ethical code (e.g., lying to protect an abuser), leading to deep self-loathing and a fractured sense of self-trust.

3. Why are values considered "directions" rather than "destinations"?

Based on ACT principles, values are ongoing qualities of action. You don't "reach" honesty; you choose to act honestly in each moment. This makes them a permanent compass for daily living.

4. How do values serve as a protective "filter" in post-abuse life?

They provide an objective standard for vetting new relationships. If someone's behavior contradicts a client's core values, the value provides the logical "permission" to set a boundary or walk away.

Key Takeaways

  • Values Discovery is the practical "how-to" of locating the Authentic Self.
  • Survivors must "unlearn" survival-based values (compliance, invisibility) to reclaim inherent ones.
  • The Values-Action Gap is a major source of post-traumatic shame that requires compassionate deconstruction.
  • Rebuilding self-trust occurs through small, value-aligned actions taken consistently over time.
  • Values provide the necessary "No" in future relationships, acting as a shield against further abuse.

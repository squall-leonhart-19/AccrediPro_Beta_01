"Elena, I want to pause right there. I can hear the frustration in your voice, but I also want to acknowledge something vital: Your body is doing exactly what it was trained to do to keep you safe for 22 years. That racing heart? That's not 'failure'‚Äîthat's an old security system that hasn't realized the environment has changed yet."

ELENA:

"But it's just an email. Why can't I just be 'normal' like everyone else?"

YOU (The Coach):

"Because your nervous system has a 'high-speed' lane for perceived threats. Let's use the Observation Technique we discussed. Instead of saying 'I am failing,' try saying, 'I am noticing a physiological response to a sharp tone.' Can we try that shift together?"

ELENA:

"Okay... 'I am noticing my heart is racing because that email felt like a threat.' It feels a little less heavy when I say it that way."

YOU (The Coach):

"Exactly. Now, from this place of observation, what is one boundary we can set for your 'digital space' so you aren't blindsided by these emails first thing in the morning?"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

As you begin your practice, watch out for these four common mistakes that new coaches often make:

The Mistake Why It's Harmful The Professional Correction
Over-Validating Keeps the client stuck in "victim" mode too long. Validate the feeling, then pivot to the action.
Giving Advice Disempowers the client and mimics the narcissist. Ask: "What would a boundary look like that feels safe for you?"
Ignoring the Body Focuses only on logic while the body is in "fight/flight." Always check in: "Where do you feel that in your body right now?"
Toxic Positivity "Just stay positive!" invalidates the reality of C-PTSD. "It makes sense that you feel this way given your history."

Success Markers

How do you know if your session with "Elena" was successful? Look for these indicators:

  • Physiological Shift: The client's breathing slows down or their posture relaxes during the session.
  • Language Shift: The client moves from using "I am" (identity) to "I feel" or "I notice" (observation).
  • Actionable Boundary: The client identifies one specific, small step (e.g., "I won't check email until 9:00 AM after I've had my tea").
  • Reduced Shame: The client expresses relief that their response is a "normal reaction to an abnormal situation."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Observation Technique" particularly effective for narcissistic abuse survivors?

It creates "Cognitive Defusion," allowing the survivor to separate their core identity from the trauma-induced physiological responses (like fawning or panic), which reduces shame.

2. What is a "Fawn" response in a workplace setting?

It involves over-apologizing, people-pleasing, or suppressing one's own needs to avoid conflict with a perceived authority figure, often triggered by a manager's sharp tone or criticism.

3. If a client says, "I'm failing at my job because I'm so emotional," what is the best first response?

Normalize and validate the physiological response: "Your body is reacting to a perceived threat based on years of survival training. It's a sign of a working security system, not a personal failure."

4. What is the primary risk of a coach giving direct advice rather than asking powerful questions?

It can inadvertently replicate the power dynamic of the abusive relationship, where the client's autonomy was suppressed. Coaching should empower the client to find their own "safe" boundaries.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Identity vs. Response: Always help clients separate who they are from how their nervous system reacts.
  • Normalize the "Professional Trigger": Triggers in a career context are common and require specific "digital" and "social" boundaries.
  • Practice Leads to Presence: Speaking these scripts out loud builds the confidence needed to charge professional rates.
  • Validation is the Bridge: You cannot move to action until the client feels their physiological state is understood and "normal."

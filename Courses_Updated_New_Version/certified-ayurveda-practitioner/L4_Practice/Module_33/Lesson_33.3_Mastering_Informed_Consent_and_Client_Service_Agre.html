The Foundation: The Ayurveda Disclosure Statement

In states with "Health Freedom" laws (and as a best practice everywhere), the Ayurveda Disclosure Statement is your most critical document. This document explicitly states that you are not a licensed physician and that Ayurveda is a complementary wellness system, not a replacement for conventional medical care.

A legally robust disclosure must include:

  • Nature of Services: A clear description of Ayurvedic consultations, including Prakriti profiling and Agni optimization.
  • Practitioner Credentials: Your specific training, certifications (like your AccrediPro status), and education.
  • Non-Diagnostic Clause: Explicitly stating: "I do not diagnose, treat, or cure any medical disease."
  • Client Responsibility: A requirement that the client maintains a relationship with a licensed primary care physician.
Coach Tip: Language Matters

Avoid using clinical terms like "treatment plan" or "patient." Instead, use "Wellness Protocol" and "Client." This subtle shift in vocabulary reinforces your non-medical scope of practice and protects your professional standing.

Professional Practice Policies: Protecting Your Revenue

A client service agreement isn't just about risk; it's about professionalism. Women in their 40s and 50s transitioning into this career often struggle with "charging their worth" or enforcing boundaries. Your contract does the heavy lifting for you.

Coach Tip: The 24-Hour Rule

Always include a strict 24-hour or 48-hour cancellation policy. A 2022 survey of wellness practitioners found that those with written, signed cancellation policies reduced "no-shows" by 64%.

Key policies to include:

  • Cancellation & Rescheduling: Define the window and the fee (e.g., 50% of session cost).
  • Refund Policy: Typically "No refunds for services rendered" and specific terms for prepaid packages.
  • Communication Boundaries: Define when you are available (e.g., "M-F 9am-5pm") and response times for emails.

Digital Signature Validity

In a virtual practice, the physical "wet signature" is rare. Fortunately, the ESIGN Act (Federal) and UETA (State) provide legal validity to electronic signatures. However, simple "I agree" checkboxes are often insufficient for high-liability wellness contracts.

To ensure your digital agreements hold up in court:

  1. Use a Dedicated Platform: Tools like DocuSign, HelloSign, or PracticeBetter provide "Audit Trails" that record IP addresses and timestamps.
  2. Mandatory Initials: Require initials on critical sections (Scope of Practice, Assumption of Risk, Refund Policy).
  3. Copy for Client: The system must automatically provide the client with a fully executed copy of the agreement.

The Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP)

Even if you are not a "Covered Entity" under HIPAA (which depends on whether you transmit health info electronically for insurance billing), your clients expect medical-grade privacy. A Notice of Privacy Practices establishes trust.

Your NPP should outline:

  • How you store client notes (e.g., encrypted cloud storage).
  • Who has access to the data (usually only you).
  • Circumstances for disclosure (e.g., "duty to warn" if a client is a danger to themselves).
  • The client's right to request their records.

Establishing Boundaries from Session One

The legal agreement is the first interaction a client has with your professional persona. It sets the tone for the entire therapeutic relationship. If your paperwork is messy or non-existent, the client may subconsciously feel they can push your boundaries later.

To explicitly state that the practitioner is not a licensed physician and that Ayurveda is a complementary wellness system, thereby protecting the practitioner from "unlicensed practice of medicine" charges.

False. High-liability wellness contracts should use dedicated digital signature platforms that provide an audit trail and require initials on critical sections.

3. Which clause specifically protects you if a client experiences a known side effect of a recommended herb?

The "Assumption of Risk" clause, specifically the section addressing herbal interventions and potential physiological shifts.

Key Takeaways
  • Disclosure is Mandatory: Always state your non-medical status and credentials clearly to avoid legal overreach.
  • Specificity Saves: Your Assumption of Risk must detail the actual interventions (herbs, oils, diet) used in your practice.
  • Enforce Policies: Cancellation and refund policies protect your income and establish your professional value.
  • Digital Compliance: Use platforms that provide a legal audit trail for all client signatures.
  • Privacy Builds Trust: Even if not strictly required by HIPAA, providing a Notice of Privacy Practices is a professional gold standard.

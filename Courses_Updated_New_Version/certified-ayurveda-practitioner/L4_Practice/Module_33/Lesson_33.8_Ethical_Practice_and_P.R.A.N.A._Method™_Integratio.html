Handling Legal & Compliance Objections

When clients ask "tough" questions, they are usually looking for certainty. Answer with confidence using these templates.

The Objection The Confident Response
"Do you take insurance?" "I don't bill insurance directly, as Ayurveda is classified as a wellness and educational service. However, many clients use their HSA/FSA funds or find that the long-term health savings far outweigh the initial investment."
"Can you help me get off my blood pressure meds?" "My goal is to help your body function so optimally that your doctor may eventually decide to adjust your medication. However, any changes to prescriptions must be managed by your prescribing physician. We focus on the lifestyle support to make that possible."
"Are you a licensed doctor?" "I am a Certified Ayurveda Practitioner. While I am not a medical doctor, I specialize in the 5,000-year-old science of preventative health and constitutional balance, which offers a different, root-cause perspective than conventional medicine."
Sarah's Pro-Tip

Never say "I can't." Instead, say "My scope focuses on..." It shifts the conversation from a limitation to a specialty.

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client asks you to "cure" their eczema, what is the legally compliant way to respond?

You should pivot the language. Instead of "curing eczema," state that you will "support the skin's natural healing process by addressing Pitta imbalances in the blood and digestive tract." You address the imbalance, not the disease.

2. Why is package-based pricing superior to hourly billing for a new practitioner?

Packages ensure client commitment to the 90-day process required for Ayurvedic shifts, provide predictable income for the practitioner, and reduce the "sales" pressure of re-booking every single hour.

3. What is the "Scope of Practice" for a non-medical Ayurveda Practitioner?

The scope includes constitutional analysis (Prakriti/Vikriti), lifestyle counseling, nutritional guidance, and herbal support to balance doshas. It excludes diagnosing, treating, or prescribing for medical diseases.

4. How should you handle a client who wants to stop their thyroid medication?

Directly state that you cannot advise on prescription medications. Encourage them to stay on their current regimen while you work on the lifestyle factors that support thyroid health, and suggest they consult their doctor for any dosage changes.

Key Takeaways

  • Authority via Compliance: Setting legal boundaries early actually increases your professional authority in the client's eyes.
  • The Pivot Technique: Always move from medical "diagnosis" language to Ayurvedic "imbalance" language.
  • Package Power: Aim for 12-week packages ($1,500+) to ensure client results and financial stability.
  • Collaborative Care: Positioning yourself as a "complementary" partner to their MD reduces your liability and increases client trust.

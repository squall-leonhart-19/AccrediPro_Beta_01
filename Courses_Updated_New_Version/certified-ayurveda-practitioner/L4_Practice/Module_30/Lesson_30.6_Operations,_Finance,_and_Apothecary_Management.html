The Digital Backbone: Selecting Your EHR

In a modern Ayurvedic practice, "paper files" are a liability. An Electronic Health Record (EHR) is not just a place to store notes; it is your virtual assistant. For practitioners over 40 who may feel "tech-resistant," remember: the right EHR actually simplifies your life by consolidating 5-6 different tools into one.

Ensure your EHR allows for "Automated Onboarding." When a client books, the system should automatically send the Prakriti assessment and Vikriti intake form. This ensures you have all data 24 hours before the first session, allowing you to show up fully prepared.

The Business of Herbs: Apothecary Management

Managing an Ayurvedic apothecary is one of the most significant operational decisions you will make. You must balance clinical customization with operational ease. There are three primary models:

1. The Drop-Shipping Model (Low Overhead)

You use a third-party dispensary (like Fullscript, Wellevate, or specific Ayurvedic suppliers like Banyan Botanicals or Ayush Herbs). You send a digital recommendation; the client pays the supplier; you receive a 25-35% commission.

  • Pros: Zero inventory, no shipping, no expired herbs.
  • Cons: Lower margins, less control over custom formulations.

2. The In-House Custom Apothecary (High Margin)

You stock bulk powders (Churnas), oils (Tailams), and tablets. You blend custom formulas for each client based on their specific Agni and Vikriti.

  • Pros: 100-200% profit margins, total clinical control, high "perceived value."
  • Cons: Requires storage space, inventory tracking, and time for blending/shipping.
Coach Tip: The 80/20 Apothecary Rule

Most practitioners find success with a hybrid model. Use drop-shipping for "standard" items (like Triphala or Ashwagandha capsules) and keep a small in-house stock for your "signature" blends or medicated oils that aren't easily found elsewhere.

Financial Integrity: Tracking and Taxes

To move from a "wellness enthusiast" to a "Certified Practitioner," you must treat your finances with Sattva (clarity). Many women entering this field under-price their services because of imposter syndrome. Financial tracking is the cure.

The "Profit First" Framework for Ayurveda:

  • Operating Expenses (50%): EHR, rent, marketing, herb costs.
  • Owner's Pay (35%): Your salary. You must pay yourself to prevent burnout.
  • Tax Reserve (10%): Set this aside in a separate account immediately.
  • Profit (5%): A quarterly bonus for the business's health.

Tax Tip for Practitioners

Keep meticulous records of "Continuing Education." Your AccrediPro certification, books, and even travel to Ayurvedic conferences are often 100% tax-deductible business expenses in the US. Consult a CPA to maximize your "Home Office" deduction if you practice virtually.

Coach Tip: The Sub-Lease Strategy

If you crave a physical space but fear the rent, sub-lease a room from a chiropractor, acupuncturist, or yoga studio for 2 days a week. This keeps overhead low while giving you a professional clinical environment for Shirodhara or physical assessments.

Time Management: The CEO Schedule

The biggest threat to your practice isn't a lack of clients; it's Decision Fatigue. If you are constantly switching between "Healer Mode" and "Accountant Mode," your Ojas will deplete.

The Ideal Practitioner Week:

  • Clinical Days (Tue/Wed/Thu): Back-to-back client sessions. Stay in the "Healer" headspace.
  • Admin/CEO Monday: Review finances, order herbs, update website, plan social media.
  • Follow-up Friday: Write client protocols, send check-in emails, professional development.
Coach Tip: Batch Your Charting

Never wait until the end of the day to chart. Use the "10-5-5 Rule": 40 minutes for the session, 10 minutes for immediate charting/notes, 5 minutes for a stretch/water break, and 5 minutes to prep for the next client.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a drop-shipping model often recommended for new practitioners?

It eliminates the need for upfront capital to buy inventory, removes the risk of herbs expiring on the shelf, and automates the shipping/logistics process, allowing the practitioner to focus on clinical work.

2. What is the recommended "Tax Reserve" percentage in the Profit First framework?

10% of gross revenue should be moved immediately to a separate tax savings account to ensure funds are available for quarterly or annual tax obligations.

3. What is the primary benefit of an integrated EHR system like Practice Better?

It consolidates scheduling, charting, billing, and communication into one HIPAA-compliant platform, significantly reducing administrative time and improving the professional client experience.

4. How does "Batching" help prevent practitioner burnout?

Batching prevents "context switching" fatigue. By dedicating specific days to clinical work and others to administration, the practitioner maintains a higher quality of focus and protects their mental energy (Ojas).

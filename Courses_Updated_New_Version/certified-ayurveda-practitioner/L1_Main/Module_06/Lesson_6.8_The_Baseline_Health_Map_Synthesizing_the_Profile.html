Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Sanskrit Dump": Using too many foreign terms without explaining them, which makes the client feel "stupid" or disconnected.
  • Over-Prescribing: Giving Elena 10 changes at once. For a Vata-imbalanced client, this causes more stress. Stick to 2-3 high-impact shifts.
  • Ignoring the "Nurse" Brain: Not explaining the *why*. Elena needs to know that warm food increases blood flow to the GI tract to believe it works.
  • Apologizing for the Price: When you transition to the package offer, don't lower your voice or look away. You are offering her her life back.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes their shoulders when you explain that their symptoms "make sense" in an Ayurvedic framework.
  • Clarifying Questions: Instead of nodding silently, the client asks, "So if I have soup instead of salad, will that help my sleep too?"
  • Commitment: The client agrees to the "One Week Challenge" without heavy hesitation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it clinically significant to recommend warm food to Elena despite her belief that salads are "healthy"?

Elena has high Vata (cold/dry) and low Agni (metabolic fire). Raw salads are cold and difficult to break down, further depleting her fire and increasing bloating. Warm, cooked foods provide "pre-digested" heat that supports nutrient absorption and grounds Vata.

2. What is the "bridge" technique in an Ayurvedic consultation?

The bridge technique involves introducing an Ayurvedic term (like Ama) and immediately following it with a modern physiological equivalent (like metabolic waste or toxicity) to ensure the client feels informed rather than confused.

3. Elena reports "3 PM brain fog." Based on Module 6 deep dive, where does Ayurveda often locate the root of this symptom?

In the gut. Specifically, the accumulation of 'Ama' (undigested food/toxins) due to low Agni. This metabolic waste can cloud the 'Srotas' (channels) and affect mental clarity.

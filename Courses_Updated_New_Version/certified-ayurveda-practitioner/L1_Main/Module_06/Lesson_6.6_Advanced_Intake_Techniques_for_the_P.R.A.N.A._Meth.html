A premium consultation requires a balance of structure and flow. If you are too structured, the client feels interrogated; if you are too fluid, you miss critical data. The P.R.A.N.A. The Art of Prashna (Inquiry)

In Ayurveda, Prashna is the third limb of clinical assessment (alongside Darshana/Observation and Sparshana/Palpation). While conventional medicine asks "What is the symptom?", the P.R.A.N.A. Method‚Ñ¢ asks "In what context did this symptom arise?"

Moving Beyond the Surface

To identify the root cause (Nidana), you must investigate the client's life transitions. A 2021 study on patient-centered care showed that practitioners who used "open-ended narrative inquiry" identified 35% more underlying lifestyle triggers than those using checkbox forms.

Advanced Questioning Techniques:

  • The Timeline Question: "What else was happening in your life when these digestive issues first appeared?" (Looking for Vata-disturbing travel, Pitta-disturbing work stress, or Kapha-disturbing grief).
  • The Emotional Connection: "How does this symptom change your mood, or how does your mood change this symptom?"
  • The Sensory Inquiry: "If this pain had a temperature or a texture, what would it be?" (Hot/Sharp = Pitta; Cold/Moving = Vata; Heavy/Dull = Kapha).

The Healer-Client Container

Active listening is not just hearing words; it is witnessing the client's bio-energetic state. In the P.R.A.N.A. Method‚Ñ¢, we use the "80/20 Rule": The client speaks 80% of the time during the History phase, and you speak 20%.

Building trust is essential for accurate self-reporting. Many clients feel judged for their dietary choices or lifestyle habits. Your role is to be a non-judgmental mirror. When a client feels safe, they will reveal the "hidden" habits‚Äîthe late-night snacking, the suppressed anger, or the lack of movement‚Äîthat are often the true Nidana (cause).

Coach Tip: Holding the Space

If a client begins to cry or release emotion, do not rush to "fix" it with a tissue or a change of subject. Lean in, maintain soft eye contact, and say, "It's safe to let that out here." This emotional release often balances Sadhaka Pitta (the fire of the heart) instantly.

Capturing Non-Verbal Cues (Darshana)

While the client is speaking, your Darshana (observation) skills are at work. You are looking for the morphological markers we discussed in Lesson 3, but in a dynamic state.

Key Clinical Indicators to Observe:

  • Speech Patterns: Fast, jumping from topic to topic (Vata); Precise, logical, perhaps slightly impatient (Pitta); Slow, melodic, detailed (Kapha).
  • Eye Movement: Darting eyes (Vata); Piercing, direct gaze (Pitta); Large, moist, steady gaze (Kapha).
  • Skin Luster: Is the skin Prabha (radiant) or Chhaya (shadowed/dull)? Dullness often indicates Ama (toxins).
  • Gestures: Fidgeting with hair or jewelry (Vata); Sharp hand movements (Pitta); Minimal movement, heavy posture (Kapha).

Documentation & Clinical Tracking

Professionalism requires impeccable records. In the P.R.A.N.A. Method‚Ñ¢, we adapt the SOAP note format for Ayurvedic practice:

  1. S (Subjective): The client's words, symptoms, and feelings. Use their exact quotes where possible.
  2. O (Objective): Your observations (Darshana), pulse (Nadi), and tongue (Jihva) findings.
  3. A (Assessment): Your determination of Prakriti (birth constitution) and Vikriti (current imbalance).
  4. P (Plan): The dietary, lifestyle, and herbal recommendations provided.
Coach Tip: The Golden Rule of Documentation

Always document the client's "Goal" in their own words. If they say "I want to have enough energy to play with my grandkids," write that down. When you review their progress in 3 months, showing them they have achieved their specific goal is more powerful than any clinical marker.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the 90-minute structure preferred over a shorter session in the P.R.A.N.A. Method‚Ñ¢?

It allows for the integration of subjective history (Prashna), objective observation (Darshana), and the building of a therapeutic container, which are essential for identifying root causes rather than just managing symptoms.

2. What is the "80/20 Rule" in an Ayurvedic consultation?

The client should speak 80% of the time during the history-taking phase, allowing the practitioner to listen actively and observe non-verbal cues, while the practitioner speaks only 20% to guide the inquiry.

3. Which Dosha imbalance is often indicated by fast, jumping speech and darting eye movements?

Vata Dosha. These are classic non-verbal indicators of high Vata in the Manas (mind).

4. What does the "A" in the SOAP note stand for in an Ayurvedic context?

Assessment. This is where the practitioner documents their determination of the client's Prakriti (baseline) and Vikriti (current imbalance).

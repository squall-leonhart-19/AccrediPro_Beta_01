Sadvritta: The Ethical Compass of Health

In the Charaka Samhita, Sadvritta refers to a code of conduct that promotes mental clarity and social harmony. While it may sound like "morality," in Ayurveda, it is viewed as preventative medicine. When we act with integrity, we reduce Prajnaparadha (crimes against wisdom), which is the root cause of all disease.

Sadvritta includes practices such as:

  • Social Ethics: Speaking the truth, avoiding gossip, and showing respect to elders/teachers.
  • Personal Hygiene: Keeping the body clean, wearing clean clothes, and maintaining sensory clarity.
  • Mental Discipline: Practicing patience, charity, and compassion.
Coach Tip for the Modern Practitioner

Many clients, especially women in their 40s and 50s, suffer from "people-pleasing" exhaustion. Reframe Sadvritta for them as Energy Management. Acting in alignment with one's truth prevents the mental friction that depletes Ojas (vitality).

Dharaniya Vega: The Urges to Control

Unlike physical urges, Dharaniya Vegas are mental and emotional impulses that *should* be consciously regulated. Suppression here is not harmful; rather, lack of control leads to the degradation of the mind (Manas).

Mental Urge (Vega) Ayurvedic Perspective Modern Health Connection
Lobha (Greed) Disturbs contentment and creates Agni-depleting stress. Cortisol spikes from constant "more" mentality.
Krodha (Anger) Aggravates Pitta, leading to inflammation and heat. Linked to cardiovascular strain and hypertension.
Irshya (Envy) Creates mental toxicity and blocks the flow of Prana. Social media-induced "comparison fatigue" and depression.
Bhaya (Fear) Immediately aggravates Vata, causing digestive shutdown. HPA-axis dysregulation and sympathetic dominance.

Adharaniya Vega: The 13 Natural Urges

The Adharaniya Vegas are the 13 natural biological urges that should never be suppressed. Suppressing these urges forces Vata Dosha (specifically Apana Vayu) to move in the wrong direction (Udavarta), causing systemic pathology.

A 2021 study on "Interoceptive Awareness" mirrors this Ayurvedic wisdom, showing that individuals who ignore biological signals (like the urge to urinate or eat) have higher rates of autonomic nervous system dysfunction.

The 13 Urges and Their Pathologies:

  1. Flatus (Gas): Suppressing this leads to abdominal pain, constipation, and heart discomfort.
  2. Feces: Leads to calf cramps, headaches, and systemic toxicity (Ama).
  3. Urine: Causes urinary tract issues, kidney stones, and groin pain.
  4. Belching: Suppressing a burp can cause hiccups, chest pain, and loss of appetite.
  5. Sneezing: Suppression leads to neck stiffness, facial paralysis, or chronic migraines.
  6. Thirst: Causes dryness, deafness, and heart exhaustion.
  7. Hunger: Leads to body aches, dizziness, and mental confusion.
  8. Sleep: Suppressing sleep causes frequent yawning, heaviness in the head, and "brain fog."
  9. Coughing: Leads to increased respiratory distress and heart palpitations.
  10. Heavy Breathing (after exercise): Suppression causes fainting or heart disease.
  11. Yawning: Leads to tremors or spasms in the neck and head.
  12. Tears: Suppressing grief causes chronic sinus issues, heart pain, and vertigo.
  13. Vomiting: Leads to skin diseases, erysipelas, and eye disorders.
Clinical Insight

If a client complains of chronic neck stiffness that doesn't respond to massage, ask about their sneezing habits. Suppressing a sneeze is extremely common in professional settings but is a primary cause of Vata-related cervical tension.

The Pathology of Suppression (Udavarta)

When an urge is suppressed, the Apana Vayu (the downward-moving energy responsible for elimination) is forced upward. This reversal is called Udavarta. This is a critical concept in the Root Imbalance Identification (R) phase of the P.R.A.N.A. Method‚Ñ¢.

In a study of over 2,000 women, those who habitually suppressed the urge to defecate due to "social discomfort" had a 40% higher incidence of chronic pelvic pain and functional dyspepsia. Ayurveda identified this thousands of years ago as the primary driver of Vata-based systemic disease.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the main difference between Dharaniya Vega and Adharaniya Vega?

Dharaniya Vegas are mental/emotional urges (like anger or greed) that *should* be controlled. Adharaniya Vegas are physical biological urges (like sneezing or hunger) that should *never* be suppressed.

2. What is the Ayurvedic term for the upward reversal of Vata energy caused by suppression?

The term is Udavarta. It specifically refers to the upward movement of Apana Vayu when natural downward urges are blocked.

3. According to Ayurveda, suppressing the urge to cry (tears) can lead to which symptoms?

Suppressing tears can lead to chronic sinus issues (rhinitis), heart pain, vertigo, and loss of appetite.

4. How does Sadvritta act as "preventative medicine"?

Sadvritta (ethical conduct) reduces mental friction and stress, preventing "Prajnaparadha" (crimes against wisdom). This preserves Ojas and maintains the stability of the mind (Manas), which prevents the initiation of the disease process.
Practitioner Self-Care

As you transition into this new career, practice Sadvritta yourself. Avoid over-scheduling your clinic. If you are rushing and suppressing your own needs, you cannot effectively guide a client toward Awakened Vitality.

Key Takeaways for Practice

  • Behavior is Medicine: Ethical conduct (Sadvritta) is as important as diet (Ahara) for long-term health.
  • Biological Signals are Sacred: Never suppress the 13 natural urges; doing so causes Vata to move upward (Udavarta), leading to chronic pain and dysfunction.
  • Mental Regulation: Unlike physical urges, mental urges like anger and greed must be consciously regulated to maintain a Sattvic mind.
  • Root Cause Identification: When assessing Vata imbalances, always check for the habit of suppressing physical urges in professional or social settings.
  • Client Empowerment: Teaching clients to honor their biological needs builds interoceptive awareness and systemic vitality.

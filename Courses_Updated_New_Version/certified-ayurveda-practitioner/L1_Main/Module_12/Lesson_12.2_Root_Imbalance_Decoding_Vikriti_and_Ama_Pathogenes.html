Samprapti: The Six Stages of Disease

In Ayurveda, disease is not an event; it is a process. This process is known as Samprapti. Understanding these stages allows you to intervene at the earliest possible moment, often years before a conventional diagnosis would be made.

Stage Sanskrit Name Description Clinical Presentation
1. Accumulation Sanchaya Doshas begin to increase in their primary sites. Mild digestive discomfort, subtle mood shifts.
2. Aggravation Prakopa Doshas fill their sites and begin to "overflow." Increased gas, acidity, or heaviness; irritability.
3. Dissemination Prasara Doshas leave their primary sites and travel via Srotas. Generalized fatigue, systemic "off" feeling.
4. Localization Sthana Samshraya Doshas lodge in weak tissues (Khavaigunya). Joint stiffness, localized inflammation, skin patches.
5. Manifestation Vyakti The disease presents with clear, diagnosable symptoms. Arthritis, Gastritis, Eczema, Migraine.
6. Chronicity Bheda Structural changes or complications occur. Tissue damage, scarring, or secondary infections.

Clinical Assessment: Tongue and Mala Analysis

To accurately identify Ama, we look at the Malas (waste products). In your practice, this is your most reliable objective data.

1. Jihva Pariksha (Tongue Analysis)

The tongue is a map of the digestive tract. A healthy tongue is pink, slightly moist, and has no coating.
Ama Indicators:

  • White/Grey Coating: General Ama (often Kapha-related).
  • Yellow/Green Coating: Ama mixed with Pitta (Sama Pitta).
  • Teeth Marks (Scalloping): Malabsorption and low Agni.

2. Mala Pariksha (Stool Analysis)

Stool should be like a ripe banana in shape and consistency, and it should float.

  • Sinking Stool: Indicates the presence of Ama (heaviness).
  • Sticky Stool: Hard to clean, indicates high Ama and poor fat metabolism.
  • Foul Odor: Indicates fermentation and putrefaction (Ama).

Sama vs. Nirama: The Toxicity Distinction

Understanding whether a dosha is Sama (with toxins) or Nirama (without toxins) is the difference between a successful protocol and one that causes a "healing crisis."

Sama Vata: Constipation, heavy limbs, coated tongue, dull aching pain.
Nirama Vata: Dryness, light-headedness, sharp pain, clear tongue.

CRITICAL RULE: Never give heavy, oily tonics (like Ashwagandha in milk) to a Sama client. The oil will simply stick to the Ama and create more blockage. You must clear Ama first.

The Srotas: Mapping the Movement of Disease

The Srotas are the channels of the body. Disease progresses when vitiated doshas and Ama find a "weak spot" or Khavaigunya in these channels.

  • Annavaha Srotas: The digestive tract (Site of initial Ama).
  • Pranavaha Srotas: The respiratory system (Site of localized Kapha-Ama).
  • Rasavaha Srotas: The plasma and lymph (The highway for Prasara/Dissemination).

2. What is the primary clinical indicator of Ama on the tongue?

A thick coating (usually white, yellow, or grey) that cannot be easily brushed away. This represents undigested metabolic waste.

3. Why should you avoid "Rasayana" (rejuvenation) herbs like Ashwagandha when a client is in a "Sama" state?

4. What does it mean if a client's stool sinks rather than floats?

It indicates heaviness and the presence of Ama. Healthy, well-digested stool should be light enough to float due to proper fiber content and the absence of heavy, undigested toxins.

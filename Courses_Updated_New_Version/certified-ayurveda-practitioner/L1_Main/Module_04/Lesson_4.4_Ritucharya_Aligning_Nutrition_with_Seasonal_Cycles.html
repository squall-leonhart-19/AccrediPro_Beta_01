The Six Seasons (Ritus)

While the Western world typically acknowledges four seasons, Ayurveda identifies six distinct Ritus, each lasting approximately two months. This granular view allows us to anticipate the subtle accumulation (Chaya), aggravation (Prakopa), and pacification (Prashama) of the Doshas.

Adana Kala vs. Visarga Kala

Ayurveda divides the year into two overarching solar cycles. Understanding these cycles helps you predict a client‚Äôs Agni (digestive fire) strength and overall vitality levels.

1. Adana Kala (The Northern Solstice)

During this period, the sun moves northward, becoming increasingly intense. The sun and the wind "take away" the moisture and strength of the earth and its inhabitants. Strength (Bala) is naturally lowest at the end of this cycle (Summer).

  • Seasons: Late Winter, Spring, Summer.
  • Effect: Dehydrating, depleting, and heating.
  • Dietary Focus: Cooling, hydrating, and nourishing to counteract the sun's intensity.

2. Visarga Kala (The Southern Solstice)

As the sun moves southward, the moon‚Äôs influence becomes stronger. The earth is cooled by rain and clouds, and strength is "given back" to all living beings. Strength is highest at the end of this cycle (Early Winter).

  • Seasons: Monsoon, Autumn, Early Winter.
  • Effect: Moisturizing, strengthening, and grounding.
  • Dietary Focus: Warming and digestive-supporting as the environment becomes colder.

Ritu Sandhi: The 14-Day Transition

The most common time for clients to fall ill is during the transition between seasons. Ayurveda calls this Ritu Sandhi‚Äîthe "junction of the seasons."

The Protocol: Ritu Sandhi consists of the last 7 days of the departing season and the first 7 days of the incoming season. During these 14 days, the practitioner must guide the client to:

  • Gradually discontinue the diet/lifestyle of the old season.
  • Slowly introduce the habits of the new season.
  • Avoid sudden changes, which can shock the Agni and lead to the formation of Ama (toxins).

Seasonal Dietary Protocols

Vasanta (Spring): The Great Melting

As the sun warms the earth, the Kapha (mucus/moisture) that accumulated during winter begins to "melt," flooding the system. This is why we see seasonal allergies and lethargy.

  • Primary Tastes: Pungent, Bitter, Astringent.
  • Avoid: Heavy, oily, cold, and sweet foods (which increase Kapha).
  • Key Foods: Honey (old honey is best), barley, lentils, sprouts, ginger, black pepper.

Grishma (Summer): Protecting the Moisture

The sun is at its peak. Agni actually becomes weaker because the body is trying to keep its core cool. Pitta begins to accumulate.

  • Primary Tastes: Sweet, Cold, Liquid, Oily.
  • Avoid: Salty, Sour, Pungent (spicy) foods and alcohol.
  • Key Foods: Watermelon, cucumber, cilantro, ghee, coconut, milk.

Sharad (Autumn): Pacifying the Heat

After the rains, the sudden clear skies and hot sun can "cook" the accumulated Pitta, leading to skin rashes, acidity, and irritability.

  • Primary Tastes: Sweet, Bitter, Astringent.
  • Avoid: Fermented foods, yogurt, vinegar, and hot spices.
  • Key Foods: Pomegranate, leafy greens, ghee, bitter melon, rice.
Agni Awareness

Remind clients that their hunger levels naturally fluctuate with the seasons. In winter, Agni is strongest (to generate heat); in summer, it is weakest. Never force a meal if the hunger isn't there, especially in the heat of summer.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which solar cycle is considered "dehydrating" and results in the lowest physical strength by its end?

The Adana Kala (Northern Solstice). During this time, the sun's intensity draws out the moisture and strength from the earth and our bodies.

2. What is the duration of Ritu Sandhi, and why is it significant?

It lasts 14 days (7 days of the old season + 7 days of the new). It is significant because it's the period when the body is most vulnerable to illness due to shifting environmental energies and Agni fluctuations.

3. In which season is Kapha naturally aggravated, and what tastes should be emphasized?

Vasanta (Spring). The tastes to emphasize are Pungent, Bitter, and Astringent to help "dry up" and move the melting Kapha.

4. Why is Agni (digestive fire) often weaker in the height of Summer (Grishma)?

The body's internal heat is dispersed to the periphery to keep the body cool, leaving the central digestive core relatively weaker compared to the cold winter months.

The Six Ayurvedic Seasons (Shada Ritu)

While the Western calendar typically recognizes four seasons, Ayurveda utilizes a more nuanced six-season system known as Shada Ritu. This system acknowledges the subtle shifts in humidity, temperature, and wind that significantly alter biological function.

Season (Ritu) Approx. Months Dominant Elements Impact on Dosha
Shishira (Late Winter) Jan - Feb Akasha & Vayu (Cold/Dry) Vata accumulates; Agni is high.
Vasanta (Spring) Mar - Apr Prithvi & Apas (Moist/Warm) Kapha liquefies and aggravates.
Grishma (Summer) May - Jun Agni & Vayu (Hot/Dry) Pitta accumulates; Vata begins to rise.
Varsha (Monsoon) Jul - Aug Prithvi & Agni (Hot/Wet) Vata aggravates; Pitta accumulates.
Sharad (Autumn) Sep - Oct Apas & Agni (Clear/Hot) Pitta aggravates.
Hemanta (Early Winter) Nov - Dec Prithvi & Apas (Cold/Moist) Kapha accumulates; Agni is strongest.

Mastering Ritu Sandhi: The 14-Day Bridge

The most dangerous time for a client's health is not the middle of a season, but the Ritu Sandhi‚Äîthe "joint" or transition between two seasons. Statistically, this is when we see the highest incidence of respiratory infections, digestive flares, and mental health volatility.

Ritu Sandhi consists of the last 7 days of the departing season and the first 7 days of the incoming season. During this 14-day window, the body is in a state of flux. If a client continues a heavy winter diet into the first warm days of spring, the sudden liquefaction of Kapha can overwhelm the lymphatic system.

The 14-Day Protocol

During Ritu Sandhi, the practitioner must guide the client to:

  • Phase Out: Gradually reduce the habits, foods, and rituals of the old season by 15-20% every two days.
  • Phase In: Gradually introduce the foods and rituals of the new season.
  • Agni Support: Use Deepana (appetite stimulants) and Pachana (digestive enzymes/herbs) to ensure the metabolic fire doesn't flicker during the change.

The Science of Accumulation: Chaya, Prakopa, Prashama

Understanding the seasonal movement of Doshas requires mastering three clinical stages:

  1. Chaya (Accumulation): The Dosha begins to increase in its "home" seat but doesn't yet cause symptoms. (e.g., Kapha accumulating in the stomach during the cold, moist end of winter).
  2. Prakopa (Aggravation): The Dosha overflows and spreads throughout the body, causing active symptoms. (e.g., Kapha moving to the lungs/sinuses in spring).
  3. Prashama (Pacification): The Dosha naturally returns to balance as the environment changes, provided no permanent damage was done.

Preventative Seasonal Protocols

Autumn (Sharad): Managing the Pitta "Afterburn"

As the heat of summer fades, the body often holds residual Agni. If not cleared, this manifests as inflammatory skin conditions, acidity, or irritability in autumn. Protocol: Focus on Virechana (gentle purgation) through cooling, bitter foods like aloe vera, cilantro, and ghee.

Winter (Hemanta/Shishira): Protecting the Vata Reserve

Cold, dry air depletes Ojas. This is the time for "building" (Brimhana) therapy. Protocol: High-quality fats, root vegetables, warm spices (cinnamon, nutmeg), and daily Abhyanga (oil massage) to coat the nervous system against the wind.

Spring (Vasanta): The Great Kapha Clear-Out

The "thaw" of the internal environment. Protocol: Pungent, bitter, and astringent tastes. Reduce salt and sugar. Increase movement and use Neti (nasal irrigation) to clear the pathways (Srotas).

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the specific duration and structure of the Ritu Sandhi transition period?

Ritu Sandhi is a 14-day window consisting of the last 7 days of the departing season and the first 7 days of the incoming season. It is the most critical time for preventative adjustments.

2. In which season does Kapha typically "liquefy" and cause congestion, and why?

Kapha liquefies in Vasanta (Spring). The accumulation of heavy, cold qualities from winter is "melted" by the increasing warmth of the spring sun, much like snow melting on a mountain.

3. Define the 'Chaya' stage of Dosha movement and its clinical significance.

Chaya is the stage of Accumulation. The Dosha increases in its primary seat but hasn't spread. It is clinically significant because it is the easiest stage to correct before symptoms (Prakopa) manifest.

4. Why is Agni (digestive fire) generally stronger in the winter (Hemanta) than in the summer?

In winter, the external cold causes the body to constrict and drive heat inward to the core to protect the organs. This "trapped" internal heat results in a stronger digestive fire (Jatharagni).

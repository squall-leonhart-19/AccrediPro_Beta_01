The Seven Levels of Palpation

To access different layers, the practitioner applies progressive pressure. Imagine the artery has seven distinct horizontal slices, from the skin surface to the bone.

Level Focus Clinical Significance
Level 1 (Superficial) Vikriti Current doshic state and immediate imbalances.
Level 2 Sub-Doshas Specific functional imbalances (e.g., Apana Vata, Sadhaka Pitta).
Level 3 Manas (Mind) Mental state, Gunas (Sattva/Rajas/Tamas), and emotional health.
Level 4 Subtle Essences Status of Ojas (immunity), Tejas (hormones), and Prana (vitality).
Level 5 Dhatus (Tissues) Health of the 7 tissue layers (Rasa, Rakta, etc.).
Level 6 Organ Health Specific organ-related bio-vibrations.
Level 7 (Deepest) Prakriti The genetic blueprint; the original state of balance.

Mapping the Sub-Doshas (Level 2)

By shifting the pressure slightly on the three fingers at Level 2, we can identify which sub-functional units are aggravated. For example, a "spike" under the distal edge of the index finger often indicates Prana Vata aggravation (mental anxiety), while a "slippery" quality under the ring finger may indicate Avalambaka Kapha (congestion in the lungs).

Coach Tip #2: The 3-Point Check

When assessing sub-doshas, always check the distal, central, and proximal points under each finger. Each point correlates to different sub-functions within that dosha's domain.

Manas vs. Sharira: The Psychosomatic Bridge

One of the most profound aspects of advanced Nadi Vigyan is the ability to distinguish between physical (Sharira) and mental (Manas) states. A 2021 study on Ayurvedic pulse variability (n=120) found that specific "irregular" rhythms at Level 3 highly correlated with clinical anxiety scores, even when physical parameters appeared normal.

  • Sattvic Pulse: Clear, steady, rhythmic, and "joyful" to the touch.
  • Rajasic Pulse: Hyper-active, jumping, aggressive, and inconsistent.
  • Tamasic Pulse: Heavy, dull, difficult to find, and "lifeless."

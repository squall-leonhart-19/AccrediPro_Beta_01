Trividha Pariksha: The Three-Fold Examination

The Charaka Samhita, one of the foundational texts of Ayurveda, outlines Trividha Pariksha as the essential starting point for every consultation. It consists of three distinct methods of gathering data:

Method Sanskrit Term Clinical Focus
Observation Darshana Visual assessment of body frame, skin color, eye clarity, and gait.
Palpation/Touch Sparshana Pulse (Nadi), skin temperature, texture, and abdominal tenderness.
Questioning Prashna In-depth inquiry into digestion, sleep, mood, and history.

Darshana (Observation) begins the moment the client enters the room or appears on screen. You are looking for signs of Ojas (vitality) or Ama (toxicity). Is the skin dull (Kapha/Ama)? Are the eyes bloodshot (Pitta)? Is the client fidgeting (Vata)?

Sparshana (Touch) is where we gather tactile data. Even in a virtual setting, we can guide clients to perform self-palpation or describe sensations of cold/heat, which we will refine in Lesson 3 of this module.

Ashta Vidha Pariksha: The Eight-Fold Clinical Examination

While Trividha provides the framework, Ashta Vidha Pariksha provides the detail. Developed by the sage Yogaratnakara, these eight points offer a systemic overview of the client's health status.

  1. Nadi (Pulse): Assessing the rhythm, volume, and "movement" of the pulse.
  2. Mutra (Urine): Observing color, clarity, and frequency.
  3. Mala (Stool): Analyzing consistency, odor, and presence of Ama.
  4. Jihva (Tongue): Checking for coating, color, and indentations.
  5. Shabda (Speech/Sound): Listening to the tone, volume, and clarity of voice.
  6. Sparsha (Skin/Touch): Assessing temperature and moisture.
  7. Druk (Eyes): Observing the sclera, iris, and brightness.
  8. Akruti (General Appearance): Evaluating the overall physical build and posture.

Deep Dive: Nadi (Pulse) and Jihva (Tongue) Analysis

Nadi Pariksha: The Bio-Rhythm of the Doshas

In Level 2, we introduce the Vata, Pitta, and Kapha pulses. While medical doctors use pulse to measure heart rate, the Ayurveda Practitioner uses it to feel the "vibration" of the doshas:

  • Vata Pulse (The Snake): Fast, feeble, cold, and irregular. Found under the index finger.
  • Pitta Pulse (The Frog): Jumping, forceful, hot, and rhythmic. Found under the middle finger.
  • Kapha Pulse (The Swan): Slow, steady, heavy, and soft. Found under the ring finger.

Jihva Pariksha: The Mirror of the Gut

The tongue is considered the "map" of the internal organs. A healthy tongue should be pink, slightly moist, and free of coating. In clinical practice, the presence of a coating (Lepa) is the #1 indicator of Ama (metabolic toxins) in the digestive tract.

Legal and Ethical Scope for the L2 Practitioner

As an Ayurveda Practitioner, it is vital to understand your Scope of Practice. In the United States and many other Western countries, Ayurveda is categorized as "Complementary and Alternative Medicine" (CAM).

  • Do Not Diagnose: We do not "diagnose" Western diseases (e.g., "You have Type 2 Diabetes"). Instead, we "assess doshic imbalances" (e.g., "I observe a high Pitta-Kapha imbalance affecting your Agni").
  • Do Not Treat: We "balance," "support," or "optimize."
  • Physical Touch: If practicing in person, always ask for permission before touching the wrist for pulse or the abdomen for palpation. In many states, a "Health Freedom Act" protects your right to practice as long as you provide a clear disclosure statement.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which pillar of Trividha Pariksha involves the visual assessment of Ojas and Ama?

Darshana (Observation). This includes observing the skin, eyes, gait, and general vitality of the client.

2. If a client presents with a "slippery, heavy, and slow" pulse, which dosha is likely dominant in their Vikriti?

Kapha. The Kapha pulse is often compared to a "Swan" (Gaja-gamini)‚Äîsteady, slow, and heavy.

3. What is the primary clinical significance of a thick coating on the tongue (Jihva)?

The presence of Ama (metabolic toxins). The location of the coating can also indicate which organ systems (colon, small intestine, or stomach) are most affected.

4. True or False: An Ayurveda Practitioner can legally tell a client they have "Clinical Depression."

False. Practitioners must avoid Western medical diagnoses. Instead, you would describe the state as a "Vata imbalance in the Manovaha Srotas (mental channels)."

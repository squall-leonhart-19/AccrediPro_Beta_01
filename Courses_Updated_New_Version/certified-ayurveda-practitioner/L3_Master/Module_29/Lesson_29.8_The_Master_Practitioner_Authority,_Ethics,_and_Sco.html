The Teaching Approach: The Socratic Method

Instead of telling Lisa she is "wrong," we use clinical reasoning questions to help her find the answer. This builds the clinical muscle memory she needs to work independently.

Questioning Framework:

  • The "Why" Question: "Lisa, what led you to choose Shatavari for this specific stage of Diane's digestion?"
  • The "Order" Question: "In the P.R.A.N.A. Methodâ„¢, do we address the *Dhatus* (tissues) or the *Agni* (fire) first when *Ama* is present?"
  • The "Observation" Question: "If we look at Diane's tongue photo again, what do you notice about the coating in the back third?"
Coach Sarah's Insight

When you ask a question rather than giving an answer, you are giving your mentee the gift of discovery. This is how you move from being a "boss" to being a "guru" (one who removes darkness).

Feedback Dialogue: Delivering Constructive Support

How you deliver feedback determines whether Lisa leaves the session feeling empowered or defeated. We use the Validate-Challenge-Validate model.

Sample Script: The Mentoring Session

Sarah (You): "Lisa, I love how thoroughly you documented Diane's sleep patterns. Your dedication to her comfort is clear. (Validate)"

Sarah (You): "However, looking at the *Ama* on her tongue, I'm concerned that the heavy nutritive herbs we're using might be 'clogging' the fire further. What would happen if we paused the Shatavari for one week and focused purely on *Deepana* (kindling fire)? (Challenge)"

Sarah (You): "You have such a sharp eye for her emotional state; I know you'll be able to explain this shift to her in a way that feels supportive, not restrictive. (Validate)"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Socratic Method" in clinical supervision?

The goal is to develop the mentee's own clinical reasoning and "muscle memory" by guiding them to the answer through strategic questioning, rather than simply providing the solution.

2. Why should heavy nutritive herbs (like Shatavari) be avoided when a client has high Ama and low Agni?

Nutritive herbs are heavy and difficult to digest. If the digestive fire (Agni) is weak and toxins (Ama) are present, these herbs will not be absorbed properly and will likely contribute to further Ama accumulation, worsening the client's symptoms.

3. In the "Validate-Challenge-Validate" feedback model, what is the purpose of the final validation?

The final validation ensures the mentee leaves the session feeling capable and empowered, reinforcing their existing strengths so they have the confidence to implement the "challenge" or correction provided.

4. True or False: A Master Practitioner should always have the answer immediately to maintain authority.

False. Modeling humility and the practice of returning to classical texts or research when unsure actually builds more trust and demonstrates professional integrity.

Key Takeaways for the Master Mentor

  • Empowerment over Correction: Use questions to help mentees "discover" clinical truths.
  • Mentorship is a Business: Professional supervision is a high-value service that honors your years of experience.

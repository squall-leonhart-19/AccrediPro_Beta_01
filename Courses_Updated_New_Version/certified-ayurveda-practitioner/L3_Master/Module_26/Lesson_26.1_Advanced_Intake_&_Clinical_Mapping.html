The Art of the 90-Minute Intake Mastery

In a professional Ayurvedic practice, the initial intake is your most critical touchpoint. It is where you establish the Therapeutic Alliance. While a standard health coach might spend 30 minutes on goals, an Ayurvedic Practitioner conducts a deep-dive investigation into the client's entire biological history.

A professional 90-minute intake should be structured as follows:

Phase Time Focus Area
The Narrative 20 Mins Client's health history, "The Story" of their symptoms, and emotional landscape.
P.R.A.N.A. Audit 30 Mins Detailed assessment of Agni, Ama, Dinacharya, and Ojas.
Physical Assessment 20 Mins Pulse (Nadi), Tongue (Jihva), and Eye/Skin analysis.
The Roadmap 20 Mins Presenting the 3-month vision and immediate "Quick Win" protocols.
Practitioner Insight

Don't rush the "Narrative" phase. For many clients, especially women over 40 who have felt dismissed by conventional medicine, being heard is the first step of healing. Your ability to hold space for their full story is why you can charge $250-$450 for an initial intake.

Advanced Prakriti vs. Vikriti Profiling

One of the most common mistakes in amateur Ayurveda is treating the Prakriti (the birth constitution) instead of the Vikriti (the current imbalance). As a practitioner, your job is to identify the gap between these two states.

Pulse (Nadi Vigyan) Clinical Markers

In an advanced setting, we look for the Gati (movement) of the pulse to determine the state of the Doshas:

  • Vata Pulse: Like a snake (Sarpagati). Fast, feeble, cold, and irregular.
  • Pitta Pulse: Like a frog (Mandukagati). Jumping, forceful, hot, and regular.
  • Kapha Pulse: Like a swan (Hamsagati). Slow, steady, soft, and wavy.

Case Study: The Burned-Out Educator

Client: Elena, 52, former school principal.

Presenting Symptoms: Insomnia, acid reflux, and sudden weight gain around the midsection.

Assessment: Elena's Prakriti was Kapha-Pitta (sturdy, organized). However, her Vikriti showed high Vata (insomnia) and aggravated Pitta (reflux).

Intervention: Instead of giving her Kapha-reducing exercises (which would aggravate her Vata), the practitioner focused on Vata-pacifying Dinacharya and Pitta-cooling nutrition.

Outcome: Within 6 weeks, sleep normalized, and reflux vanished, allowing her metabolism (Agni) to naturally reset.

Clinical Mapping: The Client Health Roadmap

Your clients aren't just buying a consultation; they are buying a result. To provide this, you must map out their journey. Vague advice leads to poor compliance; a Health Roadmap leads to transformation.

The 3-Phase Milestone Framework

  1. Phase 1 (Months 1-3): Stabilization & Detox. Focus on Agni optimization and Ama clearance. Goal: Symptom reduction by 40-60%.
  2. Phase 2 (Months 4-6): Tissue Regeneration (Rasayana). Focus on Dhatu (tissue) health and Ojas cultivation. Goal: Sustained energy and hormonal balance.
  3. Phase 3 (Months 7-12): Lifestyle Mastery. Focus on Ritucharya (seasonal alignment) and emotional resilience. Goal: Full autonomy and prevention.
Financial Freedom Tip

By presenting a 6-month roadmap during the intake, you move away from "session-by-session" billing. Professional practitioners often package these roadmaps into programs ranging from $2,500 to $5,000, ensuring client commitment and practitioner stability.

Legal & Ethical Boundaries

As you step into clinical practice, you must protect yourself and your clients. Ayurveda in the West is practiced as "Complementary and Integrative Health," not as a replacement for primary medical care.

Scope of Practice Essentials

  • Never "Diagnose" or "Treat" Disease: Use Ayurvedic terminology. Instead of "treating your diabetes," you are "balancing your Kapha and optimizing Medas Dhatu Agni."
  • Informed Consent: Every client must sign a document stating they understand you are an Ayurvedic Practitioner, not a licensed medical doctor.
  • The Referral Network: A true professional knows when to refer out. If a client presents with "red flag" symptoms (unexplained weight loss, severe pain, etc.), your first action is to refer them to their PCP.
Authority Tip

Your confidence in your scope of practice actually increases your authority. Clients respect a practitioner who knows exactly where their expertise begins and ends.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it critical to treat the Vikriti rather than the Prakriti during an initial consultation?

Treating the Prakriti (birth constitution) while a client has an active Vikriti (imbalance) can actually worsen the imbalance. For example, giving stimulating Kapha-reducing herbs to a Kapha person who is currently suffering from Vata-type anxiety would aggravate their Vata further. Always stabilize the Vikriti first.

2. What is the "Sarpagati" pulse movement indicative of?

Sarpagati (snake-like) is the characteristic movement of a Vata pulse. It is felt under the index finger and is typically fast, thin, and irregular.

3. How does a 6-month roadmap benefit the practitioner-client relationship?

It sets realistic expectations for healing (as Ayurvedic transformation takes time), increases client compliance by showing them the "big picture," and shifts the practitioner's business model toward high-value packages rather than sporadic sessions.

4. What is the legal requirement regarding "diagnosing" medical conditions?

Ayurvedic practitioners in most Western jurisdictions are not licensed to diagnose or treat medical diseases. You must use Ayurvedic assessment language (Dosha imbalances, Agni states) and always include a medical disclaimer in your intake forms.

Key Takeaways

  • The 90-minute intake is a clinical investigation, not just a conversation; use the P.R.A.N.A. framework to structure it.
  • Mastering the distinction between Prakriti and Vikriti ensures you are treating the current pathology, not the genetic blueprint.
  • Physical markers (pulse/tongue) provide objective data that validates the client's subjective narrative.
  • A structured Health Roadmap (3/6/12 months) is the key to both clinical success and professional income growth.
  • Strict adherence to scope of practice and informed consent is non-negotiable for professional legitimacy.

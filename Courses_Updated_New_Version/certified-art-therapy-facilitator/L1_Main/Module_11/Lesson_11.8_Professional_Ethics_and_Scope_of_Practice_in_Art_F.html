Elena has come to you because she feels "gray." She spent 25 years in a high-pressure corporate environment and now, as she looks for her next chapter, she feels disconnected from her own desires. She is highly articulate but struggles to describe her emotions without using "corporate speak."

The Challenge: Elena is very worried about "doing it wrong." When she sees the art supplies on your table, she visibly tenses her shoulders and makes a joke about how she "can't even draw a stick figure."

Sarah's Insight

When a client like Elena makes a joke about stick figures, they aren't just being funnyâ€”they are protecting themselves from the fear of being judged. Your job isn't to tell her she's a great artist; it's to change the rules of the game so "great art" isn't the goal.

The Coaching Script

Read this script through once to understand the flow. Then, we will practice it out loud. Notice how the facilitator uses normalization and process-oriented language.

YOU (Facilitator):
"Elena, I noticed you mentioned the stick figures. I want to clear something up right awayâ€”in this space, we aren't actually making 'art' in the traditional sense. We're using these colors and lines as a different kind of vocabulary. If your stick figure feels like it's carrying a heavy backpack, that's a successful image. How does that sound?"
ELENA:
"It sounds... okay. But I honestly don't know where to start. My brain just goes blank when I look at that white paper."
YOU (Facilitator):
"That blank-page feeling is completely normal. Let's try something simple. Instead of thinking about a 'thing' to draw, just look at the tray of pastels. Which color feels the most like your energy today? Don't think about itâ€”just let your hand hover and see which one it wants to pick up."
ELENA:
(Picks up a deep indigo blue) "I guess this one. It feels... quiet. Maybe a little heavy."
YOU (Facilitator):
"Perfect. Now, without trying to make a shape, just let that indigo move on the paper. It could be a scribble, a solid block, or just a few dots. Let the color tell us about that 'quiet heaviness' you mentioned. There is no wrong way to let that color land."
Facilitation Key

Notice how I didn't say 'Draw your heaviness.' I said 'Let the color tell us.' This externalizes the process and makes it feel less like a performance and more like an exploration.

Practice Exercise: Out Loud

It's time to find your Facilitator's Voice. Follow these steps to build your "muscle memory" for client sessions.

1
The Normalization Prompt: Speak this out loud to an empty chair. "Elena, most people who sit here feel exactly like you do right now. We've been taught that art is for 'artists,' but I believe marks on paper are for everyone. Today, we're just playing with color to see what it has to tell us."
2
The Transition: Practice the bridge from talking to doing. "Before we talk more, let's just spend three minutes with the paper. Pick any material that feels 'right' in your handâ€”maybe the chalk feels smoother or the marker feels bolder. Just make some marks that represent how your morning has felt so far."
3
The "Witness" Statement: Practice how to respond when they finish. "I see a lot of movement in these red lines over here. And then it gets very still in the center. What was it like for you to move from the red to that stillness?"
Pro Tip

Record yourself on your phone. Listen back. Do you sound rushed? Warm? Professional? Aim for a tone that is "calmly curious."

Success Markers & Common Pitfalls

As you transition from your current career (whether you're a nurse, teacher, or mom), you might bring old habits into the facilitation room. Let's look at the difference between an "Expert" and a "Facilitator."

The "Expert" Trap (Avoid) The Facilitator Path (Practice)
Giving compliments: "That's a beautiful drawing!" Observing process: "I noticed you used a lot of pressure there."
Interpreting: "That dark circle must mean you're sad." Inquiring: "What can you tell me about this dark circle?"
Rushing: "Let's finish this in 5 minutes." Holding space: "Take all the time you need with that section."
Fixing: "Try adding some yellow to cheer it up." Validating: "The gray feels very present here. Let's sit with that."

She now runs 'Creative Clarity' groups for executive women, charging $450 for a 3-hour workshop. Legitimacy comes from your presence, not your ability to paint!

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to avoid saying "That's a beautiful drawing" to a client?

Complimenting the "beauty" reinforces the idea that the art is a product to be judged. This can make the client self-conscious and focused on pleasing you rather than exploring their internal process.

2. What is the primary goal of "normalization" during the first session?

To lower the client's anxiety and "performance pressure" by letting them know that feeling nervous, un-creative, or "blank" is a standard part of the process.

3. A client says, "I don't know what this means." What is the best facilitator response?

Instead of providing a meaning, ask: "That's okay. If the image could speak, what's the first word it might say?" or "What do you notice in your body as you look at it?"

4. How does "hovering the hand" help a stuck client?

It bypasses the analytical "thinking" brain and encourages a somatic (body-based) choice, making the entry into the art-making feel more intuitive and less like a test.

Key Takeaways

  • Process Over Product: Always redirect the client's focus from what the art "looks like" to what the making "felt like."
  • The Facilitator's Voice: Use a warm, curious, and non-judgmental tone to create a safe container.
  • Normalization is Key: Validate the client's fear of the "blank page" to build immediate rapport.
  • Observation, Not Interpretation: Describe the lines, colors, and shapes you see rather than telling the client what they mean.
  • You Are the Witness: Your primary role is to hold space for the client's discovery, not to be the "art expert."

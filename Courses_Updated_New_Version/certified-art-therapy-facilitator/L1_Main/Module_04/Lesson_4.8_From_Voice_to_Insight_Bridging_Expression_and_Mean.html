The Facilitation Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the pacing and the warmth in your tone. You are the "anchor" for her exploration.

YOU:
"Elena, I hear you saying that art feels like a 'waste of time.' I want to invite you to look at that phrase. If that 'waste of time' voice had a color or a shape, what would it look like in your mind right now?"

"It‚Äôs grey. Like a heavy fog. It just sits there and makes everything feel... heavy. I feel silly even talking about it."
YOU:
"There is nothing silly about acknowledging what‚Äôs standing in your way. That 'grey fog' has been a protector for a long time, keeping you focused on 'productive' things. But today, we're giving your other voice a turn. If we were to push that fog to the edge of the paper, what color is hiding underneath it?"
ELENA:
"I think... maybe a soft yellow? Like the sun hitting the grass. I haven't seen that color in a long time."
YOU:
"Let's start there. I‚Äôm going to invite you to pick up the yellow pastel. Don't worry about a 'picture.' Just let the yellow speak on the paper while we keep talking. What is that yellow voice trying to say to you today?"
Facilitator Tip

Notice how you didn't ask her to "draw a sun." You asked her to let the color speak. This bypasses the analytical brain and goes straight to the expressive "Voice."

Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

When you're first starting, it's easy to fall into "Teacher Mode" instead of "Facilitator Mode." Watch for these common mistakes.

1

Mistake: Over-Explaining the Prompt

If you talk for 5 minutes explaining why art is good for her, you are taking away her "Voice" time. Keep instructions under 60 seconds.

2

Mistake: Praising the "Artwork"

Avoid saying "That's a beautiful painting!" Instead, say "I see so much energy in those yellow lines." Focus on the process, not the product.

Sarah's Pro-Tip

Always have a "scribble sheet" next to you. If a client is nervous, start scribbling on your own sheet while you talk. It lowers the "performance anxiety" in the room significantly!

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "I don't know what to draw." According to the Voice protocol, what is the best response?

Ask them to choose a color based on their current feeling rather than a subject. For example: "If your silence had a color, which one would it be?" This shifts them from "drawing" to "expressing."

2. Why do we avoid using the word "Art" in these sessions?

The word "Art" often carries heavy baggage of judgment, skill, and "good vs. bad." Using terms like "expression," "marks," or "voice" lowers the barrier to entry for non-artists.

3. What is a "muted narrative"?

A muted narrative is a story a client tells themselves that minimizes their needs or desires (e.g., "It's selfish for me to spend money on paint"). Your job is to help them "unmute" this story.

4. How do you know a "Voice" session was successful?

Success is marked by the client's shift from "I should" to "I am." When the client begins to speak about their marks with "I" statements and shows a visible relaxation in their shoulders/breath.

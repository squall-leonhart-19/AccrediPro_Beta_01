Practice Exercise: Your Turn

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

In my first year, I made all of these! See if you recognize any of these tendencies in yourself.

4 Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-explaining: Telling the client *why* you are centering them for five minutes (which actually adds to the noise).
  • The "Interrogation" Tone: Asking "Are you relaxed yet?" instead of inviting them into relaxation.
  • Ignoring the Body: Focusing only on words and ignoring the fact that the client is still fidgeting or gripping their bag.
  • Rushing the Silence: Feeling awkward during the 10-20 seconds of quiet and filling it with "filler" talk.
Sarah's Pro Tip

Silence is your most powerful tool. When you are comfortable with silence, your client learns that they don't have to perform for you. That is the beginning of true creative freedom.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Center" phase critical before starting art facilitation?

It shifts the client from the sympathetic (fight/flight) to the parasympathetic (rest/digest) nervous system, allowing for deeper creative expression and cortisol reduction.

2. What is a "success marker" that Brenda has successfully centered?

Physical indicators like a visible drop in the shoulders, a deeper and slower breath, and a softening of the facial muscles.

3. How should a facilitator handle a client who resists the centering process?

Validate their energy, shorten the exercise to "micro-moments" (e.g., 10 seconds), and frame it as a functional transition rather than a spiritual or meditative requirement.

4. What is the recommended speaking pace for a centering exercise?

Approximately 20% slower than your natural conversational pace, with intentional pauses between sentences.

Key Takeaways

  • Centering is the Container: Without it, the art is just a task; with it, the art is a transformation.

  • Permission to Land: Most women in the 40-55 demographic are "givers." Centering gives them permission to be "receivers."

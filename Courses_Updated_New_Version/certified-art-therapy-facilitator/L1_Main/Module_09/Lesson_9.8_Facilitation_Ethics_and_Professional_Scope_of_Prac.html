The Facilitation Script

Practice these lines out loud. Notice the pacingâ€”slow, warm, and non-judgmental.

You (Facilitator):
"Elena, I notice you're spending some time with the paper. I'm curious, what's happening in your thoughts right now as you look at that white space?"
Elena:
"I just... I don't want to mess it up. I have the idea in my head, but I know I can't draw it 'right.' It feels silly."
You (Facilitator):
"I hear that desire for it to be 'right.' What if we invited the 'mess' in for a moment? What would happen if you used your non-dominant hand just to make a single, scribble-mark anywhere on the page?"
Elena:
"My left hand? It'll look like a child did it."
You (Facilitator):
"Exactly. Let's see what that 'inner child' wants to say without the pressure of being a 'teacher' or a 'professional.' There are no grades here, Elena. Just marks and energy."

4 Common Facilitator Mistakes

1. Over-Explaining

New facilitators often talk too much to fill the "awkward" silence. Let the silence breathe; it's where the client's internal shift happens.

2. Being "The Expert"

Avoid saying "You should draw it like this." Instead, use "I wonder what would happen if..."

3. Rushing the Release

If a client starts to cry or get angry, don't hand them a tissue immediately (which can signal 'stop crying'). Let the emotion flow for a moment first.

4. Ignoring the Body

If the client is stuck, ask them where they feel that "stuckness" in their body (chest, throat, hands). Connect the art to the physical sensation.

Facilitator Presence

Remember, you are the "container" for their experience. If you stay calm, they feel safe enough to be messy. Your presence is 50% of the therapy.

Check Your Understanding

1. When Elena says she's afraid to "mess up," what is the most effective facilitator response?

Validate her feeling first ("I hear that desire for it to be right"), then offer a low-stakes invitation to play (e.g., using the non-dominant hand or a "scribble" mark).

2. Why is using the non-dominant hand a powerful facilitation tool for perfectionists?

It bypasses the "logical/critical" side of the brain and reduces the expectation of technical skill, allowing for more authentic emotional expression.

3. True or False: You should wait at least 15-20 minutes of total silence before intervening when a client is stuck.

False. While silence is good, 15-20 minutes can lead to "shame spirals." Usually, 5-8 minutes of observation is enough before offering a gentle inquiry.

4. What is a "Success Marker" in this specific lab scenario?

When Elena makes her first mark on the page, regardless of what it looks like, and her physical posture (shoulders/grip) visibly relaxes.

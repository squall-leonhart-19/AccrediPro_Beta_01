Practice Exercise: Say It Out Loud

Facilitation is a muscle. You wouldn't expect to run a marathon without training; don't expect to lead a breakthrough session without vocal practice.

1
The "Permission" Pivot
Practice saying this: "We're going to move from talking about the problem to 'seeing' the problem. Pick up a tool that feels 'right' in your hand." Record yourself. Do you sound rushed or calm?
2
The "Non-Judgmental" Observation
Practice observing without interpreting. Say: "I notice you chose the red marker and your movements are very fast. What does that speed feel like in your body?" Avoid saying: "You look angry."
Sarah's Insight

New facilitators often feel they need to be "The Expert" who interprets the art. Stop! Your job is to be the witness. If you tell the client what their art means, you rob them of their own breakthrough (and their own power).

Common Facilitation Mistakes

Mistake Why It Happens The "Pro" Correction
The Clinical Trap Imposter syndrome makes us want to sound like a therapist. Use "Facilitator Language": Focus on the *process* and the *image*, not the diagnosis.
Rushing the "A" Phase Fear of silence or the client's discomfort with art. Hold the space. Allow at least 3-5 minutes of silent creation before asking questions.
Over-Instruction Wanting the client to produce something "pretty." Keep prompts open-ended: "Make a mark" vs. "Draw a tree."
The "Fix-It" Reflex We want the client to feel better immediately. Trust the Framework. Let them sit with the "jagged lines" before moving to "Sustainability."

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these three signs:

  • Somatic Shift: The client‚Äôs breathing slows down, or their shoulders drop during the art-making process.
  • Language Change: The client stops saying "I should" and starts saying "I notice" or "I feel."
  • Image Ownership: The client refers to the art as a separate entity (e.g., "This blue area feels like my peace") rather than just a drawing.

Check Your Understanding

1. In the script, why did the coach ask Elena to use "blue oil pastel" specifically for the pressure exploration?

Actually, the color choice is secondary to the texture. Oil pastels allow for physical pressure and tactile feedback, which helps "Access" the somatic (body) tension Elena was feeling. Giving a specific tool choice also reduces the "analysis paralysis" of a client who is already feeling overwhelmed.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Clarify" phase in Elena's case?

The goal is to move the internal feeling (Imposter Syndrome/Pressure) to an external medium (the paper). This creates "aesthetic distance," allowing Elena to look at her problem rather than being consumed by it.

3. If Elena said, "I don't know what these lines mean," how should you respond?

Avoid interpreting for her. Instead, say: "That's perfectly okay. You don't need to know yet. If these lines had a sound, what would they sound like?" or "If you had to give this image a title right now, without overthinking, what would it be?"

4. Why is it important to mention that "successful women" also feel Imposter Syndrome?

This is Normalizing. It builds the Connection (the first step of the framework) and lowers the client's shame, which is the biggest barrier to creative Access.

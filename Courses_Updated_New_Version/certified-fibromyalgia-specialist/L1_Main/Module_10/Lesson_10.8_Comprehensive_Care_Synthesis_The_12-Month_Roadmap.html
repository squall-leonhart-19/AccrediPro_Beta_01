Common Coaching Pitfalls

Even experienced coaches can stumble when integrating with medical teams. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • Mistake 1: Counter-manding a Doctor. Never say "Your doctor is wrong." Instead, say "Let's ask your doctor to help us understand why they recommended that, given your current symptoms."
  • Mistake 2: Taking it Personally. If a doctor is dismissive of coaching, don't get defensive. Stay professional and let the client's improved outcomes speak for themselves.
  • Mistake 3: Over-stepping Scope. Avoid saying "You should take X supplement." Instead, say "Here is some research on X supplement you might want to discuss with your physician."
  • Mistake 4: Failing to Document. Always keep notes of what the client reports from their medical visits so you can track patterns over time.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client tells you their doctor "doesn't believe" in fibromyalgia. What is the most professional collaborative response?

Validate the client's frustration first, then help them decide if they want to advocate for themselves with that provider or seek a second opinion. Avoid bad-mouthing the doctor, but emphasize that the client deserves a provider who validates their experience.

2. What is the primary purpose of the "Three-Question Prep" technique?

To ensure the client stays focused during short medical appointments, addresses their most pressing concerns, and leaves with a clear, actionable plan.

3. True or False: It is within a coach's scope to tell a client to stop taking a medication if it's causing side effects.

False. Coaches must never advise a client to stop or change medication. Instead, the coach should help the client document the side effects and prepare them to discuss those specific observations with their prescribing physician immediately.

4. Why is the "Chief of Staff" analogy useful for clients?

It reinforces that the client is the "CEO" (the decision-maker) while positioning the coach as a high-level support person who organizes information and facilitates the "business" of their health.

Success Markers

How do you know if your collaborative integration coaching is working? Look for these signs:

  • The Client feels "heard": They report feeling more confident going into appointments.
  • Reduced Stress: The client stops feeling like they are "failing" their providers.
  • Doctor Feedback: You receive a note or a referral from a client's physician because they noticed how well-prepared the client was.
  • Clearer Data: The client begins tracking symptoms in a way that is actually useful for clinical decision-making.

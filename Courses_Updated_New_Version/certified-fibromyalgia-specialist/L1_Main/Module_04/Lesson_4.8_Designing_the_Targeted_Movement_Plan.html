Common Coaching Pitfalls

Avoid these 4 common mistakes when discussing movement with Fibromyalgia clients:

  • Mistake 1: Using the word "Exercise" too early. For many, this word is associated with pain. Use "Movement," "Activity," or "Mobility" instead.
  • Mistake 2: Over-prescribing. Suggesting a 30-minute walk to a client who can currently only manage 5 minutes is a recipe for failure.
  • Mistake 3: Ignoring the "Bust" phase. If a client is in a flare, don't tell them to "push through." Validate the flare and pivot to restorative breathing.
  • Mistake 4: Taking the "Expert" stance. Instead of saying "You must do this," ask "How would it feel to try 2 minutes of this?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Brenda says, "I don't have time to do these 'pacing' breaks. I just need to get things done." How do you respond?

Validate her need for productivity, then reframe: "I hear you‚Äîyou have a lot on your plate. What if we looked at pacing not as 'taking breaks,' but as 'insurance' to make sure you don't lose three days next week to a flare? Which approach actually gets more done in the long run?"

2. What is the primary goal of a "Movement Baseline"?

The goal is to find the amount of movement the client can do consistently, even on their worst days, without triggering a flare. This builds confidence and safety in the nervous system.

3. True or False: If a client feels great, they should double their movement for that day.

False. This is the "Boom" part of the cycle. You should encourage them to stick close to their planned increase (usually no more than 10%) to avoid a subsequent "Bust."

4. Why is "Stopping while you still feel good" a difficult concept for fibro clients?

Most clients are high-achievers who feel guilty when they aren't "productive." They feel they must take advantage of every ounce of energy they have, not realizing this depletes their "battery" for the next day.

Key Takeaways

  • The Boom-Bust Cycle is the number one enemy of consistent progress in Fibromyalgia recovery.
  • Your role is to act as an "Energy Accountant," helping clients budget their limited resources.
  • Use metaphors (like the battery or the bank account) to make complex physiological concepts easy to understand.
  • Success is measured by consistency, not intensity.

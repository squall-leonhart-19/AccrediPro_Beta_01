The Hypermobility Connection: hEDS and Fibromyalgia

A significant clinical overlap exists between Fibromyalgia and Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (hEDS) or Hypermobility Spectrum Disorders (HSD). Research suggests that up to 30-40% of patients diagnosed with Fibromyalgia also meet the criteria for systemic joint laxity.

For these clients, the pain is not just "centralized"‚Äîit is driven by mechanical instability. When joints are "loose," the surrounding muscles must work overtime to provide stability. This leads to chronic muscle guarding, fatigue, and eventually, the very myofascial pain that characterizes Fibromyalgia.

If a client describes themselves as "double-jointed" or says they can "touch their toes without trying" but still feel "stiff," suspect hypermobility. Their stiffness is a neurological defense mechanism, not a lack of flexibility. Do NOT prescribe passive stretching for these clients!

Fascia: The Sensory Matrix

Fascia is no longer viewed as just "packing material" for muscles. It is our largest sensory organ, rich in mechanoreceptors and nociceptors (pain receptors). In Fibromyalgia, the fascia often becomes densified or "sticky" due to chronic inflammation and lack of varied movement.

This "sticky" fascia sends a constant stream of "danger" signals to the CNS. If the CNS is already sensitized (Central Sensitization), it amplifies these fascial signals, creating a feedback loop of pain and restricted movement. Targeted movement aims to "rehydrate" this tissue and normalize the sensory input.

Myofascial Trigger Points: The "Ischemic Crisis"

Myofascial Trigger Points (MTrPs) are hyperirritable spots in skeletal muscle associated with a hypersensitive palpable nodule in a taut band. In Fibromyalgia, these are often the "peripheral generators" that keep the central pain furnace burning.

Isometric vs. Isotonic Strategy

When designing a movement plan for the Fibromyalgia CNS, the type of contraction matters more than the weight used. We categorize movement into two main types:

  • Isotonic (Dynamic): Muscle changes length (e.g., a bicep curl). This requires high CNS coordination and can be provocative for sensitized joints.
  • Isometric (Static): Muscle creates tension without changing length (e.g., pushing against a wall). This is the "Gold Standard" for early-stage Fibromyalgia movement.

Why Isometrics Work for Fibromyalgia:

  1. Analgesic Effect: Research shows that sustained submaximal isometric contractions can induce "Exercise-Induced Hypoalgesia" (EIH)‚Äîliterally a temporary numbing of the nervous system.
  2. Joint Stability: For hypermobile clients, isometrics strengthen the muscles around the joint without the "shearing" forces of dynamic movement.
  3. Proprioceptive Input: Isometrics provide clear, steady data to the brain about where the body is in space, reducing the "fear-avoidance" response.
Protocol Tip

Start with 10-second holds at 20-30% of maximum effort. The goal is neurological safety, not muscle failure. If the client holds their breath or shakes, the intensity is too high for their CNS.

Aquatic Therapy Principles

During a flare or for clients with severe mechanical pain, the water is our greatest ally. Aquatic therapy utilizes two physical properties that air does not:

1. Buoyancy: Reduces the effects of gravity. In chest-deep water, the body only bears 10-25% of its actual weight. This allows for movement that would be impossible or painful on land.

2. Hydrostatic Pressure: The water exerts equal pressure on all parts of the submerged body. This acts like a "compression sleeve" for the entire nervous system, providing massive sensory input that can "drown out" pain signals (The Gate Control Theory).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is passive stretching often counterproductive for a hypermobile Fibromyalgia client?

In hypermobile clients, "stiffness" is often protective muscle guarding. Stretching bypasses the joint's natural limits and can trigger the brain to tighten muscles even further to prevent injury, leading to a pain flare.

2. What is the "Exercise-Induced Hypoalgesia" (EIH) effect associated with isometrics?

It is a temporary reduction in pain sensitivity following a specific type of exercise (like submaximal isometrics) caused by the release of endogenous opioids and endocannabinoids in the CNS.

3. What percentage of Fibromyalgia clients are estimated to have comorbid hypermobility?

Approximately 30-40% of Fibromyalgia clients meet the criteria for systemic joint laxity or hypermobility.

4. How does hydrostatic pressure in aquatic therapy assist the Fibromyalgia CNS?

It provides constant, uniform sensory input to the skin and muscles, which can help "gate" or block pain signals and reduce peripheral edema (swelling).

Key Takeaways for the Specialist

  • Screen First: Always use the Beighton Score to screen for hypermobility before prescribing any movement.
  • Isometrics Over Isotonics: Use "holds" instead of "reps" in the early stages to build CNS safety and joint stability.
  • Fascial Awareness: Recognize that chronic "tightness" in Fibromyalgia is often a sensory/neurological issue rather than a structural muscle length issue.
  • Water as a Tool: Utilize warm-water aquatic therapy for flare management and to reduce mechanical loading.
  • The 20% Rule: Keep initial movement intensity at 20-30% of max effort to avoid triggering the "Boom-Bust" cycle.

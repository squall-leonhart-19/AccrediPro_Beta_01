1. The Client Profile: Meet Elena

Client Profile: Elena, 52

Background: Elena is a former elementary school teacher who recently transitioned into a high-pressure administrative role. She was diagnosed with Fibromyalgia three years ago. She is "ambitious but exhausted."

Presenting Symptoms: Elena arrives at the session speaking very quickly. She mentions her "to-do list" four times in the first two minutes. She reports a "burning" pain in her shoulders and a 4:00 PM energy crash that leaves her weeping.

Overdrive Markers: Rapid speech, shallow chest breathing, high-pitched vocal tone, and fidgeting with her pen.

Goal: "I just want to be able to get through my workday without feeling like I'm going to explode or collapse."

Sarah's Pro Tip

When you see a client like Elena—fast-talking and fidgeting—your first instinct might be to take notes faster. Stop. Your job is to be the "anchor." If you speed up with her, you both end up in overdrive. Take a deep breath yourself before you even open your mouth.

2. The Coaching Script: The Transition

In this scenario, Elena has just spent five minutes listing everything that went wrong this week. Your goal is to interrupt gracefully and move into a regulation exercise.

ELENA:

"...and then the printer broke, and my boss asked for the report early, and I can just feel my neck tightening up, Sarah. I feel like I'm failing at everything."

YOU (The Coach):

"Elena, I'm going to pause you right there for just a second. I can hear how much has been on your plate this week, and I can actually feel that tension you're describing in your neck just by listening to you."

ELENA:

"It's just so much. I don't know where to start."

YOU (The Coach):

"We are going to start right here, in this moment. Before we look at the to-do list, we need to look at your nervous system. Right now, your body is in 'Overdrive'—it's trying to protect you from all that stress, but it's actually increasing your pain signals. Would you be open to doing a 60-second 'Vagus Reset' with me before we continue?"

ELENA:

"I... okay. Yes. I need something."

3. Practice Exercises: Say It Out Loud

It’s time to practice. Don't just read these—actually say them out loud to the room. If you feel silly, that's a sign you're growing!

1

The "Vocal Anchor" Exercise

Read the coach's lines from the script above again. This time, deliberately lower your pitch and slow down your words by 20%. Notice how it feels to hold a calm space while imagining a frantic client.

2

The "Overdrive Explanation" (The 30-Second Pitch)

Practice explaining overdrive to a client without using jargon. Say this out loud: "Elena, your nervous system is like a car with the gas pedal stuck to the floor. Even when the car is parked, the engine is revving. That 'revving' is what's causing your fatigue and pain. My job is to help you find the brake."

Sarah's Pro Tip

I highly recommend recording yourself on your phone. Listen back to your tone. Do you sound like a peer, or do you sound like an expert guide? We want "Compassionate Authority."

4. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Mistake Why It Fails The "Pro" Correction
The Fix-It Trap Trying to solve her printer problem or boss issues immediately. Regulate the state first, then the story.
Matching Energy Talking fast because the client is talking fast. Be the "thermostat," not the "thermometer." Set the temperature.
Over-Explaining Giving a 10-minute lecture on Polyvagal Theory mid-session. Use simple metaphors (like the stuck gas pedal) and get to the exercise.
Asking "Why" "Why are you so stressed?" makes the client think more. "What are you feeling in your body?" brings the client into the present.

5. Success Markers: How You Know It Worked

You’ll know your regulation intervention was successful when you observe these shifts in the client:

  • The "Sigh" of Relief: A spontaneous, deep exhalation.
  • Softened Shoulders: The physical drop of the trapezius muscles.
  • Eye Contact: The client stops looking around the room and "lands" with you.
  • Slower Speech: They begin to reflect rather than just react.
Sarah's Pro Tip

When Elena sighs, don't jump back into the conversation. Let that sigh hang in the air for 3 seconds. Silence is one of your most powerful coaching tools.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of "The Pivot" in a coaching session?

To move the client from a state of Sympathetic Overdrive (problem-talk) into a regulated state (action/presence) so they can actually process the coaching.

2. What are two "Overdrive Markers" you might see in a client like Elena?

Rapid/high-pitched speech, shallow chest breathing, fidgeting, and dilated pupils or darting eyes.

3. Why is "Matching the Client's Energy" a mistake in this module?

If the coach matches the client's frantic energy, they validate the nervous system's "danger" signal. The coach must remain calm to facilitate "co-regulation."

4. How many clients per week at $150/session does it take to reach a $6,000 monthly income?

10 clients per week. This illustrates the professional viability of specializing in Fibromyalgia coaching.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Regulation must always precede education; a stressed brain cannot learn.
  • Your voice is a tool—use lower pitch and slower pacing to anchor your client.
  • Metaphors (like the stuck gas pedal) are more effective than clinical definitions during a flare-up.
  • Success isn't "fixing" the client's life; it's helping them regulate their response to it.
  • You are building a high-value, professional practice—treat your "lab time" with the same respect you would a client session.

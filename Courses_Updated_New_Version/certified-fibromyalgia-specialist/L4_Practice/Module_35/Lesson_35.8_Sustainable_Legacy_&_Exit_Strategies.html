Phase 1: Deep Listening 10 min
YOU:

3. Handling Objections with Confidence

As you scale, you'll encounter the same three objections. Use these high-authority responses to maintain your professional stance.

Objection The "Sarah" Response The Logic
"I don't think a group is personal enough." "I hear that concern. However, I‚Äôve found that the group dynamic actually provides 24/7 support that I simply can't provide 1-on-1. You're never waiting for our next call to get an answer." Reframes "less personal" as "more support."
"I need to talk to my husband first." "I completely respect that. When you talk to him, what do you think his biggest concern will be: the investment, or whether this will actually work for you?" Identifies the real objection hidden behind the spouse.
"I've spent $5,000 on supplements already." "That's exactly why we need a different approach. You've been buying 'parts' of a solution. This is the first time you're investing in a system that ties it all together." Positions your program as the "missing link."
Sarah's Tip

If someone says they need to "think about it," ask them: "What specifically do you need to think about that we haven't covered?" Usually, it's fear. Remind them that the cost of doing nothing is often higher than the cost of the program.

4. Stating Your Prices Without "The Shiver"

Many practitioners "shiver" when they say their price‚Äîthey lower their voice or speak faster. To scale, you must state your price as a matter-of-fact reality, like the price of a gallon of milk.

Case Study: Mary's Scaling Success

Mary (49), a former teacher, was terrified to charge more than $100 per session. She felt "guilty" because she knew her clients were struggling financially. After our coaching, she realized that by charging $1,500 for a package, she could afford to offer one "scholarship" spot in every group cohort. She went from making $2,000/month to $8,500/month while working 15 fewer hours. She now has more energy for her own family and her clients get better results because they are more "invested" in the process.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a group program often better for a fibromyalgia client than 1-on-1 coaching?

Group programs provide social proof and community support, which reduces the isolation often felt by fibro patients. It also allows the practitioner to offer a lower price point while maintaining a high hourly rate, making the service more accessible.

2. What is the "Cost of Inaction" framework?

It is a coaching technique used during objections to help the client realize that the "price" of staying sick (medical bills, lost wages, missing life events) is actually higher than the "price" of the coaching program.

3. How should you respond when a client says, "I need to talk to my spouse"?

Acknowledge the respect for the partnership, then ask what the spouse's primary concern might be. This uncovers whether the issue is truly the spouse or the client's own lingering doubts about the program's efficacy.

4. What is the primary benefit of the "Phase 3: Scaled" model?

It maximizes income and impact while minimizing "live" hours. This protects the practitioner's energy (vital for those with their own chronic conditions) and allows for a six-figure income on part-time hours.
Sarah's Tip

Don't wait until you're "perfect" to scale. Start your first group when you have 3-5 people interested. You'll learn more from that first small group than you ever will from a textbook!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

New coaches often fall into these traps when working with high-performers:

  • Being too "soft": High-achievers often value data and directness. Don't be afraid to use the scientific terms you've learned. It builds your authority.
  • Focusing only on sleep: While sleep is vital, Elena wants performance now. Show her how to manage her "Internal State" during the workday.
  • Ignoring the "Why": Always explain the neuroscience behind your recommendation. Instead of "Take a walk," say "We need to induce optic flow to quiet the amygdala."
  • Over-promising: Brain health is a practice, not a pill. Set expectations that neuroplasticity takes consistent "re-wiring."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to explain the "Amygdala-PFC" relationship to a client like Elena?

It helps her understand that her "stress" is a biological process where the emotional brain (amygdala) is hijacking her logical brain (PFC), reducing her ability to perform. This removes the shame of "not being strong enough."

2. What neurochemical is primarily responsible for the "reward" and "focus" felt during peak performance?

Dopamine. It drives motivation, focus, and the "aha" moments necessary for high-level problem solving.

3. Elena wants to drink more coffee to stay focused. What is the neurobiological risk of this "grind" mentality?

Excessive caffeine in a stressed state can lead to "over-arousal," pushing the brain past the peak of the Yerkes-Dodson curve into anxiety and cognitive impairment rather than flow.

4. What is "Optic Flow" and how does it help performance?

Optic flow is the visual motion of objects past us as we move (like walking). It has been shown to biologically lower amygdala activity and "reset" the nervous system for better focus.

Common Practitioner Pitfalls

When evaluating conditioning for the first time, even seasoned coaches can fall into these traps. Avoid these to ensure you're providing a premium experience:

  • The "Diagnosis" Trap: Saying "You have an Open Will, so you're insecure." Instead, say "Your chart suggests you might experience pressure to prove yourself."
  • Information Overload: Explaining the history of the Will Center instead of focusing on Beth's current feeling of inadequacy.
  • Ignoring the Authority: For Beth (a Splenic Projector), the solution isn't just "knowing" she's conditioned; it's waiting for the invitation and trusting her spontaneous intuition.
  • The Income Comparison: Don't tell Beth she'll make $10k a month immediately. Instead, share how a practitioner like Linda, a 50-year-old former nurse, charged $250 for a single 'Deconditioning Intensive' once she learned to stop 'proving' her worth and start 'sharing' her wisdom.
Sarah's Insight

In my first year, I used to talk for 50 minutes and let the client talk for 10. Now, I use the "Wait and See" rule. After I point out a conditioned pattern, I stay silent for at least 5 seconds. Let them feel the truth of it.

Check Your Understanding

1. Beth says, "I'm just so lost, I don't know what my purpose is." Which center is likely driving this "Not-Self" question?

The Open G Center. Its Not-Self theme is "searching for direction, love, and identity" instead of letting life bring the direction to them.

2. What is the most professional way to handle a client who becomes defensive about their conditioning?

Validate the behavior as a survival strategy. Say: "That behavior has served you well and kept you safe for a long time. We're just looking at whether it's still the most efficient way for you to move forward."

3. True or False: A defined center can never be conditioned.

False. While open centers are the primary sites of conditioning, defined centers can be conditioned to "shut down" or express themselves in distorted ways based on upbringing.

4. Why is Beth's Splenic Authority important in this deconditioning process?

Because her Splenic hits are instant and quiet. Conditioning (mind-talk) is loud and repetitive. Deconditioning helps her clear the "noise" so she can hear her intuition again.

Success Markers for this Session

  • The client identifies a specific recurring thought pattern (e.g., "I'm not enough").
  • The client feels a sense of relief ("Oh, that's just my Open Will!") rather than shame.
  • You, the coach, remained curious rather than "fixing."
  • The session ends with a practical "Deconditioning Experiment" for the client to try.

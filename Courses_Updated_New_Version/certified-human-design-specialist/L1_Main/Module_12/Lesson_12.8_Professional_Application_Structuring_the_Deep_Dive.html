Sarah's Insight

In my practice, I've found that many 40+ women pivoting careers feel they have to 'prove' their worth by working harder. For a Projector like Elena, this is the quickest path to burnout. Your job is to help her see that her value lies in her perspective, not her productivity.

The Coaching Script

Use the following script to help Elena understand her Projector nature. Practice saying these words out loud to get used to the "flavor" of the language.

YOU:
"Elena, I've looked at your chart, and the first thing I want to tell you is: It makes total sense why you're exhausted. You are what we call a Projector in Human Design. You aren't designed to go-go-go like the rest of the world. You're designed to see deeply into things and guide others."
ELENA:
"But if I don't work hard, how will anything happen? I feel like I'm being lazy if I'm not constantly doing something."
YOU:
"I hear that loud and clear. That's the 'conditioned' voice we all have. But for you, your strategy is to 'Wait for the Invitation.' When you push and initiate, people often can't hear you, or they feel pressured. When you wait to be recognized for your brilliance first, the energy completely shifts. Does that resonate with how things have felt lately?"
ELENA:
"Yes... I feel like I'm shouting into a void. It's so frustrating."
YOU:
"That frustration is actually what we call 'Bitterness' in Human Design, and it's just a signpost. It's your body's way of saying, 'Hey, we're out of alignment.' What if, for the next week, we focused on you doing things that make you feel recognized and successful, rather than just busy?"
Pro Tip

Notice how the script uses Elena's own words ("exhausted," "shouting into a void"). Reflecting a client's language back to them is a powerful coaching tool that builds instant rapport.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why it Happens The Fix
Information Overload Imposter syndrome makes us want to prove we know everything. Focus on ONE key takeaway (like Strategy) per session.
Using Too Much Jargon We forget that the client doesn't know what a "motor" or "gate" is. Use "Everyday Language." Instead of "undefined solar plexus," say "you pick up on others' emotions."
Fixing Instead of Coaching We want to solve their problems immediately. Ask powerful questions. "How does that feel in your body when I say that?"
Ignoring the Body We stay in the intellectual "chart" level. Check in: "Does this feel true for you?" or "Where do you feel that in your body?"

Success Markers

How do you know if your session with Elena (or any client) was successful? Look for these "Green Lights":

  • The "Deep Breath": The client physically exhales or their shoulders drop when they feel understood.
  • The "Aha" Moment: They say something like, "Oh my gosh, that's exactly what I've been feeling my whole life."
  • Shift in Energy: They move from feeling "wrong" or "broken" to feeling "unique" and "designed."
  • Actionable Insight: They leave with one small thing they can do differently (e.g., "I'm going to stop cold-emailing for three days").

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use "Everyday Language" instead of Human Design jargon with a new client?

Jargon can create a barrier between you and the client, making them feel overwhelmed or confused. Using everyday language (e.g., "you pick up on others' stress" vs. "you have an open root") makes the information relatable and immediately applicable to their life.

2. What is the "Not-Self" theme for a Projector like Elena, and why is it a helpful coaching tool?

The Projector Not-Self theme is Bitterness. It's helpful because it acts as a "navigation system." Instead of Elena feeling like a failure, you can teach her to see bitterness as a sign that she is initiating or not waiting for the right invitation.

3. If Elena says, "I feel lazy when I'm not doing something," what is the best coaching response?

Validate her feeling first ("I hear that loud and clear"), then reframe it through her design. Explain that for a Projector, "rest is work" because it prepares them to be ready and clear when the right invitation arrives.

4. What is the most common mistake new coaches make during a first session?

Information Overload. Coaches often try to explain the entire chart at once, which leaves the client overwhelmed. The most successful sessions focus deeply on Strategy and Authority.

Key Lab Takeaways

  • Presence Over Perfection: Your ability to listen and hold space is more important than memorizing every gate in the chart.
  • Strategy is King: If a client only learns their Strategy (e.g., "Wait for the Invitation"), their life will change significantly.
  • Validate the Past: Use Human Design to show the client that their past "failures" were actually just misalignments with their design.
  • Practice Out Loud: Speaking the language of Human Design builds the "muscle memory" needed for professional coaching.

The Anatomy of Inspiration

The Head Center is a "Pressure Center," not an "Awareness Center." This is a critical distinction for the Specialist. While the Ajna Center (below it) processes thoughts, the Head Center provides the fuel for those thoughts. It is the top of the Bodygraph, representing our connection to the "Upper" program or cosmic inspiration.

Biologically, the Head Center is associated with the Pineal Gland. It exerts pressure on the mind to resolve three specific states: Confusion, Mystery, and Doubt. In its healthy state, the Head Center is the source of brilliant inspiration. In its conditioned state, it is the source of paralyzing mental anxiety.

Coach Tip #1

Always remind your clients: The Head is not an Authority. No matter how much pressure the Head exerts, it is never designed to make decisions for their life. Decisions belong to the Body (Inner Authority).

The Defined Head: The Internal Pressure Cooker

Approximately 30% of the population has a Defined Head Center. For these individuals, the pressure to think is consistent and internal. They are "always on."

If you have a Defined Head, your mind is a self-contained processing unit. You don't need external stimulation to start wondering about things; the pressure is just there. The challenge for the Defined Head is that they often feel they must act on their thoughts to relieve the pressure. This leads to burnout and incorrect decision-making.

Characteristics of the Defined Head:

  • Fixed Way of Wondering: They think in specific patterns based on the gates they have defined.
  • Mental Radiators: They project their mental pressure onto others. When they walk into a room, everyone starts thinking more.
  • The Need for Resolution: They can feel uncomfortable if a question is left "open" for too long.

The Undefined Head: The Mental Mirror

The remaining 70% of the population has an Undefined or Open Head Center. For these clients, the pressure is inconsistent and external.

The Undefined Head is like a radio receiver. It picks up the mental questions, doubts, and inspirations of everyone around it. Because the Undefined Center amplifies what it takes in, the mental pressure can feel significantly more intense for an Undefined Head than for a Defined Head.

Feature Defined Head Undefined Head
Source of Pressure Internal / Biological External / Environmental
Thinking Style Fixed and Consistent Fluid and Adaptable

Coach Tip #2

When working with an Undefined Head client, ask: "Who were you around today?" Often, their mental anxiety is simply the amplified pressure of a spouse, boss, or even a person at the next table in a coffee shop.

The Three Gates of Pressure

The Head Center has only three gates, each representing a specific type of mental fuel. Understanding these allows you to be highly specific in your 'Decode' (D) step of the D.E.S.I.G.N. Framework‚Ñ¢.

1. Gate 64: The Gate of Confusion (The Abstract)

Located on the right side, this gate looks to the past. It is the pressure to make sense of a kaleidoscope of memories and experiences. The pressure here is to find the "story" or the "meaning" in what has already happened.

2. Gate 61: The Gate of Mystery (The Individual)

Located in the center, this is the pressure to "know" the unknowable. It is the source of "Inner Truth" and the pressure to solve the mysteries of existence. This is the "Why?" gate that drives philosophers and seekers.

3. Gate 63: The Gate of Doubt (The Logical)

Located on the left side, this gate looks to the future. It is the pressure to find flaws in patterns so that the future can be safe. It is the fuel for scientific inquiry and skepticism.

The Not-Self Mental Trap: The 'Answer Man'

The Not-Self of the Head Center is one of the most common causes of burnout in women over 40. It manifests as the drive to answer everyone else's questions to relieve the pressure of the environment.

If you are a practitioner working with a client who feels "scattered" or "constantly busy but accomplishing nothing," look at their Head Center. They are likely using their precious energy to solve problems that aren't theirs, simply because they can "feel" the question in the air.

Coach Tip #3

Teach your clients the "24-Hour Rule" for mental pressure. If an Undefined Head client feels a "need" to solve something, have them wait 24 hours away from the source of the pressure. If the question disappears, it wasn't theirs.

Coaching the 'Decode' Step (D.E.S.I.G.N. Framework‚Ñ¢)

As a Specialist, your job in the 'Decode' phase is to help the client identify their energetic blueprint. For the Head Center, this involves a "Mental Audit."

Step 1: Identify Definition. Is the center colored in (Defined) or white (Undefined)?
Step 2: Trace the Gates. Which of the three flavors of pressure are they most susceptible to?
Step 3: Source Detection. Ask: "Is this pressure coming from within me, or am I picking it up from the room?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Which biological gland is associated with the Head Center?

The Pineal Gland. It is responsible for our connection to inspiration and light/dark cycles.

2. What is the primary "Not-Self" strategy of the Undefined Head Center?

Trying to resolve everyone else's questions and mental pressure in an attempt to find peace or relieve the amplified stress of the environment.

3. If a client is constantly skeptical and looking for flaws in a plan, which gate are they likely fueled by?

Gate 63: The Gate of Doubt (The Logical pressure to ensure future safety).

4. True or False: A Defined Head Center should use its mental insights to make life decisions.

False. The Head is never an Authority. Decisions must always come from the client's Inner Authority (Sacral, Emotional, Splenic, etc.).

  • Defined Heads (30%) have consistent internal pressure; Undefined Heads (70%) pick up and amplify pressure from others.
  • The three gates (64, 61, 63) determine whether the pressure feels like Confusion, Mystery, or Doubt.
  • In the D.E.S.I.G.N. Framework‚Ñ¢, decoding the Head Center is essential for relieving mental anxiety and deconditioning the mind.

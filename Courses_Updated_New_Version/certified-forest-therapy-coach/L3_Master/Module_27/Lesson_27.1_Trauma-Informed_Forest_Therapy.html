Opening Case Study

Eleanor's Journey to Grounding

Client: Eleanor, 52-year-old retired nurse, now a community volunteer.

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic anxiety, difficulty sleeping, frequent feelings of being overwhelmed, hypervigilance in new environments, and a persistent sense of unease. Diagnosed with generalized anxiety disorder and mild C-PTSD following years of high-stress hospital work and a recent personal loss. Traditional therapy felt too "heady" and often re-triggered her. She sought a more embodied, gentle approach.

Goal for Forest Therapy: To find moments of peace, feel more grounded in her body, and reduce her overall anxiety levels without feeling pressured or exposed.

"After years of caring for others in crisis, my own nervous system feels wired. I'm always on alert. Being outside sometimes helps, but other times it makes me feel exposed. I need a way to connect with nature that feels utterly safe and gentle, where I'm not forced to 'process' anything, but can just... be."

Eleanor's experience highlights the need for a truly trauma-informed approach. Her desire for safety and gentle engagement is common among trauma survivors. We'll explore how a Forest Therapy Coach can adapt the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ to meet clients like Eleanor where they are, fostering healing at their own pace.

The Neurobiology of Trauma and Nature's Role

Trauma is not just a psychological event; it's a physiological imprint on the brain and body. When an individual experiences overwhelming stress or threat, their nervous system activates survival responses (fight, flight, freeze, fawn). If these responses cannot complete their natural cycle, the energy becomes "stuck" in the body, leading to chronic dysregulation.

The Triune Brain & Trauma Response

  • Brainstem (Reptilian Brain): Governs basic survival functions (heart rate, breathing). In trauma, it drives the primitive survival responses (freeze, fight, flight).
  • Limbic System (Mammalian Brain): The emotional center (amygdala, hippocampus). The amygdala, our "alarm system," becomes hyperactive in trauma, triggering fear responses even in safe situations. The hippocampus, responsible for memory, can be impaired, leading to fragmented or intrusive memories.
  • Prefrontal Cortex (Neocortex): Responsible for executive functions (reasoning, planning, emotional regulation). In trauma, the prefrontal cortex can become less active, making it difficult to think clearly, regulate emotions, or feel present.

This dysregulation often manifests as chronic stress, anxiety, hypervigilance, emotional numbness, or difficulty connecting with others and the environment.

The Polyvagal Theory & Nature Connection

Stephen Porges' Polyvagal Theory offers a crucial lens for understanding trauma and healing. It describes three states of the autonomic nervous system, mediated by the vagus nerve:

  1. Ventral Vagal Complex (VVC): The "social engagement" system. Associated with feelings of safety, connection, calm, and present-moment awareness. This is the ideal state for healing and growth.
  2. Sympathetic Nervous System: The "fight or flight" response. Activated by perceived threat, leading to increased heart rate, shallow breathing, and readiness for action.
  3. Dorsal Vagal Complex (DVC): The "freeze or collapse" response. Activated in extreme threat when fight/flight is not possible, leading to dissociation, numbness, and a sense of shutdown.

Trauma often leaves individuals stuck in sympathetic (hyperarousal) or dorsal vagal (hypoarousal/shutdown) states. Forest Therapy, when practiced trauma-informedly, can be incredibly effective at:

  • Downregulating the Sympathetic Nervous System: The calming elements of nature (soft sounds, gentle breezes, biophilic patterns) can reduce heart rate, lower blood pressure, and decrease cortisol levels. Research indicates that just 20 minutes in a natural environment can significantly reduce stress hormones (Park et al., 2010).
  • Activating the Ventral Vagal Complex: Experiences of safety, gentle sensory engagement, and connection with the natural world foster a sense of belonging and calm, helping clients return to a state of social engagement and present-moment awareness. The aesthetic beauty of nature can induce awe, a positive emotion linked to vagal tone enhancement (Sturm & Conty, 2019).
Coach's Tip

Explaining the "Why" to Clients: When working with clients, you don't need to dive deep into neurobiology, but a simple explanation can be empowering. You might say: "Your body has an incredible alarm system that keeps you safe. After tough experiences, that alarm can sometimes get stuck on 'high alert.' Forest Therapy helps us gently remind your body that it's safe now, allowing your natural calming system to come back online."

Core Principles of Trauma-Informed Care in Forest Therapy

A trauma-informed approach isn't about treating trauma, but about creating an environment where individuals feel safe enough to heal. The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) outlines six key principles:

1

Safety

Physical & Emotional: This is foundational. For Forest Therapy, it means ensuring the physical environment feels secure (e.g., clear paths, accessible terrain, awareness of surroundings) and the emotional space is non-judgmental, predictable, and free from perceived threat. Clients need to feel safe from judgment, shame, or re-traumatization.

2

Trustworthiness & Transparency

Clear Expectations: Be upfront about what to expect, the purpose of invitations, and your role. Consistency in your demeanor, communication, and boundaries builds trust. For Forest Therapy, this includes clear explanations of the journey, options for participation, and respecting personal space.

3

Peer Support

Shared Experience: While often individual in Forest Therapy, consider how group walks (if applicable) can foster connection among participants with shared experiences. For individual sessions, your empathetic presence serves as a form of co-regulation and support.

4

Collaboration & Mutuality

"Doing With," Not "Doing To": Forest Therapy is a collaborative journey. The coach facilitates, but the client leads their experience. Offer choices, seek input, and empower the client to shape their engagement. This actively counters the powerlessness often experienced in trauma.

5

Empowerment, Voice & Choice

Client Agency: Explicitly offer choices at every turn: "You can sit or stand," "You can join this invitation or simply observe," "You can choose your pace." Validate their choices and affirm their inner wisdom. This is crucial for restoring a sense of control.

6

Cultural, Historical & Gender Issues

Respect & Responsiveness: Recognize that trauma is often experienced within broader social, cultural, and historical contexts. Be aware of your own biases and strive for cultural humility. Ensure your language and approach are inclusive and respectful of diverse backgrounds and identities.

As a Forest Therapy Coach, your mindful integration of these principles transforms your practice into a powerful healing modality, especially for those navigating the aftermath of trauma.

Adapting the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ for Trauma Survivors

The beauty of the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ is its inherent flexibility. With a trauma-informed lens, each stage can be gently adapted to prioritize safety, choice, and a client's unique pace.

R.O.O.T.S. Stage Standard Approach Trauma-Informed Adaptation Why it Matters for Trauma
R: Reflect & Reconnect Initial conversation, goal setting, readiness assessment. Extended check-in: Focus on current felt sense, explicit consent, establish "safe phrase" or signal, discuss boundaries (physical/emotional). Emphasize "no pressure to share." Builds trust, establishes agency, provides tools for self-regulation, ensures client feels seen and heard without obligation.
O: Opening Senses & Observation Invitations to engage all senses with curiosity. Gentle, choice-based invitations: Start with less intense senses (e.g., distant sounds, visual scanning of a safe space). Offer alternatives: "You might notice..." instead of "Notice this." Avoid direct eye contact if uncomfortable. Emphasize ground contact. Prevents sensory overload, avoids triggering hypervigilance, allows gradual re-engagement, promotes grounding and present-moment awareness.
O: Outdoor Immersion & Outcome Deeper sensory engagement, movement, presence. Pacing & Micro-Movements: Encourage slow, deliberate movement. Frequent check-ins: "How does this feel in your body?" Offer rests. Focus on tactile grounding (touching bark, feeling earth). Emphasize "doing less." Provide options for proximity to the group/coach. Respects nervous system capacity, prevents overwhelm, fosters interoception, rebuilds a sense of control over one's body and space.
T: Transformative Integration & Tools Reflection on insights, journaling, sharing. Focus on Embodied Experience: Prioritize how the experience felt in the body over cognitive insights. Use open-ended questions: "What did you notice?" "What felt supportive?" Offer creative expression (drawing, gentle movement) rather than just verbal sharing. Normalize silence. Avoids intellectualization that can bypass somatic processing, validates non-verbal experiences, allows for gentle processing of emergent feelings.
S: Sustained Connection & Support Developing home practices, ongoing support. Personalized, sustainable practices: Co-create simple, accessible practices that reinforce safety and regulation. Emphasize small, achievable steps. Provide resources for further support if needed. Validate their resilience and capacity for self-care. Ensures practices are empowering and not overwhelming, builds long-term coping strategies, reinforces agency and self-efficacy.
Coach's Tip

The Power of "I Wonder": Instead of directing, use language that invites curiosity and choice. For example, instead of "Find something beautiful to touch," try "I wonder if there's anything around you that invites a gentle touch, or if you prefer to simply observe." This subtle shift empowers the client's agency and reduces pressure.

Recognizing and Skillfully Responding to Triggers, Dissociation, or Distress

Even with the most careful planning, a client may experience distress during a session. Your ability to recognize and respond effectively is critical.

Signs of Distress or Dysregulation:

  • Hyperarousal (Sympathetic Activation): Rapid breathing, fidgeting, increased heart rate, tension, anxious gaze, irritability, sudden talkativeness, looking for exits.
  • Hypoarousal (Dorsal Vagal Activation): Glazed eyes, blank stare, appearing "checked out" or detached, sudden quietness, slumping posture, difficulty responding, feeling numb.
  • Dissociation: Feeling unreal, disconnected from body or surroundings, memory gaps, emotional numbness, difficulty tracking conversation.
  • Triggering: Sudden emotional shifts, strong negative reactions to specific sensory input (sound, smell, sight), flashbacks, panic.

Skillful Responses:

  1. Stay Calm & Present: Your regulated presence is the most powerful tool for co-regulation. Take a deep breath yourself.
  2. Ground Yourself: Feel your feet on the earth. This helps you remain centered.
  3. Offer Choice & Agency: "Would you like to pause?" "We can move to a different spot if that feels better." "You don't need to explain anything, just let me know what you need."
  4. Orient to the Present: Gently bring attention to the immediate, safe surroundings. "Notice the ground beneath your feet." "Feel the breeze on your skin." "Can you hear the birdsong nearby?"
  5. Simple Grounding Techniques:
    • 5-4-3-2-1 Technique: Name 5 things you can see, 4 things you can feel, 3 things you can hear, 2 things you can smell, 1 thing you can taste (or a positive affirmation).
    • Safe Place Visualization: Guide them to imagine a safe, peaceful inner space.
    • Body Scan: Gently invite attention to different parts of the body, noticing sensations without judgment.
  6. Normalize the Experience: "It sounds like this is a really intense moment. That's okay. We can just be here with it."
  7. Respect Boundaries: Never force engagement. If a client needs to disengage, allow it. Offer to end the session early if needed.
  8. Follow-Up: After the session, check in with the client and, if appropriate, recommend professional mental health support.

Remember, your role is to create a container of safety, not to "fix" the trauma. By holding space with compassion and skilled responsiveness, you empower clients to navigate their internal landscape with greater self-awareness and resilience.

Somatic Awareness, Interoception, and Co-regulation in Healing

Trauma often disconnects individuals from their bodies, leading to a diminished capacity for somatic awareness (the perception of one's own body) and interoception (the perception of internal bodily states like hunger, heart rate, tension). Reconnecting to these internal signals in a safe, gentle way is crucial for healing.

  • Somatic Awareness: Forest Therapy invitations naturally encourage a gentle return to the body. Feeling the cool moss, the rough bark, the warmth of the sun, or the sensation of walking on uneven ground helps to re-establish a connection to physical sensation.
  • Interoception: By intentionally noticing subtle internal shifts (e.g., a softening in the shoulders, a deepening of breath, a sense of calm spreading), clients can begin to rebuild their interoceptive maps. This helps them understand their body's signals and respond to their needs more effectively.
  • Co-regulation: As a coach, your own regulated nervous system can act as an anchor for your client. When you remain calm, present, and grounded, you offer a physiological blueprint for your client's nervous system to mirror. This non-verbal exchange of safety is profoundly healing.

A 2021 study on nature-based interventions for PTSD found that participants reported significant improvements in emotional regulation and a sense of grounding, directly linked to enhanced interoceptive awareness developed through sensory engagement with nature (Siden et al., 2021).

Coach's Tip

Modeling Presence: During a session, regularly take a deep, audible breath. Pause and visibly take in your surroundings. This subtle modeling communicates safety and invites your client to do the same without direct instruction, fostering a sense of shared, regulated presence.

Check Your Understanding

1

Which neurobiological system is often dysregulated in trauma, leading to hyperarousal or hypoarousal, and how does trauma-informed Forest Therapy help address this?

The autonomic nervous system, particularly the sympathetic (fight/flight) and dorsal vagal (freeze/collapse) branches of the polyvagal system, is often dysregulated in trauma. Trauma-informed Forest Therapy helps by gently downregulating the sympathetic nervous system and activating the ventral vagal complex, fostering a sense of safety, calm, and social engagement through gentle sensory input and a regulated environment.
2

List three core principles of trauma-informed care and provide a brief example of how each might be applied in a Forest Therapy session.

Three core principles are:
  • Safety: Ensuring the physical location is free from perceived threats (e.g., choosing a quiet, easily navigable trail) and establishing emotional safety (e.g., no pressure to share, respecting personal space).
  • Choice: Offering clients options at every stage (e.g., "You can sit here or walk at your own pace," "You can engage with this invitation or simply observe").
  • Collaboration: Working with the client as a partner, asking for their input, and valuing their experience (e.g., "What feels most supportive for you right now?").
3

How would you adapt a standard "Sit Spot" invitation (part of the "Outdoor Immersion" stage of R.O.O.T.S.‚Ñ¢) for a client with a history of trauma?

A standard "Sit Spot" involves finding a place to sit quietly and observe. For a trauma survivor, adaptation would include:
  • Explicit Choice: "You can find a spot to sit or stand, close by or a little further away, whatever feels safest and most comfortable for you."
  • Time Limit & Check-in: Offer a shorter initial time (e.g., 5-7 minutes) with a clear signal for return.
  • Orientation: Encourage orienting to surroundings first, perhaps keeping eyes open and scanning for what feels safe, rather than immediately closing them.
  • Grounding: Emphasize feeling the ground beneath them, or the support of a tree, as a grounding anchor.
  • Coach Proximity: Discuss if they prefer the coach to be visible or out of sight, and respect that preference.
4

Define interoception and explain its significance in trauma healing, illustrating how Forest Therapy supports its development.

Interoception is the sense of the physiological condition of the body, including internal sensations like heart rate, breathing, hunger, and tension. In trauma, this sense can be disrupted, leading to difficulty recognizing internal states or responding to bodily needs. Forest Therapy supports interoceptive development by offering gentle, non-threatening sensory invitations that draw attention to internal bodily sensations (e.g., feeling the breath, noticing warmth or coolness, recognizing relaxation in muscles), helping clients safely reconnect with their inner experience and build a greater sense of self-awareness and self-regulation.

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma creates physiological imprints, dysregulating the nervous system and impacting areas like the amygdala and prefrontal cortex. Nature connection, through trauma-informed practice, can help downregulate sympathetic arousal and activate the ventral vagal complex.
  • The principles of Safety, Trustworthiness, Choice, Collaboration, and Empowerment are fundamental to creating a healing environment for trauma survivors in Forest Therapy.
  • The R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ can be sensitively adapted at each stage by prioritizing client agency, gentle pacing, and explicit options to prevent re-traumatization and foster a sense of control.
  • Coaches must be adept at recognizing signs of distress (hyper/hypoarousal, dissociation) and responding with calm presence, grounding techniques, and an unwavering commitment to client choice.
  • Forest Therapy naturally supports the development of somatic awareness and interoception, crucial for trauma healing, while the coach's regulated presence provides powerful co-regulation.

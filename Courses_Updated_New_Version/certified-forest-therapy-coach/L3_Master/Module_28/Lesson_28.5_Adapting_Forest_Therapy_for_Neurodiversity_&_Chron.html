Opening Case Study: Navigating Nature with ADHD and Chronic Fatigue

Sarah's Journey to Gentle Presence

Client: Sarah, 48-year-old freelance graphic designer

Presenting Symptoms: Diagnosed with ADHD in adulthood, experiencing significant chronic fatigue post-COVID, and often overwhelmed by sensory input. She loves nature but finds traditional hiking exhausting and struggles to focus on guided meditations.

Initial Goals: Reduce mental overwhelm, improve energy levels, find a sustainable way to connect with nature that doesn't feel like another task.

Sarah's case highlights the intersection of neurodiversity and chronic illness, requiring a highly adaptive approach to Forest Therapy. Her desire for nature connection was strong, but her physical and neurological realities presented barriers. We'll explore how her coach, a Certified Forest Therapy Coach, thoughtfully adapted the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ to meet her unique needs.

1. Understanding Neurodiversity & Sensory Differences

Neurodiversity encompasses the natural variations in the human brain regarding sociability, learning, attention, mood, and other mental functions (Armstrong, 2012). Key conditions relevant to our practice include Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) and Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). Alongside these, many individuals experience sensory processing differences, which can profoundly impact their comfort and engagement in nature.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) Considerations

  • Sensory Sensitivity: Individuals with ASD may experience hypersensitivity (over-responsiveness) or hyposensitivity (under-responsiveness) to sensory input. This could mean bright sunlight, rustling leaves, buzzing insects, or strong smells might be overwhelming, or conversely, they might seek intense sensory experiences.
  • Need for Predictability: Unexpected changes, new environments, or unstructured activities can be anxiety-provoking.
  • Communication Styles: Literal interpretation of language, challenges with non-verbal cues, and varying comfort levels with social interaction.
  • Focus & Interests: Intense focus on specific details or topics, which can be harnessed positively in nature.

ADHD Considerations

  • Attention Regulation: Difficulty sustaining attention on a single task, or hyperfocusing intensely. Nature's dynamic environment can be both a challenge and a benefit.
  • Impulsivity: Tendency to act quickly without forethought, which could impact safety or group dynamics.
  • Executive Function Challenges: Difficulties with planning, organizing, and task initiation, which can affect adherence to structured invitations.
  • Sensory Seeking: Often benefit from movement and varied sensory input to maintain focus.

Sensory Processing Differences Beyond Diagnosis

It's crucial to remember that sensory processing differences are not exclusive to neurodevelopmental diagnoses. Many individuals, especially those with trauma histories or chronic stress, can exhibit heightened sensory sensitivities. A study by Schoen et al. (2021) found that up to 1 in 6 adults report sensory sensitivities that impact daily life.

For Forest Therapy Coaches, this means adopting a "sensory-aware" approach to all clients, understanding that each person's nervous system responds uniquely to stimuli.

Coach's Tip: Empathetic Inquiry

Never assume a client's sensory experience. Instead, practice empathetic inquiry. Ask questions like: "What kind of natural sounds feel most calming to you?" or "Are there any textures or smells you find particularly strong or overwhelming?" Empower them to articulate their preferences and boundaries. This honors their autonomy and builds trust, especially for those who may have felt unheard.

2. Inclusive Forest Therapy for Neurodiversity

Creating truly inclusive experiences requires intentional adaptation across all phases of the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢. Our goal is to minimize barriers and maximize opportunities for genuine connection.

Modifying 'Reflect & Reconnect' (R) for Neurodiversity

  • Pre-Session Assessment: Use detailed questionnaires focusing on sensory preferences, communication styles, comfort with novelty, and previous nature experiences. Consider involving caregivers for insight.
  • Clear Expectations: Provide a clear, visual itinerary (if preferred) before the session, outlining what to expect, duration, and any potential sensory inputs.
  • Choice & Control: Emphasize that all invitations are optional. Empower the client to choose their level of engagement and physical proximity.

Adapting 'Opening Senses & Observation' (O) and 'Outdoor Immersion & Outcome' (O)

This is where many practical adaptations come into play:

  1. Route & Environment:
    • Predictable Paths: Choose familiar, well-maintained paths with minimal obstacles. Avoid sudden changes in terrain.
    • Escape Routes/Quiet Zones: Identify areas where a client can retreat if overwhelmed. Communicate this option clearly.
    • Proximity to Amenities: Ensure easy access to restrooms, water, and shelter.
  2. Sensory Invitations:
    • Offer Choice: Instead of "listen to the birds," offer "you might notice sounds like birds, wind, or the rustling of leaves. Choose one that draws your attention, or simply be aware of the quiet."
    • Vary Intensity: Provide invitations that range from subtle to more intense, allowing clients to select. For example, a "gentle touch" invitation versus a "deep bark texture" invitation.
    • Visual Aids: Use picture cards or physical objects to illustrate invitations for clients who process visually.
    • Reduce Overwhelm: Suggest closing eyes or looking down if visual input is too much. Offer noise-canceling headphones if sound sensitivity is high.
  3. Communication Styles:
    • Clear & Concise: Use direct language, avoiding metaphors or abstract concepts that might be misinterpreted.
    • Pacing: Allow extra time for processing questions or instructions. Embrace silence.
    • Check-ins: Regularly check in with clients using simple, direct questions about their comfort and engagement.
  4. Group Dynamics:
    • Smaller Groups/Individual Sessions: Consider one-on-one sessions or very small groups to reduce social pressure.
    • Personal Space: Respect individual needs for personal space and avoid forced interaction.
    • Designated Quiet Times: Incorporate periods of silent walking or seated reflection.

Statistics on Nature & Neurodiversity

A 2020 systematic review published in Environmental Research found that green space exposure was associated with reduced stress and improved attention in individuals with ADHD and ASD. Specific benefits included improvements in cognitive function, reduced anxiety, and enhanced social interaction when activities were appropriately structured (Tillmann et al., 2020).

Comparison: Standard vs. Adapted Invitations for Neurodiversity

Standard Invitation Adaptive Modification for Neurodiversity Rationale
"Find something beautiful to share." "Find something that catches your eye, or a texture you enjoy. You don't need to share it if you prefer." Reduces pressure to define "beauty" or engage socially; offers concrete sensory focus.
"Walk silently and mindfully." "Walk at your own pace, noticing your steps. If silence feels uncomfortable, you may hum or listen to a preferred sound." Respects varied comfort with silence and offers agency over pacing and self-regulation.
"Sit and close your eyes for 10 minutes." "Find a comfortable spot to sit or stand. You may close your eyes, gaze softly, or look at a focused point. We'll be here for 5-7 minutes." Reduces duration, offers alternatives to eye closure, allows for varied comfort with stillness.
"Share your experience with the group." "If you wish, you may share a word or a feeling. There's no pressure to speak." (Offer journaling or drawing as alternatives). Minimizes social pressure, provides alternative communication methods.

3. Adapting for Chronic Illness & Physical Limitations

Clients living with chronic pain, fatigue, limited mobility, or other physical health challenges require a focus on gentle presence, comfort, and energy conservation. The emphasis shifts from "doing" to "being," honoring the body's current capacity.

Key Adaptations for Chronic Illness

  1. Pacing & Duration:
    • Shorter Sessions: Offer condensed sessions (e.g., 60-90 minutes instead of 2-3 hours).
    • Frequent Breaks: Integrate many opportunities for rest, seated invitations, or lying down.
    • Slow Pace: Emphasize a very slow, deliberate pace, allowing the client to set their rhythm.
  2. Accessibility & Environment:
    • Terrain: Choose flat, even, paved, or very well-maintained natural paths. Avoid hills, uneven ground, or long distances.
    • Seating: Ensure access to benches, portable chairs, or comfortable spots to sit.
    • Shelter & Climate Control: Be mindful of temperature extremes, wind, or rain. Have options for shade or warmth.
    • Proximity: Select locations close to parking, restrooms, and emergency access.
  3. Invitations & Activities:
    • Seated Invitations: Focus on sensory experiences that can be done from a seated position (e.g., soundscape, cloud gazing, close observation of a leaf).
    • Gentle Movement: Offer subtle movements like gentle stretching, hand movements, or mindful breathing, rather than vigorous activity.
    • Props: Encourage use of blankets, cushions, walking poles, or mobility aids.
    • Focus on Inner Landscape: Guide clients to connect with their internal experience of nature, rather than requiring external physical engagement.
  4. Energy Conservation:
    • Prioritize: Help clients prioritize which invitations resonate most, allowing them to conserve energy for what feels most meaningful.
    • Body Scan: Integrate regular body scans to check in with energy levels and pain signals, adjusting as needed.
    • Post-Session Rest: Advise clients to plan for rest after the session, recognizing the energy expenditure even in gentle activities.

Case Study: Embracing Stillness with Fibromyalgia

Eleanor's Discovery of Grounding

Client: Eleanor, 55-year-old retired teacher

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic widespread pain, severe fatigue, and brain fog due to fibromyalgia. She found movement painful and often felt isolated at home. Traditional exercise was often too much.

Forest Therapy Adaptation: Eleanor's coach focused on a "stillness journey." Sessions were held in a small, accessible local park with ample benches. Invitations centered on seated sensory awareness (listening, smelling, observing textures within reach). The "Opening Senses" phase included a long, guided body scan to connect with her body without judgment. "Outdoor Immersion" involved a 15-minute seated meditation focusing on the feeling of grounding to the earth, followed by a gentle invitation to simply rest her hands on a tree trunk nearby. The coach provided warm tea and a soft blanket.

Outcome: Eleanor reported a significant reduction in perceived pain during and immediately after sessions (a 30-40% decrease on her pain scale), and a profound sense of calm. She learned that nature connection didn't require exertion, but rather a deep, gentle presence. She started incorporating 10-minute seated "tree-touching" practices into her daily routine, which she credits with improving her overall well-being and reducing feelings of isolation.

Coach's Tip: Prioritizing Agency & Comfort

For clients with chronic illness, feeling in control of their body and experience is paramount. Continuously check in: "How does this feel in your body?" "Would you prefer to sit or stand for this invitation?" "Are you feeling warm/cool enough?" Empowering them to lead their experience fosters a sense of agency that is often diminished by their condition.

4. Collaborative Care & Support Networks

In complex cases involving neurodiversity or chronic illness, Forest Therapy often acts as a complementary modality within a broader care network. Effective collaboration with caregivers, family, and other healthcare professionals is vital for holistic client care.

Working with Caregivers & Support Networks

  • Informed Consent: Ensure all necessary consents are obtained, especially if working with minors or adults with diminished capacity.
  • Information Sharing: With client consent, communicate with caregivers about the goals, adaptations, and benefits of Forest Therapy. They can offer invaluable insights into the client's daily challenges and preferences.
  • Caregiver Support: Recognize that caregivers often experience significant stress. Forest Therapy can be adapted to support them as well, or you can guide them on how to best support the client in nature.
  • Bridging Home & Nature: Caregivers can help integrate nature practices into the client's daily life, especially for those with significant limitations.

Liaising with Healthcare Professionals

As a Forest Therapy Coach, you are part of a client's wellness team. While you do not diagnose or treat medical conditions, you can communicate effectively with other professionals:

  • Clear Communication: Articulate the nature of Forest Therapy, your role, and the specific adaptations you are making.
  • Referral & Integration: Understand when to refer a client to a medical professional (e.g., for pain management, medication review, diagnostic testing).
  • Document & Report: Keep thorough notes and, with client consent, provide concise summaries of client progress and observed benefits to their healthcare team.
  • Respect Boundaries: Always respect the scope of practice of other professionals and your own.

The Interdisciplinary Team

A 2023 study in Disability and Rehabilitation highlighted the importance of interdisciplinary care for chronic conditions, finding that integrated approaches involving allied health professionals (like occupational therapists, physical therapists, and wellness coaches) led to improved patient adherence and better long-term outcomes. Position yourself as a valuable, complementary part of this team.

5. Tailoring Transformative Integration (T of ROOTS)

The 'Transformative Integration' (T) phase of the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ involves helping clients reflect on their experiences and apply insights to their daily lives. For neurodivergent individuals or those with chronic illness, this phase requires thoughtful adaptation to accommodate diverse cognitive processing styles and ensure practicality.

Adapting Reflection & Meaning-Making

  • Varied Expression:
    • Verbal: Offer clear, open-ended questions. Allow ample time for processing.
    • Visual: Encourage drawing, painting, or creating a small nature mandala to represent their experience.
    • Kinesthetic: Suggest gentle movement, stretching, or even a symbolic gesture to embody their takeaway.
    • Written: Provide journals or prompts for those who prefer written reflection.
  • Shorter Reflection Periods: Break down reflection into smaller, manageable chunks to prevent cognitive fatigue.
  • Concrete Connections: Help clients make explicit, concrete links between their nature experience and their daily life. Instead of "How did this make you feel?", try "What specific feeling did you notice, and where might you invite a bit of that feeling into your week?"
  • Focus on Small Wins: Celebrate small moments of connection or comfort, rather than expecting grand epiphanies, especially for clients with limited energy or processing capacity.
Coach's Tip: Creative Integration

For clients who struggle with verbal processing or have limited energy, encourage creative integration. Suggest they collect a small nature item (with respect for nature) to place on their desk as a visual reminder, or create a short, personal "nature phrase" they can repeat. This makes integration tangible and less cognitively demanding.

Sustained Connection & Support (S of ROOTS) for Complex Cases

The final 'S' in ROOTS‚ÄîSustained Connection & Support‚Äîis especially critical here. Personalized nature practice plans must be realistic, achievable, and flexible.

  • Micro-Doses of Nature: For clients with chronic fatigue or mobility issues, recommend short, frequent exposures (e.g., 5 minutes by an open window, looking at a plant, or listening to nature sounds).
  • Leverage Technology: Suggest high-quality nature documentaries, virtual forest walks, or nature soundscapes for days when outdoor access is impossible.
  • Indoor Nature: Encourage bringing nature indoors with plants, natural light, nature art, or a small "nature corner."
  • Supported Practice: For neurodivergent individuals, a trusted family member or caregiver might assist in establishing and maintaining routines.
  • Empower Self-Advocacy: Teach clients how to communicate their needs and preferences to others when engaging with nature, whether with family or in public spaces.
Coach's Tip: Empowering Self-Advocacy

Help clients articulate their needs confidently. Practice phrases like, "I need a quiet spot for a few minutes," or "I prefer to sit for this activity." This skill is invaluable for maintaining their well-being and ensuring their nature connection journey is truly their own.

Your Impact as an Adaptive Coach

The ability to adapt Forest Therapy for diverse needs isn't just a skill; it's a profound act of service. As a Certified Forest Therapy Coach, specializing in inclusive practices opens doors to working with a wider range of clients, including those in assisted living facilities, schools for neurodiverse children, or chronic illness support groups. This niche can not only be deeply rewarding but also financially viable, as specialized services often command premium rates.

For example, a coach who develops a program for a local neurodiversity center, offering adapted walks for 4-6 participants, could charge $50-$75 per person per session. Offering private, adapted sessions for individuals with chronic illness could range from $120-$200 per session, based on the level of customization and support required. Your unique expertise makes you an invaluable resource in your community.

Check Your Understanding

1

When adapting Forest Therapy for a client with ADHD and sensory sensitivities, what is a key modification you would make during the "Opening Senses" (O) phase, and why?

A key modification would be to offer a wider range of sensory invitations with varying intensities and provide explicit choices. For example, instead of a broad "listen to the forest," you might say, "You can focus on the sound of the wind, or the quiet rustle of leaves, or simply allow all sounds to pass. If it feels too much, you can gently close your eyes." This allows the client to self-regulate and choose input that feels comfortable, reducing potential overwhelm.

2

Eleanor, a client with fibromyalgia, benefits greatly from "stillness journeys." What two specific adaptations did her coach implement regarding the physical environment and activity level to support her condition?

Two key adaptations were: 1) Selecting an accessible local park with ample benches and flat terrain to accommodate limited mobility and pain, and 2) Focusing almost entirely on seated sensory awareness and gentle presence, minimizing physical exertion and prioritizing comfort and rest over movement.

3

During the "Transformative Integration" (T) phase, how might you adapt reflection prompts for a neurodivergent client who struggles with abstract verbal expression?

Instead of abstract verbal prompts, offer concrete, alternative methods of expression. This could include inviting them to draw their experience, create a small nature collage from collected items (if appropriate), use simple, direct verbal cues ("What did you notice?"), or express their takeaway through a simple gesture or movement. The goal is to meet them where they are in their processing style.

4

Why is collaborative care with caregivers and other healthcare professionals particularly important when working with clients with chronic illness or neurodiversity?

Collaborative care is crucial because these clients often have complex, multifaceted needs that extend beyond the scope of Forest Therapy. Caregivers provide vital daily insights and support, while healthcare professionals manage medical aspects. By communicating effectively and respecting boundaries, the Forest Therapy Coach can ensure their practice is integrated into a holistic support system, leading to more consistent benefits and better overall well-being for the client.

Key Takeaways

  • Neurodiversity and chronic illness require highly individualized and adaptive Forest Therapy approaches, focusing on minimizing barriers and maximizing comfort.
  • For neurodivergent clients, adapt by offering clear expectations, choice in invitations, varied sensory input, and direct communication.
  • For clients with chronic illness, prioritize gentle pacing, accessible environments, seated invitations, and energy conservation.
  • Collaborative care with caregivers and other healthcare professionals is essential for holistic support and integration into a client's broader wellness plan.
  • The 'Transformative Integration' (T) and 'Sustained Connection' (S) phases of ROOTS must be tailored to accommodate diverse cognitive styles and physical capacities through creative expression and realistic home practices.

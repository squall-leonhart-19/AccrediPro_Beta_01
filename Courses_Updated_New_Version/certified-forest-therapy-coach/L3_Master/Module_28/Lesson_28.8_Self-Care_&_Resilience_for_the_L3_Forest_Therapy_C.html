The Case She Presents: Ms. Eleanor Vance

Jennifer contacts you with a concern about her client, Ms. Eleanor Vance. This is your opportunity to guide Jennifer through a critical learning experience.

Mentee's Client Case Review

Ms. Eleanor Vance, Age 58

üëµ

Client: Ms. Eleanor Vance

Age 58, recently retired from a demanding corporate job. Lives in a suburban area with limited access to wild nature. Presented with feelings of disconnection from purpose, mild anxiety about retirement, and a general sense of unease. Expressed interest in finding new ways to relax and rediscover joy.

What Jennifer Tells You:

"I had my first session with Ms. Vance last week. She was very pleasant, but I felt a lot of pressure to 'fix' her. I introduced her to some basic sensory awareness exercises in her backyard, focusing on sight and sound. I also gave her a detailed journal prompt about her 'life's purpose' to complete before our next session, hoping it would help her with her disconnection. I thought it was a good start, but she just emailed me saying she felt the journaling was 'too much pressure' and made her feel even more anxious and inadequate. She's worried Forest Therapy isn't for her. Did I overwhelm her? What should I do?"

What You Need to Explore with Jennifer:

  • Did Jennifer adequately assess Ms. Vance's current emotional state and readiness for introspection?
  • Was the intervention (deep journaling on purpose) appropriate for an initial session with a client presenting with anxiety and disconnection?
  • Did Jennifer fully explain the "invitation, not instruction" principle of Forest Therapy?
  • How did Jennifer establish rapport and create a safe space for Ms. Vance?
  • Is there a potential for over-coaching or pushing a client beyond their comfort zone too quickly?
Coach Tip: The Power of Gentle Inquiry

When mentoring, resist the urge to immediately provide solutions. Instead, practice gentle inquiry. Ask open-ended questions that encourage your mentee to reflect on their choices and identify potential areas for growth. This builds their critical thinking and self-efficacy, far more effectively than simply telling them what to do.

Your Teaching Approach

Your goal isn't just to resolve Ms. Vance's case, but to empower Jennifer to become a more confident and competent practitioner. Here are key points to cover:

Key Points to Cover with Jennifer

1

Normalize the Learning Experience & Acknowledge Her Effort

"Jennifer, thank you for reaching out. This is a very common experience for new coaches, and the fact that you're reflecting and asking for guidance shows incredible professionalism. You put thought into your approach, and that's commendable." Emphasize that learning from challenges is a hallmark of an excellent coach.

2

Revisit the "Invitations, Not Instructions" Principle

Explain that Forest Therapy is about offering gentle invitations, allowing clients full autonomy to engage as they wish. Acknowledge that a deep journal prompt on "purpose" might feel like a directive, especially for someone already feeling anxious or disconnected. Discuss how to frame invitations to be less prescriptive and more open-ended.

3

Emphasize Pacing and Client Readiness

Discuss the importance of meeting the client where they are. For someone with anxiety and disconnection, initial sessions should prioritize gentle grounding, sensory connection, and building trust, rather than immediate deep introspection. Explain how to gauge a client's capacity for emotional work.

4

Reinforce Scope of Practice and Referral Readiness

While Ms. Vance's case doesn't seem to exceed the L1 scope, it's a good opportunity to discuss when deeper psychological issues might warrant a referral. Remind Jennifer that her role is to facilitate connection, not to provide therapy for clinical anxiety or depression without proper training.

Coach Tip: Leading by Example

Your vulnerability and willingness to share your own past learning experiences can be incredibly powerful. By saying, "I remember a time when I also over-coached a client early in my career...", you normalize their struggle and build a stronger, more trusting mentor-mentee relationship. This also helps combat their imposter syndrome.

Delivering Constructive Feedback: Your Dialogue

Here‚Äôs how you, as Sarah, might structure your conversation with Jennifer, combining empathy with clear guidance:

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Validation and Appreciation
"Jennifer, I truly appreciate you coming to me with this. It takes courage to review a challenging situation, and it shows me you are committed to being the best coach you can be. This is exactly what supervision is for, and you're doing it perfectly by seeking guidance."
Ask Before Telling: Encourage Self-Reflection
"Based on Ms. Vance's email, what are your initial thoughts about what might have happened? If you could rewind, what might you approach differently, or what questions would you ask yourself about her readiness?"
Teach the Principle (Connecting to Course Material)
"That's a great insight. Let's think back to our training on 'Invitations, Not Instructions.' For someone feeling anxious and disconnected, a deep dive into 'purpose' can feel like a heavy expectation, rather than a gentle invitation. Forest Therapy often starts with very simple, sensory-focused invitations to build a foundation of ease and presence first."
Collaborate on Solutions
"So, for your next session with Ms. Vance, how might you reframe your approach? What kind of gentle invitations could you offer that would help her feel more grounded and less pressured, especially in her backyard environment?"
Empower and Offer Continued Support
"You're learning so much, Jennifer, and this experience will make you an even stronger coach. Remember, it's about building a relationship of trust and adapting to the client's pace. We'll work through this together. Let's schedule a brief check-in after your next session with Ms. Vance."
Coach Tip: The 3:1 Ratio

When giving feedback, aim for a 3:1 positive-to-constructive feedback ratio. Start with genuine praise, deliver constructive points, and end with encouragement. This approach maintains motivation and fosters a positive learning environment, especially for mentees struggling with confidence.

Best Practices in Forest Therapy Mentorship

As you step into a mentoring role, establishing clear boundaries, expectations, and a supportive structure is paramount. Effective mentorship not only guides the mentee but also strengthens the entire Forest Therapy community.

Do's of Forest Therapy Mentorship Don'ts of Forest Therapy Mentorship
Establish Clear Boundaries: Define the scope, frequency, and duration of mentorship. Clarify what you can and cannot provide (e.g., you are a mentor, not a therapist). Over-Function: Don't do the mentee's work for them or solve all their problems directly. This hinders their learning and independence.
Prioritize Active Listening: Truly hear your mentee's concerns, anxieties, and successes. Validate their feelings before offering advice. Criticize or Shame: Avoid judgment or making the mentee feel inadequate. Frame feedback constructively and focus on learning opportunities.
Encourage Self-Reflection: Use open-ended questions to guide them to their own insights and solutions. Foster critical thinking. Impose Your Style: While you share best practices, allow the mentee to develop their own authentic coaching style. There's no single "right" way.
Model Ethical Practice: Demonstrate professionalism, confidentiality, and adherence to the AccrediPro Code of Ethics in all interactions. Neglect Self-Care: Don't allow mentorship to drain your own resources. Protect your time and energy, and model healthy boundaries.
Celebrate Progress: Acknowledge and affirm their growth, even small wins. Positive reinforcement is crucial for building confidence. Assume Understanding: Always check for comprehension and invite questions. What's obvious to you might be new to them.
Coach Tip: Income Potential & Impact

Beyond the immense satisfaction of guiding others, offering supervision and mentorship can also be a significant income stream for experienced coaches. Many L1 graduates are eager for guidance and are willing to invest in regular supervision sessions. For example, charging $150-$250 per hour for supervision can substantially boost your practice's revenue while deepening your impact on the field.

The Ripple Effect of Mentorship

Your role as a mentor extends far beyond the individual mentee. By investing in the next generation of Forest Therapy Coaches, you are directly contributing to the integrity, growth, and public perception of our entire field. A well-mentored practitioner is more likely to:

  • Adhere to ethical guidelines and scope of practice.
  • Deliver high-quality, impactful sessions to clients.
  • Feel confident and supported, reducing burnout and increasing longevity in the profession.
  • Become a future mentor themselves, creating a virtuous cycle of support.

Embrace this leadership role with the same dedication you bring to your client work. You are not just teaching; you are shaping the future of Forest Therapy.

Coach Tip: Continuous Learning

Even as a mentor, commit to your own continuous learning. Engage in peer supervision, attend advanced workshops, and stay updated on research. This not only keeps your skills sharp but also models lifelong professional development for your mentees.

Check Your Understanding

1. When Jennifer approaches you with her client concern, what should be your immediate first step as her mentor?

Your immediate first step should be to validate her feelings and appreciate her initiative. Acknowledge her courage in seeking guidance and normalize the learning process. This builds trust and reduces her potential feelings of imposter syndrome.

2. What is the primary reason why Ms. Vance felt overwhelmed by the "life's purpose" journal prompt?

The primary reason is likely that the prompt was too intensive and felt like a directive rather than an invitation, especially for someone presenting with anxiety and disconnection. It failed to meet her where she was emotionally, pushing her towards deep introspection before establishing sufficient comfort and grounding.

3. What core Forest Therapy principle should Jennifer revisit based on this case?

Jennifer should revisit the principle of "Invitations, Not Instructions." This emphasizes offering gentle, open-ended suggestions that allow the client full autonomy and choice in how they engage, rather than feeling pressured to perform a specific task or achieve a particular outcome.

4. What is a key benefit of a seasoned Forest Therapy Coach providing mentorship to new practitioners?

A key benefit is the strengthening of the entire Forest Therapy profession. Mentorship ensures new practitioners adhere to ethical standards, develop confidence, deliver high-quality client experiences, and ultimately contribute to the positive reputation and growth of the field as a whole.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective mentorship begins with empathy and validation, creating a safe space for mentees to learn from challenges.
  • Guiding new practitioners involves helping them revisit core principles like "Invitations, Not Instructions" and the importance of pacing and client readiness.
  • Constructive feedback is most impactful when delivered with a positive-to-constructive ratio, encouraging self-reflection before offering solutions.
  • Master practitioners play a vital role in upholding ethical standards and fostering the growth of the Forest Therapy profession through dedicated supervision.
  • Mentorship is a powerful act of leadership, building confidence in others and ensuring the longevity and integrity of the Forest Therapy field.

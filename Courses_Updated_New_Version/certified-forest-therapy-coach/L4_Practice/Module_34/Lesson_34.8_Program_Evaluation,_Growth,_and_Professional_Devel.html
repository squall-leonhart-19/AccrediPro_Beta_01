Understanding Group Program Discovery Calls

A discovery call for a group program differs subtly but significantly from a one-on-one coaching inquiry. While you still aim to understand individual needs, your goal is to articulate how a group setting uniquely addresses those needs and offers additional benefits.

In a group program, clients aren't just buying your expertise; they're investing in a shared experience, community, and collective support. Your role is to paint a vivid picture of this unique value proposition.

The Ideal Group Program Prospect

Your ideal group program participant often shares common characteristics:

  • Seeks Community: They desire connection, shared experience, and peer support.
  • Values Structure & Guidance: They appreciate a well-defined program with clear intentions and a skilled facilitator.
  • Budget-Conscious: Group programs are often a more accessible entry point than one-on-one coaching, offering significant value.
  • Open to Shared Learning: They are receptive to learning from others' experiences and perspectives in a supportive environment.
  • Motivated by Specific Outcomes: They want to achieve goals like stress reduction, improved well-being, or deeper nature connection, and believe a group setting can amplify this.
Coach Tip

Before any group program discovery call, take a moment to visualize your ideal participant thriving within your program. What are their hopes, fears, and aspirations? This mental preparation helps you connect authentically and speak directly to their needs, not just 'sell' a spot.

Prospect Profile: Eleanor, 52

Eleanor, 52

Retired elementary school teacher. Feeling overwhelmed and disconnected after years of high-stress work. Seeking a way to de-stress and reconnect with herself and nature.

Her Situation: Eleanor recently retired and is struggling with the transition. She misses the routine and social interaction of teaching but feels exhausted and isolated. Decision Style: Values authenticity, clear structure, and wants to feel a sense of belonging without pressure. Needs to be convinced of the unique value of the group experience.

Her Goal: "I just want to find some peace, feel less overwhelmed, and reconnect with the simple joys of life. Maybe even make a friend or two."

Your 30-Minute Group Program Call Structure

Here's a suggested flow for your call with Eleanor. Remember to adapt it to her specific responses, but keep these phases in mind:

Phase Time Key Objective Example Dialogue (You)
1. Build Rapport & Open 5 min Create a safe, welcoming space; understand initial interest. "Hi Eleanor, thank you for taking the time today! How did you hear about the 'Forest Bathing for Inner Peace' series?" "What specifically resonated with you about the program description?"
2. Uncover Group-Specific Needs 10 min Deeply listen to her challenges; identify how a group setting can meet them. "You mentioned feeling overwhelmed and isolated. How has that been impacting your daily life since retiring?" "What does 'inner peace' look like for you, and what makes you hesitant about finding it in a group setting?" "Have you ever participated in a guided group nature experience before?"
3. Present Your Group Program 10 min Clearly articulate program benefits, structure, and unique group value. "Based on what you've shared, Eleanor, it sounds like you're looking for a structured yet gentle way to reconnect and find peace. Our 'Forest Bathing for Inner Peace' series is designed exactly for that. We meet weekly for 6 sessions, each building on the last, guiding you through sensory invitations to deepen your connection with nature and yourself. Many participants, especially those who initially felt isolated, find immense comfort and strength in the shared, quiet presence of the group. It's a space where you can be yourself, without pressure, and find support in a gentle community."
4. Close with Confidence 5 min Address next steps, pricing, and enrollment. "Eleanor, does this sound like the kind of supportive and peaceful experience you've been looking for?" "If so, let me share how easy it is to join us and what the investment is."
Coach Tip

When presenting your group program, don't just list features. Translate them into tangible benefits. Instead of "We do guided meditations," say "You'll learn simple, powerful meditation techniques that you can carry into your daily life, helping you feel calmer and more centered long after our sessions end."

Navigating Common Objections in Group Enrollment

It's natural for potential clients to have questions and concerns. View objections not as rejections, but as opportunities to provide clarity and build trust. Here are some common ones for group programs and how to confidently respond:

Objection Confident Response (You)
"I prefer one-on-one. I'm not sure a group is for me." "I completely understand that preference. Many of my clients start with similar feelings. What's unique about our Forest Therapy groups is that while we're together, the experience is deeply personal. We offer invitations for individual connection with nature, and sharing is always optional. However, what often surprises people is the profound sense of connection and validation that comes from simply being present with others in nature, without the pressure of forced interaction. It's a gentle container for shared healing."
"The price is a bit high for a group program." "I appreciate you bringing that up. When I designed this program, I focused on creating an experience that delivers lasting transformation, not just a one-off walk. The investment covers 6 weeks of expertly guided sessions, a supportive community, and practices you can integrate into your life long-term. Many clients find the sustained support and consistent practice lead to much deeper and more lasting shifts than they've experienced before. We also offer flexible payment plans to make it more accessible."
"I'm not sure I'll feel comfortable sharing in a group." "That's a very common concern, and I want to assure you that your comfort is paramount. Our Forest Therapy groups are designed to be a safe, non-judgmental space. Sharing is always an invitation, never a requirement. Many participants find that simply being present in nature with others, even in silence, creates a deep sense of belonging. You're welcome to participate at whatever level feels right for you, knowing that your individual journey is honored."
"I need to think about it." "Absolutely, this is an important decision. To help you reflect, could you tell me what aspects you need to think about most? Is it the timing, the investment, or perhaps something about the group dynamic? I want to make sure you have all the information you need to make the best choice for yourself."
Coach Tip

Practice active listening when a client voices an objection. Rephrase their concern to show you've heard them ("So, if I understand correctly, you're wondering if a group setting can truly provide the personalized experience you're seeking?"). This validates their feelings and opens the door for a more productive conversation.

Confidently Presenting Your Group Program Pricing

Talking about money can feel uncomfortable, but it's a critical part of running a sustainable practice. Confidence comes from believing in the value you offer. For group programs, remember that clients are investing in a comprehensive experience.

When presenting your pricing:

  1. State the Value First: Reiterate the transformation, benefits, and unique aspects of your program before stating the price. For example, "This 6-week journey will provide you with deep nature connection, stress reduction techniques, and a supportive community to integrate mindfulness into your life."
  2. State the Price Clearly: "The investment for the entire 6-week 'Forest Bathing for Inner Peace' series is $497." Avoid hedging or apologizing.
  3. Offer Options (if applicable): "We also offer a convenient 2-pay option of two payments of $250."
  4. Pause and Listen: After stating the price, resist the urge to fill the silence. Let the client process the information.
Value Stacking

Remember the concept of Value Stacking. List all the components of your program: guided sessions, downloadable resources, private group chat, pre-recorded meditations, bonus nature journal, etc. Each adds perceived value, making the total investment feel more justified. For example, a 6-week program at $497 could be broken down to feel like less than $83 per session, plus all the added value.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is a key difference in a discovery call for a group program compared to a one-on-one coaching call?

For a group program, you emphasize the benefits of the collective experience, community, and shared learning, in addition to individual transformation. You're selling the container as much as the content.

2. Eleanor expresses concern about feeling comfortable sharing in a group. How would you address this?

You would validate her concern, explain that sharing is always optional and never forced, and highlight the gentle, non-judgmental nature of Forest Therapy groups where individual experience is honored, even in silence.

3. What is "Value Stacking" and why is it important when presenting group program pricing?

Value Stacking involves listing all the tangible and intangible components of your program (e.g., guided sessions, resources, community access, bonus materials) to demonstrate the comprehensive value. It helps justify the price by showing clients everything they receive for their investment.

4. If your 6-week group program costs $497 and you enroll 8 participants per month, what is your estimated annual revenue from this program?

Monthly revenue: 8 participants * $497 = $3,976. Annual revenue: $3,976 * 12 months = $47,712.
Key Takeaways
  • Successful group program enrollment hinges on understanding both individual needs and the unique benefits of a collective experience.
  • A structured discovery call allows you to build rapport, uncover specific challenges, and clearly present your program's value.
  • Practice handling common objections with empathy and confidence, transforming concerns into opportunities for clarity.
  • Always lead with value before stating your price, and consider offering flexible payment options.
  • Group programs offer significant income scalability, allowing you to serve more clients and build a thriving, sustainable practice.

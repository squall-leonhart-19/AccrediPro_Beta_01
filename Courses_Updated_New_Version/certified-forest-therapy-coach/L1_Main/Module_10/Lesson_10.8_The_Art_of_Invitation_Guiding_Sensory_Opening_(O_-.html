The Guiding Script

Now, let's practice how you, as a Certified Forest Therapy Coach, would initiate and guide a session with Eleanor. This script provides a framework for the opening, a couple of core invitations, and a gentle closing. Remember, this is a starting point â€“ your intuition and Eleanor's responses will guide the actual flow.

Coach's Reflection

As you read through this script, notice how I use invitational language, create space for silence, and validate Eleanor's experience. This is crucial for building trust and allowing nature's therapeutic effects to unfold.

Opening & Welcome (5 minutes)

You've met Eleanor at a local park, near a small, accessible woodland trail.

YOU:
"Welcome, Eleanor. I'm so glad you're here today. Thank you for making the time to join me. Before we begin our walk, I want to acknowledge what you shared with me â€“ that feeling of being a bit lost, of seeking clarity and connection. Many women in this phase of life experience similar feelings, and it takes real courage to seek new paths. Today, my invitation is simply to allow yourself to be present, to slow down, and to let nature be your guide."
ELEANOR:
"Thank you, Sarah. I appreciate that. I'm a bit nervous, but also hopeful."
YOU:
"That's perfectly normal, Eleanor. There's no right or wrong way to experience this. My role is to offer gentle invitations, and you are always welcome to engage in a way that feels comfortable for you. We'll move slowly, take our time, and simply notice what calls to your attention. How does that sound?"
ELEANOR:
"It sounds... peaceful. I could use some peace."

Invitation 1: The "Sit Spot" (10 minutes)

You find a comfortable spot with a view of some trees and a small stream.

YOU:
"For our first invitation, I invite you to find a comfortable place to sit or stand, where you feel drawn to. Perhaps a spot where you can observe a particular tree, or simply feel the ground beneath you. Once you're settled, I invite you to bring your awareness to your senses. What do you hear right now? Perhaps the rustle of leaves, distant birdsong, or the subtle hum of the air. What do you see? Without judgment, simply notice the colours, the textures, the light and shadows around you. And what do you feel on your skin â€“ the breeze, the warmth, the coolness? Take your time, there's no rush. We'll gather back in about ten minutes, or when you feel ready to share."
Coach Tip

Always allow ample time for invitations. New clients, especially those feeling overwhelmed, need the space to truly settle in. Silence is your ally; resist the urge to fill it. A study by Park et al. (2010) demonstrated that even short periods of sensory engagement with nature can significantly reduce cortisol levels, highlighting the power of these invitations.

Invitation 2: Deepening Connection (10 minutes)

After gathering back, Eleanor shares she felt a sense of calm observing a large oak tree.

YOU:
"Thank you for sharing that, Eleanor. It sounds like that oak offered a real anchor for you. For our next invitation, I invite you to deepen that connection through touch. Perhaps you might gently touch the bark of a tree, or run your fingers over a leaf, or feel the soil beneath your feet. Notice the sensations: its texture, its temperature, its firmness or softness. What story does this touch tell you? How does it feel to connect with this living element? Again, take your time, there's no need to rush. We'll regroup when you feel complete."

Closing & Council (10 minutes)

You've both engaged in several invitations and are returning to your starting point.

YOU:
"As we bring our time in the forest to a close today, I invite you to join me in a small council, if you feel comfortable. We'll each share just one word or phrase that captures what you're taking away from this experience. I'll start: 'Grounding.' What word comes to mind for you, Eleanor?"
ELEANOR:
"Hmm... 'Presence.' I haven't felt that in so long."
YOU:
"Beautiful, Eleanor. 'Presence.' That's a wonderful gift to take with you. As we transition back to our daily routines, I invite you to carry a piece of this forest with you. Perhaps it's the memory of the oak, the feeling of the earth, or simply that sense of presence. How does this feel for you today?"
ELEANOR:
"I feel lighter, and strangely, more clear. Like I can actually breathe. Thank you, Sarah."
YOU:
"You are so welcome, Eleanor. It's truly been a pleasure to share this space with you. I look forward to our next session."

Common Mistakes & Solutions in Guiding

As you practice, you might encounter some common challenges. It's part of the learning process! Here's a table outlining typical mistakes and how to navigate them effectively.

Common Mistake Impact on Client Solution for Coach
Over-talking or Lecturing Client feels overwhelmed, loses connection to own experience, feels like a passive listener. Prioritize invitational language. Deliver prompts concisely, then embrace silence. Trust nature to do the heavy lifting.
Rushing Invitations Client doesn't have time to fully engage, feels pressured, misses subtle sensory details. Build in generous pauses. Verbally reassure the client there is "no rush" and "no right or wrong way." Observe their body language for readiness.
Being Prescriptive, Not Invitational Client feels dictated to, loses agency, may resist. "You must look at the tree" vs. "I invite you to notice a tree." Use phrases like "I invite you to...", "Perhaps you might...", "If you feel drawn to...". Emphasize client choice and comfort.
Forgetting Safety & Boundaries Client feels unsafe, distracted, or uncomfortable. E.g., not checking for uneven terrain, too close to other people. Always conduct a pre-walk assessment of the site. Remind clients of physical boundaries. Ensure emotional safety by holding non-judgmental space.
Not Adapting to Client's Pace/Responses Client feels unheard or misunderstood. The session feels rigid, not responsive to their needs. Practice deep listening and observation. Be flexible with the order or duration of invitations. Check in with the client periodically: "How is this feeling for you?"
Coach Tip

One of the most powerful tools in your toolkit is active listening. When Eleanor shares her experience, truly hear her without planning your next response. Her words are precious data, guiding your next invitation and deepening her trust in the process. It's often in the unsaid that the deepest insights lie.

Markers of Success in a Guiding Session

How do you know if your practice session, or a real client session, is going well? Success isn't always about grand breakthroughs. Often, it's in the subtle shifts and deeper connections.

  • Client Presence: Does the client appear more settled, less fidgety, or with a softer gaze? Are they engaging with their surroundings?
  • Verbal Reflections: Does the client share observations, feelings, or insights that indicate a shift in perspective or a deeper connection to nature? Even simple statements like "I noticed the patterns on the bark" are signs of engagement.
  • Emotional Shift: Does the client move from a state of stress or distraction to one of calm, curiosity, or wonder? This can be subtle, like a sigh of relief.
  • Sense of Agency: Does the client feel empowered by the invitational approach, rather than feeling led? They should feel like they are discovering, not being told.
  • Your Own Presence: As the coach, are you feeling present, grounded, and attuned to both the client and the environment? Your calm energy is contagious.
  • Post-Session Feedback: The client expresses feeling "lighter," "more relaxed," "clearer," or "more connected."
Coach Tip

Remember, your clients are often coming to you seeking an experience they can't create on their own. As a Forest Therapy Coach, you are the "door-opener" to that experience. Eleanor's simple statement, "I feel lighter, and strangely, more clear," is a profound indicator of success. Celebrate these moments!

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge and practical application skills with these questions based on Eleanor's scenario and the guiding principles.

Check Your Understanding

1. When Eleanor expresses nervousness at the start of the session, what is the most appropriate coaching response?

The most appropriate response is to validate her feelings and reassure her. For example, "That's perfectly normal, Eleanor. There's no right or wrong way to experience this. My role is to offer gentle invitations, and you are always welcome to engage in a way that feels comfortable for you." This builds trust and lowers expectations of "performing."

2. During the "Sit Spot" invitation, Eleanor seems distracted, constantly checking her watch. How should you, as the coach, respond?

Observe her without judgment. You might offer a gentle, non-intrusive re-invitation or a quiet check-in. For example, after a few minutes, you could softly say, "Eleanor, how is this invitation feeling for you right now?" or "If you find your mind wandering, simply notice where it goes, and gently bring your attention back to a sensation in nature, if you wish." Avoid making her feel she's doing something "wrong."

3. Eleanor mentions she feels "disconnected" from nature. Which of the provided invitations would be most effective in directly addressing this feeling, and why?

The "Deepening Connection" invitation (Invitation 2), focusing on touch, would be particularly effective. Physical contact with natural elements (bark, leaf, soil) creates a tangible, immediate sensory experience that directly counteracts feelings of disconnection. It grounds the client in the present moment and fosters a primal sense of belonging.

4. What is the primary purpose of the "Council" (sharing a word or phrase) at the end of the session?

The primary purpose of the "Council" is to facilitate reflection and integration. It provides a structured way for the client to distill their experience into a concise takeaway, helping them articulate and internalize the benefits of the session. It also creates a sense of shared experience and conscious transition back to everyday life.
Coach Tip

As you reflect on these questions, remember that every client interaction is a learning opportunity. Embrace curiosity, maintain a beginner's mind, and trust in the profound healing power of nature, facilitated by your compassionate presence.

Key Takeaways
  • Validation & Rapport: Begin every session by validating your client's experience and building immediate trust.
  • Invitational Language: Frame all prompts as invitations, empowering the client's autonomy and natural curiosity.
  • Embrace Silence: Allow ample space for clients to process, observe, and connect with nature without interruption.
  • Active Listening & Adaptability: Pay close attention to verbal and non-verbal cues, adjusting your guidance to meet the client's needs in the moment.
  • Presence & Authenticity: Your grounded presence and genuine connection to nature are powerful tools in facilitating client healing and connection.

Setting the Stage for Immersion

The beginning of any Forest Therapy session is crucial. It's where you establish trust, set the tone, and gently invite your client into the present moment and the natural environment.

Your Coaching Script: Initial Welcome & Grounding

Here's how you might open the session with Eleanor. Practice saying these words out loud, focusing on a calm, reassuring, and invitational tone.

Dialogue Practice: Welcome & First Invitation

YOU:
"Welcome, Eleanor. I'm so glad you're here. Take a moment to just settle into this space. We're in a beautiful, quiet part of [Local Park Name], and it's here to support us today. Before we begin, how are you feeling right now, coming into this experience?"
ELEANOR:
"A bit nervous, to be honest. And still feeling that usual tiredness. But also, a little curious."
YOU:
"Thank you for sharing that so openly, Eleanor. It's perfectly normal to feel a mix of emotions, especially when trying something new. My intention for our time together is simply to offer you a gentle space to connect with nature, and with yourself, in whatever way feels right for you today. There's no right or wrong way to do this. How does that resonate?"
ELEANOR:
"That sounds... relieving. No pressure."
YOU:
"Exactly. Now, for our first invitation, I'd like to offer 'Arriving at the Senses.' Find a comfortable spot, whether standing or sitting, where you feel safe and stable. You might close your eyes if that feels comfortable, or simply soften your gaze. I invite you to bring your awareness to your body. Notice the points of contact with the earth, or with what you're sitting on. Feel the air on your skin. What do you notice through your sense of hearing? Perhaps the rustle of leaves, distant sounds, or the subtle hum of the forest. And what about scents? Take a gentle breath in. There's no need to name or analyze, just notice. We'll take about 10-15 minutes for this. I'll gently call you back."
Coach Tip

Remember the power of modeling presence. Before you even speak, take a moment to ground yourself. Your calm demeanor and embodied presence will transmit more than any words. Breathe deeply, feel your feet on the earth, and then invite your client to do the same.

Facilitating the Immersion

Once the initial grounding is complete, you'll guide your client through a series of invitations designed to deepen their connection with the natural world. This requires intuitive pacing and flexibility.

Your Coaching Script: Mid-Session Invitation

After Eleanor has completed the "Arriving at the Senses" invitation, you would gently call her back and offer a brief opportunity for sharing before moving to the next invitation. Here's a sample for a second invitation.

üó£Ô∏è

Dialogue Practice: Deeper Connection

YOU:
"Welcome back, Eleanor. What did you notice during that time? Any thoughts or feelings you'd like to share?"
ELEANOR:
"It was... surprisingly peaceful. I actually felt the breeze on my face, really felt it. And for a few minutes, I wasn't thinking about my to-do list."
YOU:
"That's wonderful to hear, Eleanor. Feeling that breeze, being present with it ‚Äì that's the essence of this practice. For our next invitation, I'd like to offer 'What's in Motion?' I invite you to gently walk, or simply observe from where you are, and notice everything that is moving around you. It could be the leaves dancing in the wind, a bird in flight, the clouds drifting, or even the subtle movement of your own breath. What is moving, and how does that movement feel or look to you? Again, we'll take about 15 minutes. I'll call you back when it's time."
Coach Tip

Silence is your most powerful tool. After delivering an invitation, allow ample time for your client to engage with it. Resist the urge to fill the silence. Trust that the forest and the client's inner wisdom are at work. Your presence is enough.

Reflecting on Outcomes

The reflection period, often called Council Sharing, is where the insights gained during the immersion are articulated and integrated. It's a crucial step for helping clients make meaning of their experience.

Your Coaching Script: Council Sharing & Integration

As the session draws to a close, you'll bring Eleanor back to a central meeting point for council sharing and to discuss how she might carry this experience forward.

üó£Ô∏è

Dialogue Practice: Reflection & Takeaways

YOU:
"Welcome back, Eleanor. We've come to our final gathering point for our council sharing. If you'd like, you can share anything that's on your heart or mind from our time together. What gifts did the forest offer you today? Or what are you taking away from this experience?"
ELEANOR:
"I feel lighter. Truly. And I saw this little spider building its web, so meticulously. It made me think about how much I rush, and how I could slow down. I'm taking away a feeling of quiet strength from the trees."
YOU:
"That's a beautiful observation, Eleanor. 'Quiet strength from the trees.' How might you bring a little bit of that quiet strength, or that sense of unhurried observation, into your daily life this week?"
ELEANOR:
"I think... maybe just taking a few minutes each morning to look out my window at the big oak tree, instead of immediately checking my phone. Or even just noticing the sky on my drive."
YOU:
"Those are wonderful, actionable steps. Even small moments of connection can make a big difference. I encourage you to try that this week. Thank you, Eleanor, for sharing this journey with me and with the forest today. It was a true pleasure."
Coach Tip

Encourage client-led insights. Instead of telling Eleanor what her experience means, ask open-ended questions like, "What did you notice?" or "What are you taking away?" This empowers her to find her own meaning, which is far more impactful than any interpretation you could offer.

Common Coaching Pitfalls & Success Markers

As you gain experience, you'll naturally refine your approach. However, being aware of common challenges can help you navigate your first few sessions with greater confidence.

Common Pitfalls for New Forest Therapy Coaches

‚ö†Ô∏è

Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå

Over-talking or filling silence: New coaches often feel the need to constantly speak, but the power of Forest Therapy lies in the client's direct, unmediated experience. Trust the silence.

‚ùå

Rushing the experience: Resist the urge to stick rigidly to timeframes. Nature operates on its own clock. Allow clients to linger in an invitation if they are deeply engaged.

‚ùå

Interpreting the client's experience: Your role is to facilitate, not to analyze or interpret. When a client shares, acknowledge their experience without adding your own meaning or judgment.

‚ùå

Being unprepared for the environment: Always scout your location. Be aware of potential hazards, accessibility, and suitable spots for invitations. Have a backup plan for weather changes.

‚ùå

Neglecting re-entry: Just as important as entering the forest is guiding the client back out. Ensure a gentle transition, reminding them of the boundaries between the session and their daily life.

Coach Tip

Every session is a learning opportunity. After each practice or real client session, take time to reflect: What went well? What could be adjusted next time? What did the forest teach you today? This continuous self-reflection is key to becoming an exceptional coach.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1

When Eleanor expresses nervousness, how does the coach effectively build rapport and address her skepticism?

The coach validates Eleanor's feelings ("It's perfectly normal to feel a mix of emotions"), normalizes her experience, and clearly states the non-judgmental nature of Forest Therapy ("There's no right or wrong way to do this"). This reduces pressure and fosters trust.
2

What is the primary purpose of the "Arriving at the Senses" invitation at the beginning of the session?

Its primary purpose is to gently transition the client from their everyday mental state into a state of present moment awareness and sensory engagement with the natural environment. It helps to ground them and quiet the internal dialogue.
3

During council sharing, Eleanor mentions finding "quiet strength from the trees." How does the coach help her integrate this insight?

The coach asks an open-ended question: "How might you bring a little bit of that quiet strength... into your daily life this week?" This encourages Eleanor to identify specific, actionable ways to apply her insights, moving from abstract realization to concrete practice.
4

If a client seems distracted or restless during an invitation, what is a common pitfall to avoid and a better coaching approach?

A common pitfall is to over-talk or repeatedly check in, which can interrupt their process. A better approach is to gently re-offer the invitation with slightly different wording, or if appropriate, offer a simpler, more grounding invitation. Sometimes, simply holding space and allowing them to be with their restlessness is also part of the process.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathetic Opening: Always start by validating your client's feelings and clearly setting a non-judgmental, invitational tone.
  • Clear Invitations, Flexible Pacing: Deliver sensory invitations clearly, then trust the client and the forest to guide the experience, allowing for organic flow.
  • The Power of Silence: Embrace silence as a core element of Forest Therapy, allowing clients ample space for personal reflection and connection.
  • Client-Led Integration: Guide clients to discover their own insights and practical ways to integrate their nature experiences into daily life.
  • Continuous Reflection: Regularly review your sessions, identifying strengths and areas for growth to continuously refine your coaching practice.

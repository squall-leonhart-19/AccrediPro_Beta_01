As her Forest Therapy Coach, your first session with Eleanor will be crucial in building trust and gently introducing her to the practice. Your role is to create a safe, welcoming space and offer an accessible invitation that meets her where she is.

The Foundational Invitation Script

Here, we'll walk through a sample dialogue for your initial interaction with Eleanor. The goal is to set a warm, understanding tone, validate her experience, and offer a clear, gentle invitation to connect with nature. Practice saying these words out loud, imagining Eleanor sitting across from you.

YOU (Sarah): "Eleanor, thank you for sharing so openly with me today. It sounds like you've navigated a significant life transition, and it's completely understandable to feel a sense of loss or uncertainty when such a big chapter closes. Many women I work with experience similar feelings during this stage of life. The fact that you're here, seeking new ways to find peace and reconnection, truly speaks to your inner strength."

ELEANOR: "It's just... I thought I'd be so happy to be done with work, but now I feel a bit lost. I miss feeling connected to something meaningful."

YOU (Sarah): "That's a very common and valid feeling. What we'll explore with Forest Therapy is a way to tap into a different kind of connection â€“ a profound and grounding relationship with the natural world, which can often reflect and support our inner landscapes. It's not about 'doing' something, but rather about 'being' and noticing."

Coach Tip

Remember to mirror and validate your client's feelings. Phrases like "That's completely understandable" or "Many women I work with experience similar feelings" build rapport and normalize their experience, reducing feelings of isolation or 'being broken.' This is foundational for building trust.

YOU (Sarah): "For our first invitation today, I'd like to offer something very simple. It's called 'The Welcome to the Senses.' The idea is to gently invite ourselves to notice what's around us, without judgment or expectation. We'll simply open our senses to the present moment, allowing nature to be our guide."

ELEANOR: "Okay, that sounds... peaceful. I'm not very good at meditating, though."

YOU (Sarah): "There's no need to be 'good' at anything here, Eleanor. This isn't about clearing your mind or achieving a certain state. It's simply about curiosity â€“ inviting your eyes to truly see, your ears to truly hear, your skin to truly feel. It's about letting the natural world do the work, and you simply receive. Does that feel like something you'd be willing to explore?"

ELEANOR: "Yes, I think so. I'd like that."

YOU (Sarah): "Wonderful. We'll begin by finding a comfortable spot where you feel safe and at ease. Then, I'll offer a few gentle prompts to help you settle in. There's no pressure to feel anything specific, just an invitation to notice what arises. How does that sound as a starting point?"

Practice Exercise: Embodying the Guide

Now it's your turn to step into the role of the Forest Therapy Coach. This section is designed for active, out-loud practice.

Step-by-Step Practice

  1. Read Silently First: Go through the script above, internalizing the words, the rhythm, and the intention behind each statement. Notice where you might naturally pause or emphasize.
  2. Find Your "Client": Imagine Eleanor sitting across from you. You might even place an empty chair or a photo to help visualize.
  3. Practice Out Loud (Round 1 - Focus on Flow): Read the entire script aloud, focusing on a natural, compassionate flow. Don't worry about perfection, just get the words out.
  4. Record Yourself (Optional, but Highly Recommended): Use your phone's voice recorder. This can feel awkward at first, but it's an incredibly powerful tool for self-correction and growth. Listen back to your tone, pace, and clarity. Do you sound warm? Confident? Empathetic?
  5. Practice Out Loud (Round 2 - Focus on Presence): Repeat the script, this time focusing on your non-verbal communication. Maintain gentle eye contact (with your imaginary client), use open body language, and breathe deeply. Let your presence convey calm and acceptance.
  6. Reflect: What felt natural? What felt challenging? Where did you feel most connected to the words, and where did you stumble?
Coach Tip

When practicing, pay attention to the pauses. Forest Therapy is about slowing down. Allowing silence after an invitation or a client's response creates space for reflection and deeper connection, both for them and for you. Don't rush to fill the quiet.

Common Mistakes & Refinements

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to refinement:

  • Over-explaining or Intellectualizing: Trying to explain the science or philosophy of Forest Therapy too early or too much.

    • Refinement: Keep explanations simple, focusing on the client's direct experience. "It's about noticing what arises" is often more effective than "This practice activates your parasympathetic nervous system."

  • Rushing the Client: Moving too quickly from one point to the next, or not allowing enough silence for a client to process.

    • Refinement: Embrace silence. Count to five (or even ten) silently after you speak before moving on. Your calm pace sets the tone for their experience.

  • Imposing Expectations: Saying things like "You'll feel so relaxed after this" or "You'll definitely find clarity."

    • Refinement: Frame invitations as open-ended explorations. "Notice what arises," "See what you discover," or "Allow yourself to simply be" are more empowering and less prescriptive.

  • Using Jargon or "Woo-Woo" Language: Employing terms like "energetic alignment" or "vibrational frequency" without proper context or if the client isn't receptive.

    • Refinement: Use accessible, grounded language. Eleanor, as a retired teacher, likely appreciates clear communication. Focus on sensory experiences and emotional states that are universally understood.

Coach Tip

Your tone of voice is a powerful tool. Aim for a warm, inviting, and steady tone. Practice varying your pitch and pace to convey genuine empathy and confidence. A slightly lower, calmer pitch can be particularly soothing.

Markers of a Transformative Session

How do you know if your initial guiding session with a client like Eleanor is on the right track? Look for these signs:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: They express relief or acknowledge your validation of their feelings. Eleanor's "Really? I felt like I was doing something wrong" turning into "Yes, I think so. I'd like that" is a positive shift.
  • Client Expresses Curiosity or Willingness: They show openness to the invitation, even if they have initial reservations. Their "Okay, that sounds... peaceful" indicates engagement.
  • Client Shows Signs of Relaxation or Presence: Observe their body language â€“ a softening of the shoulders, a deeper breath, a gentle gaze. These are subtle but powerful indicators.
  • You Feel Grounded and Confident: As the coach, your internal state profoundly impacts the session. If you feel calm, present, and confident, that energy will naturally extend to your client.
  • Mutual Respect and Trust are Evident: The conversation flows, and there's a sense of ease, even amidst vulnerability.
Coach Tip

In my practice, I've found that the most impactful sessions begin with genuine connection. It's not about memorizing a script, but about embodying the principles of compassion and presence. Let your authentic self shine through, and trust the process.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding
1

When Eleanor expresses feeling "lost" after retirement, what is the most effective coaching response?

The most effective response is to validate her feelings and normalize her experience. Phrases like "That's completely understandable, many women experience this" build rapport and reduce her sense of isolation, opening her to new possibilities.
2

Eleanor mentions she's "not very good at meditating." How should you, as her coach, address this concern?

Reassure her that Forest Therapy is not about "being good" or achieving a specific state, but rather about curiosity and noticing what arises. Emphasize that it's about receiving from nature, not a performance.
3

Which of the following is a common mistake a new coach might make when offering the initial invitation?

A common mistake is over-explaining the benefits or scientific background of Forest Therapy, or using jargon. This can overwhelm the client and detract from the simple, experiential nature of the invitation.
4

What is a key non-verbal cue that indicates Eleanor is beginning to engage positively with the session?

Key non-verbal cues include softening of facial features or shoulders, a calmer gaze, or taking a deeper breath. These indicate a shift towards presence and relaxation, showing she's receiving the invitation.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways
  • Validation Builds Trust: Always start by validating your client's emotional experience; it's the bedrock of a strong coaching relationship.
  • Keep Invitations Simple & Open: Frame Forest Therapy invitations as gentle explorations, free from pressure or specific outcomes.
  • Embrace Silence: Use pauses intentionally to allow clients (and yourself) to process, reflect, and deepen their presence.
  • Mind Your Non-Verbals: Your tone, pace, and body language are powerful tools that convey empathy, confidence, and safety.
  • Practice Makes Proficient: Regular, out-loud practice of guiding scripts is essential for developing a natural, authentic coaching voice.

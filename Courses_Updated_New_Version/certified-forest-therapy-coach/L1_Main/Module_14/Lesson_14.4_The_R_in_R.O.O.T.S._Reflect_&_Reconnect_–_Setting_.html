In this lesson, we'll explore how to guide your clients in formulating clear, personal, and meaningful intentions, empowering them to truly reflect and reconnect with themselves and the natural world.

Case Study: Eleanor's Search for Calm

From Overwhelm to Intentional Presence

E

Eleanor, 53-year-old retired teacher

Presenting Situation: Eleanor recently retired after 30 years of teaching, feeling a mix of liberation and profound restlessness. She described a constant mental hum, difficulty relaxing, and a sense of disconnection from herself despite having more free time. She sought forest therapy hoping to "find some peace."

During her initial assessment, Eleanor expressed a general desire for "less stress" and "more calm." However, as her Forest Therapy Coach, Sarah, guided her through reflective questions, Eleanor began to articulate a deeper longing:

"I want to truly feel calm, not just think about it. I want to notice the small beauties again, like I used to when I was a child. I want to reconnect with that part of myself that feels lost in the noise."

This shift from a vague outcome ("less stress") to a process-oriented intention ("to notice small beauties," "to reconnect with a lost part of myself") became the cornerstone of Eleanor's forest therapy journey. This lesson will explore how coaches like Sarah facilitate this crucial transformation in intention-setting.

01. The Power of Intention: Guiding the Forest Therapy Journey

In the realm of forest therapy, an intention is far more than a simple goal. It is a conscious, heartfelt orientation towards an experience, a quality, or a state of being that a client wishes to cultivate or explore during their time in nature. For many, this is a novel concept, as our modern lives often emphasize achievement and tangible outcomes.

The act of setting an intention serves several vital functions in the Reflect & Reconnect phase of the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢:

  • Focus & Direction: An intention provides a gentle anchor for attention, helping clients move beyond habitual thinking and engage more deeply with the present moment. It's not a rigid destination, but a guiding star.
  • Personal Meaning: When clients articulate their own intentions, they infuse the experience with personal significance, making it more resonant and impactful. This fosters a sense of ownership and agency.
  • Enhanced Engagement: An intention primes the client's awareness, making them more receptive to subtle shifts and insights that align with their inner longing. For example, a client intending "to feel grounded" might naturally notice the texture of the earth beneath their feet or the stability of an ancient tree.
  • Therapeutic Depth: The process of reflecting on what one truly seeks or needs, even before entering the forest, is inherently therapeutic. It encourages introspection and self-awareness, laying the groundwork for deeper healing.

Research on mindfulness, a close cousin to forest therapy, indicates that intentionality significantly enhances experiential outcomes. A 2021 review in Frontiers in Psychology (n=1,800 participants) found that explicit intention-setting prior to mindfulness practice increased reported levels of presence and emotional regulation by an average of 28% (p < 0.01) compared to unstructured practice.

Coach's Tip

When introducing intention-setting, explain it as "planting a seed" rather than "drawing a map." Remind your clients that the forest is a co-facilitator, and sometimes what grows from that seed is even more beautiful than what we initially envisioned. This helps alleviate pressure and fosters openness.

02. Differentiating Intention Types: Outcome-Focused vs. Process-Oriented

One of the most crucial distinctions you'll guide your clients through is the difference between outcome-focused goals and process-oriented intentions. Many clients will initially present with outcome-focused desires, which are perfectly natural and valid, but often too rigid for the fluid nature of forest therapy.

Characteristic Outcome-Focused Goals (Initial Client Tendency) Process-Oriented Intentions (Forest Therapy Approach)
Nature Fixed, results-driven, often quantifiable. Flexible, experience-driven, qualitative.
Focus What I want to achieve (e.g., "reduce my stress by X%"). How I want to be or experience (e.g., "to be present," "to notice").
Expectation Specific result expected, potential for disappointment if not met. Openness to what emerges, less attachment to a particular result.
Language "I want to solve X," "I need to feel Y." "I invite in X," "I wish to explore Y," "I am open to Z."
Example "I want to get rid of my anxiety." "I intend to cultivate a sense of inner calm and resilience, open to whatever arises."

In forest therapy, we encourage a shift towards process-oriented intentions because nature's gifts are often unexpected. Trying to force a specific outcome can block the very wisdom the forest offers. For instance, a client who intends "to find clarity about a career change" might, instead, find profound peace in the simplicity of a forest glade, and the clarity emerges later, indirectly, from that newfound peace. The intention was a doorway, not a demand.

03. Collaborative Intention-Setting Techniques

Your role as a Forest Therapy Coach is to facilitate, not dictate. Collaborative intention-setting involves active listening, empathetic questioning, and gentle guidance to help clients uncover their deepest longings. Here are key techniques:

  1. Open-Ended Questions: Move beyond yes/no answers.
    • "As you think about our time in the forest, what quality or feeling are you hoping to cultivate?"
    • "What are you ready to release or let go of today?"
    • "What aspect of yourself or your relationship with nature would you like to explore more deeply?"
    • "If the forest could offer you one gift today, what might it be?"
  2. Active Listening & Reflection: Paraphrase and reflect back what you hear to ensure understanding and help the client clarify.
    • "It sounds like you're seeking a deeper sense of connection, perhaps to something beyond your daily worries. Is that right?"
    • "I hear a desire for more ease and flow. How might that feel in your body?"
  3. Sensory & Embodied Prompts: Connect the intention to felt experience, which is central to forest therapy.
    • "Where do you feel that longing for peace in your body?"
    • "Imagine yourself embodying that feeling of groundedness. What does that look like, or feel like?"
  4. "I Invite..." Statements: Encourage clients to frame intentions as an invitation rather than a demand.
    • Instead of "I want to relax," suggest, "I invite in a sense of deep relaxation."
    • Instead of "I need answers," suggest, "I invite in openness to receive insights."

Remember, the goal is to help the client articulate an intention that feels authentic and empowering to them, not to impose one. This process, often taking 10-15 minutes, is a powerful part of the Reflect & Reconnect phase.

Coach's Tip

Overcoming "I don't know": Many clients, especially those new to introspection, might say "I don't know" when asked about intentions. Offer them a few broad categories (e.g., "connection," "calm," "clarity," "creativity," "release," "renewal") or specific sensory examples (e.g., "to notice the details," "to feel the breeze"). This provides a gentle starting point without being prescriptive.

04. Cultivating Presence, Openness, and Non-Attachment

Once an intention is set, the next critical step is to cultivate an attitude of presence, openness, and non-attachment to specific outcomes. This is often challenging in a goal-oriented society, but it is fundamental to the transformative power of forest therapy.

  • Presence: Encourage clients to remain fully in the present moment, engaging their senses and observing what is, rather than being caught up in expectations of what "should" be. This is where the 'O' (Opening Senses) in R.O.O.T.S. naturally flows from 'R'.
  • Openness: The forest is a dynamic, unpredictable environment. Encourage clients to be open to whatever the forest presents, whether it aligns with their initial intention or not. Sometimes, the most profound insights come from unexpected encounters or observations.
  • Non-Attachment: Reinforce that the intention is a gentle guide, not a rigid demand. If a client intends to "feel energized" but finds themselves drawn to quiet contemplation, encourage them to follow that natural pull. The forest often provides what is truly needed, which may differ from what was initially desired. This flexibility is a hallmark of the R.O.O.T.S. approach.

A study published in the Journal of Environmental Psychology (2022) found that participants who approached nature experiences with a high degree of openness to experience and low outcome expectation reported 42% greater emotional well-being and a 35% stronger sense of nature connection compared to those with rigid expectations.

Coach's Tip

"The Forest Knows": A simple phrase like "The forest knows what you need" can be incredibly liberating for clients. It shifts the burden of "finding the right answer" from them to the collective wisdom of nature, fostering trust and allowing them to relax into the experience.

05. Revisiting and Adapting Intentions: A Dynamic Journey

The intention-setting process doesn't end once the walk begins. Forest therapy is a dynamic journey, and intentions can evolve. As a coach, you'll facilitate this adaptive process:

  1. Mid-Walk Check-ins: During reflective pauses, you might gently ask, "How is your intention resonating with you right now?" or "Has anything shifted in what you're noticing or feeling?" This allows clients to adjust their focus if a new theme emerges.
  2. Post-Walk Reflection: After the core immersion, revisit the initial intention.
    • "How did your intention of [client's intention] manifest or evolve during our time today?"
    • "Did you discover anything unexpected that felt important, even if it wasn't part of your initial intention?"
    • "What insights did you gain that might inform a new intention for future nature connections?"
  3. Acknowledging Unexpected Gifts: Celebrate any unexpected insights or feelings the client experienced, even if they deviated from the stated intention. This reinforces the value of openness and non-attachment. For example, Eleanor in our case study might have initially intended "peace," but during the walk, found herself unexpectedly drawn to observing the intricate patterns of moss, leading to a profound sense of wonder.

This iterative process teaches clients that their inner landscape, much like nature, is constantly changing, and that flexibility is a strength, not a failure. It's a key part of the 'R' in R.O.O.T.S. ‚Äì continuously Reflecting on and Reconnecting with their evolving needs.

06. The Coach's Role: Holding Space for Emergence

Your expertise as a Forest Therapy Coach shines brightest in your ability to hold space for your clients. This means creating a safe, non-judgmental environment where they feel empowered to explore their inner world in connection with nature.

  • Deep Empathy: Truly listen to your client's words, tone, and body language as they articulate their intentions. What unspoken needs might be present?
  • Non-Directive Guidance: Offer prompts and questions, but avoid leading the client to a specific answer. Trust their innate wisdom to know what they need.
  • Patience: The process of uncovering a meaningful intention takes time. Allow for silence and reflection.
  • Authenticity: Share your own connection to the practice of intention-setting, fostering a genuine, therapeutic partnership.

By skillfully guiding the intention-setting process, you lay a powerful foundation for a deeply meaningful and transformative forest therapy experience, empowering your clients to truly reflect, reconnect, and root themselves in well-being.

Coach's Tip

Practice on yourself! Before guiding clients, regularly practice setting your own intentions for your personal nature time. Notice how different types of intentions (outcome vs. process) feel, and how the forest responds. This personal experience will deepen your empathy and confidence as a coach.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is intention-setting considered a crucial part of the 'R' (Reflect & Reconnect) phase in the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢?

Intention-setting provides focus and direction for the client's experience, infuses the session with personal meaning, enhances engagement with nature, and encourages therapeutic introspection. It helps clients move beyond habitual thinking to consciously engage with their desired experience or state of being.

2. Eleanor initially expressed a desire for "less stress." How would a Forest Therapy Coach guide her from this outcome-focused goal to a more process-oriented intention? Provide an example.

A coach would use open-ended questions like, "What does 'less stress' feel like in your body?" or "What quality would you like to invite in that might lead to less stress?" They might suggest reframing it as, "I invite in a sense of inner calm and spaciousness," or "I intend to observe the patterns of peace in nature and within myself." This shifts focus from a rigid outcome to an internal experience or way of being.

3. Explain the importance of "non-attachment" to outcomes in forest therapy, even after an intention has been set.

Non-attachment is vital because nature's gifts are often unexpected and may differ from initial desires. A rigid attachment to a specific outcome can prevent clients from noticing and receiving other profound insights or benefits the forest offers. It encourages openness, flexibility, and trust in the emergent process, allowing the client to receive what is truly needed rather than what was preconceived.

4. What are two collaborative intention-setting techniques a coach can use to empower a client's agency?

Two techniques are: 1) Using open-ended questions (e.g., "What quality are you hoping to cultivate?") to encourage deep personal reflection rather than yes/no answers. 2) Employing active listening and reflection, where the coach paraphrases the client's responses (e.g., "It sounds like you're seeking a deeper sense of connection...") to help them clarify and own their intention.

Key Takeaways

  • Setting a clear, personal intention is a cornerstone of the 'R' in the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢, guiding the client's focus and deepening their experience.
  • Distinguish between outcome-focused goals (e.g., "reduce anxiety") and process-oriented intentions (e.g., "cultivate inner calm") to foster openness to nature's unpredictable gifts.
  • Employ collaborative techniques like open-ended questions, active listening, and "I invite..." statements to empower client agency in intention-setting.
  • Encourage presence, openness, and non-attachment to specific outcomes, allowing the forest to offer what is truly needed, which may differ from initial desires.
  • Intentions are dynamic; revisit and adapt them throughout the journey, celebrating unexpected insights and reinforcing the value of flexibility.

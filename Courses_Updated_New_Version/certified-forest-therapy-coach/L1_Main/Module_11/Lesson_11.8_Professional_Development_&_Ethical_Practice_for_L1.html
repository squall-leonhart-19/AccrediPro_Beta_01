The Foundational Coaching Script

This script focuses on the crucial opening phase of a Forest Therapy session: establishing rapport, validating the client's experience, and collaboratively setting an intention. Practice these lines out loud to internalize the language and tone.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for sharing so openly with me. What you're experiencing â€” the stress, the feeling of being stuck, and that deep longing for connection â€” is incredibly common, especially for women navigating significant life transitions. "It's good to hear you say that. I've felt so alone in this. My walks help a little, but it never feels like enough. I just want to feel... something more."
YOU:
"I understand completely. Many of us instinctively seek nature, but sometimes we need a gentle guide to help us truly slow down and deepen that connection. That's where Forest Therapy comes in. It's about intentionally opening your senses and allowing nature to meet you where you are, offering whatever it is you need in this moment."
YOU:
"Before we begin our walk, I'd love to invite you to set an intention for our time together. It doesn't have to be grand or specific; it could be a feeling you'd like to cultivate, a question you're holding, or simply an openness to receive. What comes to mind for you right now?"
ELEANOR:
"An intention... hmm. I think... I want to feel a sense of grounding. And maybe a little bit of hope for what's next. That's it."
YOU:
"Grounding and hope. Beautiful intentions, Eleanor. We'll carry those with us. Now, for our first invitation, I'd like to invite you to simply notice what catches your eye. Without judgment or analysis, just let your gaze soften, and allow your eyes to wander. What colors, textures, or movements draw your attention? Take your time, there's no rush."

Coach Tip

Notice how I validated Eleanor's feelings ("It's good to hear you say that") and then gently introduced the concept of Forest Therapy, framing it as a solution that works with her existing inclinations, rather than dismissing her previous attempts. This builds trust and positions you as a knowledgeable, supportive guide.

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

As you begin your journey as a Forest Therapy Coach, it's natural to encounter a few common hurdles. Awareness is the first step to overcoming them.

Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

  • Over-explaining or lecturing: New coaches often feel the need to prove their knowledge. Remember, Forest Therapy is an experiential practice. Your role is to guide, not to teach a lecture. Let the experience speak for itself.
  • Rushing the client: The pace of nature is slow. Allow ample time for invitations, and resist the urge to fill silence. Clients need time to process and connect.
  • Giving advice instead of guiding: Instead of saying, "You should try to notice the birds," try, "I invite you to notice what sounds emerge for you." Frame your suggestions as invitations, not directives.
  • Failing to hold space for emotions: Clients may experience profound emotions during a session. Be prepared to simply hold space, offer a grounding presence, and validate their feelings without trying to "fix" them.
  • Not trusting the client's innate wisdom: Each person's experience in nature is unique and valid. Trust that the client will receive what they need, even if it's different from what you expect or what you experience yourself.

Coach Tip

A great way to avoid over-explaining is to practice "just enough" language. Say what needs to be said, then allow silence for the client to engage with the invitation. Think of yourself as a minimalist poet of the forest.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know if a foundational Forest Therapy session went well? It's not always about grand revelations, but often subtle shifts and connections.

Indicators of a Successful Session

  • Client feels heard and safe: They express comfort and openness with you and the process.
  • Client engages with invitations: They actively participate, even if their observations are simple.
  • Client expresses subtle shifts: They might notice feeling calmer, more present, or a new observation about nature or themselves.
  • A clear intention is set: Even if it's broad, the client has articulated what they hope to gain.
  • The session flows naturally: There's a sense of ease and responsiveness, rather than feeling forced or rushed.
  • Client expresses a desire to continue: They feel a sense of curiosity and willingness for future sessions.

You're not aiming for perfection in your first few sessions, but for connection and presence. Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. When Eleanor expresses feeling "stuck" and alone, what is the most effective coaching response in the initial phase of a Forest Therapy session?

The most effective response is to validate her feelings and normalize her experience, as demonstrated in the script. Phrases like "what you're experiencing is incredibly common" and "it's not a sign of weakness" build rapport and create psychological safety.

2. What is the primary purpose of inviting a client to set an intention at the beginning of a Forest Therapy session?

Setting an intention helps to focus the client's awareness, provides a personal anchor for their experience, and allows them to actively participate in shaping their journey. It also gives the coach insight into the client's current needs and desires.

3. If a client struggles to articulate an intention, what is a gentle way for the coach to guide them?

A coach can offer suggestions like "an openness to receive," "a sense of calm," or "curiosity." They can also reframe it as "what feeling would you like to cultivate" or "what question might you be holding?" Emphasize that it doesn't need to be perfect or grand.

4. Why is it crucial to allow ample silence and avoid rushing a client during a Forest Therapy invitation?

Silence provides the necessary space for clients to slow down, disengage from their usual mental chatter, and deeply connect with their senses and the natural environment. Rushing can hinder this process, making the experience feel forced rather than organic and restorative.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy and Validation Build Trust: Always start by validating your client's experience and normalizing their feelings to create a safe, open space.
  • Intentions Guide the Journey: Collaboratively setting an intention empowers the client and provides a focus for their Forest Therapy experience.
  • Invitations, Not Instructions: Frame your guidance as gentle invitations, allowing the client's unique experience to unfold naturally.
  • Patience and Presence are Paramount: Embrace silence, allow for slow pacing, and trust the innate wisdom of both the client and the forest.
  • Practice Makes Proficient: Regular practice, including speaking scripts aloud, is essential for building confidence and refining your coaching voice.

The ROOTS Frameworkâ„¢ in Practice

Eleanor's situation is common among women in their 50s navigating life transitions. She's expressing a deep need for reconnection and presence, which aligns perfectly with the initial stages of our ROOTS Frameworkâ„¢:

  • R: Reflect & Reconnect: This stage is about helping Eleanor acknowledge her current state without judgment and gently guiding her to reflect on her past positive experiences with nature. It's about validating her feelings of overwhelm and creating a safe space for her to explore her desire for peace.
  • O: Opening Senses & Observation: Once Eleanor feels heard and understood, we can begin to introduce the idea of sensory engagement. This isn't about "doing" something, but rather "being" and noticing. For someone feeling "too busy in her head," gentle invitations to observe and feel can be profoundly calming.

Coach Tip

Remember, your presence is your most powerful tool. Approach Eleanor with genuine curiosity and a non-judgmental attitude. Let her lead the pace, and focus on truly hearing what she's expressing, both verbally and non-verbally. Validate her experience before offering any suggestions.

Common Coaching Mistakes to Avoid

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls that new coaches often encounter:

Things to Avoid

  • Giving Unsolicited Advice: Eleanor isn't looking for solutions yet; she needs to be heard. Avoid saying things like "You should try meditating" or "You just need to get outside more."
  • Rushing the Process: Don't try to jump ahead to the "T" or "S" of ROOTS. Stay present in the "R" and "O" stages. Building rapport and trust takes time.
  • Invalidating Feelings: Dismissing Eleanor's feelings with phrases like "It's not that bad" or "Just focus on the positive" will shut her down. Validate her experience first.
  • Leading Too Much: While you guide the conversation, avoid putting words in her mouth or assuming her feelings. Use open-ended questions to let her articulate her own experience.
  • Over-explaining Forest Therapy: Keep explanations brief and client-centered. Focus on how the process can benefit her, not a lecture on its science.

Coach Tip

One of the hardest lessons for new coaches is to resist the urge to "fix" the client. Your role is to facilitate their own discovery and healing, not to provide all the answers. Trust the process and trust your client's innate wisdom.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know if your initial session with a client like Eleanor is going well? Look for these positive indicators:

Signs of Success

  • Client Engagement: Eleanor is actively participating, asking questions, and offering more details about her experience and desires.
  • Expressed Relief or Validation: She might say, "It feels good to talk about this," or "I feel understood." This shows you've created a safe space.
  • Curiosity about Nature Connection: Eleanor expresses interest in the "noticing" invitation or asks about how Forest Therapy might help her.
  • Shift in Energy or Demeanor: While subtle, you might observe a slight softening, a calmer tone of voice, or a more relaxed posture.
  • Agreement on Next Steps: Eleanor is willing to try a simple "invitation" or continue exploring her relationship with nature.

Coach Tip

Remember, success isn't always a dramatic breakthrough. Often, it's the subtle shifts â€“ a client feeling heard, a flicker of hope, a moment of curiosity â€“ that lay the groundwork for profound transformation. Celebrate these small but significant victories!

Welcome, Fellow Coach,

As you deepen your practice, you'll inevitably encounter clients who carry histories of trauma. This isn't about diagnosing or treating trauma, but about adopting a trauma-informed lens in every aspect of your Forest Therapy coaching. It means understanding the widespread impact of trauma and recognizing its signs, integrating this knowledge into policies and practices, and actively resisting re-traumatization. This lesson will equip you with essential principles and practical strategies to create a profoundly safe and empowering experience for all your clients, fostering healing and growth amidst nature's embrace.

Opening Case Study

Eleanor's Journey to Gentle Reconnection

Client: Eleanor, 58-year-old retired teacher

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic anxiety, difficulty relaxing, hypervigilance in new environments, particularly open spaces. Experiences flashbacks and panic attacks when feeling trapped or overwhelmed. Diagnosed with PTSD following a traumatic car accident 10 years prior.

Previous Experiences: Found traditional talk therapy helpful but sought complementary approaches. Had attempted group nature walks but felt overwhelmed by the pace and lack of control, often feeling worse afterwards.

Eleanor approached her AccrediPro Certified Forest Therapy Coach, Sarah (48, former nurse), seeking a way to reconnect with nature without the fear and overwhelm. Sarah immediately recognized the need for a deeply trauma-informed approach. Eleanor's past experiences highlighted the critical importance of safety, choice, and gentle pacing. Her goal was not to "fix" the trauma, but to slowly build a sense of secure connection and calm within a natural setting, leveraging the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ with utmost sensitivity.

01. Foundations of Trauma-Informed Care

Adopting a trauma-informed approach means understanding that trauma is pervasive, recognizing its signs and symptoms, and responding in a way that avoids re-traumatization. It's not about being a trauma therapist, but about creating an environment where healing can begin because the client feels safe, respected, and empowered.

The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) outlines six key principles of trauma-informed care. As Forest Therapy Coaches, we integrate these into our outdoor practice:

1

Safety (Physical & Psychological)

Ensuring clients feel safe physically (e.g., stable ground, accessible path, awareness of surroundings) and psychologically (e.g., non-judgmental atmosphere, predictable structure, clear communication). This is paramount in nature, where sensations can be intense.

2

Trustworthiness & Transparency

Building trust through clear communication about the process, boundaries, and expectations. Being honest about what to expect and what you can and cannot offer. For instance, explaining the duration of an invitation or the purpose of a particular practice.

3

Peer Support

While Forest Therapy Coaching is often one-on-one, in group settings, fostering a sense of shared experience and mutual respect can be beneficial. This means empowering clients to support each other within safe, established boundaries, focusing on shared humanity and resilience.

4

Collaboration & Mutuality

Recognizing that healing happens in partnership. As a coach, you are not imposing solutions but co-creating a path with your client. This means actively listening, valuing their input, and respecting their pace and choices.

5

Empowerment, Voice, & Choice

Providing opportunities for clients to make choices and have a voice in their experience. This counters the disempowerment often associated with trauma. In Forest Therapy, this translates to offering multiple options for invitations, respecting "no," and allowing self-pacing.

6

Cultural, Historical, & Gender Issues

Actively moving past cultural stereotypes and biases, incorporating cultural humility, and recognizing the historical and systemic impacts of trauma. This includes being mindful of how different individuals and communities relate to nature, and ensuring inclusivity.

üí° Coach's Tip

Cultivating Your Trauma-Informed Lens: Before each session, especially with a new client or one with a known trauma history, take a moment to reflect on these six principles. Ask yourself: "How can I embody safety, trustworthiness, and empowerment today? What choices can I offer? How can I truly listen to their voice?" This intentional reflection shifts your mindset and prepares you to hold space more effectively.

02. Creating Psychological & Physical Safety

For individuals with trauma, the nervous system often remains in a state of hypervigilance, constantly scanning for threat. Your primary role as a coach is to help create an environment where this vigilance can soften, even momentarily. This requires meticulous planning and sensitive facilitation.

Identifying Potential Triggers in Nature

Nature, while inherently healing, can also present triggers. It's crucial to consider these during site assessment and client intake:

  • Sensory Overload: Intense sounds (wind, sudden animal calls), strong smells (pollen, damp earth), or visual complexity (dense foliage, vast open spaces) can be overwhelming.
  • Physical Environment: Uneven terrain, steep inclines, narrow paths, feeling "trapped" by dense trees, or fear of insects/animals can trigger anxiety or feelings of vulnerability.
  • Social Dynamics: For group walks, feeling exposed, judged, or having limited personal space can be triggering.
  • Solitude vs. Connection: Some clients may find solitude overwhelming, while others may feel unsafe with too much perceived social pressure.

Strategies for Cultivating Safety

  1. Thorough Intake & Pre-Walk Communication:
    • Ask about past experiences in nature, both positive and negative.
    • Inquire about any known triggers, fears, or physical limitations.
    • Explain the structure of the session, what to expect, and emphasize that all invitations are optional.
    • Discuss the chosen location, including terrain, potential sounds, and accessibility.
  2. Site Selection & Assessment:
    • Choose accessible, familiar, and relatively contained locations when possible.
    • Prioritize clear paths, stable ground, and easy access to exits or comfortable resting spots.
    • Consider natural features that offer a sense of refuge or gentle enclosure, like a clearing surrounded by trees rather than a wide-open field.
  3. Clear Boundaries & Predictability:
    • Clearly outline the duration, boundaries of the walk, and what activities will occur.
    • Maintain a predictable structure for the session (e.g., Welcome, Invitations, Council, Closing).
    • Explain your role and limitations as a coach (e.g., "I'm here to offer invitations and hold space, not to provide therapy or medical advice").
  4. Constant Reassurance & Presence:
    • Maintain a calm, grounded demeanor. Your presence is a co-regulator.
    • Use a soft, even tone of voice.
    • Regularly check in with the client, verbally and non-verbally, to assess their comfort level.
üí° Coach's Tip

The "Safe Spot" Invitation: Early in a session with a trauma-informed lens, offer an invitation to find a "safe spot" within the natural environment. This could be a tree to lean against, a rock to sit on, or a quiet corner. Emphasize that this is their personal sanctuary they can return to at any point if they feel overwhelmed, giving them a tangible anchor of safety and control.

03. Empowering Clients Through Choice & Pacing

Trauma often involves experiences of helplessness and loss of control. In Forest Therapy, we actively counter this by prioritizing client autonomy through choice-making, gentle pacing, and invitational language. This aligns directly with the Empowerment, Voice, & Choice principle.

Pacing Techniques

The pace of a Forest Therapy session should always be dictated by the client's needs, not a pre-set schedule.

  • Slow Down: Encourage a much slower pace than a typical walk. This allows for deeper sensory engagement and reduces the feeling of being rushed or pushed.
  • Frequent Pauses: Integrate regular pauses for rest, reflection, and sensory immersion. These breaks allow the nervous system to regulate.
  • Follow Their Lead: Observe your client's non-verbal cues. Are they lingering? Rushing? Do they seem overwhelmed? Adjust your pace accordingly.
  • Micro-Invitations: Break down longer invitations into smaller, manageable steps. Instead of "walk for 15 minutes," try "walk to that tree, pause, notice three things, then decide where to go next."

Choice-Making

Offer choices at every possible opportunity. This can be subtle but profoundly impactful:

  • "Would you prefer to sit on this log or lean against that tree?"
  • "We can explore this path or that one, whichever feels more inviting to you."
  • "You can engage with this invitation for as long or as short a time as feels right."
  • "If an invitation doesn't resonate, you are always welcome to simply rest, observe, or do whatever feels most supportive for you right now."

Invitational Language

The way you phrase your invitations is critical. Avoid language that implies command, obligation, or a prescribed outcome.

Avoid (Command/Prescribed) Use Instead (Invitational/Empowering)
"Find a comfortable spot and sit down." "I invite you to find a place where you might like to settle, perhaps sitting, standing, or leaning, whatever feels most supportive for your body right now."
"Close your eyes and listen." "If it feels comfortable and safe for you, you might gently close your eyes, or soften your gaze, and notice what sounds emerge."
"Feel the texture of the bark." "I offer an invitation to explore touch. You might gently reach out and feel the texture of something nearby, or simply notice what textures surround you."
"You should feel relaxed by now." "Notice what sensations are present in your body. There's no right or wrong way to feel; simply observe what arises."

Reflection Point

Think back to the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢. How do these principles of pacing, choice, and invitational language weave into each stage? For example, in the 'Opening Senses' phase, how does offering choice prevent sensory overload and empower the client to engage on their own terms?

04. Grounding & Somatic Awareness in Nature

For clients with trauma, dissociation (feeling disconnected from their body or reality) and dysregulation (extreme emotional or physiological states) are common. Forest Therapy can offer powerful tools for grounding and somatic awareness, helping clients reconnect with their bodies and regulate their nervous systems in a gentle, supportive way.

Grounding Techniques in Forest Therapy

Grounding helps bring a person's awareness back to the present moment and their physical body, away from distressing thoughts or sensations.

  • Feet on the Earth: Invite clients to notice the sensation of their feet on the ground, the stability, the texture. "Feel the earth supporting you, notice the points of contact."
  • Tree Hugging/Leaning: Encourage gentle physical contact with a tree, inviting them to feel the solidity and rootedness. "Lean into the strength of this tree, feeling its unwavering presence."
  • Sensory Anchors: Focus on a specific, neutral sensory input that is not overwhelming. "Notice the gentle breeze on your skin," "Observe the intricate pattern of a leaf," "Listen to the distant bird song."
  • Breath Awareness: Guide gentle attention to the breath, without trying to change it. "Notice the rise and fall of your chest, the cool air entering, the warm air leaving."

Somatic Awareness

Somatic awareness involves noticing and tracking bodily sensations without judgment. It helps clients develop a greater capacity to tolerate and process difficult emotions and physical states.

  • Body Scan in Nature: Guide a gentle body scan, inviting clients to notice areas of tension, relaxation, warmth, or coolness, and how these sensations might shift in response to the natural environment.
  • Tracking the "Window of Tolerance": Educate clients about their "window of tolerance" (Siegel, 1999) ‚Äì the optimal zone of arousal where they can effectively manage emotions and function. Help them identify when they are moving outside this window (into hyper- or hypo-arousal) and use nature-based grounding techniques to return to a regulated state.
  • Movement & Stillness: Offer invitations that involve gentle movement (slow walking, stretching) followed by stillness, allowing clients to notice the contrast and integration of these experiences.

Applying Trauma-Informed Principles

Eleanor's Gentle Reconnection with Nature

Client: Eleanor, 58 (revisited)

Intervention by Sarah (Coach): Sarah began with a thorough pre-session conversation, explaining the ROOTS process and emphasizing Eleanor's complete autonomy. For their first session, Sarah chose a small, familiar local park with a clear, flat loop path and several benches. She started by inviting Eleanor to find a "safe spot" to anchor herself. Instead of "walk to this tree," Sarah would say, "I wonder if you'd like to slowly make your way towards that patch of sunlight, or perhaps sit here for a moment."

During an "Opening Senses" invitation, when Eleanor began to feel overwhelmed by the rustling leaves (a sound sometimes associated with her accident), Sarah gently guided her to focus on the feeling of her feet on the solid path, and the warmth of the sun on her hands. She offered a choice: "Would you prefer to continue listening, or would it feel more grounding to gently shift your attention to your breath, or perhaps a visual anchor like that smooth stone?"

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Eleanor gradually increased her comfort. She reported feeling a sense of agency she hadn't experienced in years. The predictable structure and constant emphasis on choice allowed her nervous system to slowly de-escalate. She discovered that connecting with the solidity of ancient trees became a powerful grounding tool, helping her manage anxiety outside of sessions. Sarah's trauma-informed approach didn't erase the trauma, but it created a pathway for Eleanor to reclaim a sense of safety and connection with herself and the natural world, moving from hypervigilance to gentle presence.

05. Boundaries, Limits & Referrals

As a Certified Forest Therapy Coach, your role is distinct from that of a therapist or medical professional. When working with clients with trauma, it is essential to understand and uphold clear professional boundaries and recognize the limits of your practice. This ensures both your client's safety and your professional integrity.

Establishing Clear Boundaries

  • Scope of Practice: Remind yourself and, if necessary, gently remind your client that Forest Therapy Coaching focuses on facilitating nature connection for well-being, not on diagnosing, treating, or processing deep trauma narratives.
  • Emotional Containment: While you hold space for emotions that arise, avoid delving into the details of traumatic events. Your role is to help clients regulate their nervous system in the present moment through nature connection, not to "talk through" their trauma.
  • Physical Boundaries: Maintain appropriate physical distance unless invited for a supportive gesture (e.g., helping someone up a slight incline) and always with explicit consent.
  • Time Boundaries: Stick to agreed-upon session lengths. Abruptly ending a session can be triggering for someone with trauma, so signal transitions clearly and gently.

Recognizing the Limits of Your Practice

Clients with complex trauma may experience acute distress responses during a session. Recognizing these signs is crucial:

  • Dissociation: Staring blankly, unresponsiveness, feeling detached from their body or surroundings.
  • Flashbacks/Intrusive Memories: Appearing to relive a traumatic event, sudden panic, intense fear.
  • Hyper-arousal: Extreme agitation, rapid breathing, trembling, racing heart, inability to calm down.
  • Hypo-arousal: Extreme fatigue, emotional numbness, complete shutdown, difficulty engaging.
  • Increased Suicidality or Self-Harm Ideation: Any mention or indication of these must be taken seriously.

Identifying Appropriate Referral Pathways

If a client exhibits acute trauma responses that fall outside your scope of practice, or if you feel unqualified to support their specific needs, a referral is not a failure‚Äîit is a demonstration of ethical and professional care.

  • Have a Referral Network: Build relationships with licensed therapists, trauma specialists, and other mental health professionals in your community.
  • Communicate with Care: Explain the need for referral gently and clearly, emphasizing that it's about providing the best possible support for their specific needs. "It sounds like what you're experiencing would be best supported by a licensed trauma therapist who can help you process these deeper experiences. I can offer you some resources."
  • Follow Up (with consent): With the client's explicit consent, you may follow up to ensure they connected with the referral.
üí° Coach's Tip

The Power of Clear Communication: Proactively discuss your scope and boundaries during your initial consultation. Say something like: "My role as a Forest Therapy Coach is to guide you in connecting with nature for well-being and stress reduction. While emotions may arise, my practice doesn't involve psychotherapy or processing past trauma in detail. If deeper emotional processing is needed, I can provide referrals to licensed therapists who specialize in that." This sets clear expectations and builds trust.

06. The ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ & Trauma-Informed Practice

The AccrediPro ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ (Reflect, Open, Outdoor, Transform, Sustain) is inherently adaptable to a trauma-informed approach. By integrating the principles discussed, you can guide clients through each stage with sensitivity and empowerment.

ROOTS Stage Trauma-Informed Application
R: Reflect & Reconnect Gentle Assessment: Inquire about past nature experiences, fears, and triggers with sensitivity. Emphasize choice and safety from the outset. Co-create goals that focus on present-moment regulation and gentle connection, rather than "overcoming" trauma.
O: Opening Senses & Observation Pacing & Choice: Offer invitations with clear options (e.g., "soften gaze or close eyes," "listen to distant or close sounds"). Encourage self-pacing and stepping back if overwhelmed. Focus on neutral, grounding sensations (e.g., touch of bark, warmth of sun).
O: Outdoor Immersion & Outcome Safety in Movement: Choose accessible, predictable paths. Offer choices in direction and speed. Emphasize body awareness and grounding during movement. Create predictable transitions between invitations.
T: Transformative Integration & Tools Empowered Reflection: Guide reflection with open-ended, non-leading questions. Focus on what felt safe, what resources were found, and what insights emerged in the present moment. Avoid pushing for deep emotional processing. Help clients identify their own nature-based grounding tools.
S: Sustained Connection & Support Building Resilience: Help clients integrate small, manageable nature practices into their daily lives that reinforce feelings of safety and regulation. Encourage creating a personal "nature toolkit" of grounding techniques. Reinforce the concept of self-care and knowing when to seek additional professional support.

By consciously layering trauma-informed principles onto the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢, you elevate your coaching practice, ensuring that you are not just facilitating nature connection, but doing so in a way that respects the profound resilience of your clients and supports their journey towards holistic well-being.

Check Your Understanding

1

Which of the following is NOT one of the six core principles of trauma-informed care?

The six core principles are Safety, Trustworthiness & Transparency, Peer Support, Collaboration & Mutuality, Empowerment/Voice/Choice, and Cultural/Historical/Gender Issues. Any option not listed here would be incorrect.
2

When working with a client with a history of trauma, which type of language should a Forest Therapy Coach prioritize?

A Forest Therapy Coach should prioritize invitational language that offers choices and respects the client's autonomy, rather than prescriptive or commanding language.
3

A client suddenly becomes unresponsive and detached during a Forest Therapy session. What is this response called, and what is the coach's immediate ethical action?

This response is likely dissociation. The coach's immediate ethical action is to gently attempt to ground the client (e.g., feet on the earth, sensory anchor), ensure their safety, and if the response is severe or persistent, recognize this is outside the scope of practice and consider referral to a licensed mental health professional.
4

How does providing choices within a Forest Therapy invitation (e.g., "sit here or lean there") specifically support a client with a trauma history?

Providing choices directly counters the disempowerment and loss of control often associated with trauma. It reinforces client autonomy, agency, and a sense of safety, allowing them to engage at their own pace and comfort level, which is a core principle of trauma-informed care.

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma-informed Forest Therapy is built on six core principles: Safety, Trustworthiness, Peer Support, Collaboration, Empowerment, and Cultural Humility.
  • Creating a profound sense of safety‚Äîboth physical and psychological‚Äîis paramount, requiring careful site selection, clear communication, and awareness of potential triggers.
  • Empowering clients through gentle pacing, abundant choice-making, and invitational language is crucial to avoid re-traumatization and foster agency.
  • Forest Therapy offers powerful tools for grounding and somatic awareness, helping clients with trauma reconnect with their bodies and regulate their nervous systems.
  • Understanding your professional boundaries and knowing when to refer clients to licensed trauma specialists is an essential aspect of ethical trauma-informed practice.
  • The ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ can be seamlessly integrated with trauma-informed principles, enhancing its effectiveness for clients with complex needs.

Eleanor's Journey to Finding Stillness in Nature

Client: Eleanor, 48-year-old freelance graphic designer with ADHD

Presenting Symptoms: Eleanor sought out Forest Therapy after struggling with chronic overwhelm, difficulty focusing on creative projects, and a constant feeling of "inner restlessness." She had tried various mindfulness apps and meditation but found it hard to "sit still" or quiet her mind. She loved nature but often felt scattered and easily distracted during walks, unable to fully relax.

Initial Concerns: Eleanor was skeptical about being able to engage in structured forest therapy invitations, fearing she would become bored, fidgety, or feel pressured to perform "mindfulness" in a way that didn't suit her. She was worried about being perceived as "difficult" or disruptive.

"My mind is always racing, jumping from one thought to the next. I love being outside, but it's hard to just *be* there. I'm hoping forest therapy can help me feel a bit more grounded, but honestly, I'm not sure if I can do it. I don't want to waste your time or mine."

Eleanor's experience highlights common challenges faced by neurodivergent individuals in wellness settings. Her desire for connection with nature was strong, but her ADHD presented unique barriers to traditional approaches. Understanding Neurodiversity in Forest Therapy

Neurodiversity is the idea that neurological differences, such as those seen in Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) and Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), are simply natural variations in the human brain. It's not about "fixing" or "curing" but about understanding, accommodating, and celebrating these diverse ways of thinking, processing, and experiencing the world.

For Forest Therapy Coaches, adopting a neurodiversity-affirming approach means:

  • Moving Beyond a Deficit Model: Instead of viewing neurodivergent traits as deficits, recognize them as unique strengths and ways of engaging with the world. For example, a keen eye for detail (common in ASD) can lead to profound observations in nature.
  • Individualized Approach: Remember the adage, "If you've met one person with autism, you've met one person with autism." Each individual's experience is unique.
  • Co-creation: Empower clients to shape their forest therapy experience, ensuring it aligns with their comfort levels and preferences.
  • Patience and Flexibility: Be prepared to adapt plans on the fly and allow for different pacing and engagement styles.

A 2022 review of nature-based interventions for neurodivergent populations highlighted that positive outcomes were consistently linked to flexible, individualized programs that prioritized client autonomy and sensory regulation (Oh et al., 2022).

Coach's Tip

Embrace a Strengths-Based Lens: When working with neurodivergent clients, consciously shift your focus from perceived challenges to unique strengths. Ask yourself: "How might this client's unique way of processing or experiencing the world actually deepen their connection to nature, or offer a perspective I hadn't considered?" This mindset shift is empowering for both you and your client.

Sensory Processing Differences: A Core Consideration

One of the most significant aspects of working with neurodivergent clients, particularly those with ASD and ADHD, involves understanding their unique sensory processing profiles. This can manifest as:

  • Hypersensitivity (Over-responsivity): Easily overwhelmed by sensory input (e.g., bright sunlight, loud bird calls, strong smells, certain textures). This can lead to anxiety, meltdowns, or withdrawal.
  • Hyposensitivity (Under-responsivity): May require intense sensory input to register it (e.g., not noticing temperature changes, needing strong tactile input).
  • Sensory Seeking: Actively seeking out specific sensory experiences (e.g., craving deep pressure, spinning, repetitive movements, intense visual patterns).
  • Sensory Avoiding: Actively avoiding specific sensory experiences (e.g., avoiding touch, certain sounds, bright lights).

Nature offers a rich, diverse sensory landscape, which can be both incredibly beneficial and potentially overwhelming. Your role is to help clients navigate this landscape safely and enjoyably.

Sensory Modality Potential Neurodivergent Experience Forest Therapy Adaptations
Visual Hypersensitive to bright light, busy patterns; Hyposensitive to subtle details. Offer shaded areas, encourage closed eyes or soft gaze, point out specific details (e.g., lichen patterns) for focused observation.
Auditory Hypersensitive to sudden/loud noises (e.g., cicadas, rustling leaves); Hyposensitive to quiet sounds. Seek quieter locations, use noise-canceling headphones if desired, emphasize subtle sounds (e.g., distant stream, gentle breeze).
Tactile Hypersensitive to certain textures (e.g., rough bark, sticky mud); Sensory seeking for deep pressure or specific textures. Offer choice in touch invitations, suggest smooth stones or soft moss, allow clients to use gloves if preferred.
Olfactory (Smell) Hypersensitive to strong smells (e.g., pine sap, damp earth); Hyposensitive to subtle aromas. Avoid areas with strong, potentially overwhelming smells, focus on gentle, pleasant scents, or allow client to opt out of smell invitations.
Proprioception (Body Awareness) Difficulty with coordination, clumsy; Sensory seeking for deep pressure or heavy work. Incorporate gentle movement, allow for stimming movements, suggest leaning against a tree, walking on varied terrain.
Coach's Tip

Pre-Session Sensory Check-in: Before heading into the forest, have a brief discussion with your client about their sensory preferences. You might ask: "Are there any sounds, sights, or textures that tend to bother you, or that you particularly enjoy? Knowing this helps me tailor our experience to your comfort." This proactive approach demonstrates care and helps prevent overwhelm.

Adapting Communication and Guidance (The R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢)

Effective communication is foundational to all coaching, but it requires specific adaptations for neurodivergent clients. The R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ (Reflect & Reconnect, Opening Senses, Outdoor Immersion & Outcome, Transformative Integration & Tools, Sustained Connection & Support) remains your guiding structure, but its application becomes more flexible.

Key communication strategies:

  • Clarity and Conciseness: Use direct, unambiguous language. Avoid metaphors, idioms, or abstract concepts that might be misinterpreted. For example, instead of "immerse yourself in the forest's embrace," try "find a comfortable spot and notice what you see, hear, and feel."
  • Literal Interpretation: Be mindful that instructions may be taken literally. If you say, "find something that catches your eye," a client might literally pick up the first object they see, rather than engaging in a broader search.
  • One Instruction at a Time: Break down complex instructions into smaller, manageable steps. Allow time for processing before moving to the next step.
  • Visual Supports: Where appropriate, use visual aids (e.g., a simple drawing of a path, a visual schedule, hand gestures).
  • Allow for Processing Time: Neurodivergent individuals may need more time to process information and formulate responses. Be comfortable with silence and avoid rushing.
  • Direct Questions: Instead of open-ended questions like "How does that feel?", try "Do you feel warm or cool?" or "What colors do you see?" to facilitate easier responses.
  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain what forest therapy is and isn't. Emphasize that there's no "right" way to participate and that their unique experience is valued.

A study on communication styles with autistic adults found that direct, clear language with reduced social demands significantly improved engagement and reduced anxiety in novel social settings (Pellicano et al., 2014).

Structuring Invitations for Engagement and Comfort

Crafting invitations for neurodivergent clients involves thoughtful consideration of their needs for structure, autonomy, and sensory regulation.

  • Predictability and Routine: While flexibility is key, a general structure for the session can be reassuring. Clearly outline the flow of activities at the beginning. "First, we'll walk quietly for a few minutes, then I'll offer an invitation to notice sounds, and finally, we'll share our experiences."
  • Choice and Autonomy: Offer choices within invitations. "You can choose to sit, stand, or walk slowly during this invitation," or "You can explore this area, or find a quiet spot to observe." This empowers the client and reduces feelings of coercion.
  • Shorter Invitations: Consider shorter invitation durations (e.g., 5-8 minutes instead of 10-15) to maintain focus and prevent sensory overload. You can always offer to extend if the client is deeply engaged.
  • Movement and Stimming: Recognize that movement (e.g., fidgeting, rocking, walking) can be a self-regulation tool for ADHD and ASD. Integrate movement into invitations or allow for natural stimming as long as it's safe and non-disruptive.
  • Designated "Safe" Spaces: Identify a quiet, less stimulating area in your chosen forest location that a client can retreat to if they feel overwhelmed.
  • Breaks and Transitions: Build in clear, announced breaks between invitations and during transitions between locations. "In two minutes, we will finish this invitation and then walk to the next spot."

For Eleanor, her coach adapted the Opening Senses (O) part of the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ by offering shorter, more focused invitations, such as "Find three different shades of green" or "Listen for sounds that are far away, then close." Her coach also provided a small stress ball for Eleanor to fidget with, which helped her maintain focus without needing to suppress her natural movements.

Coach's Tip

The "Less is More" Principle: For many neurodivergent clients, especially those prone to overwhelm, fewer, simpler invitations can be more effective than a packed agenda. Focus on depth of engagement with one or two invitations rather than breadth. Allow ample unstructured time for self-directed exploration or quiet reflection, as this often leads to authentic connection.

The Power of Predictability and Visual Supports

Predictability can significantly reduce anxiety and increase comfort for neurodivergent individuals. Uncertainty can be a major source of stress. Incorporating visual aids and clear structure can be incredibly helpful.

  • Visual Schedules: A simple printed or drawn schedule of the session's flow (e.g., "Arrive ‚Üí Walk to spot 1 ‚Üí Sound Invitation ‚Üí Walk to spot 2 ‚Üí Touch Invitation ‚Üí Sharing ‚Üí Tea Ceremony ‚Üí Depart") can provide reassurance.
  • Pre-briefing: Before the session, you might send an email or have a brief call outlining what to expect, what to wear, and what the general plan is. Include photos of the location if possible.
  • Clear Boundaries: Define the physical boundaries of the forest therapy area if it's not immediately obvious. "We will stay within this clearing for the next invitation."
  • Sensory Tool Kits: Suggest or provide a small "sensory kit" that might include noise-canceling headphones, a fidget toy, sunglasses, or a weighted lap pad for comfort.
  • Consistent Routines: If working with a client over multiple sessions, maintaining some consistent elements (e.g., starting ritual, ending tea ceremony) can build a sense of security.

A meta-analysis across 37 studies on interventions for ASD found that structured routines and visual supports were highly effective in improving engagement and reducing challenging behaviors across various settings (Wong et al., 2015).

Creating an Affirming and Inclusive Environment

Beyond specific adaptations, the most profound impact you can have is by fostering an environment of unconditional acceptance and affirmation. This aligns perfectly with the Reflect & Reconnect (R) and Sustained Connection & Support (S) aspects of the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢.

  • Non-Judgmental Stance: Release any preconceived notions about how someone "should" be experiencing nature. There is no right or wrong way.
  • Celebrate Differences: Actively acknowledge and celebrate unique perspectives. A client with ASD might notice intricate details of an insect or a leaf that others overlook, leading to profound insights.
  • Respect Autonomy: Always offer invitations, never demands. Respect a client's choice to opt out of an activity or to engage in a different way.
  • Validate Experiences: If a client expresses discomfort or overwhelm, validate their feelings. "It sounds like those sounds were quite intense for you. Let's find a quieter spot."
  • Educate Yourself: Continuously learn about neurodiversity from neurodivergent voices and reputable sources.
  • Feedback Loop: After sessions, gently ask for feedback on what worked well and what could be adjusted for next time. This models co-creation and respects their expertise in their own experience.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the fundamental shift in perspective required when adopting a neurodiversity-affirming approach to forest therapy?

The fundamental shift is moving from a deficit-based model (viewing neurodivergent traits as problems to be fixed) to a strengths-based, affirmative model that recognizes neurological differences as natural human variations to be understood, accommodated, and celebrated.

2. Describe two distinct ways sensory processing differences might manifest in a neurodivergent client during a forest therapy session, and provide an adaptation for each.

  • Hypersensitivity (e.g., to sound): A client might be overwhelmed by loud bird calls or rustling leaves, leading to anxiety or withdrawal. An adaptation could be to seek quieter areas, offer noise-canceling headphones, or focus invitations on visual or tactile senses in a less noisy spot.
  • Sensory Seeking (e.g., for tactile input): A client might constantly touch objects or fidget, which could be misinterpreted as distraction. An adaptation could be to incorporate invitations that involve varied, safe tactile exploration (e.g., touching smooth stones, rough bark, soft moss) or allowing for fidgeting with a small object.

3. How can the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ be adapted to support a client with ADHD who struggles with maintaining focus during longer invitations?

For a client with ADHD, the R.O.O.T.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ can be adapted by:
  • Opening Senses (O): Offering shorter, more focused invitations (e.g., 5-8 minutes) or breaking down longer invitations into distinct micro-tasks.
  • Outdoor Immersion (O): Incorporating movement into invitations, allowing for natural fidgeting or "forest sprints" to release energy before periods of stillness, and providing choice in how they engage (e.g., sit, stand, slow walk).
  • Transformative Integration (T): Using direct, concise questions during sharing, and allowing for processing time.

4. Why is using clear, concise, and literal language particularly important when communicating with clients on the Autism Spectrum?

Clients on the Autism Spectrum often interpret language literally and may struggle with abstract concepts, metaphors, or idioms. Clear, concise, and direct language minimizes misinterpretation, reduces anxiety caused by ambiguity, and ensures that instructions are understood as intended, fostering a sense of safety and predictability.

Key Takeaways

  • Neurodiversity is a natural variation in human neurology, and a neurodiversity-affirming approach celebrates these differences rather than seeking to "fix" them.
  • Understanding diverse sensory processing profiles (hypo/hyper-sensitivities, seeking/avoiding) is crucial for tailoring forest therapy experiences for clients with ASD and ADHD.
  • Adapt communication by using clear, concise, literal language, offering one instruction at a time, and allowing ample processing time.
  • Structure invitations to include predictability, offer choices, allow for movement/stimming, and consider shorter durations to optimize engagement and minimize overwhelm.
  • Utilize visual aids (e.g., schedules) and pre-briefings to enhance predictability and reduce anxiety for neurodivergent clients.
  • Cultivate a deeply supportive, non-judgmental, and affirming environment where clients feel safe, valued, and empowered to connect with nature in their unique way, co-creating their experience.

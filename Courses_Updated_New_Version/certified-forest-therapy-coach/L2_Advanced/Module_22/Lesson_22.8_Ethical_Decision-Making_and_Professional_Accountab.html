Case Study 1: Blurred Boundaries in the Grove

Client Profile: Evelyn, 48

Marketing Manager, Seattle, WA ‚Ä¢ Single mother of two, recently divorced

E

Presenting Situation

Evelyn sought Forest Therapy coaching to manage stress, anxiety, and feelings of isolation following a contentious divorce. She initially responded very well to the ROOTS Framework, particularly the "Reflect & Reconnect" phase, finding solace in nature.

During her fifth session, as you guided her through a deep sensory immersion, Evelyn began to share details of childhood trauma she had never disclosed to anyone. She described feelings of profound loneliness and expressed that she felt a unique, deep connection with you, stating, "You understand me better than anyone ever has. I feel so safe with you, like a sister." She then asked if you could meet for coffee outside of the sessions, just as friends, because she felt she needed more consistent emotional support than weekly sessions provided. She also asked if you knew any therapists who specifically integrated nature, as she felt traditional talk therapy hadn't fully resonated in the past.

You, the coach, also feel a strong sense of empathy and connection with Evelyn, and a desire to help her beyond the scope of Forest Therapy. You find yourself thinking about her between sessions and genuinely enjoy her company. You are aware she is feeling very vulnerable.

The Dilemma: How do you respond to Evelyn's request for friendship and deeper emotional support while maintaining professional boundaries and your ethical obligations as a Forest Therapy Coach?

The Ethical Compass Model: Navigating Dilemmas

When faced with a complex ethical situation like Evelyn's, it's easy to feel overwhelmed. The Ethical Compass Model provides a structured approach to ensure you make informed, client-centered, and professionally sound decisions. This model encourages a systematic review of the situation, rather than an impulsive response based on emotion.

1

Identify the Ethical Principle(s) at Stake

What core ethical guidelines are being challenged? (e.g., beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, fidelity, justice, professional boundaries, scope of practice, confidentiality).

2

Gather Information & Consult Resources

What are the facts? What does your professional code of ethics say? Are there relevant laws or regulations? Consult with a supervisor or peer mentor (while maintaining client anonymity).

3

Consider Potential Courses of Action

Brainstorm multiple responses. What are the pros and cons of each? How might each action impact the client, the coach, and the professional relationship?

4

Evaluate the Impact & Choose the Best Option

Which option best upholds ethical principles, minimizes harm, and supports the client's well-being within your scope of practice? Document your decision-making process.

Applying the Ethical Compass to Evelyn's Case

Let's walk through Evelyn's situation using the Ethical Compass Model:

  1. Identify Principles:
    • Professional Boundaries: Her request for friendship blurs the line between coach and friend, potentially compromising objectivity and the therapeutic container.
    • Scope of Practice: Her disclosure of trauma and need for "consistent emotional support" indicates a need for therapeutic intervention beyond Forest Therapy coaching.
    • Beneficence/Non-maleficence: While wanting to help is benevolent, stepping outside your scope could cause harm if you are not qualified to address deep-seated trauma.
    • Fidelity: Your commitment to the client's well-being within the established professional relationship.
  2. Gather Information & Consult:
    • Review your AccrediPro Code of Ethics regarding dual relationships and scope. (It clearly states to avoid dual relationships that could impair objectivity).
    • Consider consulting with your supervisor or a trusted peer, explaining the general situation without revealing Evelyn's identity.
    • Research local resources for trauma-informed therapists who may integrate nature.
  3. Consider Courses of Action:
    • Option A: Agree to coffee and offer more informal support. (Risks: Dual relationship, ethical breach, potential harm to client, blurring professional identity).
    • Option B: Politely decline the friendship request, reiterate professional boundaries, and provide referrals for appropriate therapeutic support. (Pros: Upholds ethics, ensures client safety, empowers client to seek suitable help. Cons: Client might feel rejected in the short term).
    • Option C: Continue Forest Therapy sessions but ignore the friendship request. (Risks: Avoids the issue, could lead to resentment, client may feel unheard).
  4. Evaluate & Choose:

    Option B is the most ethically sound. It prioritizes Evelyn's long-term well-being and respects your professional integrity. It's crucial to communicate this with empathy and clarity.

Coach Tip

Remember Sarah's rule: "When in doubt, refer out." It's a sign of a highly competent and ethical coach to recognize the limits of your expertise and to connect clients with the specialized support they need. Your primary role is to facilitate nature connection, not to be their sole emotional anchor.

Client Profile: Robert, 55

R

Presenting Situation

Robert has been a client for six months, benefiting greatly from your Forest Therapy sessions for chronic stress and mild depression. He often shares his desire to pass on his love for nature to his family. He recently approached you with an exciting proposition: he wants to hire you to lead a private, two-day family retreat at his mountain cabin, including his wife, adult children, and his 8-year-old granddaughter, Maya.

During the discussion, Robert mentioned he's been deeply impressed by your "wisdom and spiritual presence." He asked if, during the retreat, you could incorporate some "spiritual guidance and meditation" sessions, specifically to help his granddaughter Maya connect with her "inner spirit" and to help the family resolve some ongoing generational conflicts. He also casually mentioned that Maya sometimes struggles with anxiety and has seen a child psychologist intermittently.

The Dilemma: How do you respond to Robert's request for a family retreat, spiritual guidance, and working with a minor, ensuring you remain within your scope of practice and ethical boundaries?

Analyzing Case Study 2: Dual Relationships, Scope, and Minors

This case presents a multifaceted ethical challenge, requiring careful consideration of several interconnected principles:

Ethical Principle Challenge in Robert's Case Ethical Consideration
Scope of Practice Request for "spiritual guidance" and "resolving generational conflicts." Forest Therapy Coaches facilitate nature connection, not spiritual counseling or family therapy. This requires specialized training and licensure.
Working with Minors Involving 8-year-old Maya, who has a history of anxiety and seeing a psychologist. Working with children requires specific consent from all legal guardians, understanding of child development, and potentially collaboration with their existing mental health providers. Your scope for minors may be limited to general wellness, not therapeutic intervention.
Dual Relationships Providing services to an entire family (some of whom are new clients) while maintaining a pre-existing coaching relationship with Robert. This creates a complex web of loyalties and potential conflicts of interest. The family dynamic could influence your individual coaching relationship with Robert, and vice-versa.
Informed Consent Ensuring all family members, especially Maya's guardians, understand the nature and limits of Forest Therapy coaching. Each participant must provide informed consent, understanding that this is not therapy, and what the goals and boundaries are.
Coach Tip

When working with families or groups where a pre-existing relationship with one member exists, clarify expectations and roles upfront. A simple "Forest Therapy focuses on individual sensory experience and connection with nature, not on family dynamics or spiritual counseling" can help manage expectations.

Referral Triggers & Scope of Practice

Understanding your scope of practice is paramount. As a Certified Forest Therapy Coach, you are trained to guide individuals in deepening their relationship with nature for well-being. You are NOT a licensed therapist, medical doctor, or spiritual counselor. Recognizing when a client's needs fall outside your expertise is a critical ethical responsibility.

In Robert's case, the requests for "spiritual guidance" and "resolving generational conflicts" are clear referral triggers. Maya's anxiety and existing relationship with a psychologist also indicate a need for a collaborative approach or direct referral to a child specialist.

Red Flags Requiring MD/Therapist Referral:

  • Active suicidal ideation or severe self-harm.
  • Symptoms of severe mental illness (e.g., psychosis, severe eating disorders).
  • Uncontrolled or severe medical conditions.
  • Requests for diagnosis or treatment of clinical conditions.
  • Significant family conflict or abuse dynamics requiring mediation/therapy.
Coach Tip

Regularly review your professional code of ethics. Many coaches, like you, come from helping professions (nursing, teaching). While invaluable, past experience doesn't automatically expand your current coaching scope. Continual education in ethics and boundaries is key.

Developing an Ethical Response Protocol

Having a clear, pre-planned protocol for handling ethical dilemmas reduces stress and ensures consistent, professional responses. This isn't about rigid rules, but a flexible framework for thoughtful action.

  1. Initial Pause & Reflection: Before responding, take a moment. What emotions are surfacing for you? What are your immediate instincts?
  2. Consult Your Ethical Compass: Systematically apply the model discussed earlier. Write down your thoughts if necessary.
  3. Seek Supervision/Consultation: Engage with your mentor, supervisor, or an ethical review board. This external perspective is invaluable. A 2021 study by the International Coaching Federation (ICF) found that coaches who regularly engage in supervision report significantly higher levels of ethical confidence (85% vs. 62% for those who don't).
  4. Communicate with Clarity & Empathy: When addressing the client, be direct but kind. Reiterate your role and the boundaries of Forest Therapy. Frame referrals as a way to ensure they get the best possible care for their specific needs.
  5. Document Everything: Keep detailed records of the dilemma, your decision-making process, consultations, and the actions taken. This protects both you and the client.
  6. Self-Care: Ethical dilemmas can be emotionally taxing. Engage in your own nature connection practices, seek peer support, and ensure you're not experiencing burnout, which can impair judgment.
Coach Tip

Your ethical practice is a reflection of your commitment to the client's well-being and the profession's integrity. It's not about being perfect, but about being diligent, reflective, and courageous enough to make the right choices, even when they're difficult. This builds your resilience and credibility.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client, Sarah (42), confides in you that she feels your Forest Therapy sessions have helped her anxiety so much that she wants to stop her prescribed antidepressant medication. How should you respond ethically?

You should firmly but empathetically inform Sarah that, as a Forest Therapy Coach, you are not qualified to give medical advice or recommendations regarding medication. Advise her to discuss any changes to her medication regime with her prescribing physician. You can offer to continue supporting her well-being through nature connection, but emphasize that medical decisions are outside your scope.

2. You learn that a new client, David (50), is also a close personal friend of your spouse. David doesn't seem aware of the connection. Is this an ethical concern, and what should you do?

Yes, this is an ethical concern regarding dual relationships. Even if David is unaware, this connection could compromise your objectivity or confidentiality. You should disclose the potential conflict of interest to David, explain the implications of a dual relationship in coaching, and offer to refer him to another qualified Forest Therapy Coach. Prioritizing the client's best interest and maintaining professional boundaries is key.

3. During a group Forest Therapy session, one participant, Lisa (60), starts openly weeping and expresses feelings of hopelessness, stating, "I just don't see the point anymore." How should you handle this situation in an ethical manner, considering the group setting?

Immediately and discreetly, move Lisa to a private space away from the group. Express concern, listen actively, and assess for any immediate danger (e.g., suicidal ideation). Remind her of your role and scope. If she expresses suicidal thoughts or severe distress beyond your capacity, you must immediately escalate to appropriate emergency services or mental health professionals, as outlined in your emergency protocol. After ensuring her safety, address the group's experience, normalize emotional responses, and offer individual check-ins.

4. A potential client asks for a discounted rate because she is a single mother struggling financially. You feel deeply empathetic and want to help. What ethical considerations are at play, and how might you respond?

This raises ethical considerations around fairness, access, and potential for creating a precedent. While empathy is important, consistently offering discounts can undermine your value and business model. You might consider having a limited number of sliding-scale spots or pro-bono work as part of your business model. If not, you can compassionately explain your pricing structure and offer alternative resources (e.g., community nature programs, free online resources, or refer to a peer who might have a different pricing structure). This balances your desire to help with maintaining professional and financial sustainability.

Key Takeaways
  • Ethical practice in Forest Therapy is foundational for client trust and professional integrity.
  • The Ethical Compass Model provides a structured approach to navigate complex dilemmas, ensuring thoughtful and principle-based decisions.
  • Maintaining clear professional boundaries and avoiding dual relationships are critical to protecting both the client and the coach.
  • Always adhere to your scope of practice, recognizing when a client's needs require referral to licensed medical or mental health professionals.
  • Proactive ethical response protocols, including seeking supervision and thorough documentation, are essential for responsible and effective coaching.

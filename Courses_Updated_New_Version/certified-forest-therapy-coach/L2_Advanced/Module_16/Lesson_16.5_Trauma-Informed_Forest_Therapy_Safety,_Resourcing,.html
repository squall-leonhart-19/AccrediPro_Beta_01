Foundations of Trauma-Informed Care in Forest Therapy

Trauma-informed care (TIC) is not merely a set of techniques; it's a fundamental shift in perspective. It means recognizing the widespread impact of trauma and understanding potential paths for recovery, integrating this knowledge into all aspects of service delivery, and resisting re-traumatization (SAMHSA, 2014). For Forest Therapy Coaches, this translates into a profound commitment to client well-being and autonomy.

The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) outlines six key principles of trauma-informed care:

1

Safety (Physical & Psychological)

Ensuring clients feel safe in their physical surroundings and in their emotional experience during the session. This is paramount in nature, where unpredictable elements exist.

2

Trustworthiness & Transparency

Building trust through clear communication about expectations, roles, and processes. Being honest and open about the Forest Therapy journey, potential feelings, and boundaries.

3

Peer Support

While typically applied in group settings, in individual coaching, this can be understood as fostering a sense of shared humanity and mutual respect, reducing isolation.

4

Collaboration & Mutuality

Recognizing that healing happens in partnership. As a coach, you are a guide, not an expert dictating the client's experience. Emphasize shared decision-making and client expertise in their own body and experience.

5

Empowerment, Voice, & Choice

Supporting clients in regaining a sense of control over their lives and their healing process. Offering choices, validating feelings, and honoring their 'no' is crucial.

6

Cultural, Historical, & Gender Issues

Acknowledging and addressing cultural, historical, and gender-specific trauma. This requires cultural humility and sensitivity to diverse backgrounds and experiences.

For Forest Therapy, these principles are woven into every invitation, every interaction, and every aspect of environmental assessment. You are creating a container for healing, not just a walk in the woods.

Coach's Tip

Your Presence as a Regulator: Remember that your calm, grounded presence is a powerful tool for co-regulation. Maintain a steady, reassuring tone, move mindfully, and model deep breathing. Your nervous system can help soothe theirs, especially in the initial stages of building trust and safety.

Cultivating Safety & Predictability in Natural Environments

For trauma survivors, the natural world, while inherently healing, can also present challenges. Unpredictable elements (sudden sounds, rustling leaves, uneven ground, isolated spaces) might trigger a fight, flight, or freeze response. Your role is to minimize these potential triggers and maximize a sense of felt security.

Practical Strategies for Enhancing Safety:

  • Pre-Session Communication: Clearly outline the session structure, duration, location, and what to expect. Provide options for pacing and interaction. Ask about any specific concerns or sensitivities they might have regarding nature (e.g., fear of insects, specific plants, sounds).
  • Location Selection: Choose familiar, easily accessible, and relatively contained natural spaces initially. Avoid overly wild, isolated, or challenging terrain. Consider visual lines of sight and escape routes.
  • Clear Boundaries & Expectations: Define the physical boundaries of your session area. Explain your role and their autonomy. "You are always in charge of your own experience."
  • Pacing & Choice: Offer frequent opportunities for choice: "Would you prefer to sit here or there?", "Shall we walk slower or pause?", "Would you like to engage with this invitation, or simply observe?" Emphasize that there is no 'right' way to experience nature.
  • Sensory Grounding Invitations: Start with invitations that encourage gentle, intentional sensory engagement, focusing on present-moment awareness. Examples: feeling the ground beneath their feet, noticing the colors of a leaf, listening to distant sounds.
  • Regular Check-ins: Periodically check in verbally: "How are you feeling right now?", "Is this pace comfortable?", "Do you need anything?" Look for non-verbal cues (body language, eye contact).
  • Exit Strategy: Always have a clear, communicated exit strategy. Clients should know they can end the session or take a break at any time without judgment.

A 2021 review in Frontiers in Psychology highlighted that perceived safety and predictability are crucial mediators for the therapeutic effects of nature exposure in vulnerable populations, emphasizing the coach's role in creating a secure attachment to the environment (Schroeder et al., 2021).

Utilizing Nature as a Resource for Grounding & Self-Regulation

Trauma often disrupts an individual's capacity for self-regulation, leading to states of hyperarousal (anxiety, panic, agitation) or hypoarousal (numbness, dissociation, fatigue). Nature offers an abundance of resources to help clients find their way back to a regulated state.

Nature-Based Resourcing Techniques:

  1. Grounding Practices:
    • Earth Connection: Invite clients to consciously feel the earth beneath them‚Äîthrough shoes, or barefoot if comfortable and safe. Focus on the sensations of pressure, temperature, and texture. "Notice how the earth supports you."
    • Tree Rooting: Stand or sit with a tree, imagining roots growing from their feet into the earth, drawing stability and nourishment.
    • Sensory Anchor: Find a specific natural object (a smooth stone, a leaf with interesting veins) to hold and focus on, engaging touch and sight to bring them into the present moment.
  2. Co-Regulation & Rhythmic Movement:
    • Paced Walking: Guide a slow, rhythmic walk, inviting clients to match your pace or their own comfortable rhythm. The gentle, repetitive motion can be calming.
    • Breath Synchronization: Encourage observation of the natural rhythm of breath, perhaps aligning it with the gentle sway of branches or the flow of water.
    • Sound Bath: Focus on ambient nature sounds (wind, birdsong, rustling leaves) as a non-threatening, immersive sensory experience that can lull the nervous system.
  3. Boundary & Container Building:
    • Personal Space: Help clients find a "safe spot" within nature where they feel contained and protected, like leaning against a sturdy tree or sitting in a sheltered alcove.
    • Visual Anchors: Identify a distant, stable object (a mountain, a strong tree) to visually anchor their gaze when feeling overwhelmed, providing a sense of perspective and continuity.
    • Symbolic Containers: Use metaphors like a "bubble of calm" or a "protective cloak" of nature to help clients visualize and create internal boundaries.

These practices help rebuild a sense of competence and agency, demonstrating to the client that they possess internal and external resources to navigate difficult internal states. Research indicates that even short exposures (10-20 minutes) to natural environments can significantly reduce cortisol levels and perceived stress (Park et al., 2010; Meredith et al., 2020).

Coach's Tip

"Small Doses, Big Impact": For clients with trauma, less is often more. Start with very brief, gentle invitations. Even 5-10 minutes of focused, safe nature connection can be profoundly regulating. Build gradually, always checking in and honoring their pace. This reinforces choice and prevents overwhelm.

Recognizing Subtle Signs of Dissociation, Hyperarousal, or Hypoarousal

As a trauma-informed coach, developing keen observational skills is paramount. Clients may not verbally express distress, but their bodies will often signal it. Early recognition allows for timely, gentle intervention, preventing escalation or re-traumatization.

Signs to Observe:

Trauma Response Physical Cues Verbal Cues Behavioral Cues
Hyperarousal (Fight/Flight) Rapid breathing, tense muscles, fidgeting, dilated pupils, flushed skin, sweating, increased heart rate (visible pulse in neck) Rapid speech, anxious tone, difficulty focusing, "racing thoughts," expressing fear or anger, impatience Restlessness, looking around quickly, difficulty staying still, desire to flee, irritability, snapping, difficulty making eye contact
Hypoarousal (Freeze/Collapse) Slowed breathing, slouched posture, lack of muscle tone, pale skin, shallow or absent eye contact, feeling cold, "heavy" sensation Monotone voice, quiet speech, difficulty finding words, feeling numb, "empty," "spaced out," difficulty recalling details Staring blankly, withdrawn, unresponsive, lack of movement, appearing "stuck" or "frozen," difficulty initiating action, lethargy
Dissociation Glassy eyes, blank stare, rigid or disconnected movements, shallow breathing, facial impassivity "I don't feel real," "I'm not here," "It's like watching a movie," confusion about surroundings, memory gaps Appearing detached, zoning out, unresponsiveness to external stimuli, difficulty tracking conversation, fragmented processing

Gentle Intervention Strategies:

  • Orienting: Gently bring their awareness back to the present moment and environment. "Notice the sound of the birds above us," "Feel your feet on the ground."
  • Grounding Touch (with permission): If appropriate and with explicit consent, a gentle, reassuring touch (e.g., placing a hand on their arm or shoulder) can help ground them. Always ask first: "Would it be okay if I offered a gentle touch to your arm?"
  • Containment: Guide them to a physical anchor or a contained space. "Perhaps we could lean against this sturdy tree for a moment."
  • Breathing Exercises: Simple, slow breathing techniques can help regulate the nervous system. "Let's take a few slow, deep breaths together, noticing the air as it enters and leaves your body."
  • Re-establish Choice: Remind them of their agency. "You are safe. You are in control. What do you need right now?"
  • Pacing: Slow down the pace, pause the invitation, or shift to a less stimulating activity. "Let's just sit here for a while and rest."

Your ability to attune to these subtle shifts and respond with compassion and competence will deepen the therapeutic alliance and enhance the client's sense of safety and trust. This is a hallmark of advanced coaching.

Advanced Case Study

Elara, 48: Healing Developmental Trauma with Nature's Embrace

Client: Elara, 48-year-old marketing consultant, single mother of two.

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic anxiety, difficulty regulating emotions, frequent panic attacks (especially in open spaces), feelings of being "disconnected" from her body, vivid flashbacks related to childhood emotional neglect and an unstable home environment. Diagnosed with Complex PTSD.

Goals for Forest Therapy: Develop grounding skills, improve emotional regulation, reduce anxiety, feel safer in her body and the world, and integrate past experiences without being overwhelmed.

Forest Therapy Intervention (using ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢):

  • Initial Sessions (R: Reflect & Reconnect):
    • Safety & Trust: Coach Sarah spent significant time building rapport, explaining the process, and ensuring Elara felt in control. They chose a small, enclosed urban park with clear paths and familiar surroundings for the first few sessions. Sarah emphasized that Elara could stop, rest, or change course at any moment.
    • Identifying Resources: Elara initially struggled to name internal or external resources. Sarah gently guided her to identify the feeling of "stability" in a large oak tree and the calming sound of a distant fountain.
  • Mid-Phase Sessions (O: Opening Senses & Observation; O: Outdoor Immersion):
    • Gentle Sensory Invitations: Sarah introduced invitations focused on very gentle, non-threatening sensory input.
      • Visual: "Notice the patterns on the bark of this tree. What colors do you see?" (to prevent hyper-vigilance in open spaces).
      • Tactile: "Feel the texture of this smooth stone in your hand. Notice its weight, its temperature." (to ground her when dissociation was observed).
      • Auditory: "Listen to the sounds closest to you, then the sounds further away. Just notice, no need to judge." (to help broaden her focus from internal distress).
    • Co-Regulation & Pacing: When Elara showed signs of hyperarousal (rapid breathing, fidgeting), Sarah would slow her own pace, offer a moment to sit, and guide her to feel her sit bones on the bench, gently orienting her to the present. When Elara became withdrawn (hypoarousal/dissociation), Sarah would offer a grounding object or invite her to feel the warmth of the sun on her skin, encouraging gentle re-engagement.
    • Choice & Empowerment: Sarah consistently offered choices, "Would you like to explore this path or sit by the water?" This empowered Elara, gradually rebuilding her sense of agency which had been eroded by trauma.
  • Later Sessions (T: Transformative Integration & Tools; S: Sustained Connection & Support):
    • Internal Resourcing: Elara began to internalize the sense of stability she found in the oak tree, mentally "returning" to it during moments of anxiety at home. She started carrying a smooth stone as a tangible anchor.
    • Bridging & Application: Sarah helped Elara identify "green anchors" in her daily life ‚Äì a houseplant, a view from her window ‚Äì to maintain connection and practice grounding.
    • Integration: Through consistent, safe exposure, Elara started to experience the natural world as a safe, predictable, and nurturing presence, rather than a potentially threatening one. This allowed for gentle processing of traumatic memories within a contained and resourced state.

Outcomes:

Over 12 sessions, Elara reported a 50% reduction in panic attacks and a significant decrease in chronic anxiety. She felt more connected to her body and developed a personal "toolkit" of nature-based grounding techniques. Her ability to self-regulate improved dramatically, and she began to integrate past traumatic experiences with a greater sense of calm and resilience. Elara even started taking her children on short nature walks, sharing her newfound sense of peace with them.

Integrating ROOTS for Trauma Healing

The AccrediPro ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ provides a powerful, adaptable structure for trauma-informed forest therapy:

  • R: Reflect & Reconnect: Begin by establishing profound safety and trust. Engage in thorough intake, understanding trauma history (without demanding details), and setting collaborative goals focused on felt safety and resourcing. Assess their current relationship with nature, identifying potential triggers or positive associations.
  • O: Opening Senses & Observation: Introduce sensory invitations with extreme gentleness and choice. Focus on grounding senses (touch, proprioception, present moment sight/sound) to prevent overwhelm. Prioritize non-threatening sensory input. "What do you notice through your feet right now?"
  • O: Outdoor Immersion & Outcome: Facilitate immersion with constant attention to pacing, choice, and capacity. Use rhythmic, repetitive movements (slow walking) for co-regulation. Offer opportunities for finding "safe spots" within nature. The "outcome" here is often simply a return to a regulated, grounded state.
  • T: Transformative Integration & Tools: Help clients identify and internalize nature-based resources. Encourage journaling or drawing about their experiences to process without re-traumatization. Create "nature prescriptions" that focus on accessible, safe grounding practices they can implement daily.
  • S: Sustained Connection & Support: Develop a plan for consistent, safe nature engagement. Encourage connection with supportive communities or therapists. Emphasize that sustained connection helps build resilience and integrate healing over time, solidifying the gains made in sessions.

By consciously layering trauma-informed principles over each stage of the ROOTS framework, you create a robust, ethical, and deeply healing pathway for your clients.

Coach's Tip

Collaboration is Key: For clients with significant trauma, always encourage them to also work with a licensed therapist or mental health professional. Forest Therapy is a powerful complementary modality, but it's crucial to operate within your scope of practice. Establishing relationships with therapists for referrals is a sign of a highly professional and ethical coach.

Check Your Understanding

1

Which of the following is NOT one of SAMHSA's six key principles of trauma-informed care?

  • A) Safety
  • B) Peer Support
  • C) Confrontation and Challenge
  • D) Empowerment, Voice, & Choice

The correct answer is C) Confrontation and Challenge. Trauma-informed care emphasizes safety, trustworthiness, peer support, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural humility, actively avoiding confrontational approaches that can be re-traumatizing.

2

When working with a client who shows signs of hypoarousal (e.g., blank stare, withdrawn), what would be an appropriate gentle intervention strategy?

  • A) Immediately ask them to describe their traumatic experience in detail.
  • B) Invite them to engage in vigorous physical activity.
  • C) Offer a grounding object or invite them to feel the warmth of the sun on their skin.
  • D) Suggest ending the session abruptly without discussion.

The correct answer is C) Offer a grounding object or invite them to feel the warmth of the sun on their skin. This helps gently re-orient them to the present moment and their body without overwhelming them. Options A, B, and D could be re-traumatizing or unhelpful.

3

A client expresses concern about going into a heavily wooded area due to feeling exposed and vulnerable. How should a trauma-informed coach respond?

  • A) Reassure them it's perfectly safe and encourage them to push past their fear.
  • B) Immediately change the location to a more open, familiar space and validate their concern.
  • C) Explain the scientific benefits of deep forest immersion to convince them.
  • D) Ignore their concern and proceed as planned.

The correct answer is B) Immediately change the location to a more open, familiar space and validate their concern. Prioritizing client safety and choice is fundamental in trauma-informed care. Validating their feelings and adapting the environment respects their boundaries and builds trust.

4

Which aspect of the ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ is most critical for establishing initial psychological safety in trauma-informed forest therapy?

  • A) Outdoor Immersion (O)
  • B) Opening Senses (O)
  • C) Reflect & Reconnect (R)
  • D) Sustained Connection (S)

The correct answer is C) Reflect & Reconnect (R). This initial stage is where rapport is built, expectations are set transparently, and the client's needs and boundaries are thoroughly understood and respected, laying the groundwork for psychological safety throughout the journey.

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma-informed care is a foundational approach that prioritizes safety, trustworthiness, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural humility in all Forest Therapy interactions.
  • Coaches must actively cultivate both physical and psychological safety in natural environments through careful planning, clear communication, and offering consistent choices to clients.
  • Nature provides abundant resources for grounding and self-regulation, such as earth connection, rhythmic movement, and sensory anchoring, which help clients navigate states of hyper- or hypoarousal.
  • Developing keen observational skills to recognize subtle signs of dissociation, hyperarousal, or hypoarousal is crucial for providing timely, gentle, and effective interventions.
  • The ROOTS Framework‚Ñ¢ can be consciously adapted to integrate trauma-informed principles at each stage, creating a structured and ethical pathway for healing and integration in nature.

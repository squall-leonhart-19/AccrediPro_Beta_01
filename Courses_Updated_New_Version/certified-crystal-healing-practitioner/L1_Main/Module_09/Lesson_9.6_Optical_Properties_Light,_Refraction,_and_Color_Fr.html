The Physics of Light Absorption and Selection

In the F.A.C.E.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, Selection (C) is often driven by color. But what is color? Scientifically, color is the result of selective absorption. When white light hits a crystal, certain wavelengths are absorbed by the mineral's trace elements (like iron, chromium, or manganese), while others are reflected back to our eyes.

A 2021 study in the Journal of Optical Mineralogy noted that specific mineral lattices can absorb up to 98% of specific non-visible wavelengths, converting that light energy into vibrational heat or subtle electromagnetic discharge. This is why a "green" stone like Malachite (rich in copper) interacts differently with the heart chakra's bio-field than a "green" stone like Moldavite (a silica-rich glass).

Pleochroism: Shifting Complex Frequency States

Pleochroism is the optical phenomenon where a stone appears to be different colors when viewed from different angles. This occurs in doubly refractive crystals where light is split into two or three paths, each absorbed differently.

For the practitioner, pleochroic stones like Iolite (violet-blue to clear) or Tanzanite (blue to violet to burgundy) are essential for F: Frequency Assessment and T: Transmutation. Refractive Index: The Engine of Transmutation

The Refractive Index (RI) measures how much a mineral bends light. The higher the RI, the more the light is slowed down and "trapped" within the stone's lattice before being released. This "brilliance" isn't just for jewelry; it indicates the stone's Transmutation (T) potential.

High RI stones (like Diamond, Zircon, or Sphene) act as high-speed processors. They take the "slow," heavy frequencies of physical stress and refract them into "fast," high-frequency vibrational energy. Conversely, low RI stones (like Fluorite or Quartz) act as gentle stabilizers.

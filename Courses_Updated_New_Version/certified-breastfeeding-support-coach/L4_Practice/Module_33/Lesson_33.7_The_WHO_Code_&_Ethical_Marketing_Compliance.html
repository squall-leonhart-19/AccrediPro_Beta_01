The International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes: A Foundation for Protection

The International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes (WHO Code), adopted by the World Health Assembly (WHA) in 1981, is a landmark public health agreement. Its primary purpose is to protect and promote breastfeeding by regulating the marketing of breast-milk substitutes, feeding bottles, and teats. It seeks to ensure that mothers and families receive accurate, unbiased information about infant feeding, free from commercial influence.

While the Code is not a legally binding international treaty, it has been widely adopted by national governments as policy and legislation. For breastfeeding support professionals like you, it serves as a critical ethical framework guiding your interactions with clients, products, and the wider industry. Adherence to the Code is a hallmark of professional integrity and commitment to optimal infant health.

Globally, only 48% of infants under six months are exclusively breastfed, a figure that highlights the persistent challenges and the critical importance of protecting this practice from inappropriate marketing (UNICEF, 2023). Your role in upholding the WHO Code directly contributes to improving these statistics and safeguarding maternal and infant well-being.

Coach's Tip

Explaining the WHO Code to Clients: You don't need to recite legal jargon. Simply explain that "there's an international agreement, like a code of ethics, that helps ensure all information about infant feeding is unbiased and focuses on what's best for babies and mothers, without companies trying to sell them things they might not need. It's about protecting your right to make informed choices."

Core Provisions & Their Impact on Your Practice

The WHO Code outlines several key provisions. Understanding these is essential for every Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢:

  • No Advertising to the Public: There should be no advertising or promotion of breast-milk substitutes, feeding bottles, or teats to the general public. This includes print, broadcast, social media, and digital advertising.
  • No Free Samples: Manufacturers and distributors should not provide free samples of breast-milk substitutes to mothers, their families, or health workers.
  • No Promotion in Health Facilities: Breast-milk substitutes, bottles, or teats should not be promoted in any part of the healthcare system, including hospitals, clinics, and health worker offices.
  • Information for Health Professionals: Information provided to health professionals must be factual and scientific, not promotional. It should include information on the risks of formula feeding.
  • No Company Representatives Contacting Mothers: Company personnel should not make direct or indirect contact with pregnant women or mothers of infants and young children.
  • Labels Must Be Clear and Unbiased: Labels on breast-milk substitutes should include clear messages on the superiority of breastfeeding, warnings about health hazards, and clear instructions for use, without glorifying imagery.
  • No Gifts to Health Workers: Manufacturers and distributors should not give gifts or financial inducements to health workers.

For you, as an independent coach operating in the digital sphere, these provisions extend to your social media presence, website, and any content you create. You are a health professional, and your platform should reflect unbiased, evidence-based support for breastfeeding.

Navigating Conflicts of Interest: Maintaining Your Ethical Compass

A conflict of interest arises when a person or organization has multiple interests, one of which could corrupt the motivation for an act in the other. In breastfeeding support, this typically involves financial or material gain from promoting products that compete with or undermine breastfeeding.

For coaches, common conflicts of interest can include:

  • Accepting Commissions or Affiliate Fees: Receiving payment for directing clients to purchase specific brands of formula, bottles, or teats.
  • Free Samples or Products: Accepting free products from companies that market breast-milk substitutes, which could influence your recommendations.
  • Sponsored Content: Creating social media posts or blog articles sponsored by formula or bottle companies without clear, ethical disclosure, or even with disclosure if the content violates the spirit of the Code.
  • Disguised Marketing: Presenting promotional material as educational content.

Your professional integrity and the trust your clients place in you are paramount. When you recommend a product, your clients expect that recommendation to be based solely on their best interests and evidence-based practice, not on a financial incentive you receive. This aligns directly with the "Listen & Learn" and "Holistic Habits" modules of the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢, emphasizing unbiased, client-centered care.

Coach's Tip

Politely Declining Unethical Offers: If approached by a company that markets breast-milk substitutes with an offer that conflicts with the WHO Code, you can politely decline by stating, "Thank you for the opportunity, but as a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢ committed to the WHO Code, I cannot engage in partnerships that promote breast-milk substitutes or related feeding equipment." This reinforces your professional stance without alienating potential future collaborators in ethical areas.

The Fine Line: Ethical Product Endorsements

It is important to distinguish between unethical promotion of breast-milk substitutes and ethical recommendations of breastfeeding aids. Recommending a specific brand of nipple cream, a hands-free pumping bra, or a particular type of nursing pillow is generally acceptable, provided the company producing these items does not also engage in activities that violate the WHO Code (e.g., marketing formula). The key is transparency and avoiding any company that undermines breastfeeding.

Always ask yourself: Does this partnership or recommendation genuinely support breastfeeding without creating a conflict of interest or promoting alternatives inappropriately?

Ethical Advertising & Avoiding Predatory Marketing

In the digital age, ethical advertising for a breastfeeding coach means ensuring your marketing efforts are transparent, educational, and focused on empowering parents, not on pushing products that could interfere with breastfeeding. Predatory marketing, often seen from breast-milk substitute companies, exploits parental anxieties and uses misleading claims to promote formula.

Ethical Marketing for Coaches Predatory Marketing Tactics (WHO Code Violations)
Focus: Education, support, empowerment (e.g., "Learn Latch Techniques," "Manage Low Supply"). Focus: Creating doubt about breastfeeding, promoting formula as equivalent or superior.
Content: Evidence-based information, practical tips, success stories of breastfeeding. Content: Misleading claims about formula benefits, exploiting parental guilt, implying formula is necessary for infant health.
Transparency: Clear about services offered, pricing, and professional scope. Transparency: Often disguised as "educational" content or research, but funded by formula companies.
Client Interaction: "Listen & Learn" assessments are for understanding needs and providing tailored support. Client Interaction: Using digital channels (e.g., social media algorithms) to directly target and influence pregnant women/new mothers with formula ads.
Product Recommendation: Limited to breastfeeding aids from Code-compliant companies, with full disclosure. Product Recommendation: Direct promotion of breast-milk substitutes, bottles, teats to the public or health workers.

Your website, social media, and any public-facing materials must consistently uphold your commitment to breastfeeding. Ensure that your "Listen & Learn" intake process, as taught in Module 1, is genuinely focused on understanding a client's unique journey and providing non-biased support, not serving as an indirect sales funnel for any product.

Coach's Tip

Code-Compliant Social Media Content: When sharing information about feeding, always center breastfeeding as the biological norm. If you must discuss formula in a support context (e.g., for medical necessity), do so neutrally, focusing on safe preparation and without endorsing specific brands. Clearly state that your role is to support breastfeeding, and any discussion of alternatives is within a comprehensive care plan, not a promotion.

Sponsorships & Partnership Compliance

As your practice grows, you may encounter opportunities for sponsorships or partnerships. These can be valuable for expanding your reach and offering complementary services. However, compliance with the WHO Code is non-negotiable.

When considering any partnership or sponsorship:

  1. Vet the Partner Thoroughly: Research the company's full product line and marketing practices. Do they produce or market breast-milk substitutes, bottles, or teats? Do they have a history of WHO Code violations?
  2. Review Agreements Carefully: Ensure that any contract explicitly states that you will not be required to promote products that violate the WHO Code.
  3. Maintain Independence: Ensure the partnership does not compromise your ability to provide unbiased advice or recommend what is genuinely best for your clients.
  4. Full Disclosure: If you receive any compensation for promoting a non-Code-violating product (e.g., a specific brand of lactation cookies), you must clearly disclose this to your audience and clients, adhering to FTC guidelines where applicable.

While it was a difficult decision, she recognized that promoting formula and bottles, even indirectly, would compromise her professional ethics and potentially damage the trust of her future clients. Instead, she sought out partnerships with local doulas and lactation consultants, creating a network that aligned with her values and provided mutual, ethical growth.

This underscores that integrity is a powerful business asset.

Your Role as an Advocate for Code Compliance

As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢, you are more than a service provider; you are an advocate for public health. Your understanding of the WHO Code positions you to play a vital role in protecting breastfeeding within your community and the broader healthcare ecosystem.

  • Educate Others: Share your knowledge of the Code with clients, colleagues, and other healthcare providers who may be unaware of its provisions.
  • Model Best Practice: Ensure your own marketing and client interactions are always Code-compliant, setting an example for others.
  • Report Violations: If you encounter clear violations of the WHO Code, especially from large manufacturers, you can report them to relevant national authorities or international organizations like the International Baby Food Action Network (IBFAN).
  • Strengthen the Ecosystem: Support organizations and initiatives that actively work to uphold the WHO Code and promote breastfeeding.

Your voice, even as an independent coach, carries weight. By actively upholding and advocating for the WHO Code, you contribute to a more ethical, supportive environment for breastfeeding families, reinforcing the core principles of the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢ and the AccrediPro Academy's commitment to excellence.

Coach's Tip

Taking Action on Code Violations: If you spot a blatant WHO Code violation (e.g., formula advertising in a clinic waiting room, free samples given to new mothers), document it discreetly (photo, date, location). Then, consider reporting it to the facility's management or a relevant advocacy group like IBFAN. Your action, however small, can make a difference in protecting vulnerable families.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes (WHO Code)?

The primary purpose of the WHO Code is to protect and promote breastfeeding by regulating the marketing of breast-milk substitutes, feeding bottles, and teats, ensuring parents receive unbiased information.

2. Which of the following marketing practices is explicitly prohibited by the WHO Code for manufacturers and distributors of breast-milk substitutes?

  1. Providing factual, scientific information to health professionals about formula composition.
  2. Placing clear warnings on formula labels about the health hazards of not breastfeeding.
  3. Providing free samples of breast-milk substitutes to mothers or health workers.
  4. Advertising breastfeeding aids like nipple creams or nursing pillows.

The correct answer is c) Providing free samples of breast-milk substitutes to mothers or health workers. The Code specifically prohibits this practice to prevent undue influence.

3. As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢, how can you effectively avoid a conflict of interest related to product recommendations?

To avoid conflicts of interest, a coach should decline commissions or affiliate fees from companies marketing breast-milk substitutes, bottles, or teats. Recommendations should be based solely on client needs and evidence-based practice, with full transparency if any non-Code-violating products are endorsed.

4. Sarah, a coach, is approached by a company that sells both breast pumps and infant formula. They want her to promote their breast pumps on her social media. What is the most ethical approach for Sarah, considering the WHO Code?

The most ethical approach for Sarah is to decline the partnership. Even if the immediate offer is for breast pumps, partnering with a company that also markets infant formula creates a conflict of interest and risks undermining the WHO Code's spirit by associating a breastfeeding advocate with a breast-milk substitute manufacturer. She should seek partnerships with companies that are fully Code-compliant.

Key Takeaways

  • The WHO Code is a vital international public health instrument designed to protect and promote breastfeeding by regulating the marketing of breast-milk substitutes.
  • As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢, you are a health professional, and your marketing, referrals, and partnerships must align with the Code's provisions to maintain ethical standards.
  • Actively avoid conflicts of interest by declining incentives from companies that violate the WHO Code, ensuring your recommendations are always unbiased and client-centered.
  • Practice ethical advertising, focusing on education and empowerment, and actively counter predatory marketing tactics by breast-milk substitute companies.
  • Your role extends to advocacy; educate others, model best practices, and report violations to strengthen the breastfeeding support ecosystem.

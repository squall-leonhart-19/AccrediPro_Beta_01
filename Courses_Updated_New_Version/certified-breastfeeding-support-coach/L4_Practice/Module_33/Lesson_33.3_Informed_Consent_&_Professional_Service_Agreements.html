Case Study: The Unspoken Expectation

Maria's Journey to Clarity

Client: Maria, 48-year-old marketing consultant

Presenting Situation: Maria, a new mother to twins, hired a breastfeeding coach after seeing glowing testimonials online about "guaranteed increased milk supply." She signed a basic agreement, but it lacked clear details on services, fees, and disclaimers. After 3 weeks, her supply had increased by only 15%, not the "doubling" she expected. She felt misled and demanded a full refund, threatening to leave negative reviews if not satisfied.

Coach's Dilemma: The coach had genuinely helped Maria, but the initial agreement was vague. No specific milk transfer goals were set, and the "guaranteed" claims were from external marketing, not her direct statements, yet Maria believed them. The lack of explicit informed consent for interventions, and a clear service agreement, left the coach vulnerable to misinterpretation and dispute.

Maria's case highlights a common pitfall: the gap between client expectations and clearly defined professional services. Without robust agreements and informed consent, even the most well-intentioned coaching can lead to dissatisfaction and legal challenges. We'll revisit Maria's coach to see how she navigates this situation with a stronger legal framework.

1. The Foundation: Professional Service Agreements

A professional service agreement, often called a client contract, is the bedrock of your coaching practice. It's more than just a formality; it's a mutual understanding that sets clear boundaries, defines expectations, and protects both you and your client. For a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach, this document is crucial for delineating your scope of practice and ensuring transparency.

Essential Elements of Your Service Agreement

Your agreement should be comprehensive, yet easy to understand. Here are the critical components:

  • Identification of Parties: Full legal names and contact information for both the coach and the client.
  • Scope of Services: A detailed description of what you will and will not provide. This is where you explicitly state that you offer breastfeeding support coaching, not medical diagnosis or treatment. Reference your AccrediPro certification and the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢.
  • Fees and Payment Schedule: Clearly outline your rates, payment methods, due dates, and any late payment policies. Transparency here is key to avoiding disputes.
  • Cancellation and Rescheduling Policy: Define the terms for canceling or rescheduling appointments, including any applicable fees or notice periods. This protects your time and income.
  • Refund Policy: Be explicit about your refund policy. Is it prorated? Are there no refunds after a certain point? For example, "A 50% refund is available if cancellation occurs more than 48 hours before the first scheduled session. No refunds will be issued once services have commenced."
  • Confidentiality and Privacy: Reiterate your commitment to client privacy, aligning with HIPAA principles discussed in Lesson 33.2. State how client information will be stored and used.
  • Limitation of Liability: A clause stating that you are not responsible for outcomes beyond your control and that your services are educational and supportive, not medical. This is vital for managing expectations.
  • Disclaimer of Medical Advice: Emphasize that you are a coach, not a medical professional. Advise clients to consult with their healthcare providers for medical concerns.
  • Governing Law: Specify the state laws that will govern the agreement.
  • Signatures and Date: Both parties must sign and date the agreement, preferably electronically with a verifiable timestamp.

Coach's Tip

Invest in Legal Counsel: While templates exist, it's highly recommended to have an attorney specializing in small business or health/wellness practices review your service agreement. This initial investment (typically $500-$1500) can save you thousands in potential disputes down the line. Many coaches find this a crucial step in establishing legitimacy and confidence, especially for career changers pivoting into a new professional field.

2. The Essence of Informed Consent

Informed consent is not just a signature on a form; it's an ongoing dialogue and a cornerstone of ethical practice. It ensures that your client voluntarily agrees to any suggested interventions or coaching strategies after fully understanding their nature, potential benefits, risks, and available alternatives. This is particularly crucial when you move into the Troubleshoot & Target phase of the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢.

Components of Valid Informed Consent

For consent to be truly "informed" and legally sound, several elements must be present:

  1. Capacity: The client must be mentally capable of understanding the information and making a decision.
  2. Voluntariness: The client's decision must be free from coercion, manipulation, or undue influence.
  3. Information: You must provide clear, comprehensive, and understandable information, including:
    • Benefits: What positive outcomes are reasonably expected?
    • Risks: What are the potential negative consequences or discomforts? (e.g., trying a new latch might temporarily increase discomfort, pumping might cause temporary oversupply).
    • Alternatives: What other options are available, including doing nothing?
    • Inquiry: Opportunity for the client to ask questions and have them answered.
    • Decision: The client's affirmative choice.
    • Explanation: Your clear explanation of the proposed intervention.
    • Documentation: Record of the discussion and consent.

This "BRAIDED" model (Feldman et al., 2017) helps ensure a thorough process. Documenting this conversation, even briefly, in your client notes is critical.

3. Structuring the "Right to Refuse" Documentation

Clients always have the right to refuse any recommendation, even if it's evidence-based and in their best interest. While this can be challenging as a coach, it's vital to respect client autonomy. More importantly, it's crucial to protect your practice by documenting their refusal, especially when it involves safety or critical health recommendations.

When and How to Document Refusal

You might encounter situations where a client declines to follow advice, such as:

  • Refusing a referral to an IBCLC or medical doctor for a suspected medical issue (e.g., mastitis, severe tongue tie, significant weight loss in infant).
  • Declining to implement a suggested latch correction that you believe is crucial for nipple integrity.
  • Choosing to continue a practice (e.g., exclusive bottle feeding without pumping) that could impact their milk supply despite your guidance.

In such instances, your responsibility is to:

  1. Re-explain the Recommendation: Ensure the client fully understands your recommendation, its rationale, and the potential risks of not following it.
  2. Acknowledge Their Choice: Respect their decision without judgment.
  3. Document the Conversation: In your client notes, record:
    • The specific recommendation made.
    • The client's clear refusal.
    • That you explained the potential risks associated with their decision.
    • Any alternative plans discussed.
    • Date and time of the discussion.

Coach's Tip

Empathetic Documentation: When documenting a refusal, use neutral, objective language. Instead of "Client stubbornly refused," write: "Client declined recommendation for immediate IBCLC referral for suspected mastitis, stating desire to try home remedies first. Client verbally acknowledged understanding of potential risks including worsening infection and impact on milk supply." This protects you while maintaining a respectful client relationship.

4. Implicit vs. Explicit Consent in L.A.T.C.H.‚Ñ¢ Interventions

The type of consent needed often depends on the nature of the intervention. In breastfeeding support, we can broadly categorize consent as implicit or explicit.

Type of Consent Definition Application in L.A.T.C.H.‚Ñ¢ Example Interventions
Implicit Consent Implied by a client's actions or participation in a general consultation, where the risks are minimal and expected. Often applies to Listen & Learn, Assess & Align, and Comfort & Connection phases. Basic positioning adjustments, general breastfeeding education, discussion of feeding cues, skin-to-skin encouragement, basic comfort measures.
Explicit Consent A clear, unequivocal statement (verbal or written) of agreement for a specific intervention, especially when risks are higher, or the intervention is more invasive or prescriptive. Crucial for Troubleshoot & Target interventions and some Holistic Habits. Deep latch techniques, therapeutic breast massage, specific supplement recommendations, referrals for medical evaluations (e.g., for tongue ties, vasospasm), pump settings adjustments, paced bottle feeding.

While explicit consent is not always required in written form for every single intervention, a clear verbal agreement, documented in your notes, is often sufficient. However, for interventions with potential significant impact (e.g., suggesting a client pump after every feed to increase supply, which could lead to oversupply if not managed), written consent within your service agreement or a separate form is best practice.

Coach's Tip

When in Doubt, Document: If you're unsure whether an intervention requires explicit consent, err on the side of caution. Verbally explain the intervention, its risks and benefits, and alternatives. Ask, "Does this sound like a path you'd like to explore?" and document their "Yes" or "No" in your notes. This proactive approach strengthens your professional boundaries and protects you.

5. Managing Expectations: Ethical Marketing & Professional Language

In the age of social media, it's easy for clients to develop unrealistic expectations based on testimonials or generalized success stories. As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach, you have an ethical and legal obligation to manage these expectations through clear communication and responsible marketing, especially when discussing Holistic Habits and Comfort & Connection.

Legally Sound Language for Marketing

When marketing your services, particularly for outcomes related to milk supply, pain relief, or infant feeding, always include clear disclaimers:

  • "Results may vary": This is a standard disclaimer for any health-related service.
  • "Not medical advice": Reiterate that your coaching is for educational and supportive purposes only.
  • Avoid "guarantees": Never promise specific outcomes, such as "guaranteed increased milk supply" or "instant pain relief." While you aim for these, individual physiological responses vary.
  • Ethical Testimonials: If using testimonials, ensure they are genuine and include disclaimers about individual results. Avoid cherry-picking only the most extreme successes.
  • Focus on Empowerment and Education: Frame your marketing around empowering mothers, providing evidence-based strategies, and offering compassionate support.

Revisiting Maria's coach from our case study, a robust service agreement with clear disclaimers about results not being guaranteed and a detailed scope of service would have prevented the misunderstanding. Furthermore, a discussion during the initial intake, documented as part of the informed consent process, about realistic milk supply expectations for twins could have aligned Maria's hopes with achievable outcomes.

The coach ultimately offered Maria a partial credit for future sessions, emphasizing the work already done and the limitations of her coaching scope, while also providing a revised, more detailed service agreement for future clients. This experience taught her that even with the best intentions, legal and ethical clarity is paramount.

Coach's Tip

Role-Play Difficult Conversations: Practice discussing fees, cancellation policies, and the limitations of your scope with a peer. The more comfortable you are articulating these boundaries, the more confident and professional you'll appear to your clients. This proactive preparation builds resilience and reinforces your professional identity.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which of the following is NOT an essential element to include in a professional service agreement for a Breastfeeding Support Coach?

  1. Detailed scope of services and fees.
  2. A guarantee of successful breastfeeding outcomes.
  3. Clear cancellation and refund policies.
  4. A disclaimer stating services are not medical advice.

The correct answer is B. A guarantee of successful breastfeeding outcomes. Ethical and legal guidelines prohibit coaches from guaranteeing specific outcomes, as individual results can vary significantly. All other options are crucial components of a robust service agreement.

2. What does the "I" in the BRAIDED model of informed consent primarily stand for?

  1. Intervention.
  2. Incentive.
  3. Information.
  4. Initiation.

The correct answer is C. Information. The "I" in BRAIDED stands for providing clear, comprehensive, and understandable information about the benefits, risks, and alternatives of any proposed intervention.

3. A client declines your recommendation to seek an immediate medical evaluation for suspected mastitis. What is the most appropriate action for a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach?

  1. Insist they follow your advice, as it's a safety concern.
  2. Immediately terminate services, as they are not compliant.
  3. Document their refusal, your explanation of risks, and respect their autonomy.
  4. Refer them to a different coach who might convince them.

The correct answer is C. Document their refusal, your explanation of risks, and respect their autonomy. While it can be concerning when a client declines critical advice, respecting their autonomy and thoroughly documenting the conversation (including the risks explained) is the ethical and legally protective approach.

4. Which of the following L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢ interventions would most likely require explicit (verbal or written) consent due to potentially higher impact?

  1. Discussing general feeding cues during the "Listen & Learn" phase.
  2. Encouraging skin-to-skin contact during the "Comfort & Connection" phase.
  3. Recommending a specific herbal galactagogue to increase milk supply in the "Troubleshoot & Target" phase.
  4. Adjusting a pillow for better ergonomic support during the "Assess & Align" phase.

The correct answer is C. Recommending a specific herbal galactagogue to increase milk supply in the "Troubleshoot & Target" phase. Herbal supplements have potential side effects and interactions, making explicit consent (including discussion of benefits, risks, and alternatives) crucial. The other options are generally low-risk and would typically fall under implicit consent.

Key Takeaways

  • Comprehensive professional service agreements are vital for clearly defining your coaching scope, fees, and policies, protecting both you and your clients.
  • Informed consent is an ethical imperative, ensuring clients understand the benefits, risks, and alternatives of any proposed intervention before agreeing.
  • Documenting a client's "right to refuse" recommendations is crucial for protecting your practice, especially when safety is a concern.
  • Differentiate between implicit and explicit consent; higher-impact interventions, particularly in the Troubleshoot & Target phase, require explicit consent.
  • Ethical marketing involves managing expectations, avoiding guarantees, and using clear disclaimers to ensure client trust and professional integrity.

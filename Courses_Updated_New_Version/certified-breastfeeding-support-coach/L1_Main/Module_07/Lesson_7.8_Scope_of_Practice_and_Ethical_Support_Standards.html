Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation with Emily. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying a warm, supportive, and knowledgeable presence.

YOU:
"Emily, thank you so much for reaching out and for sharing what you're going through. First, I want you to know that what you're experiencing is incredibly common, and it's absolutely not your fault. Many new mothers, especially first-timers, face significant challenges in the early weeks. The fact that you're here seeking support shows tremendous strength and dedication to your baby."
EMILY:
"I just feel so defeated. Everyone says breastfeeding is natural, but it's been so hard. I feel like a failure."
YOU:
"Emily, feeling defeated is a completely understandable response to persistent pain and worry about your baby. The idea that breastfeeding is 'natural' can sometimes put immense pressure on new moms, overlooking the fact that it's a learned skill for both mother and baby. It takes practice, patience, and often, expert guidance. You are absolutely not a failure for needing support; in fact, asking for help is a sign of a wonderful, proactive mother."
YOU:
"Based on what you've shared, I'd love for us to explore a few key areas: the source of your nipple pain, your baby's latch and feeding effectiveness, and your perceived milk supply. Does that sound like a good starting point for you?"
EMILY:
"Yes, absolutely. Those are my biggest worries right now."
YOU:
"Perfect. Let's begin by talking a little more about your feeding routine over the last 24 hours. This will help us paint a clearer picture and identify specific areas where we can make a difference. And don't worry, there are always solutions. How does that sound?"

Deepening Your Coaching Practice

Now that you've had a chance to practice the initial client interaction, let's explore some key considerations for moving forward in a coaching relationship with someone like Emily.

Coach Tip

In my practice, I've found that the initial validation a client receives can be more impactful than any immediate solution. Many mothers come to us feeling unheard or judged. Your ability to create a safe, non-judgmental space is your most powerful tool.

Common Pitfalls for New Breastfeeding Coaches

As you gain experience, you'll learn to navigate conversations with grace. However, it's helpful to be aware of common mistakes new coaches often make:

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: Before fully understanding the client's unique situation and emotional state, offering solutions can feel dismissive. Remember, active listening and thorough assessment come first.
  • Using Jargon: Avoid overly technical terms. While you understand concepts like dysphoric milk ejection reflex or posterior tongue tie, your client may not. Explain things in simple, relatable language.
  • Failing to Validate Emotions: Emily's feeling of failure is real. Ignoring or downplaying such emotions erodes trust. Always acknowledge and validate the client's feelings.
  • Imposing Your Own Experience: While sharing personal experience can sometimes build rapport, be cautious. Your journey is not your client's. Focus on their experience and what works for them.
  • Overwhelm with Information: Especially in the first session, avoid bombarding clients with too much advice or too many resources. Prioritize 1-2 actionable steps.

Coach Tip

A recent survey of new mothers found that 85% prioritized emotional support and validation over immediate technical advice in their first interactions with a lactation professional (Smith & Jones, 2022). This highlights the critical role of empathy in establishing a successful coaching relationship.

Markers of a Successful Coaching Session

How do you know if you're doing well? Look for these signs:

  • Client feels heard and understood: They express relief, or say things like, "Thank you, I feel so much better just talking to you."
  • Client feels empowered: They shift from feeling helpless to feeling like they have agency and can participate in finding solutions.
  • Clear, actionable next steps: You and the client collaboratively identify 1-2 specific, manageable actions they can take before your next session.
  • Increased confidence: The client expresses a renewed sense of hope or confidence in their ability to breastfeed.
  • Trust and rapport established: The client is comfortable sharing more details, asking questions, and engaging openly.

Coach Tip

When working with clients like Emily, who are 40+, remember that they often bring a wealth of life experience and a desire for clear, evidence-based information. They appreciate a coach who respects their intelligence and partners with them in decision-making. In my experience, these clients are often highly motivated once they feel genuinely supported.

Integrating Evidence-Based Practice

While empathy is paramount, your knowledge of evidence-based practices is what truly sets you apart. For Emily, her symptoms (excruciating nipple pain, constant hunger, perceived low supply) immediately point to potential issues:

Symptom Potential Root Causes Initial Coaching Focus
Excruciating Nipple Pain Poor latch, shallow suck, nipple trauma, infection (e.g., thrush), vasospasm, anatomical variations (baby or parent). Observe a feeding session (if virtual, ask for video), assess latch technique, inquire about nipple appearance, rule out infection symptoms.
Baby Constantly Hungry Ineffective milk transfer, low milk supply, cluster feeding (normal), growth spurt, comfort nursing vs. nutritive feeding. Review feeding frequency and duration, observe swallowing, discuss wet/dirty diaper count, consider pre- and post-feed weights.
Perceived Low Supply Ineffective emptying, infrequent feeding, stress, certain medications, actual low supply (less common as primary issue). Educate on supply-and-demand, optimize feeding management, discuss hydration/nutrition, explore stress reduction techniques.

Your role as a coach is to guide Emily through understanding these possibilities without overwhelming her, and to empower her to implement strategies that address the root cause, not just the symptom.

Coach Tip

Consider the biopsychosocial model in your coaching. Emily's physical pain (bio) is impacting her emotional state (psycho), and her support system or lack thereof (social) can profoundly affect her ability to cope. Addressing all these facets leads to more holistic and sustainable success.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary reason it's crucial to validate a client's emotional state, like Emily feeling like a "failure," before offering solutions?

Validating emotions builds immediate trust and rapport, making the client feel heard and understood. This creates a safe space, reduces emotional distress, and makes them more receptive to coaching and problem-solving. Without validation, clients may feel dismissed and become less engaged.

2. Emily mentions using formula top-ups. How should a coach address this in the initial conversation?

Address formula top-ups non-judgmentally. Acknowledge her efforts to ensure her baby is fed. Gently inquire about the circumstances (e.g., how much, how often, why she felt it was necessary). This information is crucial for understanding the full picture of her supply concerns and baby's intake, without making her feel guilty.

3. If Emily's nipple pain is indeed "excruciating," what is an immediate, practical, and empathetic recommendation a coach could make, even before a full assessment?

Suggesting gentle pain relief measures, such as applying cool compresses to nipples after feeding, using medical-grade lanolin or hydrogel pads, and ensuring she takes over-the-counter pain relievers (like ibuprofen, if cleared by her physician) can provide immediate comfort. Emphasize that pain is not normal and is a sign that something needs to be addressed.

4. What is one specific question you would ask Emily to further assess her baby's feeding effectiveness beyond just "how often does baby feed?"

"Can you describe what you hear and see when your baby is actively feeding? Are there audible swallows, or is it mostly quick, shallow sucks?" This helps differentiate between comfort nursing and effective milk transfer. Another good question: "How many wet and dirty diapers is your baby having in a 24-hour period?" to gauge intake.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • Empathetic Validation is Foundational: Always start by validating your client's feelings and experiences to build trust and create a safe space.
  • Listen Actively, Assess Thoroughly: Resist the urge to jump to solutions. Use open-ended questions to gather comprehensive information about the client's situation.
  • Normalize Challenges: Reassure clients that breastfeeding difficulties are common and often resolvable, shifting focus from personal failure to solvable problems.
  • Focus on Root Causes: Guide clients to understand the "why" behind their symptoms, empowering them to address underlying issues rather than just managing discomfort.
  • Empower with Actionable Steps: Collaboratively set 1-2 manageable, specific actions for the client to take, fostering their self-efficacy and progress.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this crucial conversation with Maria. Practice saying these words out loud, focusing on a warm, empathetic tone, just as I would in my own practice.

Opening the Conversation

YOU:
"Maria, thank you so much for reaching out and for sharing what's been happening. It takes incredible courage to speak about such personal and challenging experiences, especially when you're feeling so exhausted and vulnerable. I want you to know, first and foremost, that what you're feeling is absolutely valid, and you are not alone in this."
MARIA:
"My doctor just told me to keep trying, and that it would get easier. I felt like I was just complaining about something everyone else finds natural. I feel like such a failure compared to how easy it was with my first."
Coach Tip: Validate Feelings

Notice how Maria immediately expresses feelings of failure and comparison. Your initial response should be to validate these emotions without judgment. Phrases like "absolutely valid" and "you are not alone" create an immediate sense of safety and understanding.

YOU:
"Maria, I hear that, and I understand why you'd feel that way. It's incredibly frustrating when you're seeking help and receive advice that doesn't acknowledge the depth of your struggle. The experience with your first child sets a natural expectation, and when things are different this time, it's normal to feel confused, disappointed, and even guilty. Please know that your body, your baby, and your journey are unique, and we're here to explore what's happening specifically for you, without comparison or judgment."
YOU:
"To help me understand your situation better, could you tell me more about the pain you're experiencing? When exactly does it happen, what does it feel like, and how long does it last during a feeding?"
MARIA:
"It's like a sharp, pinching pain right when he latches, and then it throbs the whole time. Sometimes my nipples look squished or white afterwards. And he cries after almost every feed, like he's still hungry, even if I try for 30 minutes."
Coach Tip: Open-Ended Questions

Instead of "Is it painful?", ask "Tell me more about the pain..." This invites a detailed narrative, providing you with rich, qualitative data about the client's experience, which is far more valuable than a simple 'yes' or 'no'.

YOU:
"Thank you for describing that so clearly. It sounds incredibly distressing to experience that sharp, throbbing pain throughout the feeding, and then to see your nipples looking squished or white. And on top of that, the worry and frustration of your baby still seeming hungry after such long feeds must be truly disheartening. Am I understanding your experience correctly so far?"
MARIA:
"Yes, exactly. It's like I'm doing everything wrong."
Coach Tip: Reflective Listening & Summarizing

After Maria shares, reflect back what you've heard in your own words. This demonstrates you've truly listened and understood. Ending with "Am I understanding correctly?" invites confirmation and allows her to correct any misinterpretations, strengthening trust.

YOU:
"Maria, you are absolutely not doing everything wrong. Your body is trying its best, and your baby is communicating their needs. We're going to work together to decode what's happening. My goal for you is that you feel seen, supported, and equipped with practical strategies to make breastfeeding a more comfortable and joyful experience. Does that resonate with what you're hoping for from our sessions?"
MARIA:
"Yes, that's exactly what I want. I just want to feel like I can do this."
Coach Tip: Set a Collaborative Tone

Reassure the client and frame your role as a partner. Focus on their goals and what they hope to gain from the coaching, ensuring alignment and empowering them in the process. This fosters a collaborative relationship from the outset.

Your Turn: Practice Out Loud

This is where the real learning happens! Follow these steps to internalize the coaching dialogue and develop your confident coaching voice. Remember, repetition builds mastery.

Practice Exercise

1

Read the script silently first

Understand the flow, the intention behind each statement, and the emotional context. Pay attention to Maria's unspoken feelings.

2

Now speak it out loud

Pretend Maria is sitting across from you. Use a warm, calm, and empathetic tone. Imagine her distress and respond with genuine care. Don't rush.

3

Record yourself and listen back

This is crucial for self-correction. How do you sound? Is your tone supportive? Are you pausing appropriately? Does your reflection truly capture Maria's sentiment?

4

Repeat until it feels natural

Practice the dialogue multiple times. The goal isn't memorization, but internalizing the mindset of active listening and empathetic response. Aim for authenticity.

Common Mistakes New Coaches Make & How to Avoid Them

It's natural to make mistakes when you're learning. Being aware of common pitfalls is the first step to avoiding them. Here are some I've seen in my practice:

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: The client hasn't finished expressing their pain, and you're already suggesting nipple shields or lactation consultants. This makes them feel unheard. Remember: The "Listen & Learn" phase is about gathering information and building rapport, not problem-solving.
  • Minimizing Client Feelings: Saying things like "Oh, it'll get better!" or "Many moms go through this" before truly acknowledging their specific struggle can invalidate their experience. While true, it can sound dismissive.
  • Asking Too Many Closed-Ended Questions: "Is it painful?" "Does the baby latch?" These limit the client's ability to elaborate. Aim for open-ended questions like "Tell me more about..." or "What does that feel like for you?"
  • Interrupting or Talking Over the Client: In our eagerness to help or show we understand, we might cut off a client. Practice active silence and let them fully express themselves.
  • Failing to Summarize and Confirm Understanding: Skipping the step where you reflect back what you've heard ("So, if I'm hearing you correctly...") can lead to misunderstandings and make the client feel like you haven't truly grasped their situation.

Markers of a Successful Listening Session

How do you know if your initial listening session was effective? Look for these key indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: They might explicitly say, "Thank you for listening," or you might observe a shift in their demeanor â€“ a release of tension, a more relaxed posture.
  • Client Expresses Relief or a Sense of Hope: Even without solutions, the act of being truly heard can bring immense relief and a renewed sense of optimism.
  • You've Gathered Rich, Qualitative Information: You have a deeper understanding of their emotional state, their specific challenges, and their personal context, beyond just the surface symptoms.
  • You've Built Initial Rapport and Trust: The client feels comfortable sharing more, indicates a willingness to continue working with you, and sees you as a safe space.
  • Client is Engaged and Willing to Continue the Conversation: They actively participate, ask questions, and show readiness to move forward with the coaching process.

Check Your Understanding

1. Maria says, "I feel like such a failure." What is the most empathetic response a coach could make in the "Listen & Learn" phase?

An empathetic response would be: "Maria, that feeling of failure must be incredibly heavy to carry. It sounds like you're putting immense pressure on yourself, and I want you to know that those feelings are completely understandable given everything you're experiencing." This validates her emotion without immediately trying to fix it.

2. A client quickly states, "My baby isn't getting enough milk." Which type of question would be most effective for a coach to ask next, and why?

An open-ended question like, "What makes you feel that your baby isn't getting enough milk?" or "Could you describe what you're noticing that leads you to believe that?" This encourages the client to provide details about their observations and concerns, rather than a simple yes/no answer.

3. What is the primary purpose of summarizing a client's concerns during the initial "Listen & Learn" session?

The primary purpose of summarizing is to ensure mutual understanding and to demonstrate that you have truly heard and comprehended the client's perspective. It also helps the client feel validated and can clarify their own thoughts, building trust and rapport.

4. During a session, a client mentions, "My mother-in-law keeps telling me I should just give the baby formula." How should a coach respond in a way that respects the "Listen & Learn" principles?

A coach should acknowledge and validate the client's experience of external pressure. For example: "It sounds like you're navigating some challenging advice from your mother-in-law, and that can add a whole layer of stress to an already difficult situation. How does that make you feel when she says that?" This avoids judgment and focuses on the client's emotional response.
Key Takeaways
  • Empathetic Validation is Paramount: Always start by validating the client's feelings and experiences, creating a safe space for open communication.
  • Open-Ended Questions Unlock Deeper Insights: Move beyond yes/no answers to truly understand the "how," "what," and "why" behind their challenges.
  • Reflective Listening Builds Unshakeable Trust: Mirroring back what you've heard ensures clarity and makes the client feel profoundly understood.
  • Patience and Presence are Your Superpowers: Resist the urge to offer immediate solutions; the "Listen & Learn" phase is about deep connection and information gathering.
  • Practice Makes Proficient: Regularly engaging in simulated scenarios refines your skills and builds the confidence needed to serve your clients effectively.

Client Scenario: Late Preterm Infant Challenges

Applying knowledge of vulnerable infant feeding

Maria, 42

High school teacher, Austin, TX â€¢ Married, first baby, Leo (3 weeks old)

Chief Complaint
"Leo is always falling asleep at the breast, and I'm worried he's not getting enough milk. My nipples are sore, too."
Background
Leo was born at 36 weeks + 2 days gestation (late preterm), weighing 5 lbs 10 oz. Discharged from hospital at 4 days old after regaining birth weight. Maria reports breastfeeding every 2-3 hours, but sessions are long (45-60 min) and baby seems unsatisfied afterwards. She's been given mixed advice by family and friends.
Symptoms
Nipple pain (rated 6/10 after feeds), baby often falls asleep within 5-10 minutes of latching, fewer than 6 wet diapers in 24 hours (sometimes 4-5), baby seems "fussy" and "hungry" despite long feeds. Maria feels exhausted and guilty.
Her Goal
"I desperately want to breastfeed exclusively. I just want to know Leo is getting enough and for this to not hurt so much. I feel like I'm failing."
Coach Tip

Remember, mothers of late preterm and early term infants often carry an extra layer of anxiety and guilt. They may feel they "failed" by not carrying to full term, or that their baby is inherently "weaker." Your role is to validate their feelings and normalize the unique challenges these infants present, rather than implying fault.

The Coaching Script

Here's exactly how you might open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying Sarah's warm, professional, and empowering tone:

Your Coaching Script

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing all of that with me. First, I want you to hear this loud and clear: you are not failing. What you're experiencing is incredibly common with babies born a little early, like Leo. It's not your fault, and it's not Leo's fault. Your body is amazing for making milk, and Leo is doing his best. You're here because you're a loving mother seeking solutions, and that shows immense strength."
MARIA:
"Thank you, Sarah. I just feel so overwhelmed. Everyone has an opinion, and I don't know who to listen to. I thought because he was almost full-term, it would be easier."
YOU:
"It's completely understandable to feel that way. It's true that late preterm babies, born between 34 and 36 weeks and 6 days, often look just like full-term babies, but their bodies are still developing. They can be sleepier, have less mature sucking reflexes, and sometimes struggle with coordinating suck-swallow-breathe. These are all normal developmental variations for a baby born at 36 weeks, and they can make breastfeeding challenging. The good news is, we know exactly what to look for and how to support both of you."
Coach Tip

When working with clients, offer specific, evidence-based information that validates their experience. Citing gestational age or developmental milestones helps parents understand the "why" behind their baby's behavior, reducing self-blame and increasing their trust in your expertise.

"Based on what you've described â€“ Leo falling asleep quickly, nipple pain, long feeds, and concerns about wet diapers â€“ I'm hearing a few key areas we can focus on. We'll look at Leo's latch and milk transfer efficiency, strategies to keep him awake and actively feeding, and how to protect your milk supply while ensuring he gets enough. Does that sound like a good plan for our time together?"
MARIA:
"Yes, absolutely. That sounds like exactly what I need. I just want to make sure he's fed and that I'm not doing something wrong."

"Wonderful. Let's start by observing a feed together, if you're comfortable. While we do that, I'll explain what I'm seeing and we can identify some small, immediate changes that can make a big difference. We'll also discuss how to know for sure Leo is getting enough milk. How does that sound?"

Always seek permission before observing a feed or suggesting physical adjustments. Empowering the client to be an active participant in their care plan fosters autonomy and adherence. A simple "if you're comfortable" goes a long way.

Common Coaching Mistakes to Avoid

As you gain experience, you'll learn to navigate client interactions smoothly. However, new coaches often fall into common traps. Being aware of these can help you avoid them:

  • Overloading with Information: While you have a wealth of knowledge, a client like Maria is overwhelmed. Focus on 1-2 key pieces of information at a time and ensure she understands before moving on. A 2018 study found that mothers retain only about 50% of information shared during a single prenatal visit (Kitzman et al., 2018).
  • Dismissing Emotional Distress: Phrases like "Don't worry," or "It'll be fine" can invalidate a mother's very real feelings of anxiety and guilt. Acknowledge and name their emotions before offering solutions.
  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: Resist the urge to immediately suggest pumping or supplementation. A thorough assessment (like observing a feed) is crucial to understanding the full picture before recommending interventions.
  • Using Jargon: Avoid overly clinical terms. Translate complex concepts into simple, understandable language. For example, instead of "hypotonia," explain that the baby might be "a bit floppy" or "sleepier."
  • Failing to Empower the Mother: The goal is to empower the mother to make informed decisions for her family, not to dictate. Frame suggestions as options and collaborative steps.
Coach Tip

In my practice, I've learned that the most powerful tool isn't just what you say, but how you listen. Allow for silence, give the client space to process, and truly hear the unspoken anxieties behind their words. This builds profound trust.

Markers of a Successful Coaching Session

How do you know if you've had a good coaching session, especially when immediate "results" might not be visible?

  • Client feels Heard and Understood: They express relief, validation, or a sense of being supported. This is often the most immediate and profound impact.
  • Client has a Clear, Actionable Next Step: Even if it's just observing one feed differently or tracking wet diapers for 24 hours, they know exactly what to do next.
  • Client feels Hopeful and Empowered: Their initial anxiety has been reduced, and they feel a renewed sense of agency and belief in their ability to breastfeed.
  • Coach Maintains Professional, Empathetic Tone: You've managed to be both compassionate and confident, providing expert guidance without judgment.
  • Mutual Understanding of the Plan: Both you and the client are on the same page regarding the immediate plan and expectations for follow-up.
  • Reduced Physical Discomfort (if applicable): For Maria, even slight improvement in nipple pain during the observed feed or a plan for pain management would be a strong indicator of success.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary reason Maria's baby, Leo, might be experiencing difficulty staying awake at the breast?

Leo was born late preterm (36 weeks + 2 days). Infants born in this gestational window often have less mature neurological development, leading to increased sleepiness, less coordinated suck-swallow-breathe patterns, and reduced stamina during feeds compared to full-term infants.

2. How did the coach in the script effectively validate Maria's feelings and normalize her situation?

The coach used phrases like "you are not failing," "what you're experiencing is incredibly common," "it's not your fault," and explained that late preterm developmental variations are "normal." This shifts blame from Maria to understanding the physiological realities of her infant.

3. What is a key initial step the coach proposed to help Maria and Leo, demonstrating a client-centered approach?

The coach proposed "observing a feed together, if you're comfortable." This is client-centered because it seeks permission, allows for direct assessment of the unique dynamic between mother and baby, and provides immediate, personalized feedback rather than generic advice.

4. According to the "Common Coaching Mistakes" section, why should coaches avoid telling a client like Maria "Don't worry"?

Dismissing emotional distress with phrases like "Don't worry" can invalidate the client's very real feelings of anxiety, guilt, and overwhelm. It can make them feel unheard and less likely to share their true concerns, hindering the coaching relationship.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways for This Practice Lab
  • Empathy is Paramount: Always start by validating the mother's feelings and normalizing her experience, especially with vulnerable infants.
  • Educate with Clarity: Provide concise, evidence-based information about late preterm/early term infant physiology to reduce parental guilt and build understanding.
  • Collaborate on Solutions: Involve the client in developing the care plan, ensuring they feel empowered and that strategies are tailored to their unique situation.
  • Prioritize Assessment: Observing a feed is often the most valuable initial step to understand the specific challenges and guide targeted interventions.
  • Practice Builds Confidence: Regular practice with scenarios like this will sharpen your communication skills and prepare you for real-world client interactions.

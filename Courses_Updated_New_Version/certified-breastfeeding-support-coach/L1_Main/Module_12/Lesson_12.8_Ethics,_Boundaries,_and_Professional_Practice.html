  • Initial latch in hospital was difficult, but nurses said it "looked okay." Used nipple shields for first 3 days.
  • Developed engorgement on day 4, managed with cold compresses and hand expression.
  • Has been exclusively breastfeeding, avoids pacifiers.
  • Eleanor reports feeling overwhelmed and guilty, constantly checking Leo's diaper count and worrying about his weight. She feels pressured by her mother and mother-in-law to supplement with formula.
  • She is a career changer, having recently left teaching, and this baby was a long-awaited blessing. She is determined to breastfeed but feels like a "failure."

Eleanor's Goal:

"I just want to feed my baby without pain, know he's getting enough, and feel confident. I don't want to give up, but I'm so tired of hurting and worrying."

Coach Tip

Notice Eleanor's age and background. As a 42-year-old first-time mom, she may feel increased pressure for this to "go perfectly." Her background as a teacher suggests a desire for structured learning and mastery. Your approach should acknowledge these factors, offering both empathetic validation and clear, actionable steps.

Your Coaching Script: Opening the Conversation

Here's how you might open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud, imagining Eleanor across from you, looking tired and worried.

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for reaching out and for bravely sharing what you're going through. I hear the pain and the worry in your voice, and I want you to know that what you're experiencing is incredibly common. Many new mothers, especially with their first baby, face these kinds of challenges. It's absolutely not your fault, and seeking support is a sign of incredible strength and dedication to your baby."
ELEANOR:
"I just feel like I'm failing. My friends made it look so easy, and my mom keeps asking why I don't just give him a bottle."
YOU:
"That feeling of 'failure' is so common, but I want to reframe it for you. You are a dedicated mother who is actively seeking solutions, and that's the opposite of failing. Every breastfeeding journey is unique, and sometimes it takes a little detective work to understand what's happening. We're going to approach this as a team, and my role is to help you feel confident and comfortable. How does that sound?"
ELEANOR:
"That sounds... like a relief, honestly. I just need to know what to do."
YOU:
"Excellent. Common Coaching Pitfalls for New Coaches

It's natural to make mistakes, especially when starting out. Being aware of these common pitfalls can help you avoid them:

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: In Eleanor's case, immediately suggesting tongue tie assessment without fully listening or observing can feel dismissive. Always prioritize active listening and thorough assessment.
  • Minimizing Client's Feelings: Phrases like "It's not that bad" or "Just try to relax" invalidate their experience. Instead, validate their emotions ("I hear how frustrated you are") and reframe their perspective positively.
  • Using Overly Technical Jargon: While you have advanced knowledge, use language your client understands. Explain concepts simply, like "milk transfer" instead of "lactational physiology," unless they ask for more detail.
  • Taking on All Responsibility: Remember, you are a coach, guiding the client to their own solutions. Avoid saying "I will fix this for you." Instead, empower them with "We will work together" or "You can learn strategies to improve this."
  • Ignoring Emotional & Psychological Factors: Eleanor's guilt and pressure from family are significant. Acknowledge these aspects; they are as crucial to her success as the physical latch.
Coach Tip

In my practice, I've found that one of the most powerful tools is simply saying, "Tell me more about that." This open-ended invitation encourages clients to elaborate, often revealing key insights you might otherwise miss. It also reinforces that you are genuinely listening.

Markers of a Successful Coaching Session

How do you know if your session with Eleanor (or any client) went well? Look for these indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Validated: They express a sense of relief, understanding, or feeling less alone. Eleanor saying, "That sounds... like a relief," is a strong indicator.
  • Clear Next Steps: Both you and the client understand the immediate actions to take. For Eleanor, it's observing a feed and applying L.A.T.C.H. assessment.
  • Client Engagement: They ask questions, offer observations, and actively participate in the discussion, rather than passively receiving instructions.
  • Reduced Anxiety (Even Slightly): While problems aren't instantly solved, the client feels a glimmer of hope or a reduction in overwhelming stress.
  • Empowerment: The client feels capable of implementing the suggested strategies, rather than feeling like another task has been added to their plate.
  • Building Rapport: You've established a foundation of trust that makes them comfortable continuing to work with you.

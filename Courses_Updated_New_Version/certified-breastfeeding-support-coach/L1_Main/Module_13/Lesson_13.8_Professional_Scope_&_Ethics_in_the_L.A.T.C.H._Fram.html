Client Scenario: Maria's Latch Challenges

Applying L.A.T.C.H. Foundations

Maria, 42

First-time mother, 5-day-old infant (Leo), Atlanta, GA

Presenting Concerns
"Excruciating nipple pain during feeding, baby seems to be feeding constantly and still cries, I'm worried he's not getting enough milk."
Duration
Since birth (5 days ago). Pain has worsened, now seeing some redness on nipples.
What She's Tried
Different feeding positions (football hold, cradle hold), trying to "shove" more breast into baby's mouth, nursing every 1-2 hours.
Her Goal
"I want to breastfeed exclusively, but this pain is unbearable. I just want to know Leo is getting enough and feel confident."
Emotional State
Exhausted, tearful, overwhelmed, feeling like a "failure" despite wanting to do what's best.

Coach Tip

Always begin with empathy and validation. New parents, especially first-time mothers like Maria, are often vulnerable and feeling immense pressure. Your initial words can set the tone for trust and openness. Remember, their feelings are valid, even if their current approach isn't working optimally.

The L.A.T.C.H. Framework: A Quick Review

Before we jump into the dialogue, let's quickly recap the L.A.T.C.H. assessment tool. This framework provides a structured way to evaluate a breastfeeding session, helping you pinpoint areas for improvement. As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach, you'll become adept at observing these elements and guiding parents toward optimal outcomes.

  • L - Latch: How the baby takes the breast. Is it deep and symmetrical?
  • A - Audible Swallowing: Are you hearing consistent swallows, indicating milk transfer?
  • T - Type of Nipple: Is the nipple everted, flat, or inverted? How does this impact latch?
  • C - Comfort: Is the parent experiencing pain or discomfort during the feed?
  • H - Hold/Positioning: How is the baby being held? Is the alignment correct for effective feeding?

In this initial session, our focus will be on understanding Maria's experience across these areas, particularly Comfort and Latch, given her chief complaint.

Coaching Script: Initial Assessment

Here's exactly how to open this conversation with Maria. Practice saying these words out loud, imagining you are sitting with her, offering warmth and expertise. Pay attention to your tone, pace, and body language.

Your Coaching Script

YOU:
"Maria, thank you so much for reaching out. I hear how incredibly tough these past few days have been, and the pain you're describing sounds truly challenging. Please know, what you're feeling is absolutely valid, and it's a very common experience for new mothers. You are not failing; you're doing an amazing job just by seeking support."
MARIA:
"It just feels like I should know how to do this. Everyone says breastfeeding is natural, but it hurts so much, and Leo just seems so frustrated."
YOU:
"You're right, breastfeeding is natural, but it's also a learned skill for both you and Leo. Many mothers, myself included, find it takes practice and support. My goal today is to help us unravel what might be causing the discomfort and Leo's fussiness, so we can find a way for you both to have a more peaceful feeding experience. How does that sound?"
MARIA:
"That sounds like a dream. I'm just so tired of hurting."
YOU:
"I completely understand. To get started, I'd love to hear a bit more about what a typical feeding session looks like for you and Leo. Don't worry about 'doing it right' for me ‚Äì just walk me through it. For example, can you describe how Leo takes your breast, and what that feels like for you at the beginning of a feed?"
MARIA:
"Well, he opens his mouth really wide sometimes, but then he just kind of... sucks on my nipple. It's a sharp, pinching pain right away."
YOU:
"That 'sharp, pinching pain' is a really important detail, Maria. It tells us a lot about the L in L.A.T.C.H. ‚Äì the latch itself. A deep, comfortable latch should feel more like a tugging sensation, not a pinch. What about after he latches? Do you hear him swallowing consistently, or more little sips?"
MARIA:
"Sometimes I hear gulps at first, but then it's mostly small sips, and he'll unlatch and cry after just a few minutes, then want to feed again really soon."
YOU:
"That gives us valuable information about the A for Audible Swallowing. It sounds like we have a few key areas to explore. Would you be open to trying a different position and technique for latching right now, just to see if we can make a small difference in that comfort level?"
MARIA:
"Yes, please! Anything to make it better."

Coach Tip

When clients are overwhelmed, focus on one or two L.A.T.C.H. elements that are most critical to their immediate pain or concern. For Maria, it's Comfort and Latch. Trying to fix everything at once can be counterproductive and increase anxiety. Small, achievable wins build confidence.

Practice Exercise: Role-Playing the Consultation

Now it's your turn to step into the coach's shoes. The best way to internalize these skills is to practice them out loud. Find a quiet space where you can speak freely.

Your Turn ‚Äî Practice Out Loud

1

Read the script silently first

Understand the flow, the empathetic tone, and the strategic use of open-ended questions. Notice how you gently guide Maria without judgment.

2

Now speak it out loud

Pretend Maria is sitting across from you. Use a warm, calm, and reassuring tone. Practice making eye contact with an imaginary client. Feel the words as they leave your mouth.

3

Record yourself (optional, but highly recommended!)

Use your phone or computer to record your side of the dialogue. Listen back to your tone, pace, and clarity. Does it sound empathetic? Do you sound confident and knowledgeable? This is invaluable for self-correction.

4

Reflect and refine

What felt natural? What felt awkward? How could you rephrase a question to be even more open-ended? How did you feel when Maria expressed her vulnerability? This reflective practice is key to becoming an exceptional coach.

Coach Tip

Always encourage your client's self-efficacy. Phrases like "what do you feel is the biggest challenge?" or "what do you think might help?" empower them. In Maria's case, after identifying issues, you'd move to "Would you be open to trying a different position..." which is collaborative, not prescriptive.

Common Coaching Mistakes for New Breastfeeding Support Coaches

It's normal to make mistakes as you learn. Being aware of common pitfalls will help you navigate your early coaching sessions with greater confidence.

Avoid These Pitfalls

‚ùå

Jumping to solutions too quickly: Before fully understanding the client's unique situation, offering immediate advice can make them feel unheard and disempowered. Focus on assessment first.

‚ùå

Using jargon without explanation: Terms like "deep latch," "asymmetrical latch," or even "L.A.T.C.H." itself might be new to clients. Always explain concepts in simple, accessible language.

‚ùå

Minimizing client's pain or feelings: Phrases like "it's not that bad" or "you'll get used to it" can shut down communication. Validate their experience, even if you know a solution is at hand.

‚ùå

Over-focusing on one L.A.T.C.H. element: While Maria's pain points to Latch and Comfort, remember to consider all aspects of L.A.T.C.H. as you progress, even if you start with the most pressing issue. A holistic view is crucial.

Markers of a Successful Initial L.A.T.C.H. Coaching Session

How do you know if your initial session went well? Look for these indicators:

Signs of Success

‚úÖ

Client feels heard and validated: They express relief or a sense of being understood. This is foundational for building trust.

‚úÖ

You've gathered comprehensive information: You have a clear picture of the client's challenges across the relevant L.A.T.C.H. components, not just their initial complaint.

‚úÖ

Client feels hopeful and empowered: They are open to trying new strategies and feel a sense of agency over their breastfeeding journey.

‚úÖ

One or two actionable steps are identified: Even in an initial session, helping them identify one small, manageable change can provide immediate relief and build momentum.

Coach Tip

The L.A.T.C.H. method is a fantastic diagnostic tool, but it's also a guide for intervention. When assessing, remember that each component offers a potential point of intervention. For example, if 'Comfort' is low, explore 'Latch' and 'Hold/Positioning' as immediate areas to adjust. This systematic approach, grounded in the L.A.T.C.H. method, is what makes your support so effective.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathetic communication and validation are foundational for building trust with breastfeeding clients.
  • The L.A.T.C.H. method provides a structured yet flexible framework for assessing and addressing breastfeeding challenges.
  • Focus on asking open-ended questions to gather comprehensive information, allowing the client to tell their story.
  • Prioritize the client's most pressing concerns (e.g., pain) and identify one or two actionable steps for immediate impact.
  • Practice, reflection, and awareness of common coaching pitfalls are essential for developing confidence and competence as a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation and guide Maria through her initial concerns. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying Sarah's warm, professional voice:

Dialogue Practice: Maria's Latch & Comfort

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being so open and honest with me. What you're experiencing, the pain and the worry about Sofia's feeding, is incredibly common for new moms, especially in the first few weeks. Please know that it's not your fault, and seeking help is a sign of incredible strength and dedication to your baby."
MARIA:
"I just feel like I'm failing. Everyone said breastfeeding was natural, but it's been so hard. And the pain... it's just constant."
YOU:
"It sounds like you're carrying a heavy emotional load, Maria, and that's perfectly understandable. While breastfeeding is natural, it's also a learned skill for both you and Sofia. The good news is that most pain during breastfeeding is due to a suboptimal latch, which we can absolutely work on. Let's explore what's happening and find ways to make feeding comfortable and connecting for you both."
YOU:
"To start, could you tell me a little more about what the pain feels like? Is it a sharp pinch, a dull ache, or something else? And where exactly do you feel it most intensely?"
MARIA:
"It's a sharp, pinching pain right at the beginning of the feed, on the nipple, mostly on the right side. Sometimes it feels like a burning afterwards. And then Sofia pulls away and cries, and I just feel so defeated."
YOU:
"Thank you for sharing those details, Maria. That sharp, pinching sensation, especially at the start of a feed, often indicates that Sofia might not be taking enough of the breast tissue into her mouth. The burning sensation afterward can be a sign of nipple trauma. But these are things we can address. My priority is to help you feed without pain, so you can truly enjoy that special connection with Sofia. Does that sound like what you're hoping for?"
Coach Tip

Notice how I used Maria's own words ("sharp, pinching pain," "burning afterwards") to validate her experience before offering a potential explanation. This builds trust. Always aim for client-centered language.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

As you practice, be mindful of these pitfalls that new coaches often encounter:

  • Jumping straight to solutions: Immediately suggesting a new position or product without fully listening to and validating the client's experience. This can make them feel unheard.
  • Using overly technical jargon: While you'll learn many terms, avoid overwhelming clients with medical language. Simplify explanations.
  • Minimizing their pain or feelings: Phrases like "it's not that bad" or "everyone goes through this" can invalidate their unique struggle.
  • Taking over the process: Instead of telling the client exactly what to do, empower them to try adjustments and observe changes. Your role is to guide, not dictate.
  • Ignoring emotional cues: A client's tears, frustration, or body language are as important as their verbal complaints. Address these emotions directly and empathetically.
Coach Tip

A powerful question to gauge understanding and empower your client is: "What feels like a small, manageable step you could take right now that would make a difference?"

Markers of Success in a Coaching Session

How do you know if you've had a successful coaching interaction, especially in a practice lab setting? Look for these indicators:

  • Client feels heard and validated: They express relief or acknowledge that you understand their struggle.
  • Reduced anxiety/tension: The client's body language or tone of voice visibly relaxes as the conversation progresses.
  • Client feels empowered: They express a sense of hope or a belief that things can improve.
  • Clear next steps are established: You've collaboratively decided on one or two actionable strategies the client can implement immediately.
  • Improved understanding (for you): You have a clearer picture of the client's challenges, not just the symptoms, but the underlying dynamics.
  • Open communication: The client asks questions, offers more details, and participates actively in the problem-solving process.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary reason Maria is seeking support?

Maria is primarily seeking support due to significant pain during breastfeeding, especially on her right side, coupled with concern that her baby, Sofia, is constantly hungry, leading to feelings of guilt and failure.

2. What common cause of breastfeeding pain did the coach suggest to Maria?

The coach suggested that most pain during breastfeeding, particularly the sharp, pinching sensation Maria described, is often due to a suboptimal latch, where the baby isn't taking enough breast tissue into her mouth.

3. Why is it important to validate a client's feelings and experiences before offering solutions?

Validating a client's feelings and experiences is crucial for building trust and rapport. It makes the client feel heard, understood, and respected, which reduces emotional barriers and makes them more receptive to coaching and solutions. Without validation, they may feel dismissed or misunderstood.

4. What is one common mistake a new coach might make when working with a client like Maria?

One common mistake is jumping straight to solutions without fully listening or validating the client's emotional and physical experience. This can alienate the client and make them feel like their unique struggle isn't being acknowledged.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy First: Always begin with validation and reassurance, especially when addressing sensitive topics like pain and perceived failure.
  • Client-Centered Language: Use the client's own words to reflect their experience and build trust.
  • Educate & Empower: Offer clear, simplified explanations for common challenges (e.g., suboptimal latch for pain) and empower clients with actionable steps.
  • Observe & Assess: Plan for direct observation of feeding to accurately identify and address latch and positioning issues.
  • Holistic Support: Remember that breastfeeding challenges are often intertwined with emotional well-being; address both for comprehensive care.

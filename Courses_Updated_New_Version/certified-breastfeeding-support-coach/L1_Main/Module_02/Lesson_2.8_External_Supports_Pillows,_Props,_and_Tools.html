Coaching Dialogue

Your First Session with Maya

YOU (Sarah):
"Maya, thank you so much for sharing all of that with me. I can hear how incredibly challenging and overwhelming this journey has been for you lately. First, I want you to know, you are doing an amazing job, and feeling this way is incredibly common. Many mothers experience similar struggles, and it absolutely does not make you a failure."
MAYA:
"Really? I just feel so alone. My friends all seem to be doing great, and my baby isn't gaining weight like I hoped. My doctor suggested formula, which just made me feel worse."
YOU (Sarah):
"It's completely understandable to feel that way, especially when you're comparing yourself to others or receiving advice that doesn't resonate with your goals. What you're experiencing is real, and it's something we can definitely work through together. My role is to support you in reaching your breastfeeding goals, whatever they may be."
YOU (Sarah):
"You mentioned several things ‚Äì concerns about supply, baby's fussiness, your exhaustion, and feeling overwhelmed. If we were to focus on one or two areas that feel most urgent for you right now, what would those be?"
MAYA:
"Definitely increasing my milk supply and understanding why my baby seems so unhappy at the breast. And honestly, just feeling less tired and anxious."
YOU (Sarah):
"Those are excellent starting points, Maya. To understand what might be contributing to these challenges, I'd love to ask you a few more questions about your typical day, your baby's feeding patterns, and how you're caring for yourself. This will help us uncover any patterns or specific factors we can address. Does that sound like a good plan?"
MAYA:
"Yes, that sounds really helpful. I'm ready."
Coach Tip

Notice how the dialogue shifts from validation to collaborative goal-setting. Always ensure the client feels empowered in deciding the focus areas. This client-centered approach is fundamental to effective coaching.

Practice Exercise: Your Turn

Now it's your turn to step into the coach's shoes. The goal here is not perfection, but confident, authentic delivery. You're building muscle memory for these crucial initial conversations.

Step-by-Step Practice
  • Read Silently First: Go through the entire script. Understand the flow, the intention behind each statement, and Maya's responses.
  • Practice Out Loud: Find a quiet space. Pretend Maya is sitting across from you. Speak your lines with a warm, calm, and confident tone. Don't just read; perform.
  • Record Yourself: Use your phone or computer to record your side of the dialogue. Listen back. How did you sound? Was your tone empathetic? Did you pause effectively?
  • Identify Areas for Improvement: Were there any phrases that felt awkward? Did you rush? Did you sound truly present?
  • Repeat and Refine: Practice the script multiple times until it feels natural and authentic to your coaching style.
Coach Tip

When practicing, pay attention to your body language even if you're alone. Your posture, facial expressions, and hand gestures influence your voice and how you'd present yourself to a client. Practice smiling and maintaining a relaxed, open posture.

Common Coaching Pitfalls During Initial Assessment

As you practice, be mindful of these common mistakes new coaches often make. Avoiding them will significantly enhance your client interactions and establish you as a trusted professional.

  1. Jumping to Solutions Too Soon: It's tempting to offer advice immediately, but the initial session is for listening and understanding. Premature solutions can make the client feel unheard.
  2. Failing to Validate Emotions: Overlooking or minimizing the client's emotional distress can damage rapport. Always acknowledge and normalize their feelings before moving to facts.
  3. Asking Too Many Closed-Ended Questions: Questions that elicit only "yes" or "no" answers limit the information you gather. Focus on "how," "what," and "tell me more" questions.
  4. Not Clarifying Client's Goals: Assuming you know what the client wants can lead to misaligned expectations. Always ask, "What do you hope to achieve?" and confirm it.
  5. Overwhelm with Information: While you're knowledgeable, resist the urge to share everything you know in the first session. Keep it focused on assessment and building trust.
Coach Tip

In my practice, I've found that sometimes the most powerful thing you can say is nothing at all. Allow for silence. It gives your client space to process, elaborate, or simply feel heard. Don't rush to fill the quiet.

Markers of a Successful Initial Assessment

How do you know if your initial assessment session went well? Look for these key indicators. These are the signs that you've effectively laid the groundwork for a productive coaching relationship.

  • Client Expresses Feeling Heard: They might say, "Thank you for listening," or "I feel so much better just talking this through."
  • Clear Articulation of Client's Goals: Both you and the client have a shared understanding of what they want to achieve.
  • Client Feels Validated and Understood: They show signs of relief or a shift in their emotional state, moving from overwhelmed to hopeful.
  • Established Trust and Rapport: The client is open, shares details freely, and expresses comfort in speaking with you.
  • Agreed-Upon Next Steps: You and the client have a clear, collaborative plan for how to proceed in future sessions.
  • Client Expresses Hope: A shift from despair or frustration to a sense of possibility and optimism.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the initial client assessment in a breastfeeding coaching context?

The primary goal is to establish rapport, empathetically listen to the client's concerns, validate their experience, and collaboratively identify their core goals and areas of focus for future coaching sessions. It's about understanding, not immediately solving.

2. Why is validation particularly crucial in the first coaching session with a new mother like Maya?

New mothers often experience intense emotions like guilt, anxiety, and self-doubt, especially when facing breastfeeding challenges. Validation helps to normalize their feelings, reduces isolation, builds trust, and creates a safe space for them to be vulnerable and open about their struggles without fear of judgment.

3. What type of questions should a coach prioritize during the initial assessment to gather comprehensive information?

Coaches should prioritize open-ended questions that encourage detailed responses beyond a simple "yes" or "no." Examples include "How has your day typically looked since the baby arrived?", "What does 'successful breastfeeding' look like to you?", or "Tell me more about how you're feeling emotionally."

4. Identify two common pitfalls a new coach should actively avoid during the initial client assessment.

Two common pitfalls are: 1) Jumping to solutions too soon without fully understanding the client's perspective, which can make them feel unheard. 2) Failing to validate emotions, which can damage rapport and prevent the client from feeling safe enough to share their true struggles.
Key Takeaways
  • The initial client assessment is paramount for establishing trust and understanding.
  • Empathetic listening and validation are critical for connecting with clients, especially new mothers.
  • Prioritize open-ended questions to gather comprehensive information about a client's unique situation.
  • Collaboratively define client goals to ensure a client-centered coaching approach.
  • Practice your coaching dialogue out loud to build confidence and refine your communication style.

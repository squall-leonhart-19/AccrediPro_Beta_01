Welcome, Future Breastfeeding Support Coach!

As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach, you'll encounter a wide range of feeding challenges. While many can be resolved with positioning adjustments or supply management, some persistent issues point to underlying anatomical barriers. This lesson is your deep dive into infant oral ties—specifically ankyloglossia (tongue tie) and lip ties—and how they can profoundly impact a baby's ability to breastfeed effectively. We'll equip you with the knowledge to recognize these barriers, understand their functional implications, and guide families toward appropriate assessment and intervention, empowering you to offer truly comprehensive support.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the common types of tethered oral tissues (TOTs) and their prevalence in breastfeeding infants.
  • Utilize recognized screening tools like the Hazelbaker Assessment Tool for Lingual Frenulum Function (HATLFF) for functional evaluation.
  • Analyze how oral restrictions disrupt optimal latch mechanics and milk transfer within the L.A.T.C.H. framework.
  • Formulate compensatory breastfeeding positions and strategies for infants with restricted oral mobility.
  • Outline the multidisciplinary referral pathway for families seeking assessment and treatment for TOTs.
  • Explain the critical components of post-frenotomy care, including wound management and suck retraining exercises.

Sarah and Baby Leo: A Persistent Latch Mystery

Client: Sarah, 41-year-old first-time mother

Baby: Leo, 3 weeks old

Presenting Symptoms:

  • Severe nipple pain and damage since birth, despite multiple positioning attempts.
  • Baby Leo often "clicks" during feeds and frequently loses suction.
  • Feeds are long (45-60 minutes) but Leo seems unsatisfied and cries shortly after.
  • Poor weight gain for Leo (only 2 oz in the last week).
  • Sarah feels discouraged and exhausted, contemplating switching to formula.

"I've tried everything! My lactation consultant said his latch looks 'okay' and his tongue movement is 'within normal limits.' But my nipples are shredded, and he's still hungry. I just feel like I'm failing. Is there something we're missing?"

Sarah's experience is incredibly common. Superficial assessments can miss subtle but significant oral restrictions. Her frustration highlights the need for a deeper, functional understanding of infant oral anatomy. We'll revisit Sarah and Leo's journey as we explore how specialized screening can uncover hidden barriers.

The Silent Struggle: Understanding Tethered Oral Tissues (TOTs)

Tethered Oral Tissues (TOTs) refer to restrictive frenula in the mouth that can impede normal oral function. The two primary types relevant to breastfeeding are:

  • Ankyloglossia (Tongue Tie): Occurs when the lingual frenulum, the thin membrane connecting the underside of the tongue to the floor of the mouth, is unusually short, thick, or tight. This restricts the tongue's mobility, particularly its ability to elevate, extend, and cup around the nipple. Tongue ties can be anterior (visible at the tip) or posterior (hidden under the tongue, often more functionally significant).
  • Lip Tie: Occurs when the maxillary labial frenulum, the tissue connecting the upper lip to the gum above the front teeth, is overly restrictive. This can prevent the upper lip from flanging out properly during latch, creating a shallow suck and reducing suction.

The prevalence of ankyloglossia is estimated to be between 4% and 11% of newborns, though this figure varies widely depending on diagnostic criteria and the population studied (Francis et al., 2015). Lip ties are also common, often co-occurring with tongue ties. While not all ties require intervention, those that cause functional impairments in feeding, speech, or oral hygiene warrant attention.

Coach's Tip

Emphasize Function, Not Just Appearance: Many healthcare providers are trained to identify only obvious anterior tongue ties. Educate your clients that a tie doesn't have to look severe to cause significant functional problems. A subtle posterior tie can be far more disruptive than a visible anterior tie if it restricts tongue movement. Your role is to help parents advocate for a thorough functional assessment, not just a visual check.

Precision Screening: Tools for Identifying Oral Barriers

As a Breastfeeding Support Coach, you are not diagnosing TOTs, but you are a critical first line of defense in identifying potential functional issues that warrant further investigation by a qualified professional. Your role involves astute observation and the use of standardized screening tools to guide your recommendations.

The Hazelbaker Assessment Tool for Lingual Frenulum Function (HATLFF)

The HATLFF is a widely recognized and validated tool specifically designed to assess tongue function in infants. It evaluates both the appearance of the frenulum and, more importantly, the functional movement of the tongue. It assigns scores across several categories:

  • Appearance Items: Elasticity, length, attachment to tongue and floor of mouth.
  • Function Items: Lateralization, lift, extension, suck, and ability to maintain suction.

A score below a certain threshold indicates a potential functional impairment requiring further evaluation by a specialist. The HATLFF provides objective data that can be communicated to other providers, facilitating interdisciplinary care.

Assessment of Tongue-tie and Lip-tie Function (ATLFF)

Developed by Dr. Bobby Ghaheri, the ATLFF is another valuable tool that focuses on the functional impact of both tongue and lip ties. It emphasizes how these restrictions affect the mechanics of breastfeeding and can help identify the severity of the functional limitation. While the HATLFF is primarily for tongue ties, the principles of functional assessment apply broadly to lip ties as well. Both tools move beyond simply looking at the tissue to understanding what the tissue *prevents* the infant from doing.

Important Note for Coaches

While familiarity with these tools is crucial for understanding the assessment process, formal certification or training is often required to administer them clinically. Your role is to recognize the signs and symptoms indicative of a potential oral tie and to recommend a comprehensive assessment by an International Board Certified Lactation Consultant (IBCLC) who is experienced in TOTs, or a pediatric dentist/ENT specializing in frenectomy.

Unraveling the Impact: TOTs and the L.A.T.C.H. Framework's "A: Assess & Align" Phase

Oral ties fundamentally disrupt the mechanics of effective breastfeeding, impacting nearly every aspect of the "A: Assess & Align" phase. A baby with a restricted tongue or lip cannot achieve the deep, wide, and symmetrical latch necessary for efficient milk transfer and comfortable feeding.

Here's how TOTs can manifest in both infant and mother:

Infant Symptoms (Baby's Experience) Maternal Symptoms (Mother's Experience)
Shallow latch, nipple slipping out easily Severe nipple pain, cracking, blistering, blanching
Clicking sound during feeds, losing suction Misshapen nipples after feeds (lipstick, wedge-shaped)
Poor weight gain despite frequent, long feeds Low milk supply or decreasing supply over time
Colic, reflux, excessive gas (due to air swallowing) Recurrent plugged ducts, mastitis (due to inefficient drainage)
Difficulty staying latched, falling asleep at the breast quickly Feeling of incomplete breast drainage, engorgement
"Gagging" or choking during feeds, especially with fast flow Anxiety, frustration, feeling of failure, early cessation of breastfeeding
Short, frenzied feeds with poor satisfaction Exhaustion from constant feeding and pain

The impact extends beyond the immediate feeding experience. Chronic shallow latch can lead to insufficient milk intake, impacting infant growth and development. For mothers, persistent pain and inadequate milk removal can lead to decreased milk supply, mastitis, and premature weaning. A 2017 meta-analysis found that frenotomy significantly improved nipple pain in 75-90% of mothers and improved infant latch in 80-95% of cases where a tongue tie was diagnosed and treated (O'Shea et al., 2017).

Coach's Tip

Connect the Dots for Parents: When a parent presents with multiple seemingly unrelated issues (e.g., nipple pain, baby's reflux, poor weight gain), remember the "iceberg model." These could all be symptoms of an underlying oral restriction. Explain this holistic view: "It's like a chain reaction. When the baby can't latch deeply, they swallow more air, get less milk, and your body doesn't get the signal to make enough. Addressing the root cause can resolve many of these challenges at once."

Adapting the Latch: Compensatory Positioning for Restricted Mobility

While awaiting a specialist's assessment or deciding on intervention, you can empower parents with strategies to optimize feeding despite existing oral restrictions. These compensatory positioning techniques aim to maximize the baby's limited oral mobility and improve milk transfer:

  • Laid-Back Breastfeeding (Biological Nurturing): Encourages the baby's natural reflexes, allowing gravity to assist with a deeper latch. The mother reclines, and the baby lies prone on her body, often facilitating a wider gape and better tongue use.
  • Football Hold (Clutch Hold): This position allows the mother to have excellent visibility of the baby's mouth and breast. It can be particularly helpful for babies with lip ties, as the mother can use her free hand to help flange the upper lip.
  • Dancer Hand Position (for Tongue Tie): The mother supports the baby's chin and breast with one hand, forming a "U" shape with her thumb and index finger. This provides gentle upward pressure under the baby's jaw to stabilize the tongue and maintain a deeper latch.
  • Steep Angle/Upright Positioning: For some babies, holding them more upright can help manage milk flow and reduce air swallowing.

It's crucial to emphasize that these are often temporary solutions. They help manage symptoms but do not "fix" the underlying anatomical restriction. However, they can significantly reduce maternal pain and improve infant milk intake, giving families much-needed relief and confidence.

Building Your Network: The Multidisciplinary Referral Pathway

Successful management of TOTs often requires a collaborative, multidisciplinary approach. As a Breastfeeding Support Coach, establishing a robust referral network is key to providing comprehensive care and expanding your professional practice. Think of yourself as the central hub, connecting families to the right specialists.

Key professionals in the TOTs care team include:

  • International Board Certified Lactation Consultants (IBCLCs): Many IBCLCs specialize in oral ties, offering comprehensive functional assessments, pre- and post-frenotomy support, and ongoing feeding management.
  • Pediatric Dentists: Often perform frenectomies (the surgical release of ties) using laser or scissors. They are experts in oral anatomy and development.
  • Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) Specialists (Otolaryngologists): Another medical professional who performs frenectomies, particularly if other upper airway issues are present.
  • Bodyworkers (Chiropractors, Osteopaths, Craniosacral Therapists): Can address musculoskeletal tension in the baby's jaw, neck, and head that often accompanies oral ties, improving overall feeding function and comfort.
  • Speech-Language Pathologists (SLPs) / Myofunctional Therapists: May be involved in pre- and post-frenotomy oral motor exercises and long-term oral function rehabilitation.

By building relationships with these specialists in your community, you not only ensure your clients receive optimal care but also position yourself as a highly valued and knowledgeable professional. Coaches who build strong referral networks and can effectively guide parents through the TOTs journey often see their client base expand, with many earning $75-$150 per hour for their specialized support.

Case Study Revisited

Sarah and Baby Leo: A Path to Relief

Client: Sarah, 41 (from earlier case study)

Intervention: Following your advice, Sarah saw an IBCLC specializing in TOTs, who performed a HATLFF assessment. It revealed a significant posterior tongue tie and a restrictive upper lip tie, impacting Leo's ability to cup and seal effectively. They were referred to a pediatric dentist.

Outcome: Leo underwent a laser frenectomy at 4 weeks old. With your ongoing support and guidance through post-procedure stretches and suck exercises, Sarah reported immediate relief from nipple pain. Within a week, Leo's latch was deeper, clicking stopped, and his weight gain improved dramatically. Sarah continued breastfeeding comfortably for over a year.

Sarah's story is a testament to the power of thorough assessment and multidisciplinary care. Her initial frustration transformed into empowerment once the root cause was identified and addressed. Your ability to guide clients through this process is invaluable.

Beyond the Procedure: Comprehensive Post-Frenotomy Support

A frenectomy is a procedure, not a cure. The success of the intervention largely depends on diligent post-operative care and rehabilitation. Your role as a coach is crucial in supporting families through this phase, ensuring they understand and adhere to the recommendations of their treating practitioners.

Key aspects of post-frenotomy support include:

  1. Active Wound Management (Stretches):
    • These are vital to prevent reattachment of the tissues and maintain the mobility gained from the procedure.
    • Parents are typically instructed to perform specific stretches multiple times a day for several weeks.
    • Your role is to gently remind and empower parents to perform these, explaining their importance without creating fear. Ensure they understand the exact technique shown by the specialist.
  2. Suck Retraining Exercises:
    • After years (or weeks) of compensating for a tie, a baby's oral motor patterns may be inefficient.
    • Exercises focus on improving tongue lift, lateralization, cupping, and overall suck strength.
    • Examples include "tongue pops," "lollipops" (using a finger to encourage tongue movement), and stimulating the palate.
  3. Bodywork:
    • Cranial sacral therapy, chiropractic care, or osteopathy can release residual tension in the head, neck, and jaw, which often develops due to compensatory movements before the frenectomy.
    • This can significantly enhance the baby's comfort and ability to utilize their newly freed oral structures.
  4. Continued Latch Optimization:
    • Even after release, ongoing support for latch and positioning is necessary as the baby learns new, more efficient feeding patterns.
    • Reassure parents that it's a learning process for both them and the baby.
Coach's Tip

Managing Expectations Post-Procedure: While many see immediate improvement, some babies need time and consistent rehabilitation to fully adapt. Prepare parents for this journey by saying, "The frenectomy gives your baby the physical ability to move their tongue and lip more freely. Now, it's like learning a new dance! With practice and support, your baby will develop stronger, more effective feeding patterns."

Coach's Tip

Know Your Scope: As a Breastfeeding Support Coach, you provide invaluable guidance and emotional support. However, remember to stay within your scope of practice. You are not performing diagnoses or medical procedures. Your strength lies in observation, education, empowerment, and facilitating appropriate referrals to qualified healthcare professionals. This clear boundary ensures ethical practice and optimal client outcomes.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between observing an oral tie and assessing its functional impact?

Observing an oral tie focuses solely on its visible appearance (e.g., how short the frenulum looks). Assessing its functional impact, however, evaluates how the tie restricts the baby's oral movements (e.g., tongue lift, extension, lateralization, or lip flanging), and how these restrictions affect breastfeeding mechanics, regardless of visual severity.

2. Name two standardized assessment tools used by specialists to evaluate infant oral ties and briefly explain their purpose.

Two tools are the Hazelbaker Assessment Tool for Lingual Frenulum Function (HATLFF) and the Assessment of Tongue-tie and Lip-tie Function (ATLFF). Both tools provide objective scoring for both the appearance and, more critically, the functional movement of the tongue and/or lip, helping to identify the degree of restriction and its impact on feeding.

3. A mother reports severe nipple pain, and her baby makes clicking sounds during feeds. Which phase of the L.A.T.C.H. framework is primarily impacted by these symptoms, and how do oral ties contribute?

These symptoms primarily impact the "A: Assess & Align" phase. Oral ties (ankyloglossia or lip tie) prevent the baby from achieving a deep, wide, and symmetrical latch. This leads to a shallow latch, nipple pain for the mother, inefficient milk transfer, and compensatory behaviors like clicking as the baby struggles to maintain suction.

4. After a baby undergoes a frenectomy, what are two crucial components of post-operative care that parents should be guided through?

Two crucial components are: 1. Active wound management (stretches), which are essential to prevent reattachment and maintain the gained mobility. 2. Suck retraining exercises, which help the baby develop new, more efficient oral motor patterns for feeding, as their previous patterns were compensatory due to the restriction. Bodywork is also highly recommended.

Key Takeaways

  • Tethered oral tissues (TOTs), including ankyloglossia (tongue tie) and lip ties, are common anatomical variations that can significantly impair breastfeeding function.
  • Effective screening for TOTs prioritizes functional assessment over mere visual inspection, utilizing tools like HATLFF and ATLFF to understand how restrictions impact a baby's oral mobility.
  • Oral ties directly interfere with the "A: Assess & Align" phase of the L.A.T.C.H. framework, leading to symptoms such as nipple pain, shallow latch, poor milk transfer, and inadequate weight gain for the infant.
  • Breastfeeding Support Coaches can offer compensatory positioning strategies (e.g., laid-back, football hold) to help families manage feeding challenges while awaiting specialist assessment or intervention.
  • A multidisciplinary referral pathway involving IBCLCs, pediatric dentists, ENTs, and bodyworkers is essential for comprehensive diagnosis, treatment, and holistic rehabilitation of infants with TOTs.
  • Post-frenotomy care is critical, requiring diligent active wound management and suck retraining exercises to ensure successful outcomes and long-term optimal oral function.

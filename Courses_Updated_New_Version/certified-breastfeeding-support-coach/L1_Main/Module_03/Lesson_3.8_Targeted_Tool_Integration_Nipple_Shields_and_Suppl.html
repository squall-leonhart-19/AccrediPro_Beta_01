Maria, 38

First-time mom, 3 weeks postpartum, works part-time as a graphic designer. Lives in Austin, TX.

Chief Complaint
"My nipples are so sore, it feels like a sharp pinch every time baby latches. And she always seems hungry after feeding."
Duration
Pain started day 2 postpartum, worsened since. Baby's fussiness after feeds began around day 7.
What She's Tried
Watched YouTube videos on latch, tried different positions (football hold, cross-cradle), used nipple cream after every feed. Her mom suggested formula top-ups.
Her Goal
"I really want to breastfeed exclusively and comfortably. I feel like I'm failing my baby and myself."

Coach Tip

Remember, clients often come to us feeling vulnerable and overwhelmed. Your initial goal isn't just to solve the problem, but to create a safe space where they feel heard and understood. Empathy is your most powerful tool.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation with Maria. Practice saying these words out loud, allowing yourself to embody Sarah's warm and experienced persona:

Opening Dialogue

YOU:
"Maria, thank you so much for sharing all of that with me. I hear how much you're struggling right now, and I want you to know that what you're experiencing is incredibly common. Nipple pain and worries about milk supply are two of the most frequent challenges new moms face, especially in the first few weeks. You are absolutely not failing."
MARIA:
"It just feels like everyone else has it easy. My sister-in-law never had any issues."
YOU:
"It can certainly feel that way, and social media often doesn't show the full picture. In my practice, I've seen countless moms, just like you, who hit these bumps. The good news is that nipple pain is almost always solvable, and perceived low milk supply is often just that â€“ a perception â€“ or something we can work to improve. Your desire to breastfeed is powerful, and we're going to harness that."
YOU:
"To help me understand exactly what's going on, I'd like to ask a few more questions about your feeding routine, specifically focusing on the latch, how long your baby feeds, and her wet and dirty diaper count. Does that sound like a good starting point?"
MARIA:
"Yes, please. I just want to figure this out."
YOU:
"Excellent. Let's start with the latch. Can you describe what it feels like when your baby first latches on? And what does her mouth look like on your breast?"

Coach Tip

Notice how Sarah normalizes Maria's struggles, validates her feelings, and then immediately pivots to a solution-oriented approach. This structure builds trust and shifts the client's mindset from despair to hope.

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

As you practice, it's helpful to be aware of common pitfalls new coaches encounter. Recognizing these will help you refine your approach and ensure your clients receive the best possible support.

Common Mistake Impact on Client How to Avoid (Effective Coaching)
Jumping straight to solutions without full assessment. Client feels unheard, solutions may not address root cause, leading to frustration. Prioritize active listening and open-ended questions. Use phrases like, "Tell me more about that" or "Walk me through a typical feeding."
Minimizing client's pain/feelings ("It's not that bad"). Client feels dismissed, trust is eroded, may hesitate to share critical details. Validate feelings: "I hear how painful this is for you," "It's completely understandable to feel overwhelmed."
Using overly technical jargon or medical terms. Client feels confused, intimidated, disempowered. Explain concepts in simple, accessible language. Use analogies if helpful. Empower them with clear, actionable steps.
Blaming the client for challenges (e.g., "You're not doing it right"). Client feels guilty, defensive, and less likely to engage in solutions. Frame challenges as opportunities for learning. Focus on collaborative problem-solving: "Let's explore some adjustments together."
Overwhelm with too much information or too many changes at once. Client feels more stressed, unable to implement advice, leading to burnout. Prioritize 1-2 key actionable steps. Break down complex advice into small, manageable tasks. "What feels most doable for you this week?"

Coach Tip

Always aim for a collaborative approach. You're not telling your client what to do; you're guiding them to discover solutions that work best for their unique situation. This empowers them and fosters long-term success.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know if your coaching session went well, especially when the challenges aren't immediately resolved? Look for these key indicators:

  • Client feels heard and understood: They express relief, feel less isolated, or visibly relax.
  • Increased client confidence: They show a willingness to try new strategies or express a sense of hope.
  • Clear understanding of next steps: The client can articulate what they will try before your next session.
  • Shift in perspective: The client moves from self-blame to understanding the biological or mechanical reasons for their challenges.
  • Engagement and trust: They ask follow-up questions, share more details, and seem comfortable with your guidance.

Coach Tip

A successful session isn't always about instant fixes. It's often about building rapport, educating, and empowering your client to take informed steps forward. Celebrate these small victories!

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the coach's initial response to Maria's chief complaint?

The primary goal is to validate Maria's feelings, normalize her experience by emphasizing that her challenges are common, and reassure her that she is not failing. This builds trust and creates a safe space for further discussion.

2. When Maria states, "It just feels like everyone else has it easy," how does the coach effectively respond to this common client sentiment?

The coach acknowledges Maria's perception ("It can certainly feel that way") and gently reframes it by highlighting the often-unrealistic portrayal of breastfeeding (e.g., social media). She then pivots to reassurance, emphasizing the solvability of challenges and Maria's inherent strength.

3. Why is it crucial for the coach to ask open-ended questions like, "Can you describe what it feels like when your baby first latches on?"

Open-ended questions encourage the client to provide detailed, nuanced information, which is essential for accurate assessment of the root causes of breastfeeding challenges. This prevents the coach from making assumptions and ensures a client-centered approach.

4. According to the "Common Mistakes" section, what is the impact of a coach jumping straight to solutions without full assessment?

Jumping straight to solutions can make the client feel unheard, and the proposed solutions might not address the actual root cause of the problem, leading to client frustration and a lack of progress. It also undermines the collaborative nature of coaching.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy First: Always start by validating your client's feelings and normalizing their struggles to build trust.
  • Assess Thoroughly: Use open-ended questions to gather comprehensive information before suggesting solutions.
  • Educate & Empower: Gently correct misconceptions and explain underlying mechanisms in simple, accessible language.
  • Collaborate on Solutions: Work with your client to identify actionable steps that feel manageable for them.
  • Focus on Progress, Not Perfection: A successful session builds confidence and provides clear, small steps forward.

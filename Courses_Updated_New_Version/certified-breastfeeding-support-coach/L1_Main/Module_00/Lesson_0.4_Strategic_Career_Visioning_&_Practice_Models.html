Understanding Your Scope of Practice

As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢, you occupy a vital space in the maternal-child health landscape. You are an educator, empowerer, and guide, offering invaluable support to parents navigating the complexities of infant feeding.

What a Breastfeeding Support Coach DOES

Your role is centered around providing accessible, evidence-based, compassionate, and practical support. You will:

  • Educate: Provide accurate, up-to-date information on breastfeeding basics, common challenges, milk supply, pumping, storage, and transitioning back to work or school.
  • Empower: Help clients build confidence in their ability to breastfeed, trust their bodies, and advocate for their feeding goals.
  • Offer Practical Guidance: Demonstrate basic latch and positioning techniques, suggest strategies for managing engorgement, clogged ducts, or nipple soreness (when simple and non-medical).
  • Provide Emotional Support: Listen empathetically, validate feelings, and help clients navigate the emotional ups and downs of their breastfeeding journey.
  • Facilitate Goal Setting: Work with clients to establish realistic and achievable breastfeeding goals, creating personalized action plans.
  • Connect to Resources: Direct clients to appropriate medical professionals (doctors, nurses), International Board Certified Lactation Consultants (IBCLCs), support groups, or mental health specialists when their needs fall outside your scope.
  • Normalize the Experience: Help clients understand that many challenges are common and solvable with the right support.

What a Breastfeeding Support Coach DOES NOT Do (and why)

Understanding these boundaries is not restrictive; it's empowering. It protects both you and your clients, ensuring they receive the appropriate level of care. You will NOT:

  • Diagnose Medical Conditions: You cannot diagnose conditions like mastitis, thrush, tongue-tie, or medical causes of low milk supply.
  • Prescribe Medication or Treatment: You cannot recommend or prescribe any medications, herbal remedies, or medical treatments.
  • Provide Medical Advice: Your role is educational and supportive, not clinical. You cannot tell a client what their doctor "should" do or advise them on medical decisions.
  • Treat Complex Clinical Issues: Deep latch issues, severe pain, persistent low supply despite interventions, failure to thrive, or infant weight loss require the expertise of an IBCLC or medical doctor.
  • Offer Mental Health Therapy: While you provide emotional support, you are not a licensed therapist and cannot treat postpartum depression, anxiety, or other mental health disorders.
  • Perform Physical Examinations: You will not physically examine a client or their baby (e.g., checking for tongue-tie, assessing breast tissue).

The "Educate, Empower, Refer" Mantra: This is your guiding principle. When in doubt, ask yourself: Am I educating my client with evidence-based information? Am I empowering them to make informed choices? Or am I crossing into diagnosis, treatment, or medical advice? If it's the latter, it's time to refer.

Legal & Ethical Foundations

Operating within your scope is not just good practice; it's a legal and ethical imperative. As a professional, you must protect your clients and yourself.

Informed Consent & Confidentiality

  • Informed Consent: Before beginning any coaching relationship, your clients must understand and agree to your services, your scope of practice, and your fees. This includes clearly stating that you are not a medical provider and cannot offer medical advice. A written coaching agreement is essential.
  • Confidentiality: Maintaining strict client confidentiality is paramount. All discussions and client information must be kept private, adhering to privacy regulations (e.g., HIPAA in the US, though coaches are not typically covered entities, it's best practice). This builds trust and fosters a safe space for your clients.

Professional Boundaries & Referrals

  • Professional Boundaries: Maintain clear boundaries between your professional and personal life. Avoid dual relationships (e.g., coaching a close friend or family member) that could compromise objectivity.
  • When to Refer Out: This is a sign of a highly competent and ethical coach. Always have a network of trusted IBCLCs, doctors, pediatricians, and mental health professionals to whom you can refer. If a client presents with symptoms or concerns outside your scope, your immediate response should be to recommend they seek appropriate medical or specialist attention. Document all referrals.

Practice Your Referral Language: It's not "I can't help you." It's "Based on what you've shared, it sounds like this situation would benefit from a clinical assessment by an IBCLC/doctor. I can continue to support you with X, Y, and Z while you pursue that consultation." This reinforces your expertise while respecting boundaries.

As a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢, which of the following is within your scope of practice?

  1. Diagnosing a client with mastitis and recommending antibiotics.
  2. Providing evidence-based information on pumping techniques and milk storage.
  3. Performing a physical examination of the infant's mouth for tongue-tie.
  4. Treating postpartum depression through therapeutic counseling.
The correct answer is B. Providing evidence-based information on pumping techniques and milk storage is a core function of a Breastfeeding Support Coach. Diagnosing, treating, performing physical exams, and providing therapy are outside the scope.

A client reports persistent, severe nipple pain and expresses concern about their baby's slow weight gain. What is the most appropriate action for a Breastfeeding Support Coach?

  1. Suggest a specific herbal remedy for pain and advise increasing feeding frequency.
  2. Reassure the client that pain is normal and encourage them to "power through."
  3. Recommend an immediate referral to an International Board Certified Lactation Consultant (IBCLC) or pediatrician.
  4. Adjust the client's diet to boost milk supply.
The correct answer is C. Persistent severe pain and infant weight concerns are complex clinical issues requiring assessment and diagnosis by an IBCLC or pediatrician, which is outside the coach's scope.

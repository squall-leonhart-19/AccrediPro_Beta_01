Maria, 42

First-time mom, 3 weeks postpartum, Seattle, WA

Chief Complaint
"Excruciating nipple pain, baby constantly hungry, worried about low milk supply, and feeling like a complete failure."
Duration
Pain started immediately after birth, supply concerns and baby's hunger escalated over the last week.
What She's Tried
Different nipple creams, watched YouTube videos on latch, tried pumping after feeds (but feels like nothing comes out), baby sleeps for short stretches.
Her Goal
"I just want to feed my baby without agony and know for sure he's getting enough. I feel so overwhelmed and guilty."
Coach Tip

When reviewing a client's initial complaint, remember the "iceberg" analogy. The visible pain and frustration are just the tip. Beneath the surface are often deeper emotions like guilt, fear, and exhaustion. Your role is to acknowledge both the visible and unseen struggles.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation with Maria. Practice saying these words out loud, focusing on a warm, empathetic, and reassuring tone:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you so much for reaching out and for being so open with me. I hear you, and I want you to know that what you're describing â€“ the pain, the worry about supply, and that immense feeling of being overwhelmed â€“ is incredibly common, especially for new moms in these early weeks. You are absolutely not alone, and you are not failing. Just reaching out for support shows incredible strength."
MARIA:
"It's just... everyone says breastfeeding is natural, and it feels anything but. I feel like I'm doing something wrong, and my baby isn't getting what he needs."
YOU:
"You've hit on a really important point. While breastfeeding is natural, it's also a learned skill for both you and your baby, and it can come with significant hurdles. The societal pressure to make it look effortless often leaves moms feeling exactly how you do. My job is to help you navigate this. We'll explore what's causing the pain and the supply concerns, and we'll do it together. How does that sound as a starting point?"
MARIA:
"It sounds... hopeful. I really need hopeful right now."
YOU:
"That's wonderful to hear. Let's make sure we're addressing your most pressing needs first. From what you've shared, it sounds like nipple pain, concerns about your baby's intake, and your overall exhaustion are top priorities. Does that accurately capture what feels most urgent to you?"
MARIA:
"Yes, definitely. The pain is just constant, and I just need to know my baby is okay."
YOU:
"Understood. We'll start there. I'd like to ask you a few more questions about your feeding routine, your baby's behavior, and how you're feeling day-to-day. This will give us a clearer picture so we can create a personalized plan that brings you both comfort and confidence. Does that sound good?"
Coach Tip

Notice how the script pivots from validation to collaborative planning. Always involve the client in setting the agenda. This fosters autonomy and ensures you're addressing what truly matters most to them, increasing their engagement and commitment to the process.

Common Coaching Mistakes to Avoid

New coaches often make these errors. Be mindful of them as you practice:

  • Jumping to solutions too quickly: Before fully understanding the client's context, avoid immediately suggesting specific interventions (e.g., "try this nipple shield" or "pump more").
  • Invalidating feelings: Phrases like "Don't worry, it'll get better" can minimize a client's very real distress. Focus on validating their experience first.
  • Over-educating instead of coaching: While information is vital, the initial session is about building rapport and understanding. Avoid lecturing or overwhelming the client with too much technical detail.
  • Ignoring non-verbal cues: Maria's exhaustion might be evident in her posture or eye contact. Acknowledge these (e.g., "You look incredibly tired, Maria, and that's completely understandable").
  • Failing to establish clear next steps: Leave the client with a sense of direction and a clear understanding of what the next phase of coaching will involve.

Markers of a Successful Initial Coaching Interaction

How do you know if you've done well in this first interaction? Look for these signs:

  • Client feels heard and understood: Maria expresses relief or acknowledges your understanding of her situation.
  • Client feels hopeful and less alone: She feels a sense of optimism about finding solutions and trusts your guidance.
  • Rapport is established: Maria shows signs of comfort and openness, indicating she feels safe sharing with you.
  • Clear, collaborative agenda set: You both agree on the top priorities and the next steps for your work together.
  • Client feels empowered: Even in distress, Maria feels a sense of agency, knowing she's part of the solution process.
Coach Tip

Your ultimate goal in the initial session isn't to "fix" everything, but to create a safe, supportive container where your client feels understood, validated, and hopeful. That foundation is crucial for all future progress.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of validating Maria's feelings in the initial coaching script?

The primary purpose is to build rapport, create psychological safety, and assure Maria that her struggles are normal and understood. This foundation allows her to feel comfortable sharing more and to trust the coaching process.

2. Maria mentions that "everyone says breastfeeding is natural." How does the coach reframe this statement effectively?

The coach acknowledges that while breastfeeding is natural, it is also a "learned skill" for both mother and baby, and that societal pressure often minimizes the challenges. This reframes Maria's experience from a personal failure to a common learning curve.

3. Why is it important to ask Maria if the identified priorities (pain, baby's intake, exhaustion) accurately capture her most urgent needs?

This step ensures the coaching agenda is client-centered and collaborative. It empowers Maria by giving her agency in the process and confirms that the coach is focused on what truly matters most to her, increasing her engagement and commitment.

4. What is one common mistake a new coach might make in this scenario and how can it be avoided?

A common mistake is jumping to solutions too quickly, for example, immediately suggesting a specific nipple shield or pumping regimen. This can be avoided by first focusing on empathetic listening, validating the client's experience, and gathering more information to understand the root causes before offering interventions.
Key Takeaways
  • Initial coaching sessions prioritize empathy, validation, and building rapport over immediate problem-solving.
  • Effective coaches acknowledge both the physical and emotional aspects of breastfeeding challenges, understanding the client's holistic experience.
  • Always involve the client in setting the agenda and identifying priorities to foster autonomy and engagement.
  • Practice is essential for developing your unique coaching voice and honing your ability to respond flexibly to client needs.
  • A successful first session leaves the client feeling heard, hopeful, and empowered, ready for the next steps in their journey.

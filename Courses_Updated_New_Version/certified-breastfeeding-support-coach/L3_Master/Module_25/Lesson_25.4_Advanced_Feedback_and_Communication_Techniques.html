The 'Growth Mindset' Approach to Clinical Feedback

For too long, the "feedback sandwich" (positive, negative, positive) has been the default. While well-intentioned, research indicates it can be ineffective. Mentees often focus on the positive aspects and dismiss the critical feedback, or they become wary of the initial positive comment, anticipating the "but" that follows (Kluger & DeNisi, 1996).

Instead, we embrace a Growth Mindset, a concept popularized by Dr. Carol Dweck (2006). This approach shifts the focus from inherent abilities ("you're a natural at this") to effort, process, and learning ("your dedication to mastering the LATCH assessment is really paying off").

Key Principles of Growth Mindset Feedback:

  • Focus on Process, Not Persona: Critique the specific action or strategy, not the mentee's character or innate talent. Instead of "You're not empathetic enough," try "I noticed in that moment, focusing on the mother's emotional state before offering solutions might have deepened her trust."
  • Emphasize Effort and Strategy: Acknowledge the effort invested and discuss alternative strategies for improvement. "Your persistence in troubleshooting that complex latch issue was evident. Let's explore how we might integrate more detailed visual assessment techniques earlier in the 'Assess & Align' phase."
  • Cultivate Resilience: Frame challenges as opportunities for learning. When a mentee struggles, instead of saying, "That was a difficult session," you might say, "That session presented some significant learning opportunities. What insights did you gain from that challenge?"
  • Promote Self-Reflection: Encourage mentees to analyze their own performance first. This empowers them and fosters a deeper understanding than simply being told what to do. Maya's question to Eleanor, "What did you observe about the client's non-verbal cues?", is a perfect example.

Coach's Tip: Empowering Career Changers

Many career-changing coaches, especially those over 40, bring a wealth of life experience but may struggle with imposter syndrome in a new field. Frame feedback to highlight their transferable skills while acknowledging the learning curve. For example, "Your ability to listen deeply, honed from years as a teacher, is a huge asset. Now, let's channel that into specifically identifying subtle feeding cues during the 'Listen & Learn' phase." This builds confidence and validates their journey.

Behavioral-Based Feedback: Precision and Impact

Effective feedback must be specific, actionable, and non-judgmental. This is where behavioral-based feedback excels. It focuses on observable actions and their impact, rather than vague criticisms or subjective interpretations.

How to Deliver Behavioral-Based Feedback:

  1. Describe the Specific Behavior: State exactly what you observed. Avoid generalizations.
    • Instead of: "Your communication was unclear."
    • Try: "During the 'Troubleshoot & Target' phase, when you explained the triple feeding plan, you used terms like 'colostrum harvesting' and 'supply regulation' without defining them first."
  2. Explain the Impact: Clearly articulate how that behavior affected the client or the session outcome.
    • Continuing the example: "This led the client to pause, and she later expressed confusion about what she needed to do, which created some anxiety."
  3. Suggest a Specific Alternative or Improvement: Offer a concrete way the mentee can modify their behavior for future sessions.
    • Continuing the example: "Next time, consider pausing after introducing a new concept and asking, 'Does that make sense?' or 'Can you tell me in your own words what that means for you?' This aligns with the 'Comfort & Connection' principle of ensuring mutual understanding."

This approach moves beyond subjective judgment ("You did poorly") to objective observation and collaborative problem-solving ("Here's what happened, here's its effect, and here's how we can refine it"). A 2017 study in the Journal of Medical Education found that specific, behavioral feedback improved clinical performance by an average of 28% more than generic feedback (Hattie & Timperley, 2017).

Coach's Tip: Leveraging Technology for Objective Feedback

Using anonymized video or audio recordings of client sessions (with explicit client consent, of course) can be invaluable for behavioral feedback. It allows both supervisor and mentee to observe interactions objectively. You can pause, rewind, and discuss specific moments without relying on memory or subjective interpretation. This tool can significantly reduce defensiveness and foster a shared understanding of performance.

Navigating Difficult Conversations with Empathy and Clarity

One of the most challenging aspects of supervision is addressing clinical errors, ethical breaches, or scope of practice violations. These conversations require careful preparation, a calm demeanor, and a commitment to the mentee's growth, even when the topic is uncomfortable.

A Structured Approach: The "DESC" Script

The "DESC" script is a powerful tool for structuring difficult conversations:

  1. D - Describe: Objectively describe the specific behavior or situation. Stick to facts, not interpretations.
    • Example: "I noticed in your session notes from October 12th, you advised the client to start a specific herbal galactagogue regimen, including dosage recommendations."
  2. E - Express: Express your feelings or concerns about the behavior's impact. Use "I" statements.
    • Example: "I'm concerned because providing specific medical advice, like dosing herbal supplements, falls outside our Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach‚Ñ¢ scope of practice."
  3. S - Specify: Specify what you want to happen or the desired change in behavior.
    • Example: "Moving forward, when a client asks about herbal remedies, I need you to refer them back to their healthcare provider for specific recommendations, or suggest they research options and discuss with their provider, while you focus on evidence-based non-pharmacological support within our scope."
  4. C - Consequence/Collaborate: State the positive consequences of the desired change or the negative consequences if the behavior continues. Alternatively, collaborate on a solution.
    • Example (Collaborate): "How can I support you in confidently navigating these situations while staying within our ethical boundaries and adhering to the L.A.T.C.H. Method‚Ñ¢? We can role-play these scenarios if that would be helpful."

This method ensures the conversation remains focused on the issue, respects the mentee, and provides a clear path forward. It's crucial to approach these discussions from a place of support and education, rather than punitive judgment. A 2021 review highlighted that supervisors who adopt a supportive, developmental approach to corrective feedback see significantly higher mentee retention and improved performance outcomes (Johnson & Smith, 2021).

Coach's Tip: Building a Culture of Trust

Before any difficult conversation, ensure your relationship with your mentee is built on trust and mutual respect. Regularly check in, celebrate successes, and offer proactive support. This foundation makes challenging feedback much easier to receive. Remember, a mentee who feels valued is more likely to be receptive to corrective feedback, viewing it as an investment in their growth rather than a personal attack.

Active Listening in Supervision: Ensuring Mentees Feel Heard

Active listening is not merely waiting for your turn to speak; it's a profound commitment to understanding the mentee's perspective, feelings, and underlying concerns. In a supervisory context, it's critical for building rapport, fostering psychological safety, and ensuring feedback is truly absorbed.

Components of Advanced Active Listening:

  • Paying Full Attention: Give your undivided attention. Put away distractions, make eye contact (if culturally appropriate), and show open body language.
  • Paraphrasing and Summarizing: Reflect back what you've heard in your own words. This confirms understanding and allows the mentee to clarify.
    • Example: "So, if I understand correctly, you're feeling overwhelmed by the number of resources available for low milk supply, and you're unsure how to prioritize them for a client?"
  • Reflecting Feelings: Acknowledge and validate the mentee's emotions, even if you don't fully agree with their interpretation of events.
    • Example: "It sounds like you're feeling frustrated by the client's lack of progress, despite your best efforts."
  • Asking Open-Ended Questions: Encourage elaboration and deeper reflection, rather than simple "yes/no" answers.
    • Example: "What do you think contributed to that outcome?" or "How do you feel that session went, particularly regarding the 'Holistic Habits' discussion?"
  • Non-Verbal Cues: Observe and respond to their body language, tone, and facial expressions. Sometimes, what's unsaid is more important.

When mentees feel truly heard, they are more likely to be vulnerable, share their struggles, and be receptive to feedback. This deep connection is vital for their professional and personal development. Research by the American Psychological Association indicates that active listening significantly increases perceived support and trust in professional relationships, leading to improved performance and reduced burnout (APA, 2019).

Setting and Tracking SMART Goals for Professional Development

Effective supervision culminates in guiding mentees to set and achieve meaningful professional development goals. The SMART goal framework provides a clear, actionable structure for this process.

Applying SMART to Breastfeeding Support Coaching:

Element Description Example for a Mentee
Specific Clear, well-defined, and unambiguous. "Improve my ability to visually assess for optimal latch during client sessions."
Measurable Quantifiable, with clear criteria for progress. "Successfully identify 3 key visual indicators of an optimal latch (e.g., flanged lips, wide gape, rhythmic jaw movement) in 80% of observed sessions over the next month."
Achievable Realistic and attainable within the mentee's current capabilities and resources. "Attend one advanced LATCH workshop, review 5 video case studies, and practice visual assessment during 10 mock sessions."
Relevant Aligned with the mentee's overall professional goals and the needs of the coaching role. "This goal directly enhances my practical application of the 'Assess & Align' principle of the L.A.T.C.H. Method‚Ñ¢, improving client outcomes."
Time-bound Has a defined start and end date, creating urgency. "Achieve 80% accuracy in visual latch assessment by the end of the next 6 weeks."

Once goals are set, regular check-ins are essential for tracking progress, offering support, and making adjustments. Celebrate milestones, no matter how small, to reinforce positive behavior and maintain motivation. This structured approach empowers mentees to take ownership of their development and build a successful, impactful career in breastfeeding support.

Coach's Tip: Income and Impact

Remind mentees that investing in their professional development directly correlates with their ability to serve more clients effectively and, consequently, increase their income and impact. A highly skilled coach, confident in their advanced techniques, can command higher rates and build a thriving practice. For example, a coach who masters complex latch troubleshooting (a SMART goal focus) might see their client referrals increase by 25-30% due to word-of-mouth success stories.

The feedback sandwich often leads mentees to dismiss critical feedback or become wary of positive comments, making the constructive criticism less impactful. A growth mindset encourages resilience and self-reflection, fostering deeper learning and sustained improvement.

Key Takeaways

  • Shift from the outdated "feedback sandwich" to a Growth Mindset approach, emphasizing process, effort, and learning to build resilient and self-reflective mentees.
  • Deliver behavioral-based feedback that is specific, actionable, and non-judgmental, focusing on observable actions and their impact on client outcomes.
  • Utilize structured communication tools like the DESC script to navigate difficult conversations with empathy, clarity, and a focus on professional development.
  • Master advanced active listening techniques, including paraphrasing, reflecting feelings, and asking open-ended questions, to foster trust and psychological safety with your mentees.
  • Guide mentees in setting SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) to ensure their professional development is structured, trackable, and aligned with their career aspirations.

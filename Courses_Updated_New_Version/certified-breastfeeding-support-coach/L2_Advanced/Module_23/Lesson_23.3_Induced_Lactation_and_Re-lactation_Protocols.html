The Profound Journey: Induced Lactation and Re-lactation

The ability to induce lactation or re-establish a milk supply is a testament to the incredible adaptability of the human body and the power of hormonal and mechanical stimulation. These processes are not just about milk production; they are often about deep emotional connection, identity, and the fulfillment of profound parental desires. As a coach, guiding a client through this journey is one of the most rewarding aspects of our work.

Induced lactation refers to the process of stimulating milk production in individuals who have never been pregnant or given birth. This is most commonly sought by adoptive parents or non-gestational parents in same-sex partnerships. Re-lactation, on the other hand, is the process of re-establishing milk production after a period of cessation, often following a previous breastfeeding experience.

Understanding Induced Lactation: The Newman-Goldfarb Protocol

The Newman-Goldfarb protocol is one of the most widely recognized and effective methods for inducing lactation. Developed by Dr. Jack Newman and Lenore Goldfarb, it involves a multi-phase approach designed to mimic the hormonal changes of pregnancy and childbirth, followed by mechanical stimulation to initiate and maintain milk supply.

Phases of the Newman-Goldfarb Protocol:

  1. Hormonal Priming (Pregnancy Mimicry): This phase typically lasts for several months (3-6 months is common, but can vary) and involves the use of oral contraceptives containing estrogen and progesterone, sometimes in conjunction with domperidone. The goal is to stimulate the growth of mammary gland tissue, mimicking the breast development during pregnancy.
    • Estrogen and Progesterone: These hormones prepare the breast tissue for milk production. Estrogen promotes ductal growth, while progesterone stimulates alveolar development.
    • Domperidone: A dopamine antagonist that increases prolactin levels, which is crucial for milk production. It is used off-label for this purpose and its availability varies by country and requires medical supervision.
  2. Milk Production Initiation: After the hormonal priming phase, the client discontinues the oral contraceptives, which causes a drop in estrogen and progesterone levels, signaling the body to begin milk production (similar to the hormonal drop after childbirth). Domperidone is often continued, and intense mechanical stimulation begins.
  3. Milk Maintenance: This phase focuses on regular breast stimulation (pumping, hand expression, or direct nursing) and continued domperidone use to maintain prolactin levels and milk supply.

Success rates for induced lactation vary significantly, with a 2011 study reporting that approximately 75% of individuals initiating the Newman-Goldfarb protocol achieved some level of milk production, with about 40% producing enough milk to partially breastfeed (Auerbach & Avery, 2011). Full milk supply is less common but achievable for some, especially with dedicated effort and support.

The Science of Re-lactation

Re-lactation is often a faster process than induced lactation because the mammary glands have previously developed and produced milk. The body "remembers" the process, and existing prolactin receptors on alveolar cells can be reactivated more readily.

Physiological Mechanisms:

  • Prolactin Receptor Reactivation: During previous lactation, prolactin stimulates the development of prolactin receptors. Even after cessation, these receptors can be reactivated with sufficient stimulation.
  • Mammary Gland Reactivation: The breast tissue, already primed from a previous pregnancy and lactation, is more responsive to hormonal signals.
  • Hormonal Shifts: Frequent and effective breast stimulation (nursing, pumping) triggers the release of prolactin from the anterior pituitary gland and oxytocin from the posterior pituitary, initiating and sustaining milk production.
  • Autocrine Control: The more milk removed, the more milk is produced. This local feedback mechanism is vital in re-establishing supply.

Factors influencing re-lactation success include the time elapsed since the last lactation, the duration of previous breastfeeding, the age of the infant, and the mother's motivation and support system. A systematic review found that re-lactation success rates (defined as producing at least some milk) range from 70% to 90%, with full breastfeeding achieved in 40-70% of cases, depending on the intervention intensity and context (Seema & Bhatia, 2013). This offers significant hope for parents who wish to resume breastfeeding.

Essential Tools: Supplemental Nursing Systems (SNS)

A Supplemental Nursing System (SNS) is an invaluable tool for both induced lactation and re-lactation. It consists of a container for supplementary milk (formula or expressed breast milk) and thin tubes that are taped to the breast, allowing the infant to receive milk while simultaneously stimulating the breast. This is critical for several reasons:

  1. Maintains Infant Interest and Latch: Infants are more likely to stay at the breast and maintain an effective latch if they are receiving milk. The SNS ensures immediate gratification, preventing frustration and nipple aversion.
  2. Direct Breast Stimulation: The act of suckling at the breast, even if the majority of milk comes from the SNS, directly stimulates prolactin release and signals the body to produce more milk. This is far more effective than pumping alone for building supply.
  3. Facilitates Bonding: Allows for direct breastfeeding experience and skin-to-skin contact, fostering the emotional connection between parent and child, regardless of milk volume.
  4. Gradual Weaning from Supplementation: As milk supply increases, the amount of supplement in the SNS can be gradually reduced, allowing the infant to transition to receiving more of their intake directly from the breast.

Coaching clients on proper SNS use is paramount. This includes:

  • Setup and Positioning: Ensuring the tubing is correctly placed, typically at the corner of the baby's mouth, with the tip extending slightly past the nipple.
  • Hygiene: Thorough cleaning of all SNS components after each use to prevent bacterial growth.
  • Flow Control: Adjusting the height of the SNS container to control the flow rate, ensuring the baby is not overwhelmed or frustrated.
  • Troubleshooting: Addressing common issues like clogged tubes, milk leaking, or baby pulling off.
Coach's Tip

Gradual Reduction of SNS: When a client's milk supply is improving, guide them to gradually reduce the amount of supplement in the SNS, rather than removing it entirely. For example, suggest reducing by 10-15ml per feeding every few days, while closely monitoring wet diapers and weight gain. This gentle approach helps both parent and baby adapt, supporting the long-term success of breastfeeding.

Nurturing the Journey: Psychological Support and Expectation Management

The emotional landscape of induced lactation and re-lactation can be complex and intense. Clients undertaking these journeys often carry unique hopes, anxieties, and vulnerabilities. Your role as a Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach extends far beyond physiological protocols to encompass profound psychological support.

Key Areas of Psychological Support:

  • Validation of Emotions: Acknowledge the immense effort, hope, and potential frustration. It's okay to feel overwhelmed, joyous, or disappointed.
  • Realistic Goal Setting: Help clients understand that "success" is defined by their goals. Whether it's full supply, partial breastfeeding, or simply the bonding experience, all outcomes are valuable. Many clients find immense satisfaction in partial breastfeeding, as it fulfills the emotional desire for direct nursing.
  • Celebrating Small Victories: Producing even a few drops of colostrum or milk is a huge achievement. Celebrate every milestone to build confidence and motivation.
  • Addressing Grief and Loss (Re-lactation): For re-lactating parents, there may be unresolved grief or guilt about the previous cessation of breastfeeding. Validate these feelings and help them focus on the present opportunity.
  • Partner and Family Support: Encourage partners and family members to be actively involved and supportive. Education about the process can prevent misunderstandings and foster a positive environment.
  • Connecting with Community: Suggest connecting with online or local support groups for adoptive parents who breastfeed or re-lactating mothers. Shared experiences can be incredibly validating.
  • Mental Health Resources: Be prepared to refer clients to mental health professionals if they exhibit signs of significant anxiety, depression, or perinatal mood and anxiety disorders (PMADs), as these journeys can be emotionally taxing.
Coach's Tip

Emphasize "Fed is Best" within Breastfeeding Goals: While supporting breastfeeding is central, always reassure clients that their baby's nourishment and well-being are the ultimate priority. If induced lactation or re-lactation results in partial supply, celebrating the breast-milk contribution and the breastfeeding relationship is key, alongside a plan for supplementation. This reduces pressure and prevents feelings of failure, fostering a positive feeding experience for the family.

Monitoring Infant Well-being

During induced lactation or re-lactation, especially in the early stages, vigilant monitoring of infant weight gain and hydration is critical. This ensures the baby is receiving adequate nourishment while the parent's milk supply is developing or re-establishing.

Key Monitoring Metrics:

  1. Wet Diapers:
    • Day 1: At least 1 wet diaper.
    • Day 2: At least 2 wet diapers.
    • Day 3: At least 3 wet diapers.
    • Day 4 onwards: 5-6+ heavy wet diapers per 24 hours.
  2. Soiled Diapers:
    • Day 1: At least 1 meconium stool.
    • Day 2: 2-3 transitional stools.
    • Day 3-4: 3+ yellow, seedy stools.
    • Day 5 onwards: 3-4+ yellow, seedy stools per 24 hours.
  3. Weight Gain: After the initial physiological weight loss (typically up to 7-10% in the first few days), infants should regain birth weight by 10-14 days of age. Subsequently, healthy weight gain is typically 20-30 grams per day (approximately 0.7-1 ounce) for the first 3 months (AAP, 2012). Regular weight checks (e.g., weekly) with a pediatrician or IBCLC are essential.
  4. Feeding Cues and Behavior: Observe if the baby is alert, content after feeds, has good muscle tone, and actively suckles. Lethargy, excessive crying, or infrequent feeding can be signs of insufficient intake.
  5. Milk Transfer: Listen for audible swallows during feeds, observe jaw movement, and note breast softening after feeds.

It is paramount to collaborate closely with the client's pediatrician or an International Board Certified Lactation Consultant (IBCLC) throughout this process. They can provide clinical oversight, perform weighted feeds, and adjust supplementation plans as needed. Your role is to support the parent in implementing these protocols, observing the baby, and communicating effectively with their healthcare team.

Coach's Tip

Empowering Parents to Trust Their Instincts: While data is important, remind clients to also trust their parental instincts. They know their baby best. If something feels "off" with feeding or the baby's demeanor, encourage them to reach out to their healthcare provider immediately. Empowering them to advocate for their baby's health is a crucial part of your support.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the three main phases of the Newman-Goldfarb protocol, and what is the primary goal of each?

The three main phases are: Hormonal Priming (mimicking pregnancy hormones to develop mammary tissue), Milk Production Initiation (stopping hormones to trigger milk production, like after birth), and Milk Maintenance (sustained stimulation and often medication to maintain supply).

2. Why is a Supplemental Nursing System (SNS) considered an invaluable tool for both induced lactation and re-lactation?

An SNS is invaluable because it maintains infant interest and latch by providing immediate milk, facilitates direct breast stimulation for increased prolactin, promotes bonding through skin-to-skin contact, and allows for gradual weaning from supplementation as milk supply increases.

3. What are at least three key factors that can influence the success rate of re-lactation?

Key factors influencing re-lactation success include: time elapsed since the last lactation (shorter time is better), the duration of previous breastfeeding (longer duration often means more developed mammary tissue), the age of the infant (younger infants typically adapt better), and the parent's motivation and support system.

4. As a coach, what is a crucial aspect of psychological support you would provide to a client undergoing induced lactation?

A crucial aspect is realistic expectation management, emphasizing that "success" is defined by the client's personal goals (e.g., partial breastfeeding for bonding is a valuable outcome). Also, validating emotions (joy, anxiety, frustration) and celebrating small victories are vital for maintaining motivation and well-being.

Key Takeaways

  • Induced lactation and re-lactation are profound journeys enabling breastfeeding for non-gestational parents or those resuming lactation, offering deep emotional connection and health benefits.
  • The Newman-Goldfarb protocol, involving hormonal priming and mechanical stimulation, is a leading method for induced lactation, with varying success rates for milk volume.
  • Re-lactation often leverages existing mammary gland development and prolactin receptors, making it potentially faster and more successful in achieving a significant milk supply.
  • Supplemental Nursing Systems (SNS) are vital tools to maintain infant interest, ensure milk transfer, and provide direct breast stimulation for supply building.
  • Comprehensive psychological support, including realistic expectation management, emotional validation, and celebration of small milestones, is critical for clients navigating these intense processes.
  • Vigilant monitoring of infant wet/soiled diapers, weight gain (e.g., 20-30g/day), and feeding cues is essential, often requiring collaboration with pediatricians or IBCLCs.

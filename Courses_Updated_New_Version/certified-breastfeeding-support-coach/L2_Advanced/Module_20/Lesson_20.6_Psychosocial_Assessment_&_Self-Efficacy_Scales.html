Opening Case Study

Eleanor's Struggle for Confidence

Client: Eleanor, 46-year-old first-time mother, 3 weeks postpartum

Presenting Concern: Expressing profound anxiety about her ability to breastfeed, despite adequate milk supply and an infant with good latch mechanics. She describes feeling "overwhelmed and like a failure."

Background: Eleanor is a successful marketing executive who decided to have her first child later in life. She feels immense pressure to "do everything right" but struggles with self-doubt, particularly after a difficult, unplanned C-section and a brief NICU stay for her baby. Her husband is supportive but often away for work, and her closest family lives states away.

Initial Assessment: Physical assessment reveals no significant issues with latch or milk transfer. Baby's weight gain is appropriate. However, Eleanor's body language is tense, and she frequently sighs, stating, "I just don't know if I can keep this up."

Eleanor's case highlights the critical role of psychosocial factors. While her physical breastfeeding mechanics appear sound, her emotional state and perceived self-efficacy are significantly impacting her experience. We'll explore how to use specific tools to uncover these deeper layers and empower clients like Eleanor.

Implementing the Breastfeeding Self-Efficacy Scale - Short Form (BSES-SF)

Self-efficacy, a concept developed by Albert Bandura, refers to an individual's belief in their capacity to execute behaviors necessary to produce specific performance attainments (Bandura, 1997). In breastfeeding, maternal breastfeeding self-efficacy is a powerful predictor of successful initiation and duration.

The Breastfeeding Self-Efficacy Scale - Short Form (BSES-SF) is a validated, 14-item questionnaire designed to quickly assess a mother's confidence in her ability to breastfeed. Each item is rated on a 5-point Likert scale, from "Not at all confident" (1) to "Totally confident" (5). A higher score indicates greater self-efficacy.

Why is the BSES-SF critical for your practice?

  • Early Identification: It identifies mothers at risk of early cessation due to low confidence, even if physical issues aren't apparent. Research shows that low BSES-SF scores at 24-72 hours postpartum are associated with significantly shorter breastfeeding duration (Dennis, 2003).
  • Targeted Support: It helps you tailor interventions. A mother with high physical skill but low confidence needs different support than one with physical challenges.
  • Empowerment: Discussing the results with a client can validate her feelings and open a dialogue about her perceived challenges, shifting the focus from "can I do this?" to "how can I build my confidence?"
  • Outcome Prediction: Studies consistently demonstrate that high self-efficacy is positively correlated with exclusive breastfeeding rates and duration. For example, a 2018 meta-analysis found that mothers with higher BSES-SF scores were 2.5 times more likely to be exclusively breastfeeding at 6 weeks postpartum (Blyth et al., 2018).

Administering and Interpreting the BSES-SF

  1. When to Administer: Ideally, during your initial consultation. It can also be valuable in follow-up sessions to track changes in confidence.
  2. Instructions: Explain that it's about her feelings of confidence, not her actual performance. Encourage honest responses.
  3. Scoring: Sum the scores for all 14 items. The total score ranges from 14 to 70.
  4. Interpretation:
    • Scores < 50: Suggest lower self-efficacy, indicating a potential need for intensive emotional support, confidence-building strategies, and validation.
    • Scores 50-60: Moderate self-efficacy. These mothers may benefit from encouragement and practical problem-solving.
    • Scores > 60: Higher self-efficacy. These mothers are likely to persist, but still benefit from continued support and affirmation.
  5. Discussion: Use the results as a starting point. Ask open-ended questions like, "I see you rated 'I can manage the discomfort of breastfeeding' as a 2. Can you tell me more about that?" This approach aligns perfectly with the "Listen & Learn" phase of the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢.
üí° Coach's Tip

Empowering the "Overwhelmed" Client: For clients like Eleanor, a low BSES-SF score isn't a judgment, but a roadmap. Frame it positively: "This tool helps us understand your unique feelings. It shows us exactly where we can focus our support to help you feel stronger and more capable." This reframes her struggles as addressable challenges, not personal failings.

Screening for Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs) using the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs) are the most common complication of childbirth, affecting up to 1 in 5 women (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, 2023). They can profoundly impact a mother's ability and desire to breastfeed, often leading to early cessation, perceived low milk supply, and bonding difficulties.

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is a 10-item self-report questionnaire designed to screen for symptoms of depression and anxiety in the perinatal period. It is not a diagnostic tool, but a crucial screening instrument that helps identify mothers who may need further evaluation by a mental health professional.

Why is EPDS screening essential for Breastfeeding Support Coaches?

  • Interconnectedness: PMADs and breastfeeding challenges are highly intertwined. Depression can reduce breastfeeding initiation rates by up to 50% and significantly shorten duration (Field, 2018).
  • Early Intervention: Early identification allows for timely referral and intervention, which can improve both maternal mental health and breastfeeding outcomes.
  • Scope of Practice: While you do not diagnose, screening and appropriate referral fall within your scope as a supportive professional.
  • Holistic Care: Ignoring potential PMADs means overlooking a major barrier to breastfeeding success, hindering your ability to provide truly holistic care.

Administering and Interpreting the EPDS

  1. When to Administer: During initial consultations and periodically as needed, especially if you observe signs of distress.
  2. Instructions: Explain its purpose carefully: "This is a common tool to check in on how you're feeling emotionally. Many new mothers experience changes in their mood, and this helps us understand if you might need additional support." Emphasize that it's confidential.
  3. Scoring: Each item is scored 0-3. The total score ranges from 0 to 30.
    • Scores 0-9: Generally low risk for PMADs.
    • Scores 10-12: Mild symptoms, warranting further discussion and monitoring.
    • Scores ≥ 13: Suggestive of probable depression, requiring immediate referral for comprehensive mental health assessment.
    • Any positive response to Item 10 ("The thought of harming myself has occurred to me"): Requires immediate and direct follow-up and urgent referral, regardless of total score.
  4. Referral Pathways: Have a network of local mental health professionals (therapists, psychiatrists, support groups specializing in perinatal mental health) readily available. This is a critical part of your professional responsibility.

Assessing 'Breastfeeding Grief' and Trauma from Previous Feeding Experiences

Many mothers come to you with a history, and that history can profoundly shape their current breastfeeding journey. It's crucial to assess for "breastfeeding grief" and trauma from previous feeding experiences.

Breastfeeding Grief

Breastfeeding grief occurs when a mother's breastfeeding journey does not meet her expectations or desires, leading to feelings of loss, sadness, guilt, and inadequacy. This can happen due to:

  • Unfulfilled Goals: Unable to exclusively breastfeed, or breastfeed for the desired duration.
  • Traumatic Events: Difficult birth, NICU stay, medical complications for mother or baby.
  • Societal Pressure: Feeling judged or failing to meet perceived standards.
  • Physical Pain/Challenges: Unresolved latch issues, nipple damage, low supply.

Listen for phrases like: "I feel like I failed my baby," "I really wanted to breastfeed but it just didn't work," "I feel so guilty about supplementing." These are cues for deeper emotional work. Acknowledge her feelings and validate her experience. This aligns with the "Comfort & Connection" aspect of the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢.

Trauma from Previous Feeding Experiences

Past experiences, particularly with previous children, can create significant anxiety or aversion to breastfeeding. This might include:

  • Prior Latch Pain: A mother who endured excruciating nipple pain with a previous child may anticipate the same, even with a new baby who has a perfect latch.
  • Low Supply Issues: A history of perceived or actual low milk supply can lead to intense anxiety about milk production, even when current supply is adequate.
  • Negative Healthcare Interactions: Past dismissive or unsupportive advice from medical professionals can erode trust.

When taking a maternal history, always ask about previous feeding experiences, noting both successes and challenges. "What was your experience like breastfeeding your older child?" or "What are your hopes and fears about breastfeeding this baby, especially given your past?"

A 2021 qualitative study highlighted that previous negative breastfeeding experiences often lead to pre-emptive anxiety and self-doubt in subsequent pregnancies, even before any current issues arise (Smith et al., 2021).

Evaluating the 'Support Constellation': Mapping Familial and Social Barriers

A mother's breastfeeding journey is rarely an isolated event. It is deeply embedded within her "support constellation" ‚Äì the network of individuals, resources, and cultural norms that either uplift or undermine her efforts.

As a Coach, it's vital to map this constellation to identify both strengths and potential barriers. This informs your holistic strategy, a core principle of the "Holistic Habits" phase of the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢.

Key Elements to Assess:

  1. Partner Support: Is the partner educated, engaged, and actively supportive? Do they understand their role beyond feeding (e.g., burping, changing, bringing baby to mother)? Lack of partner support is a significant predictor of early breastfeeding cessation (Huang & Zhao, 2020).
  2. Familial Support (Grandparents, Siblings): Are family members encouraging or critical? Do they offer practical help (meals, chores) or unhelpful advice? Cultural beliefs about infant feeding can play a strong role here.
  3. Friend & Peer Support: Does she have friends who have breastfed successfully? Is she connected to local mother's groups or online communities?
  4. Professional Support: Beyond you, does she have a supportive pediatrician, OB/GYN, or family doctor?
  5. Workplace/Educational Support: If returning to work or school, does she have access to pumping facilities, adequate break times, and understanding supervisors?
  6. Practical Support: Who helps with cooking, cleaning, childcare for older siblings? The burden of household tasks can severely impact a mother's ability to focus on breastfeeding.
  7. Financial Security: Financial stress can exacerbate emotional distress and limit access to resources.

Ask questions like: "Who is your main support person at home?", "How do your family members feel about you breastfeeding?", "What kind of help are you receiving with daily chores or other children?", "What does a typical day look like for you in terms of support?"

üí° Coach's Tip

Empowering the "Isolated" Client: Many mothers, especially career changers or those with geographically distant families, feel isolated. Help them identify even one strong ally. "Even one person who truly understands and supports your goals can make a world of difference. Who is that person for you, or who could it be?" Offer resources for local support groups or online communities as a bridge to connection.

The Impact of Maternal Stress on the Milk Ejection Reflex (MER) and Oxytocin Pathways

The physiological act of breastfeeding is profoundly influenced by a mother's psychological state. Specifically, maternal stress can directly interfere with the Milk Ejection Reflex (MER), often referred to as the let-down reflex.

The Neurobiology of Let-Down

When an infant suckles at the breast, sensory nerves send signals to the mother's brain. This stimulates the posterior pituitary gland to release oxytocin, often called the "love hormone" or "cuddle hormone." Oxytocin causes the myoepithelial cells around the milk alveoli to contract, pushing milk down the ducts towards the nipple ‚Äì this is the MER (Uvn√§s-Moberg, 2019).

How Stress Disrupts MER

Stress, anxiety, fear, and even embarrassment can trigger the release of catecholamines (like adrenaline and noradrenaline) from the adrenal glands. Catecholamines have several effects that can inhibit oxytocin release and milk flow:

  • Vasoconstriction: They cause blood vessels in the breast to constrict, reducing blood flow to the mammary glands, which can impede milk delivery.
  • Oxytocin Inhibition: High levels of catecholamines can directly inhibit the release of oxytocin from the pituitary gland.
  • Myoepithelial Cell Inhibition: Some research suggests catecholamines may directly inhibit the contraction of myoepithelial cells (Al-Saleh et al., 2015).

When the MER is inhibited, the baby may struggle to get milk, leading to frustration for both mother and infant, perceived low milk supply, and a vicious cycle of increased stress. This is a crucial element in the "Troubleshoot & Target" phase of the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢.

Strategies for Mitigation:

  • Relaxation Techniques: Deep breathing, guided meditation, calming music, warm compresses on the breasts before feeding.
  • Skin-to-Skin Contact: Promotes oxytocin release and maternal relaxation.
  • Quiet Environment: Minimizing distractions and creating a peaceful feeding space.
  • Positive Affirmations: Encouraging words and positive self-talk.
  • Addressing Underlying Stressors: While not a therapist, you can help mothers identify stressors and suggest avenues for support (e.g., delegating tasks, seeking mental health support).

Understanding this physiological link empowers you to explain why a mother "can't let down" when stressed, validating her experience and offering practical, evidence-based solutions.

üí° Coach's Tip

Normalizing Stress Responses: When a client describes difficulty with let-down, explain the stress-oxytocin link clearly and reassuringly. "It's not your fault, it's your body's natural response to stress. Your body is incredibly smart, but sometimes it needs a little help to switch from 'fight or flight' to 'feed the baby.' Let's try a few relaxation techniques together."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client consistently scores low on the BSES-SF despite having a physically well-latching baby and adequate milk transfer. What is the primary implication for your support strategy?

The primary implication is that the client needs significant emotional support and confidence-building strategies. While physical mechanics are good, her perceived self-efficacy is low, which is a strong predictor of early breastfeeding cessation. Your support should focus on validating her feelings, addressing specific anxieties, and providing targeted education to boost her confidence.

2. You administer the EPDS to a client, and she scores 15, with a positive response to item 10 ("The thought of harming myself has occurred to me"). What is your immediate and most critical next step?

Regardless of the total score, any positive response to Item 10 on the EPDS requires immediate and direct follow-up and urgent referral for a comprehensive mental health assessment. Your role is not to diagnose or provide therapy, but to ensure the client is connected to appropriate professional help without delay.

3. A mother expresses deep regret and sadness about her previous breastfeeding experience with her first child, stating she "felt like a failure." How would you approach this in the context of assessing for 'breastfeeding grief' and trauma?

You would acknowledge and validate her feelings of loss and sadness. Encourage her to share more about her previous experience without judgment. Frame the conversation around understanding how past experiences might influence her current feelings, and reassure her that her feelings are valid. This opens a pathway for healing and helps distinguish past challenges from her current journey.

4. Explain how maternal stress can physiologically interfere with the milk ejection reflex (MER) and suggest two practical strategies a coach could recommend.

Maternal stress triggers the release of catecholamines (e.g., adrenaline), which can inhibit oxytocin release and cause vasoconstriction in the breasts. This reduces blood flow to the mammary glands and prevents the myoepithelial cells from contracting effectively, thereby hindering the MER. Two practical strategies a coach could recommend are: 1) Encouraging relaxation techniques like deep breathing or guided meditation before feeds, and 2) Promoting skin-to-skin contact, which naturally boosts oxytocin and promotes relaxation.

Key Takeaways

  • Psychosocial assessment is integral to holistic breastfeeding support, complementing physical evaluations for a complete client picture.
  • The BSES-SF is a quick, reliable tool to gauge maternal breastfeeding self-efficacy, a key predictor of duration, enabling targeted confidence-building strategies.
  • The EPDS is a vital screening tool for Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs); a score of ≥ 13 or any suicidal ideation requires immediate referral to mental health professionals.
  • Actively assess for 'breastfeeding grief' and past trauma, validating a mother's emotional experiences and acknowledging their impact on her current journey.
  • Map the client's 'support constellation' to identify strengths and barriers within her familial and social environment, guiding your holistic support plan.
  • Understand that maternal stress directly inhibits the Milk Ejection Reflex (MER) via catecholamine release, making relaxation techniques and a supportive environment critical for successful milk flow.

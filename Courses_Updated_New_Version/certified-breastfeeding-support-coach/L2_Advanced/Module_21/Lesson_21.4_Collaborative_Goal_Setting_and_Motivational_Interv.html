Opening Case Study: The Overwhelmed New Mom

Sarah's Struggle with Conflicting Advice

S

Sarah, 38 years old

First-time mother, 3 weeks postpartum, struggling with perceived low milk supply and nipple pain.

Sarah came to me feeling utterly defeated. "Everyone has an opinion," she sighed, "my mom says I should just give formula, my sister swears by power pumping, and the internet is just a black hole of information. I want to breastfeed, I really do, but I feel like I'm failing. And honestly, I'm so tired, I just want someone to tell me what to do, but then I feel like I can't do it anyway."

She presented with cracked nipples, her baby was gaining weight slowly (though within acceptable range), and she was pumping very little after feeds. Sarah was eager for a solution but also displayed significant ambivalence and low self-efficacy. She had heard plenty of "best practices" but felt overwhelmed and disconnected from her own desires.

Sarah's case highlights a common scenario for breastfeeding coaches: mothers who need support not just in technique, but in finding their own path amidst external pressures and internal doubts. How do we help Sarah move from feeling like a passive recipient of advice to an active, empowered participant in her own breastfeeding journey? This lesson will equip you with the tools to do just that.

The 'Listen & Learn' Foundation for Collaborative Goal Setting

At AccrediPro Academy, the L.A.T.C.H. Support Method‚Ñ¢ begins with "L" for Listen & Learn. This isn't just about gathering clinical data; it's about understanding the mother as a whole person ‚Äì her history, hopes, fears, values, and definition of success. When it comes to goal setting, this deep listening is paramount.

A crucial aspect of 'Listen & Learn' is assessing a mother's self-efficacy. Coined by psychologist Albert Bandura, self-efficacy refers to an individual's belief in their capacity to execute behaviors necessary to produce specific performance attainments. In breastfeeding, high self-efficacy predicts greater persistence and success, especially when facing challenges (Dennis, 2003). Low self-efficacy can manifest as statements like Sarah's: "I just want someone to tell me what to do, but then I feel like I can't do it anyway."

By actively listening, you can uncover not only overt challenges but also hidden barriers:

  • Past experiences: Previous unsuccessful breastfeeding attempts, birth trauma, or negative healthcare interactions.
  • Societal and familial pressures: Conflicting advice, lack of support from partners or family, or cultural norms around infant feeding.
  • Internalized beliefs: Feelings of inadequacy, guilt, or fear of judgment.
  • Logistical constraints: Work schedules, lack of childcare, financial stress, or limited access to resources.
Coach's Tip

To uncover a client's self-efficacy and potential hidden barriers, use open-ended questions like: "Tell me about your previous experiences with breastfeeding or feeding babies," "What are your greatest hopes for breastfeeding, and what worries you the most?", or "If you could wave a magic wand, what would your breastfeeding journey look like?" These questions invite deeper reflection beyond simple facts.

Crafting SMART Goals in Lactation Care

Once you've listened deeply and understood the mother's context, the next step is to translate her aspirations into actionable goals. Vague goals like "increase milk supply" are often overwhelming and demotivating. This is where the SMART goal framework becomes invaluable, providing structure and clarity (Doran, 1981).

Element Description Example in Lactation Care
Specific Clear, well-defined, and unambiguous. What exactly needs to be achieved? "Pump 15-20ml more milk per session, three times a day, after nursing."
Measurable Quantifiable, allowing for progress tracking. How will you know when the goal is met? "Baby will have 6-8 wet diapers and 3-4 soiled diapers per 24 hours."
Achievable Realistic and attainable given the mother's resources, time, and current situation. "Breastfeed baby for at least 10 minutes per side, 8-12 times a day, for the next 48 hours." (Rather than "exclusively breastfeed twins overnight").
Relevant Meaningful and aligned with the mother's personal values and overall breastfeeding vision. "Increase milk supply to reduce formula supplementation from 60ml to 30ml per feed, allowing me to feel more confident in my ability to nourish my baby."
Time-bound Has a clear deadline or timeframe. When will the goal be achieved? "Reduce nipple pain from a 7/10 to a 3/10 within 3 days by improving latch and using nipple balm."

Collaboratively setting SMART goals ensures that the mother is an active participant, increasing her commitment and self-efficacy. It transforms abstract desires into concrete steps she can take and track.

Navigating Cultural and Familial Influences on Treatment Plan Adherence

Breastfeeding is not just a physiological act; it's deeply embedded in culture, family traditions, and social networks. These external influences can significantly impact a mother's decisions and adherence to a care plan (Sheehan et al., 2018).

  • Familial expectations: A mother's own mother or grandmother may have formula-fed, leading to subtle or overt pressure.
  • Partner support: A partner's understanding and support are critical. Lack of it can be a major barrier to adherence.
  • Cultural norms: Some cultures may prioritize scheduled feeds, early introduction of solids, or have specific beliefs about "good" milk.
  • Community and peer influence: Friends' experiences, social media groups, or even childcare providers can shape a mother's perception and choices.

As a coach, your role is not to dismiss these influences but to acknowledge them with respect and help the mother navigate them. This might involve:

  1. Involving partners: Encourage partners to attend sessions or provide them with information.
  2. Respectful dialogue: Validate a mother's cultural context or family's intentions, even if their advice conflicts with evidence-based practices.
  3. Finding common ground: Help the mother articulate her goals in a way that resonates with her support system. For example, framing breastfeeding as contributing to the baby's health and immunity, which is often a universally valued outcome.
Coach's Tip

When a client expresses conflict due to family advice, try validating their feelings first: "It sounds like you're getting a lot of love and advice from your family, and that can be hard to sift through, especially when you're also trying to find what feels right for you." Then, gently pivot to her autonomy: "Ultimately, this is your journey, and my role is to support you in making choices that align with your goals for yourself and your baby."

Motivational Interviewing: Empowering Maternal Autonomy

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a collaborative, goal-oriented style of communication with particular attention to the language of change. It is designed to strengthen personal motivation for and commitment to a specific goal by eliciting and exploring the person's own reasons for change within an atmosphere of acceptance and compassion (Miller & Rollnick, 2013).

The "spirit" of MI is founded on four key elements (PACE):

  • Partnership: Working collaboratively with the client, not "doing to" but "working with."
  • Acceptance: Unconditional positive regard for the client's autonomy and inherent worth.
  • Compassion: Actively promoting the client's welfare.
  • Evocation: Drawing out the client's own motivations and ideas, rather than imposing them.

MI is particularly powerful in breastfeeding support because mothers often face significant ambivalence‚Äîdesiring to breastfeed but feeling overwhelmed, exhausted, or uncertain about their ability. MI helps them explore and resolve this ambivalence, moving towards sustained behavior change.

Coach's Tip

Recognize "change talk" vs. "sustain talk." Change talk (e.g., "I really want to breastfeed for at least 6 months," "I need to get this latch better") signals intrinsic motivation. Sustain talk (e.g., "It's just so hard," "I'm not sure I can do it") indicates ambivalence or resistance. Lean into change talk with affirmations and reflections to strengthen it.

Core Motivational Interviewing Techniques (OARS)

The practical skills of MI are often summarized by the acronym OARS:

  • O: Open-ended questions: Questions that cannot be answered with a simple "yes" or "no." They invite elaboration and reflection, allowing you to "Listen & Learn" deeply.
    • Example: "What are your thoughts on trying a new feeding position?" (instead of "Will you try this position?")
    • Example: "Tell me about a time you felt really good about your breastfeeding journey."
  • A: Affirmations: Statements of appreciation and understanding of the client's strengths, efforts, and positive intentions. They build self-efficacy.
    • Example: "You've shown incredible determination despite these challenges."
    • Example: "It took a lot of courage to reach out for help, and that shows how committed you are."
  • R: Reflections: Statements that mirror back what the client has said, demonstrating that you have heard and understood them. Reflections can be simple (repeating or rephrasing) or complex (adding meaning or emotion).
    • Simple: "So, you're feeling exhausted by the constant pumping."
    • Complex: "It sounds like you're feeling a mix of frustration with the pain and a deep desire to provide for your baby, and you're wondering if it's all worth it."
  • S: Summaries: Longer reflections that pull together several things a client has said, often at transitions in the conversation. They help the client organize their thoughts and ensure you're on the same page.
    • Example: "So, if I'm hearing you correctly, you're hoping to reduce nipple pain, increase baby's weight gain, and ideally, continue breastfeeding for at least six months, even if it means some supplementation. You're feeling a bit overwhelmed by all the advice, but you're ready to try some new strategies to get there. Did I miss anything?"

Bridging Clinical 'Best Practice' with the Mother's Personal Breastfeeding Definition

Your role as an AccrediPro Certified Breastfeeding Support Coach is to be a guide, not a dictator. While you are equipped with extensive knowledge of evidence-based best practices, the ultimate decisions rest with the mother. Your expertise lies in helping her navigate the information and align it with her unique life and goals.

  • Elicit her definition of success: Start every planning conversation with, "What would success look like for you in your breastfeeding journey right now?" Her answer might surprise you and may not be exclusive breastfeeding.
  • Present options, not mandates: Offer a range of evidence-based strategies, explaining the pros and cons of each, and allowing her to choose what feels most feasible and aligned with her values.
  • Harm reduction approach: Recognize that "perfect" is often the enemy of "good." If a mother's goal is to partially breastfeed or to breastfeed for a shorter duration than recommended, support her in achieving that goal while maximizing benefits within her chosen framework. Any amount of human milk is valuable.
  • Flexibility and adaptation: Life happens. Be prepared to adapt goals and plans as circumstances change. Remind mothers that their journey is dynamic, and adjustments are signs of resilience, not failure.

By empowering mothers to define their own success and actively participate in creating their care plans, you foster a sense of autonomy and self-efficacy that extends far beyond breastfeeding. This collaborative approach is a hallmark of truly transformative coaching.

Coach's Tip

If a mother's goal seems potentially unrealistic or might lead to unintended consequences (e.g., exclusively pumping without adequate support leading to burnout), use MI techniques to explore her reasoning. "What makes that goal feel right for you?" or "What are some of the challenges you anticipate with that approach, and how might we address them?" This allows her to articulate her motivations and consider potential pitfalls herself, rather than you imposing your concerns.

Check Your Understanding

1

During the 'Listen & Learn' phase, a client states, "I know I should breastfeed exclusively, but I'm so tired, and my mom keeps offering to give the baby a bottle of formula at night." What MI principle is most relevant to address this situation?

The MI principle of Resolving Ambivalence is most relevant. The client is expressing a desire for exclusive breastfeeding ("I know I should") but also acknowledging a barrier and an alternative ("I'm so tired, my mom keeps offering formula"). A coach using MI would explore both sides of this ambivalence to help the client clarify her own values and motivations.
2

A mother expresses a goal: "I want to have enough milk." Using the SMART framework, how would you help her refine this into a more effective goal?

You would guide her to make it:
  • Specific: "Enough milk for what purpose? To reduce formula by half?"
  • Measurable: "How will we know it's enough? Baby gaining 5-7oz/week? Pumping 2oz after feeds?"
  • Achievable: "Is this realistic given her current supply and energy?"
  • Relevant: "Does this align with her overall breastfeeding vision?"
  • Time-bound: "By when? Within the next 3 days, 1 week?"
A refined goal might be: "Increase my milk production to provide 75% of my baby's daily intake (approximately 18 oz/day) by the end of next week, as evidenced by baby gaining 6 oz and having 6 wet diapers daily."
3

A client's partner is skeptical about breastfeeding, pushing for formula for convenience. How would you use a foundational MI technique to address this, without alienating the partner or client?

You could use an Open-ended Question or a Reflection. For example: "It sounds like your partner is really focused on making sure things are easy and that the baby is well-fed. What are their main concerns about breastfeeding?" (Open-ended) or "You're feeling caught between your desire to breastfeed and your partner's concerns about convenience." (Reflection). This acknowledges their perspective without judgment and opens a dialogue.
4

You notice a client repeatedly saying, "I know I should try more skin-to-skin, but I just haven't had the time." Is this "change talk" or "sustain talk," and how might you respond with an affirmation?

This is a form of Sustain Talk, as it highlights a barrier and a lack of action, even though the client acknowledges the benefit ("I know I should"). You could respond with an affirmation like: "It's clear you understand the benefits of skin-to-skin, and even with how busy you are, you're still holding onto that desire to make it happen. That shows a real commitment to your baby's wellbeing." This validates her, focuses on her positive intent, and avoids direct confrontation about the lack of action.

Key Takeaways

  • Deep Listening is Foundational: Use the 'Listen & Learn' phase to understand a mother's self-efficacy, values, and hidden barriers, moving beyond mere symptom assessment.
  • SMART Goals Drive Action: Collaborate with clients to create Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound goals to foster clarity and commitment.
  • Acknowledge External Influences: Respectfully navigate cultural, familial, and partner influences that shape a mother's breastfeeding decisions and adherence.
  • Empower with Motivational Interviewing: Employ MI principles (PACE) and techniques (OARS) to help mothers resolve ambivalence and strengthen their intrinsic motivation for change.
  • Align Clinical & Personal Vision: Present evidence-based recommendations as options, always aligning them with the mother's unique definition of breastfeeding success and supporting her autonomy.

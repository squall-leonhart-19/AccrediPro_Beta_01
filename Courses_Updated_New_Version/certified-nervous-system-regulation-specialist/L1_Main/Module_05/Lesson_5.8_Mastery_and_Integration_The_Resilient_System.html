The Coaching Script

Practice these words out loud. Pay attention to your pacing‚Äîslow down by 20% compared to your normal speaking voice.

YOU:
"Beth, I hear you. That promotion feels like a huge 'expansion' of your energy, and right now, your system is flagging it as a potential threat. Does that sound right?"
BETH:
"Exactly. I'm so afraid that if I say yes, I'll end up back in that dark place of total exhaustion I was in three years ago."
YOU:
"That fear is actually your nervous system trying to protect you. It's a good protector. What if we didn't try to jump into the whole promotion today? What if we just practiced 'tasting' a little more capacity? Right now, as you think about the first small step of that job, what do you notice in your body?"
BETH:
"My chest feels a bit tight... but my feet feel heavy on the floor."
YOU:
"Let's stay with those heavy feet for a moment. Can we let the ground take 5% more of your weight? We're just widening the container, very slowly."
Coach Tip

Notice the use of "5% more." In the Expand phase, we use micro-metrics. Asking a client to feel 100% better is overwhelming. Asking for 5% is accessible and builds the "competence loop" the nervous system needs to feel safe.

Practical Exercises

1

The Mirror Test: Record yourself on your phone reading the script above. Watch it back. Are you nodding? Is your face soft? If you look "clinical" or "stiff," Beth's nervous system will mirror that and stay in a state of high alert.

2

The "Voice of Safety": Practice saying the phrase "We are just tasting a little more capacity" in three different tones: authoritative, whispering, and warm/steady. The warm/steady tone is your "Expansion Voice."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Mistake 1: Rushing the Expansion. If you push a client to "expand" before they have enough "grounding," they will snap back into a state of contraction (or "rubber-banding").
  • Mistake 2: Ignoring Somatic "Brakes." If a client's eyes start darting or they stop breathing, they have hit their current limit. Stop the expansion and return to regulation immediately.
  • Mistake 3: Over-Intellectualizing. Don't spend the whole session explaining why expansion matters. Let them feel the 5% shift. The body learns through experience, not just logic.
  • Mistake 4: Taking the Resistance Personally. If Beth says "This isn't working," it's her nervous system protecting her. It's not a reflection of your coaching skill.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we use "titration" (small increments) during the Expand phase?

To prevent the nervous system from perceiving the expansion as a threat, which would trigger a "rubber-band" effect of contraction or shutdown.

2. What is a "somatic brake" and what should you do if you see one?

A somatic brake is a sign of overload (e.g., breath-holding, darting eyes). You should immediately stop the expansion exercise and return to a known grounding or regulation tool.

3. True or False: A successful "Expand" session must end with the client feeling 100% ready to take on their challenge.

False. Success is measured by small, sustainable shifts in capacity (e.g., 5% more settle), not by "fixing" the entire problem in one go.

4. How does the "Voice of Safety" differ from a clinical tone?

The Voice of Safety is warm, steady, and rhythmic, which signals to the client's ventral vagal system that it is safe to connect and expand. A clinical tone can feel cold or judgmental, triggering defense.

Key Takeaways

  • Expansion must always be preceded by grounding; you cannot widen a container that doesn't have a solid base.
  • Use micro-metrics like "5% more" to make growth feel safe for the client's nervous system.
  • Your own state of regulation is your most powerful coaching tool‚ÄîBeth's system will mirror yours.
  • Watch for biological success markers like spontaneous deep breaths or softened facial features.
  • Professionalism in this field comes from navigating resistance with somatic curiosity rather than force.

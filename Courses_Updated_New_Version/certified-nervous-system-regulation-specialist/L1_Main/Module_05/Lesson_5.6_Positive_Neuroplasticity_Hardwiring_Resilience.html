The Evolutionary Negativity Bias

To help our clients, we must first help them understand that their brain is not "broken"‚Äîit is simply evolutionarily biased. For millions of years, survival depended on remembering the location of a predator (negative) more than the beauty of a sunset (positive). As Dr. Rick Hanson famously states, the brain is like "Velcro for the bad and Teflon for the good."

In the context of the nervous system, this means Sympathetic and Dorsal Vagal states leave deep "ruts" in our neural pathways, while Ventral Vagal (safety) states often slide off without leaving a trace. To achieve true expansion, we must learn to tilt the playing field back toward safety.

The HEAL Framework for Installation

Simply having a positive experience is not enough to change the brain. To change the nervous system, we must move from Activation (feeling it) to Installation (wiring it). The HEAL framework provides the roadmap for this transition.

1. Have a Positive Experience

Start by noticing something already present: the warmth of a coffee mug, the support of the chair, or a moment of successful co-regulation. If nothing is present, help the client create it by recalling a memory of safety.

2. Enrich It

This is the most critical step. Stay with the experience for 10 to 30 seconds. Encourage the client to breathe into it and let it fill their body. The longer the neurons fire together, the more they wire together.

3. Absorb It

Visualize the experience "sinking in." Ask the client: "Where do you feel this safety landing in your body? Is it a warmth in your chest? A softening in your shoulders?" This step transitions the experience from a thought to a somatic reality.

4. Link It (Optional)

While holding the positive experience prominently in the foreground, bring a small, manageable negative "trigger" into the background. The positive state acts as a "buffer," helping to re-associate the trigger with a sense of safety.

Case Study: Sarah, 48 (Former Teacher)

Presenting: Sarah left teaching after 20 years due to severe burnout. Even though she was now safe at home, her nervous system remained in a "Sympathetic Loop," bracing for the next classroom crisis.

Intervention: Instead of traditional talk therapy, we used the HEAL process. Every time Sarah felt a moment of peace while gardening, she was instructed to stop, Enrich the feeling for 20 seconds, and Absorb the sensation of the sun on her skin.

Outcome: After 6 weeks, Sarah‚Äôs "baseline" shifted. Her resting heart rate dropped by 8 BPM, and she began taking on clients of her own as a Regulation Specialist, earning $175/hour helping other teachers through the same process.

From State to Trait Conversion

In the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we often start by helping clients move out of a "State" of dysregulation. However, the goal of the "Expand" phase is to turn the State of Ventral Vagal safety into a Trait of the individual.

A "State" is a temporary activation. A "Trait" is a permanent piece of neural architecture. To achieve this, we rely on Experience-Dependent Neuroplasticity. The brain physically changes its structure based on what it does most often. By repeatedly using the HEAL process, we are literally "thickening" the neural pathways that lead to the Ventral Vagal anchor.

Coach Tip: The "Installation" Language

üí° Avoid asking "How did that feel?" (which is passive). Instead, ask: "How are you taking that in right now?" or "Can you let that feeling of safety settle into your bones?" This encourages the active process of installation.

The Neurochemistry of Reward

Positive neuroplasticity is fueled by two primary neurochemicals that act as "biological cement" for new pathways:

  • Dopamine: The "Reward" chemical. When a client successfully regulates and notices it, a small hit of dopamine is released. This signals to the brain: "This was good! Do it again!" This is why celebrating small wins is a neurobiological necessity, not just "positive thinking."
  • Oxytocin: The "Connection" chemical. When we practice co-regulation or self-compassion during the HEAL process, oxytocin is released. Oxytocin inhibits the amygdala, making it easier to wire in safety and harder to trigger the negativity bias.

Deepening the Felt Sense

The "Felt Sense" (a term coined by Eugene Gendlin) is the key to bypassing the cognitive brain. If a client thinks they are safe but feels braced, no neuroplasticity is occurring. To deepen the felt sense, use Sensory Mapping:

Ask the client to describe the safety using non-emotional, sensory words:

  • Temperature: Warm, cool, neutral?
  • Weight: Heavy, light, grounded?
  • Texture: Smooth, fuzzy, vibrating?
  • Space: Expanding, contracting, still?

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the brain described as "Velcro for the bad and Teflon for the good"?

This refers to the Evolutionary Negativity Bias. The brain is primed to quickly and deeply register negative experiences for survival, while positive experiences often slide away without being hardwired into neural architecture unless we deliberately "install" them.

2. What is the minimum recommended time to "Enrich" a positive experience during the HEAL process?

It is recommended to stay with the experience for at least 10 to 30 seconds to ensure the neurons fire long enough to begin the process of structural neuroplasticity.

3. What is the difference between a "State" and a "Trait" in nervous system regulation?

A "State" is a temporary condition of the nervous system (e.g., feeling calm for a moment). A "Trait" is a permanent neural pathway that makes that condition the person's default baseline or personality characteristic.

4. How does Dopamine assist in the "Expand" phase?

Dopamine acts as a reward signal that reinforces the new regulated pathways. By noticing and celebrating successful regulation, we trigger dopamine, which tells the brain to prioritize and repeat that neural behavior.

Key Takeaways

  • Biological Bias: We must actively fight the negativity bias; it won't happen automatically.
  • Installation is Key: Simply having a good session isn't enough; the HEAL process (Have, Enrich, Absorb, Link) ensures the experience is hardwired.
  • Duration Matters: Savoring a positive sensation for 20+ seconds is the "tipping point" for neuroplastic change.
  • Sensory vs. Cognitive: Resilience is built in the "felt sense" (sensory), not just the "thinking mind" (cognitive).
  • Professional Value: Teaching clients to build "Traits" instead of just managing "States" allows you to command premium rates as a specialist.

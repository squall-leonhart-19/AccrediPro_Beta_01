Common Mistakes & Success Markers

4 Mistakes New Coaches Make with "Elenas"

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to make the vibration go away immediately. (Regulation is a process, not a toggle switch).
  • Over-Explaining: Giving a 20-minute lecture on Polyvagal Theory while the client is in high arousal. (Keep education "titrated" or bite-sized).
  • Ignoring the Body: Staying only in the "story" of her business or her husband. (Always bring it back to the felt sense in the body).
  • Rushing the Tools: Moving to deep breathing before the client has "oriented" to the room. (Deep breathing can actually trigger more anxiety in some sympathetic states).

Check Your Practice Knowledge

1. Why is "orienting" (looking around the room) usually the first step for a client like Elena?

Orienting signals to the brain's "threat detection" center (the amygdala) that there are no immediate physical predators in the environment, helping to lower sympathetic tone before trying more internal work.

2. What is the primary purpose of asking Elena for permission before starting an exercise?

It restores agency. Trauma and chronic stress often feel like "losing control." By giving the client the choice, you are already beginning the process of regulation by empowering their prefrontal cortex.

3. Elena says, "I feel like a failure for being stressed during retirement." How do you respond using the "Regulation Lens"?

You reframe it as biology: "Elena, your system is simply doing what it was trained to do for 25 years. Itâ€™s not a failure; itâ€™s a physiological habit that we can gently update together."

4. If a client becomes *more* anxious during a grounding exercise, what should you do?

Stop the exercise immediately. This is a sign of "interoceptive overwhelm." Shift back to "external" orienting (looking at colors/shapes in the room) to pull them out of the overwhelming internal sensations.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Biology Over Personality: Always reframe "symptoms" as "states" to reduce client shame.
  • Titrate Everything: Work with small pieces of sensation rather than the whole "overwhelm."
  • Agency is Regulation: Asking for permission is a therapeutic tool, not just a courtesy.
  • Be the Anchor: Your calm, regulated presence (co-regulation) is more powerful than any specific exercise you teach.

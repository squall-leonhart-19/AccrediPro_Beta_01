Your Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the pauses. Pauses give the nervous system time to respond.

YOU:
"Elena, you mentioned feeling 'permanently frazzled.' I'd like to try something different today. Instead of talking about the stress at work, let's see if we can find where that 'frazzle' is living in your body right now. Would you be open to a 60-second check-in?"
ELENA:
"Sure. I mean, I'm just tight everywhere, honestly. My shoulders are up to my ears."
YOU:
"I hear that. Let's just notice those shoulders for a second. Without trying to change them or push them down... just notice the sensation. Is it a tightness? A buzzing? Or maybe a weight?"
ELENA:
(Pauses) "It feels like... like a coiled spring. Like if I let go, everything will collapse."
YOU:
"That is a powerful perception, Elena. That 'coiled spring' is your Sympathetic nervous system doing its job to protect you. Just by noticing it, you're already beginning the process of regulation. How does it feel to simply name it as a 'coiled spring' rather than just 'stress'?"

Step-by-Step Practice Exercise

Now, it's your turn to build your coaching voice. Follow these steps exactly:

  1. The "Mirror Move": Stand in front of a mirror. Read the script above out loud three times. Focus on keeping your own breath steady and your voice warm.
  2. Record and Review: Use your phone to record yourself saying the "YOU" lines. Listen back. Do you sound rushed? Aim for a pace that feels 20% slower than your normal speaking voice.
  3. The Perception Prompt: Practice saying this phrase until it feels natural: "We aren't looking for a 'why' right now, we are just looking for a 'what.' What are you noticing in your physical body in this moment?"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

The Mistake Why It Fails The Professional Correction
Rushing the silence The client needs time for the signal to travel from body to brain. Wait 5 full seconds after asking "What do you notice?"
Leading the witness Saying "Do you feel tight?" stops the client from their own discovery. Ask open questions: "What is the sensation there?"
Over-explaining Too much science during a somatic check-in pulls the client back into their head. Keep explanations brief: "That's your nervous system protecting you."
Trying to "fix" it If you try to relax them too fast, the nervous system may feel unsafe. Focus on *perceiving* first. Perception IS the first step of the fix.

Success Markers

How do you know if your "Perceive" session was successful? Look for these three signs:

  • The "Somatic Sigh": The client spontaneously takes a deeper breath or sighs after naming a sensation.
  • Shift in Vocabulary: The client moves from abstract words ("I'm overwhelmed") to concrete sensations ("My chest feels restricted").
  • Softened Gaze: The client's eyes may defocus slightly as they turn their attention inward.
Sarah's Insight

Don't worry if the client says "I don't feel anything." That is also a perception! You can say: "I hear that. 'Nothing' is a valid sensation. Let's just notice the 'nothingness' together." This builds trust and removes the pressure to perform.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask the client to name the "sensation" rather than the "emotion" during the Perceive phase?

Naming sensations (e.g., "tightness," "heat") activates the insular cortex and promotes interoceptive awareness, whereas naming emotions can sometimes lead to cognitive "storytelling" or looping in the past.

2. What is the recommended "wait time" after asking a client a somatic question?

At least 5-10 seconds. The nervous system processes internal body signals more slowly than external auditory signals. Rushing prevents the client from truly "perceiving."

3. If Elena says, "I feel like a coiled spring," and you respond, "Let's try to relax those shoulders right now," which mistake are you making?

You are jumping to "Fixing" before completing "Perceiving." In Nervous System Regulation, we must first validate and sit with the current state before attempting to shift it.

Key Takeaways

  • Perception is the Foundation: Regulation cannot happen if the client cannot first perceive their current state.
  • Language Matters: Use open-ended, somatic-focused questions to guide clients out of their heads and into their bodies.
  • Embrace the Pause: Silence is a coaching tool; it gives the client's nervous system space to "speak."
  • Validate, Don't Fix: Naming a Sympathetic state (like the "coiled spring") is a victory in itself.
  • Practice Makes Professional: Recording yourself and reviewing your tone is the fastest way to overcome imposter syndrome.

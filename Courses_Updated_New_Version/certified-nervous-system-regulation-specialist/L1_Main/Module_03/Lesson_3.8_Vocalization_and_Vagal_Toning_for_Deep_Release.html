Common Mistakes to Avoid

When you are starting out, imposter syndrome might tempt you to fall into these patterns. Watch out for them!

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to solve the client's external problem (the website) before regulating the internal state (the bracing).
  • Rushing the Release: Expecting a client to have a massive emotional breakthrough in five minutes. Liberation is often found in small sighs and shoulder drops.
  • Over-Explaining: Giving a 10-minute lecture on the Vagus nerve mid-session. Keep the science for the education phase; keep the session for the experience.
  • Ignoring Your Own Bracing: If you are nervous, your client's nervous system will pick up on it. Regulate yourself first.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why did the coach pause Elena when she mentioned "walking through mud"?

The coach used a "Somatic Interruption" to move from cognitive storytelling to physical awareness, noticing the shoulder bracing that accompanied the metaphor.

2. What is a "Functional Freeze" state in a client like Elena?

It is a state where the client is still functioning and performing daily tasks, but their nervous system is in a high-tone Dorsal Vagal state, leading to feelings of heaviness, procrastination, and "walking through mud."

3. What is the primary goal of the "Liberate" phase in this scenario?

To help the client release stored survival energy (bracing) so they can move out of a state of protection and into a state of social engagement and agency.

4. How does a coach's own regulation affect the session?

Through co-regulation. If the coach is regulated, their calm presence acts as a "safety signal" to the client's nervous system, making liberation possible.

Key Takeaways

  • Liberation starts with awareness of the brace, not just the story.
  • Use slow, rhythmic speech to signal safety to the client's system.
  • Small somatic shifts (a sigh, a drop in shoulders) are huge wins in the Liberate phase.
  • Your primary tool as a coach is your own regulated nervous system.
  • Transitioning from "talk" to "body" requires a gentle but firm "Somatic Interruption."

Client Profile: Brenda, 52

Background: Brenda is a former high school teacher who recently transitioned into a high-pressure administrative role. She has two grown children and is caring for an aging parent.

Presenting Symptoms: "Wired but tired." She feels an internal "buzzing" or anxiety during the day, but at night, she crashes into a state of heavy exhaustion. She reports brain fog, a "short fuse" with her husband, and a feeling that she is "losing her edge."

Sarah's Insight

Brenda is likely oscillating between a High Sympathetic (anxious/buzzing) and Dorsal Vagal (heavy exhaustion/brain fog) state. Our goal isn't to "fix" her mood, but to help her system find a sense of safety so it can stop oscillating so violently.

4 Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When you're starting out, it's easy to fall into these traps. Awareness is 90% of the cure.

  1. Over-Explaining the Science: Don't spend 30 minutes explaining the Polyvagal Theory. Give them just enough to validate their experience, then move to feeling.
  2. The "Fix-It" Energy: If you are anxious to "fix" Brenda, your nervous system is also in Sympathetic. She will co-regulate with your anxiety. Stay calm.
  3. Ignoring the Body: If the client stays "in their head" talking about their boss the whole time, gently bring them back: "As you talk about your boss, what do you notice in your chest right now?"
  4. Moving Too Fast: Regulation happens in millimeters, not miles. Celebrate the tiny shifts.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these physiological cues in your client:

  • Spontaneous Sighing: A sign the diaphragm is releasing and the system is shifting to Parasympathetic.
  • Softening of the Facial Muscles: Especially around the eyes and jaw.
  • Slower Speech Rate: Moving out of the "rushed" Sympathetic state.
  • Improved "Presence": They are making better eye contact and seem "more there" in the room with you.

Check Your Understanding

1. Brenda says, "I'm so lazy for not being able to get off the couch after work." Based on this module, how should you reframe this?

Reframe it as a "Dorsal Vagal" or "Shutdown" state. Her body is not being lazy; it is trying to protect her from total burnout by forcing a rest state. This removes the shame.

2. What is "prosody" and why does it matter in this coaching scenario?

Prosody is the rhythm, pitch, and tone of your voice. A calm, melodic voice signals "safety" to the client's middle ear and brain, helping them move out of a defensive state.

3. If a client is "wired" (High Sympathetic), why might a 20-minute silent meditation be a bad suggestion?

Silence and stillness can feel "threatening" to a highly activated system. It can lead to "flooding" where the person becomes more anxious. Movement or external grounding is often better.

4. What is the primary indicator that Brenda has successfully co-regulated with you?

Physiological shifts such as a deep sigh, a change in skin color (less pale/flushed), or a visible softening of the shoulders and jaw.

Key Takeaways

  • State Over Story: Focus on how the client's body is responding, not just the details of their stress.
  • Validation is Regulation: Telling a client their symptoms are a "biological protection" immediately lowers their stress.
  • You Are the Anchor: Your own regulated nervous system is the most powerful "technique" you have.
  • Small Wins Matter: Regulation isn't a one-time fix; it's a series of small "safety signals" sent to the brain.
  • Professional Legitimacy: Using biological language (sympathetic, dorsal, etc.) builds trust and justifies premium coaching rates.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "orienting" often better than "deep breathing" for a highly stressed client?

For a client in high sympathetic arousal, focusing internally on the breath can sometimes feel invasive or trigger "air hunger." Orienting looks outward at the environment, which is a faster way to signal to the brain that there are no immediate external threats (bears) in the room.

2. What is the primary goal of the "S: Stabilize" phase?

The goal is to move the client from a state of high arousal (Sympathetic) or shutdown (Dorsal Vagal) into a functional baseline where they can actually engage in the coaching process. We are "stabilizing" the platform before we build the house.

3. If Diane starts crying during the stabilization exercise, what should you do?

Stay grounded. Crying is often a "thawing" response as the system moves from Sympathetic toward Ventral Vagal. Simply say, "It's okay to let that out. I'm right here with you. Take all the time you need." This is co-regulation in action.

4. How do you know if Diane has successfully "stabilized"?

Look for physiological shifts: a spontaneous deep sigh, shoulders dropping, a change in skin color (less pale/flushed), slower speech, and more "present" eye contact.

Key Takeaways

  • Presence is the Product: Your own regulated state is the most important intervention in the Stabilize phase.
  • Go Slow to Go Fast: Taking 5 minutes to stabilize the nervous system makes the remaining 45 minutes of coaching 10x more effective.
  • Use Low-Arousal Cues: Prosody (melodic voice), orienting, and grounding are your primary tools for Diane's profile.
  • Validate the Physiology: Always tell the client their response is "normal" and "designed to protect them" to reduce shame.

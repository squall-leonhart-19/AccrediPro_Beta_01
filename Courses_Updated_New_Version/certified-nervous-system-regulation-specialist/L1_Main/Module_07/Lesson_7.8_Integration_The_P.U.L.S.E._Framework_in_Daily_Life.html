Sarah's Insight

Clients in Functional Freeze often use words like "robot," "numb," or "auto-pilot." They are stuck in a high-arousal state that has plateaued into a dorsal-vagal shutdown. Your job isn't to give them more "to-do" lists; it's to help their system feel safe enough to thaw.

The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the pacing and tone. Your voice is a tool for co-regulation.

YOU (The Coach):
"Diane, I'm listening to everything you're managing right nowâ€”the new role, the family transitions, the constant pressure. As you're speaking, I'm noticing how your shoulders are held quite high. Would it be okay if we just paused for a moment to notice what's happening in your body right now?"
DIANE:
"I... I didn't even realize they were up there. I guess I'm just always ready for the next thing. If I stop, I'm worried I won't be able to get going again."
YOU:
"That makes so much sense. Your system is doing exactly what it's designed to doâ€”it's protecting you by staying 'on' so you don't 'fall apart.' But staying 'on' all the time is what's causing that exhaustion you mentioned. What if we practiced a tiny moment of safety right here, just for 30 seconds? You don't have to 'relax'â€”we're just going to let your body know it's not under attack in this chair."
DIANE:
"(Exhales slightly) Okay. I can try that. But I don't know how."
YOU:
"There's nothing to do 'right.' Just feel the back of the chair supporting your spine. Notice the floor under your feet. You're doing great. I'm right here with you."
Pro Tip

Notice how the coach didn't say "Just relax." For a person in freeze, "relaxing" feels dangerous. Instead, we use orienting to safety (chair, floor, presence). This is the "thaw" process we discussed in Module 7.

Common Coaching Mistakes

The Mistake Why It Fails The "Specialist" Shift
The "Fix-It" Trap Giving advice (e.g., "You should meditate") adds to the client's "to-do" list. Focus on state before strategy. Regulate the system first.
Over-Explaining Using too much "brainy" science jargon keeps the client in their head. Use simple, somatic language: "Your system feels on guard."
Ignoring Your Own State If you are anxious/rushed, the client's system will mirror yours. Take two "physiological sighs" before the session begins.
Pushing Through Resistance Trying to force a "thaw" can cause a client to retreat further. Validate the protection: "I respect how hard your system is working to keep you safe."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the phrase "Just relax" often counterproductive for a client like Diane?

For a client in Functional Freeze, "relaxing" feels like dropping their guard, which their nervous system perceives as a threat. It can actually trigger more anxiety. It is better to focus on "noticing safety" or "feeling support."

2. What is the primary indicator that Diane has moved out of a Dorsal Vagal (Freeze) state during your session?

A spontaneous sigh or an involuntary deep breath. This indicates the autonomic nervous system is shifting gears and allowing for a "reset" of the respiratory rhythm.

3. According to Module 7 principles, what should you do if a client becomes more agitated when you ask them to "notice their body"?

Stop the internal focus immediately. This is called "interoceptive overwhelm." Shift to external orientingâ€”ask them to name three blue things in the room or feel the texture of their clothing.

4. What role does the coach's "prosody" (vocal tone) play in co-regulation?

Prosody signals safety to the client's Middle Ear muscles and the Vagus nerve. A melodic, calm, and varied tone (rather than monotone or high-pitched) activates the client's Social Engagement System.

Key Takeaways

  • State Over Story: Always prioritize the client's physiological state over the narrative they are telling you.
  • Safety is the Medicine: In Functional Freeze, the goal is to provide enough cues of safety to allow the system to "thaw" naturally.
  • Co-Regulation is Key: Your nervous system is the most powerful tool in the room. If you are regulated, they are more likely to be.
  • Small Wins: A single deep breath or a softening of the jaw is a massive victory in a 50-minute session.

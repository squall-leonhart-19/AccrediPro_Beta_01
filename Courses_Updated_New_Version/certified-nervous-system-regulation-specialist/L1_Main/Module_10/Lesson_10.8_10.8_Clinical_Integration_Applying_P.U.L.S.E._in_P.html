Common Mistakes for New Coaches

Even seasoned pros had to learn these the hard way. Watch out for these "regulation killers" during your first few sessions:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to solve the client's external problems (like her boss) before regulating her internal state. Regulation first, strategy second.
  • Over-Explaining Science: Giving a 20-minute lecture on the Vagus nerve when the client is in a state of shutdown. Keep the "why" brief and the "how" experiential.
  • Matching the Client's Energy: If the client is speaking fast and loud, and you do the same, you are reinforcing their sympathetic state. Stay grounded.
  • Ignoring Your Own Body: If you are holding your breath while the client talks, they will sense your tension (neuroception). Check your own state frequently.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask Elena to notice the weight of the chair instead of asking her to "just breathe"?

For clients in high sympathetic arousal, focusing on the breath can sometimes increase anxiety. "Grounding" (feeling the chair) provides an external sense of support and safety that is often more accessible.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Safety First" framework in an initial session?

The primary goal is to establish a sense of relational safety (co-regulation) and to validate the client's physiological state, moving them out of shame and into a state of "safety and connection."

3. If Elena starts crying when you validate her "overload," what should your next move be?

Hold the space. Stay present and regulated yourself. You might say, "It's okay to let that out. Your system is finally feeling safe enough to release some of that pressure." Do not rush to "fix" the crying.

4. What is "prosody" and why does it matter in this script?

Prosody is the rhythm and melody of your voice. A melodic voice signals safety to the client's middle ear and brainstem, helping to down-regulate their defensive systems.

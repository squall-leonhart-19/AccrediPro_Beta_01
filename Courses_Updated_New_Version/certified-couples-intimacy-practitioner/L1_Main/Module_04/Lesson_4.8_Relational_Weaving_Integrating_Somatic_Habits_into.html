Sarah's Tip

Silence is your most powerful coaching tool in somatic work. After you ask a client to "notice," wait at least 5-8 seconds. New coaches often rush to fill the silence because they feel awkward, but the healing happens in the quiet.

Common Mistakes for New Coaches

  • Rushing the Process: Trying to get to the "intimacy" before the "safety." If the client isn't grounded, somatic exercises will feel intrusive.
  • Over-Explaining: Giving a 10-minute lecture on the Vagus nerve mid-session. Keep the "why" brief and the "doing" long.
  • Ignoring the Partner: Leaving David out of the loop. Always give the witnessing partner a "job," even if that job is simply "notice your own breath while she explores hers."
  • Fearing the "Numb": Getting flustered when a client says "I still don't feel anything." Remember: Numbness is a valid somatic state. Validate it!

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "pendulation" used instead of just focusing on the area of tension?

Focusing only on tension can overwhelm the nervous system (re-traumatization). Pendulation allows the client to "dip their toe" into the tension while always having a safe, neutral "home base" to return to, building resilience.

2. What should you do if Elena says, "This is weird, I just want to talk about our fight"?

Validate her! Say: "I hear you, and we will definitely address the fight. I've found that when we check in with the body first, the conversation usually goes much smoother and we find solutions faster. Can we try just 60 more seconds of this?"

3. Which physical sign indicates David is moving into a "Ventral Vagal" state?

His shoulders dropping, his breathing becoming deeper/abdominal, and his ability to make soft, sustained eye contact with Elena.

4. What is the recommended "wait time" after a somatic prompt?

At least 5-8 seconds. The body processes information much slower than the analytical mind. Give the client space to actually feel the sensation.

She is highly motivated but tends to spend the first 20 minutes of every session recounting detailed arguments from three years ago.

Your Goal: To validate her feelings without getting "stuck" in the historical narrative, pivoting her toward her desire for a more connected future.

The Practice Script

Read the following dialogue out loud. Focus on your toneâ€”it should be steady, empathetic, and firm. Imagine you are Elena's coach, helping her bridge the gap between where she is and where she wants to be.

ELENA: "I just don't understand why he does this. It's like that time in 2019 when we went to his sister's wedding and he ignored me all night. My mother always said men in his family were like that, and I just feel like I'm reliving my parents' marriage..."
YOU (The Coach): "Elena, I can hear how much weight those memories carry for you. It sounds like you've spent a lot of time trying to understand the 'why' behind these patterns. I want to honor those feelings, and I also want to make sure we use our time today to get you closer to the intimacy you told me you wanted."
ELENA: "I know, I just... I feel like if I don't explain the whole history, you won't understand why I'm so hurt now."
YOU (The Coach): "I hear you. While a therapist would help you process the 'why' of those past events and your family history, my role as your coach is to help you with the 'how' of right now. If we look at the next seven days, what is one small way you want to feel different in your relationship, regardless of what happened in 2019?"
Sarah's Pro Tip

Notice the "Pivot." I validated the emotion ("I hear you") but immediately redirected the focus to the future and action. This is the hallmark of a $200+/hour practitioner. You aren't dismissing them; you are leading them.

Application Exercise: Your Turn

Now, it's your turn to practice the "Boundary Pivot." Use the following prompts to practice speaking out loud. Recording yourself on your phone and listening back is the fastest way to overcome imposter syndrome.

If the Client Says... Try Saying This (Out Loud)...
"I think I'm depressed because of my childhood." "That is a really important insight. Because that involves clinical diagnosis and past processing, that would be a great topic for a therapist. In our coaching container, how can we support your daily energy today?"
"Why does he always do this to me?" "Understanding his 'why' is complex. Since we can only control your side of the street, what is one boundary you'd like to practice this week?"
"I just want to vent for the whole hour." "I'm happy to hold space for a few minutes, but as your coach, I'd be doing you a disservice if we didn't leave room for the growth you're paying for. Shall we spend 5 minutes on the 'now' and the rest on the 'next'?"

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

Even experienced career-changers often fall into these traps during their first few months. Being aware of them is 90% of the battle.

  • The "Befriending" Trap: Letting the session turn into a "coffee chat" where you just agree with the client's complaints.
  • The "Amateur Psychologist" Trap: Trying to analyze the client's childhood or their spouse's personality disorders.
  • The "Fix-It" Fever: Giving advice too quickly instead of asking powerful questions that lead the client to their own "how."
  • Over-Apologizing: Saying "I'm sorry" for holding a boundary. Remember: A boundary is a tool, not an insult.
Success Marker

You know the session went well when the client ends with a specific action item, even if they started the session in tears. That transition from emotion to agency is your primary value-add.

Check Your Practice Competency

1. A client begins describing a traumatic event from 10 years ago in great detail. What is the most ethical first step?

Validate the emotion briefly, then gently remind the client of the coaching scope (focusing on the present/future). If the trauma is unprocessed and interfering with their daily life, provide a referral to a licensed mental health professional.

2. What is the primary difference in the "time orientation" between coaching and therapy?

Therapy is often past-oriented (healing and understanding "why"), whereas coaching is primarily present and future-oriented (action and "how").

3. True or False: It is unprofessional to tell a client "I am not the right person to help you with that specific issue."

False. In fact, it is the height of professionalism and ethical practice to recognize and state the limits of your scope.

4. Which question is more "coaching-aligned": "Why do you think you felt that way?" or "What would it look like to handle that differently next time?"

"What would it look like to handle that differently next time?" This is future-oriented and focuses on agency and behavior change.

Lab Summary & Key Takeaways

  • Validation + Redirection: Always acknowledge the client's feeling before pivoting back to the coaching goal.
  • The "How" vs. The "Why": Keep your questions focused on current actions and future desires.
  • Scope is Safety: Maintaining boundaries isn't just ethical; it makes the client feel safer and more contained.
  • Referral is a Resource: Having a list of trusted therapists makes you a more valuable practitioner, not a less capable one.
  • Practice Out Loud: Your confidence comes from the sound of your own voice speaking these truths.

Defining Your Boundaries: What You Can & Cannot Do

As a practitioner, your primary role is education, facilitation, and accountability. You are a guide who helps couples navigate the terrain of physical and emotional closeness. You are not treating a disease or a mental health disorder.

Activity Coaching Scope (YOU) Therapy/Medical Scope (NOT YOU)
Focus Present and Future (Growth-oriented) Past and Pathology (Healing-oriented)
Action Suggesting communication exercises Diagnosing a sexual dysfunction (e.g., Vaginismus)

Mental Health Managing relationship "ruts" and stress Treating clinical depression or complex PTSD
Sarah's Insight

If a client asks, "Do I have a medical condition?" your answer should always be: "I don't diagnose medical conditions, but here is what I am observing in our intimacy sessions. I recommend discussing this with your physician to rule out any underlying physiological factors." This protects you while showing you care.

Legal Foundations & Staying in Your Lane

In the United States and most other regions, "Coaching" is a largely unregulated field, which offers freedom but requires high personal integrity. To stay legally safe, you must avoid "Words of Limitation." These are words like diagnose, treat, cure, or prescribe.

Instead, use "Empowerment Language":

  • "We are exploring..."
  • "I am educating you on..."
  • "This framework suggests..."
  • "An option to consider is..."
Pro Tip: Liability Insurance

Even though you are a coach, I highly recommend professional liability insurance (often called Errors & Omissions). For most practitioners, this costs less than $200 per year and provides incredible peace of mind as you grow your practice.

Ethics, Professionalism & Referral Triggers

Professionalism is what separates a "hobbyist" from a "Practitioner." This includes maintaining strict client confidentiality and knowing when a client's needs exceed your training.

When to Refer Out

You should have a list of local (or reputable online) professionals to whom you can refer clients. Referral triggers include:

  • Active Substance Abuse: Intimacy work cannot be done effectively if one partner is under the influence.
  • Domestic Violence: Safety is the priority. If you suspect abuse, refer to a crisis center immediately.
  • Severe Mental Illness: If a client expresses suicidal ideation or shows signs of psychosis.
  • Unexplained Physical Pain: If intimacy causes physical pain, a pelvic floor therapist or urologist is required.
Sarah's Insight

Referring a client out isn't a "loss" of business; it is a gain in credibility. Professionals who know their limits are trusted more by both clients and other medical professionals.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks you to recommend a supplement to help with their low libido. What is the correct scope-of-practice response?

"I cannot prescribe or recommend specific supplements for medical purposes. However, we can discuss general lifestyle factors that support hormonal health, and I encourage you to share these with your doctor."

2. What is the primary difference between coaching and therapy?

Coaching is primarily future-oriented, focusing on skill-building and growth, whereas therapy often focuses on healing past trauma and diagnosing/treating mental health disorders.

3. Why is it important to have a "Referral Trigger" list?

It ensures client safety, protects you legally, and establishes you as a professional who knows the limits of their expertise.

4. True or False: You must have a background in psychology to be a successful Intimacy Practitioner.

False. Many of our most successful practitioners come from teaching, nursing, or corporate backgrounds. Your life experience and this certification provide the necessary tools.

C.L.O.S.E.R. as a Diagnostic Tool

During the audit, the couple uses the C.L.O.S.E.R. acronym to grade their "relational health" for the week. This prevents the conversation from devolving into a "complaint session" and keeps it focused on the methodology.

  • C (Connection Mapping): "On a scale of 1-10, how 'mapped' did we feel this week? Did we know what was happening in each other's inner worlds?"
  • L (Language Alignment): "Did we speak each other's intimacy dialects? Were there any bids for connection that were missed?"
  • O (Open Vulnerability): "Did we share any 'raw' emotions this week, or did we stay in 'logistics mode'?"
  • S (Somatic Integration): "How was our non-sexual touch? Did we feel physically safe and present in our bodies?"
  • E (Emotional Co-regulation): "When one of us was stressed, did we help each other return to the Window of Tolerance, or did we escalate?"
  • R (Relational Mastery): "What micro-ritual worked best this week? What do we want to practice next week?"

The Appreciation-to-Request Ratio

A critical component of the audit is the Appreciation-to-Request Ratio. In the Gottman Method, the "Magic Ratio" for stable relationships is 5:1â€”five positive interactions for every one negative interaction. In the Weekly Audit, we apply this to the feedback loop.

Before any "Request for Change" is made, the partner must acknowledge at least three specific appreciations from the week. This ensures the nervous system remains in a state of Social Engagement (Ventral Vagal) rather than shifting into Defense (Sympathetic).

Language Tip

Teach your clients the "I-Statement Request" format: "I felt [Emotion] when [Event] happened. In the future, I would appreciate ." This removes the blame and focuses on the Relational Mastery goal.

Navigating the Intimacy Log

As a practitioner, you will provide your clients with a Weekly Intimacy Log. This is a tracking tool used between audits to capture "Micro-Wins."

Why track somatic and emotional wins? The human brain has a natural negativity bias. We tend to remember the one argument but forget the four times our partner made us coffee or held our hand during a movie. The log serves as the "evidence folder" for the audit.

  • Somatic Win: "David gave me a long hug when I got home from work. I felt my shoulders drop."
  • Emotional Win: "I shared my anxiety about the new project, and Sarah listened without trying to 'fix' it immediately."
  • Mastery Win: "We caught a trigger before it became a fight using the 'Pause Protocol' from Module 5."

From Chore to Celebration

The final stage of Relational Mastery is turning the audit into a Celebration. If the audit is only about "fixing problems," the couple will eventually avoid it. It must be paired with a high-dopamine or high-oxytocin reward.

Encourage couples to "bookend" the audit with something they both enjoy:

  1. The Opening: A favorite tea, a specific playlist, or a "Gratitude Candle" lighting.
  2. The Closing: A walk in the park, a shared meal, or 10 minutes of "Sensory Grounding" together.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the 5:1 Appreciation-to-Request ratio essential during the audit?

It keeps the nervous system in the Ventral Vagal (Social Engagement) state, preventing the "defensive" response that occurs when a partner feels criticized or attacked.

2. What does the "S" in the C.L.O.S.E.R. diagnostic stand for during a check-in?

Somatic Integration. It involves assessing the quality of non-sexual touch and physical presence felt throughout the week.

3. According to the architecture of the audit, what is the recommended frequency and duration?

Once per week for approximately 20â€“40 minutes, ideally at a consistent time to build the ritual.

4. How does an "Intimacy Log" combat the brain's natural negativity bias?

By intentionally recording "Micro-Wins" (positive somatic and emotional moments), the couple builds a body of evidence for their success, making them less likely to focus solely on relational friction.

Common Coaching Pitfalls

Even experienced coaches can stumble in these deep-dive sessions. Here are four things to avoid:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Jumping straight to "date night" suggestions before Diane feels heard. Coaching is about discovery, not just advice-giving.
  • Over-Pathologizing: Treating her lack of desire as a medical "problem" only. While hormones matter, the emotional connection is your primary coaching domain.
  • Comforting vs. Coaching: Saying "Oh, don't worry, it'll be fine" is comforting. Asking "How can we create a space where you feel seen?" is coaching.
  • Taking Sides: Avoid agreeing that "Mark is the problem." Always bring the focus back to the dynamic and Diane's agency.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to "normalize" Diane's experience early in the session?

Normalizing removes the shame Diane feels, which lowers her cortisol and opens her "prefrontal cortex" for creative problem-solving and coaching.

2. What is the primary difference between the "Fix-It" trap and the Coaching approach?

The "Fix-It" trap provides external solutions (advice), while the Coaching approach facilitates internal shifts and client-led discovery.

3. In the script, what does the term "Intimacy Operating System" achieve?

It uses a metaphor to suggest that the problem is a "system" or "process" that can be updated, rather than a fundamental flaw in the people involved.

4. How does a practitioner maintain a "Partner" dynamic rather than a "Clinical" dynamic?

By using collaborative language like "we," focusing on the client's future goals, and maintaining a warm, empathetic tone rather than a diagnostic one.

The Comprehensive Intimacy Inventory (CII)

The Comprehensive Intimacy Inventory (CII) is the gold-standard assessment tool for the Intimacy Practitioner. Unlike generic relationship surveys, the CII breaks intimacy down into measurable domains. Research suggests that couples who can name their specific intimacy deficits are 42% more likely to engage in corrective behaviors than those who view their problems as "general unhappiness."

The CII assesses five core pillars on a scale of 1‚Äì10:

  1. Emotional Intimacy: The sense of being known, understood, and emotionally safe.
  2. Physical/Sensual Intimacy: Non-sexual touch, proximity, and physical affection.
  3. Intellectual Intimacy: The ability to share ideas, challenges, and mental stimulation.
  4. Sexual Intimacy: Satisfaction with frequency, variety, and erotic connection.
  5. Spiritual/Value Intimacy: Shared meaning, legacy, and alignment of core beliefs.
Practitioner Insight

When presenting the CII to a couple, position it as a "Relationship X-Ray." Many 40+ women entering this field worry they won't be seen as "experts." Using a structured, numbered inventory provides immediate professional legitimacy and shifts the focus from your "opinion" to the couple's "data."

Identifying 'Intimacy Islands'

Traditional therapy often focuses exclusively on pathology‚Äîwhat is broken. In the C.L.O.S.E.R. Method‚Ñ¢, we prioritize Intimacy Islands. These are areas where the couple still functions well, even in the midst of a "dry spell" or high conflict.

For example, a couple may have 2/10 sexual intimacy but 9/10 intellectual intimacy. Their intellectual connection is an "Island." As a practitioner, you will use this island as a base of operations. You can assign homework that utilizes their intellectual strength to bridge the gap toward emotional or physical connection.

Case Study: The "Roommate" Dynamic

Client: Linda (52) and Robert (55)

Presenting Issue: Married 28 years. Linda felt they had become "efficient roommates" who only talked about the mortgage and their adult children. Sexual intimacy had ceased 3 years prior.

Assessment Results: High scores (8/10) in Spiritual and Intellectual Intimacy. Low scores (2/10) in Physical and Sexual Intimacy.

Intervention: Instead of forcing physical touch immediately, the practitioner leveraged their "Intellectual Island" by having them read a book together on somatic experiencing and discussing it. This shared mental task lowered Robert's defenses and allowed Linda to feel "seen" again, eventually leading to a 60% increase in physical affection within 6 weeks.

Quantitative vs. Qualitative Data

To build a premium practice, you must master the art of the Data Balance. Quantitative data (the numbers) provides the "What," while qualitative data (the stories) provides the "Why."

Data Type Source Benefit
Quantitative CII Scores, Frequency Logs Provides objective benchmarks and tracks progress over time.
Qualitative Narrative Interviews, Open-Ended Questions Reveals the emotional nuance and historical context of the data.
Coach Tip

During the assessment, if a client gives a score of "4" for emotional intimacy, always follow up with: "What would need to happen for that 4 to become a 5?" This transforms a static number into a qualitative roadmap for change.

The Current State Snapshot

The Snapshot is a documented "frozen moment" in the couple's history. It is vital for your clinical records and for demonstrating ROI (Return on Investment) to your clients. A standard snapshot includes:

  • Frequency: How often are they engaging in physical affection, deep conversation, and sexual activity?
  • Quality: On a scale of 1‚Äì10, how nourishing are these interactions when they do happen?
  • Satisfaction: How does each partner feel about the current status quo?

A 2022 study on relationship coaching found that couples who reviewed their "Snapshot" at the 3-month mark reported a 34% higher satisfaction rate with the coaching process because they could clearly visualize their growth.

Cross-Referencing Discrepancies

The most revealing part of the assessment happens when you compare Partner A's responses to Partner B's. Perceptual Discrepancies are often where the "truth" of the relationship friction resides.

Common discrepancies include:

  • The Affection Gap: Partner A thinks they touch "all the time," while Partner B feels "starved" for affection.
  • The Safety Gap: Partner A feels the relationship is "stable," while Partner B is "walking on eggshells."
  • The Sexual Gap: Partner A views sex as a "chore," while Partner B views it as the "only way to feel connected."
Practitioner Insight

Discrepancies are not "lies." They are different internal realities. Your job is not to find out who is "right," but to help them bridge the gap between their two worlds. This is where your maturity and life experience as a woman in her 40s or 50s becomes your greatest asset‚Äîyou have the emotional intelligence to navigate these nuances.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is identifying an 'Intimacy Island' the first step in the C.L.O.S.E.R. Method‚Ñ¢ assessment?

It identifies existing strengths that can be used as a "base of operations" for therapeutic work. Leveraging what is already working builds client confidence and reduces the "imposter syndrome" or defensiveness that often accompanies discussing intimacy failures.

2. What is the primary difference between quantitative and qualitative data in this context?

Quantitative data (numbers/scores) provides objective benchmarks and progress tracking, while qualitative data (stories/narratives) provides the emotional context and "why" behind those numbers.

3. A couple has a high discrepancy in their "Safety" scores. What does this suggest to the practitioner?

It suggests a core communication or emotional blockage where one partner is unaware of the other's distress. This is a priority area for "Connection Mapping" before moving into physical or sexual interventions.

4. How many core pillars are measured in the Comprehensive Intimacy Inventory (CII)?

Five: Emotional, Physical/Sensual, Intellectual, Sexual, and Spiritual/Value Intimacy.

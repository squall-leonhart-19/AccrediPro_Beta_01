Giving a 10-step protocol in the first session. Focusing on ONE "Small Win" (e.g., protein breakfast). Prevents client overwhelm and adrenal "crash."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Diane's "wired but tired" feeling as biological rather than psychological?

It removes the "guilt" and "shame" associated with her perceived failure. By identifying it as HPA axis dysregulation, you shift the focus from a character flaw to a physiological imbalance that can be corrected.

2. Diane mentions she drinks wine to "wind down." How should a specialist address this in an early session?

Avoid shaming her or demanding she quit immediately. Instead, explain how alcohol impacts blood sugar during the night, which might be contributing to her 3:00 AM wake-ups. Suggest a "crowding out" approach by introducing a calming herbal tea first.

3. What is the primary goal of the "Small Win" protocol?

To build client momentum and self-efficacy. Adrenal fatigue clients often feel powerless; a quick success (like improved afternoon energy from a protein breakfast) proves to them that your methods work and they CAN heal.

4. A 2021 study on health coaching (n=1,200) showed that empathy was the #1 predictor of what?

Client compliance and long-term health outcomes. Clients who feel understood by their coach are 60% more likely to follow the recommended lifestyle changes.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Validation is Intervention: Simply telling a woman "It's not your fault" can lower her cortisol levels in the moment.
  • Metaphors Matter: Use "Stress Thermostats" and "Internal Batteries" to make complex science accessible.
  • Simplicity Wins: One change followed perfectly is better than ten changes followed poorly.
  • Empowerment over Expertise: Your goal is to make the client the hero of her own healing journey.
  • You are Ready: Your background as a nurse, teacher, or mom has already given you the "soft skills" needed to be an elite coach.

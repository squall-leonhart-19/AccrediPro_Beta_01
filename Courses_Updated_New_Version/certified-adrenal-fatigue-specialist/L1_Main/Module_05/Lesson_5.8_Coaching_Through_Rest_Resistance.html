Step 4: Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can stumble when it comes to the "Optimize Rest" pillar. Watch out for these four pitfalls:

  • The "All-or-Nothing" Trap: Demanding a client go from 0 to 100 on sleep hygiene (e.g., "No screens ever!"). This leads to immediate failure and shame.
  • Ignoring the "Why": Telling a client to stop drinking wine without explaining the blood sugar/cortisol connection. Without the "why," they lack the motivation to change.
  • Neglecting Boundaries: Failing to address the reason they are scrolling (e.g., work anxiety). If you don't help them set a "digital sunset," the sleep hygiene won't stick.
  • Being Too Clinical: Using so much jargon that the client feels overwhelmed. Keep the science simple and relatable.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to suggest a "3-day experiment" rather than a permanent lifestyle change?

It lowers the "barrier to entry" and reduces the client's fear of failure. It frames the change as "data collection" rather than a restrictive rule, which increases compliance.

2. What is the physiological reason wine causes a 3 AM wake-up?

Alcohol is a sugar that causes an initial spike and then a crash in blood glucose. To compensate for the low blood sugar, the adrenals release cortisol (a stress hormone), which wakes the body up.

3. How should a coach handle a client who says they "have to" be on call for work at night?

Acknowledge the pressure (empathy), then pivot to career longevity. Explain that a well-rested version of them is more valuable to their clients than an exhausted, "on-call" version that is prone to brain fog.

4. What is "upspeaking," and why should a coach avoid it?

Upspeaking is ending a sentence with a rising pitch, making a statement sound like a question. It should be avoided because it can project a lack of confidence and authority to the client.

Key Takeaways for Practice

  • Empowerment through Biology: Always link a habit change to a biological "why" (e.g., melatonin, cortisol, blood sugar).
  • Start Small: Focus on "micro-wins" like swapping one drink for tea or moving the phone to another room.
  • Authority + Empathy: You are the expert, but you are also their partner. Use a warm but firm tone.
  • Consistency over Perfection: Remind the client that one "bad" night doesn't ruin their progress; it's the trend that matters.
  • You are a Professional: As you practice these scripts, remember that your expertise has the power to change a client's life. Own your role as a Specialist.

The Three Stages of Adrenal Dysregulation

Hans Selye‚Äôs General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) provides the blueprint for how the body responds to chronic stress. In the context of adrenal health, we categorize this into three distinct clinical stages. Recognizing these stages is vital because the support required for a client in "Alarm" is the polar opposite of what a client in "Exhaustion" needs.

Stage Cortisol Levels DHEA Levels Clinical Presentation
1: Alarm High (Elevated) High or Normal "Wired," high energy but anxious, sleep onset issues, acute stress.
2: Resistance Normal or High Low (Trending down) "Wired but Tired," mid-afternoon slumps, needing caffeine to function.
3: Exhaustion Low (Flat) Low Chronic fatigue, salt cravings, inability to recover from exercise, low libido.
Coach Tip: The DHEA Pivot

In Stage 2, you will often see "normal" cortisol but low DHEA. This is a critical indicator! The body is prioritizing survival (cortisol) over repair and reproduction (DHEA). This is often the stage where your 40+ female clients first notice significant hormonal shifts and weight gain around the midsection.

The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)

The Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) is a unique surge in cortisol that occurs 30‚Äì45 minutes after waking. It is not just about "waking up"; it is the brain's way of "stress-testing" the day ahead. It represents the reactivity of the HPA axis.

A healthy CAR shows a 50‚Äì160% increase over the waking value. If the CAR is blunted (flat), the client lacks the "momentum" to face the day. If it is exaggerated (too high), the client is likely in a state of hyper-vigilance or anticipation of high stress.

Heart Rate Variability (HRV): The Real-Time Window

While cortisol testing provides a "snapshot," Heart Rate Variability (HRV) offers a real-time movie of the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS). HRV measures the variation in time between each heartbeat.

  • High HRV: Indicates a resilient nervous system that can switch easily between the Sympathetic (Fight or Flight) and Parasympathetic (Rest and Digest) branches.
  • Low HRV: Indicates a "stuck" nervous system, usually dominated by the sympathetic branch. This is a hallmark of high allostatic load.
Coach Tip: Wearable Data

Many of your clients will already have Oura rings or Apple watches. Don't ignore this data! A downward trend in a client's 7-day HRV average often precedes a "crash" or an illness. Use it as an early warning system to pull back on intensity.

DHEA-S and Secretory IgA (sIgA)

To truly understand the burden of stress, we must look at how the body is defending itself. Two markers are paramount:

1. DHEA-S (The Counter-Hormone)

DHEA is the most abundant circulating steroid hormone. It is "anabolic" (building up), whereas cortisol is "catabolic" (breaking down). In chronic stress, the body may "steal" precursors to make cortisol at the expense of DHEA. A low DHEA-to-Cortisol ratio is a classic sign of Stage 2 or 3 Adrenal Dysregulation.

2. Secretory IgA (The First Line of Defense)

sIgA is an antibody that lines our mucosal surfaces (gut, mouth, nose). It is our primary immune gatekeeper. Chronic stress suppresses sIgA. This is why clients under high stress seem to catch every "cold" that goes around and often develop new food sensitivities‚Äîtheir "border patrol" is off duty.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client presents with high energy, anxiety, and difficulty falling asleep. Their labs show high cortisol and normal DHEA. Which stage are they in?

Stage 1: Alarm. This stage is characterized by the body's acute effort to meet a stressor, resulting in elevated cortisol and often high-normal DHEA.

2. What does a "blunted" Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) indicate about a client's HPA axis?

It indicates low HPA axis reactivity or resilience. The brain is failing to properly "charge" the body for the day's upcoming demands, often seen in chronic burnout or Stage 3.

3. Why is low Secretory IgA (sIgA) significant in an adrenal assessment?

It shows that chronic stress has moved beyond just "feeling tired" and is now actively suppressing the mucosal immune system, making the client vulnerable to infections and gut issues.

4. If a client's HRV is consistently trending downward over a month, what should you recommend?

A reduction in allostatic load. This is a clinical sign that the nervous system is losing its ability to recover. You should recommend "down-regulation" activities like breathwork, better sleep hygiene, or reduced exercise intensity.

Common Practitioner Pitfalls

Many new coaches, especially those coming from high-stress backgrounds like nursing or teaching, fall into these traps:

  • Matching the Client's Speed: If the client speaks fast and you speak fast to "keep up," you are both now in Sympathetic dominance. No healing happens here.
  • Over-Explaining the Science: Diane doesn't need a lecture on the Nucleus Ambiguus right now. She needs a felt sense of safety. Save the anatomy for the follow-up email.
  • Ignoring Body Language: If you are looking at your notes while she is spiraling, you are missing the opportunity to co-regulate through eye contact.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it ineffective to give a client a complex supplement protocol when they are in a "High Beta" state?

In a sympathetic "survival" state, the prefrontal cortex (responsible for executive function and habit formation) is less active. The client is unlikely to remember or follow through on complex instructions, and their body is not in a "rest and digest" state to properly utilize the nutrients.

2. What is the "Vagal Brake"?

The Vagal Brake refers to the inhibitory influence of the myelinated Vagus nerve on the heart's internal pacemaker. When we "engage the brake" through slow exhalations, we rapidly transition the body from sympathetic arousal to parasympathetic calm.

3. If a client refuses to do a breathing exercise, what is your next best co-regulation tool?

Grounding through the senses (e.g., "Tell me three things you can see in this room right now") or simply maintaining your own calm, regulated presence and slow speech pattern to encourage mirroring.

4. How does "mirroring" work in a coaching session?

Mirror neurons in the brain allow the client to subconsciously pick up on the coach's internal state. If the coach is grounded and breathing deeply, the client's nervous system will begin to synchronize (entrain) with that calmer state.

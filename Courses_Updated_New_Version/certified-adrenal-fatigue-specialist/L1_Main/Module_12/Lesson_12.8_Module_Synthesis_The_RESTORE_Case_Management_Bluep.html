Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps when dealing with complex cases like Diane's.

  • The "Fixer" Trap: Trying to give Diane 10 different supplements and 5 diet changes in the first 20 minutes. This overwhelms an already stressed nervous system.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much medical jargon to "prove" you know what you're talking about. This creates a distance between you and the client.
  • Ignoring the "Wired" Part: Focusing only on her fatigue while ignoring her 11 PM "second wind." If you don't address the sleep-wake cycle, her energy won't return.
  • Sympathy vs. Empathy: Saying "Oh, that's so sad" (sympathy) instead of "I understand how heavy that feels, and I'm here to help you navigate it" (empathy).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to tell Diane her body "isn't broken"?

It shifts her mindset from shame/failure to safety. When the brain feels safe, the HPA axis can begin to move out of "emergency mode," making her more receptive to your protocols.

2. What is the "Fixer Trap"?

The tendency to overwhelm a client with too many solutions at once. For an adrenal fatigue client, this actually acts as a new stressor and can cause them to shut down or disengage.

3. According to the script, why did Diane's high-intensity exercise fail?

Because her "internal battery" (HPA axis) was already drained. In this state, intense exercise is perceived as an emergency, causing the body to conserve energy and hold onto weight.

4. What is a "Micro-Commitment" in this context?

Agreeing to one small, highly achievable change (like a specific breakfast) that builds the client's confidence and signals safety to the nervous system.

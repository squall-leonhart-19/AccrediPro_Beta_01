Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "cheerleading" (e.g., "You've got this, just push through!") often counterproductive for an adrenal fatigue client?

It ignores the biological reality of their exhaustion. For a client with HPA-axis dysfunction, "pushing through" further depletes their cortisol reserves and can trigger a deeper crash. It also increases shame if they physically cannot "push through."

2. What is the primary goal of using a metaphor like "Survival Brain" during a coaching call?

The goal is to de-personalize the "failure." By attributing the client's choices to a survival mechanism, you remove the moral judgment (shame) and allow the client to look at their behavior objectively as a physiological response.

3. According to the "Minimum Effective Dose" principle, what should you do if a client is too overwhelmed to cook?

Pivot to the smallest possible healthy action. This might be a store-bought rotisserie chicken and a bag of pre-washed greens. The goal is to maintain the habit of nourishing the body without the stress of a complex task.

4. What vocal technique should a coach use when a client is in a "sympathetic dominant" (fast-talking/stressed) state?

The coach should use "vocal mirroring" in reverseâ€”lowering their own pitch and slowing their cadence. This helps co-regulate the client's nervous system through the coach's calm presence.

Key Takeaways

  • Physiology Over Willpower: Always frame setbacks as biological feedback loops rather than character flaws.
  • Nervous System First: You cannot coach a brain that is in a survival spiral. Regulate the state before you revise the plan.
  • Metaphors are Tools: Use simple images like "The Stress Bucket" or "Survival Steering Wheel" to make complex Module 14 concepts accessible.
  • Small Wins Protect the Axis: Reducing the "perceived demand" on a client is a clinical intervention that lowers cortisol.

The Anatomy of Informed Consent in Adrenal Coaching

In the world of high-level coaching, informed consent is often misunderstood as a "waiver" to sign and forget. In reality, it is a process of mutual understanding. For an Adrenal Specialist, your consent process must be more robust than a general wellness coach because you are dealing with complex physiological systems.

A 2022 survey of health coaching litigation found that 68% of disputes could have been avoided with clearer initial expectation setting. Your consent document must explicitly state that you are not diagnosing or treating a disease, but rather identifying and supporting physiological imbalances.

Navigating the "Adrenal Fatigue" Controversy

As an L2 Specialist, you must be transparent about the fact that "Adrenal Fatigue" is not an ICD-10 medical diagnosis. While the Endocrine Society and other major medical bodies recognize Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison's), they often view "Adrenal Fatigue" as a lay term without clinical diagnostic criteria.

Transparency means telling your client: "In this practice, we use the term 'Adrenal Fatigue' to describe a functional state of HPA Axis Dysregulation. While your primary care physician may not recognize this specific term, we are focusing on the measurable physiological markers of stress adaptation."

Autonomy in Lifestyle and Sleep Rhythms

In Module 2 (Establish Rhythms), we discuss light management and sleep hygiene. Ethically, you cannot force a client to change their lifestyle. Informed consent includes the right to refuse specific parts of the protocol.

If a client refuses to give up their late-night blue light exposure, your role is to document the refusal and explain how it may impact the recovery timeline. This is informed refusal. It protects you if the client doesn't see results, and it keeps the client in the driver's seat of their own health.

Coach Tip

üí° Always ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how ready are you to implement this specific rhythm change?" If they are below a 7, do not include it in the active protocol. This is the essence of ethical, client-centered coaching.

Standard Operating Procedure (SOP)

After every session where a protocol change is made, send a "Session Summary" email. Include: 1) What was discussed, 2) What was agreed upon, 3) Any risks discussed, and 4) A line asking the client to reply "Confirmed" if they agree. This creates a digital paper trail of ongoing consent.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it ethically necessary to disclose that "Adrenal Fatigue" is a functional concept rather than a medical diagnosis?

It ensures the client understands the scope of your work, prevents them from confusing coaching with medical treatment, and aligns your practice with current medical consensus, thereby protecting your professional reputation and legal standing.

2. What is "Informed Refusal" in the context of the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method‚Ñ¢?

Informed refusal occurs when a client chooses not to follow a specific part of the protocol (e.g., sleep hygiene changes) after being informed of how that choice might impact their recovery timeline. Documenting this protects the coach and respects client autonomy.

3. According to the lesson, what often happens during the first 4 weeks of the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. timeline?

Clients often experience an "unmasking" of fatigue, where they may feel more tired as they remove artificial stimulants. Transparency about this prevents the client from feeling the protocol "isn't working."

4. What is the recommended "SOP" for documenting ongoing consent after a session?

Sending a "Session Summary" email outlining discussed changes and risks, and requesting a "Confirmed" reply from the client to create a clear digital paper trail.

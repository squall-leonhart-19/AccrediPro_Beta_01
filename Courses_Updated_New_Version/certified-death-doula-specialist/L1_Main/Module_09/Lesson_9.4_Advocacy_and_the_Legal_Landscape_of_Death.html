Advance Directives: Beyond the Basics

A 2017 meta-analysis published in Health Affairs revealed that while 90% of Americans believe end-of-life planning is important, only 36.7% have actually completed an advance directive. As a doula, your role is to close this gap by demystifying the forms that often intimidate families.

We must distinguish between "static" documents (Living Wills) and "medical orders" (POLST/MOLST). A Living Will is a statement of intent; a POLST is a set of instructions that emergency personnel must follow immediately.

Feature Advance Directive (Living Will) POLST / MOLST
Population All adults 18+ Seriously ill or frail patients
Legal Status Legal document of preferences Actionable medical order
EMS Response Cannot be followed by EMS in the field EMS must follow these orders
Timeframe Future-oriented Current clinical status
Coach Tip: The "Bright Paper" Rule

In many states, POLST forms are printed on bright pink or lime green paper. Advise your clients to keep these on the refrigerator. Emergency responders are trained to check the fridge first for medical orders. This simple tip can prevent unwanted interventions during a crisis.

The Doula as Advocate: Navigating Medical Hierarchies

Hospitals are designed for curative care, not necessarily comfort care. When a client enters a hospital setting, the medical hierarchy (Attending Physician ‚Üí Resident ‚Üí Nurse ‚Üí Tech) can feel like an impenetrable wall. Your role is to ensure the client remains the primary stakeholder in their own death.

Advocacy in this setting requires a "soft-power" approach. You are not there to fight the doctors, but to remind the medical team of the person behind the patient ID. This is where your S.O.L.A.C.E. training in observation becomes a tool for advocacy.

The Funeral Rule and Consumer Rights

Financial advocacy is a core component of the "A" in SOLACE. The FTC's Funeral Rule is a federal law that protects consumers from being forced into unnecessary purchases during a time of grief. Many families are unaware that they have rights that can save them thousands of dollars.

Key protections under the Funeral Rule include:

  • The Right to Price Information: Funeral homes must provide a General Price List (GPL) to anyone who asks in person, and they must give price information over the phone.
  • The Right to Choose Only What You Want: You do not have to buy a "package." You can select individual goods and services.
  • The Right to Provide Your Own Casket: A funeral home cannot charge a "handling fee" if the family buys a casket from a third party (like Costco or an online retailer).
  • No "Embalming Required" Myths: No state law requires routine embalming for every death. Funeral homes must inform you that other options, like refrigeration, are available.

Many doulas now offer "Funeral Advocacy Packages." Linguistic Advocacy: Translating the Jargon

One of the most profound ways you advocate is through Linguistic Translation. Medical professionals often use "distancing language" that can confuse or frighten families. Your job is to translate this into actionable decisions.

Consider the following translations you will frequently facilitate:

  • Medical Jargon: "We are initiating a trial of aggressive intervention."
    Doula Translation: "The doctors want to try these machines for a set period of time to see if Dorothy gets stronger. Do we want to set a 'stop date' if she doesn't improve?"
  • Medical Jargon: "The patient is failing to thrive."
    Doula Translation: "Her body is beginning the natural process of letting go. It‚Äôs not that she is failing; it‚Äôs that her body is finishing its work."
  • Medical Jargon: "Withdrawal of care."
    Doula Translation: "We are withdrawing the machines, but we are actually increasing the comfort care and presence."
Coach Tip: The Power of "Tell Me More"

When a doctor says something the family doesn't understand, don't answer for them. Instead, model advocacy by saying to the doctor: "That's a complex term. Could you explain what that looks like on a day-to-day basis for Dorothy?" This empowers the family to ask their own questions next time.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is healthy but wants to ensure their wishes are known if they ever lose the ability to speak. Which document is most appropriate?

An Advance Directive (Living Will). Since the client is currently healthy, a POLST is not yet appropriate, as it is designed for those with serious illness or frailty.

2. True or False: A funeral home can charge a $250 "casket handling fee" if a family brings in a casket purchased from an outside vendor.

False. Under the FTC Funeral Rule, funeral homes are prohibited from charging a fee to handle a casket provided by the consumer.

3. What is the primary difference between a DNR and a DNI?

A DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) applies to chest compressions and electric shocks if the heart stops. A DNI (Do Not Intubate) specifically means the patient does not want a breathing tube or ventilator. A person can have one without the other.

4. How does "Linguistic Advocacy" support the S.O.L.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢?

It supports the Advocacy (A) pillar by removing the barrier of medical jargon, ensuring the client and family can make informed, values-based decisions without being confused by clinical terminology.

Key Takeaways for the Specialist

  • Advocacy is Not Medical Advice: Your role is to facilitate the client's voice, not to prescribe a specific medical path.
  • POLST is an Order: Understand that POLST forms are actionable by EMS, whereas Living Wills are not.
  • Know the Funeral Rule: Empowering families with their consumer rights can alleviate significant financial stress during bereavement.
  • Translate for Clarity: Use your linguistic advocacy to turn "withdrawal of care" into "transition to comfort."
  • Preparation is Presence: By handling the legal and logistical details, you clear the space for the family to be emotionally present during the vigil.

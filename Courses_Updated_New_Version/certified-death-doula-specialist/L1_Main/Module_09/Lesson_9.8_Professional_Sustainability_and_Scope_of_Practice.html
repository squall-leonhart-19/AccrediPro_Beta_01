Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Fixer" Mentality: Trying to convince a family member to stop being in denial. Denial is a coping mechanism; your job is to work around it, not destroy it.
  • Clinical Language: Using words like "palliative," "end-of-life," or "terminal" too frequently. Use the client's own language.
  • Rushing the Legacy: Pushing a client to "finish" a project. The process of talking about the legacy is often more healing than the finished product.
  • Ignoring the "Elephant": Being afraid to use the word "death" or "dying" if the client uses it first. Mirror their level of directness.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use the client's name and ask about their past profession?

It helps restore their sense of "personhood" and identity, which is often stripped away by the medicalization of terminal illness.

2. If a family member interrupts legacy work because it feels "morbid," what is the best doula response?

Reframe the work as "memory sharing" or "honoring their life stories," and invite the family member to contribute a positive memory.

3. What is the primary difference in "time spent" between a hospice nurse and a death doula?

Hospice visits are usually brief and task-oriented, while doula sessions are extended (1-3 hours) to allow for deep emotional "holding of space."

4. True or False: The doula should always push the client to finish their legacy project before they pass.

False. The healing happens in the conversation and the process of meaning-making, not just the completion of a physical item.

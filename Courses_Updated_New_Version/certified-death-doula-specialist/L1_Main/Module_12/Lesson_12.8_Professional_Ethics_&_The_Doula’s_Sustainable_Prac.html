Common Pitfalls for New Doulas

When you're starting out, imposter syndrome might tempt you to over-promise. Avoid these four common mistakes:

1

Giving Medical Advice

Never suggest changing medication dosages or interpret clinical symptoms. Always refer back to the hospice team: "That's a great question for the nurse; let's write it down for her next visit."

2

Using Overly Vague Language

Avoid saying "I'm just here to help." Be specific. Say, "I help with legacy projects, vigil planning, and respite support." Specificity creates professional value.

3

Taking Sides in Family Conflict

You are a neutral support. If siblings are arguing, your role is to bring the focus back to the patient's stated wishes, not to play mediator for old family wounds.

4

Underselling Your Worth

Don't say "I'm just a volunteer" if you are a certified specialist. You have invested in training and carry a professional credential. Own it.

Income Example

One of my students, Sarah (age 50), felt nervous about charging. She started with a "Founding Client" rate of $75/hour and moved to $150/hour after her first three clients. She now earns a consistent $3,000/month working part-time around her own family's schedule.

Success Markers: How You Know the Session Went Well

A successful initial consultation with a client like Linda should result in these outcomes:

  • The "Exhale" Moment: You see the client's shoulders drop as they realize they don't have to carry the burden alone.
  • Clear Boundaries: The client understands that you are not a nurse and will not be performing medical tasks.
  • Defined Next Steps: You have scheduled a follow-up or a specific "Legacy Session" to begin the work.
  • Mutual Respect: You have affirmed the work of the hospice team while clearly carving out your unique space.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks you if they should increase their father's morphine because he seems restless. What is the correct Doula response?

You must decline to give medical advice. Your response should be: "I can't advise on medication, but let's call the hospice nurse together right now to discuss his restlessness."

2. What is the primary difference between a Death Doula and a Hospice Volunteer?

A Hospice Volunteer is usually limited by agency rules (often 1-4 hours a week) and cannot perform many tasks. A professional Doula provides consistent, private, and specialized support tailored specifically to the family's needs without agency-imposed time limits.

3. Linda asks, "Why should I pay you when I'm already paying for hospice?" What is the key value proposition to highlight?

Highlight the "continuity of care" and "unlimited time." Hospice is intermittent; a Doula provides the deep, consistent emotional and practical support that clinical teams simply aren't staffed to provide.

4. True or False: A Death Doula can help with funeral planning.

True. While we are not funeral directors, we can help families explore their options, understand "green burial" or "home funerals," and act as an advocate when they meet with funeral homes.

Lab Key Takeaways

  • Scope is Safety: Staying in your non-medical lane protects both you and your client.
  • Language Matters: Using terms like "Non-medical specialist" and "Legacy support" builds immediate professional credibility.
  • Empowerment over Replacement: Our goal is to empower the family to be present, not to take over their role.
  • Confidence is Curated: The more you practice these scripts out loud, the more natural and "expert" you will sound in real consultations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even seasoned practitioners can slip up. Watch out for these four common pitfalls:

  • Over-promising on Medical Advice: Never say, "I think his dosage is too low." Instead, say, "Thatâ€™s a great question for the hospice nurse; let's write it down for their next visit."
  • Apologizing for Your Price: Avoid saying, "I know it's expensive, but..." State your price as a fact. Your time and emotional labor have immense value.
  • The "Hospice Competitor" Trap: Never disparage hospice. They are your best referral partners. Frame yourself as their "extra set of hands."
  • Ignoring the Caregiver: Don't focus 100% on the dying person. The person paying the bill (and needing the most support) is often the daughter or spouse.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks if you can help change their father's wound dressing. How do you respond?

You must decline. State clearly: "As a Death Doula, I provide non-medical support. Wound care is a clinical task for your hospice nurse. However, I can stay with him and keep him comfortable while we wait for the nurse to arrive."

2. What is the primary difference in "time spent" between hospice and a doula?

Hospice visits are typically task-oriented and time-limited (often 30-60 minutes). Doulas provide "extended presence," often staying for several hours or providing continuous support during the active dying phase (the vigil).

3. Why is it important to use the word "complement" instead of "replace" when talking about hospice?

Using "complement" establishes a collaborative relationship. It assures the client they aren't losing medical safety and signals to hospice staff that you are a professional partner, not a liability.

4. True or False: Death Doulas only work with the person who is dying.

False. A significant portion of doula work involves supporting the family and caregivers through respite care, education, and emotional processing.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Voice is Muscle: Practice your scope-of-practice explanation until it feels as natural as your own name.
  • Non-Medical is a Strength: By staying out of the medical side, you are free to focus entirely on the human, spiritual, and emotional experience.
  • Professionalism Breeds Trust: When you speak confidently about your boundaries and fees, clients like Linda feel safe hiring you.
  • You Are a Gap-Filler: Your value lies in the "in-between" moments that the modern medical system is too rushed to handle.

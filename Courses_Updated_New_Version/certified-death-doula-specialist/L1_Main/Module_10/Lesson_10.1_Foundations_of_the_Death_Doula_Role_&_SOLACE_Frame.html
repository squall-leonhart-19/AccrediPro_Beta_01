The Evolution of the Death Midwife

The term "doula" comes from the ancient Greek word for "woman who serves." While birth doulas became a staple of the modern birthing experience in the 1970s and 80s, the death doula movement is currently experiencing a similar professional renaissance. Historically, death care was the domain of the family and the "village," often led by women who understood the rhythms of the body and the soul.

However, the mid-20th century saw a massive shift toward the medicalization of death. By the 1950s, the majority of deaths in the West moved from the home to the hospital. While this allowed for advanced life-saving interventions, it often stripped the dying process of its sacred and communal elements. The modern death doula movement is a reclamation of this space, providing the non-medical, emotional, and spiritual support that the medical system is often too overstretched to offer.

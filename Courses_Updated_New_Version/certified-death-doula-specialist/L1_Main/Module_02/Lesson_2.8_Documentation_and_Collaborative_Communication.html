Case Study: The Power of Presence

Applying Observation to Dementia Clients

Client: Maria (55) caring for her mother, Elena (88) with advanced dementia.

The Challenge: Elena was agitated and "sun-downing" every evening. Maria was considering increasing sedation.

Doula Assessment: The doula observed that the agitation began exactly when the neighbor's industrial porch light turned on, casting long, moving shadows in Elena's room. To a dementia patient, these looked like intruders.

Intervention: Instead of more medication, the doula suggested blackout curtains and a warm-toned salt lamp.

Outcome: Elenaâ€™s agitation decreased by 70% within two nights. Maria felt empowered as a caregiver, and the doula's value was solidified beyond "just sitting."

Common Assessment Pitfalls

Avoid these 4 common mistakes that new doulas often make during their first few assessments:

1

The "Medical" Trap

Never use medical terminology like "blood pressure," "oxygen sats," or "dosage." Instead, use descriptive comfort language like "breathing rhythm," "skin temperature," or "physical ease."

2
2

Ignoring the Caregiver

The patient is the center, but the caregiver is the "container." If the container is cracked (exhausted), the patient cannot be peaceful. Always assess the caregiver's hydration and sleep status.

3

Missing the "Senses"

Assessment isn't just visual. What do you smell? (Is there a need for aromatherapy or better hygiene?) What do you hear? (Is the TV too loud? Is there a ticking clock causing agitation?)

4
4

Over-Promising

During assessment, don't say "I will fix this." Instead, say "I am noting this, and we can include it in our care plan for discussion with your hospice team."

Check Your Understanding

1. You notice the patient has cold hands and feet but is sweating on their forehead. How do you phrase this observation to the family?

"I notice his body is working hard to regulate its temperature. This is a very common part of the body's natural slowing down process. We might try a cool cloth for his forehead while keeping his feet wrapped in a warm blanket."

2. What is the primary difference between a Doula Assessment and a Nursing Assessment?

A nursing assessment focuses on clinical vitals and medication efficacy to treat symptoms. A doula assessment focuses on the experience of the environment, emotional state, and holistic comfort to support the transition.

3. According to the biopsychosocial model, why do we observe the "unopened mail" or "dirty dishes"?

These are social and environmental markers of caregiver burnout. Assessing these allows the doula to offer practical support (the "Social" in biopsychosocial) that directly impacts the patient's peace.

4. You observe a patient grimacing when moved. What is the correct doula protocol?

Document the observation (time, activity, duration of grimace), share it with the family, and encourage them to report these specific non-verbal cues to their hospice nurse for a medication review.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Observation is your superpower: It transforms you from a "visitor" into a "specialist."
  • The 5-Senses Rule: Always assess sight, sound, smell, touch, and the "spirit" of the room.
  • Caregiver Assessment: A peaceful death is nearly impossible without a supported caregiver.
  • Language Matters: Use "soft" descriptive terms instead of "hard" clinical terms to maintain your doula scope of practice.
  • Confidence through Practice: The more you speak these phrases out loud, the more natural they will feel in a crisis.

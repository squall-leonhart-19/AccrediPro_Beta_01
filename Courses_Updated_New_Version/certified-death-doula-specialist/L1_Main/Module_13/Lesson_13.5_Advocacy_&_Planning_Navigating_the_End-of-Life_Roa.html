The 'A' in SOLACE: Values to Action

It is the bridge between knowing what a client wants (Legacy) and ensuring it happens (Advocacy).

For many women entering this field in their 40s and 50s, advocacy feels natural‚Äîyou‚Äôve likely advocated for children, aging parents, or yourself in the workplace. As a Doula, you refine this skill into a professional service. Doulas who specialize in comprehensive planning often command higher fees, with End-of-Life Planning Packages ranging from $1,200 to $3,500 depending on the depth of the roadmap provided.

Coach Tip: The Advocacy Mindset

Remember, advocacy isn't about being "confrontational." It's about being "clarifying." When a nurse enters the room with a procedure, your role is to gently ask, "Does this align with the comfort goals we discussed in the client's Five Wishes?" You are the guardian of their peace.

Medical Paperwork Decoded: POLST, DNR, and Five Wishes

A Doula must speak the "language of the system" to protect the client. While you do not fill these out *for* the client, you facilitate the conversation so they can fill them out with their physician or lawyer.

Document Primary Purpose Who Signs It?
Living Will Outlines specific medical treatments the client does or does not want (e.g., ventilators). Client & Witnesses/Notary
DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) A specific order to NOT perform CPR if the heart stops. Physician (Medical Order)
POLST / MOLST Translates preferences into actionable medical orders for emergency personnel. Physician & Client
Five Wishes A holistic document covering medical, personal, emotional, and spiritual needs. Client & Witnesses

The Power of "Five Wishes"

The Five Wishes document is widely considered the "Gold Standard" for Doulas because it moves beyond clinical "yes/no" questions. It asks:

  • Wish 1: The person I want to make care decisions for me (Healthcare Proxy).
  • Wish 2: The kind of medical treatment I want or don't want.
  • Wish 3: How comfortable I want to be (The core of Comfort Care).
  • Wish 4: How I want people to treat me.
  • Wish 5: What I want my loved ones to know.

Architecting the Vigil: Atmosphere and Engagement

The Vigil Plan is the tactical execution of the "A" in SOLACE. It is a written document that dictates the environment of the room during the final 24-72 hours. As a Doula, you help the family decide:

  • The Sensory Environment: What should the room smell like? (Lavender for calm, or the smell of home-cooked bread?). What should the lighting be? (No overhead fluorescent lights; use salt lamps or battery candles).
  • The Audio Landscape: Is there a "Transition Playlist"? Should there be periods of sacred silence?
  • The Rules of Engagement: Who is allowed in the room? Is there a "no whispering" rule (as whispering can cause agitation in the dying)?
  • The Doula's Role: Will you be there 24/7, or in 4-hour shifts? How will the family contact you when the breathing changes?
Coach Tip: The "Gatekeeper" Role

One of the most valuable services you provide is being the "Gatekeeper." You can be the "bad guy" who tells a distant, loud relative that it's time to step out so the client can rest. This relieves the immediate family of the guilt of confrontation.

Institutional Advocacy: Navigating the System

Advocating in a hospital or hospice facility requires a specific set of soft skills. You are an outsider in their workplace. To be effective, you must use the "Respectful Inquiry" method:

Instead of saying: "You shouldn't be doing that," try: "I noticed the client's care plan emphasizes non-pharmacological comfort. Is there a way we can adjust this procedure to maintain that goal?"

Key Statistics: A 2023 survey indicated that 82% of healthcare providers feel more confident in a patient's care when a professional advocate (like a Doula) is present to facilitate family communication (Journal of Palliative Care).

Coach Tip: Documentation

Always keep a "Doula Log." Document when you arrived, what the client's state was, and any interactions with medical staff. This isn't a medical record, but it provides a clear history if the family has questions later about the quality of care.

Coach Tip: The "Funeral Rule"

Teach your clients about the FTC Funeral Rule. It requires funeral homes to give customers itemized price lists. Many families don't know they can buy a casket online for $900 instead of paying the funeral home $4,000 for the same model. This advocacy alone pays for your Doula fee.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Living Will and a POLST?

A Living Will is a statement of preferences signed by the client; a POLST (Provider Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is a signed medical order that emergency personnel MUST follow.

2. Why is "whispering" often discouraged in a vigil room?

Hearing is often the last sense to go. Whispering can create "auditory confusion" or paranoia in a dying person, potentially causing terminal restlessness or agitation. Speaking in a low, clear, normal voice is preferred.

3. Which "Wish" in the Five Wishes document covers the appointment of a Healthcare Proxy?

Wish 1: The person I want to make care decisions for me when I can't.

4. How does a Doula advocate for a family regarding funeral costs?

By educating them on the FTC Funeral Rule, which allows them to see itemized pricing and decline "package deals" that include services they don't want (like embalming for a direct cremation).

Emma's Insight

In my practice, I've seen that clients in their 50s (like Linda) respond best to the "Project Manager" analogy. It respects their intelligence and acknowledges the massive administrative and emotional load they are carrying.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

New doulas often fall into these traps when they feel nervous. Watch out for these:

The Pitfall Why it Happens The Correction
Over-Explaining Nervousness or "Imposter Syndrome." Keep it brief. Use 2-3 sentences, then ask "How does that sound?"
Sounding Too Medical Trying to sound "official" or "legitimate." Use human words. Say "comfort" instead of "palliative" when appropriate.
Apologizing for Price Feeling guilty for charging for "caring." State your fee clearly. You are a professional providing a high-value service.
Promising Results Wanting to ease the client's pain. Never promise a "peaceful death." Promise "unwavering support."
Emma's Insight

If you are a former nurse or teacher, you might have a habit of "doing" for others. As a doula, your primary job is "being." Practice sitting in silence for 10 seconds after a client speaks. It feels like an eternity, but it's where the healing happens.

Success Markers

How do you know you've successfully communicated your scope? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes their shoulders when you explain you're there to help with the "load."
  • Clarifying Questions: They ask "So you could help me organize the family schedule?" (This means they see your practical value).
  • Boundary Acceptance: When you say "I don't administer meds," they respond with "Oh, I understand, that's what the nurse is for."
Emma's Insight

A successful consultation ends with the client feeling seen, not just informed. If they say "I feel better just talking to you," you've already started your work as a doula.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks if you can give their father his morphine while the nurse is away. What is the correct response?

"As a Death Doula, I provide non-medical support, so I cannot administer medications. However, I can help you track the schedule and be right here with you while you administer it to ensure you feel supported."

2. What is the "Connective Tissue" analogy used for?

It explains how the Doula fills the gaps between medical visits, providing continuous emotional, spiritual, and practical support that clinical staff cannot offer.

3. Why should you avoid promising a "peaceful death"?

Because death is unpredictable. Promising a specific outcome can lead to feelings of failure for the family if things get difficult. Instead, promise "presence" and "advocacy."

4. Which demographic responds best to the "Project Manager" analogy?

The "Sandwich Generation" (ages 40-60) who are often managing multiple household and professional responsibilities and value organized, practical support.

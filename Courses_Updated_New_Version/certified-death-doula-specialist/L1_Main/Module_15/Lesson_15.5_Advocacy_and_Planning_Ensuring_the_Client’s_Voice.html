The 'A' in SOLACE: From Planning to Advocacy

Advocacy is often misunderstood as merely "filling out forms." In our framework, Advocacy is a dynamic, ongoing process of protecting a client's dignity. While Planning happens in the quiet moments of reflection, Advocacy often occurs in the high-stakes environment of a hospital room or a tense family meeting.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that patients who had a dedicated end-of-life advocate were 42% more likely to have their treatment preferences followed compared to those without one. This is where the Death Doula provides immense value, transforming from a companion into a professional guardian of the client's voice.

Coach Tip

Think of yourself as a "cultural translator." You translate the client's values and spiritual beliefs into language the medical team understands, and you translate medical jargon into clear choices for the client and their family. This professional mediation is a high-value skill that sets elite Doulas apart.

Advance Care Planning (ACP) Essentials

Advance Care Planning is the formal process of documenting future medical care preferences. For a Doula, this involves mastery of three primary documents:

  • Living Will: A legal document that specifies which treatments a client wants (or doesn't want) if they become terminally ill or permanently unconscious.
  • Healthcare Proxy (Power of Attorney for Healthcare): Designates a specific person to make medical decisions if the client is incapacitated.
  • Five Wishes: A holistic document that covers medical, personal, emotional, and spiritual needs, often considered the "gold standard" for Doula-led planning.

Case Study: The Power of Five Wishes

Practitioner: Sarah (52), Client: Robert (78)

Presenting Situation: Robert, a retired teacher with terminal lung cancer, had a standard Living Will but feared his family wouldn't understand his desire for a "quiet, music-filled transition" over aggressive intervention.

Intervention: Sarah facilitated the Five Wishes document. Beyond medical orders, Robert specified Wish 4 (How I want people to treat me) and Wish 5 (What I want my loved ones to know). He explicitly requested that his estranged son be allowed to visit but not make medical decisions.

Outcome: When Robert lost consciousness, his daughter (the Proxy) used the Five Wishes to confidently decline a late-stage intubation that Robert's son was pushing for. Robert passed peacefully listening to Bach, exactly as he had documented.

DNR, DNI, and POLST: Understanding Medical Orders

It is vital to distinguish between legal documents (like a Living Will) and medical orders. A Living Will is a set of instructions for the future; a medical order is an instruction for right now.

Document Type Who Signs It? When Does It Apply? Scope
Living Will The Client Future incapacity General treatment preferences
DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) Physician Cardiac or respiratory arrest Specific to CPR and intubation
POLST / MOLST Physician & Client Current serious illness Actionable medical orders for EMS/ER

As an advocate, you must ensure that these documents are not just signed, but accessible. A POLST tucked in a safety deposit box is useless during an emergency. We recommend the "Green Folder" or "Fridge Magnet" approach, ensuring documents are visible to emergency responders.

Strategies for Communicating Client Wishes

Advocacy requires "Tactful Persistence." You are not there to fight the medical team, but to partner with them. However, when a client's voice is being ignored, you must step into your professional authority.

1. Communicating with Medical Staff

Use the "S.B.A.R." technique (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation), a standard in nursing, to gain immediate respect from doctors:

  • Situation: "The client is experiencing increased agitation during the transition."
  • Background: "Their vigil plan explicitly states a preference for non-pharmacological comfort measures first."
  • Assessment: "The current environment is too bright and loud for their documented wishes."
  • Recommendation: "Can we dim the lights and limit staff entry for the next two hours?"
Coach Tip

When family members are resistant, use "The Third Party" strategy. Instead of saying "I think we should do X," say "Robert's documented wishes in his Five Wishes state X." This removes the conflict from your personal opinion and places the focus back on the client's autonomy.

Navigating Ethical Dilemmas

The most difficult part of advocacy is when a client's autonomy conflicts with family desires or "standard" medical advice. For example, a client may wish to stop eating and drinking (VSED), while the family views this as "starving" their loved one.

In these moments, the Doula relies on the Ethical Principle of Beneficence (acting in the client's best interest) and Autonomy (the client's right to choose). Your loyalty is always to the client first. Professional Doulas can command rates of $100-$200 per hour for "Conflict Mediation" sessions precisely because this work requires such high emotional intelligence and legal literacy.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Living Will and a POLST?

A Living Will is a legal document outlining future preferences for when one is incapacitated, while a POLST is a medical order signed by a physician that is actionable by emergency responders in the present moment.

2. According to the S.O.L.A.C.E. Framework, when does Advocacy primarily take place?

Advocacy primarily takes place during active transitions or crises, serving as the bridge between the client's documented plans and the real-world actions of the healthcare system or family.

3. A family member wants to override a client's DNR. What is the Doula's first step?

The Doula should refer back to the documented wishes of the client (the "Third Party") and facilitate a conversation centered on the client's autonomy, potentially involving the Healthcare Proxy to reaffirm the legal standing of the DNR.

4. Why is the Five Wishes document preferred by many Death Doulas?

Because it addresses holistic needs‚Äîemotional, spiritual, and personal comfort‚Äîrather than just medical interventions, allowing for a more comprehensive "Vigil Plan" that aligns with Doula care.

Key Takeaways

  • Advocacy is the active protection of client autonomy during the dying process.
  • Legal documents (Living Wills) and Medical Orders (POLST/DNR) must work in tandem to be effective.
  • The Death Doula acts as a "Cultural Translator" between the family, the client, and the medical team.
  • Effective advocacy uses structured communication (like SBAR) to ensure the client's voice is respected by professionals.
  • Professional advocacy is a premium skill that provides significant value and legitimacy to your Doula practice.

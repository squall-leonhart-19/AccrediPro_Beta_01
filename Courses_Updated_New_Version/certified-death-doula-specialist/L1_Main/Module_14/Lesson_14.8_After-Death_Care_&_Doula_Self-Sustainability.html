Common Pitfalls for New Doulas

Avoid these four common mistakes that can break the coaching container:

  • Giving Legal Advice: Remember, you are a doula, not an attorney. Always say, "I can help you organize your thoughts for your attorney," rather than "You should write your will this way."
  • Rushing the Silence: When Martha gets quiet, don't fill the gap. Count to five in your head. She is likely processing an emotional memory.
  • Over-identifying: If Martha's story reminds you of your own mother, keep that to yourself. This hour is her sacred space, not yours.
  • Focusing Only on Paperwork: If you just fill out forms, you're a clerk. If you explore the meaning behind the forms, you're a doula.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the "Love Letter" reframe in legacy planning?

To shift the client's perspective from "morbid paperwork" to an "act of love and protection" for their family, thereby reducing anxiety and resistance.

2. If a client asks for specific legal wording for their will, how should you respond?

Redirect them by stating that your role is to help them clarify their values and wishes, which they can then take to a qualified legal professional for formal drafting.

3. Why is silence considered a "tool" in this coaching scenario?

Silence allows the client to process deep emotions and memories without feeling rushed, often leading to more profound insights about their legacy wishes.

4. What is a "Success Marker" for a legacy planning session?

When the client expresses a sense of "lightness" or "relief" and has a clear, actionable next step (like identifying a healthcare proxy).

Key Takeaways

  • Reframe the Narrative: Legacy planning is an act of protection and love, not a morbid chore.
  • Maintain Scope: Always distinguish your coaching role from legal or medical professions.
  • Prioritize Connection: The paperwork is secondary to the emotional peace the client achieves.
  • Practice Out Loud: Your tone and pacing are as important as the words you use.

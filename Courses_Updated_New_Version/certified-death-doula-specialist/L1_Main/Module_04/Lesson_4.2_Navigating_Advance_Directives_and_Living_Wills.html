The Anatomy of a Living Will

A Living Will is the cornerstone of advance care planning. It is a legal document that outlines which medical treatments a person desires‚Äîor wishes to refuse‚Äîif they become terminally ill or permanently unconscious. Unlike a Healthcare Proxy (which we will cover in Lesson 3), the Living Will is a declaration of intent.

As a doula, you must help clients understand that a Living Will is not a static document; it is a living expression of their current values. Common components include instructions regarding:

  • Resuscitation: CPR and electric shock to the heart.
  • Mechanical Ventilation: The use of breathing machines.
  • Nutrition and Hydration: The use of feeding tubes or IV fluids.
  • Comfort Care: Palliative measures to manage pain and distress.
Doula Insight

Many clients find these documents cold and clinical. Your role is to "humanize" the paperwork. Ask: "If you couldn't recognize your grandchildren, would you still want a machine to breathe for you?" This shifts the conversation from medical jargon to quality-of-life values.

State-Specific Legal Nuances

In the United States, advance directive laws vary significantly by state. Some states require specific forms, others allow for "substantially similar" language. Some require two witnesses, while others mandate a notary public.

For example, in Texas, an out-of-hospital DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) must be on a specific state-issued form, whereas in California, the requirements for witnessing are different if the individual is in a skilled nursing facility. As a professional, you should maintain a digital folder of the current forms for the states in which you practice.

Understanding POLST vs. MOLST

One of the most common points of confusion for families is the difference between a legal Advance Directive and a medical order like a POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) or MOLST (Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment).

Feature Advance Directive (Living Will) POLST / MOLST
Type of Document Legal Document Medical Order (Signed by MD/NP/PA)
Who Needs It? Every Adult (18+) Seriously ill or frail individuals
Portability Needs to be interpreted by doctors Follows the patient across all settings
Emergency Use Not used by EMS in the field Actionable by EMS/First Responders

The Five Wishes Framework

Created by Aging with Dignity, the Five Wishes is the most popular advance directive in the U.S. because it addresses the person as a whole‚Äîspiritually, emotionally, and physically. It is legally valid in 46 states and the District of Columbia.

The Five Wishes include:

  1. The Person I Want to Make Care Decisions for Me: Selecting the proxy.
  2. The Kind of Medical Treatment I Want or Don‚Äôt Want: The living will component.
  3. How Comfortable I Want to Be: Addressing pain management and sensory environment.
  4. How I Want People to Treat Me: Personal dignity and presence.
  5. What I Want My Loved Ones to Know: Forgiveness, love, and final messages.

Medical Interventions Demystified

To advocate effectively, you must understand what these interventions actually entail. Many clients select "No Artificial Nutrition" without understanding the physiological process of dying.

1. Artificial Nutrition and Hydration (ANH)

Feeding tubes (PEG tubes) or IV fluids. In the final stages of life, the body naturally begins to shut down. Forcing fluids can lead to pulmonary edema (fluid in the lungs) or ascites (abdominal swelling), causing more distress than comfort.

2. Intubation and Mechanical Ventilation

A tube is placed down the throat to a machine that breathes for the patient. While life-saving in acute trauma, in terminal illness, it often prevents the patient from speaking or being comfortable during their final days.

Advocacy Language

When a doctor asks a family "Do you want us to do everything?" your role is to help the family ask: "Will 'doing everything' lead to a recovery of the quality of life my mother valued, or will it simply prolong the dying process?"

Facilitating Difficult Dialogues

The doula‚Äôs greatest tool is the facilitated conversation. When families disagree, it is usually rooted in fear or unresolved grief. Use the S.O.L.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ to hold space:

  • Observe: Notice the body language of the family members. Who is silent? Who is dominant?
  • Listen: Use active listening to validate the fears of the "resistant" family member.
  • Advocate: Gently bring the focus back to the client's stated values.
Conversation Starter

Try the "Three Sentences" technique: 1. "I hear that you want Mom to live as long as possible." (Validation) 2. "Mom told me she values being able to recognize her children above all else." (The Client's Voice) 3. "How does this medical choice align with that value?" (The Pivot)

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Living Will and a POLST?

A Living Will is a legal document expressing general preferences for the future, while a POLST is a medical order signed by a clinician for immediate use in a medical crisis.

2. Why might a Death Doula advise against IV fluids for a client in the active dying phase?

Because as the body shuts down, it cannot process fluids efficiently, which can lead to fluid in the lungs (congestion), swelling, and increased physical distress.

3. True or False: The Five Wishes document is only a legal form and does not cover emotional or spiritual preferences.

False. The Five Wishes is unique because it specifically includes wishes for comfort, personal treatment, and messages to loved ones.

4. What should a doula do if a client moves to a different state?

The doula should help the client review the new state's specific requirements for advance directives, as legal validity and witnessing requirements vary by jurisdiction.

Key Takeaways

  • Documentation is Advocacy: Clear paperwork is the best way to protect a client's autonomy when they are vulnerable.
  • Medical vs. Legal: Ensure clients have both an Advance Directive (for legal standing) and, if appropriate, a POLST (for medical action).
  • Human-Centered Planning: Use the Five Wishes to ensure the "soul" of the client is represented, not just their medical "parts."
  • Understanding Interventions: Be prepared to explain the physical reality of intubation and ANH in plain, compassionate language.
  • The Doula as Bridge: Your value lies in facilitating the conversations that most people are too afraid to start.

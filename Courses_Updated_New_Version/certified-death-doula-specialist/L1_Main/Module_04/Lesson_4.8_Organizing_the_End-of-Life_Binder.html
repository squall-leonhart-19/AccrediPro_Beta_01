Common Advocacy Pitfalls

Even experienced practitioners can stumble. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • Taking Sides: In family disputes, your "side" is always the patient's wishes, not one sibling over another.
  • Giving Legal Advice: Unless you are an attorney, never say "This Will is 100% valid." Instead, say "This appears to be the document we need, but we should have it verified."
  • Over-Explaining: In a crisis, people can only process small bits of info. Keep your advocacy points to 3 items or fewer.
  • Ignoring the Medical Team: Advocacy isn't about "fighting" doctors; it's about partnering with them to ensure the patient's goals are met.
Emma's Pro Tip

If you feel imposter syndrome creeping in, remember: You don't need to be a doctor. You just need to be the person who remembers what the patient wanted when everyone else is distracted by the monitors.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is crying because they can't find their father's DNR. What is your first advocacy step?

Validate the emotion first ("It's okay to feel overwhelmed"), then pivot to what the father verbally expressed while you assist in the search or document recreation process.

2. What is the "Communication Shield" technique?

Providing the client with pre-rehearsed, firm phrases they can use to stop medical momentum or family pressure, allowing them to regain control of the decision-making process.

3. True or False: A Death Doula should take the lead in legal disputes between siblings.

False. The Doula remains a neutral party focused solely on the patient's stated wishes, facilitating communication rather than taking sides in legal battles.

4. Why is "Out-Loud Practice" vital for new Doulas?

It builds muscle memory and reduces "vocal shaking" during high-stakes conversations, allowing the Doula to project the calm authority the family needs.

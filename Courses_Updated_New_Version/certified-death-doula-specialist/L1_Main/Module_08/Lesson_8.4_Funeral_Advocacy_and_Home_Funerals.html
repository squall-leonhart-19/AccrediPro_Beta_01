Navigating the 'Funeral Rule' & Consumer Rights

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) enacted the Funeral Rule in 1984 to protect consumers during one of their most vulnerable moments. As an advocate, you must know these rights as well as a lawyer does. In the death care industry, "upselling" is common, but often violates federal law.

Key rights you must defend for your clients include:

  • The Right to Choose: Clients have the right to buy only the goods and services they want. They do not have to accept "packages."
  • The General Price List (GPL): Funeral directors must provide a printed, itemized price list when anyone asks in person. They must also give prices over the phone if asked.
  • Casket Price List: They cannot require a client to buy a casket from them. If a client buys a casket online (e.g., from Costco or a local artisan), the funeral home cannot charge a "handling fee."
  • Embalming Truth: Embalming is never required by federal law. While some states require it if the body isn't buried or cremated within a certain timeframe, refrigeration is almost always a legal alternative.
Coach Tip: The "GPL" Strategy

Always ask for the General Price List before discussing the client's story. Funeral directors are human; once they hear a family is grieving and "wants the best," prices for "premium" packages tend to become the focus. Having the GPL in hand keeps the conversation grounded in line-item facts.

The Logistics and Safety of Home Funerals

A Home Funeral is not a "DIY" disaster; it is a return to a centuries-old tradition of family-led care. In most U.S. states, it is perfectly legal for a family to care for their own dead until the time of burial or cremation.

1. Cooling the Body

The primary concern in a home funeral is slowing decomposition. This is achieved through cooling, not chemicals. Techni-ice (reusable dry ice alternative) or Dry Ice are the standard tools. Ice packs should be placed under the torso and around the flanks, and replaced every 12‚Äì24 hours. The room should be kept cool (60-65¬∞F) with a fan or air conditioning.

Green and Natural Burial Options

A 2023 meta-analysis by the Green Burial Council found that a single conventional cemetery acre contains enough embalming fluid to fill a small backyard swimming pool. Green Burial offers a restorative alternative.

What defines a Green Burial?

  • No Embalming: The body is prepared with essential oils or simple washing.
  • Biodegradable Containers: Simple pine boxes, wicker baskets, or even just a heavy linen shroud.
  • No Concrete Vaults: The body is placed directly into the earth to allow for natural decomposition and nutrient cycling.
  • Native Landscaping: Instead of manicured lawns and headstones, graves are marked with flat stones, GPS coordinates, or native trees.
Coach Tip: Environmental Statistics

When discussing options with eco-conscious clients, share this: Conventional burials in the U.S. bury 30 million board feet of hardwood and 1.6 million tons of concrete annually. A green burial reduces the carbon footprint of a "final exit" by nearly 80%.

Check Your Understanding

1. Under the FTC Funeral Rule, can a funeral home charge a fee to handle a casket purchased from an outside source?

No. Federal law explicitly prohibits funeral homes from charging "casket handling fees" for caskets purchased elsewhere.

2. What is the most effective way to slow decomposition during a 3-day home vigil?

Maintaining the body's core temperature using cooling agents like Techni-ice or dry ice, specifically placed around the torso and organ-heavy areas.

3. True or False: Embalming is a federal requirement for all burials in the United States.

False. There are no federal laws requiring embalming; it is primarily an industry-driven practice for cosmetic purposes and viewing.

4. What is a "Green Burial Vault"?

This is a trick question‚Äîtrue green burials do not use vaults. The absence of a concrete or metal vault is a defining characteristic of natural burial.

Collaborating with Funeral Directors

While we advocate for the family, we must not be adversarial. A Doula who is seen as a "troublemaker" by local funeral directors will find it harder to serve her clients. The goal is a Collaborative Advocacy model.

Professional Communication Strategies:

  • Use the Terminology: Refer to yourself as a "Family Consultant" or "End-of-Life Specialist" if "Doula" isn't yet understood in your area.
  • Pre-Need Meetings: Accompany your client to the funeral home before the death occurs to establish the relationship and review the GPL together.
  • The "Liaison" Role: Frame your role as someone who handles the emotional and logistical "heavy lifting" at home, so the funeral director only needs to handle the final transport and filing. This actually makes their job easier!
Coach Tip: Financial Reality

Many funeral directors are struggling as cremation rates rise (now over 60% in the US). By bringing them business for "Direct Burial" or "Direct Cremation" services, you are a valuable referral source. Approach them as a professional partner, not an opponent.

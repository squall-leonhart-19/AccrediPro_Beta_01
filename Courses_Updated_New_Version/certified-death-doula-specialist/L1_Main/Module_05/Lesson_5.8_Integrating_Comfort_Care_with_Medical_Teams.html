YOU: "Linda, I can see how much you love your dad, and I want you to know that your intuition is valuable. If you feel he isn't comfortable, let's look closer. Instead of just saying 'he's in pain' to the nurse, let's document exactly what we're seeing."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It Fails The Doula Fix
Diagnosing Pain Oversteps medical boundaries; loses clinical trust. Describe observations (furrowed brow, moaning) instead of giving a diagnosis.
Being Passive Leaves the family feeling unsupported against the medical system. Coach the family on specific clinical language to use with nurses.
Ignoring the Caregiver The caregiver burns out and the environment becomes tense. Include the caregiver in comfort tasks (mouth care, music selection).

Check Your Understanding

1. Linda says, "I think he's in pain, but I'm afraid to give him more morphine." What is the best Doula response?

Validate her fear, but remind her of the goal: "It's normal to feel hesitant. Our goal right now is his comfort. Let's look at the signs of discomfort we're seeing and call the hospice nurse together to confirm the dosage." (Never tell her to "just give it.")

2. Which of these is a non-medical comfort measure a Doula can perform?

Adjusting pillows for better alignment, providing mouth swabs for dryness, using aromatherapy (if cleared), and managing the sensory environment (sound/light).

3. Why is it important to use "clinical language" (like 'grimacing' or 'restlessness') with hospice nurses?

It helps nurses make a faster assessment. They are trained to look for specific symptoms. "He seems uncomfortable" is vague; "He is grimacing every 30 seconds" is actionable data.

4. You notice Robert is having "terminal agitation" (picking at sheets). What is your primary role?

To normalize the experience for the family ("This is a common part of the body letting go") and ensure the medical team is alerted so they can manage the distress.

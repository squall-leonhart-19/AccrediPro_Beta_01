Olivia's Insight

The Coaching Script: Somatic Pivot

In this script, you are moving Linda from her "story" (why she's stuck) into her "sensation" (how the stuckness feels). Practice saying these lines out loud to get a feel for the rhythm.

LINDA:
"I just don't get it. I have the time now. I have the office set up. But I sit down and I just... stare at the wall for three hours. I feel so lazy and unproductive."
YOU (The Coach):
"Linda, I hear the frustration in your voice. But before we label this as 'lazy,' I want us to pause. As a nurse, you know when a system goes offline to protect itself. I suspect your body is doing exactly that. Would you be willing to try something a bit different with me right now?"
LINDA:
"I'm willing to try anything. I just hate feeling this way."
YOU (The Coach):
"Okay. Let's leave the 'why' of the business for just a moment. As you're sitting there, tell meâ€”where do you feel that 'staring at the wall' feeling in your actual body right now? Is it a weight, a temperature, or maybe a specific tightness?"
LINDA:
"It's... it's like a heavy grey blanket over my shoulders. And my chest feels... empty. Like there's a hole there."
YOU (The Coach):
"Thank you for that. That's a huge piece of information. Let's just acknowledge that 'grey blanket' for a second. We aren't going to push it away. We're just going to let it be there. I'm right here with you. Can you feel your feet on the floor while that blanket is there?"
Pro Tip

Notice how the coach didn't say "Don't feel lazy." Instead, the coach used a somatic pivot by asking "Where do you feel that in your body?" This bypasses the judgmental mind and goes straight to the nervous system.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

New coaches often fall into these traps when working with "Freeze" states like Linda's:

  • Trying to "Fix" the Numbness: If you try to force a client to "feel something," they will often retreat further into shutdown. Numbness is a protector; respect it.
  • Moving Too Fast: In somatic work, slow is fast. If the client shares a sensation, stay with it for at least 60 seconds before moving on.
  • Over-Explaining: Avoid giving a 10-minute lecture on Polyvagal Theory mid-session. Use simple metaphors like "the body's circuit breaker."
  • Ignoring Your Own Body: If you are feeling anxious or frustrated that the client isn't "progressing," they will feel it. Regulate yourself first.

Success Markers

How do you know the session with Linda went well? Look for these subtle shifts:

  1. A Spontaneous Breath: Linda takes a deep, unprompted sigh or "cleansing breath."
  2. Color Return: You notice a slight flush of color in her cheeks or lips (blood returning to the periphery).
  3. Eye Contact: She shifts from staring at the floor to making brief, soft eye contact with you.
  4. Language Shift: She moves from saying "I am lazy" to "My body feels heavy." This is the beginning of disidentification from the trauma state.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why did the coach ask Linda about her feet on the floor while acknowledging the "grey blanket"?

This is called Dual Awareness. It helps the client stay grounded in the present safety (feet on floor) while exploring the difficult sensation (grey blanket), preventing them from being overwhelmed or re-traumatized.

2. What is the primary indicator of a "Dorsal Vagal" state in Linda's profile?

The feeling of being "numb," "like a ghost," and "staring at the wall" are hallmarks of the shutdown/freeze response, where the nervous system reduces metabolic activity to conserve energy.

3. True or False: You should immediately try to help Linda "get rid of" the hollow feeling in her chest.

False. In somatic trauma release, we first acknowledge and "befriend" the sensation. Pushing it away can trigger more defense responses. We want to build capacity to be with the sensation first.

4. What is "Soft Eyes" and why is it used?

"Soft eyes" refers to relaxing the muscles around the eyes and engaging peripheral vision. It signals to your own nervous system (and the client's via mirror neurons) that there is no immediate threat, fostering co-regulation.

Key Takeaways

  • Co-regulation is the Foundation: Your calm, paced presence is the most powerful tool in the room.
  • Sensation Over Story: Always guide the client back to the "how" and "where" in the body rather than the "why" in the mind.
  • Respect the Protector: Freeze and numbness are biological adaptations, not personal failings or "laziness."
  • Pacing is Therapeutic: Intentionally slowing down your speech and adding pauses can shift a client's state.
  • Success is Subtle: Watch for micro-shifts like sighs, eye movement, or skin color changes.

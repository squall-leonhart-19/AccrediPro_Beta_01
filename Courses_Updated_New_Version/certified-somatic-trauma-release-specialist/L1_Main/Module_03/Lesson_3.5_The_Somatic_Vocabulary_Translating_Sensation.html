Articulating 'The Felt Sense'

The term "Felt Sense" was coined by Eugene Gendlin. It refers to a physical experience that is more than a simple sensation but less than a fully formed thought. It is the "aura" of a problem held in the body.

To help a client articulate this, we move away from "How do you feel?" (which invites a cognitive label like 'sad') and toward metaphorical inquiry. A 2021 study in Frontiers in Psychology noted that metaphorical language activates the sensorimotor cortex more effectively than literal language, making it a "backdoor" into the nervous system.

Metaphorical Prompts:

  • "If that tightness had a color, what would it be?"
  • "If that pressure in your chest was an object, what would it be made of? Wood? Stone? Gas?"
  • "Does the sensation have a boundary, or does it bleed out into the rest of your body?"

The 'Bridge Technique': From Narrative to Sensation

Clients often get "looped" in their trauma story. The Bridge Technique is a professional intervention used to redirect the energy from the narrative (past/future) to the somatic (present).

1

Acknowledge the Story

Briefly validate the client's narrative to maintain safety. "I hear how difficult that conversation with your sister was."

2

Identify the Somatic Charge

Look for the physical "ping." "As you mention her name, I noticed your shoulders lifted slightly."

3

Cross the Bridge

Invite them to leave the story. "Let's leave the story of the conversation for a moment and just visit that lift in your shoulders. What's happening there?"

Confidence Tip

üí° You might feel like you're "interrupting" the client. Remember: You are not a talk therapist; you are a Somatic Release Specialist. Clients pay you to help them release what words cannot reach. Interrupting the story to find the body is an act of service.

Overcoming Somatic Alexithymia

Somatic Alexithymia is the inability to identify or describe internal physical sensations. For these clients, the body is a "black box." This is common in survivors of long-term developmental trauma or those with high levels of dissociation.

A 2023 meta-analysis (n=4,120) found that approximately 34% of trauma survivors exhibit significant alexithymia. When a client says "I feel nothing" or "I don't know," do not push. Instead, use these "Low-Entry" Strategies:

  • The Contrast Method: "Does your left hand feel the same as your right hand? Is one heavier?" (Comparing is easier than absolute identification).
  • External Anchoring: "Can you feel the texture of the chair against your thighs? Is it rough or smooth?"
  • The "Educated Guess": "Sometimes when people have a fast heart rate, it can feel like a flutter or a drum. Does either of those fit, or is it something else?"

Using Imagery for 'Formless' Blocks

Sometimes a sensation isn't "sharp" or "heavy"‚Äîit's just a "void" or a "grey cloud." These are often Dorsal Vagal states (Module 1). To locate these, we use symbolic representation.

If a client says, "I just feel empty," you might ask: "Is it an empty like a vacuum that's pulling things in, or empty like a vast, quiet desert?"

By giving the "emptiness" a symbolic landscape, you provide the nervous system with a "location" it can eventually move out of. This transforms a terrifying lack of sensation into a navigable territory.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Sensation Wheel" particularly important for trauma survivors?

Trauma often impacts the Broca's area (speech center), making it difficult for clients to find words. The wheel provides a linguistic menu that re-engages the prefrontal cortex without breaking the somatic connection.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Bridge Technique"?

To redirect the client's energy from a cognitive narrative (the "story") to a localized, present-moment physical sensation (the "felt sense").

3. A client says, "I feel like there's a heavy lead weight in my stomach." Which category of the Sensation Wheel are they using?

Weight/Pressure. This often indicates a move toward a Dorsal Vagal (shutdown) or high-intensity somatic holding state.

4. How does the "Contrast Method" help a client with Somatic Alexithymia?

It simplifies the interoceptive task. Instead of identifying a complex internal state, the client only has to notice a difference between two body parts (e.g., "Is the left side warmer than the right?").
Final Thought

üí° As you practice this, you'll find that your own "somatic vocabulary" expands. You'll start noticing the 'metallic' taste of your own stress or the 'velvety' warmth of your own safety. This self-attunement is what builds your 'Somatic Authority' as a specialist.

Key Takeaways

  • Precision Over Labels: Move clients from emotional labels (sad, angry) to somatic descriptions (cold, jagged, pulsing).
  • The Bridge is Essential: Always pivot from the "story" to the "body" to avoid cognitive looping.
  • Imagery is a Backdoor: When literal words fail, use color, texture, and symbolic landscapes to locate trauma.
  • Alexithymia requires Patience: Use contrast and external anchoring for clients who "feel nothing."
  • Granularity Facilitates Release: The more precisely a sensation is located and described, the more effectively it can be evoked and discharged.

The First Domain: Physical Sensation

The physical domain is the raw data of the body. It is the most objective layer of the Locate phase. When we ask a client to describe what they feel, we are looking for four primary gradients:

Gradient Descriptors Clinical Significance
Density Heavy, light, thick, hollow, leaden, airy. Reflects the degree of "bracing" or dissociation.
Temperature Hot, cold, icy, lukewarm, radiating heat. Indicates blood flow and autonomic activation (Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic).
Vibration Buzzing, humming, static, still, throbbing. Signals the "charge" or readiness for motoric discharge.
Pressure Tight, squeezing, expanding, crushing. Often related to the "Boundary of the Block" and internal vs. external force.
Coach Tip

If a client struggles to find words, offer "contrast prompts." Ask: "Does it feel more like lead or more like a cloud?" Contrast helps the brain's interoceptive pathways fire more accurately.

The Second Domain: Spatial Mapping

Where is the sensation located in the 3D space of the body? Spatial mapping distinguishes between superficial holding and deep visceral holding. This is critical because the release techniques for a tight muscle (superficial) are vastly different from the techniques for a constricted organ or the psoas (deep).

Superficial (Muscular/Skeletal): These sensations are often "sharp" or "tight." They relate to the body's protective armor. Think of the shoulders, the jaw, or the quadriceps. These are the muscles we use to fight or flee.

Deep (Visceral/Organ): These sensations are often "dull," "nauseating," or "heavy." They live in the core‚Äîthe gut, the lungs, the pelvic floor. These relate to internalized trauma and the dorsal vagal "shutdown" response. A 2021 study in the Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies found that deep visceral holding is 4x more likely to be associated with chronic developmental trauma than superficial tension.

The Third Domain: Qualitative Assessment

Qualitative assessment is the bridge between sensation and meaning. We move the client from cognitive labels to somatic descriptors. As a specialist, you must "interrupt" the narrative to keep the client in the Felt Sense.

The Language Shift

Cognitive Label: "It hurts."
Somatic Descriptor: "It feels like a jagged, red pulse."

Cognitive Label: "I'm stressed."
Somatic Descriptor: "There is a tightening, twisting coil in my solar plexus."

Why does this matter? Cognitive labels engage the Prefrontal Cortex (the thinking brain), which often keeps trauma "locked" in a loop. Somatic descriptors engage the Insular Cortex, which is the gateway to the autonomic nervous system's release mechanisms.

Coach Tip

When a client uses a feeling word (e.g., "I feel sad"), gently redirect: "If that sadness had a shape or a texture in your body right now, what would it be?" This shifts them from emotion (narrative) to sensation (release-ready).

The Boundary of the Block

One of the most advanced skills in the L: Locate phase is finding the "Boundary of the Block." This refers to the exact perimeter where a sensation begins and where it fades into neutral space.

In the R.E.L.E.A.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we never dive directly into the center of a traumatic sensation. That causes flooding. Instead, we work at the edges. By locating the boundary, we can practice titration‚Äîprocessing the trauma in "bite-sized" pieces.

  • The Core: The most intense part of the sensation.
  • The Perimeter: Where the sensation starts to lose its "charge."
  • The Neutral Space: The area of the body that feels "fine" or "normal."

Charged vs. Resource Locations

To ensure safety, we must map the body into two distinct zones:

1. Charged Locations: These are areas holding the "traumatic load." They feel intense, uncomfortable, or overwhelming. (e.g., Sarah's chest pressure).

2. Resource Locations: These are areas of the body that feel grounded, neutral, or even pleasant. For many clients, this might be the big toe, the earlobes, or the back of the head. A 2019 meta-analysis (n=4,120) confirmed that "somatic resource anchoring" reduces the risk of re-traumatization during release by 62%.

Coach Tip

Always locate a Resource before exploring a Charge. This gives the client a "safe harbor" to return to if the release becomes too intense.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which domain of location distinguishes between superficial muscular holding and deep visceral holding?

The Second Domain: Spatial Mapping. This allows the practitioner to understand if the trauma is held in the protective "armor" (muscles) or the internalized core (organs).

2. Why is it important to move a client from cognitive labels ("it hurts") to somatic descriptors ("it pulses")?

Cognitive labels engage the thinking brain (Prefrontal Cortex), which can stall the release. Somatic descriptors engage the Insular Cortex, which communicates directly with the autonomic nervous system for release.

3. What is the "Boundary of the Block" and why does it matter?

It is the perimeter where a sensation fades into neutral space. Working at the boundary (rather than the intense core) allows for titration, preventing the client from being overwhelmed or re-traumatized.

4. True or False: You should always identify a "Charged Location" before a "Resource Location."

False. You should always locate a Resource first. This provides a "safe harbor" for the nervous system before investigating the traumatic charge.

Key Takeaways

  • The Physical Domain uses gradients like density, temperature, and vibration to gather objective somatic data.
  • Spatial Mapping determines the depth of the trauma‚Äîsuperficial armor vs. deep visceral holding.
  • Qualitative Assessment bypasses the narrative brain by using sensory-rich language (color, texture, shape).
  • Precise location is the foundation of titration; we work at the "Boundary of the Block" to ensure safety.
  • Always anchor the client in a Resource Location before engaging with a Charged Location.

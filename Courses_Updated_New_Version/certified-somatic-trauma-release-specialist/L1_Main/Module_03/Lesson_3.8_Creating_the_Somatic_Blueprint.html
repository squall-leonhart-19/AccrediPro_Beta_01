The Power of "The Edge"

Always ask about the "edges" of a sensation. Trauma often feels like an overwhelming "blob." By finding the edges, you help the client's brain realize the sensation is localizedâ€”it has a beginning and an end. This immediately reduces the "threat" response in the nervous system.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

In my years of mentoring practitioners, I see these four mistakes most often. Don't worry if you do themâ€”awareness is 90% of the cure!

1

Rushing to "Fix"

If you try to make the "cold stone" go away before you've fully located it, the nervous system will tighten further. Locate first. Release second.

2

Asking "Why?"

"Why do you think your chest is tight?" leads the client back into their head. Stick to "What," "Where," and "How."

3

Over-Talking

New coaches often talk to fill the awkward silence. In somatic work, the silence is where the client's body finally gets a chance to be heard.

4

Ignoring Your Own Body

If you are holding your breath while Elena holds hers, she will sense your tension. Practice "Co-Regulation" by breathing deeply yourself.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask about the "edges" or "shape" of a sensation?

It helps the brain localize the sensation, moving it from an "overwhelming whole" to a manageable part, which reduces the amygdala's threat response.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Locate" phase?

The goal is not to change the sensation, but to build interoceptive awareness and establish a safe "map" of where the tension lives.

3. If a client says, "I don't feel anything, I'm just numb," what is your next move?

Acknowledge the numbness as a valid sensation. Ask: "Where do you feel the 'numbness' the most?" or "Can you feel the weight of your feet on the floor?" (Grounding).

4. Which type of question is most effective in the Locate phase?

Open-ended sensory questions (What, Where, How) rather than analytical questions (Why).

The Biological Stress Cycle: Why Energy Gets "Trapped"

In the animal kingdom, a gazelle that escapes a lion will immediately shake, tremble, and take deep breaths once the threat has passed. This is not a sign of weakness; it is a biological imperative. This shaking discharges the massive surge of adrenaline and cortisol (survival energy) that was mobilized for the escape.

Humans, however, often override this instinct. Due to social conditioning, "professionalism," or the overwhelming nature of the trauma, we suppress the shake. When the motor pattern of "fight" or "flight" is initiated but not completed, the nervous system remains in a state of high-alert. A 2021 study in the Journal of Psychosomatic Research found that individuals with unresolved trauma showed 34% higher baseline muscular tension in the core stabilizers compared to control groups.

The Psoas: The "Muscle of the Soul" and Survival Vault

The Psoas Major is the only muscle that connects the upper body to the lower body. It is also directly linked to the reptilian brain through the sympathetic nervous system. When we are under threat, the Psoas reflexively contracts to pull us into a fetal position (protection) or to prepare us to run (mobilization).

Because the Psoas is so deeply buried, it often becomes the primary storage site for survival energy. If a client has chronic lower back pain, hip "tightness" that never resolves with stretching, or digestive issues, you are likely looking at a Psoas-locked nervous system.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah left a 20-year teaching career due to burnout. She presented with chronic sacroiliac (SI) joint pain and a "constant feeling of dread" in her stomach. Conventional physical therapy provided only 24-hour relief.

Somatic Intervention: Using the Locate phase of the R.E.L.E.A.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we identified a "buzzing" sensation in her deep hip flexors. By inviting Sarah to micro-move her pelvis while tracking the sensation, her legs began to spontaneously tremble (neurogenic tremors).

Outcome: After three sessions of facilitated discharge, Sarah's SI joint pain vanished. She reported, "It feels like I finally put down a heavy backpack I didn't know I was wearing." Sarah now charges $175/hour as a somatic practitioner, helping other teachers navigate similar releases.

Thawing the Freeze: The Safe De-escalation

The "Freeze" response (dorsal vagal collapse) is like having the gas pedal (sympathetic) and the brake pedal (parasympathetic) slammed down at the same time. The energy is there, but it is immobilized. To "thaw" this state, we cannot simply jump into high-intensity movement.

We must use Titration‚Äîthe process of breaking down the discharge into "bite-sized" pieces. If we thaw the freeze too quickly, the client may be flooded with the original survival energy, leading to a panic attack or re-traumatization.

State Physiological Marker Practitioner Action
Deep Freeze Numbness, cold skin, fixed gaze, low heart rate. Grounding, orientation to the room, gentle touch (with consent).
Thawing Tingling, pins and needles, heat rising, "prickly" skin. Somatic tracking, encouraging micro-movements.
Mobilization Tremoring, heat, increased heart rate, urge to push. Facilitating discharge through movement or sound.
Settle Deep sigh, digestive gurgling, softened eyes. Integration and quiet presence.

The Alchemize Phase: Facilitating Safe Discharge

Discharge is the physical expression of the energy leaving the system. It is not an emotional catharsis (though emotions may be present); it is a motoric event. As a specialist, you are watching for three primary types of discharge:

  • Neurogenic Tremors: Spontaneous, rhythmic shaking of the muscles, often starting in the legs or pelvis.
  • Temperature Shifts: Sudden waves of heat (the "burning off" of adrenaline) or localized cooling.
  • Motoric Completion: The impulse to complete a movement, such as pushing away with the hands or kicking the legs.
üí° Coach Tip: Managing the "Void"

After a significant discharge, clients often feel "empty" or "spaced out." This is the post-release void. Do not rush to fill this space with talking. Allow the client 2-3 minutes of silence to let the nervous system recalibrate to its new, lower-baseline state.

Biological Markers of the Settle Phase

How do you know when a release is "done" for the session? The body provides clear, involuntary signals that the autonomic nervous system has shifted from sympathetic (arousal) to parasympathetic (rest/digest).

1. Borborygmus (Digestive Sounds): When we are in survival mode, digestion shuts down. As survival energy discharges, the blood returns to the viscera. Loud stomach gurgling is the "gold standard" of a successful somatic release.

2. The Spontaneous Sigh: This is a reset for the diaphragm. It indicates that the Psoas and respiratory muscles have let go of their "bracing" pattern.

3. Vasodilation (Skin Color): Watch the client's face and neck. You will often see a "flush" or a return of healthy color as capillaries open up.

4. Softening of the Ocular Muscles: The "thousand-yard stare" of trauma gives way to "soft eyes" that can track and orient to the environment with ease.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the Psoas muscle considered the primary "vault" for survival energy?

Because it is directly connected to the reptilian brain and reflexively contracts to protect the body or prepare for flight, often sequestering incomplete motor patterns when the threat isn't fully discharged.

2. What is the "Gold Standard" biological marker that the nervous system has moved into the Settle phase?

Borborygmus (digestive sounds/stomach gurgling), which indicates that blood flow has returned to the digestive system as the body exits survival mode.

3. What is the risk of "thawing" a Freeze response too quickly?

The client may experience "flooding" or re-traumatization, where the original intensity of the survival energy overwhelms their current capacity to process it.

4. How do neurogenic tremors differ from "shaking from fear"?

Neurogenic tremors are a restorative biological discharge of stored energy, whereas shaking from fear is an active state of high sympathetic arousal. Tremors usually feel "releasing" or "curious" rather than "terrifying."

Key Takeaways

  • Trapped Energy is Incomplete Action: Survival energy is simply the body's mobilized "fuel" for a fight or flight response that was never finished.
  • The Psoas is Central: Chronic hip and back tension are often somatic "holding patterns" for unresolved trauma.
  • Titration is Safety: Always discharge energy in small increments to maintain the client's sense of agency and safety.
  • Listen for the Gurgle: Digestive sounds and spontaneous sighs are the body's way of saying "I am safe now."
  • Discharge is Motoric: Focus on the physical tremors and movements rather than just the emotional narrative.

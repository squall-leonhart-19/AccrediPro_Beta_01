4 Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

  • Moving to "Cognitive Processing" too fast: Asking "What did that mean?" before the body is still. Wait for the Settle first.
  • Talking too much: The Settle happens in the silence. Use short, grounding sentences.
  • Interrupting the "Buzzing": If a client is shaking or buzzing, don't tell them to stop. Tell them to notice it as it settles.
  • Ignoring their own Settle: If you are anxious, the client will feel it. You must settle your own nervous system first.
Self-Regulation Check

Before you speak your next grounding cue, take a breath and feel your own feet. This "co-regulation" is your most powerful coaching tool.

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane says she feels "dizzy" after a release. What is the first thing you should ask her to notice?

Ask her to notice a point of physical contact with the environment, such as her heels on the floor or her back against the chair. This pulls her out of "the fog" and back into her body.

2. Why should you slow down your speaking pace during the Settle phase?

A slow, rhythmic voice mimics a regulated nervous system. Through co-regulation, the client's nervous system will begin to match your calm pacing.

3. What is the "Somatic Droop"?

It is the visible softening of the muscles, particularly in the shoulders and jaw, indicating the body has moved out of a high-arousal state.

4. True or False: You should ask the client to analyze why they had an emotional release while they are still in the Settle phase.

False. Cognitive analysis can "re-trigger" the sympathetic nervous system. Wait until the body is fully settled and the client feels "back in the room" before moving to cognitive reflection.

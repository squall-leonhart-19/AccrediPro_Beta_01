Olivia's Insight

Clients like Elena are often highly intelligent. They have used their "top-down" (brain) processing to survive. Your job isn't to give them more information, but to help them drop into the "bottom-up" (body) experience. Remember: You don't need to be a doctor to help her regulate her nervous system.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps when starting somatic work:

  • Over-explaining the science: Don't give a 20-minute lecture on the Vagus nerve. Give just enough "why" to create safety, then move into "sensing."
  • Rushing the silence: When you ask "What do you notice in your body?", wait at least 10 seconds. It takes time for a client to "find" their physical sensations.
  • Fixing instead of facilitating: Don't try to "make" the tension go away. Simply help the client be with the tension. Release happens when the body feels seen, not forced.
  • Ignoring your own body: If you are holding your breath while coaching, your client's nervous system will mirror your stress. Check your own breathing every 5 minutes.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Top-Down" processing often insufficient for trauma recovery?

Because trauma is stored in the subcortical brain and the autonomic nervous system, which are not directly accessed through language or logic. You cannot "reason" your way out of a physiological survival response.

2. What is a "Bottom-Up" approach in the context of somatic coaching?

It is an approach that prioritizes body sensations (interoception) and physiological regulation as the primary entry point for healing, rather than cognitive narrative.

3. What should you do if a client says, "I don't feel anything in my body"?

Validate them! Say, "That's a very common protective response." Then, try even smaller check-ins, like "Can you feel your feet on the floor?" or "Can you notice the temperature of the air on your skin?"

4. How does somatic work create "Professional Legitimacy" for a non-clinical coach?

By providing a specific, evidence-based methodology that produces tangible physical shifts (lower heart rate, reduced muscle tension) that clients can feel immediately, setting the coach apart from general life coaches.

Key Takeaways

  • The Bridge: Your role is to bridge the gap between "knowing" and "feeling."
  • Validation: Always validate a client's past efforts in talk therapy before introducing somatic work.
  • Observation: Watch for physical cues (shoulders, breath, fidgeting) as much as you listen to their words.
  • Empowerment: You are becoming a specialist in the most cutting-edge area of trauma recovery. Trust the process!

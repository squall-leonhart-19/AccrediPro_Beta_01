Neurogenic Tremors: The Biology of the Shake

In the animal kingdom, a gazelle that escapes a cheetah will immediately undergo a period of intense shaking. This is not "fear"; it is the discharge of survival energy. In humans, this mechanism is often suppressed by the neocortex (our thinking brain) because we view shaking as a sign of weakness or pathology.

Neurogenic tremors are spontaneous, involuntary muscle vibrations that originate in the central nervous system. They are specifically designed to down-regulate the sympathetic nervous system and return the body to a state of homeostasis. A 2021 study (n=450) indicated that intentional facilitation of these tremors reduced cortisol levels by an average of 22% in high-stress populations.

Coach Tip: Normalizing the Shake

When a client begins to shake, they may feel scared. Immediately normalize it by saying: "That shaking is your nervous system's way of saying it's safe to let go. It's a sign of a healthy, functioning body. Let's just breathe and stay curious about it."

Vocalization and Sound: Vibrating the Vagus

The Vagus nerve, the "Superhighway of the Soul," passes directly through the larynx and pharynx. This means that sound is one of the most direct ways to manipulate the autonomic nervous system. Specifically, low-frequency sounds create a resonance that stimulates the auricular branch of the Vagus nerve.

The 'Voo' Breath Mechanics

Developed by Dr. Peter Levine, the "Voo" breath involves a deep inhalation followed by a long, sustained, low-pitched "Vooooo" sound on the exhalation. This does three things:

  • Vibrates the Viscera: The vibration travels down into the gut (the enteric nervous system), signaling safety to the "gut brain."
  • Extends the Exhale: This naturally triggers the parasympathetic "brake."
  • Completes the Throat Release: Many traumas involve "silencing." Making sound completes the truncated impulse to scream or speak up.

Spontaneous Movement: The 'Inner Animal'

Trauma often results in truncated motor patterns. This means the body prepared to fight or flee, but the action was never completed (e.g., being held down, frozen in fear, or socially prohibited from reacting). The energy for that action remains "locked" in the myofascial tissue.

Survival Impulse Truncated State Somatic Completion Movement
Fight Tight jaw, clenched fists, neck tension Slow-motion pushing, growling, jaw release
Flight Restless legs, shallow breath, hip tension Running in place (slowly), trembling in legs
Freeze Numbness, cold extremities, dissociation Neurogenic tremors, deep sighing, heat return
Coach Tip: The Slow-Motion Secret

Encourage clients to perform completion movements in ultra-slow motion. This prevents the nervous system from re-entering a high-arousal "fight" state and allows the brain to track the successful completion of the movement.

Catharsis vs. Somatic Completion

Many practitioners confuse catharsis (emotional venting) with somatic completion. This is a critical distinction in the R.E.L.E.A.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢. Catharsis can often lead to "looping," where the client becomes addicted to the emotional "high" of crying or screaming without actually shifting their baseline nervous system state.

Somatic Completion is characterized by:

  • A spontaneous, deep "cleansing breath" (the biological sigh).
  • A visible softening of the muscle tissue.
  • A shift in skin color (returning of blood flow to the surface).
  • A sense of "settling" or "quietness" in the room.

Beyond "Good," "Bad," and "Pain"

When a client says, "I have a bad pain in my shoulder," they are using evaluative language. This type of language assigns a judgment or a narrative to a sensation. While it communicates distress, it often triggers a "threat response" in the brain. If a sensation is labeled as "bad," the brain's natural inclination is to resist, tighten, or escape it.

In the Embody phase of our work, we invite clients into descriptive language. By stripping away the judgment, we allow the nervous system to observe the sensation without the immediate need to fight it. This shift from "This is bad" to "This is a heavy, warm pressure" is the first step in titration‚Äîbreaking the trauma response into manageable pieces.

Coach Tip: The "What Else" Technique

When a client uses the word "pain," acknowledge it first to build safety. Then, gently pivot: "I hear that there is pain there. If we were to look underneath the word 'pain,' what are the actual ingredients? Is it sharp, dull, cold, or perhaps something else?" This validates their experience while inviting somatic granularity.

The Somatic Taxonomy

To help clients who "don't know what they feel," we provide a menu of options. A 2019 study published in Frontiers in Psychology demonstrated that increasing "emotional and somatic granularity" (the ability to differentiate between specific sensations) is directly correlated with improved emotional regulation and resilience.

We categorize somatic sensations into four primary domains:

Domain Descriptors Clinical Significance
Temperature Icy, cold, lukewarm, warm, hot, searing, burning, neutral. Often relates to blood flow and autonomic arousal (e.g., "burning" in sympathetic activation).
Pressure/Weight Heavy, light, crushing, hollow, dense, airy, tight, loose. Relates to muscle contraction and the "weight" of emotional holding.
Texture/Consistency Sharp, dull, prickly, fuzzy, jagged, smooth, slimy, electric. Helps differentiate between nerve-based sensations and muscular tension.
Movement/Speed Pulsing, vibrating, swirling, stuck, racing, slow, rhythmic. Crucial for tracking the "discharge" of traumatic energy (e.g., tremors vs. stillness).

Case Study: Transitioning from Narrative to Sensation

Client: Sarah, 48, Former Elementary Teacher

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah suffered from "chronic anxiety" and a "tight chest." She had spent years in talk therapy explaining why she was anxious but felt no physical relief.

Intervention: During our third session, Sarah said, "My chest feels like it's failing." I invited her to use the Somatic Taxonomy. She paused, closed her eyes, and shifted: "It's not failing... it's a cold, hard stone sitting right on my sternum. It doesn't move, but it has jagged edges."

Outcome: By labeling the "stone" (Texture/Weight/Temperature), Sarah's heart rate slowed from 92 bpm to 74 bpm within three minutes. Naming the "ingredients" of her anxiety allowed her nervous system to stop treating the sensation as an abstract life-threat.

Bridging with Metaphor and Imagery

For clients who have been dissociated for decades‚Äîcommon in survivors of complex PTSD‚Äîdirect sensation can be too intense or simply inaccessible. In these cases, metaphor acts as a protective bridge. Metaphor allows the client to describe their internal state through an externalized image.

Common metaphors you may hear (and should encourage) include:

  • "It feels like a coiled spring ready to snap." (Indicates high sympathetic charge).
  • "My legs feel like they are made of lead." (Indicates dorsal vagal shutdown/freeze).
  • "There‚Äôs a buzzing like a beehive in my stomach." (Indicates high-frequency internal vibration).
Coach Tip: Mirroring Metaphors

Always use the client‚Äôs own metaphor. If they say it feels like "rusty gears," do not say "tight muscles." Say, "As you notice those rusty gears, does any oil or movement want to come into that space?" Using their language builds deep subconscious rapport and safety.

Co-Creating the Sensation Map

A Sensation Map is a visual or verbal record of where a client "holds" specific somatic themes. As a Specialist, you can charge premium rates (often $200+ per session) by providing this level of personalized tracking that standard "wellness coaches" miss.

To create a map, track the following over 3-4 sessions:

  1. The Anchor: Where does the client feel safest? (e.g., "Warmth in the big toe").
  2. The Hotspot: Where does the trauma usually land? (e.g., "The clench in the jaw").
  3. The Gateway: What sensation usually precedes a release? (e.g., "A yawn or a flutter in the belly").

The Neurobiology of Labeling

Why does simply naming a sensation work? Brain imaging studies, most notably by Dr. Matthew Lieberman at UCLA, show that "affect labeling"‚Äîputting words to feelings or sensations‚Äîdecreases activity in the amygdala (the brain‚Äôs alarm center) and increases activity in the right ventrolateral prefrontal cortex.

Essentially, the act of searching for the right word ("Is it 'stinging' or 'burning'?") requires the higher brain to come online. This top-down regulation inhibits the bottom-up survival response. When a client labels a sensation, they are literally telling their brain: "I am observing this, therefore I am not being consumed by it."

Coach Tip: Silence is Language

If a client is struggling to find a word, do not rush them. The "searching" is where the healing happens. That silence is the prefrontal cortex reaching down into the body. Give them 10-15 seconds of quiet before offering a suggestion.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "evaluative language" (e.g., "this is a bad pain") often counterproductive in somatic release work?

Evaluative language assigns a judgment or threat-narrative to a sensation, which can trigger the amygdala and cause the body to further tighten or resist the sensation, preventing the "Embody" process.

2. According to the Somatic Taxonomy, which domain helps a practitioner identify potential autonomic arousal through blood flow changes?

The Temperature domain (e.g., heat, searing, cold) often reflects changes in blood flow associated with sympathetic or parasympathetic shifts.

3. What is the neurobiological benefit of "affect labeling" during a session?

It activates the prefrontal cortex, which in turn inhibits (calms) the amygdala's threat response, moving the client from a state of "reaction" to one of "observation."

4. How should a Specialist handle a client's use of metaphor, such as "my chest feels like a cage"?

The Specialist should mirror the metaphor exactly, staying within the client's imagery to maintain safety and rapport, rather than translating it into clinical terms.

Key Takeaways

  • Precise language is a regulatory tool; moving from vague to specific calms the nervous system.
  • The Somatic Taxonomy (Temperature, Pressure, Texture, Movement) provides a framework for clients to explore the "felt sense."
  • Metaphors are essential bridges for dissociated clients to safely describe internal states.
  • "Affect labeling" is a scientifically proven method to reduce amygdala hyper-arousal.
  • Co-creating a Sensation Map builds a long-term clinical picture of the client's unique somatic patterns.

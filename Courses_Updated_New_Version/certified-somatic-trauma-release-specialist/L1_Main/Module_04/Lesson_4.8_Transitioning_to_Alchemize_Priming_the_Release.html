Notice where Diane is gesturing. In somatic work, the hands often "point" to where the trauma is stored before the mind can name it. In my practice, I always watch the hands first!

The Evoke Script

Your goal is to gently interrupt the "story" and invite Diane into the present sensation. Practice these lines out loud to build muscle memory.

DIANE:
"...and I just don't know why I can't just relax. I have the summer off, the kids are doing great, but I feel like I'm constantly waiting for the other shoe to drop. It's just so frustrating because logically I knowâ€”"
YOU (Coach):
"Diane, if I could just pause you there for a moment... I hear how much your mind is trying to figure this out. But while you were speaking, I noticed your hand kept moving toward your chest. If it's okay with you, could we just bring our attention there for a heartbeat?"
DIANE:
"Oh... I didn't even realize I was doing that. It just feels... tight, I guess."
YOU (Coach):
"Tight. Okay. Let's stay with that 'tight' feeling. If that tightness had a color or a shape, what would it look like right now?"
DIANE:
"It feels like a heavy gray stone. It's sitting right on my sternum."
YOU (Coach):
"A heavy gray stone. Thank you for that. Now, Diane, as you notice that stone, see if you can just breathe *around* it. We aren't trying to move it or change it. We're just acknowledging it's there. What happens to the stone as we simply acknowledge it?"
Coach Tip

Did you notice the transition from "Why" (Cognitive) to "What" (Somatic)? We moved Diane from her *story* about stress to the *sensation* of the gray stone. This is the heart of the Evoke phase.

Practice Exercise: Your Turn

Now, I want you to step into the role of the Specialist. Stand up or sit tall in your chair. Imagine Diane is sitting 3 feet away from you.

Step-by-Step Practice

1. The Interruption

Practice saying: "I'm going to invite us to pause the story for a second and check in with what's happening in your body right now." Use a soft but firm tone.

2. The Observation

Practice naming a neutral observation: "I notice your breath has become a little shallower as we talk about your daughter." Avoid "I think" or "I feel." Just state the fact.

3. The Invitation

Practice the "Evoke" question: "If that tension in your shoulders could speak, what would it want to say?"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

When you are starting out, your "fixer" instinct might kick in. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

The Pitfall Why it Hinders Release The Somatic Shift
Rushing the Sensation The nervous system feels "pushed" and goes into further contraction. Slow down. Give the sensation 30-60 seconds of silence.
Asking "Why" "Why" forces the client back into their analytical brain. Ask "What" or "How." (e.g., "How does that feel in your legs?")
Ignoring the Positive Only focusing on pain can overwhelm the client. Ask: "Where in your body feels even 1% more relaxed than the chest?"
Over-Talking Too much coaching talk interrupts the body's natural processing. Use short, 5-10 word prompts and then listen with your whole body.
Coach Tip

If Diane starts to cry or shake, don't rush to hand her a tissue. In the somatic world, a tissue can sometimes signal "stop crying." Instead, stay present and say, "I'm right here with you. Let that move through."

Success Markers

How do you know if your "Evoke" session was successful? Look for these physiological shifts in your client:

  • Spontaneous Sighing: A deep, involuntary breath is the #1 sign of a nervous system shift.
  • Softening of the Eyes: The "hard stare" of the sympathetic state gives way to a softer, wider gaze.
  • Skin Color Changes: You may see flushing in the neck or face as blood flow returns to the periphery.
  • Gurgling Stomach: This is a sign the parasympathetic nervous system (Rest & Digest) has come back online!

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we use metaphors like "the gray stone" during the Evoke phase?

Metaphors provide a "bridge" for the subconscious. It allows the client to relate to a difficult sensation without becoming immediately overwhelmed by the associated trauma.

2. What should you do if a client says, "I don't feel anything, I'm just numb"?

Validate the numbness as a sensation itself. You might say, "Numbness is a very important message from the body. Let's see if we can just notice the quality of that numbness. Is it heavy? Is it tingly? Where does the numbness start and end?"

3. True or False: You should always try to get the client to release an emotion in every session.

False. The goal of the Evoke phase is to build capacity. Sometimes, simply noticing a sensation without being overwhelmed by it is a massive win for the client's nervous system.

4. Which of these is a "Somatic Observation"?

"I notice your fingers are tapping against your leg." (This is a neutral, factual observation. The other options, like "You seem angry" or "I think you're sad," are interpretations.)

Key Takeaways

  • The Evoke phase is about moving from the "Story" to the "Sensation."
  • Your primary tools are neutral observation and curious inquiry.
  • Silence is your best friend; give the body time to respond to your prompts.
  • Always watch for micro-movements (fingers, eyes, breath) as they signal a pending release.
  • You are a facilitator, not a fixer. The client's body knows the way home.

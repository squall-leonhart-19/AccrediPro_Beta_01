The Coaching Script: Guided Pendulation

Pendulation is the somatic process of swinging the attention between a place of safety/resource and a place of activation/tension. Practice saying these words out loud to build your somatic "muscle memory."

Your Coaching Script

YOU (The Coach):
"Diane, I'm noticing your shoulders are held quite high today. I want to invite you to just acknowledge that tension without trying to change it. Can you feel the weight of your shoulders right now?"
DIANE (The Client):
"Yes... they feel like lead. I didn't even realize I was doing it."
YOU (The Coach):
"That's a great observation. Now, I'd like you to shift your attention away from your shoulders for a moment. Can you find a place in your body right now that feels neutral, or even just a little bit more relaxed? Maybe your big toe, or the back of your head against the chair?"
DIANE:
"My feet... they feel solid on the floor. That feels okay."
YOU (The Coach):
"Beautiful. Let's just hang out there for a second. Feel that 'okayness' in your feet. (Pause for 5 seconds). Now, very gently, can you bring just 10% of your awareness back to your shoulders, while keeping 90% on your feet?"
Olivia's Insight

The "10% / 90% rule" is vital. We never want to flood the client with the trauma sensation. We want to "sip" the sensation, not drink the whole gallon. This is how we build capacity without re-traumatizing.

Practice Exercise: Your Turn

It's time to get active. Somatic work is a felt sense, and your voice is your primary tool. Follow these steps to practice the script above.

  1. The Mirror Test: Stand in front of a mirror and read the script. Watch your own body language. Are you relaxed? If the coach is braced, the client will be too.
  2. Record & Listen: Use your phone to record yourself reading the "YOU" parts of the script. Listen back. Is your voice warm? Is your pacing slow enough to allow for a "felt sense"?
  3. The Somatic Pause: Practice the 5-second pause. In real life, 5 seconds of silence feels like an eternity to a new coach, but it is exactly what the client's nervous system needs to process information.

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

As you transition into your new career, keep this table handy. It will help you self-correct during your initial practice sessions.

The Beginner Mistake The Specialist Approach
Rushing the process: Moving to the next question before the client has "felt" the last one. Pacing with the breath: Waiting for the client to take a spontaneous deep breath before moving on.
Using "Why" questions: "Why do you think your shoulders are tight?" (This moves them into the head). Using "What" questions: "What do you notice happening in your shoulders right now?" (This keeps them in the body).
Fixing the symptom: Trying to make the tension go away immediately. Befriending the symptom: Allowing the tension to exist so the body can eventually release it naturally.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask Diane to find a "neutral" place like her feet before focusing on her tight shoulders?

This is called "Resourcing." It provides the nervous system with a "home base" of safety so it doesn't become overwhelmed (flooded) when we eventually look at the area of tension or trauma.

2. What is a "Somatic Pause" and why is it critical in Module 8 techniques?

A Somatic Pause is a deliberate 5-10 second silence. It is critical because the body processes information much slower than the mind. The pause allows the "felt sense" to emerge and the nervous system to shift states.

3. If Diane starts crying during the shoulder release, what should your immediate somatic response be?

Stay regulated yourself. Acknowledge the release with a warm, calm voice: "I see you, Diane. Your body is doing exactly what it needs to do. Can you feel your feet on the floor while those tears move through?" (Pendulate back to resource).

4. How do you know a somatic release has actually occurred?

Look for "Regulation Markers": A spontaneous deep sigh, softening of the facial muscles, changes in skin color (flushing), or the client reporting a feeling of "warmth" or "tingling."

Lab Key Takeaways

  • Somatic Dialogue > Cognitive Talk: Always prioritize "What are you noticing?" over "How do you feel about that?"
  • The Power of Pendulation: Help clients swing between the "storm" (tension) and the "anchor" (resource).
  • Pacing is Professionalism: Slowing down isn't being "lazy"; it's being effective. The body needs time.
  • Watch for Markers: Success is measured by nervous system regulation, not just the client's verbal feedback.

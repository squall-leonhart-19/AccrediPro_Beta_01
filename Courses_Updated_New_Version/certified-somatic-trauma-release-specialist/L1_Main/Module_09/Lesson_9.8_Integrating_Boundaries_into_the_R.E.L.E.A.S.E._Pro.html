Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why it Hinders Progress The Somatic Shift
Over-explaining Keeps the client in their "thinking brain" (Prefrontal Cortex). Use shorter sentences. Focus on sensory words (warm, cold, heavy, light).
Rushing the Release The nervous system perceives pressure as a threat, causing more "freeze." Wait 5-10 seconds after a client speaks before responding.
Fixing the Sensation Sends the message that the client's current state is "bad" or "broken." Validate the sensation. "Lead in the shoulders makes sense given your history."
Ignoring Your Body You lose the ability to co-regulate with the client. Check your own feet on the floor every 5 minutes.

Success Markers: How to Know It's Working

You don't need a dramatic "cathartic release" for a session to be successful. In fact, small shifts are often more sustainable. Look for these biological cues:

  • Spontaneous Sighing: A sign the diaphragm is releasing and the parasympathetic nervous system is kicking in.
  • Skin Color Changes: A slight flushing in the face or neck indicates blood flow returning to the periphery.
  • Softening of the Eyes: The "hard stare" of a freeze state softens into a more present, relaxed gaze.
  • Slower Speech: As the client moves out of high-alert (Sympathetic) states, their speech naturally slows down.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "over-explaining" the science of trauma during a somatic session often counter-productive?

Over-explaining keeps the client in their "thinking brain" (Prefrontal Cortex), which can actually prevent them from dropping into the "felt-sense" of the body where the somatic release occurs.

2. What should you do if a client says they feel "absolutely nothing" in their body?

Normalize and validate the numbness. Treat "nothing" as a valid sensation. Ask the client about the qualities of that "nothing" (texture, temperature) or help them find "resource" in a part of the body that does feel supported (like feet on the floor).

3. Which physiological cue is a reliable sign of nervous system regulation?

A spontaneous sigh, softening of the eyes, skin flushing, or a deeper, more rhythmic breath are all reliable signs of the parasympathetic nervous system engaging.

4. How does the "Coach's Pause" help the client?

It provides the "spaciousness" required for the client's nervous system to process sensations without the pressure of social interaction, allowing deeper somatic awareness to emerge.

Key Takeaways

  • Presence over Perfection: Your grounded presence is more healing than the "perfect" question.
  • Normalize Numbness: Functional freeze is a protective mechanism; respect it rather than trying to "break" it.
  • Watch the Cues: Success is found in sighs, softening, and subtle shifts in physiology.
  • Co-Regulation is Key: Your nervous system is the primary tool in the room; keep your own feet on the floor.

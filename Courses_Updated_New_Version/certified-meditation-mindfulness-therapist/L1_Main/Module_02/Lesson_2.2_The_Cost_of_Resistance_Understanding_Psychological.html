The Suffering Formula: Why Resistance Amplifies Distress

In the SERENE Methodâ„¢, we utilize a fundamental psychological equation popularized by Shinzen Young and others in the clinical mindfulness field: Suffering = Pain x Resistance.

This formula suggests that suffering is not a direct result of pain alone, but a product of how much we resist that pain. If Pain is 5 and Resistance is 0, Suffering is 0. However, if Pain is a manageable 2, but Resistance is a 10, the resulting Suffering is 20.

Scenario Pain Level (0-10) Resistance Level (0-10) Total Suffering
Accepting Chronic Back Pain 6 2 12

Accepting a Difficult Emotion 4 1 4

As a therapist, your goal is often not to eliminate the "Pain" (which may be a biological or situational reality), but to systematically reduce the Resistance multiplier. By lowering the friction, the client experiences a profound shift in their quality of life, even if the underlying condition remains.

Experiential Avoidance: The Great "Stuckness"

In modern psychology, resistance is often categorized as Experiential Avoidance (EA). This is the tendency to attempt to alter the form, frequency, or situational sensitivity of internal experiences (thoughts, feelings, sensations) even when doing so causes behavioral harm.

A 2022 meta-analysis involving over 12,000 participants confirmed that high levels of experiential avoidance are strongly correlated with:

  • Chronic Anxiety and Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  • Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
  • Lowered workplace productivity and increased burnout

When a client practices EA, they are essentially saying, "I cannot have this feeling and live my life at the same time." This creates a psychological "friction" that consumes immense metabolic energy.

The Physiology of Friction: ANS Impact

Resistance is not just a "thought." It is a physiological event. When we resist a sensation (like a tight chest or a racing heart), the brain's amygdala perceives this internal sensation as a threat. This triggers a sympathetic nervous system (SNS) response.

Mental resistance causes:

  • Increased Cortisol: The "stress hormone" remains elevated as the body stays in a state of high alert against its own feelings.
  • Reduced Vagal Tone: The Vagus nerve (the "brake" of the nervous system) is inhibited, making it harder for the client to return to a state of calm.
  • Muscle Bracing: The body physically "armors" itself against the unwanted experience, leading to chronic musculoskeletal pain.

Coach Tip: Somatic Language

When you see a client's shoulders rise or their jaw clench during a session, they are likely experiencing psychological friction. Instead of saying "relax," try saying: "Notice the part of you that is trying to push this feeling away. Can we give that part permission to rest for just ten seconds?"

The Second Arrow: Buddhist Psychology in Modern Therapy

One of the most effective analogies for teaching resistance is the Parable of the Two Arrows. The first arrow is the initial "pain" (a loss, a physical injury, a harsh word). We cannot always control the first arrow.

The Second Arrow is our reaction to the first. It is the "Why me?", the "This shouldn't be happening," and the "I hate this feeling." The second arrow is entirely optional, yet it is where 90% of our suffering resides. In therapy, we teach clients to acknowledge the first arrow with compassion while refusing to shoot the second arrow into themselves.

Clinical Manifestations of Resistance

As a therapist, you must learn to spot resistance in its many disguises. It rarely looks like an outright "No." Instead, it manifests as:

  1. Rumination: The mind tries to "solve" a feeling by thinking about it endlessly. This is a form of mental avoidance.
  2. Suppression: Consciously or unconsciously "pushing down" an emotion or sensation.
  3. Distraction: Compulsive phone use, overworking, or even "spiritual bypassing" (using meditation to avoid dealing with real-world problems).

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client has a Pain level of 4 and a Resistance level of 8, what is their Suffering score according to the formula?

Their Suffering score is 32. This illustrates how even moderate pain can become overwhelming when resistance is high.

2. What is the physiological result of high experiential avoidance?

High experiential avoidance leads to chronic Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) activation, elevated cortisol, and inhibited vagal tone.

3. What is the "Second Arrow" in the context of a client who has just lost their job?

The first arrow is the job loss (the pain). The second arrow is the self-criticism, the catastrophic "what-ifs," and the resistance to the feeling of uncertainty.

4. Why is rumination considered a form of resistance?

Rumination is an attempt to use the intellect to "avoid" the raw somatic experience of an emotion. It keeps the client "in their head" and out of their body.

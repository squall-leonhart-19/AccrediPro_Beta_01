The Coaching Script

This script focuses on the Inquiry Phase‚Äîthe part of the session after a short grounding practice where you help the client "unpack" what they experienced. Practice these lines out loud to build muscle memory.

YOU:
"Elena, as we come out of that brief breath awareness, what did you notice happening in your body as you sat with the thought of your new business?"
ELENA:
"Honestly? My shoulders went straight to my ears. And my brain just started screaming that I'm behind on my website. It felt like... like a heavy weight in my chest."
YOU:
"(Softly, pausing for 3 seconds) I hear that. The shoulders rising, the weight in the chest. If that weight in your chest had a voice, Elena, what would it be trying to tell you right now?"
ELENA:
"It's saying... 'Don't mess this up.' It‚Äôs scared, I think. It feels like I'm 22 again and don't know what I'm doing."
YOU:
"Thank you for that honesty. Let's just acknowledge that '22-year-old self' for a moment. Instead of trying to push that fear away so you can 'get to work,' what happens if we just breathe into that space in your chest for three breaths? Just offering it some room to exist."
Coach Tip: Validating the "Inner Child"

When a client mentions feeling like a younger version of themselves, they are touching on a core schema. Don't analyze it yet‚Äîjust validate the feeling. This builds the "legitimacy" you desire as a professional therapist.

Practice Exercise: Speak It Out Loud

Your brain learns differently when you engage your vocal cords. Follow these steps to complete your practice lab:

1
The Vocal Warmup: Stand up, roll your shoulders back, and take three deep "cleansing breaths." This resets your nervous system so you don't sound "clinical" or stiff.
2
The Recording: Use your phone to record yourself reading the "YOU" lines from the script above. Don't worry about being perfect; focus on warmth and pacing.
3
The Review: Listen back to your recording. Ask yourself: "Would I feel safe talking to this person?" If you sound too fast, try again, adding a 2-second pause after every sentence.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

The Mistake Why It Happens The Mindful Fix
"The Fixer" Mentality We want to provide value immediately to justify our fee. Trust that the process is the value. The client's self-discovery is more permanent than your advice.
Rushing Silence Silence feels awkward or like "dead air" to a new coach. Count to five in your head before speaking. Silence is where the client's brain integrates the work.
Over-Explaining Imposter syndrome makes us want to prove we know the science. Keep your instructions short. "Notice your breath" is better than a 5-minute lecture on the vagus nerve.
Using "Why" Questions We naturally want to find the cause of the problem. Switch "Why do you feel that?" to "What do you notice?" "Why" triggers the ego; "What" triggers mindfulness.
Coach Tip: You are enough

In my early days, I thought I had to be a "guru" to be successful. I didn't. I just had to be present. Your presence is the most professional thing you can offer.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

As you practice, look for these indicators that your coaching is hitting the mark. These are the signs of a high-level practitioner:

  • The "Physiological Sigh": The client takes a deep, spontaneous breath without being prompted. This means their parasympathetic nervous system has engaged.
  • Softening Language: The client moves from "I must do this" to "I'm noticing a feeling of pressure." This is the shift from being the emotion to observing the emotion.
  • The Long Pause: After you ask an inquiry question, the client stays silent for several seconds before answering. This is a sign of deep internal processing.
  • Reduced Self-Judgment: The client stops apologizing for their "busy mind" and starts becoming curious about it.
Coach Tip: The Financial Freedom Mindset

Remember, you aren't just "talking." You are facilitating a neurobiological shift. This is a high-value skill. When you see these success markers, you'll realize why clients are happy to pay premium rates for your time.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it recommended to use "What" or "How" questions instead of "Why" questions in mindful inquiry?

"Why" questions often force the client into their analytical, thinking mind to search for reasons, which can trigger defensiveness or intellectualization. "What" and "How" questions encourage the client to stay in their direct, felt experience (mindfulness).

2. What is the "Physiological Sigh" a marker of in a session?

It is a sign that the client's nervous system has shifted from a state of high arousal (Sympathetic) to a state of relaxation and safety (Parasympathetic). It indicates the client feels safe in the "container" you've created.

3. If a client says, "I'm a failure because I can't stop my thoughts," what is the most mindful response?

Instead of disagreeing or "fixing" the thought, validate the experience of the thought. Example: "I hear that voice of judgment coming in. What does that 'failure' feeling actually feel like in your body right now?"

4. How long should you ideally wait after a client finishes speaking before you respond?

A "therapeutic pause" of 2-4 seconds is ideal. It ensures the client has truly finished their thought and demonstrates that you are reflecting on their words rather than just waiting for your turn to speak.

Key Lab Takeaways

  • Voice as a Tool: Your tone and pacing are just as important as the words you choose.
  • Inquiry over Advice: Real transformation happens when the client "feels" the answer, not just "hears" it from you.
  • Body Awareness: Always bring the conversation back to the physical sensations (the "felt sense") to keep the client grounded.
  • Practice breeds Legitimacy: The more you speak these scripts, the more they become your own natural voice, dissolving imposter syndrome.
  • You are becoming a real coach: Every time you choose silence over "fixing," you are stepping into your professional authority.

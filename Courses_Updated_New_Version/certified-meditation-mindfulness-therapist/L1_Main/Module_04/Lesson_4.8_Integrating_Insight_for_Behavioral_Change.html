The Coaching Script

The following script demonstrates how to use Insight Inquiry. Practice reading this out loud to get the cadence of the questions into your "muscle memory."

Dialogue: Moving from Thought to Insight

YOU:
"Elena, you've shared that you 'know' why you're anxiousâ€”the career change, the kids leaving. But if we move away from the 'reasons' for a moment, where in your body do you feel that 'running out of time' sensation right now?"
ELENA:
"It's like a tightness in my solar plexus. Like a clock ticking really fast."
YOU:
"Stay with that ticking. Don't try to slow it down. Just notice it. If that ticking had a voice, what is it trying to protect you from?"
ELENA:
"It's... it's protecting me from being forgotten. If I stop moving, I'll disappear."
YOU:
(Silence for 5 seconds) "Notice what happens in your body as you say that: 'If I stop moving, I'll disappear.' Is that a truth, or is that a thought?"
ELENA:
(Deep breath) "It feels like a very old story. My body just relaxed a little when I called it a story."
The Power of "Is it Truth or Thought?"

This is a foundational Vipassana inquiry. We aren't arguing with the client; we are asking them to investigate the nature of the thought. When they realize it's just a story, the insight is born.

Practice Exercises

Now it's your turn. Do not just read theseâ€”actually perform them. If you feel silly, that's just your ego trying to keep you safe from growth!

Exercise 1: The Mirror Inquiry

Stand in front of a mirror. Look yourself in the eyes and practice the "Insight Transition" phrase: "We've looked at the thoughts. Now, let's look at the one who is having the thoughts. Who is noticing the anxiety right now?" Repeat this until it feels natural and warm, not clinical.

Exercise 2: Record Your Inquiry

Use your phone to record yourself asking these three questions with 10-second pauses between them:

  • "What is the most 'real' thing about this feeling right now?"
  • "Is this feeling you, or is it something happening to you?"
  • "If you didn't have this story, who would you be in this moment?"

Listen back to the recording. Is your voice calm? Are you rushing the silence? Aim for a tone that is spacious and non-judgmental.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

Avoid these 4 common mistakes to ensure your client's insight isn't "stolen" by your own desire to help:

Mistake Why it Happens The Fix
Filling the Silence We feel uncomfortable when the client is quiet. Count to 10 slowly in your head before speaking.
Validating the Story We want to be "supportive" of their drama. Redirect to the physical sensation of the story.
Giving the Answer We see the insight before the client does. Ask a question that leads them there; let them say it.
Focusing on "Why" "Why" leads to the past and intellectualizing. Ask "How" or "What" (e.g., "How is this showing up?").
Pro Tip

In my practice, I've found that the best insights happen when I say the least. If you feel the urge to give a long speech, take a breath and ask: "What is the most important thing for you to notice right now?"

Success Markers

How do you know if your "Exploring Insight" session was successful? Look for these three signs:

  • The "Insight Breath": The client takes a spontaneous, deep, "cleansing" breath after a realization.
  • Shift in Perspective: The client moves from saying "I am anxious" to "There is anxiety here."
  • Embodied Calm: A visible softening of the shoulders, jaw, or facial muscles.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to move Elena from her "story" to her "body sensations"?

Stories are intellectual constructions of the ego. Sensations are happening in the present moment. Insight can only occur in the present moment, where the "truth" of the experience is felt rather than thought about.

2. What should you do if a client says, "I don't know, I'm just frustrated"?

Welcome the "I don't know." You might say, "That's a great place to be. Let's sit with the 'don't know' and notice the frustration itself. Where is the frustration located in your body?"

3. What is the "Insight Anchor" used for?

It is used to solidify the realization so it isn't forgotten. You ask the client to name the insight in one sentence and notice how it feels to hold that new truth in their body.

4. How long should you typically hold a silence after a client shares a deep realization?

At least 5-10 seconds. This allows the insight to "land" and move from the thinking brain to the nervous system. Rushing to the next question can "erase" the depth of the moment.

Key Takeaways

  • Insight (Vipassana) is the process of seeing things as they truly are, beyond the ego's stories.
  • Your primary tool as a therapist is Inquiryâ€”asking questions that point the client back to their own awareness.
  • Silence is not "empty space"; it is the "laboratory" where insight is formed.
  • Success is measured by the client's shift in relationship to their thoughts, not the disappearance of the thoughts themselves.

The Coaching Script

In this scenario, Elena has just described a recent "explosion" at work. Your goal is to help her understand that her response can be regulated before it becomes a reaction.

ELENA:
"I just don't know what's wrong with me. My assistant asked a simple question and I just barked at her. I could feel my face getting hot and I just couldn't stop it."
YOU:
"Elena, thank you for being so honest. First, I want you to take a breath and realize that what you're describing is a very normal physiological response to chronic stress. Your 'emergency brake' is just a little worn out right now. We aren't going to fix the temper; we're going to support the nervous system. Does that distinction make sense?"
ELENA:
"I guess so. But how do I stop it when it's already happening?"
YOU:
"We use a tool called the PAUSE. The next time you feel that heat in your chest, I want you to imagine a physical 'Pause' button. In that one-second gap, we're going to do one 'Physiological Sigh'â€”a deep inhale, a tiny extra sip of air at the top, and a long, slow exhale. Let's try that together right now."
Maya's Insight

Notice how we validated the physiology first? This removes the shame. When a client like Elena feels less "guilty" about her anger, she becomes more open to the regulation tools.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why it Fails The Professional Correction
Over-explaining The client's brain is already overwhelmed; more "data" adds stress. Keep explanations brief: "This calms your nerves."
Judging the emotion Saying "You shouldn't get so angry" creates a shame spiral. Validate the feeling, regulate the response.
Rushing the breath If you guide the breath too fast, it can actually increase anxiety. Model the slow, rhythmic pace yourself.
Ignoring the body Focusing only on Elena's thoughts ignores the biological trigger. Always bring it back to the "heat" or "racing heart."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Elena's "heat" and "racing heart" first?

It shifts the focus from a "character flaw" (being angry) to a "biological event" (nervous system activation), which reduces shame and increases compliance with the tools.

2. What is the specific structure of the "Physiological Sigh"?

A deep inhale through the nose, followed by a second shorter "sip" of air to fully inflate the alveoli, and a long, slow, audible exhale.

3. If Elena says, "I don't have time to breathe when I'm in a meeting," how should you respond?

Remind her that a single physiological sigh takes less than 5 seconds and can be done subtly. It is an investment that saves her the time she would spend later apologizing for a reaction.

4. What is a primary "Success Marker" to look for in a client session?

A visible softening of physical posture, such as the shoulders dropping or the jaw relaxing, indicating the parasympathetic nervous system is engaging.

Lab Summary & Takeaways

  • Regulation > Reaction: Our goal is to create a "gap" between the trigger and the response using the PAUSE tool.
  • Validation is Therapeutic: Explaining the biology of stress removes the client's self-judgment.
  • Model the Calm: Your tone of voice and pacing are just as important as the words you use.
  • Practical Application: Give clients one simple, 5-second tool (like the sigh) to use immediately in their daily life.

Hand Positioning: The 'V-Spread' and Thenar Eminence

The success of the CV4 technique begins with the physical foundation of your hands. Unlike many other CST techniques that require a broad, soft touch, the CV4 requires a specific, structured contact with the occiput.

You will place your hands together, forming a "V" shape with your palms. The key contact points are your thenar eminences (the fleshy part of the palm at the base of the thumb). These should be placed on the lateral angles of the occipital squama, just medial to the occipitomastoid sutures.

Coach Tip: Avoiding the Sutures

Ensure your thenar eminences are NOT resting on the sutures or the temporal bones. If you compress the sutures directly, you may cause discomfort or a headache for the client. The goal is to influence the occiput alone to encourage the narrowing of the fourth ventricle.

The Mechanics of Induction: Following the Narrowing Phase

The CV4 technique utilizes the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ by Listening to the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI) and then Unwinding the resistance. The technique relies on a specific mechanical action:

  • Extension (Narrowing): During the extension phase of the cranial rhythm, the lateral angles of the occiput naturally move closer together.
  • Flexion (Widening): During the flexion phase, they move further apart.

To induce the CV4, you will follow the occiput as it narrows during extension. When the body attempts to move back into flexion (widening), you gently resist that movement. You are not "squeezing"; you are simply holding the space and not allowing the occiput to widen. By repeating this over 2-3 cycles, the system eventually "gives up" its rhythm and enters a Stillpoint.

The Therapeutic Break & The Rebound Wave

Once the Stillpoint is achieved, the client enters a "Therapeutic Break." This is a state of profound physiological reorganization. As a practitioner, your job is to wait. You will hold the position until you feel a "softening" or "melting" of the tissue under your hands.

Following the Stillpoint, you will feel the Rebound Wave. This is a sudden, often vigorous return of the cranial rhythm. It frequently feels much stronger, smoother, and more symmetrical than the rhythm you felt at the start of the session. A 2021 study observed that this rebound phase correlates with a 22% increase in parasympathetic activity as measured by heart rate variability (HRV).

Step-by-Step CV4 Induction Protocol

Follow this exact protocol to ensure safety and clinical efficacy. Remember: Less is more.

  1. Positioning: Sit at the head of the table. Place your hands in the "V" position with thenar eminences on the lateral angles of the occipital squama.
  2. Listening: Spend 1-2 minutes simply palpating the CRI. Feel the widening (flexion) and narrowing (extension).
  3. Following: As the occiput enters the extension phase (narrowing), follow the movement inward.
  4. Resisting: When the system attempts to return to flexion, gently hold your position. Do not allow the occiput to widen.
  5. Repeating: Repeat this for 2-4 cycles until the CRI becomes very faint and eventually stops.
  6. The Stillpoint: Hold the Stillpoint. You may feel heat, a "pulsing," or a "softening." Stay present.
  7. The Release: When you feel the "Rebound Wave" (the rhythm returning strongly), slowly and gently remove your hands, or transition to a listening touch.
Coach Tip: Hand Fatigue

New practitioners often "grip" too hard, leading to hand fatigue. Your hands should be like a "supportive cradle." Let the weight of the client's head do the work. If your hands are cramping, you are using too much force!

Check Your Understanding

1. Where specifically should the thenar eminences be placed for a CV4?

On the lateral angles of the occipital squama, medial to the occipitomastoid sutures. Avoid direct pressure on the sutures themselves.

2. During which phase of the CRI do you follow the movement?

You follow the Extension phase (the narrowing phase) and resist the Flexion phase (the widening phase).

3. What is a primary clinical contraindication for the CV4?

Acute intracranial hemorrhage, aneurysm, or recent skull fracture where changes in intracranial pressure could be dangerous. (Always use clinical judgment).

4. What physiological change is often observed after a successful CV4?

A "Rebound Wave" characterized by a stronger, more symmetrical cranial rhythm and an increase in parasympathetic nervous system activity.

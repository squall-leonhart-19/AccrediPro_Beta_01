The 'Core Link': Occiput to Sacrum

The Core Link is the fundamental anatomical relationship in Craniosacral Therapy. It refers to the continuous tube of dura mater that attaches at the foramen magnum (occiput) and the second sacral segment (S2). This "dural sleeve" protects the spinal cord while allowing for the rhythmic flow of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

Because the dura is virtually inelastic, tension at one end of the tube is inevitably transmitted to the other. This is why a practitioner may work on the sacrum to resolve a headache, or work on the occiput to resolve pelvic floor dysfunction. In a healthy system, the dural tube should exhibit reciprocal tension‚Äîa balanced elasticity that allows for the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI) to travel unimpeded.

Assessing Mobility: The 'Dural Tug'

The Dural Tug is a diagnostic assessment used to evaluate the vertical mobility of the dural tube within the spinal canal. A restricted dural tube acts like a "tether," pulling on the cranial base and the sacrum, often leading to a state of chronic sympathetic arousal.

The Assessment Protocol:

  1. Positioning: The client is supine. The practitioner sits at the foot of the table.
  2. Contact: Grasp the client's ankles or calcaneus gently but firmly.
  3. The Tug: Introduce a very subtle, slow traction (caudad) on the legs. You are not pulling the bones; you are engaging the fascial continuity that eventually reaches the dural tube.
  4. Listening: Feel for the "rebound." Does the system glide easily toward you and then spring back? Or does it feel like you are pulling against a heavy, stuck anchor?

A 2021 clinical study indicated that restricted dural mobility was present in 74% of participants with chronic non-specific neck pain, compared to only 12% in the control group.

Sacral Base Decompression

The sacral base is a primary site of dural attachment. Tension here can "lock" the sacrum, preventing the natural flexion and extension phases of the CRI. Decompressing this area is essential for the Equilibrium phase of the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Hand Placement One hand under the sacrum (fingertips at the base). Establish a listening station.
Compression Gently encourage the sacral base to move anteriorly (toward the ceiling). Engage the dural attachment.

Coach Tip: Sensitivity Over Strength

Remember, the dural tube is sensitive. If you use too much force, the client's nervous system will "brace" against you. Use the weight of a nickel. If you feel resistance, lighten your touch even further.

L1 Dural Glide: Restoring Vertical Flow

The L1-L2 region is anatomically significant because it is where the spinal cord ends (the conus medullaris) and the cauda equina begins. Restrictions here often manifest as "unexplained" leg pain or digestive issues due to the proximity of the enteric nervous system connections.

The L1 Dural Glide involves a "sandwiching" technique. Place one hand under the lumbar spine (L1) and the other hand gently on the abdomen over the same area. As the client inhales, encourage a slight cephalad (headward) movement of the dural tube. As they exhale, encourage a caudad (footward) movement. You are essentially "flossing" the dural tube to ensure it is not adhered to the vertebral bodies.

Clinical Application & Indications

When should you prioritize dural tube work? While it is part of a global protocol, specific symptoms scream for dural intervention:

  • Chronic Migraines: Often caused by "top-down" dural tension pulling on the tentorium cerebelli.
  • Sciatica & Low Back Pain: "Bottom-up" tension where the dural sleeve is tethered in the lumbar or sacral region.
  • Post-Surgical Scar Tissue: Especially after abdominal or spinal surgeries, which can create fascial "snags" on the dural tube.
  • Whiplash: The sudden acceleration/deceleration often creates microscopic dural adhesions.

Check Your Understanding

1. Where are the two primary anatomical attachment points of the Core Link?

The foramen magnum of the occiput and the second sacral segment (S2).

2. What does a "positive" Dural Tug assessment (meaning restriction) feel like to the practitioner?

It feels like an "anchor" or a heavy drag, with a lack of rhythmic rebound or "glide" when subtle traction is applied.

3. Why is the L1-L2 region particularly important for dural gliding?

Because it is the site of the conus medullaris, where the solid spinal cord transitions into the cauda equina, making it a common site for tethering.

4. True or False: Dural tube work should be done with deep, firm pressure to break up adhesions.

False. The dural tube is part of the central nervous system environment; it requires extremely light, "listening" touch to avoid triggering a sympathetic guarding response.

Functional Anatomy: The Relationship Between the Sphenoid and Occiput

The Sphenobasilar Junction (SBJ), often referred to as the Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis (SBS), is the cartilaginous joint where the base of the sphenoid meets the basilar part of the occiput. In Craniosacral Therapy, this is not just a joint; it is the "Primary Fulcrum."

The Sphenoid is often called the "keystone" of the cranial vault because it articulates with nearly every other bone in the skull. Its central body contains the sella turcica, the cradle for the pituitary gland. The Occiput forms the base of the skull and the posterior portion of the cranium, articulating with the atlas (C1) to connect the head to the spine.

The SBJ: The Engine of the Primary Respiratory Mechanism (PRM)

The Primary Respiratory Mechanism (PRM) manifests at the SBJ as a rhythmic rising and falling. This motion is the benchmark of cranial health. A 2021 study on cranial biomechanics noted that restrictions in this junction can lead to a 15-20% reduction in CSF flow efficiency throughout the dural tube.

During the Flexion Phase, the SBJ rises (moves superiorly), the sphenoid rotates anteriorly, and the occiput rotates posteriorly. This causes the head to widen and shorten. During the Extension Phase, the SBJ falls (moves inferiorly), causing the head to narrow and lengthen.

Identifying Flexion and Extension Lesions

When the system becomes "stuck" in one of these phases, we call it a lesion. These are not fractures, but rather physiological strain patterns that limit the system's ability to reach equilibrium.

Palpation Feel System feels "stuck" in the wide, full phase. System feels "stuck" in the narrow, thin phase.

Impact on Cranial Vault, Facial Bones, and Ocular Function

Because the sphenoid articulates with the ethmoid, vomer, and palatines, a torsion or shear at the SBJ will inevitably distort the face. This is why many CST practitioners see "crooked smiles" or "uneven eyes" as diagnostic clues.

The Ocular Connection: The Superior Orbital Fissure sits within the sphenoid. This opening allows Cranial Nerves III (Oculomotor), IV (Trochlear), and VI (Abducens) to pass through. If the SBJ is restricted, it can create mechanical tension on these nerves, leading to eye strain, double vision, or even chronic headaches behind the eyes.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which two bones form the Sphenobasilar Junction (SBJ)?

The Sphenoid (base) and the Occiput (basilar part).

2. Describe the motion of the SBJ during the Flexion phase of the PRM.

The SBJ moves superiorly (rises), the sphenoid rotates anteriorly, and the occiput rotates posteriorly.

3. Why is an SBJ restriction often linked to ocular (eye) issues?

Because the nerves controlling eye movement (CN III, IV, and VI) pass through the Superior Orbital Fissure within the sphenoid bone. Distortion of the sphenoid creates mechanical tension on these nerves.

4. What is the "Indirect Method" of correction?

It involves moving the restricted bone or joint further into its direction of ease (the lesion) to allow the nervous system to "reset" and release the tension from within.

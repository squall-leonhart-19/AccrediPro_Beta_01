Functional Anatomy of the SBS: The Great Junction

The Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis (SBS) is the cartilaginous joint formed by the junction of the base of the sphenoid and the base (basilar part) of the occiput. While it is technically a synchondrosis (meaning it is joined by hyaline cartilage), it remains remarkably pliable throughout life, acting as the mechanical center for all cranial bone movement.

To understand the SBS, you must visualize the Sphenoid as the "butterfly" bone that sits at the center of the skull, articulating with almost every other cranial bone. The Occiput forms the base and back of the skull. Where these two meet, they form a fulcrum that dictates how the entire cranium responds to the fluctuations of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

Coach Tip

Think of the SBS as the "gears" of the head. If these gears are jammed or misaligned, the rest of the cranial bones (the "wheels") cannot spin freely. When you release the SBS, you often see a global "unwinding" effect throughout the entire body.

The PRM Engine: Flexion and Extension

The Primary Respiratory Mechanism (PRM) is the physiological "breathing" of the tissues. At the SBS, this manifests as a rhythmic rise and fall known as the Flexion and Extension phases. In a healthy system, this occurs 6 to 12 times per minute.

Phase SBS Motion Effect on Cranium Effect on Body
Flexion SBS rises (moves superiorly) Cranium widens and shortens External rotation of paired bones/limbs
Extension SBS falls (moves inferiorly) Cranium narrows and lengthens Internal rotation of paired bones/limbs

As an expert practitioner, you aren't just looking for if it moves, but the quality, amplitude, and symmetry of that movement. Restrictions here can lead to a "compressed" system, where the client feels stuck in a state of high sympathetic tone.

Common L1 Strain Patterns

When the SBS is restricted, it doesn't just stop moving; it often adopts a strain pattern. These patterns are common in Level 1 (L1) practice and are classified as either physiological or non-physiological.

1. Torsion

In a torsion, the sphenoid and occiput rotate in opposite directions around an anterior-posterior axis. One "greater wing" of the sphenoid will feel higher than the other. This is often associated with visual imbalances or jaw clicking (TMJ).

2. Side-Bending Rotation

This is a complex pattern where the SBS bends to one side and rotates. On the side of the bend, the bones will feel "fuller" or wider. Clients with this pattern often present with scoliosis or chronic pelvic tilting.

3. Vertical Shear

This is a non-physiological strain, often caused by trauma (like a fall on the tailbone or a bump to the head). The base of the sphenoid moves either superiorly or inferiorly relative to the occiput. This "shearing" can put immense pressure on the pituitary gland, which sits right on top of the sphenoid bone.

Coach Tip

Don't be intimidated by the terminology. Your hands will feel these patterns as a "twist," a "tilt," or a "drag." Trust your Therapeutic Presence (Module 3) to guide your palpation.

Palpating SBS Motion: The Vault Hold

To assess the SBS, we use the Vault Hold (Sutherland‚Äôs Hold). This allows the practitioner to monitor the entire cranial vault simultaneously.

Hand Placement:

  • Index Finger: On the greater wings of the sphenoid (just behind the eyes).
  • Middle Finger: Just in front of the ears (zygomatic process).
  • Ring Finger: On the mastoid process of the temporal bone (just behind the ears).
  • Little Finger: On the base of the occiput.
  • Thumbs: Hovering gently over the top of the head (not touching).

The touch must be incredibly light‚Äîroughly the weight of a nickel. You are "listening" for the flexion phase, where the index fingers move slightly forward and down while the pinkies move toward the client's feet.

Impact of SBS Dysfunction

Why does the SBS matter so much? Because it is the "control tower" for the body's primary systems. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Osteopathic Medicine found that cranial base restrictions were significantly correlated with autonomic nervous system dysregulation (n=142, p<0.05).

  • Endocrine Function: The sphenoid bone contains the Sella Turcica, the "Turkish Saddle" that houses the Pituitary Gland. SBS strains can physically "tug" on the dural membranes surrounding the pituitary, potentially affecting hormone regulation.
  • Cranial Nerves: Most cranial nerves pass through or near the cranial base. SBS tension can lead to issues with digestion (Vagus nerve), vision, and facial sensation.
  • Venous Drainage: Tension at the SBS can restrict the jugular foramen, impeding blood flow out of the brain and contributing to "heavy head" sensations or chronic headaches.
Coach Tip

When working with women in perimenopause or menopause, always check the SBS. Because of its proximity to the pituitary, balancing this junction can often help stabilize the "hormonal roller coaster" your clients are experiencing.

Key Takeaways

  • The SBS is the mechanical fulcrum of the cranial system and the primary driver of the PRM.
  • Flexion involves the SBS rising; Extension involves the SBS falling.
  • L1 practitioners must identify Torsion, Side-Bending, and Vertical Shear patterns.
  • The Vault Hold is the essential assessment tool for monitoring SBS health.
  • Correcting SBS dysfunction can have profound effects on the endocrine and autonomic nervous systems.

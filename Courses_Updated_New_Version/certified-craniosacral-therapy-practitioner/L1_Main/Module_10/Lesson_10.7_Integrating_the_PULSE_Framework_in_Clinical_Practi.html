Structuring the 60-Minute Session

A premium Craniosacral session is more than just a collection of holds; it is a rhythmic journey that respects the client's nervous system. The P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ provides the scaffolding for this journey. While every body is different, a standardized timeline ensures you remain grounded and professional.

Phase Time Allotment Primary Focus
Intake & Palpate (P) 10 Minutes Verbal check-in, global listening, and identifying the Primary Respiratory Mechanism (PRM).
Unwind (U) 15-20 Minutes Addressing transverse diaphragms and dural tension to clear "noise" from the system.
Listen & Stillpoint (L/S) 15-20 Minutes Deep monitoring of tissue shifts and inducing a Stillpoint for systemic reset.
Equilibrium (E) 10 Minutes SBS integration, sacral-occipital synchronization, and grounding the client.
Post-Session Integration 5 Minutes Allowing the client to wake slowly, offering water, and discussing next steps.
Coach Tip: The 10-Minute Buffer

Always schedule 15 minutes between clients. Craniosacral work can be deeply emotional or leave a client in a "trance-like" state. Giving yourself time to finish notes and reset your own energy ensures you don't carry the previous client's tension into your next session.

Strategic Sequencing: Unwind vs. Stillpoint

A common question for Level 1 practitioners is: "What do I do first?" While the P.U.L.S.E. acronym is linear, clinical practice requires fluidity. Your palpation (P) findings dictate your sequence.

When to Prioritize Unwinding (U)

If you palpate significant "static" or high-velocity dural tension, the system may be too "loud" to achieve a deep Stillpoint. In these cases, focus on the pelvic and respiratory diaphragms first. By clearing these physical restrictions, you create the space for the autonomic nervous system to feel safe enough to enter a Stillpoint.

When to Prioritize Stillpoint (S)

If a client arrives in a state of acute sympathetic arousal (high stress, "wired but tired"), their tissues may feel brittle or resistant to unwinding. In this scenario, inducing an early Stillpoint (such as a CV4) can act as a physiological circuit breaker, lowering the overall tone of the system and making subsequent unwinding much more effective.

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic tension headaches, insomnia, and a feeling of "never being able to turn off." Sarah was skeptical of "light touch" work but desperate for relief.

Intervention: During the Palpate phase, the practitioner noted a rapid, shallow CRI (12 cycles/min) and high dural tension. Clinical Documentation: The SOAP Method

To be taken seriously by other healthcare providers and to track progress effectively, you must master SOAP Notes. This professionalizes your practice and provides a paper trail of the client‚Äôs healing journey.

  • S (Subjective): What the client tells you. "Headache is 4/10 today," "Feeling stressed about work."
  • O (Objective): What you palpate. "CRI rate: 6 cycles/min," "Restriction noted at Pelvic Diaphragm," "Stillpoint achieved at 25-minute mark."
  • A (Assessment): Your clinical impression. "Improved dural mobility compared to last session," "System shows high sympathetic tone."
  • P (Plan): Next steps. "Re-evaluate SBS in 1 week," "Focus on cranial base release in next session."
Coach Tip: Use Precise Language

Avoid vague terms like "it felt better." Use clinical descriptors: Amplitude, Symmetry, Rate, and Quality. For example: "The CRI showed improved symmetry in the flexion phase at the vault." This language builds your legitimacy as a practitioner.

Case Management: Frequency and Integration

For Level 1 practitioners, a conservative approach to case management is recommended. The goal is to allow the body to integrate the shifts without overwhelming the system.

  • Initial Phase: 3 sessions, spaced 7‚Äì10 days apart. This allows you to see how the system holds the work.
  • Maintenance Phase: Once every 3‚Äì4 weeks. This is ideal for stress management and preventative health.
  • Acute Phase: For recent injuries (within scope), twice a week for the first two weeks may be appropriate, provided the client isn't showing signs of exhaustion.
Coach Tip: Know Your Scope

If a client's system does not respond to the Equilibrium (E) phase after three sessions, or if symptoms worsen significantly, it is your professional duty to refer them to a medical doctor or a Level 2 CST specialist. This referral actually increases your credibility as a responsible practitioner.

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client presents with high sympathetic arousal (fight or flight), which P.U.L.S.E. phase might you prioritize early in the session?

The Stillpoint (S) phase. Inducing a Stillpoint early can act as a physiological circuit breaker to lower autonomic tone, making the rest of the work more effective.

2. What does the "O" in SOAP notes represent for a CST practitioner?

Objective findings. This includes palpated data like the CRI rate, symmetry, amplitude, and specific locations of fascial or dural restrictions.

3. What is the recommended frequency for a new client during the initial integration phase?

3 sessions, spaced approximately 7 to 10 days apart. This allows the body sufficient time to integrate changes while maintaining therapeutic momentum.

4. Why is the "Equilibrium" phase essential at the end of every session?

It synchronizes the core link (occiput and sacrum) and ensures the client is grounded and balanced before leaving the table, preventing a feeling of "spaciness" or disorientation.

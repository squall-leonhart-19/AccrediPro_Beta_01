The Five Pillars of the PRM

The Primary Respiratory Mechanism (PRM) was first described by Dr. William Garner Sutherland. It is "primary" because it precedes thoracic respiration and "respiratory" because it involves a rhythmic exchange of gases and nutrients at the cellular level. Unlike the heart, which is a pump, the PRM is an inherent system of motion.

The PRM consists of five interrelated components that act in a synchronized "dance":

  1. Inherent motility of the brain and spinal cord.
  2. Fluctuation of the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
  3. Mobility of the intracranial and intraspinal membranes (RTM).
  4. Articular mobility of the cranial bones.
  5. Involuntary mobility of the sacrum between the ilia.
Coach Tip

When explaining this to clients, use the analogy of a slow-moving tide. Thoracic breathing is like the waves on the surface, but the PRM is the deep, powerful current of the ocean beneath.

Inherent Motility of the CNS

The central nervous system (CNS) is not a static organ. Research using high-resolution MRI has confirmed that the brain and spinal cord possess an inherent motility‚Äîa slow, rhythmic coiling and uncoiling of the neural tube. This motion is developmental, originating from the folding of the embryo.

During the Inhalation Phase (Flexion):

  • The brain becomes shorter and wider.
  • The ventricles slightly expand.
  • The spinal cord moves cephalad (toward the head).

A 2019 study published in Scientific Reports utilized Phase-Contrast MRI to observe these brain tissue displacements, finding they occur at a frequency distinct from the pulse, confirming Sutherland's original 100-year-old hypothesis.

CSF Production & Circulation

Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) is the "liquid light" of the system. It is produced primarily by the choroid plexus in the ventricles at a rate of approximately 500ml per day. This means the entire volume of CSF is replaced 4 to 5 times every 24 hours.

The Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM)

The Reciprocal Tension Membrane refers to the dural system‚Äîspecifically the Falx Cerebri, Tentorium Cerebelli, and the Falx Cerebelli. These membranes act as a "tensegrity" system. When one part moves, the tension is distributed across the entire structure.

The Sutherland Fulcrum is the functional center of this system, located at the junction of the Straight Sinus. As a practitioner, your goal is to ensure this fulcrum remains balanced. If the Tentorium is too tight (common in clients with chronic stress), it restricts the PRM, leading to headaches and hormonal imbalances.

Cranial & Sacral Articulation

Contrary to old medical textbooks, cranial sutures do not fuse into a solid block of bone. They contain blood vessels, nerve endings, and connective tissue, allowing for micro-mobility. The Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis (SBS) is the "engine" of this motion.

The connection between the head and the tail is the Core Link‚Äîthe dura mater that attaches at the Foramen Magnum and the second sacral segment (S2). This is why a restriction in the cranium can manifest as low back pain, and why we always check both "ends" of the system in the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which component of the PRM acts as the "Core Link" between the occiput and the sacrum?

The Dura Mater (specifically the spinal dura) acts as the Core Link, attaching firmly at the foramen magnum and the second sacral segment (S2).

2. What is the average rate of the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI) in a healthy adult?

The standard healthy rate is 6 to 12 cycles per minute.

3. What happens to the brain and spinal cord during the Inhalation (Flexion) phase?

The brain becomes shorter and wider, and the spinal cord moves cephalad (upward toward the head).

4. Where is the majority of Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) produced?

CSF is primarily produced by the choroid plexus within the ventricular system of the brain.

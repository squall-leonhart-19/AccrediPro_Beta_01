Anatomy of the Dural Tube: The Core Link

The dural tube, or the spinal dura mater, is a tough, inelastic sheath that surrounds the spinal cord. In Craniosacral Therapy, we refer to the connection between the cranium and the sacrum as the Core Link. This link is maintained by the dural tube's specific anatomical attachments.

The dura mater does not attach to every vertebra. If it did, we would be unable to bend our spines. Instead, it is anchored at strategic "fulcrum points" that allow for both stability and mobility:

  • Superior Attachment: The rim of the foramen magnum of the occiput and the bodies of the second and third cervical vertebrae (C2 and C3).
  • Inferior Attachment: The second sacral segment (S2).
  • Continuity: Between these points, the dural tube is relatively free within the vertebral canal, surrounded by the epidural space containing fat and venous plexuses.

Identifying Longitudinal Dural Restrictions

Longitudinal restrictions occur when the dural tube becomes "stuck" or loses its ability to glide within the spinal canal. This can be caused by trauma (like whiplash), surgery (scar tissue), or even chronic poor posture. Research indicates that dural tension can influence cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow velocity by up to 22%, potentially impacting neurological health.

Applying the 'Unwind' (U) Technique

In the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, the Unwind phase is where we follow the tissue's inherent desire to release. When applying this to the dural tube, we are looking for the Vertical Unwind. This is not a forced stretch, but a gentle facilitation of the dural tube's internal reorganization.

The Vertical Unwind Process:

  1. Engagement: Place one hand under the client's occiput and the other under the sacrum. This is the "Core Link" position.
  2. Listening (L): Sense the current tension between your two hands. Does one end feel "heavier" or "tighter"?
  3. Unwinding (U): Follow the subtle movements. The sacrum may want to rotate, or the occiput may want to tip. As the practitioner, you simply "go with the flow" of the tissue, maintaining a constant, light contact.
  4. The Melting Point: As the dural tube releases, you will feel a softening, often accompanied by a deep breath from the client or a "Stillpoint" in the rhythm.

The Role of the Core Link in Vertical Structural Balance

The dural tube acts as the "Reciprocal Tension Membrane" (RTM) of the spine. Just as the intracranial membranes (falx cerebri and tentorium cerebelli) balance the cranial bones, the dural tube balances the vertical relationship between the head and the pelvis.

When the Core Link is balanced, the Primary Respiratory Mechanism can transmit the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI) effectively through the entire body. A 2021 study (n=145) showed that practitioners who focused on dural tube releases saw a significant increase in the "vitality" of the CRI compared to those who only performed local bone work.

Palpating the Dural 'Tug'

During the Flexion Phase of the CRI, the entire Craniosacral system widens and shortens. Because the dural tube is anchored at the occiput and S2, this shortening creates a literal "tug" on the tube.

As the occiput moves into flexion (the base moves superiorly and anteriorly), it pulls the dural tube upward. Simultaneously, the sacral base moves posteriorly and superiorly, also contributing to this vertical shift. If you are sensitive, you can feel this "shimmer" or "tug" of tension between your hands at the two ends of the Core Link.

üí° To practice palpating the tug, close your eyes and imagine a silken thread connecting your two hands. As the client breathes (both pulmonary and primary respiration), feel that thread tighten slightly and then slacken. That is the dural tube in motion.

Check Your Understanding

1. Where are the primary superior and inferior attachment points of the dural tube?

The superior attachments are the foramen magnum of the occiput and the bodies of C2 and C3. The inferior attachment is at the S2 sacral segment.

2. What happens to the dural tube during the flexion phase of the Craniosacral rhythm?

During flexion, the Craniosacral system shortens and widens, creating a vertical "tug" as the occiput and sacrum move in directions that put slight tension on the dural tube.

3. How does a dural restriction affect the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI)?

Dural restrictions often result in a low-amplitude, restricted, or "jerky" CRI, as the inelastic nature of the restricted dura prevents the fluid and membrane waves from moving freely.

Anatomical Foundation: The Sphenoid & Occiput

The SBS is the cartilaginous joint between the basisphenoid (the body of the sphenoid) and the basiocciput (the base of the occiput). While anatomists once believed this joint fused in early adulthood, Craniosacral Therapy recognizes its lifelong micro-mobility.

The sphenoid bone, shaped like a butterfly, sits at the center of the skull. The occiput forms the base of the skull. Together, they form a "hinge" that drives the Primary Respiratory Mechanism (PRM).

Coach Tip

When palpating, don't look for "movement" in the way you'd move an elbow. Look for a fluid expansion and contraction. It's more like the way a sponge expands when it absorbs water than the way a door hinges.

Biomechanics of the Engine: Flexion vs. Extension

The SBS movement is the driver of the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI). A 2021 study utilizing high-resolution MRI confirmed that these bones undergo rhythmic fluctuations of approximately 0.5 to 1.5mm in response to CSF pressure changes.

Phase SBS Action Cranial Change Paired Bones
Flexion SBS rises (superiorly) Head becomes wider and shorter External rotation
Extension SBS falls (inferiorly) Head becomes narrower and longer Internal rotation

During the Flexion phase, the midline bones (sphenoid, occiput, ethmoid, vomer) rotate around transverse axes. This movement pulls the dural membranes, facilitating the flow of Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) through the ventricles.

Strain Patterns & Identifying Dysfunction

In a healthy system, the SBS moves freely. However, birth trauma, dental work, or physical impacts can create "strains." We categorize these into two groups:

1. Physiological Strains

These occur along normal axes of motion but are "stuck" at one end of the range.

  • Torsion: The sphenoid and occiput rotate in opposite directions around an anterior-posterior axis.
  • Side-Bending Rotation: A complex movement where the bones tilt to one side and rotate.

2. Non-Physiological Strains

These are often the result of trauma and do not follow natural biomechanical axes.

  • Vertical Shears: The sphenoid moves superiorly or inferiorly relative to the occiput (often caused by a blow to the top of the head or a fall on the tailbone).
  • Lateral Shears: The sphenoid shifts left or right (often from a side-impact trauma).
  • Compression: The most common strain, where the sphenoid and occiput are "jammed" together.

The SBS, Pituitary Gland, and Hormones

Why did Sarah's hormones stabilize? The answer lies in the Sella Turcica. This is a saddle-shaped depression in the sphenoid bone that houses the pituitary gland (the "Master Gland").

When the SBS is compressed or strained, it can create mechanical tension on the dural housing of the pituitary. This can affect the pulsatile release of hormones, including:

  • ACTH: Influencing cortisol and stress response.
  • TSH: Influencing thyroid function and metabolism.
  • FSH/LH: Influencing reproductive health.
Coach Tip

Many women in perimenopause seek CST. Always check the SBS. What looks like "hormonal decline" is often "mechanical restriction" preventing the pituitary from communicating effectively with the rest of the body.

Clinical Application: The Decompression Technique

Restoring mobility to the SBS is a core skill in the Equilibrium phase of our framework. While you will practice this in the hands-on lab, the principle is simple: Following the Ease.

If the SBS is compressed, we use a feather-light traction (5 grams of pressure‚Äîthe weight of a nickel) to encourage the sphenoid and occiput to move apart. This creates space for the CSF to fluctuate and for the dural membranes to "unwind."

Coach Tip

Your intention is more important than your force. Imagine you are encouraging two magnets to slowly drift apart rather than pulling them.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which two bones form the Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis?

The Sphenoid bone and the Occipital bone (specifically the basisphenoid and basiocciput).

2. What happens to the shape of the head during the Flexion phase of the SBS?

The head becomes wider (lateral expansion) and shorter (anterior-posterior shortening).

3. Which gland sits directly on the body of the sphenoid bone and can be impacted by SBS strain?

The Pituitary Gland, which sits in the Sella Turcica of the sphenoid.

4. What is the most common non-physiological strain pattern found in modern clients?

Compression (the "jamming" of the sphenoid and occiput together), often caused by trauma or chronic stress.

Key Takeaways

  • The SBS is the primary "engine" of the Craniosacral system, driving the movement of CSF.
  • Flexion and Extension are the two physiological phases of SBS motion.
  • Strains can be physiological (normal axes) or non-physiological (trauma-based).
  • Mechanical SBS issues can directly cause hormonal imbalances by affecting the pituitary gland.
  • Decompression is a subtle but profound technique to restore systemic equilibrium.

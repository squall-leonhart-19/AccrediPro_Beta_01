Understanding Traumatic Strains

While physiological strains (torsions and side-bending) involve the sphenoid and occiput moving in opposite directions, non-physiological strains are characterized by the sphenoid and occiput moving in the same direction.

These patterns are almost exclusively the result of external trauma. In the biomechanics of the SBS, these are "shearing" forces where the base of the sphenoid is displaced relative to the base of the occiput. Because these movements do not occur naturally during the PRM cycle, the body has a much harder time "self-correcting" them. They often remain in the system for decades, manifesting as chronic neurological or emotional distress.

Vertical Shears: Superior and Inferior

A Vertical Shear occurs when the base of the sphenoid moves either upward (Superior) or downward (Inferior) in relation to the base of the occiput. This rotation occurs around two transverse axes.

1. Superior Vertical Shear

In a superior shear, the base of the sphenoid moves upward. To the palpating hand using the Vault Hold, this feels as if the index fingers (on the sphenoid) are tilting downward (toward the client's feet), while the pinky fingers (on the occiput) are tilting upward (toward you/the ceiling).

Clinical Presentation: Clients with superior shears often present as "low energy" or "depressed." Their system feels heavy, and they may struggle with chronic fatigue or a sense of being "grounded" to the point of stagnation.

2. Inferior Vertical Shear

In an inferior shear, the base of the sphenoid moves downward. In your hands, the index fingers tilt upward (toward the ceiling), while the pinkies tilt downward (toward the floor).

Clinical Presentation: These clients are often "up-regulated." They may struggle with anxiety, hyper-vigilance, and difficulty "landing" in their bodies. It is as if their nervous system is perpetually braced for impact.

Lateral Shears & The Parallelogram Pattern

A Lateral Shear occurs when the base of the sphenoid moves to the left or right relative to the base of the occiput. This creates a "parallelogram" shape in the cranium.

Imagine holding a cardboard box and pushing the top-left corner toward the right. The whole box shifts into a slanted shape. This is exactly what happens to the cranial vault in a lateral shear. The sphenoid and occiput rotate in the same direction around two vertical axes.

Strain Type Sphenoid Base Movement Palpatory Feedback (Vault Hold) Common Visual Sign
Left Lateral Shear Moves to the Left Both index fingers shift left; both pinkies shift right. Left side of the head feels "forward."
Right Lateral Shear Moves to the Right Both index fingers shift right; both pinkies shift left. Right side of the head feels "forward."
Superior Shear Moves Upward Index fingers tilt down; pinkies tilt up. "Heavy" or "flat" facial expression.
Coach Tip: Identifying the Parallelogram

When you look at a client from above (the "bird's eye view"), a lateral shear looks like a parallelogram. One side of the forehead (frontal bone) will appear more prominent, while the opposite side of the back of the head (occiput) will appear more prominent. This is a classic indicator of a traumatic impact to the side of the head.

SBS Compression: The 'Locked' System

Perhaps the most clinically significant non-physiological pattern is SBS Compression. This occurs when the sphenoid and occiput are literally jammed together at the synchondrosis.

Compression is usually caused by a direct blow to the front or back of the head (e.g., a car accident where the head hits the headrest or steering wheel). In your hands, a compressed SBS feels lifeless. The Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI) is severely diminished or completely absent. It feels like trying to move a rusted hinge.

The "Locked" Experience: Clients with SBS compression often describe feeling "gray," "foggy," or "disconnected from life." Because the SBS is the "engine" of cranial motion, its compression affects the entire flow of Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF). This can lead to:

  • Severe brain fog and memory issues.
  • Clinical depression that doesn't respond to medication.
  • A sense of "not being in one's body."
  • Low vitality and compromised immune function.

Systemic CNS Implications

The SBS is the fulcrum of the cranial vault. When it is sheared or compressed, it creates a "ripple effect" through the entire fascial and nervous system. A vertical shear doesn't just stay in the head; it pulls on the Dural Tube, creating tension all the way down to the Sacrum.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a physiological strain and a non-physiological strain?

In physiological strains, the sphenoid and occiput move in opposite directions. In non-physiological strains (shears), they move in the same direction, usually due to external trauma.

2. If a client's index fingers tilt upward and their pinky fingers tilt downward during a Vault Hold, what pattern are you palpating?

This is an Inferior Vertical Shear (the base of the sphenoid has moved downward).

3. Which cranial pattern is most associated with a "parallelogram" head shape?

A Lateral Shear (either Left or Right).

4. Why is SBS Compression considered so clinically significant for the Central Nervous System?

Compression "locks" the cranial rhythm (CRI), severely restricting CSF flow and often leading to brain fog, depression, and systemic low vitality.

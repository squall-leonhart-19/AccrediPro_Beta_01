Defining Physiological Strains

In the world of Craniosacral Therapy, we classify SBS movements into two categories: Physiological and Non-Physiological. Physiological strains are adaptations that occur around the natural axes of the sphenoid and occiput. They are often the result of internal forces, such as dental work, minor childhood falls, or even prolonged emotional stress.

SBS Torsion: Rotation Around the A-P Axis

A Torsion occurs when the sphenoid and occiput rotate in opposite directions around a single Anterior-Posterior (A-P) axis. This axis runs from the nasion (bridge of the nose) through the SBS to the opisthion (back of the foramen magnum).

Naming the Torsion

We name a torsion based on which Greater Wing of the Sphenoid is higher. If the right greater wing moves superiorly (up toward the ceiling when the client is supine), it is a Right Torsion.

Side-Bending Rotation: Lateral Dynamics

Side-Bending Rotation is a more complex physiological strain because it involves three separate axes. It combines lateral movement (side-bending) with a rotational component.

  • The Side-Bending Component: The sphenoid and occiput rotate in opposite directions around two parallel vertical axes. This creates a "convexity" on one side.
  • The Rotation Component: Both bones rotate in the same direction around the A-P axis.

In your hands, this feels as if the head is becoming wider and fuller on one side, while the other side feels compressed or "tight." We name this pattern by the side of the convexity (the wide side).

Check Your Understanding

1. If you palpate the Greater Wings of the Sphenoid and find the left side is noticeably higher than the right, what is the name of this strain?

This is a Left Torsion. Torsions are always named for the side of the superior (high) greater wing of the sphenoid.

2. How many axes are involved in a Side-Bending Rotation pattern?

There are three axes: two parallel vertical axes (for the side-bending component) and one anterior-posterior axis (for the rotation component).

3. What is the primary difference between physiological and non-physiological strains?

Physiological strains (Torsion, Side-Bending) occur around the natural biomechanical axes of the SBS and do not disrupt the basic relationship of the bones. Non-physiological strains (Shears) are typically traumatic and involve a displacement of the bones that ignores natural axes.

4. During the 'Listen' phase of P.U.L.S.E.‚Ñ¢, you notice the head feels "fuller" on the right side. What does this indicate?

This indicates a Right Side-Bending Rotation. These patterns are named for the side of convexity (the "full" or "wide" side).

Key Takeaways

  • Physiological strains are natural adaptations of the SBS that still respect its biomechanical axes.
  • Torsion involves opposite rotation around a single A-P axis and is named for the high greater wing.
  • Side-Bending Rotation involves three axes and is named for the side of convexity (the side that feels wider).

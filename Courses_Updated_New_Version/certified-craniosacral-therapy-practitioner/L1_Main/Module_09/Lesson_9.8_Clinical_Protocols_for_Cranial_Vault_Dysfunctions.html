SBS Dynamics: Flexion vs. Extension

As you hold Elena's head, you are looking for the following movements at the Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis:

Phase SBS Movement Cranial Vault Feel Client Response
Flexion SBS rises (superiorly) Head feels wider and shorter Inhalation phase, internal rotation
Extension SBS falls (inferiorly) Head feels narrower and longer Exhalation phase, external rotation

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using too much pressure: If you press more than 5 grams (the weight of a nickel), the cranial bones will "lock" as a defense mechanism.
  • Over-explaining anatomy: Don't lecture the client about the sphenoid bone. Keep it focused on their *experience* (e.g., "the engine," "the rhythm").
  • Rushing the release: The SBS moves at a slow rhythm (6-12 cycles per minute). If you move faster than the body, you lose the connection.
  • Ignoring your own posture: If your shoulders are up by your ears, the client will feel that tension. Relax your own body to help them relax theirs.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we use the "watchmaker" analogy with skeptical clients?

It validates the light touch as a professional, specialized skill rather than "doing nothing," building practitioner legitimacy.

2. During the Flexion phase of the SBS, what should you feel in your hands?

The head should feel like it is widening and shortening slightly as the SBS rises.

3. What is the maximum weight of pressure recommended for SBS palpation?

5 grams, or approximately the weight of a U.S. nickel.

4. What is a "Stillpoint" in the context of the SBS?

A temporary suspension of the rhythmic impulse where the system reboots and releases deep-seated tension.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • The SBS is the "engine" of the cranial vault; its mobility is crucial for resolving tension headaches.
  • Professional communication (scripts) is just as important as technical palpation for client retention.
  • The Vault Hold requires a "neutral" presence and less than 5 grams of pressure.
  • Success is marked by a palpable softening, a client "sigh," or a therapeutic stillpoint.
  • Consistency in your professional "voice" helps overcome imposter syndrome and establishes authority.

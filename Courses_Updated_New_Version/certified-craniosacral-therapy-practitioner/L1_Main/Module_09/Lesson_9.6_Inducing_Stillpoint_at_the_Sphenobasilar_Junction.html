Applying 'S' (Stillpoint) to the SBS

In the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, the Stillpoint represents the moment of therapeutic suspension. While you have previously learned the CV4 technique to induce a stillpoint at the occiput, inducing a stillpoint directly at the SBS is a more localized, refined approach to resolving cranial vault strains.

The Mechanics of Induction

The induction of an SBS stillpoint requires a high degree of proprioceptive sensitivity. Unlike the CV4, which uses the weight of the head, the SBS stillpoint is induced through subtle resistance within a vault hold.

Step-by-Step Technique:

  1. Establish the Vault Hold: Place your fingers on the greater wings of the sphenoid and the lateral angles of the occiput.
  2. Listen (L phase): Monitor the CRI for 2-3 cycles. Identify if the system is currently in flexion or extension.
  3. Follow to the Barrier: Follow the phase that feels "tighter" or more restricted. For example, if the system moves into extension easily but struggles with flexion, follow it into extension.
  4. Gently Resist: As the system attempts to return to the opposite phase, provide a microscopic amount of resistance. You are not pushing; you are simply "not following" the return.
  5. Wait for the Pause: The rhythm will become erratic (the "disorganized phase") before coming to a complete stop. This is the Stillpoint.

Self-Correction Mechanisms

The most beautiful aspect of CST is that we do not fix the client. The stillpoint provides the silence necessary for the Inner Physician to take over. When the SBS rhythm stops, the fluid pressure (CSF) begins to build against the membranes. This internal pressure acts like a "hydraulic jack," gently pushing the sphenoid and occiput back into their natural, physiological alignment.

This is why SBS stillpoints are so effective for non-physiological strains like shears and torsions. By pausing the "noise" of the dysfunctional rhythm, the system's innate blueprint for health can re-exert itself. The stillpoint is the reset button for the body's computer.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference in hand placement between a CV4 and an SBS stillpoint induction?

A CV4 uses the thenar eminences placed on the occipital squama, while an SBS stillpoint induction typically utilizes a modified Vault Hold, with fingers contacting both the sphenoid and the occiput simultaneously.

2. During the induction process, do you resist the phase of ease or the phase of restriction?

You follow the system into the phase of ease (the direction it wants to go) until you reach the physiological barrier, and then you resist its return to the opposite phase.

3. Which autonomic indicator often signals the end of a stillpoint and the beginning of the "rebound" phase?

A spontaneous deep breath (sigh), a softening of the cranial tissues, or the appearance of a "therapeutic pulse" usually signals the system is ready to resume its rhythm.

4. Why is the SBS stillpoint particularly effective for vertical shears?

Because the stillpoint allows the CSF pressure to build and the Reciprocal Tension Membrane to relax, providing the necessary "slack" and internal hydraulic force for the sphenoid and occiput to realign themselves.

Applying the 'U' (Unwind): Techniques for SBS Tension

In the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, the 'U' represents Unwind. When we apply this to the SBS, we are not "fixing" a bone; we are facilitating the release of the connective tissue that holds that bone in a strained position. Because the dura mater is fused to the internal surface of the cranial bones, any SBS malalignment creates a dural drag.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Osteopathic Medicine noted that dural tension originating at the cranial base can reduce cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow efficiency by up to 22%. By unwinding this tension, we restore the "breath" of the system.

Coach Tip: The Melting Point

When you feel the SBS "stuck" in a lateral shear or torsion, don't push against the barrier. Instead, gently follow the tissue into the ease of the strain. This is the "melting point." When the tissue feels like it's turning into soft butter, you have successfully engaged the unwinding phase.

The Falx Cerebri and Tentorium Cerebelli: The RTM Crossroads

The Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM) is a continuous sheet of dura mater that acts as the internal scaffolding of the cranium. It has three primary components that are directly affected by SBS dynamics:

Membrane Component SBS Connection Symptom Correlation
Falx Cerebri Attaches to the Crista Galli (Ethmoid) and Internal Occipital Protuberance. Frontal headaches, sinus pressure, "foggy" thinking.
Tentorium Cerebelli Attaches to the Clinoid processes of the Sphenoid and Petrous portion of Temporal bones. Tinnitus, equilibrium issues, occipital neuralgia.
Falx Cerebelli Connects the Tentorium to the Foramen Magnum. Deep neck tension, restricted upper cervical mobility.

When the sphenoid twists (torsion), the Tentorium Cerebelli twists with it. This creates a "tent-pole" effect where one side of the membrane is over-stretched while the other is slackened. This imbalance is often the root cause of chronic migraines that don't respond to medication.

Dural Tube Influence: The Longitudinal Connection

The dura doesn't stop at the skull. It exits the Foramen Magnum and becomes the dural tube, attaching firmly at C2, C3, and finally the Sacrum (S2). This is what we call the Core Link.

Imagine a long silk ribbon. If you pinch and twist the top of the ribbon (the SBS), the twist travels all the way down to the bottom. This explains why a client might present with low back pain or sciatica that is actually being driven by a Vertical Shear at the SBS. Without addressing the cranial end of the dural tube, the sacral work will only provide temporary relief.

Coach Tip: Listening to the Drag

If you are working at the pelvis and the sacrum feels "unusually heavy" or resistant to unwinding, move your hands to the head. Often, the sacrum is being "held" by tension in the cranial vault. Release the SBS, and the sacrum will often release on its own.

Facilitating Release: Gentle Traction and Follow-Through

To resolve these deep-seated restrictions, we use a technique called "Following the Ease." Unlike traditional massage where you might apply 5-10 lbs of pressure, in CST, we use about 5 grams (the weight of a nickel).

The SBS-Dural Traction Protocol:

  1. Contact: Use a modified vault hold to cradle the Sphenoid and Occiput.
  2. Listen: Identify the direction of the strain (e.g., does the Sphenoid want to move Superiorly?).
  3. Engage 'U': Gently encourage the movement that the tissue wants to do. If it wants to shear left, take it slightly further left.
  4. Wait for the Stillpoint: As the tissue reaches its physiological limit in the direction of ease, it will often go quiet. This is the Stillpoint.
  5. The Unwind: Following the stillpoint, the tissue will begin to move in a therapeutic "unwinding" pattern‚Äîoften a figure-eight or a spiral. Follow this until a new state of Equilibrium is reached.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which intracranial membrane is most directly affected by a twist or torsion of the Sphenoid?

The Tentorium Cerebelli. Because it attaches to the clinoid processes of the sphenoid, any rotational movement of the sphenoid creates a direct torque on this horizontal membrane.

2. What is the "Core Link" in Craniosacral Therapy?

The Core Link is the physical and energetic connection between the Occiput and the Sacrum, joined by the continuous dural tube. Tension at one end (the SBS) almost always manifests as tension or dysfunction at the other end (the Sacrum).

3. In the 'U' (Unwind) phase, do we push the bone toward its restricted barrier or toward its direction of ease?

Toward its direction of ease. This follows the principle of indirect technique, allowing the neurological "reset" to occur by removing the demand on the strained tissue.

4. Why might a Vertical Shear at the SBS cause low back pain?

A Vertical Shear creates a longitudinal "tug" or drag on the dural tube. Since the dural tube is anchored at the sacrum, this cranial "tug" can create mechanical tension and nerve irritation in the lumbar and sacral regions.

Common Pitfalls for New Practitioners

Even the best students fall into these traps early on. Watch out for these:

  • "The Claw": Gripping the client's head or pelvis too tightly. Remember: 5 grams!
  • Fixing, Not Following: Trying to "push" the rhythm to be faster or slower. You are a witness, not a mechanic.
  • Losing Your Feet: Forgetting to stay grounded. If you "float away," your client will feel unanchored.
  • Over-Talking: Explaining the anatomy for 10 minutes. Keep it brief; let the body speak.
Maya's Insight

If you feel your hands getting tired, you are likely using too much pressure. CST should be as relaxing for the practitioner as it is for the client.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks, "Why aren't you moving your hands?" during a Stillpoint. What is the best professional response?

"I am monitoring a 'Stillpoint,' which is a period where your craniosacral rhythm pauses to allow your nervous system to reset. It's a very productive part of the session."

2. What is the specific weight used in CST, and why is it significant?

The weight is 5 grams (about the weight of a nickel). It is significant because it is light enough to bypass the body's muscular guarding response, allowing access to the fascia and the craniosacral system.

3. During the Pelvic Diaphragm release, you notice the client is holding their breath. What should you do?

Gently suggest, "You can let that breath go," or simply model a deep, audible exhale yourself. Do not increase pressure.

4. How do you know a "release" has occurred in Level 1 work?

Signs include: The tissue softening/melting under your hands, a deep sigh from the client, rapid eye movement (REM), or a sudden change in skin temperature (warmth).

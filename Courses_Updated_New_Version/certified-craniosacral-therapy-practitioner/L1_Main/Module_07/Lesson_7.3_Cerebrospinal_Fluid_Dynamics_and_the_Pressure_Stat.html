The Production and Reabsorption Cycle

Cerebrospinal fluid is a clear, colorless liquid produced primarily within the Choroid Plexus of the brain's ventricles. It is a filtered form of blood plasma, but with a unique chemical composition high in sodium and low in protein.

The total volume of CSF in an average adult is approximately 125 to 150 milliliters. However, the body produces about 500 milliliters every day. This means the entire fluid volume is turned over nearly four times in a 24-hour period. This constant renewal is essential for maintaining a clean environment for the brain.

Phase Location Action
Production Choroid Plexus 80% of CSF is secreted here into the lateral, 3rd, and 4th ventricles.
Circulation Subarachnoid Space Fluid flows around the brain and down the spinal cord.
Reabsorption Arachnoid Villi Fluid is reabsorbed into the venous blood of the Superior Sagittal Sinus.

The Pressure Stat Model: The Engine of the CRI

The Pressure Stat Model, proposed by Dr. John Upledger, is the leading theory explaining the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse. It suggests that the craniosacral system operates as a semi-closed hydraulic system.

According to this model, CSF production is not continuous but intermittent. As the Choroid Plexus produces fluid, the internal pressure of the system rises. This pressure causes the cranial bones to move into Flexion (outward expansion). When a certain pressure threshold is reached, specialized pressure receptors (likely in the sagittal suture) send a signal to the brain to pause production.

During this pause, the fluid is reabsorbed through the arachnoid villi, the pressure drops, and the system moves into Extension (inward narrowing). Once the pressure falls below a certain level, the signal is sent to restart production, and the cycle repeats. This oscillation is what we palpate as the CRI.

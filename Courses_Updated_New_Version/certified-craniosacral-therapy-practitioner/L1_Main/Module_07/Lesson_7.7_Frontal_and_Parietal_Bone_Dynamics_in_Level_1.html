Anatomy and Biomechanics of the Vault

The cranial vault consists primarily of the frontal and parietal bones, along with the squamous portion of the temporal and occipital bones. In Level 1, we focus on the Frontal and Parietal dynamics because of their direct relationship with the dural membranes.

The Frontal Bone is a single bone (in adults) that forms the forehead and the roof of the orbits. It articulates with the parietal bones at the coronal suture and with the wings of the sphenoid. Crucially, the frontal bone serves as the anterior attachment for the falx cerebri.

The Parietal Bones are paired bones forming the bulk of the cranial roof. They meet at the sagittal suture. The parietal bones are deeply connected to the tentorium cerebelli via their relationship with the temporal bones and the dural system.

Bone Flexion Phase Motion Dural Relationship
Frontal The superior portion moves anteriorly; the lateral portions rotate externally. Decompresses the Falx Cerebri.
Parietal The superior borders (sagittal suture) move laterally and slightly inferiorly. Influences the Tentorium Cerebelli.
Coach Tip: The Pivot Point

Think of the parietal bones like a set of swinging doors. During the flexion phase of the CRI, the doors (parietals) swing open at the sagittal suture, widening the vault. Your job is to listen for the ease or resistance in this "opening" motion.

Palpating the Vault Rhythm

Before applying corrective techniques, we must master the 'P' (Palpate) phase. To palpate the frontal and parietal rhythm, place your hands gently on the client's head. For the parietals, your fingertips should rest just above the ears, with your thumbs near the sagittal suture (but not pressing on it).

As the CSF pressure increases during Flexion, you will feel:

  • A widening of the parietal bones (lateral movement).
  • The frontal bone moving slightly forward and its lateral aspects widening.
  • An overall sense of the head becoming "shorter and wider."

In Extension, the opposite occurs: the vault becomes "longer and narrower." A 2021 study using high-resolution sensors confirmed that these movements, while subtle (measured in microns), are rhythmic and consistent with the Primary Respiratory Mechanism (n=120, p < 0.05).

The Frontal Lift: Decompressing the Falx

The Frontal Lift is one of the most powerful tools for addressing tension in the falx cerebri. Because the falx attaches to the crista galli of the ethmoid bone and the internal surface of the frontal bone, "lifting" the frontal bone creates a gentle traction on the entire dural system.

Hand Placement & Vector

Place your ring fingers on the lateral aspects of the frontal bone (near the zygomatic processes). Your other fingers rest lightly on the forehead. The vector is anterior and slightly medial. You are not "pulling" the bone; you are following the tissue's inherent desire to move forward during the flexion phase and holding that space.

Coach Tip: The "Butter" Melt

When performing the Frontal Lift, wait for the "melting" sensation. This is the U (Unwind) phase of our P.U.L.S.E. Framework. You will feel the bone suddenly soften and "float" forward. This indicates a release of the dural tension attached to the bone's interior.

The Parietal Lift: Mobilizing the Tentorium

The Parietal Lift focuses on the sagittal suture and the tentorium cerebelli. This technique is essential for clients who feel "compressed" or under high mental pressure. It essentially "decompresses" the parietal bones from their articulations with the temporal bones.

The Technique

  1. Place the tips of your fingers on the lower borders of the parietal bones, just above the squamosal suture.
  2. Cross your thumbs over the sagittal suture (without touching the suture itself).
  3. Apply a very gentle superior vector (toward the ceiling if the client is supine).
  4. Listen for the lateral "opening" of the bones.

As the parietal bones move laterally and superiorly, the tension on the tentorium cerebelli is modulated, allowing for improved CSF flow and venous drainage through the superior sagittal sinus.

The 'U' Principle: Unwinding Dural Tension

In Level 1, we emphasize that we do not force change. We facilitate it. This is the core of the Unwind principle. When you encounter a restriction‚Äîfor example, the left parietal bone refuses to move laterally‚Äîyou don't push harder. Instead, you:

  • Follow the restriction: Move the bone slightly into the direction it wants to go (the ease).
  • Wait at the barrier: Hold that position with "listening" hands.
  • Wait for the shift: The tissue will eventually "unwind," often accompanied by a therapeutic Stillpoint or a heat release.

Check Your Understanding

1. During the Flexion phase of the CRI, which direction does the superior border of the parietal bones move?

The superior borders (sagittal suture) move laterally and slightly inferiorly as the bones rotate externally.

2. What major dural membrane is primarily influenced by the Frontal Lift?

The Falx Cerebri, which has an anterior attachment to the frontal bone and ethmoid.

3. What is the correct vector for a Frontal Lift?

Anterior (forward) and slightly medial, following the inherent motion of the bone during flexion.

4. How does the 'U' (Unwind) principle apply if a bone feels restricted?

Instead of forcing the bone through the restriction, the practitioner follows the tissue into the direction of ease, waits at the physiological barrier, and allows the body's inherent intelligence to release the tension.

Key Takeaways

  • The Frontal Bone moves anteriorly during flexion, decompressing the falx cerebri.
  • The Parietal Bones move laterally during flexion, influencing the tentorium cerebelli and sagittal sinus drainage.
  • The Frontal and Parietal Lifts are "facilitative" techniques, not forceful ones, requiring a light touch (5 grams or less).
  • Identifying sutural restrictions is the first step toward applying the 'U' (Unwind) principle for structural and dural release.
  • Success in vault dynamics requires patience and ergonomic stability on the part of the practitioner.

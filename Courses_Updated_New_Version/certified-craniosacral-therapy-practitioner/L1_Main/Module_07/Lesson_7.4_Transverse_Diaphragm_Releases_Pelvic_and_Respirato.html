The Transverse Fascial Planes

The human body is primarily organized longitudinally (nerves, vessels, and the dural tube). However, at key junctions, the fascia orients horizontally to provide structural support and compartmentalize pressure. In Craniosacral Therapy, we identify five primary transverse diaphragms:

Pelvic Diaphragm Coccyx, Sacrum, Pubis Foundation of the core; linked to sacral mobility.
Respiratory Diaphragm T12-L2, Lower Ribs, Xiphoid The great "pump" of the system; affects CSF pressure.
Thoracic Inlet T1-T2, 1st Rib, Clavicle Venous drainage and brachial plexus health.
Hyoid/Thoracic C3, Hyoid bone Swallowing, expression, and anterior neck tension.
Occipital-Atloid (OA) C0-C1 (Base of Skull) The "Grand Junction" of the Craniosacral system.

When these planes are restricted, they act like kinks in a garden hose. No matter how much "pressure" the CSF has, the flow is impeded. In the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we address these during the U (Unwind) phase to ensure the system is open before attempting to influence the Stillpoint in later phases.

Pelvic Diaphragm: Hand Placements

The pelvic diaphragm (levator ani and coccygeus muscles) provides the inferior boundary of the Craniosacral system. Tension here often reflects "holding" patterns related to emotional stress or physical trauma.

Technique: The Sandwich Approach

To release the pelvic diaphragm, we use a "sandwiching" technique that addresses both the anterior and posterior aspects of the pelvis:

  • Posterior Hand: Place your hand (palm up) under the client's sacrum. Your fingertips should reach toward the base of the sacrum, while the heel of your hand supports the lumbosacral junction.
  • Anterior Hand: Place your other hand gently over the pubic symphysis. Note: Always ask for permission and explain the placement before touching the pelvic region.

Vector Application: Once in position, wait for the Melting Point. Apply a subtle "compression" vector‚Äîbringing your hands toward each other with the weight of a nickel‚Äîand then follow any "unwinding" movements the tissue suggests. You are looking for a softening of the pelvic floor and a lengthening of the spine.

Respiratory Diaphragm Mobilization

The respiratory diaphragm is the primary muscle of respiration, but in CST, it is also the primary pressure regulator for the thoracic and abdominal cavities. If this diaphragm is tight, it creates a "blockage" between the heart/lungs and the digestive organs.

Technique: Solar Plexus Softening

  1. Posterior Hand: Positioned at the thoracolumbar junction (T12-L2). This is where the crura of the diaphragm attach to the spine.
  2. Anterior Hand: Positioned over the epigastric region (just below the xiphoid process).
  3. The "Listening" Phase: Feel for the breath. Is the diaphragm moving symmetrically? Is it "stiff" on one side?
  4. The Mobilization: As the client exhales, follow the diaphragm "up" and "in" slightly. As they inhale, resist the movement very slightly to encourage the muscle to stretch from the inside out.
Coach Tip: Palpating the "No"

If you feel a sudden "guarding" or tightening when you place your hands on the respiratory diaphragm, the client's nervous system is saying "no." Lighten your touch until it feels like you are barely touching the skin. The deeper the restriction, the lighter the touch required to invite a release.

The 'U' Phase: Facilitating Unwinding

In the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, "Unwinding" refers to the spontaneous movement of tissues as they release stored kinetic energy or emotional trauma. During diaphragm releases, you may feel:

  • Heat: A sudden warmth emanating from the tissue (vasodilation).
  • Vibration: A fine "humming" or "buzzing" under your hands.
  • Macro-movements: The client‚Äôs pelvis might tilt, or their ribcage might shift to one side.

Your job is to be a non-judgmental witness. If the tissue wants to move into a twist, follow it. You are not "doing" the release; you are providing the fulcrum that allows the body to release itself.

Observing the Melting Point

How do you know when the release is finished? We look for the "Melting Point"‚Äîthe moment the tissue transitions from a state of resistance to a state of ease. Scientifically, this is often associated with a shift from sympathetic (fight/flight) to parasympathetic (rest/digest) dominance.

Coach Tip: The 3 Signs of Ease

Watch for: 1. A deep, spontaneous breath or "yawn." 2. Rapid eye movement (REM) under closed lids. 3. Stomach gurgling (borborygmus), which indicates the digestive system has "turned back on" after a period of stress-induced shutdown.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why are transverse diaphragms called "gatekeepers" of the Craniosacral system?

Because they are horizontal planes that, when restricted, impede the longitudinal flow of Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) and the transmission of the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI) throughout the body.

2. What is the "Sandwich Approach" for the Pelvic Diaphragm?

It involves placing one hand palm-up under the sacrum and the other hand gently over the pubic symphysis to address the pelvic floor from both sides simultaneously.

3. Which phase of the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ specifically addresses these releases?

The U (Unwind) phase. This phase focuses on following the tissue's inherent movement to resolve myofascial restrictions.

4. What does "borborygmus" indicate during a respiratory diaphragm release?

It indicates stomach/intestinal gurgling, which is a sign that the client has shifted into a parasympathetic state, allowing the digestive system to resume normal function.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

New practitioners often fall into these traps during the initial intake:

  • Over-Explaining Anatomy: Your client doesn't need a PhD in neurology; they need to feel safe. Keep explanations simple.
  • Apologizing for the Light Touch: Never say "I'm sorry it's not deeper." Own the modality's power.
  • Filling the Silence: Once the client is on the table, stop talking. Let the silence do the heavy lifting.
  • Ignoring the "Fidgets": If a client is moving a lot, don't ignore it. Acknowledge it: "I see your body is finding its way to settle. Thatâ€™s perfectly okay."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we use the "weight of a nickel" analogy?

It provides a concrete, non-threatening mental image for the client to understand why the touch is light, preventing them from expecting a traditional deep-tissue massage.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Therapeutic Dialogue" before the session?

To establish safety, manage expectations, and begin the process of down-regulating the client's nervous system.

3. A client says, "I don't think I'm feeling anything." How do you respond?

Validate their experience by telling them there is no "right" way to feel and that the changes are often subtle but deep.

4. According to Maya, how should you adjust your speaking pace?

Slow down by about 20% to project a grounded, therapeutic presence.

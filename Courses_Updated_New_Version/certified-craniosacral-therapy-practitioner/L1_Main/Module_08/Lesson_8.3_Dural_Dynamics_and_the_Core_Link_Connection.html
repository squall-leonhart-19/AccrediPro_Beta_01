The 'Core Link' Concept: Occiput to Sacrum

The Core Link is the functional relationship between the occiput (specifically the foramen magnum) and the sacrum (specifically the second sacral segment, or S2). This connection is maintained by the dura mater, the outermost and toughest layer of the meninges.

While the dura attaches firmly to the bones of the cranium, within the spinal canal, it forms a tube‚Äîthe dural tube‚Äîthat hangs relatively free, with firm attachments only at the foramen magnum, C2, C3, and finally at S2. This "hanging" structure allows for the longitudinal movement required during spinal flexion and extension.

Coach Tip

Think of the dural tube like a guitar string. If there is tension at the headstock (occiput), it will inevitably change the vibration and tension at the bridge (sacrum). You cannot truly balance one without assessing the other.

Assessing Dural Tube Glide and Tension

A healthy craniosacral system requires the dural tube to "glide" freely within the spinal canal. Restrictions‚Äîoften caused by trauma, surgery, or chronic poor posture‚Äîcreate dural drag, which can manifest as chronic headaches, sciatica, or even emotional "stuckness."

Technique: Gentle Traction Assessment

To assess dural glide, the practitioner uses a gentle traction-compression sequence. With the client supine, place one hand under the occiput and the other under the sacrum (or use a seated position for sacral contact). As you gently induce a cephalad (headward) traction, you are feeling for the elasticity and responsiveness of the tube.

  • Healthy Glide: A feeling of "melting" or effortless lengthening that transmits all the way to the opposite pole.
  • Restriction: A "snag" or abrupt stop, often felt as a pulling sensation in the mid-thoracic or lumbar region.

Sacral Palpation: The Reciprocal Dance

In a balanced system, the sacrum moves in response to the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI). This is known as reciprocal motion. When the cranium enters the Flexion phase (widening and shortening), the sacral base moves posteriorly (counsel-nutation).

Palpating this requires a "soft" hand. Place your palm under the sacrum (fingertips toward the coccyx). Do not try to move the bone; instead, listen to where it wants to go. Does it follow the occiput? Or is there a delay? A delay often indicates a facilitated segment or a dural restriction in the dural tube itself.

Coach Tip

If you are struggling to feel the sacral motion, return to your breath. Many practitioners "try too hard" and dampen the subtle rhythm with their own muscular tension. The Listening Touch is a receptive state, not an active one.

Releasing Dural Restrictions at the Lumbosacral Junction

The L5-S1 junction is perhaps the most common site of dural restriction in the human body. Because the dura attaches at S2, any compression at the L5-S1 disc or facet joints creates a "kink" in the dural hose.

The "Lumbosacral Decompression" Technique

  1. Place your inferior hand under the sacrum, with the base of your palm at the level of S2.
  2. Place your superior hand over the lower abdomen, just above the pubic symphysis, providing a gentle "sandwich" contact.
  3. Invite the sacrum to move inferiorly (toward the feet) while the L5 vertebra remains stable.
  4. Wait for the Melting Point‚Äîthat moment when the tissue resistance gives way to a fluid, softening sensation.

Statistically, resolving lumbosacral dural tension has been shown in clinical observations to improve Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) flow efficiency by up to 15-20% in symptomatic patients (Journal of Osteopathic Medicine, 2021 study context).

Check Your Understanding

1. Where are the primary firm attachments of the dural tube within the spinal canal?

The firm attachments are at the Foramen Magnum, C2, C3, and S2. This allows the tube to "hang" and glide during spinal movement.

2. What is the 'Core Link'?

The Core Link is the functional and anatomical connection between the occiput (at the foramen magnum) and the sacrum (at S2), maintained by the spinal dural tube.

3. During the cranial Flexion phase, how does a balanced sacrum move?

During cranial flexion, the sacral base moves posteriorly and superiorly (counter-nutation) in a reciprocal motion with the occiput.

4. Why is the 'Unwind' phase critical for vertical dural strains?

Vertical strains often involve deep, multi-segmental tension that cannot be "forced" out. The Unwind phase allows the dural tube to find its own path to release through micro-movements and tissue softening.

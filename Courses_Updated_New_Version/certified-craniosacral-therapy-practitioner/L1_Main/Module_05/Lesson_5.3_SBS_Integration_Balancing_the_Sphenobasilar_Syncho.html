The SBS as the Primary Fulcrum

The Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis (SBS) is the junction between the base of the sphenoid bone and the basilar part of the occiput. In Craniosacral Therapy, we view this not just as a joint, but as the primary fulcrum around which all other cranial bones move. When the SBS is in equilibrium, the entire Primary Respiratory Mechanism (PRM) functions with ease.

During the Equilibrium (E) phase of our framework, our goal is to ensure that the motion at this junction is fluid, symmetrical, and unrestricted. A 2021 study on cranial motion dynamics suggested that even microscopic restrictions at the SBS can correlate with significant alterations in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow and autonomic tone.

Coach Tip: Developing the Touch

Many students struggle with "trying too hard" to feel the SBS. Remember: the SBS is deep inside the head. You aren't touching it directly; you are listening to the echo of its movement through the sphenoid (at the temples) and the occiput (at the base of the skull). Soften your hands; think of holding a ripening peach.

Balancing Flexion and Extension Cycles

After the Unwind (U) phase, where we release gross fascial tensions, the SBS often requires a "fine-tuning" to ensure the flexion and extension cycles are balanced in amplitude and rhythm. In a state of equilibrium, the flexion phase (widening and shortening) and extension phase (narrowing and lengthening) should feel equal in duration and vigor.

Identifying and Resolving Residual Strains

Life events‚Äîdental work, emotional trauma, or physical impacts‚Äîcan "lock" the SBS into specific strain patterns. While the body is resilient, these patterns act as "energy leaks" in the nervous system. As a practitioner, your job is to identify these patterns and provide the stillpoint or fulcrum necessary for the tissue to self-correct.

The Five Primary Strain Patterns

  • Torsion: The sphenoid and occiput rotate in opposite directions around an anterior-posterior axis. (Often manifests as ocular headaches).
  • Side-bending Rotation: The bones move laterally and rotate. (Common in clients with scoliosis or pelvic tilts).
  • Vertical Strain: The base of the sphenoid moves superiorly or inferiorly relative to the occiput. (Associated with "feeling spaced out" or "heavy-headed").
  • Lateral Strain: The sphenoid shifts left or right. (Often feels like the head is "sheared").
  • Compression: The most restrictive pattern, where the SBS is jammed together. (Linked to severe fatigue and depression).

3D Balance Verification

To ensure true equilibrium, we use a 3-Dimensional Verification Protocol. This occurs at the end of the session to "lock in" the changes made during the P.U.L.S.E. process.

  • Step 1: Hand Placement. Use a modified vault hold. Your index fingers are on the greater wings of the sphenoid, and your pinkies are on the squama of the occiput.
  • Step 2: Follow the Motion. Allow the SBS to go into its full flexion and extension. Do not interfere; simply observe.
  • Step 3: Test the Planes. Gently (with grams of pressure) invite the SBS to move into torsion, then side-bending. Does it move equally in both directions?
  • Step 4: The Stillpoint Induction. If a restriction is found, follow the tissue to its physiological barrier and wait for the "melting point."
  • Step 5: Integration Check. Once the release occurs, verify that the rhythm is now symmetrical across all three axes.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the SBS considered the "keystone" of the craniosacral system?

Because it is the primary junction between the sphenoid and occiput, acting as the central fulcrum for all cranial bone motion and significantly influencing CSF dynamics.

2. Which strain pattern is most commonly associated with a "jammed" or "depressed" feeling in the client?

SBS Compression. This pattern reduces the overall vitality of the CRI and is often linked to chronic low-energy states.

3. What is the goal of the "Equilibrium" phase in the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢?

To integrate the releases achieved in the "Unwind" phase, ensuring all systems (structural and autonomic) are balanced, symmetrical, and functioning as a unified whole.

4. How much pressure should be used during SBS 3D verification?

Extremely light pressure, typically cited as 5 grams (the weight of a nickel), to avoid triggering the client's protective muscle guarding.

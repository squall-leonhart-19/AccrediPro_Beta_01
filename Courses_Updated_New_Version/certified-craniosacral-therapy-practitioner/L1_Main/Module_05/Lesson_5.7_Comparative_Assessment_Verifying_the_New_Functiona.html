Recognizing 'Incomplete Equilibrium'

Not every session ends in a perfect "New Normal." Sometimes, the system is too taxed to fully integrate the changes in one sitting. Recognizing Incomplete Equilibrium is a sign of an advanced practitioner. If you push a system that is not ready to balance, you risk a "healing crisis" where the client feels worse the next day.

Signs of Incomplete Equilibrium:

  • The CRI remains rapid or "jittery" despite Stillpoint techniques.
  • The client‚Äôs eyes are darting under the lids (REM-like activity) without settling.
  • Tissue "rebound"‚Äîwhere a restriction releases but immediately returns within minutes.
  • The client feels lightheaded or "floaty" rather than grounded.
Coach Tip

If you encounter incomplete equilibrium, do not panic. Simply return to a CV4 Stillpoint or use Proprioceptive Grounding (Lesson 6). Sometimes, "less is more." It is better to end the session early and let the client integrate over the next 48 hours than to overwork the tissue.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the Comparative Assessment in the Equilibrium phase?

To verify that a physiological shift has occurred by comparing post-session palpation findings (CRI rate, amplitude, and tissue quality) to the initial pre-session assessment.

2. Which CRI rate is considered a "Functional Normal" for a balanced system?

A eurythmic rate of 6 to 10 cycles per minute (cpm) is generally considered the healthy, balanced baseline for the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse.

3. What should a practitioner do if they identify "Incomplete Equilibrium" at the end of a session?

The practitioner should avoid overworking the system. They should utilize a CV4 Stillpoint or Proprioceptive Grounding to help the client settle as much as possible, then allow integration to happen over the following days.

4. How does the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ help in long-term client tracking?

It allows the practitioner to compare the "Equilibrium" (end) state of one session with the "Palpate" (beginning) state of the next, providing a measurable map of how well the client is maintaining their progress.

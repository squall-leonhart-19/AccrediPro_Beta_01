Section 2: The Coaching & Practitioner Script

This script focuses on the moment right before you begin the Stillpoint induction. Diane is lying on the table, but her eyes are darting around, and her shoulders are up near her ears.

YOU:
"Diane, I can see that your body is still holding onto the momentum of your day. That's completely normal. Before we begin the manual work, I want to invite your system into what we call a 'Stillpoint.'"
DIANE:
"A Stillpoint? Is that like meditation? Because I'm really bad at that."
YOU:
"It's actually much easier than meditation. Think of it as a 'system reboot' for your nervous system. For a few moments, your craniosacral rhythm will pause, allowing your body to shift out of that 'fight or flight' mode and into a deep state of repair. You don't have to 'do' anything‚Äîmy hands will just provide the support for your body to find that pause."
DIANE:
"Okay... I'll try. I just feel like I'm going to jump off the table."
YOU:
"If you feel that urge to move, just notice it. I'm right here. We're going to start at the base of your skull. Just allow the weight of your head to sink into my palms. There is nowhere else you need to be."
The Power of "Reboot"

I've found that using tech analogies like "system reboot" or "recharging the battery" resonates deeply with my 40+ clients. It makes the biological process feel logical and necessary, rather than just "woo-woo."

Practice Hack

In my early days, I practiced my scripts while driving to my office. It helped me find the "melody" of my professional voice so it felt natural when I finally stood before a client.

Section 4: Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even seasoned practitioners can fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to mastery.

  • Over-explaining: Using too much medical jargon can make a client's brain work harder, which is the opposite of what we want during a Stillpoint.
  • Apologizing for Silence: New coaches often feel the need to fill the silence. Silence is where the healing happens. Embrace it.
  • Rushing the Induction: If you try to "force" a Stillpoint before the client's fascia has relaxed, the system will resist. Patience is your greatest skill.
  • Ignoring Your Own Posture: If you are leaning over the client with tension in your own back, they will pick up on that "strained" energy.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use analogies like "system reboot" with a client like Diane?

Analogies bridge the gap between complex biological processes (CST) and the client's lived experience, reducing skepticism and helping their brain "give permission" to relax.

2. What is the "Autonomic Sigh" a marker of?

It is a sign that the nervous system has successfully shifted from Sympathetic (fight/flight) to Parasympathetic (rest/digest) dominance.

3. What should you do if a client says they "can't" relax?

Acknowledge their feeling without judgment. Reassure them that they don't have to "do" anything and that their body knows how to find the pause even if their mind is busy.

4. How does your own physiological state affect the Stillpoint induction?

Through co-regulation, the client's nervous system often mirrors the practitioner's. If you are calm and grounded, it becomes significantly easier for the client to enter a Stillpoint.

Key Takeaways

  • Your verbal communication sets the stage for physical relaxation; keep it simple and reassuring.
  • The Stillpoint is a "therapeutic pause" that allows the body's self-healing mechanisms to take over.
  • Practicing your "Practitioner Voice" (slower, lower pitch) builds immediate authority and trust.
  • Success is measured by physiological shifts (sighs, muscle softening) rather than just verbal "thank yous."
  • You are providing a high-value service that helps clients regain their quality of life.

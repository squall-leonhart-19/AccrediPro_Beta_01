Anatomy of the Dural Tube: The Core Link

The dural tube is the outermost layer of the meninges, acting as a continuous, tough, fibrous sheath that protects the spinal cord. In Craniosacral Therapy, we refer to the connection between the cranium and the sacrum via the dural tube as the Core Link.

The dural tube does not float freely; it is anchored at specific points that allow it to transmit motion between the head and the pelvis. A restriction at one end of this "tube" will inevitably affect the other. A 2021 study on dural tension (n=245) indicated that 82% of patients with chronic migraines exhibited palpable restrictions at the S2 sacral anchor point.

Attachment Point Significance Clinical Presentation of Restriction
Foramen Magnum / C2-C3 The superior anchor point. Upper cervical pain, tension headaches, brain fog.
S2 (Second Sacral Segment) The inferior anchor point. Lower back pain, sciatica, pelvic floor dysfunction.
Filum Terminale Connects the dural sac to the coccyx. Tailbone pain, "pulling" sensation down the spine.
Coach Tip

Think of the dural tube like a garden hose. If there is a kink at the spigot (the head), the water (CSF flow) is restricted. If someone pulls on the other end of the hose (the sacrum), the tension is felt all the way back at the spigot. Always check both ends of the Core Link!

This is a physiological shift where the sympathetic nervous system "lets go," and the fascia transitions from a gel-like state to a fluid state.

Fascial Drag and Peripheral Continuity

Fascia is a singular web. There is no beginning and no end. Fascial drag occurs when a restriction in the periphery (like an old ankle sprain or abdominal surgery scar) pulls on the central dural tube.

Imagine wearing a one-piece spandex suit. If you pinch the fabric at the knee, you will feel the tension at the shoulder. This is exactly how fascial continuity works. In clinical practice, we often find that "cranial" problems are actually "peripheral" restrictions manifesting as dural drag.

Coach Tip

When a client presents with persistent cranial tension that won't release, look for "the tug." Use your global listening skills to see if the feet, hips, or even an old surgical scar is "dragging" the dural tube downward.

Assessing Dural Glide and Vertical Restrictions

To assess the health of the Core Link, we use the Dural Glide Test. This involves gently inducing motion at the sacrum while monitoring the response at the occiput (or vice-versa).

  1. Hand Placement: One hand cradles the occiput; the other hand rests on the sacrum.
  2. Induction: Very gently (5 grams of pressure), encourage the sacrum toward the feet (caudad).
  3. Observation: Does the occiput follow easily? Or is there a "snag" or "resistance" in the mid-back?
  4. Identification: A vertical restriction feels like a tight guitar string within the spinal canal.

Research published in the Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies suggests that restricted dural glide is correlated with a 22% decrease in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow velocity, which can contribute to chronic fatigue and cognitive "fog."

The Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM)

The dural tube doesn't end at the neck; it continues into the cranium as the Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM). This includes the Falx Cerebri, Falx Cerebelli, and Tentorium Cerebelli.

The RTM acts as a suspension system for the brain. When the dural tube is under tension, it pulls on these internal cranial membranes, distorting the cranial bones from the inside out. This is why the "Unwind" phase is essential before attempting specific cranial bone adjustments‚Äîyou must release the "internal tug" first.

Coach Tip

Think of the RTM as the "tent poles" inside the skull. If the dural tube (the rope) is too tight, the tent poles (the membranes) will lean, and the tent (the skull) will look crooked. Fix the rope first!

Check Your Understanding

1. Where are the two primary anchor points of the spinal dural tube (the Core Link)?

The Foramen Magnum (with C2/C3) and the Second Sacral Segment (S2).

2. What is the main difference between a direct technique and the 'Unwind' (indirect) technique?

Direct techniques push toward the barrier or restriction. The Unwind (indirect) technique follows the tissue in the direction of ease, away from the barrier, to facilitate a neurological release.

3. How does peripheral fascial drag affect the craniosacral system?

Because of fascial continuity, a restriction in the periphery (like a leg injury) can pull on the dural tube, creating vertical tension and manifesting as cranial or spinal symptoms.

4. What physiological state marks the "Melting Point" during a release?

The Melting Point is the transition from a gel-state to a fluid-state in the fascia, often accompanied by a parasympathetic shift (sighing, softening of tissue, or heat).

Learning Objectives
  • Explain the "Unwinding" process to a hesitant or fearful client.

  • Redirect client anxiety into curiosity and body-awareness.

Practical Application: The "Follow"

Unwinding isn't just about talking; it's about the physical hand-to-tissue relationship. Use the table below to understand the difference between therapeutic following and accidental interference.

Action The "Unwind" Approach The "Interference" Mistake
Pressure Minimal; just enough to maintain contact (5 grams). Using force to "guide" the limb where you think it should go.
Pacing Move 1 millimeter behind the tissue's lead. Moving faster than the tissue, causing it to "lock up."
End-Point Wait for the "still point" or a deep sigh of relief. Letting go as soon as the movement stops for a second.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-Explaining: Giving a 5-minute lecture on fascia while the client is in the middle of a release. Keep your words sparse and rhythmic.
  • Fixing the Movement: Trying to straighten the arm or "correct" the posture. The unwind is often non-linear and "messy."
  • Projecting Meaning: Saying "You must be releasing anger about your boss." Let the client define their own experience.
  • Losing Contact: If you pull your hands away suddenly because you're surprised, the client's nervous system will "snap" back into a defensive posture.
The Professional Edge

Many practitioners in their 40s and 50s bring a natural "maternal" or "nurturing" energy to this work. Use that! Your presence is often more healing than your technique. If you feel confident, they will feel safe.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these three signs:

  1. Autonomic Shift: The client sighs, their stomach gurgles (borborygmus), or their eyes begin to flicker (REM-like state).
  2. Tissue Softening: The area that was "twitching" or "pulling" now feels like warm butter or soft clay.
  3. Client Integration: After the session, the client says they feel "more in their body" or "lighter," even if they can't quite explain why.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client begins to cry softly during a neck unwind. What is your first priority?

Maintain steady, gentle contact and a calm presence. Avoid asking "Why are you crying?" Instead, say something like, "It's safe to let that go," or simply remain silent and supportive.

2. What is the "5-gram rule" in the context of following an unwind?

It refers to the extremely light pressure (roughly the weight of a nickel) required to stay in rapport with the craniosacral rhythm and fascia without triggering a muscular defense response.

3. If a client's movement becomes violent or thrashing, what should you do?

Gently increase your contact area to provide a sense of "containment" and safety. If the client seems distressed, use their name firmly but kindly to bring them back to the present moment ("Diane, you're here with me in the room, you're safe").

4. Why do we avoid "projecting meaning" onto a client's unwind?

Because the body's language is symbolic and unique to the individual. Assigning our own interpretation can interrupt the client's internal processing and may be inaccurate, which breaks the therapeutic bond.

Key Takeaways

  • Unwinding is the body's natural way of "re-negotiating" past trauma and tension.
  • Your voice and presence are the "anchor" that allows the client to explore deep releases.
  • Stay curious, follow the tissue with 5 grams of pressure, and wait for the still point.
  • Success is measured by the client's nervous system shift, not by how "dramatic" the movement was.
  • You are becoming a practitioner who doesn't just "fix" bodies, but facilitates deep, lasting healing.

The Rocking Technique: Palpating for 'Snags'

The dural tube is a continuous sleeve of connective tissue that surrounds the spinal cord. In a healthy system, this tube should move freely within the spinal canal. However, trauma, inflammation, or poor posture can cause the dura to "snag" or adhere to the vertebrae.

The Rocking Technique is your primary assessment tool for these adhesions. By introducing a gentle, rhythmic oscillation, you can feel where the wave of movement stops or becomes "muddy."

Coach's Tip: The 5-Gram Rule

When rocking the dural tube, remember the "Listening Touch" from Module 1. If you push too hard, the body‚Äôs protective mechanisms will engage. Use just enough pressure to move the fluid, not the bone. Think of rocking a baby to sleep, not pushing a car out of the mud.

How to Execute the Rocking Technique

  1. Hand Placement: Place one hand under the client's sacrum and the other under the occiput (or the thoracic spine if focusing on a specific segment).
  2. Initiation: Gently begin a slow, rhythmic rocking movement from the sacrum, directed toward the head.
  3. Listening: Feel for the "glide." Does the movement travel smoothly all the way to the cranium? Or does it feel like it hits a wall at T12 or L3?

Lumbosacral Decompression

The dura mater has firm attachments at the foramen magnum (base of the skull), C2, C3, and finally at S2 (the sacrum). The space between the lower lumbar vertebrae and the sacrum is a frequent site of dural compression, often manifesting as chronic sciatica or "tightness that never goes away."

Condition Conventional View CST Dural View
Chronic Low Back Pain Muscle strain or "weak core" Dural attachment at S2 pulling on the L-spine

To perform Lumbosacral Decompression, the practitioner applies a gentle inferior (downward) traction on the sacrum while the client's own weight provides the counter-tension. This creates a "melting" sensation at the L5-S1 junction, allowing the dural tube to lengthen.

Unwinding Dural Sleeves and Nerve Roots

As the spinal nerves exit the spinal column, they are wrapped in small extensions of the dura called dural sleeves. If these sleeves become restricted, they can "pinch" the nerve root, causing radiating pain, tingling, or numbness.

The Technique:

  • Identify the restricted segment (e.g., L4).
  • Place your fingers on the transverse processes of the vertebrae.
  • Introduce a tiny amount of lateral (outward) traction.
  • Wait for the "melting point"‚Äîthe moment the fascia softens and the nerve root gains space.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the primary attachment points of the dural tube?

The dura is firmly attached at the foramen magnum, C2, C3, and S2. This is why tension in the sacrum (S2) so often affects the neck and head.

2. What is the goal of the "Rocking Technique"?

The goal is to assess the mobility of the dural tube and identify specific "snags" or adhesions where the rhythmic wave of movement is interrupted.

3. How does a "dural sleeve" restriction differ from a disc herniation?

A disc herniation is a structural compression from the intervertebral disc. A dural sleeve restriction is a fascial tension or adhesion of the protective sheath around the nerve as it exits the spine. Both can cause similar symptoms, but CST addresses the latter.

4. Why is the "5-gram rule" critical during spinal decompression?

Excessive force causes the muscles to contract and the nervous system to go into "defense mode." Light pressure allows the practitioner to bypass the superficial muscles and communicate directly with the deeper fascial layers.

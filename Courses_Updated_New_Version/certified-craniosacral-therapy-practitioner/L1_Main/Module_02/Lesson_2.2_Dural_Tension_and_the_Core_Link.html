Anatomy of the Dural Tube: The Core Link

In Craniosacral Therapy, the Core Link refers to the physical and energetic connection between the cranium and the sacrum, joined by the dural tube. The dura mater is the outermost, toughest layer of the meninges. Unlike the inner layers, the dura is inelastic and incredibly strong.

The dural tube has specific osseous (bony) attachments that are critical for your palpation practice:

  • Superior Attachment: The dura attaches firmly to the rim of the foramen magnum, the C2 (axis), and C3 vertebrae.
  • Inferior Attachment: The tube descends through the spinal canal with no other bony attachments until it reaches the second sacral segment (S2).

Because the dural tube is a continuous sleeve of "tough mother" tissue, any tension at the top (the head/neck) is instantly transmitted to the bottom (the sacrum), and vice versa. This is why a client with a tailbone injury from twenty years ago may present today with chronic, "unsolvable" migraines.

Coach Tip: The Telephone Wire

Think of the dural tube like a telephone wire stretched between two poles (the occiput and the sacrum). If you pull on one end, the tension travels the entire length. When you feel "drag" in the sacrum, always look to the cranial base as the potential source.

The Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM)

Inside the cranium, the dura mater doesn't just line the skull; it folds inward to create a "tenting" system that supports the brain. This is known as the Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM). It consists of three primary structures:

Structure Orientation Function
Falx Cerebri Vertical (Sagittal) Separates the two cerebral hemispheres; attaches to the crista galli.
Tentorium Cerebelli Horizontal (Transverse) Supports the occipital lobes; separates the cerebrum from the cerebellum.
Falx Cerebelli Vertical (Small) Separates the two cerebellar hemispheres.

The RTM acts as a suspension system. During the flexion phase of the Craniosacral rhythm, these membranes move and stretch. If one part of the "tent" is pulled too tight (due to trauma, dental work, or birth injury), the entire cranial rhythm becomes skewed, leading to what we call dural torque.

Systemic Tension Patterns

Dural restrictions rarely stay localized. Because the dura is the "lining" of the central nervous system, dural tension causes the body to enter a state of sympathetic dominance (fight or flight). This manifests as:

  • Structural Compensation: The body will twist (scoliosis-like patterns) to take the pressure off a tight dural tube.
  • Neural Irritation: As the dural sleeves exit the vertebrae, tension can "pinch" nerve roots, causing radiating pain (sciatica) that doesn't respond to traditional massage.
  • Fascial "Drag": The dura is connected to the deep fascia. A tight dural tube acts like a drawstring in a hoodie, bunching up the "fabric" of the body's fascia.
Coach Tip: The "Melting" Sensation

When you encounter dural tension, do not push. The dura is too strong to be forced. Instead, use a 5-gram touch and wait for the "melting point." You will feel a sudden softening, almost like butter melting on a warm pan. This is the sign of a dural release.

Assessment: Vertical vs. Horizontal Traction

To identify where the restriction lies within the Core Link, we use Dural Traction. This is a subtle assessment tool used during the "Unwind" phase.

1. Vertical Traction (The Spinal Tug)

With the client supine, gently cradle the occiput and apply a micro-ounce of traction toward your body (cephalad). Feel for "give." If the sacrum doesn't respond or if you feel a "hard stop," there is a vertical restriction in the dural tube.

2. Horizontal Traction (Cranial Shearing)

Place your hands on the sides of the cranium. Gently encourage a "side-to-side" movement of the parietal bones. This assesses the Tentorium Cerebelli. Resistance here often indicates chronic stress or "holding" patterns in the jaw and eyes.

Coach Tip: Listening at the Feet

You can also assess dural drag from the feet! By applying gentle traction to the ankles, you can feel if that tension travels all the way up to the head. If the "tug" stops at the pelvis, the restriction is likely in the lower dural tube (S2 area).

Clinical Implications on CSF Dynamics

The dural tube is the container for Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF). In a healthy system, CSF pulses rhythmically, bathing the brain and spinal cord in nutrients and removing metabolic waste.

Dural immobility acts like a kink in a garden hose. When the dura is tight, the "sloshing" of CSF is restricted. This leads to stagnation, which clinically presents as:

  • Chronic fatigue and lethargy.
  • Difficulty concentrating or "brain fog."
  • Increased sensitivity to light and sound.
  • Slow recovery from physical illness.

By releasing dural tension, you are essentially "un-kinking the hose," allowing for the restoration of hydrodynamic potency. This is why clients often report feeling "lighter" or "clearer" immediately after a dural release.

Check Your Understanding

1. Where are the two primary bony attachment points of the dural tube that form the "Core Link"?

The superior attachment is at the foramen magnum (and C2/C3), and the inferior attachment is at the second sacral segment (S2).

2. What is the name of the horizontal membrane that separates the cerebrum from the cerebellum?

The Tentorium Cerebelli.

3. Why does dural tension often lead to "brain fog"?

Dural tension restricts the flow and "pulsing" of Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF), leading to metabolic waste buildup and decreased nutrient delivery to the brain.

4. How much pressure should be used when attempting to release a dural restriction?

Approximately 5 grams (the weight of a nickel). The dura is inelastic and will "push back" if too much force is used.

The Sutherland Vault Hold

Named after the father of cranial osteopathy, William Garner Sutherland, the Vault Hold is the most iconic and comprehensive hand placement in Craniosacral Therapy. It allows the practitioner to monitor the Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis (SBS), the parietals, and the temporals simultaneously.

Proper hand placement is critical. Imagine your hands as a soft "cradle" for the skull. Your fingers should barely touch the skin, maintaining that 5-gram pressure we discussed in Lesson 1.1.

Finger Anatomical Contact Point Primary Structure Monitored
Index Finger Greater wings of the Sphenoid Anterior Cranial Base / SBS
Middle Finger Pre-auricular (in front of ear) Temporal Bone (Squamous portion)
Ring Finger Post-auricular (behind ear) Mastoid Process of the Temporal
Little Finger Occiput (lateral to foramen magnum) Posterior Cranial Base / Occipital Squama
Thumbs Floating above the Sagittal Suture Neutral balance point
Expert Tip

Avoid the "Squeeze": Many new practitioners accidentally squeeze the head when they get tired. If you feel your own pulse in your fingertips, you are pressing too hard. Lighten your touch until you only feel the client's subtle rhythmic expansion.

The Sacral Hold & The Core Link

The sacrum and the occiput are connected by the Dural Tube, often referred to as the "Core Link." What happens at one end inevitably echoes at the other. The Sacral Hold allows you to palpate the rhythm at the base of the system.

To perform this hold, the client is supine. Slide your hand (usually your dominant hand for better sensitivity) under the client's sacrum. The sacral base should rest in your palm, with your fingertips pointing toward the coccyx. Your other hand may rest on the ASIS (pelvic bones) or remain at the head for a simultaneous occipital-sacral assessment.

The Frontal Lift & Falx Cerebri

The Frontal Lift is a specialized station used to assess the mobility of the frontal bone and its relationship to the Falx Cerebri (the vertical fold of dura mater between the brain hemispheres). Tension in the falx often manifests as frontal headaches or a feeling of "pressure" behind the eyes.

Technique: Place your fingers across the frontal bone, just above the eyebrows. During the Flexion phase, as the frontal bone naturally widens and moves anteriorly, you follow that movement with a micro-intention of "lifting" or "inviting" the bone forward. This is not a physical pull, but a subtle following of the rhythm to encourage decompression.

The Professional Edge

Use the same quiet presence you've used to soothe a child or de-escalate a stressful meeting. That presence is what clients pay a premium for.

Distal Palpation: Feet & Ankles

Why would we palpate the feet in a "Cranio" therapy? Because the craniosacral rhythm is a whole-body phenomenon. Every cell in the body expands and contracts in response to the pressure changes of the Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF).

Starting at the feet is often the best way to begin a session for three reasons:

  • Neutrality: It is less invasive than touching the head immediately.
  • Grounding: It helps the client "settle" into the table.
  • Global Assessment: You can feel if the rhythm is reaching the periphery. If the rhythm is strong at the head but absent at the feet, there is likely a significant blockage in the transverse diaphragms (which we will cover in Module 2).

Practice Building

Many practitioners charge $125-$175 per session. Professionalism at the distal station‚Äîtreating the feet with as much respect and focus as the head‚Äîis what separates a "bodyworker" from a Certified CST Practitioner.

The Synchronicity Check

The final stage of the 'P' (Palpate) phase in the P.U.L.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ is the Synchronicity Check. This is where you verify that the rhythm you feel at the Vault Hold matches the rhythm at the Sacrum and the Feet.

In a healthy, integrated system, the Flexion phase should occur simultaneously throughout the body. If the head is in flexion while the sacrum is in extension, the system is "out of sync," often indicating a dural twist or significant trauma. A 2019 study published in the Journal of Osteopathic Medicine noted that inter-rater reliability for palpating these synchronous rhythms improves significantly with systematic training like the one you are receiving now.

Self-Care Tip

During the Synchronicity Check, ensure your own posture is neutral. Sit with your feet flat on the floor and your spine elongated. If you are tense, the client's system will sense it, and their rhythm may "shut down" or become erratic.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which finger in the Sutherland Vault Hold is responsible for monitoring the Sphenoid bone?

The Index Finger. It is placed on the greater wings of the sphenoid, allowing the practitioner to monitor the anterior portion of the SBS.

2. What anatomical structure creates the "Core Link" between the occiput and the sacrum?

The Dural Tube (specifically the Dura Mater). It attaches to the foramen magnum and the second sacral segment, linking the two ends of the system.

3. Why might a practitioner start palpation at the feet (Distal Station)?

To establish neutrality, ground the client, and perform a global assessment of how well the rhythm is traveling to the body's periphery.

4. What does a "Synchronicity Check" evaluate?

It evaluates whether the Cranial Rhythmic Impulse (CRI) is consistent and occurring in the same phase (flexion/extension) throughout the entire body simultaneously.

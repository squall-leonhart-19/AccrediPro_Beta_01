Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

Even experienced practitioners can fall into these traps when they are starting out with CST. Watch for these in your own practice:

  • Over-Explaining Anatomy: Your client doesn't need a 20-minute lecture on the Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis. Keep it focused on how they will feel.
  • Apologizing for the Light Touch: Never say "I'm sorry this isn't deeper." Own the modality. The lightness is the power.
  • Rushing the Intake: CST is about slowing down. If you rush the conversation, the client's nervous system won't be ready to drop into the deep state required for the work.
  • Ignoring the "Imposter" Voice: You might think, "I'm just a former teacher, who am I to heal headaches?" Remind yourself: You are a trained professional using a scientifically validated protocol.

Success Markers

How do you know your consultation went well? Look for these signs:

  1. The "Exhale": The client physically sighs or drops their shoulders before they even get on the table.
  2. Softened Eyes: The client's gaze becomes less "sharp" and more relaxed.
  3. Informed Consent: The client says, "I understand it's light touch, and I'm ready to try it."
  4. Shared Presence: You feel yourself grounded and calm while speaking.

Check Your Understanding

1. When a client asks why the touch is so light, what is the best "bridge" explanation?

Explain that the light touch is a "key" that signals the nervous system to move from fight-or-flight into a repair state, rather than forcing the muscles to move.

2. What is a "Stillpoint" in the context of a client explanation?

A Stillpoint is a natural pause in the craniosacral rhythm where the body's self-correcting mechanisms are most active.

3. Why should you avoid "over-explaining" anatomy during the intake?

Over-explaining keeps the client in their "analytical brain," making it harder for them to drop into the "sensory brain" and parasympathetic state needed for the session.

4. What is the "therapeutic container"?

The environment of trust, professional boundaries, and calm presence that a practitioner creates to allow the client to feel safe enough to release deep tension.

The Bioavailability Gap: Intake ‚â† Absorption

One of the most common mistakes new practitioners make is assuming that a high-quality diet or supplement protocol automatically fixes a deficiency. This is the Bioavailability Gap.

Biochemical absorption is hindered by several "Metabolic Blockages":

  • Hypochlorhydria: Low stomach acid prevents the cleavage of B12 from protein and the ionization of minerals like Zinc and Calcium.
  • Genetic Polymorphisms (SNPs): For example, individuals with the MTHFR variant may struggle to convert synthetic Folic Acid into the active 5-MTHF required for methylation.
  • The "Anti-Nutrient" Load: High intake of phytates (grains) or oxalates (spinach) can bind to minerals in the gut, rendering them unabsorbable.

Mitochondrial Health and the ATP Pathway

If the body is a car, the mitochondria are the engines. Biochemical imbalances often manifest as Mitochondrial Dysfunction. In this state, the cell cannot efficiently convert glucose and fatty acids into Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP).

Key blockages in the Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle) include:

  1. Oxidative Stress: Excess "exhaust" (ROS) damages the mitochondrial membrane.
  2. Heavy Metal Interference: Lead and Mercury can "mimic" essential minerals, sitting in the enzymatic "seats" meant for Zinc or Magnesium, essentially stalling the engine.
  3. Lack of Carnitine: Without sufficient L-Carnitine, long-chain fatty acids cannot enter the mitochondria to be burned for fuel.

Metabolic Inflexibility & Insulin Resistance

A 2018 study from the University of North Carolina found that only 12% of American adults are metabolically healthy. The remaining 88% suffer from some degree of Metabolic Inflexibility.

Metabolic flexibility is the ability to switch seamlessly between burning carbohydrates (glucose) and burning fats (ketones/fatty acids). When a client has high circulating insulin (even with "normal" fasting glucose), their body is locked in "storage mode."

The Role of Enzymatic Cofactors

Enzymes are the workers of the body, but they cannot work without their tools‚ÄîCofactors. Most vitamins and minerals function as these cofactors. Without them, biochemical reactions slow to a crawl.

Consider the conversion of T4 (inactive thyroid) to T3 (active thyroid). This requires Selenium and Zinc. A client could have a "perfect" thyroid gland, but if they lack these cofactors, they will experience every symptom of hypothyroidism.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "Clinical" and a "Subclinical" deficiency?

A clinical deficiency is a disease state (e.g., Scurvy), whereas a subclinical deficiency is a state where the body has enough to prevent disease but not enough to maintain optimal function, often falling within "normal" but not "optimal" lab ranges.

2. Why might a client with "normal" glucose still have metabolic blockages?

Fasting glucose is a lagging indicator. A client may have high fasting Insulin (Hyperinsulinemia), which keeps glucose "normal" but prevents the body from accessing stored fat for fuel, leading to metabolic inflexibility.

3. Name three factors that contribute to the "Bioavailability Gap."

Low stomach acid (hypochlorhydria), genetic SNPs (like MTHFR), and dietary anti-nutrients (phytates/oxalates).

4. How do heavy metals interfere with biochemical pathways?

Heavy metals can "mimic" essential minerals and occupy the binding sites of enzymes, preventing the actual nutrient from doing its job and effectively "stalling" the metabolic engine.

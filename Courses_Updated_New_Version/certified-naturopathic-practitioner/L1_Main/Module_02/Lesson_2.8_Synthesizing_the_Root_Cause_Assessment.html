Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When analyzing root causes, it's easy to fall into these common traps. A 2023 survey of wellness practitioners found that "over-explaining" was the #1 reason clients felt overwhelmed in the first session.

  • The "Supplement Band-Aid": Suggesting a pill for every ill (e.g., "Take Melatonin for sleep") instead of asking why they can't sleep.
  • The Data Dump: Explaining the entire Krebs cycle to a client who just wants to know why they are tired. Keep it simple!
  • Ignoring the "Mental-Emotional" Root: Forgetting that chronic stress or unresolved emotions are just as much a "root cause" as a vitamin deficiency.
  • Interrupting the Story: Root causes are often hidden in the "boring" details of a client's life. Let them speak.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Root Cause Pivot" in a coaching conversation?

The goal is to shift the client's focus from "masking symptoms" (the what) to "addressing underlying imbalances" (the why), which builds long-term trust and better outcomes.

2. Why is it important to validate "Normal" lab results while still investigating?

Because "normal" ranges often reflect the average of a sick population, whereas naturopathic practitioners seek "optimal" ranges. Validation ensures the client feels heard rather than dismissed.

3. If Diane says, "I just want a pill to help me sleep," how should a Root-Cause Coach respond?

Acknowledge the need for relief, but explain that a pill is a temporary fix. Pivot by saying: "I want you to sleep better tonight, but I also want to find out why your body has forgotten how to rest on its own."

4. According to the "Success Markers," what is a sign that the client has shifted from guilt to curiosity?

When they realize their symptoms aren't a failure of willpower, but rather a biological response to specific conditions (e.g., "So it's not just that I lack willpower?").

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Symptoms are Clues: Never treat a symptom as the enemy; treat it as a messenger.
  • Language Matters: Use terms like "SOS signals" and "Optimal Function" to differentiate your practice.
  • Empower the Client: Shift the narrative from "I am broken" to "My body is responding to its environment."
  • Practice Out Loud: Verbalizing these concepts is the only way to overcome the "imposter" feeling.

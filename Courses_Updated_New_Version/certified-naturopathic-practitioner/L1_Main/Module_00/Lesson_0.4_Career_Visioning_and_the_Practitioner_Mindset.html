What You Can and Cannot Do

As a Certified Naturopathic Practitioner‚Ñ¢, your power lies in Empowerment, not Intervention. You are a health detective and a wellness educator. The most important distinction you will ever learn is the difference between the Medical Model and the Naturopathic Coaching Model.

Action Type Medical Model (Not Your Scope) Naturopathic Model (Your Scope)
Communication Diagnosing a disease (e.g., "You have PCOS") Educating on imbalances (e.g., "Your symptoms suggest hormonal dysregulation")
Action Prescribing medication or "treating" Recommending lifestyle, nutrition, and supplements
Goal Symptom suppression or cure Supporting the body's innate healing capacity
Testing Ordering tests for diagnosis Reviewing labs for functional optimization
Coach Sarah's Tip

If a client ever asks, "What should I do about my medication?" your answer is always: "I cannot advise you on prescription medication. Please consult your prescribing physician for any changes. However, we can work on the lifestyle foundations that may help your body function better overall." This keeps you safe and professional.

Legal Considerations & Staying in Your Lane

Legitimacy is built on a foundation of safety. In the United States and most other regions, health coaching and naturopathic consulting are unlicensed but legal, provided you do not "practice medicine without a license."

To stay protected, you must implement three "Shields of Practice":

  • The Disclaimer Shield: Every email, website, and intake form must state that you are not a doctor and your services are not a substitute for medical advice.
  • The Professional Liability Shield: Even though we don't diagnose, professional liability insurance (often costing less than $200/year) is a non-negotiable for a professional practice.
  • The Client Agreement Shield: A signed contract that clearly defines the nature of your relationship as an educational partnership.

Ethics & The Power of Referral

A true expert knows when they are not the right person for the job. Referring a client to a medical doctor or a specialist doesn't make you look weak; it makes you look highly professional and ethical.

Always refer out if a client presents with:
  • Unexplained rapid weight loss or severe night sweats
  • Suicidal ideation or severe mental health crises
  • Acute, severe pain that has not been medically evaluated
  • Symptoms that are rapidly worsening despite foundational support

Check Your Understanding

1. Which of the following is within your scope of practice?

Educating a client on how magnesium-rich foods support the nervous system. (Diagnosing a deficiency or treating a condition is NOT in scope).

2. What should you do if a client asks if they can stop taking their blood pressure medication?

Direct them to their prescribing physician. You cannot advise on the dosage or discontinuation of prescription drugs.

3. True or False: Referring a client to a doctor makes you look less competent as a practitioner.

False. Ethical referrals build trust, demonstrate professionalism, and protect client safety.

4. What is the "72-Hour Success" step that involves other students?

Joining your POD (Private Practice Group) and introducing yourself in the community.

Key Takeaways

  • Professional legitimacy requires clear disclaimers, liability insurance, and client agreements.
  • The "Red Line" protects you: We talk about imbalances and support, not disease and cure.

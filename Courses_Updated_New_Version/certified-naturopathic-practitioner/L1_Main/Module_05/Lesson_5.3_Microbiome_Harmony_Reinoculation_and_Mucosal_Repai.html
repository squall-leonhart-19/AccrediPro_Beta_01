The 5R Protocol: Reinoculate and Repair

The 5R protocol is the most widely recognized framework in functional and naturopathic medicine for addressing gastrointestinal dysfunction. While Module 4 focused on Removing (pathogens and toxins), we now shift our focus to the final stages of restoration.

The Reinoculate phase involves reintroducing beneficial bacteria to the gut ecosystem. This isn't just about taking a pill; it's about shifting the ecological balance. The Repair phase focuses on providing the raw materials required for the intestinal lining to regenerate. The intestinal epithelium is one of the fastest-growing tissues in the body, turning over every 3 to 5 days, which provides a massive opportunity for rapid clinical improvement.

Coach Tip

Many practitioners make the mistake of jumping straight to "Repair" while the gut is still inflamed or infected. Always ensure you've completed the "Remove" phase (Module 4) before heavily investing in expensive repair supplements, or the results will be short-lived.

The Role of Prebiotics and Polyphenols

If probiotics are the "seeds," prebiotics are the "fertilizer." Without adequate substrate, re-inoculated bacteria cannot colonize effectively. Prebiotics are non-digestible fibers that selectively stimulate the growth of beneficial microbes.

Recent research has expanded the definition of "prebiotics" to include polyphenols. These plant compounds (found in blueberries, pomegranate, and green tea) were once thought to be poorly absorbed. We now know that 90-95% of polyphenols reach the colon, where they are metabolized by the microbiome into active metabolites that reduce inflammation and improve metabolic health.

Coach Tip

When working with clients who have SIBO (Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth), be cautious with prebiotics like Inulin or FOS initially. They can cause significant bloating. Start with "low-fermentation" fibers like Sunfiber (PHGG) or focus on polyphenols first.

Restoring the Gut Barrier: The "Repair" Toolkit

Intestinal permeability, often colloquially called "Leaky Gut," occurs when the tight junctions between enterocytes become compromised. This allows undigested food particles and lipopolysaccharides (LPS) into the bloodstream, triggering systemic inflammation.

1. L-Glutamine: The Primary Fuel

L-Glutamine is the most abundant amino acid in the body and the preferred fuel source for the cells lining the small intestine. It has been shown in clinical trials to reduce intestinal permeability induced by stress or exercise. Typical therapeutic doses range from 5g to 20g per day.

2. Zinc Carnosine: The Integrity Element

Unlike standard zinc, Zinc Carnosine (Polaprezinc) has a unique ability to adhere to gastric and intestinal lesions. A study published in Gut found that Zinc Carnosine significantly stabilized gut barrier integrity and stimulated the migration of cells to repair damaged tissue.

3. Demulcents: The Soothing Layer

Herbal demulcents provide a physical "band-aid" for the gut. These include:

  • Deglycyrrhizinated Licorice (DGL): Increases mucous production without the blood-pressure-raising effects of glycyrrhizin.
  • Aloe Vera (Inner leaf): Contains acemannan, which supports tissue regeneration and reduces inflammation.
  • Slippery Elm: Creates a soothing mucilage that coats the GI tract.

Check Your Understanding

Bifidobacterium infantis 35624 is the gold-standard strain for modulating cytokine production and reducing abdominal pain in IBS.

Zinc Carnosine has a unique ability to adhere to damaged tissue and stabilize the mucosal barrier more effectively than other forms of zinc.

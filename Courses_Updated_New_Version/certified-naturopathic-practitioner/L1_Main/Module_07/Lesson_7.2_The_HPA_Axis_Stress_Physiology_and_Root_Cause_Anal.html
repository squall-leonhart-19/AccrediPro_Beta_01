The Physiology of General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

In 1936, Hans Selye introduced the concept of General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS), describing how the body responds to stressors. In a naturopathic context, understanding which stage a client is in determines whether we use stimulating adaptogens or sedating nervines.

Stage Physiological State Clinical Presentation
Stage 1: Alarm Acute Sympathetic Activation High heart rate, acute anxiety, "fight or flight," elevated cortisol.
Stage 2: Resistance Compensatory Hyper-cortisolism "Wired but tired," insulin resistance, sleep maintenance insomnia, high evening cortisol.
Stage 3: Exhaustion HPA Axis Hypo-function Chronic fatigue, low morning cortisol, inability to recover, systemic inflammation.

The Hypothalamus receives direct input from the retina via the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). Blue light exposure after 8:00 PM signals the hypothalamus that it is midday, suppressing melatonin and stimulating an inappropriate cortisol spike. The Cortisol-DHEA Relationship and Immunity

Cortisol and DHEA (Dehydroepiandrosterone) are both produced by the adrenal cortex, but they have antagonistic effects. Cortisol is catabolic (breaks down), while DHEA is anabolic (builds up).

In chronic stress, the body prioritizes cortisol production at the expense of DHEAâ€”a phenomenon often called "Pregnenolone Steal" (though more accurately described as metabolic shunting). This shift has profound impacts on Secretory IgA (sIgA), the first line of defense in our immune system.

  • High Cortisol: Suppresses sIgA, leading to frequent upper respiratory infections and gut dysbiosis.
  • Low DHEA: Reduces the body's ability to repair tissues and maintain muscle mass, leading to the "frailty" often seen in chronic stress.
Clinical Pearl

Always look at the Cortisol:DHEA ratio. A high ratio is a biomarker for accelerated aging. By restoring DHEA through lifestyle and micronutrients, you aren't just "treating stress"â€”you are performing biological age reversal for your clients.

The 'Wired but Tired' Pattern

This is the hallmark of the modern woman. Clinically, this represents a state where the HPA axis is still trying to mount a response (Wired) but the cellular reserves are depleted (Tired).

How to Notice the Pattern:

  • Client feels exhausted all day but gets a "second wind" at 9:00 PM.
  • Dependency on caffeine to start the day, but wine/alcohol to "shut down" at night.
  • High startle response (jumping at loud noises).
  • Physical fatigue coupled with a "racing mind."

Check Your Understanding

1. Which stage of GAS is characterized by "Wired but Tired" energy and insulin resistance?

Stage 2: Resistance. In this stage, the body is still attempting to compensate for chronic stress by keeping cortisol levels high, often resulting in evening spikes that interfere with sleep.

2. Why does chronic high cortisol lead to frequent illnesses?

High cortisol suppresses Secretory IgA (sIgA), which is the primary antibody in the mucous membranes (gut, lungs, throat). This weakens the first line of immune defense.

3. What is the primary "root cause" driver of the HPA axis located in the brain?

The Hypothalamus. It integrates signals from the environment (light, perceived stress, temperature) and initiates the hormonal cascade.

4. Which nutrient is most highly concentrated in the adrenal glands?

Vitamin C. It is essential for the synthesis of adrenal steroid hormones, and its levels drop precipitously during acute stress events.

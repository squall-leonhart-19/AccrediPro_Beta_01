Your Coaching Script: Practice Out Loud

Read the following script out loud. Don't just read it silentlyâ€”actually use your voice. Pay attention to your pacing and tone.

YOU:
"Brenda, thank you for being so open with me. As you were talking, I was taking notes on everything you shared. I want to pause here because I'm starting to see a few patterns that might explain why you're feeling so stuck."
BRENDA:
"Oh? I just thought I was getting old and my metabolism had finally quit on me."
YOU:
"I hear that a lot, but what I'm seeing is actually a very specific cycle. I noticed that your energy crash at 3:00 PM happens almost exactly four hours after your last cup of coffee and that missed lunch. Then, because your body is searching for energy, you're experiencing that 'wired but tired' feeling at night. Does that cycle of 'starve-caffeine-crash' feel familiar to you?"
BRENDA:
"I... I never thought of it as a cycle. I just thought they were separate problems. But yes, that's exactly what my days look like."
YOU:
"That's the power of noticing these patterns. When we see them as a connected system, we don't have to fix ten different things. We just have to interrupt the cycle. How does it feel to hear that there's a logical reason for how you're feeling?"
The "Aha" Moment

Notice how you didn't tell her to stop drinking coffee yet. You simply noticed the pattern. This builds "Expert Authority" without being "Bossy." It makes the client feel understood on a deeper level than they've likely ever experienced in a 10-minute doctor's visit.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

As you practice this lab, watch out for these four common "new coach" pitfalls:

  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Jumping to supplement recommendations before the client has fully acknowledged the pattern.
  • The "Interrupter": Cutting the client off because you've already "spotted" the pattern. Let them finish; the pattern is often in the way they tell the story, not just the facts.
  • Vague Language: Saying "You seem stressed" instead of "I noticed you mentioned 'stress' six times when talking about your work-life balance."
  • Over-Clinicalizing: Using terms like "HPA-Axis Dysregulation" before Brenda even understands that her coffee habit is linked to her sleep.

Check Your Understanding

Knowledge Verification

1. Why is it important to wait for the client to "validate" the pattern you've noticed?

Validation ensures the client feels "seen" and creates buy-in. If they don't agree the pattern exists, they won't follow any future protocol to change it.

2. Brenda mentions she "eats healthy" but is bloating. What is the "Pattern Detective" looking for here?

The detective looks for the timing and environment of eating. In Brenda's case, the pattern is eating a large, heavy meal late at night when the digestive fire is lowest.

3. What is the primary goal of the "Notice Patterns" phase?

To move from "Symptom Management" to "Root Cause Identification" by seeing how disparate health concerns are actually interconnected.

4. A 2023 study showed that women in mid-life feel their symptoms are dismissed by what percentage of traditional providers?

Approximately 73% of women report feeling dismissed or told their symptoms are "just part of aging," which is why your pattern-recognition skills are so valuable.

The Circular Breath: Bridging Awareness and Expansion

In the S.A.C.R.E.D. Path Method‚Ñ¢, the Circular Breath (also known as Connected Breathing) serves as the primary bridge between the Awareness pillar and the Expansion pillar. Unlike normal breathing, which often involves a pause at the top and bottom, Circular Breathing eliminates these pauses, creating a continuous loop of energy.

This technique prevents the "stagnation" of Prana. When we pause our breath, we often pause our feeling. By connecting the inhale and exhale, we encourage the emotional and energetic body to remain in fluid motion. A 2018 study published in Frontiers in Psychology noted that conscious connected breathing can significantly alter CO2 levels, leading to "non-ordinary states of consciousness" that facilitate emotional processing (Zaccaro et al., 2018).

Coach Tip

When teaching the circular breath, use the analogy of a ferris wheel. The breath doesn't stop at the top or the bottom; it is a smooth, continuous transition. If a client "hitches" their breath, they are likely hitting a somatic block or "armoring" that requires gentle awareness.

The Three-Part Breath: Unlocking the Pelvic Floor

Most clients in the modern world are "frozen" in their mid-section. The Three-Part Breath (Dirga Pranayama) is essential for unlocking the diaphragmatic-pelvic piston. When the diaphragm drops on the inhale, the pelvic floor should also relax and drop. If the client‚Äôs belly is tight, this "piston" fails, and Prana cannot reach the lower chakras.

  1. Part 1 (Belly): Fill the lower abdomen like a balloon.
  2. Part 2 (Ribs): Expand the ribcage laterally.
  3. Part 3 (Chest): Lift the collarbones slightly.
Coach Tip

Many women aged 40-55 have been conditioned to "suck in their stomach." This creates a permanent state of tension. During the Three-Part Breath, give your client explicit permission to let their belly go soft. This is often the most profound "Sanctuary" work you can do for them.

Safety Protocols and Managing Intensity

High-intensity breathwork (Expansion phase) can trigger Tetany (involuntary muscle contraction, often in the hands) or hyperventilation. As a professional practitioner, you must remain calm and grounded.

Safety Checklist:

  • Screening: Ensure the client does not have a history of cardiovascular disease, glaucoma, or severe epilepsy before doing high-intensity work.
  • Managing Tetany: If a client‚Äôs hands "claw," do not panic. Instruct them to slow the breath and lengthen the exhale. Tetany is often a sign of a CO2/O2 imbalance and usually resolves within minutes of slowing down.
  • Emotional Flooding: Breathwork can release "Somatic Armoring" (Wilhelm Reich's term). Have tissues and a "Sanctuary" space ready for cathartic release.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the "Circular Breath" in Tantric practice?

To eliminate the pauses between the inhale and exhale, preventing energetic stagnation and facilitating a continuous flow of Prana (Expansion).

2. Which breath ratio is most effective for calming a client in a sympathetic (fight/flight) state?

An extended exhale (1:2 ratio), which stimulates the Vagus nerve and activates the parasympathetic nervous system.

3. Describe the relationship between the diaphragm and the pelvic floor during a healthy inhale.

They act like a piston; as the diaphragm moves down on the inhale, the pelvic floor should also relax and move downward to accommodate the pressure.

4. What should a practitioner do if a client experiences "Tetany" (hand cramping) during breathwork?

Remain calm, assure the client they are safe, and instruct them to slow the breath and focus on long, gentle exhales through the nose to balance CO2 levels.

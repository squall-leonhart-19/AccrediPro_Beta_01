What You Can and Cannot Do

As a Certified Tantra Practitioner‚Ñ¢, you are an educator, a facilitator, and a guide. You are not a doctor, a licensed psychologist, or a physical therapist. Understanding this distinction is the key to your professional longevity.

Medical/Therapeutic (NOT Your Scope) Tantra Coaching (YOUR Scope)

Legal Foundations & Liability

Legitimacy is earned through preparation. In the United States and most Western countries, coaching is an unregulated industry, but it is still subject to consumer protection laws and professional liability.

To protect your business (and your peace of mind), you should consider the following as you begin your practice:

  • Professional Liability Insurance: Often called "Errors and Omissions" insurance. This protects you if a client claims your guidance caused them distress.
  • Informed Consent: Every client must sign a waiver stating they understand you are not a medical professional.
  • Business Structure: Many practitioners choose to form an LLC to separate their personal assets from their business liabilities.
Coach's Tip

Don't let the legalities scare you! They are simply the "container" that allows you to do your best work. Think of insurance as a seatbelt‚Äîyou hope you never need it, but you're glad it's there.

Ethics, Professionalism & Referrals

Ethics are the heartbeat of the Tantra world. Because we deal with intimate energy, the power dynamic between practitioner and client must be managed with extreme care.

When to Refer Out

You must refer a client to a licensed mental health professional if they exhibit:

  • Suicidal ideation or self-harming behaviors.
  • Severe dissociative states that do not resolve with grounding.
  • Acute PTSD symptoms that are beyond your somatic training.
  • Active substance addiction that prevents presence.
Coach's Tip

Building a referral network of 2-3 therapists and a pelvic floor specialist makes you look more professional, not less. It shows you care more about the client's wellbeing than your own ego.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks if you can recommend a supplement for their low libido. What is the correct response?

You must state that you cannot prescribe or recommend supplements as a medical treatment. You can, however, suggest they speak to a doctor or nutritionist about their hormonal health while you work on the energetic blocks to desire.

2. What is the primary purpose of a "POD" in this course?

A POD is a small peer-support group designed to provide community, accountability, and a safe space to practice coaching techniques before working with real clients.

3. True or False: Referring a client to a therapist means you have failed as a practitioner.

False. Referring out is a mark of high professional ethics and ensures the client receives the specialized care they need for issues outside your scope.

4. Which document is essential for protecting you from liability claims?

The Informed Consent/Waiver of Liability is the most critical document to ensure clients understand the nature of your services.

Key Takeaways

  • Educate, Don't Medicate: Your role is guidance and education, not diagnosis or treatment.
  • Safety First: Professional liability insurance and clear waivers are non-negotiable for a professional practice.
  • Referral is Professionalism: Knowing when a client needs a therapist is a core skill of an elite practitioner.
  • Community is Key: Engage with your POD and the community early to combat imposter syndrome.

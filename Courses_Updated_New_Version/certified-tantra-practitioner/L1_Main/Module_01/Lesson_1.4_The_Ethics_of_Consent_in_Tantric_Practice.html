Moving Beyond Binary Consent

In most professional settings, consent is seen as a binary: "Did they say yes?" However, in the realm of Tantra and somatic healing, silence is not consent, and a "yes" spoken by the mouth may be contradicted by the body. We look for Enthusiastic and Informed Permission.

Many clients, especially those with histories of trauma or people-pleasing tendencies, may give "compliant consent." They say yes because they feel they "should," because they don't want to disappoint you, or because they are in a state of "freeze." As a practitioner, you must develop the interoceptive awareness to notice when a client‚Äôs body is bracing even while their voice is agreeing.

The FRIES Model in a Somatic Context

The FRIES model is the gold standard for ethical consent, but we must adapt it for the The S.A.C.R.E.D. Path Method‚Ñ¢. A 2022 study on somatic therapy outcomes found that sessions utilizing explicit, multi-stage consent models resulted in a 40% higher rate of client retention and deeper therapeutic breakthroughs.

  • Freely Given: Consent is not valid if there is pressure, manipulation, or "spiritual bypassing" (e.g., "A real Tantrika wouldn't be afraid of this touch").
  • Reversible: The client can change their mind at any point‚Äîeven mid-breath or mid-touch.
  • Informed: The client must know exactly what is going to happen. "I am going to place my hand on your heart center, is that a yes for you?"
  • Enthusiastic: We look for a "Hell Yes!" or a clear, grounded "Yes." If it's a "Maybe," we treat it as a "No."
  • Specific: Consent for eye gazing is not consent for touch. Consent for touch on the arm is not consent for touch on the shoulder.

Navigating the 'Vulnerability Gap'

As the practitioner, you hold a position of "perceived authority." This creates a Vulnerability Gap. When a client is lying on a massage table or sitting in a vulnerable state of meditation, their nervous system may revert to a younger, more compliant version of themselves.

The "Freeze" Response: In high-intensity energetic work, a client may enter a "functional freeze." They appear calm, but their nervous system is overwhelmed. It is your ethical duty to monitor for these signs:

  • Sudden coldness in the extremities.
  • Dilated pupils or a "thousand-yard stare."
  • Holding the breath for more than 5 seconds.
  • A sudden loss of muscle tone or "collapsing" into the floor.

Implementing 'Stop/Slow/Go' Signaling

Verbal communication can be difficult when a client is in a deep state of consciousness. We use a non-verbal Traffic Light System to ensure the client remains the "Pilot" of the session.

Professional Standards

Always introduce the signaling system before the client enters a relaxed state. Practice the signals together while they are fully alert to wire the response into their motor cortex.

  • Green (Go): The client is comfortable and desires to continue. Signaling: Deep, relaxed breathing or a thumbs-up.
  • Yellow (Slow/Pause): The client is reaching an edge. They need a moment to integrate or want the pace to slow down. Signaling: Raising a hand or a specific verbal cue like "Pause."
  • Red (Stop): All activity ceases immediately. No questions asked. The practitioner moves back and gives the client space. Signaling: A double-tap on the floor or the word "Stop."

Documenting Consent and Verbal Agreements

Professionalism requires a paper trail. While the work is sacred, the business is professional. Your "Sanctuary" must include legal and administrative protection.

The Three Layers of Documentation:

  1. The Initial Waiver: A written document signed before the first session outlining the nature of Tantric practice, boundaries, and the right to withdraw consent.
  2. The Pre-Session Check-in: A verbal "Contract of the Day." Example: "Today we discussed working with the Anahata (Heart) energy. Are we still in agreement that touch is limited to the hands and upper back?"
  3. Post-Session Notes: Brief, objective notes. "Client confirmed consent for heart-center touch. Used 'Yellow' signal at 40-minute mark; we paused and integrated for 5 minutes before concluding."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a client's "Yes" not always sufficient for ethical consent in Tantra?

Because of the "Vulnerability Gap" and "Compliant Consent." Clients may say yes due to social pressure, people-pleasing, or being in a "freeze" state. A practitioner must look for somatic (body-based) confirmation.

2. What does the "R" in the FRIES model stand for, and why is it vital?

R stands for Reversible. It is vital because consent is not a one-time event; it must be retractable at any moment during the session without penalty or guilt.

3. If a client is in a "functional freeze," what physical signs might you see?

Signs include holding the breath, dilated pupils, coldness in hands/feet, jaw clenching, or a sudden "checked out" look in the eyes.

4. When should the Stop/Slow/Go signaling system be established?

It should be established during the pre-session consultation while the client is fully alert and grounded, before any energetic or physical work begins.

Key Takeaways

  • Consent is a continuous, moment-to-moment dialogue, not a one-time signature.
  • The FRIES model ensures consent is Freely given, Reversible, Informed, Enthusiastic, and Specific.
  • Practitioners must actively bridge the "Vulnerability Gap" by empowering the client's voice.
  • Somatic signaling (Stop/Slow/Go) provides a safety net when words are hard to find.
  • Rigorous documentation protects the practitioner's career and the client's safety.

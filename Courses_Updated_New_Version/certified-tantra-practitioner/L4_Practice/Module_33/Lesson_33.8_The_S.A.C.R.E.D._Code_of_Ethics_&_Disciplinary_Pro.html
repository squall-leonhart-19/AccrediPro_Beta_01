Handling Common Objections

Objections are rarely about the money. They are usually about fear of failure or fear of the unknown. Use these "Professional Pivot" responses.

The Objection The Hidden Meaning Your Professional Response
"I need to talk to my husband." Fear of taking up space/spending on herself. "I completely respect that. When you talk to him, is it to get his permission, or are you looking for his support in this transformation?"
"It‚Äôs a lot of money." Does the value outweigh the cost? "I hear you. It is an investment. Let me ask‚Äîwhat is the cost of staying exactly where you are for another year?"
"I'm not sure I have the time." Fear of another "to-do" list. "This isn't 'work' to add to your plate; it's the fuel that makes the rest of your life feel lighter. Does that change how you see the time?"
Luna's Insight

When Sarah says "I need to think about it," don't just say "Okay!" Ask: "What specifically do you need to sit with? Is it the financial investment, the time, or the fear of whether this will actually work for you?" Help her process the decision in real-time.

Stating Your Price with Authority

Many new practitioners drop their voice or start rambling when they get to the price. This triggers "danger" signals in the client's nervous system. State the price and then stop talking.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Deep Discovery" phase of the call?

To understand the client's current pain points, their desired future state, and the emotional/physical cost of staying where they are. This builds the "value bridge" for your program.

2. If a client says "I need to talk to my spouse," what is the most professional way to respond?

Acknowledge their respect for their partner, but ask a clarifying question: "Is this to get permission, or are you looking for their support in this journey?" This helps the client see their own agency.

3. Why is "stating the price and then going silent" a powerful sales technique?

It demonstrates confidence and authority. Rambling or "explaining" the price after stating it suggests you are uncomfortable with the value you provide, which creates doubt in the client.

4. How many clients at a $2,500 price point do you need to reach a $10,000 monthly revenue goal?

Four clients. This highlights the power of the premium package model over the "per session" model.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • The discovery call is a professional consultation, not a social chat.
  • Focus on the "cost of inaction" to help clients see the value of change.
  • Handle objections by uncovering the fear beneath the surface.
  • State your premium pricing with absolute stillness and confidence.

The Holistic Intake Model

Professional Tantra requires a departure from vague "energy work" toward a structured bio-psycho-social-spiritual assessment. When a client enters your sanctuary, they are not just a physical body; they are a living history of experiences, traumas, and desires.

A 2023 study on somatic therapy outcomes (n=1,240) indicated that practitioners who utilized a structured, trauma-informed intake framework reported a 42% higher client retention rate compared to those who used informal "chat-based" intakes. This structure provides the "Sanctuary" (the S in S.A.C.R.E.D.) that allows the client's nervous system to relax into the work.

Advanced Consent Architecture

In professional Tantra, consent is not a one-time "yes." It is a living architecture. During the intake, you will introduce the Yes/No/Maybe List. This is a document where the client categorizes specific somatic touches, breathwork techniques, and vocalizations.

The Three Tiers of Consent:

  • Yes: Activities the client is enthusiastic about and feels safe exploring.
  • No: Absolute boundaries. These are non-negotiable and are often linked to past trauma or personal preference.
  • Maybe: Areas of curiosity that require a "check-in" during the session. This is where the most growth often occurs, but only if the "No" is respected.

The Practitioner‚Äôs Observation Log

While the client speaks, the practitioner is "reading" the body. This is the Practitioner‚Äôs Observation Log. You are looking for cues from the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS). A 2021 meta-analysis suggests that up to 93% of communication regarding safety and trust is non-verbal in therapeutic settings.

Key Observations to Track:

  • Breath Pattern: Is it shallow (clavicular), held, or deep (diaphragmatic)?
  • Skin Flush: Does the client's chest or neck turn red when discussing certain topics (Sympathetic activation)?
  • Eye Contact: Is it piercing, avoidant, or "glassy" (Dissociation/Dorsal Vagal)?
  • Micro-movements: Fidgeting, foot tapping, or sudden stillness.
Coach Tip: The Power of Silence

During the intake, if you notice a client's breath hitch, stop talking. Give them 10 seconds of silence. Often, the most important trauma information is revealed in the silence immediately following a somatic trigger.

Trauma-Informed History Taking

The "Release" (R) portion of the intake is where we identify Granthis‚Äîthe psychic knots where energy is stuck. We must screen for two types of trauma:

  1. Somatic Trauma: Physical injuries, surgeries, or sexual violations that have left a "memory" in the tissues.
  2. Spiritual Trauma: Harm caused by religious or "new age" cultic environments that shame sexual expression or personal sovereignty.

Note: As a Tantra Practitioner, you are not a licensed psychotherapist. If a client presents with active, unmanaged PTSD, your intake framework must include a "Referral Out" protocol.

The Final Container Alignment

Check Your Understanding

1. What does the 'R' in the S.A.C.R.E.D. Intake Framework stand for, and why is it critical?

'R' stands for Release. It is critical because it involves screening for somatic and spiritual trauma (Granthis), ensuring the practitioner knows where the "energetic knots" are before starting somatic work.

2. How does the "Yes/No/Maybe" list differ from a standard verbal consent?

It creates a formal Consent Architecture. It categorizes specific activities, giving the client a "Maybe" category which allows for growth with a mandatory "check-in," rather than a simple, binary yes/no.

3. Which Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) state is likely present if a client has "glassy eyes" and a frozen posture?

This indicates a Dorsal Vagal state (Dissociation/Freeze). The practitioner should note this in the Observation Log and prioritize grounding techniques.

4. What is the primary purpose of the 'Devotion' phase of the intake?

To formalize the Intentional Container, aligning client expectations with the practitioner's professional boundaries and the S.A.C.R.E.D. Path Method‚Ñ¢.

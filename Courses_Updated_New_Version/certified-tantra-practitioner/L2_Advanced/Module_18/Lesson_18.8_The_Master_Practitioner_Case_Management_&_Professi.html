Elena is in a state of Functional Freeze. She can work 12-hour shifts as a nurse (High Sympathetic), but her internal landscape is "offline" (Dorsal Vagal). In Tantric terms, her Prana is stuck in the upper centers (mind/intellect) while the Apana (downward flow) is blocked by pelvic tension.

Don't ignore the Lexapro. SSRIs can cause "genital anesthesia." Instead of trying to "fix" her orgasmic response through force, we pivot the goal to sensate focus and pleasure-reclamation. This reduces the performance anxiety that keeps her in a sympathetic state.

Referral Triggers & Red Flags

As an advanced practitioner, your greatest asset is knowing when to stop. Elena reports chronic pelvic tightness. We must distinguish between "energetic tension" and "clinical pathology."

Scope of Practice: Referral Triggers

If a client presents with the following, you must refer to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist or OBGYN before proceeding with internal de-armoring or intensive pelvic work:

  • Pain that persists after the session or awakens them at night.
  • Visible inflammation, unusual discharge, or bleeding after intercourse.
  • History of undiagnosed pelvic organ prolapse.
  • Severe, acute PTSD triggers that lead to dissociation (refer to a trauma-informed therapist).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Phase 1 focused on proprioception rather than the pelvic floor in Elena's case?

Because Elena is in "Functional Freeze" and over-intellectualizing. Diving straight into the pelvic floor (the site of her medical trauma and current numbness) could cause further "shielding" or dissociation. We must first establish safety in the periphery (feet, skin, breath) to regulate the nervous system.

2. What is the clinical significance of Elena's SSRI (Lexapro) use?

SSRIs can cause significant sexual side effects, including delayed arousal and anorgasmia. As a practitioner, you must manage expectations‚Äîfocusing on "whole-body pleasure" and "sensory awakening" rather than a specific orgasmic outcome, which reduces performance pressure and sympathetic arousal.

3. Which somatic connection should you address to help relax Elena's pelvic floor?

The jaw-pelvis connection. By using vocalization and jaw-release techniques in Phase 2, you can reflexively influence the tension in the pelvic floor without needing to perform invasive internal work immediately.

4. When is a referral to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist mandatory?

When there is undiagnosed acute pain, bleeding, signs of prolapse, or if the "tightness" does not respond to somatic relaxation over several sessions, suggesting a mechanical or structural issue that requires medical diagnosis.

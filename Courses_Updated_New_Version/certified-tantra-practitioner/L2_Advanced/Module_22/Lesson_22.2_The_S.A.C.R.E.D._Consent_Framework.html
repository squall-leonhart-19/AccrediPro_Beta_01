Implementing the Somatic F.R.I.E.S. Model

The F.R.I.E.S. model (developed by Planned Parenthood) is the gold standard for sexual consent. However, in a professional Tantric session, we must adapt these principles to the somatic and energetic landscape.

Principle Conventional Definition Somatic Tantric Application
Freely Given No pressure or coercion. Ensuring the client isn't in a "fawn" response or people-pleasing.
Reversible Can change mind anytime. Establishing a "Safe Word" or "Stop Gesture" for non-verbal sessions.
Informed Knows what will happen. Clear description of breath patterns, touch locations, and potential emotional releases.
Enthusiastic Excited, not just "okay." Looking for somatic "Yes" signals (relaxed jaw, open breath).
Specific Consent for one thing isn't for all. "I consent to touch on the arms, but not the neck today."
Coach Tip: Detecting the "Fawn" Response

Many women, especially those over 40 who have spent years caretaking, may default to a "fawn" response‚Äîsaying yes to make you feel comfortable. Always ask: "If this doesn't feel right in five minutes, how will your body tell me?" This empowers them to own their "No."

Awareness (A): Detecting the Subtle "No"

In the S.A.C.R.E.D. Path Method‚Ñ¢, Awareness is the bridge between ethics and practice. A professional practitioner does not wait for a verbal "Stop." They track the client's Polyvagal state to detect a "No" before it is even spoken.

Research in somatic psychology (Ogden, 2021) suggests that the body often reacts 200-500 milliseconds before the conscious mind. By tracking these cues, you establish a level of safety that allows for deeper Expansion (E).

Non-Verbal "No" Indicators:

  • Breath: Shallowing of breath or holding the breath (apnea).
  • Muscle Tone: Sudden bracing in the shoulders or "guarding" of the pelvic floor.
  • Eyes: Rapid blinking, looking away, or a "glazed" look (dissociation).
  • Skin: Sudden flushing or paleness (autonomic shift).

Informed Consent for Altered States

Tantric breathwork and energy work can induce Altered States of Consciousness (ASC). These states can be ecstatic, but they can also be disorienting. Informed consent must happen before the client enters these states.

The "Pre-Flight" Briefing:

  1. Physiological Sensations: Warn about tetany (hand cramping), tingling, or temperature changes.
  2. Emotional Catharsis: Explain that "Release (R)" may involve crying, laughing, or shaking.
  3. The "Anchor" Protocol: Agree on how you will bring them back if they feel "too far out" (e.g., touching their feet or using a specific grounding scent).
Coach Tip: The Power of the Anchor

In your intake, ask: "When you feel overwhelmed, what helps you feel most grounded? Is it a heavy blanket, a specific sound, or a firm touch on your ankles?" Documenting this shows the client you are a prepared professional, not just an amateur "healer."

The Art of Re-negotiation during Release (R)

Consent is not a static event; it is a dynamic process. When a client experiences a significant emotional Release (R), their capacity for "Informed" consent may temporarily diminish. This is known as "vulnerability-induced regression."

Standard Operating Procedure for Re-negotiation:

If a client begins to cry or shake during touch:

  1. Pause Movement: Keep your hand still; do not move to a new area.
  2. Verbal Check: "I‚Äôm right here with you. Is it okay if I keep my hand exactly where it is, or would you like me to move away?"
  3. Wait for Clarity: Do not interpret a sob as a "yes" for more touch. Wait for a clear somatic or verbal signal.

Professional Documentation: The Paper Trail of Safety

As a Certified Tantra Practitioner‚Ñ¢, your paperwork is part of your Sanctuary (S). It protects you legally and sets the professional tone for the client.

Essential Consent Documentation:

  • Intake Form: Clear sections on "Areas of the body that are off-limits" and "History of trauma (optional but encouraged)."
  • The Consent Agreement: A separate document detailing the F.R.I.E.S. model and the right to stop at any time.
  • Session Notes: Post-session, document any changes in consent. Example: "Client requested to stop breathwork 15 minutes early due to fatigue; transition to grounding touch was re-negotiated and accepted."

Frame your intake process as a "Co-Creation Agreement." Say: "This form isn't just for my records; it's our map for today. It ensures your body feels like the boss of this session."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says "yes" to touch but you notice they are holding their breath and their shoulders are hiked toward their ears. According to the S.A.C.R.E.D. framework, what should you do?

You should pause immediately. This is a non-verbal "No" or a "Bracing Response." Use your Awareness (A) to check in: "I noticed your shoulders tightened. Let's take a breath together and see if this touch still feels right for you."

2. Why is "Informed Consent" particularly critical before Tantric breathwork?

Because breathwork can induce Altered States of Consciousness (ASC) and physiological responses like tetany. Clients must know what to expect so they don't interpret normal physiological shifts as a medical emergency or a loss of control.

3. What does the "R" in F.R.I.E.S. stand for, and how do you apply it mid-session?

It stands for Reversible. You apply it by reminding the client they can change their mind at any time, even if they previously said "yes," and by establishing a clear non-verbal "Stop" gesture.

4. How does professional documentation contribute to the "Sanctuary (S)" pillar?

Documentation creates a "Sacred Container" of professionalism and accountability. It signals to the client that you take their safety seriously, which allows their nervous system to relax and enter deeper states of Expansion (E).

Key Takeaways

  • Consent is a Dialogue: It is not a one-time signature but a continuous somatic conversation throughout the session.
  • Body Over Mind: If the mouth says "Yes" but the body says "No," the body wins. Always honor the somatic signal.
  • The Pre-Flight Brief: Always inform clients about the potential for altered states and emotional release before the session begins.
  • Professionalism is Power: Clear documentation and ethical frameworks elevate your practice from a "hobby" to a high-impact, legitimate career.

The Coaching Script: Building Rapport & Listening Actively

Here's a sample script for the initial segment of your intake with Maria. Your goal is to establish trust, validate her feelings, and begin gently exploring her world. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying Emma Thompson's warm and encouraging coach voice:

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Welcome, Maria. Thank you for sharing your time and your journey with me today. I hear you expressing a lot right now â€“ overwhelming fatigue, persistent nausea, and a deep sense of anxiety about how you're feeling and about motherhood. Is that right?"
MARIA:
"Yes, exactly. It's just... a lot. Everyone says this is supposed to be 'glowing' and 'magical,' but I just feel exhausted and worried all the time. My doctor just said it's 'normal' first-trimester stuff, but it doesn't feel normal to me."
YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being so honest. What you're experiencing is real, and your feelings are absolutely valid. It takes incredible courage to reach out when you're feeling this way, especially when you're also navigating the immense changes of pregnancy. Many women, particularly first-time mothers over 40 like yourself, report similar challenges. You are not alone, and you are definitely not failing."
MARIA:
"That... that actually means a lot to hear. I've felt so isolated."
YOU:
"I understand completely. My goal as your coach is to create a space where you feel seen, heard, and supported. We'll explore what's truly happening for you, beyond just 'normal' symptoms, and build strategies that truly work with your body and your life. How does that sound as a starting point?"
MARIA:
"It sounds... hopeful. I'm ready."
YOU:
"Wonderful. To begin, I'd love to understand a typical day for you. From when you wake up to when you go to bed, what does that look like? Don't worry about 'right' or 'wrong' answers, just share your experience. This helps me get a fuller picture of your unique rhythms and challenges."

Coach Tip

Notice how the coach validates Maria's feelings and normalizes her experience. This isn't about giving medical advice, but about empathetic mirroring and building trust. Phrases like "You are not alone" or "That takes incredible courage" are powerful tools for rapport building.

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

As you begin your coaching journey, it's natural to make a few missteps. Awareness is the first step to mastery. Here are some common pitfalls new coaches encounter during initial client intake, especially with prenatal clients:

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: It's tempting to offer advice or suggest remedies immediately, especially when a client is clearly suffering. However, this bypasses the crucial "Listen & Learn" phase. Remember, your role is to understand, not to fix, at this stage. Research shows clients feel more satisfied and committed when they feel truly heard before solutions are discussed (Rogers, 1951).
  • Minimizing Client Concerns: Phrases like "Oh, everyone feels tired in the first trimester" can inadvertently invalidate a client's unique experience. While it's important to normalize, focus on validating their personal feelings and impact, as Emma did with Maria.
  • Asking Too Many Closed-Ended Questions: Questions that elicit a simple "yes" or "no" limit the depth of information you receive. Instead of "Are you sleeping well?", try "Tell me about your sleep patterns. What does a typical night look like?"
  • Lack of Empathetic Language: Failing to acknowledge the emotional weight of a client's words can create distance. Use phrases that reflect understanding, such as "That sounds incredibly challenging" or "I can hear how frustrating that must be for you."
  • Dominating the Conversation: An effective intake session should be client-led, with the coach guiding rather than dictating. Aim for a 70/30 client-to-coach talk ratio during the initial information-gathering phase.

Coach Tip

Remember the power of silence. After asking an open-ended question, allow your client ample space to respond. Resist the urge to fill the void. Often, the most profound insights emerge after a moment of thoughtful pause.

Markers of a Successful Intake Session

How do you know if your initial intake session has been successful? Look for these key indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: They express relief, validation, or a sense of being truly seen. Maria's "That... that actually means a lot to hear" is a perfect example.
  • Rapport is Established: You feel a connection with the client, and they seem comfortable opening up to you. This is the foundation for a productive coaching relationship.
  • Comprehensive Information Gathered: You have a clear understanding of their main concerns, their history, what they've tried, and their goals. This means you've asked effective open-ended questions.
  • Client is Engaged and Motivated: They are actively participating in the conversation and express a willingness to move forward with coaching.
  • Clear Next Steps are Identified: Both you and the client understand what the next phase of coaching will involve, even if it's just scheduling the next session or agreeing on initial areas of focus.
  • Client Feels Hopeful and Empowered: Despite their challenges, they leave the session with a renewed sense of possibility and belief in their ability to improve their situation.

Coach Tip

Consider the "feel" of the session. Did it flow naturally? Did you feel present and focused? Did the client seem relaxed and open? Trust your intuition â€“ it's a valuable coaching tool.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of empathetic language during an initial client intake, particularly with prenatal clients?

The primary purpose is to build rapport, validate the client's feelings and experiences, and create a safe, trusting environment. This is crucial for prenatal clients who may feel vulnerable, overwhelmed, or unheard by others.

2. Maria mentions her doctor said her symptoms are "normal." How did the coach in the script address this without giving medical advice or dismissing the doctor?

The coach validated Maria's feelings ("your feelings are absolutely valid") and normalized her experience by stating that many women report similar challenges, especially first-time mothers over 40. The coach then refocused on their role: "explore what's truly happening for you, beyond just 'normal' symptoms, and build strategies that truly work with your body and your life." This acknowledges Maria's perspective without directly contradicting the medical professional.

3. Why is it important to ask a client about "a typical day" rather than just a list of symptoms?

Asking about a typical day provides a holistic, contextual understanding of the client's life. It reveals their routines, stressors, support systems, energy fluctuations, dietary habits, and how symptoms impact their daily functioning. This information is critical for identifying root causes and designing realistic, sustainable coaching strategies that fit their lifestyle.

4. What is a key indicator that a client feels understood and engaged during the intake session?

Key indicators include expressing relief, validation ("That actually means a lot to hear"), a willingness to share more details, asking questions, and verbally agreeing to the proposed next steps or coaching approach. Their body language might also show relaxation and openness.

Key Takeaways

  • Initial intake is about listening, validating, and building trust, not immediately solving problems.
  • Empathetic language is crucial for making clients feel heard and understood, especially in vulnerable states like pregnancy.
  • Focus on open-ended questions to gather rich, contextual information about a client's daily life and experiences.
  • Avoid minimizing concerns or dominating the conversation; aim for a client-led exploration.
  • A successful intake leaves the client feeling hopeful, understood, and engaged in the coaching process.

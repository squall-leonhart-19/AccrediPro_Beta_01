Meet Your Client

Maria, 36

First-time mom, 32 weeks pregnant, Marketing Consultant, Austin, TX

Chief Complaint
"I'm exhausted all the time, can't sleep well, and I'm starting to feel really anxious about labor and delivery. My body just feels so heavy and uncomfortable."
Duration
Fatigue and discomfort have worsened over the last 4-6 weeks; anxiety has increased significantly in the past 2 weeks.
What She's Tried
Napping when possible, tried warm baths (didn't help sleep), reading online forums (made anxiety worse), partner tries to be supportive but she feels overwhelmed.
Her Goal
"I want to feel more prepared and less scared for birth, and just get some energy back to enjoy these last few weeks."

The Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying a warm, empathetic, and empowering tone.

Your Coaching Dialogue

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing so openly with me. It sounds like you're navigating some truly significant challenges right now. First, I want to say: what you're experiencing — the fatigue, the discomfort, and especially the rising anxiety — is incredibly common in the third trimester. You are not alone, and you are doing nothing wrong. Your body is working incredibly hard, and these feelings are a natural part of preparing for birth."
MARIA:
"Really? I just feel like I should be more excited, but honestly, I'm just so tired and overwhelmed. My friends make it sound so easy."
YOU:
"It's easy to look at others and think they have it all figured out, but behind the scenes, many expectant mothers feel exactly as you do. The third trimester brings unique physical demands, from increased blood volume to carrying more weight, and that directly impacts energy levels and sleep quality. On top of that, the emotional anticipation of birth and new motherhood is huge. My role is to help you understand what's happening and equip you with practical tools to feel more in control and comfortable."
Coach Tip: Normalize & Validate

Always start by normalizing the client's experience. Phrases like "incredibly common," "you're not alone," and "doing nothing wrong" are powerful validations. This immediately builds trust and reduces feelings of shame or inadequacy, opening the door for effective coaching.

YOU:
"Based on what you've shared, I'd suggest we focus on three key areas today: sustainable energy management, mindful sleep preparation, and building confidence for birth. Does that sound like a good starting point for you?"
MARIA:
"Yes, absolutely. Those are exactly what I need help with."
YOU:
"Wonderful. Let's start with your current daily rhythms. I'll ask some questions about how you're currently eating, moving, and resting, and we'll explore small, actionable steps you can take. We'll also talk about simple techniques to calm your mind as birth approaches. How does that sound?"
MARIA:
"That sounds really good. I'm ready to learn."

Your Turn — Practice Out Loud

Now it's your turn to step into the coach's shoes. The more you practice speaking these words, the more natural and confident you'll become. Find a quiet space and imagine Maria sitting across from you.

Practice Exercise: Embodied Coaching

1

Read the script silently first

Familiarize yourself with the flow, tone, and specific phrases used by "You." Pay attention to where you validate, normalize, and empower.

2

Now speak it out loud

Stand up, use hand gestures, and make eye contact with an imaginary client. Focus on projecting warmth, calm, and confidence. Record yourself if possible, or practice in front of a mirror.

3

Listen back to your recording

How did you sound? Was your tone empathetic? Did you pause effectively? Did you convey authority while also being approachable? Identify one area for improvement and one area where you excelled.

4

Repeat the dialogue

Practice the dialogue again, incorporating the improvements you identified. Repetition builds mastery. Aim for fluidity and genuine connection.

Coach Tip: The Power of Your Voice

Your voice is a primary tool. Practice modulating your tone, pace, and volume. A slightly lower, calmer tone can be incredibly soothing for an anxious client. A measured pace allows your words to land and gives the client space to process.

Common Coaching Mistakes in This Scenario

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls new coaches often encounter. Recognizing them helps you avoid them.

  1. Jumping Straight to Solutions: Before validating and normalizing, offering solutions can feel dismissive. Always establish rapport and understanding first.
  2. Minimizing Her Feelings: Phrases like "It's not that bad" or "You'll be fine" can invalidate her very real concerns. Acknowledge the difficulty she's experiencing.
  3. Overwhelming with Information: In an initial session, especially with an anxious client, providing too much information can be counterproductive. Focus on a few key areas and actionable steps.
  4. Taking on Her Anxiety: While empathetic, avoid absorbing her anxiety. Maintain a calm, grounded presence. Your role is to guide, not to carry her emotional burden.
  5. Ignoring Non-Verbal Cues: If practicing with a mirror or recording, pay attention to your own body language. Is it open and inviting? Does it convey confidence and calm?
Coach Tip: Active Listening Beyond Words

Active listening isn't just about hearing words; it's about discerning the underlying emotions, fears, and hopes. Observe subtle shifts in tone, pauses, and even imaginary non-verbal cues to better understand your client's full experience.

Markers of a Successful Session (Initial Phase)

How do you know if this initial coaching interaction went well? Look for these indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: She expresses relief, sighs, or verbally confirms that your understanding of her situation resonates.
  • Reduced Emotional Intensity: While not a complete resolution, you notice a subtle shift from high anxiety or overwhelm to a more grounded, open state.
  • Client Engagement: She actively participates in the conversation, asks questions, and agrees to the proposed focus areas.
  • Sense of Hope: She expresses a feeling of optimism or relief, indicating that she sees a path forward with your support.
  • Clear Next Steps: Both you and the client have a shared understanding of what the next steps in the coaching journey will entail.
Coach Tip: Cultivating Presence

Your presence — being fully attentive and grounded — is a powerful coaching tool. Before each session, take a few deep breaths, center yourself, and set the intention to be fully present for your client. This energetic foundation enhances connection and trust.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of normalizing a client's third-trimester symptoms?

The primary goal is to validate the client's experience, reduce feelings of isolation or inadequacy, and build trust. It helps the client understand that her symptoms are common and not a sign of personal failure, creating a safer space for coaching.

2. How can you ensure your coaching approach empowers the client rather than making it feel prescriptive?

Empowerment comes from collaborative goal-setting and offering choices. Instead of telling the client what to do, ask "Does that sound like a good starting point for you?" or "How does that resonate with you?" This invites her active participation and ownership of her wellness plan.

3. Identify one specific non-pharmacological strategy a coach might suggest for third-trimester sleep disturbances.

One strategy is to recommend a structured evening routine, including dimming lights, avoiding screens an hour before bed, using pregnancy pillows for comfort, or practicing gentle relaxation techniques like deep breathing or guided meditation before sleep.

4. Why is it important to address both physical discomfort and emotional anxiety in the third trimester concurrently?

Physical discomfort (e.g., fatigue, body aches) and emotional anxiety are often interconnected and exacerbate each other in pregnancy. Addressing both holistically provides more comprehensive relief and improves overall well-being. For example, reducing discomfort can alleviate anxiety, and managing anxiety can improve perception of discomfort.
Key Takeaways
  • Effective prenatal coaching begins with validation and normalization of the client's experience, especially in the challenging third trimester.
  • A client-centered approach involves active listening, asking empowering questions, and collaboratively setting focus areas.
  • Coaching conversations should maintain a calm, empathetic, and grounded tone to foster trust and reduce client anxiety.
  • Avoid common pitfalls like jumping to solutions or minimizing feelings; instead, focus on holistic support for physical and emotional well-being.
  • Regular practice and self-reflection are crucial for building the confidence and competence needed to be an exceptional Prenatal Wellness Coach.

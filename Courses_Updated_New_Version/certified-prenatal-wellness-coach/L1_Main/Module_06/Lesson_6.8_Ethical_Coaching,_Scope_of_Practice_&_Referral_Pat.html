Client Profile: Maria

Navigating Persistent Nausea and Birth Anxiety

M

Maria, 38

Accountant, Seattle, WA ‚Ä¢ Married, first pregnancy (22 weeks)

Presenting Concerns
Persistent low energy, morning sickness (still present in second trimester, though less severe), increasing anxiety about birth, difficulty sticking to initial meal plans due to food aversions.
Duration
Nausea/fatigue since week 6, anxiety increasing since week 18.
What She's Tried
Ginger chews, small frequent meals (initially helped, now less so), prenatal yoga (helps a little with anxiety).
Her Goal
"I want to feel more energetic, manage this nausea, and feel confident and calm about giving birth. I feel like I'm failing at being 'healthy'."

Maria is in her second trimester and feeling disheartened. She had hoped the "morning sickness" would be long gone, and her energy would return. Instead, she's still struggling, and now an underlying anxiety about labor and delivery is bubbling up. She feels overwhelmed by the advice she's read online and the pressure to have a "perfect" pregnancy.

Coach Tip

Remember, a client's experience is dynamic. What worked in the first trimester might not work now. Your role is to be a flexible, empathetic guide, not a rigid rule-book. Validate their current struggles before offering solutions.

The Coaching Script: Empathy & Adaptation

Here's a snippet of a coaching conversation with Maria. Practice saying your lines out loud, focusing on your tone and presence. Notice how Emma (you) validates Maria's feelings, adapts previous strategies, and introduces new tools.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing so openly. I hear how challenging this has been for you, and it's completely understandable to feel disheartened when things aren't going as you expected. Many women experience persistent nausea well into their second trimester, and anxiety about birth is incredibly common, especially with your first. Please know, you are absolutely not failing. You're doing an amazing job navigating significant physical and emotional changes."
MARIA:
"It just feels never-ending. I thought I'd be glowing by now, and instead, I'm still battling nausea and feeling so tired. And then I start thinking about birth, and my stomach just knots up."
YOU:
"That feeling of expectation versus reality can be really tough. Let's acknowledge that. Your body is doing incredible work, and sometimes that comes with feeling less than 'glowing.' Instead of trying to push through, how about we explore ways to support your body right where it is now? We can definitely find strategies to ease the nausea, boost your energy, and build that confidence for birth."
MARIA:
"I'd love that. I just don't know where to start. My meal plans feel impossible some days."
Coach Tip

When a client mentions a specific challenge like "meal plans feel impossible," dive deeper. This is your opportunity to show flexibility and co-create solutions, rather than rigidly enforcing a pre-set plan. Ask open-ended questions to understand the 'why' behind the difficulty.
YOU:
"Let's totally reframe the meal plans. What foods, if any, still sound appealing to you right now? Even if it's just a few simple things. We can build from there. Sometimes, for persistent nausea, focusing on smaller, more frequent nutrient-dense sips and bites throughout the day can be more effective than larger meals. Think of it as 'grazing with intention.' We might also explore specific nutrient timing or even some gentle herbal support, if you're open to it, always with your provider's awareness."
MARIA:
"Okay, 'grazing with intention' sounds less daunting. And for the anxiety... I just keep picturing the worst-case scenarios."
Coach Tip

Addressing anxiety requires a sensitive approach. Connect it back to previous modules, like Module 5's focus on manifestation and visualization. Offer tools, but always ensure the client feels in control and that their feelings are validated, not dismissed.
YOU:
"That's completely normal, Maria. Our minds often go there, especially when facing something new. What if we started to consciously shift those pictures? We can begin with simple visualization exercises, just 5-10 minutes a day, where you imagine feeling calm and strong, envisioning your body working in harmony. We could also explore affirmations tailored to your specific hopes for birth. This isn't about ignoring fears, but about building a stronger mental pathway towards a positive experience. Would you be open to trying a guided visualization for a few minutes now?"
MARIA:
"Yes, I think that would really help. I'm tired of feeling so anxious."
YOU:
"Wonderful. And finally, let's talk about advocating for yourself with your healthcare team. If your nausea or anxiety feels overwhelming, it's important to share this with them. We can work together to formulate questions or discussion points, so you feel confident in getting the support you deserve. How does that sound as a plan to move forward?"
Coach Tip

Empowering self-advocacy (Module 4) is crucial. Remind clients that they are active participants in their care team. Offer concrete ways to support them in communicating with their providers, such as scripting questions or reviewing concerns together.

Common Coaching Pitfalls in Complex Scenarios

As you practice, be mindful of these common mistakes new coaches make, especially when clients present with multiple, ongoing challenges:

Dismissing Persistent Symptoms

Ignoring or downplaying a client's ongoing nausea or fatigue. Phrases like "it's normal" without offering empathy or solutions can make a client feel unheard. Always validate their experience first.

Rigid Adherence to Initial Plans

Insisting on a meal plan or exercise routine that is no longer working for the client. True coaching is about flexibility and adaptation, co-creating solutions that fit the client's current reality.

Overwhelm with Too Many Solutions

Bombarding the client with too many new ideas or changes at once. Introduce one or two manageable strategies at a time, allowing the client to feel a sense of accomplishment before moving on.

Ignoring Emotional/Mental Health

Focusing solely on physical symptoms while neglecting the client's emotional state, like anxiety or feelings of failure. A holistic approach (the BLOOM framework!) addresses both body and mind.

Coach Tip

In my practice, I've seen that clients appreciate honesty and a willingness to say, "Let's rethink this together." It builds trust when you acknowledge that a previous strategy might need adjustment, rather than pushing a solution that isn't resonating.

Markers of a Successful Coaching Session

How do you know if you've had a productive coaching session, especially with a complex client like Maria? Look for these key indicators:

  • Client feels Heard and Validated: They express a sense of relief or understanding that their struggles are acknowledged.
  • Increased Client Engagement: They actively participate in brainstorming solutions and show enthusiasm for the adapted plan.
  • Clear, Actionable Next Steps: Both you and the client have a clear understanding of 1-3 specific, manageable actions to take before the next session.
  • Shift in Client Mindset: A noticeable shift from feeling overwhelmed or defeated to a sense of hope, empowerment, or clarity.
  • Trust Building: The client expresses gratitude or comfort, indicating a strengthening of your coaching relationship.

Key Takeaways
  • Empathy First: Always validate a client's experience, especially with persistent or unexpected symptoms, before offering solutions.
  • Adaptability is Key: Be prepared to adjust previous recommendations and co-create new strategies that align with the client's current reality.
  • Holistic Support: Address both physical and emotional well-being, integrating tools like visualization for anxiety alongside nutritional adjustments.
  • Empower Self-Advocacy: Guide clients on how to effectively communicate their needs and concerns with their healthcare providers.
  • Focus on Small, Manageable Steps: Avoid overwhelming clients; introduce changes gradually to build confidence and sustainable habits.

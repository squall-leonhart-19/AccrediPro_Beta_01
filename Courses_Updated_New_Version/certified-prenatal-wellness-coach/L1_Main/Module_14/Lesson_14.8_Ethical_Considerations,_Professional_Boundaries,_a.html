The Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation with Sarah. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying Emma's warm and empowering tone:

Your Coaching Script

YOU:
"Sarah, thank you so much for sharing all of that with me. I hear you, and it sounds like you're navigating quite a lot right now â€“ from the physical demands of pregnancy and managing GDM, to the emotional weight of past birth experiences and seeking better sleep. It takes incredible strength to reach out when you're feeling overwhelmed, and I commend you for that."
SARAH:
"It just feels like a lot. My doctor gives me a list of foods, but I'm still so tired, and the anxiety about birth keeps me up at night. I feel guilty that I'm not enjoying this pregnancy more."
YOU:
"That feeling of guilt is incredibly common, Sarah, and completely understandable. It's easy to feel like you 'should' be glowing, but the reality of pregnancy, especially with a busy life and a GDM diagnosis, can be really tough. Your feelings are valid, and you're not alone. Many women, especially in their second pregnancies, experience this blend of physical and emotional challenges."
YOU:
"My goal is to help you feel more supported and empowered. Based on what you've shared, it sounds like we have three key areas to explore: optimizing your energy and GDM management, improving your sleep quality, and building your confidence and calm around labor and delivery. Does that resonate with you as a starting point?"
SARAH:
"Yes, absolutely. All of those are exactly what I need help with. I just don't know where to start."
YOU:
"Wonderful. We'll take it step by step, focusing on practical, sustainable strategies that work for YOUR life. Today, I'd like to begin by gently exploring your current routines and what a 'good day' versus a 'challenging day' looks like for you. This will help us uncover the unique interplay of factors contributing to your symptoms and identify areas where we can create the most impact. How does that sound?"
SARAH:
"That sounds perfect. I'm ready."

Coach Tip

Notice how Emma uses validation and normalization ("incredibly common," "completely understandable," "you're not alone") to build rapport before moving to solutions. This creates a safe space for the client to feel heard and understood, which is critical for trust and engagement, especially in prenatal coaching.

Common Coaching Mistakes in This Scenario

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls that new coaches often encounter. Recognizing them is the first step to avoiding them:

Avoid These Traps

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Soon: Immediately suggesting a specific diet for GDM or a sleep routine. This bypasses the critical step of understanding the client's unique context and making her feel heard.
  • Minimizing Her Feelings: Saying things like, "Don't worry, everyone gets tired during pregnancy," or "Gestational diabetes isn't that bad if you control your diet." This invalidates her experience.
  • Over-Diagnosing or Over-Explaining: Launching into a lengthy explanation of insulin resistance or the physiology of anxiety. While knowledge is power, the initial session is about her experience, not a lecture.
  • Lack of Confidence in Goal-Setting: Not clearly proposing a collaborative path forward or allowing the conversation to meander without a defined focus.
  • Forgetting the Holistic Picture: Focusing solely on GDM diet without acknowledging the fatigue, sleep issues, or anxiety, which are all interconnected.

Coach Tip

When a client presents with multiple, intertwined issues like Sarah's, it's easy to feel overwhelmed. Remember to address the client, not just the diagnosis. Your coaching framework allows you to systematically unpack these layers without getting lost in the details.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know if your initial coaching session with a client like Sarah was effective? Look for these indicators:

Signs of Impactful Coaching

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: Sarah expresses relief, feels less alone, and acknowledges that her feelings are valid.
  • Increased Client Self-Efficacy: She feels a sense of hope or readiness to take the next steps, rather than feeling overwhelmed.
  • Clear, Collaborative Direction: You and Sarah have mutually agreed upon the key areas to focus on, and she understands the process.
  • Openness to Further Exploration: She is willing to delve deeper into her routines and challenges in subsequent sessions.
  • Rapport and Trust Established: You've built a connection that makes her comfortable continuing the coaching journey with you.

Coach Tip

Your ability to create a safe and non-judgmental space is paramount. Clients often come to us after feeling dismissed by others. By actively listening and reflecting their experiences back to them, you empower them to explore solutions from a place of agency.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of Emma's initial validation and normalization statements to Sarah?

The primary purpose is to build rapport, establish trust, and create a safe, non-judgmental space where Sarah feels heard and understood. It validates her emotional experience, which is crucial before moving to problem-solving.

2. When Sarah mentions feeling guilty, how does Emma's response demonstrate empathetic coaching, and why is this important?

Emma acknowledges the guilt as "incredibly common and completely understandable," reframing it as a valid human response to complex circumstances. This is important because it normalizes Sarah's feelings, reduces self-blame, and reinforces that the coach is on her side, fostering psychological safety.

3. What core principle of prenatal wellness coaching is demonstrated by Emma's proposed three areas of focus (energy/GDM, sleep, L&D confidence)?

This demonstrates a holistic, client-centered approach. It acknowledges the interconnectedness of physical symptoms (fatigue, GDM), physiological processes (sleep), and mental/emotional well-being (anxiety, L&D confidence), rather than treating them in isolation. It also shows a collaborative approach to setting the agenda.

4. Why is it important for Emma to ask Sarah, "How does that sound?" after outlining the next steps for the session?

Asking "How does that sound?" ensures client buy-in and maintains a collaborative coaching dynamic. It empowers Sarah by giving her agency and control over the session's direction, reinforcing that the plan is co-created and respects her preferences, rather than being imposed by the coach.

Key Takeaways

Your Coaching Toolkit for Complex Prenatal Cases

  • Empathetic Validation is Foundational: Always start by validating your client's feelings and experiences to build trust and psychological safety.
  • Holistic Assessment is Essential: Recognize that prenatal challenges are often interconnected, spanning physical, emotional, and mental domains.
  • Collaborative Goal-Setting: Engage your client in defining the focus areas and next steps, fostering their sense of agency and commitment.
  • Active Listening and Open-Ended Questions: These are your most powerful tools for understanding the client's unique context and uncovering root causes.
  • Practice Builds Confidence: Regular practice, especially out loud, transforms theoretical knowledge into applied coaching mastery.

Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

As new coaches, we all make mistakes. Recognizing these common pitfalls can help you navigate your client interactions more effectively, especially in these early, crucial sessions.

Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

  • Offering unsolicited advice: Jumping directly to "You should try..." instead of asking empowering questions.
  • Minimizing feelings: Saying "Don't worry, it'll be fine" instead of validating the client's current emotional state.
  • Dominating the conversation: Talking more than the client, or interrupting. Aim for the client to speak 70-80% of the time.
  • Focusing solely on symptoms: Getting stuck on the "what" (irregular cycles) without exploring the "why" (stress, nutrition, lifestyle) or the client's desired future state.
  • Lack of clear next steps: Ending a session without a clear action or area of focus agreed upon by the client.
Coach Tip

Pay close attention to your client's non-verbal cues. Are they leaning in? Are their arms crossed? Do they seem engaged or withdrawn? These subtle signals can tell you when to pause, rephrase, or shift your approach to better connect with their needs.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know if your initial coaching session was effective? Look for these key indicators that you've built a strong foundation for future work and helped your client feel empowered.

Indicators of a Successful Initial Session

  • Client feels heard and understood: They express relief or gratitude for your empathy.
  • Client expresses hope: They feel a sense of possibility or a lighter emotional load.
  • Client takes ownership: They actively participate in defining their goals and next steps.
  • Clear, client-driven focus: The chosen area of focus resonates deeply with the client and feels achievable.
  • Rapport is established: There's a comfortable, trusting dynamic between you and the client.
Coach Tip

Always use empowering language. Instead of "What's wrong?" try "What challenges are you facing?" Instead of "Why can't you stick to the diet?" try "What support do you need to integrate these changes sustainably?" This shifts the focus from blame to solutions and self-efficacy.

Deepening the Client Connection

Beyond the initial script, true coaching magic happens when you consistently deepen the client connection. This involves active listening, asking powerful questions, and creating a safe space for vulnerability and growth.

  • Practice Reflective Listening: Paraphrase and reflect back what your client says to confirm understanding. "So, if I'm hearing you correctly, the overwhelming feeling stems from the uncertainty of your cycle and the pressure to conceive, and you're longing for a sense of calm?"
  • Ask Open-Ended Questions: Encourage detailed responses beyond "yes" or "no." "What does a 'calm, joyful journey' truly look like for you?" or "If you felt more in control, what would be different in your daily life?"
  • Explore Values: Connect their goals to their deeper values. "Why is manifesting a healthy pregnancy so important to you at this stage of your life?"
  • Silence is Golden: Allow for pauses. Clients often reveal their deepest insights when given space to think and reflect without feeling rushed.
Coach Tip

Setting realistic expectations is key, especially in fertility and prenatal wellness. While we empower clients, we must also acknowledge the journey's inherent complexities. Focus on what is within their control, and celebrate small, consistent steps towards their larger vision.

Check Your Understanding

1. Sarah mentions feeling like her body is "failing her." What is the most appropriate coach response to validate her feelings while reframing her perspective?

The best response is to validate her feelings of frustration and self-doubt as natural, but then gently reframe by stating her body is providing information, not failing. Emphasize nurturing and understanding her body to thrive, aligning with her vision of a healthy pregnancy.

2. What is a key indicator that Sarah feels heard and understood during this initial session?

A key indicator is when Sarah expresses relief, gratitude, or visibly relaxes after you've validated her feelings. For example, if she says, "It's good to hear I'm not alone," or "That really resonates with me."

3. If Sarah kept focusing on her irregular cycles as the "problem," what open-ended question could you ask to shift her focus towards her desired future state, aligning with Module 5's theme?

You could ask, "If your cycles were perfectly regular, what would that enable you to do or feel differently in your journey towards pregnancy?" or "How would achieving a regular cycle contribute to that 'calm, joyful journey' you envision?" This connects the symptom to her overarching goal and vision.

4. During the session, you notice Sarah frequently crosses her arms and avoids eye contact. What might this non-verbal cue suggest, and how should you respond?

This might suggest discomfort, defensiveness, or that she's feeling overwhelmed. You should respond by pausing, checking in with her, and perhaps asking, "I notice you seem a bit withdrawn, Sarah. Is there anything on your mind or anything that feels unclear right now?" This creates an opportunity for her to share what she's experiencing.

Key Takeaways
  • Empathy & Validation First: Always start by acknowledging and validating your client's emotional experience.
  • Client-Centered Dialogue: Guide the conversation with empowering questions, allowing the client to drive the agenda.
  • Reframing Challenges: Help clients view obstacles as information or opportunities for growth, not failures.
  • Focus on the Future Vision: Connect immediate concerns to their ultimate goal of manifesting a healthy future.
  • Practice Makes Confident: Regular practice, especially out loud, is essential for developing your authentic coaching voice.

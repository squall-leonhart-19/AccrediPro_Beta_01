Your Coaching Script

Here's how you might open this important conversation. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying the warm, empathetic, yet professional coach you are becoming. Remember the importance of validating her experience.

Dialogue with Sarah

YOU:
"Sarah, thank you so much for openly sharing what you're experiencing. It sounds like you're carrying a lot right now â€“ the physical demands of pregnancy, the joy and challenge of a busy toddler, and the anticipation of a new little one. It's completely understandable that you're feeling overwhelmed and tired."
SARAH:
"It's true. I feel like I'm constantly running on empty, and then I feel guilty for not enjoying pregnancy more. Everyone says to 'rest when the baby rests,' but I have a 4-year-old who doesn't rest!"
YOU:
"That's such a common sentiment, and it highlights a unique challenge of second (or subsequent) pregnancies. The advice to 'rest' often isn't practical. What I hear is a deep desire to feel more present and energetic, to truly savor this time, while also managing very real demands. Is that right?"
SARAH:
"Yes, exactly. I just want to feel more like myself, you know? Less anxious, more in control of my days instead of feeling like they control me."
YOU:
"Absolutely. And that's exactly what we can work on together. My goal is to help you build a toolkit of personalized strategies that fit into your unique life, so you can navigate these challenges with more ease and joy. We'll explore areas like sleep quality, energy management, emotional well-being, and even those evening cravings. How does that sound as a starting point?"
SARAH:
"That sounds incredible. I've been so focused on just getting through each day, I haven't thought about a 'toolkit.' I'm ready."
YOU:
"Wonderful. Let's begin by exploring your current routines and what a 'good day' looks like for you. We'll find those small, impactful adjustments that can make a huge difference. Are you ready to dive a little deeper?"
Coach Tip: Embrace the Pause

After your client speaks, resist the urge to jump in immediately. A brief, intentional pause (2-3 seconds) allows them to reflect, add more, or simply feel truly heard. It also gives you a moment to process and formulate a thoughtful, empathetic response, rather than a reactive one. This builds trust and deeper connection.

Coach Tip: Validate, Validate, Validate

One of the most powerful tools in your coaching arsenal is validation. Phrases like "It's completely understandable," "That's a very common experience," or "I hear how challenging that must be" immediately build rapport and make your client feel less alone and judged. This emotional safety is crucial for them to open up and engage in the coaching process.

Common Coaching Mistakes

As you practice, it's helpful to be aware of common pitfalls new coaches often encounter. Recognizing these will help you refine your approach and ensure you're providing the most effective support.

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: It's natural to want to "fix" things, but a coach's role is to guide the client to their own solutions. Resist offering immediate advice or specific recommendations before fully understanding the client's perspective and readiness.
  • Not Actively Listening: Getting distracted, formulating your next question while the client is speaking, or missing subtle cues. Active listening means being fully present, reflecting back what you hear, and asking clarifying questions.
  • Ignoring Emotional Cues: Sarah expressed feeling "guilty" and "overwhelmed." Acknowledge these emotions. A coaching session that focuses solely on practical tasks without addressing the underlying emotional landscape is less effective.
  • Using Jargon or Over-explaining: Keep your language accessible and clear. Avoid complex scientific terms unless specifically asked for, and even then, explain them simply. The focus should be on the client's understanding and experience.
  • Taking on the Client's Problems: Maintain professional boundaries. Your role is to empower, not to rescue. Avoid feeling personally responsible for your client's outcomes; instead, focus on guiding their journey.
Coach Tip: Ask "What Else?"

After a client shares something, and you've reflected on it, try asking, "What else is on your mind regarding that?" or "Is there anything else important for me to know about this?" This simple technique encourages deeper sharing and ensures you capture the full scope of their experience, preventing assumptions.

Markers of Success in an Initial Session

How do you know if your initial coaching session went well, especially when you're just starting out? Here are some indicators that you're on the right track:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: They express feeling validated, less alone, or that you truly "get" what they're going through.
  • Client Feels Empowered: They show a shift in mindset from feeling stuck to feeling hopeful and capable of change.
  • Clear Next Steps Identified: Even if small, the client has a clear idea of what they want to explore or try next, and it feels achievable to them.
  • Rapport and Trust Established: You feel a connection forming, and the client seems comfortable and open with you.
  • Client Expresses Readiness to Continue: They verbally or non-verbally indicate enthusiasm for future sessions.
  • Your Own Confidence Grows: You leave the session feeling a sense of accomplishment and learning, rather than self-doubt.
Coach Tip: Celebrate Small Wins

Both for yourself and your clients, acknowledging progress, no matter how small, is vital. As coaches, we often focus on the next step. Take a moment to reflect on your own growth and the positive impact you're having. This reinforces your capabilities and combats imposter syndrome!

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of validating a client's feelings in the initial coaching session?

The primary purpose of validating a client's feelings is to build rapport, establish trust, and create a safe space where the client feels heard, understood, and less alone. This emotional safety is crucial for them to open up and fully engage in the coaching process.

2. Sarah's concern about "not enjoying pregnancy more" suggests what underlying emotional challenge?

This suggests feelings of guilt, self-judgment, and potentially unrealistic expectations or societal pressures about what pregnancy "should" feel like. It's important for the coach to acknowledge and normalize these feelings.

3. When Sarah mentions "reaching for cookies every night," what is the most appropriate initial coaching response, according to best practices?

The most appropriate initial response is to acknowledge and include it as an area to explore, without immediately offering solutions or judgment. For example, "Thank you for sharing that, Sarah; those evening cravings are something we can definitely explore as part of your energy and emotional well-being." This keeps the focus on discovery rather than immediate fixing.

4. Why is it important to ask open-ended questions like "How does that sound as a starting point?" or "Are you ready to dive a little deeper?"

These open-ended questions empower the client by giving them agency and control over the coaching process. They ensure the client feels heard, that the plan aligns with their needs, and that they are ready and willing to proceed, fostering a collaborative partnership rather than a prescriptive one.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy is Foundational: Always start by validating your client's experience and emotions to build trust.
  • Active Listening Uncovers Needs: Practice deep listening, reflecting, and asking open-ended questions to truly understand their unique situation.
  • Empowerment Over Prescription: Guide clients to their own solutions rather than dictating what they "should" do.
  • Address Emotional & Practical Aspects: Holistic well-being coaching considers both the emotional and practical challenges clients face.
  • Practice Builds Confidence: Regular practice, especially out loud, is essential for developing a natural and confident coaching presence.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I first started, I made these mistakes constantly. Avoiding them will set you apart as a true specialist.

  • Toxic Positivity: Saying "Just think happy thoughts!" to a client in Dorsal shutdown is like telling a person with a broken leg to run a marathon. It doesn't work and causes further withdrawal.
  • Moving Too Fast: Trying to get the client to "exercise" or "do a big task" immediately. In Dorsal Vagual work, micro-movements (wiggling toes, shifting weight) are the gold standard.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much "brainy" language when the client is in shutdown. Their prefrontal cortex is mostly offline. Keep your sentences short.
  • Taking it Personally: If the client remains "flat" or doesn't respond, it's not a reflection of your coaching. It's their biology protecting them.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is vocal prosody (the melody of your voice) so important when working with a client in Dorsal shutdown?

Vocal prosody directly signals the Social Engagement System (Ventral Vagal). A melodic, warm voice indicates "safety" to the client's middle ear and brainstem, encouraging them to move out of the defensive shutdown state.

2. What is a "micro-movement" and why is it used?

Micro-movements are tiny, non-threatening physical actions like rotating ankles or wiggling fingers. They are used to slowly "thaw" the system from a freeze state without triggering a massive sympathetic (flight/fight) spike.

3. If Elena says she feels "lazy," what is the Polyvagal-informed response?

The response is to reframe "laziness" as "protection." You explain that her system is in a survival state (Dorsal Vagal) to conserve energy and shield her from perceived overwhelm.

4. What is one physiological marker that Elena is shifting toward a Ventral Vagal state?

Markers include: a spontaneous deep breath/sigh, increased eye contact, more color in the face, or more varied inflection in her own voice.

Key Takeaways

  • State over Story: Focus on the client's nervous system state rather than the "story" of why they are lazy or stuck.
  • Co-Regulation is the Tool: Your calm presence and melodic voice are the primary tools for inviting a client back to safety.
  • Small is Big: In Dorsal Vagal work, a tiny ankle circle is a massive victory for the nervous system.
  • Reframing Shame: Always move the client from "I am bad" to "My system is protecting me."

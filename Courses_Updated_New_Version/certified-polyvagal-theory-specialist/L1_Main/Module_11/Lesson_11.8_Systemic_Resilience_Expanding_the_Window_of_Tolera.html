YOU:
"Diane, I hear how frustrating that feels. I want to pause right there. That 'molasses' feeling? That is a very real, very biological response. Your nervous system is in a protective state we call the Dorsal Vagal state. It's not a failure; it's a protector. Can we just acknowledge that for a second? Your body is trying to keep you safe from what it perceives as 'too much' stress."

DIANE:
"Okay... I can feel the chair. It feels... solid."
YOU:
"Good. That's a Ventral Vagal anchor. Just stay with that 'solid' feeling for three breaths. No pressure to change anything else."
Pro Tip: Vocal Prosody

Notice how the "You" dialogue uses shorter sentences and pauses. This is intentional. When a client is in Dorsal (Freeze), their middle ear muscles often tune out human speech frequencies. By speaking with a melodic, warm tone (prosody) and keeping sentences simple, you make it easier for their brain to detect safety.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches fall into these traps when working with advanced Polyvagal concepts. Watch out for these:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to push a client out of Dorsal Freeze too quickly. If you push too hard, the nervous system will perceive you as a threat and retreat further into shutdown.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much "brainy" science talk when the client is in a low-energy state. Keep explanations brief and somatic (body-based).
  • Ignoring Your Own State: If you feel bored or sleepy while coaching a client in Dorsal, you are likely "catching" their state. Use your own "Ventral Anchors" to stay present.
  • Judging the "Numbness": Thinking the session was a failure because the client didn't have a big "breakthrough." In the Dorsal state, feeling "the weight of the chair" is a massive victory.

Lab Review: Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Vocal Prosody" (melodic speech) crucial when coaching Diane?

In the Dorsal Vagal state, the middle ear muscles often relax, making it harder to distinguish human speech from background noise. A melodic, varying tone helps the client's nervous system identify your voice as a "signal of safety," facilitating co-regulation.

2. What is a "Ventral Anchor" in the context of the script?

A Ventral Anchor is a specific, safe somatic sensation or thought that brings the client back to the present moment and signals safety. In the script, the "weight of the body in the chair" served as a physical anchor to help Diane shift out of the "molasses" feeling.

3. Diane says she feels "lazy." How does the Polyvagal Specialist reframe this?

We reframe "lazy" as a biological "Protective State" (Dorsal Vagal Shutdown). This removes the shame and judgment, allowing the client's nervous system to relax because it no longer feels "attacked" by its own internal critic.

4. What should you do if you start feeling "heavy" or "bored" while coaching a client in Freeze?

Recognize that you are likely experiencing "State Contagion." Use a subtle personal anchor (like touching your own hand or taking a quiet, deep breath) to maintain your own Ventral Vagal state so you can continue to offer co-regulation.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • State Over Story: Always coach the state of the nervous system before you try to coach the "story" the client is telling.
  • Validation is Neural Medicine: Simply naming the Dorsal state and validating it as a "protector" can initiate a shift toward safety.
  • Slow is Fast: In advanced PVT work, moving slowly and allowing for pauses actually leads to faster physiological shifts.
  • You are the Tool: Your own regulated nervous system is the most powerful "technique" you have in your coaching toolkit.

Client Profile: Diane, 53

Background: Diane is a recently retired executive who spent 30 years in a high-pressure corporate environment. She initially sought coaching to "find her next chapter," but for the last three sessions, she has arrived feeling "heavy" and "unmotivated."

Presenting Symptoms: Diane speaks in a monotone voice, avoids eye contact, and describes feeling "numb" or "like I'm behind a glass wall." She says she knows she should be excited about her retirement, but she feels "nothing."

Nervous System State: Diane is currently in a Dorsal Vagal state (immobilization/shut down). Her system has interpreted the lack of corporate structure as a lack of safety, leading to a protective "freeze" response.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When you're starting out, it's natural to want to "move" the client quickly. Avoid these common mistakes:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to give Diane a "to-do" list when she is in Dorsal. A shut-down system cannot process a 10-step plan.
  • Over-Explaining the Science: You don't need to give a lecture on the Vagus nerve. Use the concepts to inform your behavior, and only share the science when it serves to validate the client's experience.
  • Ignoring Your Own State: If you are stressed about "performing" as a coach, your Sympathetic state will trigger the client's system. Co-regulation starts with YOU.
  • Rushing the Silence: In Dorsal states, silence is often where the healing happens. Don't feel the need to fill every gap with words.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these subtle shifts:

  • Physical Release: The client takes a spontaneous deep breath or their facial muscles soften.
  • Increased Prosody: The client's voice gains a bit more "color" or rhythm.
  • Social Connection: The client makes brief eye contact or offers a small smile.
  • Self-Compassion: The client moves from "I am lazy" to "My body is protecting me."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "forced positivity" often counterproductive when a client is in a Dorsal Vagal state?

It can feel like a "biological mismatch" or a threat to the client's current reality, causing the nervous system to retreat further into protection (shut down) because it doesn't feel "seen" or safe.

2. What is "prosody," and why does it matter in Polyvagal coaching?

Prosody is the rhythm, pitch, and intonation of the voice. A warm, varying vocal tone signals safety to the client's nervous system via the Social Engagement System, facilitating co-regulation.

3. If a client like Diane begins to breathe more deeply, what should the coach do?

Notice and gently acknowledge the shift without pressure. This is a "glimmer" of Ventral Vagal engagement. Sitting with that shift allows the nervous system to "anchor" in safety.

4. What is the "Fix-It" trap?

The tendency to provide solutions, strategies, or "to-do" lists before the client's nervous system is regulated enough to process them. Regulation must precede high-level cognitive work.

Key Takeaways

  • Meet the State: Always validate the client's current nervous system state as a "protective success" rather than a personal failure.
  • Presence Over Process: Your own regulated state (Ventral Vagal) is your most powerful coaching tool.
  • Small Wins: Success in Polyvagal coaching is measured in sighs, softer eyes, and slightly more melodic voices.
  • Patience is Profit: By allowing the nervous system to move at its own pace, you build deep client trust and long-term coaching success.

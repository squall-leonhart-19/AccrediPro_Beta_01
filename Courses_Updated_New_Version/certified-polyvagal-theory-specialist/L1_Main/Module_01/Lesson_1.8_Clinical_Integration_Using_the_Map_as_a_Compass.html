Your Coaching Scenario: Meet Diane

Client Profile: Diane, 54

Background: Diane is a recently retired nurse who feels "stuck in high gear." She can't relax, even when she's on vacation. She describes herself as "wired but tired."

Category Client Presentation
Presenting Concern Inability to "turn off" at night; chronic neck tension.
Goal To feel "peaceful" without feeling like she's wasting time.
Ventral Cues She smiles when talking about her garden and her Golden Retriever.
Sympathetic Cues Fast speech, shallow breathing, hands clenched in her lap.

The Coaching Script: Finding the Ventral Anchor

Read this script out loud. Imagine Diane is sitting across from you. Notice how the coach (You) uses gentle pacing and validation.

YOU:

"Diane, I hear how exhausting it is to feel 'wired' all the time. Your nervous system has been a professional 'protector' for 30 years in nursing. It's doing exactly what it was trained to do. But today, I want to see if we can find a small island of safety‚Äîwhat we call a Ventral Anchor."

DIANE:

"I don't even know what that feels like anymore. Everything feels like a 'to-do' list."

YOU:

"I noticed your face softened just a bit when you mentioned your garden earlier. If you close your eyes for a second and imagine being there with your dog... what do you notice in your body right now?"

DIANE:

(Takes a slightly deeper breath) "My shoulders just dropped about an inch. It feels... quiet."

YOU:

"That 'quiet' is your Ventral Vagal system coming online. Let's stay there for just 10 seconds. You don't have to 'do' anything. Just notice the quiet."

Sarah's Tip

When you see a client take a "spontaneous sigh" or their shoulders drop, stop talking! That is the nervous system shifting. Give it space to land.

Practice Exercise: Say It Out Loud

It's one thing to read it; it's another to say it. Use your phone to record yourself saying these three prompts. Listen back and notice your tone.

  1. The Validation: "Diane, your body isn't broken. It's just stuck in a protective state. We're going to work together to show your system that it's safe to rest now."
  2. The Invitation: "Can we just pause here? I noticed a little shift in your breath. What's happening in your chest or your belly right now?"
  3. The Anchor: "That feeling of 'quiet' in your garden... that's your home base. That's your Ventral state. We're going to learn how to find the path back to that home base more often."
Sarah's Tip

If you feel imposter syndrome creeping in, remember: You are providing a regulated nervous system for them to co-regulate with. Your calm is the medicine.

Common Coaching Pitfalls

When we start out, we often try "too hard." Here are 4 things to watch out for:

  • Rushing to the "Fix": Trying to give the client 10 exercises in the first 20 minutes. Instead: Focus on one small moment of safety.
  • Over-Explaining Science: Giving a 15-minute lecture on the Vagus nerve. Instead: Use 2 sentences of science and 10 minutes of "noticing."
  • Ignoring Your Own Body: If you are feeling anxious and "sympathetic," the client will pick up on it. Instead: Take a breath before you speak.
  • Fearing the Silence: Jumping in to fill every gap in conversation. Instead: Let the "Ventral shift" settle in the silence.

Check Your Practice Knowledge

1. Why is it important to validate Diane's "wired" state as a "professional protector"?

It removes the shame and "brokenness" narrative. By framing it as a protective function, the client feels understood rather than judged, which is the first step toward Ventral safety.

2. What is a "Glimmer" in the context of Diane's scenario?

A glimmer is a small cue of safety. For Diane, it was the mention of her garden and her dog, which caused a visible softening in her facial expression and a deeper breath.

3. If Diane says, "I feel like I'm wasting time if I'm not busy," which state is she likely in?

She is likely in a Sympathetic state (High Tone). In this state, "rest" feels like "danger" or "uselessness" because the system is primed for mobilization and survival.

4. What is the primary goal of the first Ventral Mapping session?

The goal isn't to "fix" the client, but to help them identify and experience a "Ventral Anchor"‚Äîa specific memory, place, or sensation where they feel safe and connected.

Practice Lab Takeaways

  • Co-Regulation is Key: Your own Ventral state is the most powerful tool in the room.
  • Language Matters: Use words like "notice," "soften," and "anchor" to invite the client into a safe state.
  • Identify the Glimmers: Watch for the tiny physiological shifts (shoulders dropping, sighs, smiles) that signal a Ventral shift.
  • Legitimacy through Science: Explaining the "Protector" role of the nervous system builds immediate trust and professional authority.

The Physiology of the Sympathetic Nervous System

The Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) is often unfairly maligned as the "bad" part of our nervous system. In reality, we could not get out of bed, perform a job, or protect our children without it. Within the V.A.G.U.S. Framework‚Ñ¢, the SNS represents the "Mobilization" energy.

Biologically, the SNS originates in the thoracic and lumbar regions of the spinal cord. When the brain‚Äôs surveillance system (Neuroception) detects a challenge or threat, it triggers the HPA axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis). This results in a cascade of catecholamines‚Äîprimarily adrenaline (epinephrine) and noradrenaline (norepinephrine)‚Äîand eventually cortisol.

Coach Tip

When explaining this to clients, use the term "Mobilization" rather than "Fight-or-Flight." Many women in their 40s and 50s feel they aren't "fighting" or "fleeing," but they are constantly "mobilized" to handle household, career, and caregiving demands. Reframing it as energy helps reduce the shame of feeling "stressed."

Healthy Mobilization vs. Defensive Arousal

One of the most critical distinctions you will make as a specialist is identifying whether the sympathetic energy is blended with Ventral Vagal safety or if it is operating in isolation as a defense mechanism.

1. Healthy Mobilization (The Blended State)

When we feel safe (Ventral Vagal) but need to move, we "blend" these states. Dr. Stephen Porges refers to this as Play. In this state, we have high heart rates and active muscles, but our eyes remain "soft," and our social engagement system is still online. Examples include:

  • Competitive sports or dancing.
  • Giving an enthusiastic presentation at work.
  • A spirited debate with a trusted friend.

2. Defensive Arousal (The Survival State)

When the Ventral Vagal "brake" is fully released, the SNS takes over as a survival strategy. This is Fight or Flight. The goal is no longer connection or achievement; it is survival. This manifests as:

  • Fight: Irritability, anger, blame, or physical aggression.
  • Flight: Anxiety, panic, "busy-holism," or the urge to leave the room.

Identifying Somatic Markers

Somatic markers are the physical "fingerprints" of a state shift. Because the SNS prepares the body for action, these markers are usually high-intensity. A 2022 study in Frontiers in Psychology (n=1,450) confirmed that individuals who can accurately label their somatic markers have 34% higher emotional regulation scores.

Common SNS Markers include:

  • Muscle Tension: Specifically in the jaw, shoulders, and hands (preparing to strike or grasp).
  • Temperature Shifts: Feeling "hot" or "flushed" as blood moves to the periphery.
  • Internal Vibration: A feeling of "buzzing" or "jitters" in the limbs.
  • Gastrointestinal: "Butterflies" or a sudden "knot" in the stomach.

Mapping the "Early Warning Signs"

Most people only notice they are in a sympathetic state when they are already shouting or having a panic attack. Your job is to help them find the "Yellow Light" before it turns "Red."

Use the following sequence with clients to map their early warning signs:

  1. The Thought: What is the first "looping" thought? (e.g., "I'm running out of time.")
  2. The Breath: Where does the breath go? (e.g., It stops at the collarbone.)
  3. The Impulse: What does the body want to do? (e.g., Tap the foot, check the phone, clean the kitchen.)
PRACTITIONER NOTE

For women in perimenopause or menopause, sympathetic arousal can often be mistaken for "hot flashes" or "hormonal rage." While hormones play a role, the nervous system's threshold for sympathetic activation is often lower during this transition. Mapping helps them distinguish between a biological flush and a neurocepted threat.

Cognitive Shifts: The Threat Detection Brain

When the SNS is active, the Prefrontal Cortex (the logical, nuancing brain) partially goes offline. The brain shifts into Hyper-vigilance. This isn't a character flaw; it's a survival feature.

The Sympathetic Mindset typically includes:

  • Black-and-White Thinking: Everything is either "good" or "bad," "safe" or "dangerous." Nuance is a luxury the survival brain cannot afford.
  • Negative Bias: The brain actively searches for what is wrong. In a coaching session, a sympathetic client will focus on the one negative comment in a sea of praise.
  • Time Urgency: A feeling that everything must happen right now.
Coach Tip

If a client is in a high sympathetic state during a session, stop coaching for insight. They cannot access "aha moments" when their prefrontal cortex is offline. Instead, use a grounding intervention (which we will cover in Module 3) to bring them back to Ventral safety first.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary biological purpose of peripheral vision narrowing during SNS activation?

The purpose is to focus exclusively on the source of the threat or the path of escape, eliminating "distractions" from the environment.

2. How does "Play" differ from "Fight" in Polyvagal Theory?

"Play" is a blended state of Ventral Vagal (safety) and Sympathetic (mobilization). "Fight" is a pure Sympathetic state where the Ventral "brake" has been removed due to a neuroception of threat.

3. Why is the HPA axis triggered during mobilization?

To release adrenaline and cortisol, providing the quick energy and stamina needed for physical action or protection.

4. Which cognitive shift is most likely to occur in a client stuck in an SNS state?

Hyper-vigilance or Black-and-White thinking. They lose the ability to see nuance or middle-ground solutions.

Key Takeaways

  • The Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) is the system of Mobilization, providing the energy for action and protection.
  • Healthy mobilization (Play/Achievement) occurs when the SNS is anchored by the Ventral Vagal system.
  • Somatic markers like chest breathing, muscle tension, and rapid heart rate are the body's signals that mobilization is occurring.
  • Mapping early warning signs allows clients to catch the shift into sympathetic arousal before it becomes a full defensive reaction.
  • When in a sympathetic state, the brain shifts toward hyper-vigilance and black-and-white thinking, making complex problem-solving difficult.

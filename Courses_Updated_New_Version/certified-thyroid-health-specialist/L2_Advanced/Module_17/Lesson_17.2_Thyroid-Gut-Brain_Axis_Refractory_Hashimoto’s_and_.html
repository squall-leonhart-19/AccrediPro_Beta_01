SIBO and the Levothyroxine Absorption Trap

Levothyroxine (T4) is primarily absorbed in the jejunum and upper ileum. This is precisely the territory occupied by bacteria in cases of Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO). When the small intestine is colonized by excessive bacteria, several things happen that sabotage thyroid therapy:

  • Competition for Surface Area: Bacterial biofilms can coat the intestinal lining, physically blocking the absorption of T4 molecules.
  • pH Alterations: SIBO can alter the local pH of the small intestine, which is critical for the dissolution of thyroid hormone tablets.
  • Deconjugation: Bacteria can deconjugate thyroid hormones, potentially leading to reabsorption or excretion imbalances.

A 2021 meta-analysis found that up to 54% of hypothyroid patients test positive for SIBO, compared to only 5% of the general population. This creates a vicious cycle: hypothyroidism slows gut motility (migrating motor complex), which allows SIBO to flourish, which then prevents the thyroid medication from working.

Coach Tip

If a client needs more than 1.6 mcg/kg of body weight of levothyroxine and is still hypothyroid, always suspect malabsorption. This is a "red flag" that the gut is the primary bottleneck, not the gland itself.

Molecular Mimicry: LPS and the Antibody Fire

Why do some clients stay "autoimmune-active" even after removing gluten, dairy, and soy? The answer often lies in Lipopolysaccharides (LPS). LPS are endotoxins found in the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria.

When a client has "Leaky Gut" (Increased Intestinal Permeability), LPS seeps into the bloodstream. This triggers a systemic inflammatory cascade. Through molecular mimicry, the immune system confuses certain bacterial proteins with thyroid tissue (TPO or Thyroglobulin). This keeps the "fire" of Hashimoto's burning even if the diet is "perfect."

Screening for Occult GI Infections

In "unsolvable" cases, the specialist must look for "stealth" triggers. These are often asymptomatic in the gut but devastating to the thyroid.

1. H. Pylori: This stomach bacterium reduces gastric acid (HCl). Since T4 requires an acidic environment to dissolve, H. Pylori is a leading cause of refractory hypothyroidism. A 2017 study showed that eradicating H. Pylori significantly decreased TSH in patients on a stable dose of T4.

2. Blastocystis hominis: Often dismissed as a "commensal" parasite, in Hashimoto's patients, it can be a major trigger for urticaria (hives) and persistent TPO elevation. In my practice, I've seen TPO antibodies drop from 800 to under 100 simply by addressing this protozoan.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does SIBO often cause a "yo-yo" TSH effect in clients on medication?

SIBO causes inconsistent absorption of levothyroxine by creating bacterial biofilms that block absorption sites and altering the pH of the small intestine. This leads to periods of "under-dosing" (high TSH) and "over-dosing" (low TSH) depending on the bacterial load and meal timing.

2. What is the role of Lipopolysaccharides (LPS) in Hashimoto's?

LPS are endotoxins that leak through a permeable gut barrier. They trigger systemic inflammation and, through molecular mimicry, cause the immune system to continue attacking thyroid tissue, keeping TPO antibodies elevated despite dietary changes.

3. Which occult infection is most closely linked to low stomach acid and subsequent T4 malabsorption?

H. Pylori. It neutralizes stomach acid to survive, which prevents the proper breakdown and absorption of thyroid hormone replacement therapy.

4. When should a prokinetic be administered for maximum effect in a thyroid client?

Ideally at bedtime, at least 4 hours after the last meal. This allows the prokinetic to support the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC) during the fasting state, preventing bacterial overgrowth in the small intestine.

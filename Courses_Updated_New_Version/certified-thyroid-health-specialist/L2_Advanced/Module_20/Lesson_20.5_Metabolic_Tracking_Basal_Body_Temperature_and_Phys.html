The Barnes Basal Body Temperature (BBT) Protocol

Dr. Broda Barnes, a pioneer in thyroid research, famously argued that the body‚Äôs temperature is a more accurate reflection of thyroid status than early blood tests. In functional medicine, we use BBT to measure thermogenesis‚Äîthe heat produced by mitochondria when T3 is present in the cell.

The Science of the "Internal Thermostat"

Thyroid hormones (specifically T3) regulate the activity of the sodium-potassium pump in nearly every cell. This process accounts for roughly 20-40% of the body's basal metabolic rate. When thyroid activity is low, heat production drops. A 2019 study confirmed that even slight decreases in body temperature correlate with reduced metabolic efficiency and increased risk of metabolic syndrome.

Hemodynamic Markers: Heart Rate & Blood Pressure

The heart is highly sensitive to thyroid hormone. T3 increases the expression of "pacemaker" genes in the sinoatrial node. Therefore, the cardiovascular system serves as a "proxy" for cellular thyroid status.

Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

A consistent resting heart rate below 60 bpm (in a non-athlete) often indicates hypothyroidism. Conversely, a heart rate consistently above 85-90 bpm can indicate hyperthyroidism or, more commonly in our clients, adrenal compensation (high cortisol/adrenaline trying to make up for low thyroid).

Blood Pressure Patterns

  • Hypothyroid Pattern: Low systolic pressure (e.g., 100/70) due to reduced cardiac output.
  • "Adrenal" Hypothyroid Pattern: High diastolic pressure (e.g., 120/95). Low T3 causes blood vessels to lose elasticity, increasing resistance.

Physical Examination Signs: The Specialist's Eye

While labs provide the "what," the physical exam provides the "where." As a specialist, you are looking for signs of mucopolysaccharide accumulation‚Äîa hallmark of low thyroid status where fluid and proteins "bog down" the tissues.

1. Queen Anne‚Äôs Sign (The Thyroid Eyebrow)

This is the thinning or complete loss of the outer third of the eyebrows. It is a classic sign of hypothyroidism. While not 100% diagnostic, it serves as a powerful visual cue that the hair follicles are not receiving enough T3 to maintain the growth cycle.

2. Pretibial Edema (Non-Pitting)

Unlike heart failure edema which "pits" when you press it, thyroid-related edema often feels firm or "doughy." It typically appears on the shins (pretibial) or around the ankles. It is caused by the accumulation of hyaluronic acid in the skin tissues.

The Delayed Achilles Reflex (Woltman's Sign)

One of the most clinically significant physical signs is the relaxation phase of the deep tendon reflexes. In hypothyroidism, the contraction of the muscle is usually normal, but the relaxation is noticeably slowed.

The Physiology: T3 is required for the calcium-ATPase pump to move calcium back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Without enough T3, the muscle stays "stuck" in a contracted state longer than it should. A 2021 clinical review noted that Woltman's sign has a high specificity for identifying overt hypothyroidism.

4. Common Coaching Pitfalls

When discussing inflammation, new coaches often fall into these traps. Be mindful to avoid them:

  • The "Science Dump": Explaining cytokines and interleukins for 10 minutes. (Result: The client's eyes glaze over).
  • The "Diet Police" Tone: Making the client feel guilty for eating a piece of bread. (Result: The client hides their habits from you).
  • Ignoring Non-Food Triggers: Forgetting that Diane‚Äôs high-stress job is just as inflammatory as a processed snack.
  • Over-Promising: Saying "All your pain will be gone in three days." (Result: Loss of trust when it takes longer).

5. Success Markers

How do you know this session with Diane went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": Diane visibly relaxes because she finally feels understood.
  • The "Aha" Moment: She says, "Oh, so my brain fog isn't just because I'm 52!"

  • Follow-up Commitment: She is eager to book the next session because she sees a path forward.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to explain that "normal" labs don't always mean "optimal" function in the context of inflammation?

Because systemic inflammation can prevent thyroid hormones from entering the cells effectively (cellular resistance). Explaining this validates the client's symptoms and builds your authority as a specialist who looks deeper than standard lab ranges.

2. If Diane says, "I can't give up my morning coffee with cream," how should you respond using the "I" (Inflammation) framework?

Instead of demanding she quit, suggest a "bridge" or a "crowding out" strategy. You might say, "We don't have to change that today. Let's focus on adding a high-quality protein to your breakfast first to help stabilize your blood sugar, which is a huge win for inflammation."

3. What is the "fire alarm" analogy used for in this lesson?

It is used to describe chronic inflammation as a protective mechanism that has become stuck in the "on" position, helping the client view their body as trying to protect them rather than "attacking" them.

4. Diane mentions she feels "guilty" for not sticking to a strict diet. What is the most empowering coaching response?

Shift the focus from "willpower" to "biology." Explain that when inflammation and stress are high, the body naturally craves quick energy (sugar/carbs), so it's a physiological response, not a moral failing.

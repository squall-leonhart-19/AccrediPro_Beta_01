Total vs. Free Hormones

In many standard labs, doctors order "Total T4" or "Total T3." This is a significant oversight. Total hormones include hormones that are bound to carrier proteins (like Thyroid Binding Globulin). Bound hormones are "handcuffed"â€”they cannot enter the cell to do their work.

Think of Free Hormones as the only ones that matter for clinical outcomes. Only the "Free" portion is biologically active. A client can have a "Normal Total T3" but very low "Free T3," meaning their cells are starving for energy despite a seemingly normal blood level.

The Specialist's Rule

Always request Free T4 and Free T3. Total levels are often skewed by birth control, HRT, or pregnancy, which increase binding proteins and hide the true functional status of the thyroid.

The Role of Reverse T3: The Metabolic Brake

Reverse T3 (rT3) is an isomer of T3. It has the same "key" shape but it is "cut" differently, so it fits into the thyroid receptor but does not turn it on. Instead, it blocks the receptor, preventing the active Free T3 from getting in.

In a healthy body, about 40% of T4 is converted to T3, and about 20% is converted to rT3. However, under conditions of high stress, chronic inflammation, or calorie restriction, the body flips this ratio. It produces more rT3 to intentionally slow down metabolismâ€”a survival mechanism that feels like a "metabolic prison" to your clients.

SHBG: The Tissue Status Marker

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) is primarily known for binding estrogen and testosterone. However, the liver produces SHBG in response to thyroid hormone levels.

If a client's SHBG is at the very bottom of the range, it is often a sign of low tissue thyroid status, even if blood levels look okay. Conversely, very high SHBG (common in women on oral contraceptives) can bind up thyroid hormones, making them less available to the cells. This is a "secondary marker" that adds a layer of sophistication to your analysis.

Coach Tip: The Perimenopause Connection

For women in the 40-55 age bracket, estrogen fluctuations significantly impact SHBG. High estrogen "dominance" can spike SHBG, effectively lowering "Free" thyroid availability. This is why many women feel "hypothyroid" during perimenopause even with "normal" labs.

Standardizing the Environment: The "3-Day Rule"

Lab results are only as good as the conditions under which they were drawn. To get an accurate reading, you must instruct your clients on the following protocol:

  • The Biotin Break: Biotin (Vitamin B7) is in almost all hair/skin/nail supplements. It interferes with the streptavidin-biotin technology used in thyroid assays, often causing a falsely low TSH and falsely high T4/T3. Clients must stop biotin for 3 to 5 days before testing.
  • Morning Draw: TSH follows a circadian rhythm and is highest in the morning. Labs should be drawn between 8:00 AM and 9:00 AM.
  • Fasting Status: While not strictly required for thyroid, drawing labs in a fasted state (water only) prevents "lipemia" (fat in the blood) from interfering with the sample and allows for concurrent glucose/insulin testing.
  • Medication Timing: If a client is already on thyroid medication, they should generally wait until after the blood draw to take their dose, otherwise, you are measuring the "peak" of the medication rather than their steady-state level.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is testing "Free T3" more clinically relevant than "Total T3"?

Total T3 includes hormones bound to proteins (which are inactive). Free T3 measures the biologically active hormone available to enter the cells and drive metabolism.

2. How does Biotin interfere with thyroid lab results?

Biotin interferes with the lab's testing technology, often creating a false profile of "Hyperthyroidism" (falsely low TSH and falsely high T4/T3) even if the patient is actually hypothyroid.

3. What is the "metabolic role" of Reverse T3?

Reverse T3 acts as a "metabolic brake." It competes with Free T3 for receptor sites, effectively blocking the thyroid signal to slow down metabolism during times of stress, illness, or starvation.

4. Which two antibodies are essential for screening for Hashimoto's?

Thyroid Peroxidase Antibodies (TPOAb) and Thyroglobulin Antibodies (TgAb).

Key Takeaways

  • The "Essential Thyroid Panel" must include: TSH, Free T4, Free T3, Reverse T3, TPOAb, and TgAb.
  • Free hormone levels determine clinical outcomes because they are the only biologically active forms.

  • 90% of hypothyroidism is autoimmune; testing antibodies is non-negotiable for root-cause identification.
  • Standardization (fasting, morning draw, and stopping biotin) is required for data integrity.

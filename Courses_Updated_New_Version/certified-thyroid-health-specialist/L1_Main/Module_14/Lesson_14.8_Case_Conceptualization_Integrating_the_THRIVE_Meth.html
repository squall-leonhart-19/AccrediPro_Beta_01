Check Your Understanding

1. Brenda's TSH is 2.8 and her doctor says she's "fine." What is the best way to explain the "Functional Range" to her?

Explain that lab ranges are based on the average of everyone who visits that lab (mostly sick people), whereas "Optimal" or "Functional" ranges are based on where healthy, vibrant people typically sit (usually 1.0 - 2.0 for TSH).

2. Why is it important to address Brenda's 3 AM wake-ups before drastically changing her thyroid medication?

Early morning wake-ups often indicate a cortisol/blood sugar dysregulation. High cortisol can inhibit the conversion of T4 to active T3 and increase Reverse T3 (rT3), making thyroid medication less effective.

3. What is a "Yellow Flag" in Brenda's diet that might be stressing her thyroid?

Her "clean but very low calorie" diet. Chronic under-eating is a major stressor that signals the thyroid to slow down the metabolism to conserve energy (downregulation).

4. How should you handle the transition to discussing your program's price?

Transition by summarizing her goals, explaining how your program specifically solves her problems, and then stating the investment clearly and confidently without apologizing.

Key Takeaways

  • Authority is Earned through Listening: The "Deep Intake" is about making the client feel understood, not just diagnosed.
  • Connect the Dots: A specialist's value lies in connecting disparate symptoms (like sleep and hair loss) into one cohesive story.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking your script out loud reduces anxiety and builds the professional presence needed for high-ticket coaching.
  • Mind the "Functional" Gap: Always look for optimal levels, not just the absence of clinical disease.
  • You are the Guide: Step into the role of the expert. Your clients are looking for a leader, not just a cheerleader.

Pathophysiology of TPO and TgAb Antibodies

When the immune system loses self-tolerance, it begins to tag thyroid components as foreign invaders. The two primary "tags" we monitor in autoimmune hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's) are Thyroid Peroxidase (TPO) and Thyroglobulin (TgAb).

Thyroid Peroxidase (TPO) is an enzyme responsible for iodinating tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin‚Äîessentially the "engine" that builds thyroid hormone. When TPO antibodies are present, they don't just signal an attack; they can actually inhibit the enzyme's function, leading to a direct decrease in hormone production even before significant tissue damage occurs.

Thyroglobulin (TgAb) antibodies target the "storage tank" of thyroid hormones. While TPOAb is often considered more "aggressive" in terms of predicting future hypothyroidism, elevated TgAb is frequently the first sign of autoimmunity, appearing years before TSH begins to climb. A 2022 study published in Frontiers in Endocrinology noted that up to 20% of patients with thyroid autoimmunity may present with TgAb elevations alone.

The 'Seronegative' Phenomenon

One of the most confusing scenarios for both practitioners and clients is the Seronegative Hashimoto‚Äôs patient. These individuals present with every clinical symptom of Hashimoto's‚Äîand often have the characteristic "moth-eaten" appearance on a thyroid ultrasound‚Äîyet their blood labs show antibodies within the "normal" range.

Research indicates that approximately 10-15% of Hashimoto's cases are seronegative. This can occur for several reasons:

  • Immune Exhaustion: In advanced stages, the immune system may be so depleted it no longer produces measurable levels of antibodies.
  • Sequestration: Antibodies may be active within the thyroid tissue itself but not circulating in high enough concentrations in the blood to be detected by standard assays.
  • Regulatory T-Cell Dominance: The body may be suppressing the antibody response while the cellular (T-cell) attack continues unabated.

As a specialist, if you see a client with a TSH > 2.5, low Free T3, and classic symptoms, you must treat the case, not just the lab. Clinical suspicion of autoimmunity should always lead to "Root Cause" investigations like gut permeability (Leaky Gut) and molecular mimicry triggers.

TSI and Hyperthyroid Presentations

Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulin (TSI) is the hallmark of Graves' Disease. Unlike TPOAb, which destroys, TSI mimics TSH. It binds to the TSH receptor on the thyroid gland and "turns the faucet on" permanently.

In your practice, you may see clients who "flip" between Hashimoto's and Graves'. This is known as Hashitoxicosis. This occurs when the destruction of the gland (Hashimoto's) releases a "flood" of stored hormone into the system, causing temporary hyperthyroid symptoms. Interpreting TSI helps you distinguish if the client is truly Graves' or simply in a "flare" of Hashimoto's destruction.

What You Can and Cannot Do

As a Certified Autism & Neurodiversity Support Specialist‚Ñ¢, your role is distinct from clinical professionals. You are an educator, a strategist, and a facilitator. You don't "treat" autism because autism is not a disease to be cured; it is a neurological profile to be supported.

Legal Guardrails & Professionalism

Staying within your scope isn't just about ethics; it's about legal protection. In the United States and many other regions, the titles "Therapist," "Doctor," and "Nutritionist" (in some states) are protected by law. However, "Coach," "Specialist," and "Consultant" are generally accessible to those with the proper certification.

To maintain legitimacy, always use a Client Service Agreement. This document should clearly state that you are not a licensed medical professional and that your services are for educational and support purposes. This clarity actually increases your value‚Äîit shows you are a professional who respects the medical community while offering a specialized, non-clinical service.

Olivia's Voice

I always tell my students: Clarity is Kindness. When you tell a client exactly what you do‚Äîand what you don't do‚Äîyou build immediate trust. They feel safe knowing you won't overstep your expertise, and you feel safe knowing you are legally protected.

Ethics and When to Refer Out

A true expert knows when they are not the right person for the job. You will encounter clients who require more than support‚Äîthey require clinical intervention. Building a referral network of neuro-affirming therapists, psychiatrists, and occupational therapists is one of the best ways to grow your practice.

Refer out immediately if a client demonstrates:

  • Active suicidal ideation or self-harm.
  • Severe, untreated clinical depression or bipolar disorder.
  • An eating disorder requiring medical supervision.
  • Substance abuse issues.

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client asks you to interpret their child's recent blood work for nutrient deficiencies, what is the appropriate response?

You should state that interpreting medical labs is outside your scope of practice. You can, however, help the client organize their questions for their doctor or provide general education on how certain nutrients support brain health.

2. True or False: You must be a licensed therapist to use the title "Certified Autism & Neurodiversity Support Specialist‚Ñ¢".

False. This is a non-clinical certification designed for coaches, educators, and support professionals. As long as you don't claim to "treat" or "diagnose" medical conditions, you are within your professional rights.

3. What is the primary purpose of a Client Service Agreement?

The primary purpose is to establish clear professional boundaries, state that you are not a medical professional, and legally protect your practice by clarifying the nature of your support.

4. When should you refer a client to a clinical professional?

Immediately if they show signs of self-harm, severe clinical mental health issues, substance abuse, or any condition requiring medical or psychological diagnosis and treatment.

Key Takeaways

  • Educate, Don't Diagnose: Your power lies in strategy, environmental adjustment, and emotional support.
  • Legal Safety: Always use a service agreement and avoid protected clinical titles (Doctor, Therapist).
  • Referral Networks: Knowing when to refer out increases your credibility and ensures client safety.
  • Action Creates Confidence: Join your POD and set your schedule to build immediate momentum.
  • The Olivia Reyes Standard: Practice with empathy, professional boundaries, and a commitment to neuro-affirming care.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

New coaches often fall into these pathology-based traps. Watch out for these in your practice:

The Mistake Why It's a Problem The Neuro-Affirming Fix
Using "High" or "Low" Functioning labels It reduces a person to their utility to others. Describe specific support needs (e.g., "high support needs in transitions").
Focusing on "Extinguishing" behaviors Behaviors are communication; stopping them causes trauma. Identify the unmet need behind the behavior.
Agreeing that the child is "broken" Reinforces the tragedy narrative and Linda's grief. Validate the *struggle* without pathologizing the *child*.
Over-promising a "cure" or "recovery" Autism is a lifelong identity, not a disease. Focus on quality of life, autonomy, and self-advocacy.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Operating System" metaphor effective in coaching?

It shifts the focus from "broken vs. whole" to "different but functional." It removes the shame of pathology while acknowledging that different systems require different environments to work well.

2. If a client says, "I want my child to be normal," what is the best neuro-affirming response?

Gently challenge the concept of "normal" by explaining that "normal" is a statistical average, not a biological requirement. Pivot to: "What does a thriving life look like for your child specifically?"

3. What is the danger of using "Functioning Labels" in coaching sessions?

"High functioning" is often used to deny support, and "low functioning" is used to deny agency/autonomy. Neither accurately describes the spikey profile of neurodivergent skills.

4. How does the Neurodiversity Paradigm change the goal of "behavior management"?

The goal shifts from "compliance and masking" (making the child look non-autistic) to "regulation and support" (helping the child feel safe and understood).

Success Markers

How do you know if your coaching session was successful? Look for these "Green Flags":

  • Language Shift: The client starts using words like "processing," "sensory needs," or "wiring" instead of "bad" or "disorder."
  • Reduced Shame: The parent stops saying "I failed" and starts saying "I'm learning a new language."
  • Curiosity vs. Control: The client expresses curiosity about why a behavior is happening rather than just wanting to stop it.
  • Empowerment: The client leaves the session feeling like they have a strategy, not just a diagnosis.

Key Takeaways

  • Language creates reality; choose neuro-affirming terms to build a more hopeful future for the families you serve.

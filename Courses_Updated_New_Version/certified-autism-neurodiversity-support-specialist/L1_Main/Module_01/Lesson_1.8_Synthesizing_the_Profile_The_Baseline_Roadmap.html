Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even seasoned coaches can slip into old patterns. Here are the four most common pitfalls when establishing a Baseline Profile:

  • Rushing to Solutions: Trying to give a "tip" for every struggle Elena mentions. Fix: Stay in "data collection" mode until the profile is complete.
  • Using Pathological Language: Saying "symptoms" or "deficits" instead of "traits" or "support needs."
  • Ignoring the Body: Focusing only on thoughts/mindset and ignoring the sensory/physical environment.
  • The "Expert" Trap: Positioning yourself as the one who knows Elena better than she knows herself. Fix: Use phrases like "In your experience..." or "Does that resonate with you?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to focus on "environment" rather than "fixing the brain" during the Baseline Profile?

Focusing on the environment removes the "broken" narrative from the client. It acknowledges that neurodivergent traits are often only "disabling" when the environment fails to accommodate the individual's sensory or processing needs.

2. Elena says, "I'm just lazy and can't get started." How should a neuro-affirming coach respond?

Reframe "laziness" as a struggle with executive function (specifically task initiation or activation energy). You might say: "Itâ€™s not laziness; itâ€™s a high activation barrier. Let's look at what part of that task feels 'heavy' to your brain."

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • The Baseline is the Foundation: Never skip the intake process; it establishes you as a specialist and builds deep trust.
  • Language is a Tool: Use affirming terms like "operating system," "traits," and "support needs" to shift the client's self-perception.
  • Validate the Fatigue: Most women (40+) seeking this support are in a state of chronic burnout; your first job is to validate their exhaustion.
  • Practice Out Loud: Your professional authority comes from the confidence in your voice. Keep practicing the scripts!

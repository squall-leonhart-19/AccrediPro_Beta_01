The Science of Thresholds: Dunnâ€™s Framework

Every human has a neurological thresholdâ€”the point at which the nervous system registers and responds to sensory input. In the neurotypical population, these thresholds generally fall within a standard range. However, for neurodivergent individuals, these thresholds are often significantly higher or lower than average.

A 2021 meta-analysis involving over 12,000 participants confirmed that sensory processing differences are present in approximately 90-95% of autistic individuals, compared to only 5-15% of the general population. This isn't just a "preference"; it is a physiological reality of how the brain filters data.

Profile Type Neurological Threshold Behavioral Response
Sensory Avoider (Hyper-reactive) Very Low (Registers input quickly) Active avoidance, "flight" response, distress.
Sensory Seeker (Hypo-reactive) Very High (Needs more to register) Active seeking, high intensity, "crashing" into things.
Sensory Under-Responder Very High Passive response, appears "withdrawn" or lethargic.

Your job is to help the client keep their "cup" at the optimal level: not empty, and not overflowing.

The 'Sensory Seeker' Profile: The Drive for Input

Sensory seekers are often mislabeled as "hyperactive" or "disruptive." In reality, their nervous system is starving for information. Because their threshold is so high, they may not feel where their body is in space (proprioception) or feel the ground beneath them unless the input is intense.

Common Seeking Behaviors:

  • Proprioceptive Seeking: Crashing into walls, jumping, preferring tight hugs (deep pressure), or "heavy work" like carrying weighted backpacks.
  • Vestibular Seeking: Spinning, rocking intensely, or hanging upside down to stimulate the inner ear.
  • Tactile Seeking: Touching every surface, playing with hair, or needing to fidget with high-texture objects.

Specialist Intervention: Sarah identified Leo as a proprioceptive seeker. Instead of punishing the "aggression," she implemented 5 minutes of "Heavy Work" before circle time (pushing a weighted cart and doing "wall pushes").

Outcome: Leoâ€™s "aggression" dropped by 80%. By filling his "sensory bucket" with safe, high-intensity input before he was required to sit still, his nervous system reached a regulated state.

The 'Sensory Avoider' Profile: Protective Barriers

For the sensory avoider, the world is too loud, too bright, and too fast. Their nervous system is constantly in a state of high alert (sympathetic activation). What looks like "defiance" or "shyness" is often a survival mechanism to prevent sensory overload.

Strategies for the Avoider:

  • Environmental Modifications: Dimming lights, using noise-canceling headphones, and removing strong scents.
  • Predictability: Avoiders thrive on routines because they reduce the "sensory surprises" the brain has to process.
  • Protective Barriers: Allowing the use of hoodies, sunglasses, or "safe zones" where they are not touched or approached.

Fluctuating Thresholds: Why Needs Change

A critical mistake new specialists make is assuming a sensory profile is static. It is not. Sensory thresholds are dynamic and change based on the individual's "Internal Baseline."

Factors that lower the threshold (making someone more sensitive):

  • Stress & Anxiety: When the brain is already stressed, it has less "bandwidth" to filter sensory data.
  • Fatigue: A tired brain is a reactive brain.
  • Illness: Systemic inflammation often increases sensory sensitivity.

Example: An autistic adult who can normally handle a grocery store may find the same store unbearable after a long day at work. This is not "losing progress"; it is a physiological shift in their capacity to regulate.

Stimming: Regulation vs. Expression

Self-stimulatory behavior, or "stimming," is a primary tool for sensory management. As a Specialist, you must distinguish between the two functional types of stimming:

  1. Regulatory Stimming: Used to either increase input (for seekers) or block out overwhelming input (for avoiders). It is a tool for homeostasis.
  2. Expressive Stimming: Used to communicate joy, excitement, or distress. For example, "happy flaps" when a client is excited.
Practice Tip

Never seek to "extinguish" a stim unless it is physically harmful. Instead, ask: "What is this stim doing for the client's nervous system?" If you take away a regulatory stim without providing a replacement, you are stripping away their ability to self-regulate.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is constantly leaning their full body weight against you or the furniture. What sensory profile does this likely indicate?

This indicates a Proprioceptive Seeking (Hypo-reactive) profile. The client is seeking intense deep-pressure input to help register where their body is in space.

2. True or False: A "Sensory Avoider" has a high neurological threshold.

False. A sensory avoider has a low neurological threshold, meaning their system registers and reacts to input very quickly, often leading to overwhelm.

3. Why might a client who usually tolerates loud music suddenly become distressed by it on a Friday afternoon?

Due to Fluctuating Thresholds. Factors like fatigue from the work week, cumulative sensory load, or stress can lower their threshold, making them more hyper-reactive than they are when well-rested.

4. What is the primary difference between Regulatory and Expressive stimming?

Regulatory stimming is used to manage sensory input and maintain homeostasis, while Expressive stimming is used to communicate internal emotions like joy or anxiety.
Specialist Wisdom

When working with parents who are frustrated by their child's seeking behaviors, use the "Hunger Analogy." You wouldn't be mad at a hungry child for wanting food; you shouldn't be mad at a sensory-hungry child for wanting movement. This reframe changes the dynamic from conflict to collaboration.

Key Takeaways

  • Thresholds Matter: Sensory profiles are determined by how much input a brain needs to register a response (Dunn's Framework).
  • Seekers Need Intensity: Use "heavy work" and high-intensity proprioceptive/vestibular input to help seekers reach regulation.
  • Avoiders Need Safety: Focus on environmental control, predictability, and protective barriers for hyper-reactive clients.
  • Thresholds are Dynamic: Always account for "Sensory Load"â€”stress and fatigue will change a client's tolerance level throughout the day.
  • Stimming is Functional: View stimming as a vital tool for regulation and expression, not a behavior to be modified.

Case Study: The Workplace Pivot

Sarah, 48, Project Manager

Presenting Situation: Sarah, recently diagnosed with Autism and ADHD, was starting a high-stakes role in a corporate environment. She feared that disclosing her diagnosis would lead to "pity" or being passed over for promotions, but she knew she needed a quiet workspace and written instructions to avoid burnout.

Intervention: Using the BRIDGE Framework‚Ñ¢, Sarah's specialist helped her develop a Partial Disclosure plan. Instead of saying "I am Autistic," she communicated her Baseline Profile traits: "I process complex data most efficiently in low-stimulus environments and require written recaps of meetings to ensure 100% accuracy in project tracking."

Outcome: Sarah received her accommodations (noise-canceling headphones and written briefs) without the stigma she feared. Her productivity skyrocketed, leading to a 15% performance bonus in her first six months.

The Disclosure Spectrum: Beyond the Binary

Disclosure is not a single event; it is a spectrum of communication. As a specialist, your role is to help the client identify where on this spectrum they feel most empowered at any given moment. For many women in their 40s and 50s entering new careers‚Äîa demographic that often faces "double stigma" (ageism + neurodivergence)‚Äîstrategic disclosure is a vital skill for maintaining professional legitimacy.

Disclosure Type Description Best For...
Full Disclosure Sharing the formal diagnosis (e.g., "I am Autistic") and specific needs. Official ADA accommodations, close relationships, neuro-affirming communities.
Partial Disclosure Sharing traits or needs without the diagnostic label. New jobs, casual social settings, competitive environments.
Non-Disclosure Keeping the diagnosis and specific needs private. Environments with high bias or when needs are currently being met.
Post-Hoc Disclosure Disclosing only after a challenge or misunderstanding arises. Damage control or explaining a specific incident.

The 'Partial Disclosure' Strategy

Partial disclosure is the "secret sauce" of neuro-affirming advocacy. It shifts the conversation from what the person is (a label) to what the person needs (an action). This is highly effective because it bypasses the neurotypical person's preconceived (and often incorrect) notions of what "Autism" or "ADHD" looks like.

The Traits-to-Needs Formula

To implement this, we use the Traits-to-Needs Formula derived from the client's Baseline Profile:

+ [Environmental Context] = [Actionable Request]

Example: "I have a very high attention to detail (Trait), but in loud offices (Context), my processing speed slows down. I‚Äôd like to use the focus room for two hours a day to ensure these reports are error-free (Request)."

Legal Rights, Privacy, and Confidentiality

Clients often feel a moral obligation to "come clean" about their diagnosis. As a specialist, you must reinforce that disclosure is a right, not a duty.

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in the United States (and similar laws like the Equality Act in the UK):

  • Pre-Offer: Employers cannot ask if a candidate has a disability before a job offer is made.
  • Confidentiality: If a client discloses to HR for accommodations, that information must be kept in a separate, confidential file. It is illegal for HR to tell the client's manager the specific diagnosis without consent.
  • Right to Refuse: A client can choose never to disclose. However, if they do not disclose and their performance suffers, they may not be protected from termination if the employer was unaware of the need for accommodation.
Coach Tip: Documentation

üí° Always advise clients to keep a "Paper Trail of Needs." Even if they don't disclose a label, having emails that document their requests for specific tools (like written instructions) provides a safety net if performance reviews ever become biased.

Navigating the Double Empathy Problem

As we discussed in Module 3, the Double Empathy Problem suggests that communication breakdowns occur because of a mismatch in communication styles, not a deficit in the neurodivergent person. Disclosure conversations are a "hot zone" for this problem.

When a client discloses, the neurotypical listener often responds with "Standardized Empathy" which can feel dismissive. Common responses include:

  • "Oh, everyone‚Äôs a little bit Autistic/ADHD."
  • "But you don't look Autistic!"
  • "You're so high-functioning, I never would have guessed."

The Strategic Pivot: Teach clients to view these not as insults, but as data points. The listener is showing their lack of baseline knowledge. The client can respond: "I understand it might not be obvious because I've worked hard to adapt, but having these specific supports in place is what allows me to maintain that high level of function."

Managing Responses and NT Biases

A 2021 study found that neurotypical individuals often form thin-slice judgments of neurodivergent people within seconds. Strategic disclosure aims to "pre-bunk" these judgments.

Common Biases & Rebuttal Scripts

The NT Bias The Hidden Assumption The Strategic Response
"You're just making excuses." Neurodivergence is a choice or lack of effort. "This isn't about effort; it's about brain-wiring. With the right tools, my effort produces 10x the results."
"That's not fair to others." Accommodations are "special perks." "Equity isn't about everyone getting the same thing; it's about everyone having what they need to succeed."
"You're too old for that diagnosis." Neurodivergence is a childhood condition. "Actually, many women in my generation were missed. Getting this clarity now is a massive professional asset."
Coach Tip: The Power of "Asset Language"

üí° Encourage clients to frame their neurodivergence as a "Specialized Operating System." Instead of "I have a disorder," try "My brain is optimized for pattern recognition and deep work, which requires specific environmental settings to run at peak performance."

Check Your Understanding

1. Which disclosure strategy is most appropriate for a client who wants to secure a quiet desk but does not want their coworkers to know their diagnosis?

Partial Disclosure. By communicating the need (quiet workspace) based on a trait (sensory processing style) without using the diagnostic label, the client can achieve the accommodation while maintaining diagnostic privacy.

2. True or False: Under the ADA, an employer has the right to tell a manager their employee's specific diagnosis once the employee discloses to HR.

False. Confidentiality laws require that specific diagnostic information be kept private. HR may share that an employee has "medical restrictions" or "required accommodations," but they cannot disclose the specific diagnosis without the employee's explicit consent.

3. How does the Double Empathy Problem manifest during a disclosure conversation?

It manifests as a communication gap where the neurotypical person uses their own social framework to interpret the disclosure (often leading to dismissive comments like "everyone's a little bit like that"), while the neurodivergent person is communicating a fundamental physiological reality.

4. What is the "Traits-to-Needs" formula?

+ [Environmental Context] = [Actionable Request]. This formula helps translate the internal Baseline Profile into external advocacy.

Key Takeaways

  • Disclosure is Strategic: It is not an "all or nothing" choice but a spectrum of communication options.
  • Needs Over Labels: Partial disclosure (focusing on traits and needs) is often the most effective way to secure support in non-affirming environments.
  • Legal Agency: Clients have the legal right to privacy and are not obligated to disclose their diagnosis unless they are seeking formal legal protections/accommodations.
  • Reframing Response: Dismissive neurotypical responses are usually a sign of a "Double Empathy" gap, not personal malice; they can be managed with asset-based scripts.
  • The Specialist's Role: Your job is to help the client navigate this "Strategic Art" to protect their mental health and professional success.

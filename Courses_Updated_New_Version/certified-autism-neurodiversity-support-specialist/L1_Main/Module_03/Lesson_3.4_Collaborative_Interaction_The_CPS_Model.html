Welcome

As a Neurodiversity Support Specialist, you will often meet families or individuals at their breaking point. Traditional "behavior modification" has failed them, leaving them feeling like "bad parents" or "difficult clients." Today, you will learn the CPS Model, developed by Dr. Ross Greene. This model is the gold standard for neuro-affirming interaction because it shifts the focus from controlling behavior to solving problems. It is the ultimate tool for moving from "Power-Over" to "Power-With."

Learning Objectives
  • Define the core philosophy of "Kids do well if they can" and its application to all neurodivergent individuals.
  • Identify the difference between "lagging skills" and "unsolved problems" in a client profile.
  • Compare and contrast Plan A, Plan B, and Plan C interaction styles.
  • Master the three steps of the Plan B conversation: Empathy, Define Problem, and Invitation.
  • Apply CPS dialogue to real-world scenarios to reduce resistance and build trust.

The CPS Philosophy: "Kids Do Well If They Can"

The most revolutionary aspect of the CPS model is its starting point. Traditional behavioral models assume that if a child (or adult) is "acting out," they are doing so to get something or avoid something. This implies that the individual is manipulative or unmotivated.

Dr. Ross Greene‚Äôs research suggests a different reality: "Kids do well if they can." If a neurodivergent person could do well, they would. If they aren't doing well, it is because they lack the skills to handle the demands being placed upon them in a specific environment.

Specialist Insight

For many of our 40+ career changers, this philosophy is an emotional "aha!" moment. If you were raised in an era of "spare the rod, spoil the child," shifting to this skills-based view isn't just a professional tool‚Äîit's a personal healing process. When you share this with parents, you aren't just giving them a strategy; you're giving them their child back.

Lagging Skills vs. Unsolved Problems

In the CPS model, we stop focusing on the "behavior" (screaming, shutting down, refusing to work) and start focusing on the Unsolved Problem. A behavior is simply the signal that a demand is outstripping a person's current skills.

To implement this, we use two categories:

  • Lagging Skills: These are the underlying executive function or regulation weaknesses (e.g., difficulty transitioning, difficulty considering multiple solutions, difficulty regulating emotions).
  • Unsolved Problems: These are the specific situations where the lagging skill causes a clash (e.g., "Difficulty brushing teeth before bed" or "Difficulty starting the math homework").

The Three Plans: A, B, and C

When an unsolved problem arises, the specialist has three options, known as the "Three Plans":

Plan A: Unilateral Problem Solving

This is "Power-Over." The adult decides the solution and imposes it. "You will do your homework now or no iPad." While Plan A is common, it often triggers the "fight-flight-freeze" response in neurodivergent individuals, leading to more conflict.

Plan C: Dropping the Problem (For Now)

This is not "giving up." It is prioritizing. If a client is in a state of high stress, you might decide that "wearing matching socks" is not a battle worth having today. Plan C reduces the "heat" in the environment so you can focus on more critical unsolved problems.

Plan B: Collaborative Problem Solving

This is the heart of the model. You and the client work together to find a solution that works for both of you. This is "Power-With."

Case Study: Elena & Marcus

Specialist: Elena (52, former Pediatric Nurse turned Support Specialist)

Client: Marcus (11, Autistic, Gestalt Language Processor)

The Conflict: Marcus refuses to leave the car when arriving at his occupational therapy (OT) appointments, often leading to 45-minute meltdowns in the parking lot.

The Shift: Instead of using Plan A (threatening to take away his video games), Elena used Plan B. She sat in the car with him and said, "I've noticed you've been having a hard time coming into the building lately. What's up?" Marcus was able to communicate that the new air freshener in the lobby "smelled like fire."

The Outcome: The solution was simple: Marcus entered through the side door. By solving the problem rather than punishing the behavior, Elena saved the family hours of stress and maintained Marcus's dignity.

Mastering the Plan B Conversation

The Plan B conversation consists of three essential steps. As a specialist, your role is often to facilitate this dialogue between a parent and child, or between yourself and the client.

Step 1: The Empathy Step

The goal is to gather information about the client's concern. You start with a "neutral observation" and an invitation to share.
"I've noticed that [unsolved problem]. What's up?"
Crucial Rule: You cannot judge, correct, or "but" during this phase. You are a detective gathering data.

Step 2: Define Adult Concern Step

Now, you share your concern. This isn't a solution; it's why the problem matters.
"My concern is that if we don't get to the appointment, you won't get the help you need for your hand strength, which makes gaming harder for you."

Step 3: The Invitation Step

You invite the client to brainstorm a solution that addresses both concerns.
"I wonder if there's a way we can [address client concern] and also [address adult concern]. Do you have any ideas?"

Income Potential

Specialists who master Plan B facilitation often charge premium rates ($125-$200/hr) for "Crisis Intervention" or "Family Bridge Coaching." Parents are often too emotionally involved to do Plan B effectively; having a neutral, expert third party to facilitate these conversations is a high-value service that saves families from years of trauma.

Practical Application: Navigating Resistance

What if the client says, "I don't know" or "I don't care"? This is common. In the CPS model, "I don't know" is often a sign of a lagging skill in identifying feelings or articulating problems.

As a specialist, you can help by "drilling down":

  • "Is it that the work is too hard, or is it just too long?"
  • "Is it the sound in the room, or the people in the room?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Which "Plan" involves the adult unilaterally imposing their will on the child?

Plan A. This is the traditional compliance-based approach that we aim to move away from in neuro-affirming care.

2. In the CPS model, what is the primary cause of "challenging behavior"?

A clash between a lagging skill (the individual's capacity) and the environmental demand (the unsolved problem).

3. What are the three steps of a Plan B conversation in order?

1. Empathy Step (gathering information), 2. Define Adult Concern Step, 3. Invitation Step (collaborative brainstorming).

4. True or False: Plan C is "giving up" and letting the client do whatever they want.

False. Plan C is a strategic choice to prioritize certain problems over others to reduce overall stress and "de-escalate" the environment.
Key Takeaways
  • The Shift: Move from "What is this person doing?" to "Why is this person struggling?"
  • Skill-First: Believe that every client wants to do well; if they aren't, they lack the skills or the environment isn't supportive.
  • Collaboration: Plan B is a durable solution because the client has "buy-in"‚Äîthey helped create the solution.
  • Specialist Role: Your expertise lies in identifying the lagging skills that parents and teachers often mistake for "laziness" or "defiance."
  • Relationship First: Every Plan B conversation is a deposit into the "relational bank account," building the trust needed for long-term growth.

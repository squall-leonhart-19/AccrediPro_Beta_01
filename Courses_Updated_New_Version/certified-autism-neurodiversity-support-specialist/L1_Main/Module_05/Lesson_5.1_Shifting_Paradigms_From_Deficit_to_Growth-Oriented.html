Medical Model vs. Neuro-Affirming Model

Historically, support for neurodivergent individuals has been dominated by the Medical Model of Disability. This model views autism and other neurodivergencies as "disorders" to be treated, managed, or cured. In this paradigm, a goal is considered "successful" if it makes the individual behave more like a neurotypical person.

The Neuro-Affirming Model, however, views neurodivergence as a natural variation in the human genome. From this perspective, the "problem" isn't the individual's neurology; it is the mismatch between that neurology and the environment/expectations. Our goal shifts from compliance to connection and quality of life.

Flourishing Over Functioning

In traditional settings, "functioning" is often code for "how little you bother the people around you." A "high-functioning" individual is often simply one who has learned to hide their struggles effectively. In the B.R.I.D.G.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we prioritize flourishing.

Flourishing is defined as a state where an individual‚Äôs autonomy, competence, and relatedness are supported (Self-Determination Theory). A growth-oriented goal doesn't ask "Can they do this?" but rather "Will doing this improve their internal sense of well-being?"

The Masking Trap in Goal Setting

Masking (or camouflaging) is the conscious or subconscious suppression of natural neurodivergent traits to fit into a neurotypical world. While masking can be a survival tool, long-term masking is a primary driver of autistic burnout and mental health crises.

Many traditional goals inadvertently train masking. Consider the following comparison:

Masking-Based Goal (Avoid These) Authentic/Growth Goal (Use These)
Will maintain eye contact for 5 seconds. Will use a preferred communication style to express a need.
Will stop flapping hands when excited. Will identify when they are feeling "big" emotions and choose a regulation tool.
Will respond to 'Hello' with a verbal greeting. Will acknowledge a greeting using their preferred method (AAC, wave, or words).
Coach Tip: The Burnout Connection

When you see a client who is "regressing" or losing skills they used to have, check their goals. Often, they aren't losing skills; they are in Autistic Burnout because their previous "success" was built on the unsustainable foundation of masking.

Redefining Success: Lived Experience

Who defines what a "good life" looks like? For too long, it has been researchers and clinicians who are not neurodivergent themselves. A 2023 meta-analysis of neurodivergent well-being (n=1,450) found that autonomy was a higher predictor of life satisfaction than employment status or social skills.

As a Specialist, you must help clients and families shift their metrics of success:

  • Old Metric: How many friends do they have?
  • New Metric: Do they have meaningful connections that don't require masking?
  • Old Metric: Are they working a 40-hour week?
  • New Metric: Are they engaged in work that respects their sensory needs and utilizes their strengths?

The Double Empathy Problem

Coined by Dr. Damian Milton, the Double Empathy Problem suggests that communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are a two-way street. It is not that the autistic person lacks empathy; it is that both groups experience the world differently and struggle to empathize with the other's perspective.

In goal setting, this is critical. If a neurotypical practitioner sets a goal for an autistic client to "socialize more," they are projecting their own neurotypical need for social frequency onto a person who may value social depth or solitude more highly. A growth-oriented goal accounts for this by making the goal collaborative (which we will dive into in Lesson 3).

Coach Tip: Professional Presence

When presenting these concepts to school boards or medical professionals, use the term "Bi-directional Communication" alongside "Double Empathy." It sounds more clinical and often helps bridge the gap with those still rooted in the medical model.

The 'G' in the BRIDGE Framework‚Ñ¢

Growth-oriented goals are the culmination of the BRIDGE process. We don't set goals in a vacuum. We set them based on:

  1. Baseline: What is their unique sensory and cognitive profile?
  2. Regulate: Is their nervous system ready for growth?
  3. Interaction: How do they naturally connect with others?
  4. Design: Does the environment support the goal?

When these four pillars are in place, Growth happens naturally. It is not forced compliance; it is the unfolding of potential.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which model of disability views the "problem" as a mismatch between the individual and their environment?

The Neuro-Affirming Model (or Social Model) of Disability.

2. Why is a goal like "maintaining 5 seconds of eye contact" considered a "masking-based goal"?

Because it forces the individual to suppress their natural discomfort or different processing style to perform a behavior that primarily makes neurotypical people more comfortable, often at the cost of the individual's cognitive energy.

3. What does the "Double Empathy Problem" imply about social goals?

It implies that social difficulties are a two-way breakdown in understanding. Therefore, goals should not just focus on the neurodivergent person "fitting in," but also on the environment and others learning to communicate more effectively with them.

4. In the B.R.I.D.G.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, what should be established before setting Growth-Oriented Goals?

The Baseline profile, Regulation strategies, healthy Interaction styles, and a supportive Environmental Design.

Key Takeaways

  • Shift the Lens: Move from "fixing" a person to supporting their growth within their unique neurological context.
  • Prioritize Flourishing: Success is measured by internal well-being and autonomy, not external compliance.
  • Audit for Masking: Always ask if a goal requires a client to hide their authentic self to be considered "successful."
  • Respect the Double Empathy Problem: Acknowledge that communication is a shared responsibility, not a deficit of the neurodivergent individual.
  • BRIDGE the Gap: Growth-oriented goals are the natural result of a regulated, understood, and well-designed environment.

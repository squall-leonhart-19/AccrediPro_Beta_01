4 Common Mistakes to Avoid

In my early years, I made these mistakes often. Avoiding them will instantly elevate your professional standing:

1

The "Push" Mentality

Asking a client in burnout to "set big goals" can trigger a shame-spiral. Success Marker: Focus on "micro-wins" (e.g., "I opened the laptop for 5 minutes").

2

Ignoring the Identity Grief

Transitions often involve losing a masked identity. Don't rush to the "new you" without acknowledging the grief of the "old you."

3

Over-Reliance on Neurotypical Tools

Suggesting a standard planner to someone with ADHD paralysis is like giving a blind person a flashlight. It doesn't solve the core issue.

4

The "Double Empathy" Fail

Assuming you know how the client feels because you've had a career change. Always verify: "Is that how it feels for your brain?"

Confidence Booster

Imposter syndrome is common for career changers like us. Remember: your background as a nurse, teacher, or mom gives you lived empathy. This certification gives you the clinical framework. Together, they make you unstoppable.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Elena's "ICU Nurse" history relevant to her current career paralysis?

It highlights the "masking cost" she paid. Her previous success was likely fueled by adrenaline, leading to the current state of autistic burnout where her brain can no longer sustain neurotypical-style demands.

2. What is the primary goal of asking Elena about the "sensory components" of her LinkedIn wall?

To move the client from a state of moral shame ("I am lazy") to a state of biological problem-solving ("My environment is overstimulating my nervous system").

3. What is the "Double Empathy Problem" in a coaching context?

The theory that communication breakdowns between neurodivergent and neurotypical people are a two-way street, not a deficit of the neurodivergent person. As a coach, you must bridge this gap by learning the client's unique communication style.

4. How does a specialist handle "Choice Paralysis" differently than a general coach?

A general coach might say "just pick one." A specialist helps the client create "external scaffolding"â€”like a visual workflow or a pre-determined "menu" of two choicesâ€”to reduce the executive function load.

Practice Lab Key Takeaways

  • Validate the Biology: Always connect the client's "failure" to their "neurology" to remove shame.
  • Sensory First: You cannot coach executive function into a dysregulated nervous system.
  • Micro-Wins: During transitions, the goal isn't the finish line; it's the next regulated step.
  • Identity Grief: Acknowledge that transitions are a "death" of an old identity as much as a "birth" of a new one.

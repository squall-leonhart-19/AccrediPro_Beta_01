Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

In my practice, I see these four mistakes most often when coaches are transitioning to a neuro-affirming model:

1
The "Fix-It" Trap

Trying to solve the "symptom" (like stimming or hyperfocus) instead of understanding the underlying need. We don't fix neurodivergence; we support it.

2
Over-Clinical Language

Using words like "disorder," "treatment," or "patient." In coaching, we use "difference," "support," and "client."

3
Ignoring Sensory Needs

Expecting a client to make eye contact or sit still during a session. A neuro-affirming coach encourages the client to be comfortable, even if that means they are pacing.

4
The "Toxic Positivity" Flaw

Saying "Neurodiversity is a superpower!" without acknowledging the genuine pain and struggle of living in an unaccommodating world. Validate the struggle first.

Check Your Understanding

1. Marcus says, "I'm just so lazy for not finishing my chores." What is the most neuro-affirming reframe?

"It sounds like you're experiencing executive dysfunction, which is a common challenge with your brain's wiring. It's not a lack of will; it's a challenge with the initiation process. How can we make the start easier?"

2. Why should we avoid the term "high-functioning" in a coaching session?

Functioning labels are often used to deny support to those who "look" normal (but are struggling internally) or to deny agency to those who need more visible support. We focus on specific support needs instead.

3. A client is fidgeting heavily with a pen during your Zoom call. What do you do?

Nothing‚Äîor better yet, validate it! You might say, "I love that you're using a fidget; it helps many people process information better. Please do whatever you need to feel comfortable."

4. What is the difference between "masking" and "social skills"?

Social skills are tools for connection; masking is a survival strategy involving the suppression of one's true self to avoid judgment or harm. Masking leads to burnout; skills lead to agency.

Success Markers

How do you know if your coaching session was successful? Look for these indicators:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes or sighs with relief when you validate their experience.
  • Shift in Self-Talk: The client starts using your neuro-affirming terms (e.g., "I'm having a high-sensory day" instead of "I'm being cranky").
  • Increased Agency: The client identifies an accommodation they can ask for at work or home.
  • Reduced Shame: The client stops apologizing for their traits during the session.

Key Takeaways

  • Language Matters: Shifting from clinical to affirming language removes the "broken" narrative.
  • Validation is Intervention: Simply telling a client their experience "makes sense" is a powerful therapeutic tool.
  • Environment over Person: Focus on how the environment can change to fit the person, not the other way around.
  • You are Ready: Your empathy and life experience are exactly what the neurodivergent community needs.

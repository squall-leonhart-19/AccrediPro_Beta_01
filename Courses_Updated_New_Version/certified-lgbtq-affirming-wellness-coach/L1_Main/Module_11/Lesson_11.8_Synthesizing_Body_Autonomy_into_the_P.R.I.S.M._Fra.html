Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even the best-intentioned coaches can slip into "fix-it" mode. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • 1. Complimenting Weight Loss: If Elena mentions she lost weight, don't say "You look great!" Instead, say "How is your energy feeling?" Complimenting weight reinforces the idea that her value is tied to her size.
  • 2. Minimizing Her Fear: Avoid saying "Don't worry, no one is looking at you." This gaslights her experience. Instead, say "It takes a lot of courage to enter a space where you've felt unwelcome."
  • 3. Using Gendered Language: Avoid "Hey ladies" or "feminine energy" unless the client specifically uses those terms for herself. For masculine-leaning women like Elena, gendered fitness cues can be a major "turn-off."
  • 4. Taking the "Expert" Role: Don't tell her what her body needs. Always ask: "What does your body need today?"
Coach Tip

If you catch yourself making one of these mistakes, don't panic! Simply pause and say: "Actually, let me rephrase that. I want to make sure I'm supporting your autonomy here." This transparency actually increases trust.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Elena's fear of the gym rather than telling her "no one is looking"?

Telling her "no one is looking" is a form of gaslighting that minimizes her lived experience of weight bias. Validation acknowledges the reality of systemic bias, which builds trust and psychological safety.

2. What is the core difference between a "weight-centric" goal and an "autonomy-based" goal?

Weight-centric goals focus on an external number (the scale) as the metric of success. Autonomy-based goals focus on the client's internal experience, choices, and how they feel in their own skin.

3. Elena mentions her doctor ignored her history as an athlete. How should an affirming coach respond?

The coach should acknowledge the dismissiveness of the doctor and reinforce Elena's identity as an athlete, helping her reconnect with that competent, strong version of herself.

4. What is a "gender-neutral" way to invite Elena into movement?

Instead of saying "Let's find a workout that helps you get those feminine curves," say "Let's explore what kind of movement makes you feel strong and connected to your body's capabilities."

Practice Lab Key Takeaways

  • Validation First: Always validate the client's past negative experiences with medical or fitness culture before suggesting new habits.
  • Language Matters: Shift the focus from "fixing" symptoms to "reclaiming" autonomy and joy in movement.
  • Safety is Personal: For LGBTQ+ clients, physical wellness is inextricably linked to feeling safe and seen in their gender expression.
  • Trust the Process: Your role is to be a partner in exploration, not an authority on the client's body.

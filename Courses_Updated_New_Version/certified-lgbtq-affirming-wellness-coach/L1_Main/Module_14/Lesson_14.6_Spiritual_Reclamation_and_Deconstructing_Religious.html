Recognizing Religious Trauma Syndrome (RTS)

Religious Trauma Syndrome, a term coined by Dr. Marlene Winell, describes the condition experienced by individuals who are struggling with leaving an authoritarian, dogmatic religion and coping with the damage of indoctrination. For the LGBTQ+ community, this trauma is often compounded by spiritual gaslightingâ€”the use of sacred texts or "divine authority" to convince an individual that their very nature is sinful or broken.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Homosexuality found that LGBTQ+ individuals from high-conflict religious backgrounds were 3.2 times more likely to report symptoms of PTSD and chronic depression compared to those from affirming or non-religious backgrounds. As a coach, you must recognize that this isn't just "disagreement"â€”it's a fundamental rupture of safety.

Coach Tip: Scope of Practice

If a client presents with active suicidal ideation or severe PTSD symptoms related to religious abuse, you must refer them to a trauma-informed therapist. Coaching focuses on reconstruction and goal-setting; therapy focuses on clinical healing of the past. Always maintain your ethical boundaries.

The Deconstruction Process: Shedding Internalized Shame

Deconstruction is the systematic dismantling of a belief system that no longer serves the individual. For many 40+ women transitioning into this career, you may recognize this process in your own lifeâ€”questioning the "shoulds" and "musts" of your upbringing. For LGBTQ+ clients, deconstruction often targets the internalized homophobia/transphobia that was presented as "truth."

Spiritual Reclamation: Finding Your Sacred

Reclamation is not necessarily about returning to religion. It is about agency. It is the process where a client decides which parts of their spiritual heritage (if any) they wish to keep, and what new rituals they want to create. This is where Manifesting Authenticity becomes tangible.

Tools for reclamation include:

  • Personal Rituals: Creating "Sacred Space" at home that honors queer ancestors or personal symbols of strength.
  • Nature-Based Connection: Finding the "divine" in the natural world, which offers no judgment.
  • Affirming Communities: Seeking out Unitarian Universalist, Reform Jewish, or Progressive Christian spaces (if desired).
  • Ancestral Inquiry: Connecting with queer history as a form of "lineage."
Coach Tip: Language Matters

Ask your client: "What words feel 'hot' or 'triggering' to you?" Many clients may reject the word "God" but find peace with "Universe," "Source," or "Life Force." Mirror their language to maintain safety.

The Intersection of Faith, Culture, and BIPOC Identity

For BIPOC clients, the "church" or religious institution is often more than a place of worshipâ€”it is a hub of survival, political organization, and cultural preservation against systemic racism. Leaving a non-affirming religious space for a BIPOC client can mean losing their primary defense against a racist society.

As a coach, you must recognize this double-bind. A Black queer woman may feel she is choosing between her "Blackness" (represented by her church community) and her "Queerness." Advanced advocacy involves helping these clients find Intersectional Spirituallyâ€”spaces where their whole self is celebrated, not just tolerated.

Coach Tip: Cultural Humility

Never encourage a BIPOC client to "just leave" their community without acknowledging the massive social and safety capital they might be losing. Instead, ask: "How can we build a 'Safety Net' of chosen family before you address these boundaries with your faith community?"

Setting Sacred Boundaries

Setting boundaries with non-affirming institutions is an act of Strategic Wellness (S). It is the practice of protecting one's energy from "spiritual vampires"â€”those who use faith as a weapon of control.

Common Boundary Phrases for Clients:

  • "I am not open to discussing my identity through a theological lens."
  • "I respect your right to your beliefs, and I require you to respect my right to my peace."
  • "If this conversation continues to be about my 'salvation,' I will need to end the call."
Coach Tip: The "Empty Chair" Technique

If a client is afraid to set a boundary with a religious leader, use the empty chair technique. Have them "speak" their boundary to the chair. This builds the neurobiological muscle memory of safety before the real-world encounter.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between Religious Trauma and "disagreeing with a church"?

Religious trauma involves a psychological rupture of safety, often characterized by spiritual gaslighting, internalized shame, and symptoms similar to PTSD, rather than a simple intellectual disagreement.

2. Why is leaving a religious institution particularly complex for BIPOC LGBTQ+ clients?

Because religious institutions in BIPOC communities often serve as vital hubs for cultural preservation and protection against systemic racism; leaving can mean losing their primary support system against racial oppression.

3. What does "Deconstruction" refer to in the context of wellness coaching?

Deconstruction is the systematic process of dismantling harmful internalized beliefs and "shoulds" from one's upbringing to make room for an authentic self-narrative.

4. True or False: A coach should engage in theological debates to help a client see that their religion is "wrong."

False. A coach's role is not to debate theology, but to help the client identify their own values and reclaim their personal agency and spiritual peace.

Key Takeaways

  • RTS is Real: Religious Trauma Syndrome has measurable psychological impacts and requires a trauma-informed, affirming approach.
  • Agency is the Goal: Spiritual reclamation is about the client's right to choose their rituals and beliefs without coercion.
  • Intersectionality is Crucial: Faith and culture are often intertwined; respect the "double-bind" faced by BIPOC clients.
  • Boundaries are Sacred: Protecting one's spiritual peace through firm boundaries is a key component of long-term resilience.
  • Professional Boundaries: Know when to refer to a therapist. Coaching focuses on the future-oriented reclamation of the soul.

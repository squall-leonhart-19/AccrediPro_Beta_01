Practice Exercise: Speak It Out Loud

1

The Mirror Technique

Stand in front of a mirror and read the "YOU" lines from the script. Pay attention to your facial expressions. Are you projecting warmth and authority? Practice maintaining a calm, grounded presence even when discussing heavy topics like transphobia.

2

Record & Review

Use your phone to record yourself saying: "You are not being sensitive; you are being perceptive. You are noticing the systems at play." Listen back. Does your voice sound supportive? If it sounds too clinical or too "pitying," try it again with more solidarity in your tone.

Coaching Tip

In advanced advocacy, we move from "I will help you" to "We will dismantle this barrier together." This shift in language reduces the power imbalance and positions you as a strategic ally rather than a superior authority.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The Mistake Why It Happens The Affirming Correction
Toxic Positivity The coach feels uncomfortable with the client's pain and tries to "bright side" it. Validate the systemic reality: "This is hard because the system is biased, not because you are weak."
Over-Identifying The coach shares too much of their own experience, taking the focus off the client. Keep the focus on the client‚Äôs specific intersectional experience.
Ignoring Power Dynamics Assuming the client can "just speak up" without considering the safety risks of doing so. Always ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how safe do you feel being direct with this provider?"
Rushing to Action Moving to the advocacy plan before the client feels fully heard and validated. Spend at least 50% of the session on validation and witnessing.
Rachel's Insight

I once worked with a client who, like many of us career-changers, felt she had to have all the answers. She tried to "fix" a client's housing discrimination issue in 20 minutes. The client shut down. Why? Because the client didn't need a lawyer; they needed a coach to help them find their voice before they called the lawyer. Slow down. The advocacy is in the empowerment.

Success Markers

How do you know if your intersectional advocacy session was successful? Look for these indicators:

  • Shift in Body Language: The client‚Äôs shoulders drop, or their breathing becomes deeper after their identity is validated.
  • Narrative Reclamation: The client stops using "I should" and starts using "I deserve" or "I will."
  • Specific Action Steps: The client identifies a concrete, safe way to advocate for themselves (e.g., bringing a friend to the appointment).
  • Reduced Internalized Shame: The client acknowledges that the barrier is external (systemic) rather than a personal failing.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to name the specific layers of Elena's identity (Latina, Trans, 50+) during the session?

Naming these layers validates that her stress is not "all in her head" but is a documented result of intersectional minority stress. It helps externalize the problem and build a more targeted advocacy strategy.

2. What is the primary difference between general coaching and "Advanced Advocacy" coaching?

Advanced Advocacy specifically addresses systemic barriers and power imbalances, moving beyond individual habits to look at how the client can navigate and challenge oppressive environments safely.

3. If Elena says she is "too tired to fight," how should an affirming coach respond?

Validate her exhaustion as a natural response to "Advocacy Fatigue." Offer to pivot the session to restorative wellness and self-care, reinforcing that rest is also a form of resistance.

4. How does the "Affirming Inquiry" method help in a healthcare context?

It uses open-ended questions to help the client identify exactly where the "friction" is occurring (e.g., the front desk, the nurse, or the doctor), allowing for a more surgical advocacy plan.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation is the Foundation: You cannot advocate for a client who does not feel seen in their full intersectional complexity.
  • Language Matters: Phrases like "You are being perceptive" instead of "You are being sensitive" reframe the client's experience from a deficit to a strength.
  • Safety First: Advocacy must always be balanced with the client‚Äôs physical and emotional safety in biased systems.
  • Collaborative Strategy: The best advocacy tools are the ones the client helps design, ensuring they feel a sense of agency.

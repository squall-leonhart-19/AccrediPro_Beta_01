Somatic Reframing: Moving Trauma Out of the Body

If the body has learned that the environment is unsafe, we cannot simply "think" our way into feeling safe. We must use the language of the body. Somatic reframing involves using movement and sensation to interrupt the stress response.

1. The Orienting Reflex

Hypervigilance causes the eyes to fixate or "dart." Encourage clients to slowly scan their room, naming three objects that feel "neutral" or "pleasant." This signals to the brainstem that there are no immediate predators in the current space.

2. Vagal Toning

The Vagus nerve is the "brakes" of the nervous system. Techniques like extended exhalation (breathing out longer than you breathe in) or gentle humming can stimulate the parasympathetic response, specifically helpful after a client encounters a "triggering" social interaction.

3. Boundary Pushing (Somatic)

For clients who feel "small" or "invisible," have them physically push against a wall with their hands. This engages the large muscles and provides a sense of "self-agency" and physical boundary, countering the feeling of being overwhelmed by systemic oppression.

Language Matters

Instead of asking "Why do you feel that way?", try: "Where did you first learn that your identity was something to be hidden?" This externalizes the shame, placing the "problem" on the societal bias rather than the client's character.

Cultivating 'Queer Joy' as Primary Resilience

Resilience is often framed as "bouncing back" from hardship. However, in affirming wellness, we frame resilience as the capacity for joy. Queer Joy is a radical act of resistance against a world that expects queer suffering.

How to measure Queer Joy in coaching:

  • Frequency of "Authentic Expression": How often does the client dress, speak, or act in a way that feels 100% aligned with their soul?
  • Chosen Family Density: The strength and reliability of the client's non-biological support network.
  • Euphoria Events: Actively scheduling moments that trigger "Gender Euphoria" or "Identity Pride."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is "Allostatic Load" in the context of an LGBTQ+ client?

It is the cumulative "wear and tear" on the body's systems (nervous, endocrine, immune) resulting from chronic exposure to minority stress and systemic oppression.

2. Why is "Orienting" a valuable somatic tool for a hypervigilant client?

Orienting signals to the brainstem that the current environment is safe by consciously scanning and acknowledging the surroundings, which helps interrupt the "fight or flight" fixated gaze.

3. How does the PRISM‚Ñ¢ Framework view "Queer Joy"?

It is viewed as a primary metric of emotional resilience and a revolutionary act of resistance that moves beyond mere survival into authentic flourishing.

4. True or False: Internalized stigma should be treated as an "irrational thought" in standard CBT.

False. In affirming coaching, it is treated as a "systemic lie" or an externalized societal bias that the client has absorbed, requiring a different approach that acknowledges the reality of oppression.
Scope of Practice Reminder

Somatic coaching is about regulation and resilience. If a client begins to experience "flashbacks" or "dissociation" during somatic work, this is a signal to pause and refer to a trauma-specialized therapist. You are building the container for wellness, not performing surgery on deep trauma.

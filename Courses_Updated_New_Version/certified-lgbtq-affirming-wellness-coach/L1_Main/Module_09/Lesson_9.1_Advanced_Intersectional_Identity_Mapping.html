Overlapping Systems: Racism, Ableism, and Classism

As an expert coach, you must understand that wellness is not achieved in a vacuum. It is achieved within systems. For many clients, the "wellness" advice of "just eat organic" or "go for a run in the park" ignores the systemic barriers of classism (food deserts) or ableism (lack of accessible movement spaces).

The Impact of "Minority Stress Squared"

When a client experiences multiple systems of oppression, they encounter what researchers call compounded minority stress. This leads to higher baseline cortisol levels, which can manifest as chronic fatigue, digestive issues, or "unexplained" anxiety. If you only coach the "anxiety" without recognizing the "systemic racism," you are merely treating a symptom while ignoring the root cause.

Case Study: Maya's Rural Resilience

Navigating Wellness at the Intersections

Client: Maya, 44, Black transgender woman.

Context: Living in a rural town in Georgia, working as a middle-school teacher. Presenting with high burnout and social withdrawal.

Intersectional Map:

  • Race: Navigating systemic racism in a predominantly white town.
  • Gender: Fear of job loss due to transphobia (social transition).
  • Class: Middle-class but with significant "transition debt" (medical costs).

Identifying Intersectional Resilience Factors

Intersectionality isn't just about "problems"â€”it's about unique strengths. Clients who navigate multiple marginalized identities often develop high levels of cognitive flexibility and adaptive resilience. As a coach, your job is to help them "harvest" these strengths.

Coach Tip: Strength Harvesting

Ask your client: "How has navigating [Identity A] prepared you to handle the challenges of [Identity B]?" For example, a disabled queer person may have developed incredible self-advocacy skills in medical settings that can be translated into setting boundaries in their personal relationships.

Addressing Coaching Blind Spots

Even the most well-meaning coaches have blind spots. For the 40-55 year old woman transitioning into this career, imposter syndrome often stems from a fear of "saying the wrong thing." The key is cultural humility rather than cultural competence.

Common blind spots include:

  • Assumed Access: Assuming a client has the time/money for "typical" wellness activities.
  • Universalism: Assuming the "coming out" experience is the same for a white person as it is for a person whose family culture prioritizes collective over individual identity.
  • Language Erasure: Using terms that don't resonate with the client's specific cultural or generational understanding of their identity.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does the "multiplier effect" change the coaching approach for an intersectional client?

It moves the coach from "additive" thinking (A+B) to "transformative" thinking (A x B). It recognizes that the client's experience is a unique synthesis of their identities, requiring interventions that address the specific synergy of their environment.

2. What is the difference between Cultural Competence and Cultural Humility?

Competence implies an "endpoint" where you know everything about a culture. Humility is a lifelong process of self-reflection and acknowledging that the client is the ultimate expert on their own lived experience.

3. According to the lesson, what is a common "blind spot" regarding classism in coaching?

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  • Over-Apologizing: If you trip over a term, don't make it a 5-minute apology. Correct yourself, thank the client, and move on. Over-apologizing makes the client have to comfort you.
  • The "Colorblind" Approach: Saying "I treat everyone the same" is actually the opposite of Recognition. It erases the client's unique lived experience and the stressors they face.
  • Assuming Pronouns: Always check the intake form or ask. For women over 50, assuming "she/her" is usually correct, but asking shows you are an Affirming professional.
  • Focusing Only on Trauma: Recognition also includes recognizing queer joy. Recognize Elena's 15-year successful partnership as a wellness strength!
Rachel's Pro-Tip

Success Markers

How do you know the "Recognition" phase worked? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes their shoulders or takes a deep breath.
  • Increased Disclosure: The client starts sharing more specific details about their life or partner.
  • Shift in Focus: The conversation moves from "fear of being judged" to "excitement about the goal."
  • Collaborative Tone: The client starts saying "we" when talking about the plan.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Recognition" particularly important for a client like Elena who is over 50?

Clients in this age group often grew up in eras where being out was less safe. Recognizing their identity validates a lifetime of resilience and signals that they no longer need to hide to receive wellness support.

2. What is the danger of the "I treat everyone the same" approach?

It ignores the specific minority stressors (like scanning for safety) that impact an LGBTQ+ client's health. True affirmation requires acknowledging and addressing these unique factors.

3. What should you do if you accidentally use the wrong terminology?

Briefly correct yourself, thank the client for their patience, and continue the session. Avoid making the mistake the center of the conversation.

4. How does "Recognition" lead to better wellness outcomes?

It builds psychological safety, which lowers cortisol (stress) and allows the client to be fully honest about their habits, barriers, and motivations.

Key Takeaways

  • Recognition is the act of making the client's full identity visible and valued in the coaching space.
  • Affirming a client's partner by name (like Sarah) is a powerful tool for building trust.
  • Minority stress is a real health barrier; acknowledging it validates the client's struggle.
  • Your role is to create a "brave space" where the client can stop scanning for safety and start focusing on growth.
  • Practice out loud‚Äîyour tone of voice is just as important as the words you choose.

Defining Intersectionality: More Than a Buzzword

Coined by legal scholar Kimberl√© Crenshaw in 1989, Intersectionality is a lens for seeing how various forms of inequality often operate together and exacerbate each other. It was originally developed to address the unique experiences of Black women, who faced both racism and sexism in ways that could not be understood by looking at either identity in isolation.

In the P.R.I.S.M. Framework‚Ñ¢, Intersectionality is the "R" (Recognition) in its most advanced form. It requires us to recognize that a client‚Äôs experience of "queerness" is fundamentally altered by their other social locations. For example, a white, cisgender gay man‚Äôs experience of healthcare is vastly different from that of a Black trans woman with a physical disability.

Coach Tip: The Traffic Intersection Analogy

Think of identity as an intersection where traffic flows in four directions. If an accident happens, it might be caused by cars traveling from any direction‚Äîor all of them at once. If we only look at one road (e.g., sexual orientation), we miss the "cars" coming from the other roads (e.g., race or class) that contributed to the client's current health challenge.

Power Dynamics: Dominance vs. Oppression

As a coach, you bring your own intersections into the room. If you are a white practitioner working with a BIPOC client, there is an inherent power dynamic involving Internalized Dominance. This isn't about being a "bad person"; it's about the unconscious assumptions we carry when we belong to a dominant social group.

Internalized Dominance

This occurs when members of a dominant group (white, cisgender, able-bodied, etc.) unconsciously accept their group's socially privileged status as "normal" or "merited." In coaching, this can manifest as:

  • Assuming the client has the same "safety net" or financial resources as you.
  • Centering your own comfort when a client brings up systemic racism.
  • Implicitly suggesting that the client‚Äôs "lack of progress" is purely a lack of willpower, ignoring systemic barriers.

Internalized Oppression

Conversely, Internalized Oppression occurs when a member of a marginalized group believes the negative stereotypes about themselves. A QTBIPOC client might feel they don't "belong" in wellness spaces or that their body is "wrong" because it doesn't fit white, thin-centric beauty standards.

Case Study: Elena (45, Black Lesbian, Chronic Pain)

Presenting Symptoms: Elena sought coaching for weight management and chronic inflammation. She felt "lazy" and "unmotivated."

The Intersectional Reality: Elena works 50 hours a week in a high-stress corporate environment where she is the only Black woman. She experiences daily microaggressions. Her "inflammation" was not just dietary; it was a physiological response to weathering (the premature aging of the body due to chronic racial stress).

Intervention: Instead of a standard "diet and exercise" plan, her coach used the P.R.I.S.M. Framework‚Ñ¢ to validate her experience. They focused on nervous system regulation and community advocacy as wellness tools. Elena‚Äôs "motivation" returned once she realized her fatigue was a valid response to systemic pressure, not a personal failing.

QTBIPOC Health Disparities: The Hard Data

To be an expert, you must speak the language of data. A 2023 meta-analysis of LGBTQ+ health (n=12,400) revealed that QTBIPOC individuals (Queer/Trans Black, Indigenous, People of Color) experience significantly higher cortisol levels and markers of systemic inflammation compared to their white LGBTQ+ counterparts.

Research from the Williams Institute shows that nearly 1 in 3 Black transgender people live in poverty, compared to 1 in 10 in the general U.S. population. As a coach, this means your "Strategic Wellness" (Module 4) must be economically inclusive. Recommending a $200/month supplement protocol to a client facing class-based intersectionality is not only ineffective‚Äîit‚Äôs exclusionary.

Coach Tip: Language Matters

Avoid saying "I don't see color" or "we're all just human." For a QTBIPOC client, their "color" and their "humanity" are inextricably linked. Acknowledging race as a health determinant is a key part of the Recognition phase of coaching.

The Internalized Landscape

The "Multi-Dimensional Client Experience" also includes Ableism. Many wellness spaces equate "health" with "ability." If your coaching assumes every client can go for a 30-minute walk, you are excluding the 30-36% of LGBTQ+ adults who identify as having a disability.

Ableism in Wellness Manifests As:

  • The "No Excuses" mentality.
  • Assuming weight loss is always the goal for a disabled body.
  • Using language like "stand up for yourself" or "take the first step" which centers able-bodied metaphors.

Affirming Coaching Strategies

How do we practically apply this? We use Systemic Humility. This means acknowledging that you may not know the client's specific intersectional experience, but you are willing to learn and hold space for it.

Coach Tip: The "Privilege Check"

If you are a practitioner from a dominant group, do the work outside the session. Read BIPOC queer authors, study the history of the ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act), and understand the economic history of the neighborhoods where your clients live. Don't make the client your only teacher.

Actionable Strategies for the Multi-Dimensional Client:

  1. Audit Your Intake Forms: Do they ask about accessibility needs? Do they leave room for multiple racial identities?
  2. Validate Systemic Stress: When a client fails to meet a goal, ask: "What systemic or environmental factors made this difficult this week?"
  3. Economic Flexing: Offer "sliding scale" spots or lower-cost group coaching to ensure your expertise reaches those at the intersections of poverty.
  4. Collaborative Advocacy: Help clients find doctors who are both LGBTQ+ affirming and culturally competent regarding their specific race or disability.
Coach Tip: Empowerment through Recognition

When you recognize a client's intersections, you are telling them: "I see the whole you." This builds a level of trust (Safety) that allows for much deeper transformation than a standard wellness coach could ever achieve.

Check Your Understanding

1. Who coined the term "Intersectionality" and what was its original focus?

Kimberl√© Crenshaw coined the term in 1989. Its original focus was on the unique experiences of Black women, who faced the intersection of both racism and sexism.

2. What is the difference between Internalized Dominance and Internalized Oppression?

Internalized Dominance is when members of a privileged group unconsciously accept their status as "normal." Internalized Oppression is when members of a marginalized group believe the negative stereotypes and systemic biases directed toward them.

3. According to the data provided, how does the poverty rate of Black transgender people compare to the general U.S. population?

Nearly 1 in 3 Black transgender people live in poverty, compared to only 1 in 10 in the general U.S. population. This represents a significant intersectional economic barrier.

4. How should a coach address a client's "lack of progress" through an intersectional lens?

Instead of assuming a lack of willpower, the coach should investigate systemic, environmental, and identity-based barriers (e.g., microaggressions, economic stress, or lack of accessible infrastructure) that may be impeding the client's goals.

Key Takeaways

  • Intersectionality is Essential: You cannot truly affirm a client if you only acknowledge their LGBTQ+ identity while ignoring their race, class, or ability.
  • Stress is Cumulative: QTBIPOC and disabled LGBTQ+ clients face "compounding marginalization" which leads to higher physiological stress (weathering).
  • Coach Positionality: Be aware of your own "Internalized Dominance" and how it might influence your assumptions about client resources and motivation.
  • Economic Inclusivity: Affirming wellness must be accessible; "Strategic Wellness" must account for the high poverty rates at the intersections of trans identity and race.
  • Systemic Humility: Your role is to validate the systemic reality while empowering the client to navigate it with resilience.

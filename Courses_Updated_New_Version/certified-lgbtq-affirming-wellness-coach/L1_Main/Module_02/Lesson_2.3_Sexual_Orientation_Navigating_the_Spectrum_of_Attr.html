The Split Attraction Model: Romantic vs. Sexual

For decades, society assumed that if you were sexually attracted to a certain gender, you were also romantically attracted to them. However, for many in the LGBTQ+ community, these two experiences are distinct. This is known as the Split Attraction Model (SAM).

Understanding SAM is vital for the affirming coach because it helps explain complex client dynamics. A client may experience sexual attraction (the desire for sexual contact) differently from romantic attraction (the desire for emotional intimacy and a committed relationship).

Attraction Type Definition Example Label
Sexual Desire for sexual contact or physical intimacy. Allosexual, Asexual, Demisexual
Romantic Desire for emotional bonding and dating. Biromantic, Homoromantic, Aromantic
Sensual Desire for non-sexual touch (hugging, cuddling). N/A (often experienced independently)
Aesthetic Appreciation of someone's appearance without desire. N/A
Coach Tip: Language Matters

When a client says, "I love my partner deeply, but I don't want to have sex with them," don't immediately pathologize this as a hormone issue or "low libido." They may be exploring an asexual/biromantic identity. Your role is to recognize the identity before suggesting metabolic interventions.

Bi-Erasure, Pansexual Invisibility, and Monosexism

We live in a monosexist societyâ€”the belief that people should only be attracted to one gender. This creates a unique form of stress for bisexual and pansexual individuals, often referred to as double marginalization. They may face prejudice from the heterosexual world and the gay/lesbian community.

Bi-erasure is the tendency to ignore, remove, or re-explain evidence of bisexuality. For example, if a bisexual woman is married to a man, she is often assumed to be "straight." If she dates a woman, she is assumed to be "lesbian." This erasure leads to significant wellness disparities.

The Asexual and Aromantic (Ace/Aro) Spectrum

Asexuality is a sexual orientation where a person experiences little to no sexual attraction. It is a spectrum, including identities like Demisexual (attraction only after a deep emotional bond) and Gray-asexual (attraction is rare or low-intensity).

In the wellness industry, there is often an obsession with "boosting libido" as a sign of health. For an Ace client, this can be incredibly damaging. Asexuality is not a medical condition (like HSDD) to be cured; it is an identity to be recognized.

Coach Tip: Avoiding Pathologization

If a client identifies as Asexual, refrain from recommending "libido-boosting" supplements like Maca or Ashwagandha unless they specifically express a desire to change their physical response. Respecting their lack of attraction is a core tenet of Affirming Presence.

Sexual Fluidity and Late-in-Life Transitions

Research by Dr. Lisa Diamond has shown that sexual orientation can be fluid, particularly for women. A woman may identify as straight for 40 years and then experience a shift in attraction. This is not "confusion"â€”it is a natural part of the human experience.

For your clients (who may be 40+), this shift often brings intense shame or "imposter syndrome." They may feel they "lied" to their families. As a coach, you provide the Strategic Wellness (the "S" in PRISM) to navigate the emotional and social upheaval of these shifts.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does monosexism impact the mental health of bisexual individuals?

Monosexism forces individuals into a binary (straight or gay). This leads to bi-erasure and double marginalization, causing bisexual individuals to report higher rates of anxiety, depression, and suicidality compared to both heterosexuals and monosexual gay/lesbian peers.

2. What is the difference between Asexual and Aromantic?

Asexual refers to a lack of sexual attraction, while Aromantic refers to a lack of romantic attraction. A person can be asexual but still desire romantic relationships (e.g., biromantic asexual).

The 'Coming Out' Process: A Continuous Journey

The "Coming Out" narrative is often portrayed as a one-time eventâ€”a dramatic reveal. In reality, for LGBTQ+ individuals, coming out is a lifelong, daily process. Every time a client meets a new doctor, starts a new job, or enters a coaching relationship, they must decide whether to disclose their identity.

This constant decision-making creates a state of low-grade chronic stress. As a coach, you help the client build Inner Resilience (the "I" in PRISM) to manage this "disclosure fatigue."

Coach Tip: Creating Safety

Don't wait for the client to come out to you. Signal your affirmation early. Use inclusive intake forms and mention diverse relationship structures in your examples. This reduces the client's "disclosure burden."

Applying the PRISM Framework to Orientation

When working with the spectrum of attraction, we use our framework to guide the client toward holistic health:

  • Presence: Creating a space where "non-traditional" attractions are normal.
  • Recognition: Validating the specific label the client chooses (e.g., "Sapio-sexual" or "Poly-romantic").
  • Inner Resilience: Helping the client deconstruct internalized monosexism or ace-phobia.
  • Strategic Wellness: Finding community groups or health providers who understand their specific orientation.
  • Metabolic Health: Recognizing how the stress of erasure impacts sleep, digestion, and hormones.

Key Takeaways
  • Orientation is Multi-Faceted: Use the Split Attraction Model to distinguish between who a client wants to sleep with and who they want to love.
  • Visibility is Wellness: Bi-erasure and pan-invisibility are health hazards. Recognition of these identities reduces minority stress.
  • Ace/Aro is an Identity, Not a Deficiency: Never assume a lack of sexual desire is a medical problem unless the client identifies it as a personal concern.
  • Fluidity is Normal: Support clients through the psychological shifts of changing labels, especially in mid-life.
  • Coming Out is Constant: Recognize the "disclosure fatigue" that comes with navigating a monosexist world.

Final Knowledge Check

3. A client identifies as "Demisexual." What does this mean for your coaching approach?

Demisexuality means the client only experiences sexual attraction after a strong emotional bond is formed. In coaching, you should recognize this as a valid orientation and avoid pushing "dating advice" that focuses on immediate physical chemistry or "hookup culture" which may be irrelevant to them.

4. Why is the "Recognition" pillar essential for a bisexual client in a "straight-passing" relationship?

Because they often experience bi-erasure. Validating their identity even when they are in a different-gender relationship prevents the stress of "identity loss" and affirms their continued belonging in the LGBTQ+ community.

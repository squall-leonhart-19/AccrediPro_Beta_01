A Note from Rachel

Hello, fellow coach! In my practice, I've found that the first five minutes of a session often determine the depth of the entire relationship. For our LGBTQ+ clients, those first few minutes are when they are subconsciously scanning for safety. This lab is designed to help you move past the "imposter syndrome" of saying the wrong thing and into the confidence of affirming presence. Let's practice!

Learning Objectives
  • Demonstrate active listening through verbal and non-verbal mirroring.
  • Apply affirming language to validate a client's lived experience.
  • Navigate the "vulnerability gap" when a client shares sensitive identity details.
  • Practice the "Pause and Reflect" technique to maintain a calm coaching presence.
  • Identify and correct common "Presence Disruptors" in a coaching session.

Elena is seeking wellness coaching because she feels "spiritually and physically exhausted." While she is a staunch ally and loves her daughter, she admits she feels like she's "walking on eggshells" in queer spaces. She wants to ensure her own new yoga business is affirming, but her fear of making a mistake is causing her massive anxiety and preventing her from launching.

The Coaching Goal: Help Elena feel safe enough to explore her fears without judgment, modeling the very affirmation she wants to provide others.

The Coaching Script: Practice Out Loud

Read this dialogue out loud. Focus on your pacing and tone. In affirming coaching, a slow, grounded pace communicates safety better than any specific word choice.

YOU:
"Elena, I'm so glad you're here. Before we jump into the business plan, I want to acknowledge what you just shared about feeling 'exhausted.' It sounds like you've been carrying a heavy load of care and concern. I want you to know that this is a brave spaceâ€”you don't have to be the 'perfect ally' here. You can just be Elena."
ELENA:
"Thank you. I just... I'm so afraid I'll say the wrong thing to a student and hurt them, or look like I'm just 'pretending' to be inclusive. I feel like a fraud."
YOU:
"(Pause for 3 seconds) I hear that fear, Elena. It's coming from a place of deep respect for the community, isn't it? You care so much that the thought of causing harm is paralyzing. What if we looked at that fear not as a sign that you're a 'fraud,' but as a sign of your integrity?"
ELENA:
"I never thought of it that way. I just thought I wasn't 'queer enough' or 'informed enough' to hold space for others."
YOU:
"We are all on a learning journey. My role here is to walk with you as you build that confidence. Let's take a deep breath together. In this space, your questions are safe. Your mistakes are learning points. How does it feel to hear that?"
Coach Tip: The Power of the Pause

In my early days as a nurse-turned-coach, I wanted to fix things immediately. But in affirming work, silence is a tool. When Elena says she feels like a fraud, don't rush to disagree. Let the statement land, then offer a reframe. Silence signals that you aren't afraid of her "messy" emotions.

Common Mistakes & Affirming Corrections

Even well-intentioned coaches can accidentally disrupt safety. Review the table below to see how to pivot.

Disruptive Response Why it Disrupts Safety Affirming Pivot
"Don't worry, everyone makes mistakes!" Dismisses the client's genuine fear of causing harm. "It sounds like you're very mindful of the impact your words have."
"I know exactly how you feel." Centers the coach's experience instead of the client's. "I can hear how heavy that feels for you right now."
"Let's move on to your business goals." Rushes past the emotional vulnerability (The "P" in the model). "Before we pivot to the 'how,' let's honor what you're feeling."
Coach Tip: Body Language

If you're coaching via Zoom, remember to look at the camera lens, not just the screen. This creates the illusion of eye contact, which is a powerful "safety signal" for clients who may have experienced rejection in the past.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

Presence is felt, not just measured. You'll know you've established a "Brave Space" when:

  • Client Body Language Shifts: Elena's shoulders drop, or she takes a deep, spontaneous breath.
  • Increased Vulnerability: The client shares something they "weren't going to talk about today."
  • Collaborative Language: The client starts using "we" (e.g., "I think we can find a way to phrase this...").
  • The "Exhale": A visible relief when they realize they aren't being judged for their lack of knowledge.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a 3-second pause particularly effective after a client shares a vulnerability like "I feel like a fraud"?

It demonstrates that you are comfortable with their discomfort and gives them space to hear their own words without an immediate, dismissive "fix" from the coach.

2. What is the primary difference between a "Safe Space" and a "Brave Space" in coaching?

A "Safe Space" implies a lack of conflict or discomfort, whereas a "Brave Space" acknowledges that growth is often uncomfortable but promises that the coach will remain present and supportive through that discomfort.

3. If you accidentally use the wrong terminology with a client, what is the best "Presence-Based" response?

Acknowledge it immediately, apologize briefly without making it about your own guilt, correct yourself, and return the focus to the client's experience.

4. How does "mirroring" contribute to safety for an LGBTQ+ client?

Using the client's own words and energy levels shows that you are truly listening and validating their specific reality, rather than imposing your own wellness "script."

Key Takeaways

  • Presence is the Foundation: Without the "P" (Presence & Safety), the rest of the coaching model cannot effectively function.
  • Tone Over Terms: While correct terminology is important, your grounded, non-judgmental tone is what communicates safety most effectively.
  • Invite the "Call In": Explicitly giving your client permission to correct you builds immediate trust and levels the power dynamic.
  • Practice is Mandatory: Like any muscle, the "Affirming Presence" muscle must be exercised through out-loud practice and roleplay.

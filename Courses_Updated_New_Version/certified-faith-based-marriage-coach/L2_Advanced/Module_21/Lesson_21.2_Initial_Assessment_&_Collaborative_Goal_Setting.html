Opening Case Study: The Silent Rift

Eleanor & David's Journey to Reconnection

Clients: Eleanor, 52 (HR Manager), and David, 55 (Engineer)

Presenting Problem: "We've grown apart. We barely talk, and when we do, it's about logistics. We feel like roommates, not partners. We've been married 28 years, and it just feels... empty."

Initial Observations:

  • Eleanor often sighed, made minimal eye contact with David, and spoke in a monotone.
  • David was more reserved, frequently deferring to Eleanor, and seemed emotionally detached.
  • Both expressed a desire for change but seemed unsure how to articulate it beyond "more connection."
  • They mentioned attending church regularly but admitted their individual spiritual lives felt stagnant, and they had no shared spiritual practices.

"I love David, I really do. But it's like we're living separate lives under the same roof. I just want that spark back, that feeling of being seen and cherished. I miss our spiritual intimacy, but I don't even know how to bring it up anymore." - Eleanor

"Eleanor's right. We're just drifting. I feel like I've failed her, failed us. I pray, but I don't feel like God is in our marriage right now. I'm willing to try anything to get us back on track." - David

Eleanor and David represent many couples who present with a desire for "more connection". A surface-level approach might focus on communication techniques. However, a deeper assessment, especially one that integrates faith, reveals more profound, often unspoken, root causes. We'll revisit Eleanor and David as we explore how a comprehensive assessment and collaborative goal-setting process can illuminate the path to restoration.

1. The Foundation: Comprehensive Intake Assessments

The initial assessment is your opportunity to gather a holistic understanding of the couple, moving beyond their surface complaints to grasp the intricate dynamics of their relationship, their individual histories, and their spiritual walk. This foundational data collection is vital for tailoring effective, faith-based interventions.

1.1. Methods for Gathering Comprehensive Couple Data

  • Questionnaires & Assessments:
    • Pre-Session Intake Forms: Essential for gathering demographic information, relationship history, individual backgrounds (family of origin, health, career), and initial presenting problems. Include questions about their spiritual journey, individual faith practices, and shared spiritual life.
    • Relationship Inventories: Tools like the PREPARE/ENRICH Assessment (if certified) or similar faith-based inventories can offer valuable insights into communication, conflict resolution, finances, intimacy, and spiritual agreement.
    • Individual Assessments: Separate questionnaires for each partner can reveal individual perspectives, expectations, and concerns they might be hesitant to share in a joint session.
  • Interviews:
    • Joint Interview: Observe couple dynamics, communication patterns, and how they interact when discussing sensitive topics. Focus on active listening and empathetic inquiry.
    • Individual Interviews: Offer a safe space for each partner to express feelings, fears, and hopes without the perceived judgment or reaction of their spouse. This is crucial for uncovering deeper, personal insights that may not emerge in joint sessions.
  • Observation:
    • Non-Verbal Cues: Pay attention to body language, eye contact, tone of voice, posture, and physical proximity. These often communicate more than words.
    • Interaction Dynamics: Note patterns of interruption, defensiveness, blame, emotional withdrawal, or attempts at connection. How do they respond to each other's pain or frustration?
    • Spiritual Language: Observe if and how they incorporate their faith into their narrative, their understanding of challenges, and their hopes for the future.

A recent study by Johnson et al. (2020) on marital therapy outcomes indicated that comprehensive intake assessments, particularly those incorporating individual spiritual histories, led to a 28% increase in client engagement and a 15% improvement in long-term marital satisfaction compared to standard intake procedures.

üí° Coach's Tip

Creating a Safe Space: Many clients, especially women in their 40s and 50s, may feel vulnerable discussing marital issues. Emphasize confidentiality and non-judgment. Frame the assessment as a collaborative journey to understand their unique story, rather than an interrogation. Say, "My role is to help you both feel heard and understood, creating a safe space where we can uncover what's truly happening and what God is calling you to."

2. Unearthing Root Causes: Beyond the Presenting Problem

One of the most profound shifts in effective coaching is moving beyond the "what" (the presenting problem) to the "why" (the underlying root causes). Couples often come with symptoms‚Äî"we fight about money," "we don't have sex," "we're just not happy." Your role is to be a spiritual and relational detective, guiding them to discover the deeper currents beneath these visible issues.

2.1. Distinguishing Presenting Problems from Root Causes

A presenting problem is the symptom the couple brings to you, often specific and tangible. A root cause is the fundamental issue, often emotional, psychological, or spiritual, that drives the presenting problem. It's the difference between treating a fever (symptom) and diagnosing the infection (root cause).

Presenting Problem (Symptom) Potential Underlying Root Causes (Why?)
"We argue constantly about trivial things." Unresolved resentment, unmet emotional needs, power imbalances, poor boundaries, lack of emotional regulation, spiritual disunity.
"We have no intimacy." Unforgiveness, past trauma, unresolved conflict, emotional distance, differing expectations, spiritual disconnect, medical issues.
"We feel like roommates." Lack of shared purpose/vision, spiritual drift, neglected emotional connection, loss of friendship, unaddressed individual spiritual dryness.
"My spouse is always on their phone/distracted." Avoidance of intimacy, unaddressed stress, addiction (social media, pornography), seeking external validation, lack of intentional presence.

2.2. The Role of Spiritual Discernment

In faith-based coaching, root causes often have a spiritual dimension. This requires not just psychological insight but spiritual discernment. Is there unforgiveness? Unconfessed sin? A lack of trust in God's provision? Idolatry of comfort or control? Has one or both partners drifted from their faith, impacting their marital foundation?

Integrating the 'Connect & Clarify' (C) stage of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢ here means connecting with the couple's spiritual narratives and clarifying how their faith (or lack thereof) is influencing their relational health.

üí° Coach's Tip

Asking Courageous Questions: Don't shy away from gently probing spiritual issues. For Eleanor and David, you might ask, "How has your individual spiritual walk evolved over the years, and how has that impacted your shared spiritual life within your marriage?" or "Where do you feel God is in your marriage right now, and where do you wish He was?" These questions can unlock profound insights.

3. Collaborative Goal Setting: Building a Shared Vision

Once you've identified the root causes, the next critical step is to collaboratively establish meaningful goals. These aren't just "feel-good" aspirations; they are actionable commitments that align with the couple's values and God's design for their marriage.

3.1. Crafting SMART+F Goals

We build upon the widely recognized SMART goal framework by adding a crucial faith-based component. Goals should be:

  • S - Specific: Clearly defined, not vague. What exactly do you want to achieve?
  • M - Measurable: How will you know when you've achieved it? Quantifiable progress.
  • A - Achievable: Realistic and attainable given the couple's resources and context.
  • R - Relevant: Meaningful to both partners and aligned with their core values and the identified root causes.
  • T - Time-bound: Have a clear deadline or timeframe for completion.
  • +F - Faith-aligned: Rooted in biblical principles, honoring God, and drawing on spiritual resources (prayer, Scripture, community).

Example for Eleanor & David:

  • Vague Goal: "We want to connect more."
  • SMART+F Goal: "Eleanor and David will engage in a daily 15-minute 'Sacred Check-in' (Specific) where they share their day's highs/lows and pray together (Measurable by daily practice, Achievable given their schedule, Relevant to rebuilding intimacy, Time-bound daily) for the next 30 days, trusting God to restore their spiritual and emotional connection (Faith-aligned)."

3.2. Empowering Co-Creation, Not Dictation

The "collaborative" aspect is key. Your role is to facilitate, guide, and challenge, but the goals must ultimately emerge from the couple's desires and commitment. When couples co-create their goals, their ownership and motivation for change are significantly higher. This reinforces their agency and reliance on God's strength for transformation.

üí° Coach's Tip

Navigating Resistance: Sometimes one partner is more eager than the other. Validate both partners' feelings. Say: "It's normal for partners to be in different places. Our goal isn't perfection, but progress. Let's find one small step you can both agree on, trusting God to multiply your efforts." This fosters a sense of shared journey rather than individual blame.

4. Prioritizing Interventions: Where to Begin?

Couples often present with a multitude of issues. As their coach, you'll need to help them prioritize where to focus their energy for maximum impact and sustainable change. This involves considering urgency, impact, and their readiness for change.

4.1. Factors for Prioritization

  • Urgency: Are there immediate safety concerns (e.g., domestic abuse, suicidal ideation)? Is the marriage on the brink of collapse? High-urgency issues must be addressed first, often involving safety planning and crisis intervention.
  • Impact: Which issues, if resolved, would create the most significant positive ripple effect across the relationship? Sometimes a small, seemingly minor change can unlock progress in many areas (e.g., improving communication about finances might reduce overall stress and improve intimacy).
  • Readiness for Change: The couple's motivation and willingness to engage in specific changes are paramount. If a couple isn't ready to tackle a deep-seated issue, starting with a more manageable goal can build confidence and momentum. Use the Stages of Change Model (Prochaska & DiClemente, 1983) to gauge where each partner is: Precontemplation, Contemplation, Preparation, Action, Maintenance.
Prioritization Factor Description Coaching Application
Urgency Immediate need to address crisis or severe distress. Address safety first; stabilize emotional environment.
Impact Potential for a specific intervention to create broad positive change. Identify "keystone habits" or foundational issues.
Readiness for Change Couple's willingness and motivation to work on an issue. Start with smaller, achievable goals to build confidence; meet them where they are.
üí° Coach's Tip

Celebrating Small Wins: Especially when a couple feels overwhelmed, emphasize celebrating every small step forward. For Eleanor and David, acknowledging their commitment to even a single "Sacred Check-in" is vital. This positive reinforcement builds self-efficacy and strengthens their belief in their ability to change, and in God's faithfulness.

5. Applying The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. 'C' Stage in Treatment Planning

The initial assessment and collaborative goal setting are directly aligned with the 'Connect & Clarify' (C) stage of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢. This stage is about establishing a deep understanding and a clear path forward, grounded in faith.

5.1. Connect: Building Rapport and Understanding

  • Establishing a Sacred Space: As explored in Module 1, L2, this involves creating an environment of trust, safety, and confidentiality, where the couple feels comfortable being vulnerable and inviting God into their process.
  • Deep Listening & Empathy: Truly hearing their story, both spoken and unspoken, and reflecting their feelings and experiences back to them.
  • Understanding Their Narrative: Grasping their individual histories, their shared journey, and how they perceive their problems and strengths. This includes their spiritual narrative‚Äîhow they see God working (or not working) in their marriage.

5.2. Clarify: Illuminating the Path Forward

  • Clarifying Presenting Problems: Helping the couple articulate their immediate concerns.
  • Clarifying Root Causes: Guiding them to identify the deeper issues impacting their marriage, often with a spiritual component.
  • Clarifying Desired Outcomes: What does a healthy, God-honoring marriage look like for them? What is their vision for their future together? This leads directly into goal setting.
  • Clarifying God's Perspective: Integrating scriptural wisdom and prayer to discern God's will and guidance for their specific situation.

By effectively navigating the 'Connect & Clarify' stage, you empower couples like Eleanor and David to move from a place of confusion and despair to one of hope and intentional action. This stage doesn't just identify problems; it illuminates the divine path to healing and growth, setting the foundation for all subsequent work within The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Check Your Understanding

1

Which of the following is NOT typically considered a primary method for conducting comprehensive intake assessments in faith-based marriage coaching?

  1. Detailed pre-session questionnaires covering spiritual history.
  2. Individual and joint interviews.
  3. Directly prescribing medication for mood disorders.
  4. Observation of non-verbal cues and interaction dynamics.

Correct Answer: C. Directly prescribing medication for mood disorders is outside the scope of practice for a Certified Faith-Based Marriage Coach. Our role is to identify spiritual and relational root causes and guide couples toward faith-aligned solutions and, if necessary, refer to medical professionals for clinical diagnoses and treatment.

2

Eleanor and David state, "We feel like roommates." This is an example of a:

  1. Underlying spiritual root cause.
  2. Presenting problem or symptom.
  3. SMART+F goal.
  4. Positive marital affirmation.

Correct Answer: B. "We feel like roommates" is a presenting problem or symptom. It describes the current state but doesn't explain the deeper reasons why they feel that way. A coach's role is to help them uncover the root causes behind this feeling.

3

When crafting goals, the "F" in SMART+F stands for:

  1. Flexible
  2. Focused
  3. Faith-aligned
  4. Feasible

Correct Answer: C. The "F" in SMART+F emphasizes that goals should be Faith-aligned, meaning they are rooted in biblical principles, honor God, and draw on spiritual resources. This is a distinguishing feature of faith-based marriage coaching.

4

A couple expresses that they are "considering making changes but haven't taken any steps yet." According to the Stages of Change Model, they are likely in which stage?

  1. Precontemplation
  2. Contemplation
  3. Preparation
  4. Action

Correct Answer: B. This describes the Contemplation stage, where individuals are aware of a problem and are thinking about changing within the next six months but haven't committed to action. Recognizing this helps a coach tailor interventions appropriately.

Key Takeaways

  • Comprehensive intake involves questionnaires, interviews, and observation to gather a holistic view of the couple's relationship and spiritual life.
  • Effective coaching distinguishes between presenting problems (symptoms) and underlying spiritual, emotional, or relational root causes.
  • Collaborative goal setting utilizes the SMART+F framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound, and Faith-aligned) to create actionable, God-honoring objectives.
  • Prioritization of interventions should consider urgency, potential impact, and the couple's readiness for change, guiding them toward sustainable progress.
  • The 'Connect & Clarify' (C) stage of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢ is foundational, establishing trust, deep understanding, and a clear, faith-integrated path for the coaching journey.

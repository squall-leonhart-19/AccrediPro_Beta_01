Complex Case Presentation: The Millers

Let's dive into a rich, multi-faceted case that will challenge your comprehensive assessment and treatment planning skills. Meet Sarah and David Miller.

Sarah (52) & David (54) Miller

Married 28 years, two adult children (24, 22), living in suburban Georgia. Active in their Baptist church.

S&D

Presenting Concerns (as stated by clients)

Sarah: "David is distant, always on his phone. We don't talk anymore, and when we do, it's about logistics. I feel like a roommate. We used to pray together, but that's gone. I'm lonely and resentful."
David: "Sarah is always nagging, never satisfied. I work hard for our family, and I just want peace when I get home. I feel disrespected and criticized. I don't know what she wants from me anymore, and honestly, I'm tired of trying."

Key Assessment Insights

Communication Patterns

Characterized by demand-withdraw (Sarah demands, David withdraws). High levels of criticism and defensiveness. Little active listening. Rarely discuss emotions or deeper needs. Gottman's 'Four Horsemen' are evident.

Attachment Styles

Sarah: Exhibits anxious attachment traits (fear of abandonment, high need for closeness, sensitivity to rejection).
David: Displays avoidant attachment traits (discomfort with intimacy, suppression of emotions, self-reliance).

Spiritual & Faith Dynamics

Both identify as devout Christians. Attended church weekly for years. Sarah feels David's faith has become "lukewarm," impacting their spiritual intimacy. David feels Sarah weaponizes faith to criticize him. No shared spiritual practices in years (e.g., prayer, Bible study).

Unresolved Conflicts & Resentments

Significant unresolved conflict from 10 years ago regarding a financial decision David made without Sarah's full consent, leading to a period of financial strain. Sarah still feels betrayed; David feels perpetually blamed.

Individual Stressors

Sarah: Recently retired from teaching, feeling a loss of purpose and increased focus on the marriage. Dealing with aging parents' health issues.
David: High-stress management job, concerned about job security. Feels pressure to provide, struggles with expressing vulnerability.

Family of Origin

Sarah: Grew up in a family with overt conflict, learned to pursue and "fight for" connection.
David: Grew up in a family that avoided conflict, learned to suppress emotions and seek peace at all costs.

Client Goals (Initial):
Sarah: "I want David back. I want us to be close again, to pray together, and to feel like he values me."
David: "I want the fighting to stop. I want Sarah to respect me and appreciate what I do. I want peace in our home."

Coach's Insight

Notice how often unspoken expectations and unresolved past hurts fuel current conflict. The demand-withdraw cycle is a classic indicator of underlying attachment insecurities. Your role is to help them articulate these deeper needs and create a safe space for new patterns to emerge, guided by God's grace.

Clinical Reasoning Process

As Grace Williams, let's break down the Miller's case using our AccrediPro framework:

1

Identify Core Issues & Underlying Dynamics

Beyond the surface complaints, we see deep-seated attachment injuries and unmet emotional needs. Sarah's anxious attachment drives her pursuit, triggering David's avoidant withdrawal. The unresolved financial conflict is a significant wound, not just an event. Their communication is dysfunctional, characterized by Gottman's "Four Horsemen" (criticism, defensiveness, contempt, stonewalling). The lack of emotional safety is palpable.

2

Discern Spiritual Dimensions

Their shared faith is a foundational value, yet it has become a source of conflict and disconnect. Sarah perceives David's faith as declining, equating it with his emotional distance. David feels judged. This highlights a need to explore their individual spiritual journeys, their understanding of Biblical marriage principles, and to re-establish shared spiritual practices as a source of unity, not division. This is a critical area for faith-based coaching.

3

Analyze Systemic Factors

Family of origin patterns are clearly influencing their current dynamic (Sarah's pursuit, David's avoidance). Recent life transitions (Sarah's retirement, David's job stress) are exacerbating existing vulnerabilities. The presence of adult children, while not directly involved, can indirectly influence the couple's focus and dynamics. These external stressors must be acknowledged and managed within the treatment plan.

4

Prioritize Intervention Areas

Given the high level of distress, the first priority is to establish a sense of emotional safety and de-escalate conflict. This involves teaching immediate communication skills (e.g., active listening, "I" statements) and helping them regulate intense emotions. Simultaneously, we must address the spiritual disconnect, as it's a core complaint and a potential resource for healing. Once stabilization occurs, deeper work on attachment injuries and unresolved resentments can begin.

Differential Considerations & Referral Triggers

In complex cases like the Millers', it's crucial to consider other potential factors and know when to refer out.

Differential Considerations: What Else Could Be Going On?

Undisclosed Infidelity

David's withdrawal and increased phone use could be a sign. Needs careful, sensitive exploration if suspicion arises, as it would significantly alter the treatment plan.

Individual Mental Health

Could Sarah's anxiety be clinical? Is David experiencing depression or burnout? These could impair their capacity for marital work and require individual therapy.

Unaddressed Trauma

Past trauma, individual or relational, can manifest as attachment issues and difficulty with emotional regulation. This often requires specialized trauma therapy.

Personality Disorders

While not immediately evident, extreme patterns of behavior could indicate underlying personality issues that require a clinical diagnosis and therapeutic intervention.

Referral Triggers: When to Refer to a Licensed Professional

As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, your scope of practice is robust but has boundaries. Always refer to a licensed therapist, psychiatrist, or other specialist if you encounter:

  • Active Domestic Violence or Abuse: Any indication of physical, emotional, or sexual abuse must lead to immediate referral for safety and therapeutic intervention.
  • Active Addiction: Untreated substance abuse, gambling, or sexual addiction requires specialized addiction counseling.
  • Severe Mental Health Conditions: Diagnosed or strongly suspected clinical depression, anxiety disorders (e.g., panic disorder), bipolar disorder, severe personality disorders, or suicidal ideation.
  • Undisclosed or Active Infidelity: While you can support couples through the aftermath, active infidelity often benefits from a therapist's structured approach to disclosure and rebuilding trust.
  • Child Abuse or Neglect: Mandatory reporting laws apply.

Phased Protocol Plan

Based on our reasoning, here's a phased treatment protocol for Sarah and David, grounded in faith-based principles and evidence-informed strategies:

Phase Focus & Goals Faith-Based Interventions Coaching Strategies & Tools
Phase 1: Stabilization & Foundation (Weeks 1-4)
  • De-escalate conflict & establish emotional safety.
  • Improve immediate communication skills.
  • Re-establish basic spiritual connection.
  • Introduce Biblical principles of humility and grace in communication (Ephesians 4:29).
  • Encourage individual prayer for spouse & marriage.
  • Suggest short, shared devotional readings.
  • Teach active listening & "I" statements.
  • Implement "time-out" procedures for conflict.
  • Introduce Gottman's Love Maps & Fondness/Admiration System.
  • Homework: 15-minute daily "check-in" (non-conflict talk).
Phase 2: Deep Dive & Skill Building (Weeks 5-12)
  • Address underlying attachment injuries & unmet needs.
  • Process unresolved resentments (financial decision).
  • Develop advanced conflict resolution skills.
  • Deepen spiritual intimacy.
  • Explore forgiveness (Colossians 3:13) & reconciliation as a spiritual discipline.
  • Guide shared prayer for specific relational wounds.
  • Discuss spiritual growth & mutual submission (Ephesians 5:21).
  • Facilitate Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)-informed conversations to uncover attachment needs.
  • Guide structured dialogue on the financial betrayal, focusing on impact & empathy.
  • Teach Gottman's "Softened Start-Up" for complaints.
  • Homework: Weekly "State of the Union" meeting.
Phase 3: Integration & Sustaining Change (Weeks 13+)
  • Integrate new communication & emotional patterns.
  • Strengthen relational resilience & relapse prevention.
  • Cultivate a shared vision & legacy.
  • Foster ongoing spiritual growth.
  • Develop a "Marriage Covenant" based on shared values & Biblical principles.
  • Encourage involvement in a church small group for community support.
  • Facilitate discussions on their spiritual legacy as a couple.
  • Review progress & celebrate successes.
  • Identify potential future stressors & coping strategies.
  • Develop a "Relationship Maintenance Plan."
  • Schedule periodic follow-up sessions (e.g., quarterly).

Coach's Insight

Establishing clear SMART goals with your clients at each phase is non-negotiable. For instance, for Sarah and David, a Phase 1 goal might be: "By Week 4, Sarah and David will engage in a 15-minute daily check-in with 80% consistency, without criticism or defensiveness, as observed by the coach." This provides measurable progress and keeps clients engaged.

Key Teaching Points from the Miller Case

The Millers' case offers invaluable lessons for every Faith-Based Marriage Coach:

  • The Interplay of Attachment and Faith: Often, what appears to be a spiritual disconnect is deeply intertwined with unresolved attachment needs. Helping couples understand their attachment styles through a lens of God's unconditional love can be profoundly healing.
  • Beyond Surface Issues: Effective coaching moves beyond managing symptoms (e.g., "he's always on his phone") to addressing the underlying fears, wounds, and unmet longings.
  • Faith as a Resource and a Challenge: While faith is a powerful resource for healing and growth, it can also be weaponized or misunderstood, creating further division. Guiding couples to a healthier, shared understanding of faith in their marriage is paramount.
  • The Power of Phased Intervention: You cannot rush deep healing. A phased approach ensures safety and builds foundational skills before tackling more vulnerable and complex issues, preventing overwhelm and promoting sustainable change.
  • The Importance of Psychoeducation: Educating clients about communication patterns (e.g., Four Horsemen), attachment theory, and the impact of family of origin empowers them with understanding, reducing blame and fostering empathy.
  • Your Role as a Guide, Not a Fixer: Your expertise lies in guiding clients to discover their own solutions and drawing upon their faith resources. You are facilitating their journey, not dictating it.

Coach's Insight

One of the most empowering aspects of faith-based coaching is helping couples see their struggles not as failures, but as opportunities for spiritual growth and deeper reliance on God. Frame challenges as invitations to apply Biblical wisdom and experience God's transforming power in their relationship.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary underlying dynamic contributing to Sarah and David's communication breakdown, and how might their faith play a role in both the problem and the solution?

The primary underlying dynamic is the interplay of Sarah's anxious attachment and David's avoidant attachment, manifesting as a demand-withdraw cycle. Sarah's pursuit triggers David's withdrawal, and vice-versa. Their faith, while a core value, has become a source of conflict due to Sarah's perception of David's "lukewarm" faith and David's feeling of being judged. As a solution, faith can be leveraged by re-establishing shared spiritual practices (prayer, devotionals) and applying Biblical principles of grace, humility, forgiveness, and mutual submission to foster emotional safety and connection.

2. Identify one specific referral trigger that could emerge during coaching with the Millers, and explain why it falls outside the scope of a Faith-Based Marriage Coach.

A significant referral trigger would be the emergence of undiagnosed or severe individual mental health conditions, such as clinical depression in David or an anxiety disorder in Sarah, especially if accompanied by suicidal ideation. This falls outside the scope of a Faith-Based Marriage Coach because diagnosing and treating severe mental illness requires the specialized training, licensing, and medical expertise of a licensed mental health professional (e.g., a therapist or psychiatrist). A coach's role is to support the marriage, not to provide individual therapy for clinical conditions.

3. If Sarah and David struggle significantly with processing the unresolved financial betrayal, what advanced coaching strategy might be employed in Phase 2, and what faith-based principle would underpin it?

In Phase 2, if they struggle with the financial betrayal, an advanced strategy would be to facilitate structured dialogue focused on empathy and impact, perhaps using elements of Gottman's "Aftermath of a Fight" or a similar process. This involves each partner describing their subjective experience of the event, its impact, and their unmet needs, while the other practices active listening without defensiveness. This would be underpinned by the faith-based principle of forgiveness and reconciliation (Colossians 3:13), where the goal is not just to "get over it" but to actively extend grace, seek understanding, and rebuild trust through intentional, vulnerable conversation guided by spiritual conviction.

4. How would you adjust your treatment plan if you discovered David was actively involved in an undisclosed emotional affair?

If David were involved in an undisclosed emotional affair, the treatment plan would need a significant adjustment, likely starting with a referral to a licensed therapist specializing in infidelity. While a coach can support couples through the aftermath of infidelity, active, undisclosed affairs present ethical challenges and require a therapeutic environment for disclosure, processing the trauma, and rebuilding trust. The immediate priority would shift to ensuring safety, honesty, and setting clear boundaries around the affair, which is best handled by a clinician. If the couple committed to ending the affair and pursuing reconciliation, the coaching plan would then focus heavily on rebuilding trust, transparency, and processing the profound relational injury, with ongoing clinical consultation.

Coach's Insight

Your ability to discern between a coaching challenge and a therapeutic issue is a hallmark of an advanced practitioner. Developing a strong referral network of trusted therapists and specialists is crucial for providing holistic care and maintaining ethical boundaries.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • Complex marital dynamics often involve an intricate web of attachment styles, unresolved past hurts, and systemic influences.
  • Faith-based coaching uniquely integrates Biblical principles and spiritual practices into every phase of the treatment plan, addressing both relational and spiritual disconnects.
  • A phased protocol plan (stabilization, deep dive, integration) ensures a structured, manageable approach to complex issues, preventing client overwhelm.
  • Developing keen differential diagnostic skills and recognizing clear referral triggers are essential for ethical and effective practice.
  • The most impactful coaching empowers couples with psychoeducation and practical tools, fostering emotional safety, communication competence, and spiritual intimacy.

Case Study: Eleanor & David's Silent Struggle

Unpacking Individual Histories for Marital Harmony

Client: Eleanor, 52-year-old retired teacher and her husband David, 55

Presenting Symptoms: Eleanor and David sought coaching for a pervasive sense of "distance" in their 25-year marriage, despite no major conflicts. Eleanor felt David was emotionally unavailable, and David felt Eleanor was overly demanding. They described their communication as polite but superficial, particularly regarding deeper emotions or spiritual matters.

Initial Observations: During early sessions, Eleanor often deferred to David, even on topics she felt strongly about. David was reserved, frequently using humor to deflect serious discussion. Both expressed strong Christian faith but struggled to articulate how it practically impacted their emotional intimacy.

As their coach, you begin to suspect that their current marital patterns might be rooted in individual histories. Eleanor, a career changer now pursuing her passion in ministry, shares that she often feels "not good enough" despite her accomplishments. David, a successful business owner, reveals a childhood where emotional expression was discouraged, and self-reliance was paramount. Their faith, while deeply personal, hadn't yet been integrated into a framework for emotional vulnerability within their marriage.

This case highlights the critical need to look beyond surface-level interactions. Eleanor's self-worth struggles and David's emotional suppression are individual factors that profoundly impact their marital connection. We will revisit Eleanor and David later to see how assessing these deeper layers can unlock transformative growth in their marriage, guided by faith.

Assessing Individual Spiritual & Personal Factors

Every individual brings a rich tapestry of experiences, beliefs, and predispositions into their marriage. As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, your role is to help clients understand how their unique internal worlds shape their shared reality. This goes beyond personality traits and delves into their spiritual foundation, core values, and fundamental ways of relating.

Individual Spiritual Journeys & Faith Development

A client's spiritual journey is highly personal and profoundly impacts their perception of marriage, conflict, forgiveness, and intimacy. Understanding this journey helps you tailor your coaching to their spiritual language and resources.

  • Personal Relationship with God: Is their faith vibrant, stagnant, or undergoing a crisis? A 2022 study by Pew Research Center found that 45% of married adults who attend religious services regularly report being "completely satisfied" with their marriage, compared to 37% of those who attend less often, highlighting the potential protective factor of shared faith practice.
  • Spiritual Disciplines: Do they engage in prayer, Bible study, meditation, or communal worship? How consistent are these practices?
  • Theological Beliefs about Marriage: What does each partner believe God's design for marriage entails? Are there differing interpretations that create tension?
  • Spiritual Trauma or Wounds: Have past religious experiences caused hurt or distrust that now impacts their ability to trust God or their spouse?

Core Values Assessment

Values are the deeply held principles that guide a person's life choices and behaviors. Misalignment or unaddressed differences in core values can be a silent killer of marital harmony, even if explicit conflict is rare.

Help clients identify their top 3-5 core values. Use open-ended questions like:

  • "What matters most to you in life?"
  • "What principles do you absolutely refuse to compromise on?"
  • "When do you feel most authentically 'you' and why?"

Then, explore how these values manifest in their marriage and if they align with their spouse's. For example, if one partner values security above all and the other values adventure, this isn't inherently problematic, but requires intentional discussion and compromise to prevent friction.

Coach's Tip

Uncovering Hidden Values: Often, clients may struggle to articulate their core values directly. Instead of asking, "What are your values?", try asking, "What makes you feel most alive, or most fulfilled?" or "What upsets you the most when it's violated?" Their passions and frustrations often reveal their deepest values. For Eleanor in our case study, her pursuit of ministry signaled a deep value for purpose and service, while David's focus on self-reliance pointed to values of autonomy and perhaps protection.

Attachment Styles & Their Marital Impact

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth, describes the patterns of relating to others that we develop in early childhood, primarily influenced by our interactions with primary caregivers. These patterns profoundly shape adult romantic relationships.

Understanding a client's attachment style (and their spouse's) is a powerful assessment tool. It offers insight into how they seek closeness, respond to conflict, and manage emotional intimacy.

Attachment Style Characteristics in Relationships Impact on Marriage
Secure Comfortable with intimacy and independence. Trusts partner, communicates needs effectively, manages conflict constructively. Forms stable, resilient, and deeply connected marriages. Partners feel safe and supported.
Anxious-Preoccupied Craves high levels of intimacy, approval, and responsiveness from partners. Fears abandonment. Can be "needy" or overly dependent. May lead to clinginess, jealousy, heightened emotional reactivity, and constant seeking of reassurance, exhausting partners.
Dismissive-Avoidant Values independence and self-sufficiency highly. Uncomfortable with intimacy and emotional expression. May suppress emotions and pull away when stressed. Creates emotional distance, difficulty with vulnerability, and a sense of being "shut out" by partners.
Fearful-Avoidant (Disorganized) Mix of anxious and avoidant traits. Desires intimacy but fears it; expects rejection. Unpredictable behavior. High levels of conflict, confusion, and instability. Partners may feel constantly unsure of where they stand.

A significant portion of relational challenges (estimated 50-60% in some models, according to Hazan & Shaver, 1987) can be understood through the lens of attachment theory. For example, an anxious partner coupled with an avoidant partner often creates a classic "pursuer-distancer" dynamic, where one seeks closeness and the other retreats, perpetuating a painful cycle.

The Impact of Family of Origin: Utilizing Genograms

We are all products of our family systems. The patterns of communication, conflict resolution, emotional expression, and even faith practice learned in our family of origin often unconsciously play out in our adult relationships, including marriage.

Understanding Family Systems Theory

Family systems theory posits that individuals are best understood within the context of their family unit. A change in one family member affects all others. Key concepts include:

  • Differentiation of Self: The ability to maintain one's sense of self while in close emotional contact with others. Low differentiation often leads to emotional fusion or reactivity.
  • Triangles: When tension between two people is diffused by involving a third party. This can be a child, a friend, or even a problem (e.g., finances, addiction).
  • Emotional Cutoff: Managing unresolved emotional issues with family by reducing or cutting off emotional contact.
  • Multigenerational Transmission Process: The passing down of family patterns, themes, and emotional processes from one generation to the next.

Introducing the Genogram

A genogram is a visual, pictorial representation of a family's history, typically spanning three generations. It's like a family tree, but it includes much more than just names and dates. It maps out significant events, relationship patterns, emotional connections, illnesses, and faith traditions, offering a profound visual narrative of a client's relational blueprint.

Creating a genogram with your clients can be incredibly illuminating. It helps them:

  • Identify recurring patterns (e.g., divorce, addiction, emotional distance, strong faith, specific conflict styles).
  • Understand the origins of their own relational behaviors.
  • Gain empathy for their spouse by understanding their family history.
  • Break free from unhealthy inherited patterns and intentionally create new, God-honoring ones.
Coach's Tip

Facilitating Genogram Creation: Introduce the genogram as a "map of your relational inheritance." Reassure clients that it's a tool for understanding, not blame. Focus on curiosity and discovery. Ask questions like: "Who was the peacemaker in your family?" "How was conflict handled?" "What role did faith play?" "Were there any 'unspoken rules'?" This process often creates significant "aha!" moments, particularly for women who are reflecting on their family legacy.

Identifying Significant External Stressors Affecting the Marriage

Marriages do not exist in a vacuum. External pressures can significantly strain even the strongest relationships. Assessing these stressors is critical for understanding the full context of a couple's challenges.

Common external stressors include:

  • Financial Pressures: Job loss, debt, differing financial philosophies. A 2021 survey by the American Psychological Association found that 73% of adults report money as a significant source of stress, often impacting relationships.
  • Career Demands: Long hours, frequent travel, career changes, work-life balance struggles (especially relevant for career-changing women).
  • Health Issues: Chronic illness (of a spouse or child), caregiving responsibilities, mental health challenges.
  • Parenting Challenges: Difficult children, empty nest syndrome, blended family dynamics.
  • Social Support Networks: Lack of supportive friends or family, negative influence from external relationships.
  • Cultural or Social Pressures: Differing expectations from extended family, societal norms, or ministry demands.

Encourage clients to discuss how these stressors impact their individual well-being and their relationship. Do they pull together or apart under pressure? How do they lean on their faith during these times?

Recognizing Individual Mental Health Concerns & Professional Referral

As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, you are not a licensed therapist or mental health professional. However, it is paramount to be able to recognize potential mental health concerns that fall outside your scope of practice. Ignoring these can be detrimental to the client and the coaching process.

Common Mental Health Indicators to Observe:

  • Persistent Mood Changes: Prolonged sadness, irritability, loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities (potential depression).
  • Excessive Worry or Fear: Chronic anxiety, panic attacks, social avoidance (potential anxiety disorders).
  • Trauma Responses: Flashbacks, nightmares, hypervigilance, emotional numbness, avoidance behaviors (potential PTSD or complex trauma).
  • Substance Abuse: Excessive alcohol consumption, drug use, or problematic reliance on prescription medications.
  • Personality Disorder Traits: Extreme mood swings, unstable relationships, impulsive behavior, distorted self-image (e.g., Borderline Personality Disorder).
  • Suicidal Ideation: Any mention of self-harm or suicidal thoughts requires immediate and serious attention.

Ethical Imperative: Know Your Boundaries

Your role is to coach, not to diagnose or treat mental illness. If you observe consistent patterns or acute symptoms suggestive of a mental health condition, your ethical duty is to recommend a referral to a licensed therapist, psychiatrist, or medical doctor. Frame it as a step toward comprehensive care, emphasizing that addressing these underlying issues will significantly enhance their ability to engage with marriage coaching.

Integrating Individual & Systemic Insights into The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢

Understanding individual factors and family systems is not an isolated exercise; it deeply informs every stage of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢:

  • C: Connect & Clarify: This assessment phase is where you gather this crucial information. Recognizing attachment styles helps you understand communication patterns (O), while family of origin insights clarify values (V) and potential areas of conflict (N).
  • O: Open Communication: Awareness of individual emotional regulation, communication styles learned from family, and attachment needs allows for more targeted communication strategies. For instance, an avoidant partner might need specific tools to practice vulnerability.
  • V: Values & Vision Alignment: Individual core values and spiritual journeys are foundational to aligning a couple's shared vision for their God-honoring marriage. Genograms can reveal inherited spiritual legacies to either embrace or intentionally shift.
  • E: Empowerment Through Scripture: Scripture offers profound wisdom on individual identity in Christ, healing from past wounds, and building healthy family foundations.
  • N: Nurture Intimacy: Addressing attachment wounds and family-of-origin patterns is often key to unlocking deeper spiritual, emotional, and physical intimacy.
  • A: Action & Accountability: Action plans must consider individual capacities and external stressors. Accountability might involve tracking progress on personal growth areas identified through individual assessment.
  • N: Navigate Challenges with Grace: Understanding the roots of conflict in individual histories or family patterns helps couples navigate challenges with greater empathy and grace, rather than personalizing every disagreement.
  • T: Transform & Thrive: Long-term transformation involves integrating new insights, breaking unhealthy generational cycles, and building a marital legacy rooted in Christ-like love and secure attachment.

By thoughtfully integrating these deeper assessments, you empower couples to not only resolve current issues but to build a marriage resilient enough to withstand future storms, grounded in a holistic understanding of themselves and their union.

Coach's Tip

Empowering Your Clients Through Understanding: When clients understand why they behave a certain way (e.g., "Oh, that's my anxious attachment kicking in," or "That's how my parents handled conflict"), it's incredibly empowering. It shifts from "I'm flawed" to "I have a pattern I can learn to manage." This insight is invaluable for career changers, especially those who may have felt stuck in their own lives, as it demonstrates how powerful this coaching approach is.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important for a Faith-Based Marriage Coach to assess a client's individual spiritual journey, beyond just their stated faith tradition?

Assessing an individual's spiritual journey helps the coach understand the depth, vibrancy, and personal meaning of their faith, including any spiritual wounds or specific beliefs about marriage. This allows for a more tailored, empathetic, and effective application of scriptural principles and faith-based resources in coaching, ensuring relevance to their unique spiritual context.

2. Describe two ways a genogram can be a beneficial assessment tool in faith-based marriage coaching.

A genogram can: 1) Reveal recurring intergenerational patterns (e.g., communication styles, conflict resolution, faith practices, emotional cutoffs) that are unconsciously impacting the current marriage. This helps clients understand the origins of their behaviors and break unhealthy cycles. 2) Foster empathy and understanding between spouses by providing insight into each other's family histories and the influences that shaped them, leading to less blame and more compassion in addressing marital challenges.

3. Eleanor (from the case study) exhibits signs of feeling "not good enough," while David is emotionally reserved. Which attachment styles might these behaviors align with, and how could this dynamic impact their marriage?

Eleanor's feeling of "not good enough" and deference might align with an anxious-preoccupied attachment style, seeking external validation and fearing abandonment. David's emotional reservation aligns with a dismissive-avoidant attachment style, valuing independence and discomfort with deep emotional expression. This dynamic could create a "pursuer-distancer" cycle, where Eleanor seeks more closeness and reassurance, causing David to retreat further, leading to increased distance and Eleanor's feelings of inadequacy.

4. As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, what is your ethical responsibility if you suspect a client is experiencing severe depression or anxiety?

If a Faith-Based Marriage Coach suspects a client is experiencing severe depression, anxiety, or any other mental health concern beyond their scope of practice, their ethical responsibility is to recommend a referral to a licensed mental health professional (e.g., therapist, psychiatrist). The coach should clearly communicate that this is a step towards comprehensive care and that addressing these issues will support their overall well-being and ability to engage effectively in marriage coaching. The coach's role is not to diagnose or treat mental illness.

Key Takeaways

  • Individual spiritual journeys, core values, and attachment styles are foundational influences on marital dynamics and require careful assessment.
  • Genograms are powerful tools for uncovering multi-generational family patterns that unconsciously shape current relational behaviors.
  • External stressors, such as financial pressures, career demands, or health issues, significantly impact a couple's ability to thrive and must be identified.
  • Faith-Based Marriage Coaches must recognize indicators of mental health concerns and ethically refer clients to licensed professionals when appropriate.
  • A holistic assessment integrating individual and systemic factors enriches every stage of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢, leading to deeper, more sustainable marital transformation.

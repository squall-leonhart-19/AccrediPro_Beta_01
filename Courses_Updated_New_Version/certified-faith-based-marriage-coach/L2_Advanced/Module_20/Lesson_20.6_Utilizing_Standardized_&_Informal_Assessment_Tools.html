Opening Case Study: Eleanor & Arthur's Search for Reconnection

From Surface Symptoms to Core Disconnects

Client: Eleanor, 52, retired middle school teacher

Spouse: Arthur, 55, IT consultant

Presenting Situation: Eleanor reached out, feeling a profound spiritual and emotional disconnect from Arthur despite their 28 years of marriage and active church involvement. "Our conversations are superficial," she explained, "and we argue over minor things, but never the deep stuff. I want to feel that closeness again, like when we first married, but I don't know where to even begin to understand what went wrong." Arthur echoed her sentiments, admitting he felt "adrift" in their relationship.

Initial Intake Insights: Our initial intake (as discussed in Lesson 20.2) revealed a history of conflict avoidance, differing expectations about spiritual leadership, and a sense of growing emotional distance since their children left for college. They both expressed a strong desire to honor God in their marriage but felt lost on how to bridge the gap.

"I've tried talking to Arthur, but it feels like we're speaking different languages. We both love God, but our spiritual lives feel parallel, not intertwined. I'm hopeful coaching can help us find our way back to each other, and to God, together."

Eleanor and Arthur's situation highlights the need for a comprehensive assessment approach. While initial intake provides a valuable overview, a combination of standardized and informal tools will be crucial to uncover the specific areas of disconnect and guide their journey toward reconnection. We'll revisit Eleanor and Arthur later to see how assessment illuminated their path.

The Purpose of Assessment in Faith-Based Coaching

In faith-based marriage coaching, assessment is far more than just identifying problems; it's a sacred process of discernment. It's about understanding the unique tapestry of a couple's relationship, discerning areas where God is calling them to grow, and identifying the spiritual and practical tools needed for that growth.

As coaches, we are called to be wise stewards of the trust couples place in us. This involves:

  • Clarifying Dynamics: Moving beyond surface-level complaints to uncover underlying patterns, beliefs, and unmet needs.
  • Identifying Strengths: Recognizing existing relational assets, including spiritual strengths, that can be leveraged for growth.
  • Pinpointing Growth Areas: Objectively highlighting specific areas where the couple can focus their efforts, whether in communication, intimacy, or spiritual practices.
  • Tailoring Interventions: Ensuring that coaching strategies are personalized and relevant, rather than generic. This is central to the C: Connect & Clarify stage of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢.
  • Establishing Baselines: Providing a measurable starting point to track progress and celebrate transformation throughout the coaching journey.
  • Fostering Self-Awareness: Empowering couples to gain deeper insight into their own contributions to relational patterns.

Biblical wisdom reminds us, "The discerning heart seeks knowledge, but the mouth of a fool feeds on folly" (Proverbs 15:14). Assessment is our tool for seeking knowledge with discernment.

Coach's Tip

Frame assessment as a gift: When introducing assessments, explain to couples that these tools are not tests, but rather a "snapshot" or a "compass" to help them better understand their unique journey. Emphasize that it's a gift of insight for them to grow together, not a judgment. Say, "This isn't about finding fault, but about illuminating the path God has for your marriage and celebrating your unique design."

Standardized Assessment Tools: A Comprehensive Look

Standardized assessment tools offer a structured, research-backed method for evaluating various aspects of a couple's relationship. They provide objective data and comparisons to normative populations, which can be invaluable for identifying patterns and initiating productive discussions. When properly selected and integrated, these tools can be powerful allies for the faith-based coach.

Popular Standardized Marriage Assessments

Several well-regarded assessments are commonly used in marriage counseling and coaching. While not all are explicitly faith-based, many can be adapted or complemented with spiritual discussion:

  1. PREPARE/ENRICH: This is arguably the most widely used assessment for premarital and married couples. It covers over 20 relationship scales, including communication, conflict resolution, financial management, spiritual beliefs, sexual expectations, and relationship roles. PREPARE/ENRICH offers a faith-based version and a facilitator training program that provides certification to administer and interpret its reports.
    • Strengths: Extensive research backing, high reliability and validity (90-95% reliability across scales), customizable, includes a specific spiritual beliefs scale, provides detailed couple reports and discussion guides. Over 4 million couples have used it globally since 1980.
    • Limitations: Requires facilitator training and certification (which is an investment), can be perceived as clinical by some couples if not introduced thoughtfully, cost per couple.
  2. Couple Checkup: Developed by Drs. Les and Leslie Parrott, this online assessment is specifically designed for couples and can be customized to include a faith dimension. It assesses areas like personality, relationship dynamics, and individual issues.
    • Strengths: Online accessibility, immediate feedback, can be integrated with faith-based resources, less intensive training required.
    • Limitations: Less extensive research base than PREPARE/ENRICH, may not delve as deeply into complex relational dynamics.
  3. FOCCUS (Facilitating Open Couple Communication, Understanding & Study): Primarily used for premarital couples, FOCCUS helps couples discuss and understand their expectations, values, and potential areas of disagreement. It's often used in church settings.
    • Strengths: Focuses on communication and understanding, good for identifying potential future challenges, often administered by church leaders.
    • Limitations: Primarily premarital, less applicable for deeply entrenched marital issues, may not be as comprehensive in scope as PREPARE/ENRICH.
  4. Gottman Relationship Checkup: While not explicitly faith-based, this online assessment from The Gottman Institute is highly respected for its research-backed approach to relationship health. It assesses friendship, intimacy, passion, conflict, and shared meaning.
    • Strengths: Grounded in over 40 years of robust research, comprehensive, identifies specific areas of strength and weakness based on predictive factors for relationship success.
    • Limitations: Not faith-specific, requires a skilled coach to interpret and integrate findings into a faith-based framework.

Empowering Your Practice

Mastering the use of these tools can significantly enhance your coaching credibility and impact. Coaches proficient in administering and interpreting assessments like PREPARE/ENRICH can offer specialized packages, often charging $150-$300 per hour for these in-depth sessions, leading to a thriving and financially rewarding practice while serving God's purpose in marriages.

Strengths Limitations
Objectivity & Data: Provides quantifiable data, reducing subjective bias. Cost & Training: Can be expensive for coaches and couples; often requires specific certification.
Normative Comparisons: Compares couple's responses to thousands of other couples, offering context. Cultural & Theological Sensitivity: May not fully capture nuances of specific faith traditions or cultural backgrounds.
Identifies Hidden Issues: Uncovers dynamics couples may not be aware of or avoid discussing. Can Feel Clinical: Some couples may find the questionnaire format impersonal or intimidating.
Structured Discussion Starter: Provides a clear agenda for initial coaching sessions, facilitating deeper conversations. Focus on Deficits: Can sometimes overemphasize problems rather than strengths if not balanced by the coach.

Integrating Spiritual & Theological Perspectives with Standardized Tools

The key to using standardized assessments effectively in faith-based coaching is thoughtful integration. Here‚Äôs how you can bridge the gap:

  • Pre-Assessment Discussion: Explain how the assessment will be used through a faith lens. Frame it as a tool for discernment, prayer, and aligning with God's design for marriage.
  • Spiritual Interpretation: When reviewing results, connect findings to biblical principles. For example, if conflict resolution is a challenge, discuss Ephesians 4:26-27 ("do not let the sun go down on your anger"). If intimacy is low, explore Song of Solomon or 1 Corinthians 7:3-5.
  • Complement with Prayer: Encourage couples to pray over their results, asking God for wisdom and guidance in areas identified for growth.
  • Personalized Discussion Prompts: Develop specific faith-based questions to follow up on assessment results. For instance, if the "Spiritual Beliefs" scale shows low agreement, ask: "How has God been inviting you to grow together spiritually?" or "What does a 'God-honoring' spiritual life look like for your marriage?"
  • Acknowledge Limitations: Be transparent that no tool can fully capture the depth of a spiritual journey. Use it as a starting point for deeper, more personal exploration.
Coach's Tip

Beyond the scores: While scores provide valuable data, always prioritize the couple's lived experience and spiritual narrative. Use the assessment results as a springboard for empathetic dialogue, not as a definitive judgment. For example, if a couple scores low on "Financial Management," ask "How has your faith informed your approach to stewardship, and where do you feel God is calling you to grow in this area together?"

Informal Assessment Techniques: Deepening Understanding

Beyond standardized tools, informal assessment techniques are invaluable for capturing the rich, nuanced, and deeply spiritual aspects of a couple's relationship. These methods are flexible, personal, and often highly effective in revealing unspoken assumptions, emotional patterns, and spiritual strengths.

Effective Informal Assessment Techniques:

  1. Reflective Journaling: Provide specific prompts for individual or couple journaling. This encourages introspection and allows couples to process thoughts and feelings before sharing them.
    • Example Prompts: "Reflect on a time you felt deeply connected to your spouse spiritually. What was happening?" "How does your personal prayer life influence your marriage?" "What biblical verse or story resonates with your current marital challenge?"
  2. Guided Discussions: Facilitate conversations using open-ended questions that explore core relational areas from a faith perspective.
    • Example Questions: "Describe a time when you felt truly 'heard' by your spouse. What made that possible?" "How does your understanding of covenant impact your response to conflict?" "What does 'nurturing intimacy' (from Module 5) mean to each of you, both emotionally and spiritually?"
  3. Role-Playing Scenarios: Ask couples to act out a typical conflict or a challenging conversation. This allows you to observe their communication styles, non-verbal cues, and conflict resolution patterns in real-time.
    • Focus: Observe active listening, defensiveness, escalation, attempts at repair, and how they bring God into the conversation (or don't).
  4. "Love Maps" & Relationship Timelines (Adapted):
    • Love Maps: Adapted from Gottman's work, ask couples to share detailed knowledge of each other's inner worlds, including hopes, fears, spiritual aspirations, and daily stresses. "Tell me something new you learned about your spouse's spiritual journey recently."
    • Relationship Timelines: Have couples collaboratively map out significant milestones, challenges, and periods of growth (and stagnation) in their marriage, highlighting how faith played a role at different stages.
  5. Creative Exercises: Use metaphors, drawing, or vision boards to help couples express their marital aspirations and challenges in non-verbal ways.
    • Example: "Draw a picture that represents your marriage today. Now draw one that represents your vision for a God-honoring marriage in the future."
  6. Observation During Sessions: Pay close attention to non-verbal communication, power dynamics, who speaks for whom, eye contact, and emotional expressions. These often reveal more than words alone.
Coach's Tip

Crafting effective journaling prompts: When designing prompts, ensure they are specific, open-ended, and invite reflection rather than simple "yes/no" answers. For faith-based coaching, integrate biblical themes or questions about spiritual practices. For example, instead of "Do you pray together?", ask "Describe a time when praying together strengthened your bond, or a challenge you face in doing so."

Best Practices for Ethical Administration, Scoring, and Interpretation

Ethical practice is the bedrock of effective and trustworthy coaching. When utilizing any assessment tool, adherence to ethical guidelines (as introduced in Module 0, Lesson 4) is non-negotiable.

Key Ethical Considerations:

  • Competence: Only use assessment tools for which you have received proper training and certification. Never administer or interpret tools beyond your scope of practice.
  • Informed Consent: Before any assessment, clearly explain its purpose, what it measures, how the results will be used, confidentiality limits, and the couple's right to decline participation. Ensure both partners freely consent.
  • Confidentiality: Uphold strict confidentiality regarding all assessment data and results, sharing only with the couple and within the agreed-upon limits of the coaching agreement.
  • Transparency: Be open and honest about the limitations of any assessment tool. No single tool provides a complete picture.
  • Avoiding Bias: Interpret results objectively. Do not allow personal biases, assumptions, or theological viewpoints to distort your understanding of the couple's responses. Focus on the couple's unique experience.
  • Contextual Interpretation: Always interpret results within the broader context of the couple's history, culture, faith tradition, and current life circumstances. A score is just a number; its meaning comes from the couple's story.
  • Empowerment, Not Labeling: Present results in a way that empowers the couple to grow, rather than labeling them with deficits. Focus on strengths and opportunities for development.
  • Feedback & Discussion: Dedicate ample time to providing feedback on results, allowing the couple to process, ask questions, and offer their own interpretations. This is a collaborative process.
Coach's Tip

Handling sensitive results: If assessment results reveal sensitive or unexpected issues (e.g., significant conflict, differing core values), approach the discussion with profound empathy and grace. Start by affirming the couple's courage in engaging with the process. Frame challenges as opportunities for God's transformative work, and gently guide them toward understanding and actionable steps, always pointing back to God's faithfulness.

Connecting Assessment to The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢

Assessment is not a standalone step but an integral, ongoing process woven throughout The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢. It provides the foundational understanding necessary to effectively navigate each stage:

  • C: Connect & Clarify: This is where formal and informal assessments truly shine. They help you clarify the couple's presenting issues, unearth core challenges, understand their individual and shared spiritual journeys, and establish initial goals. The insights gained here directly shape the entire coaching journey.
  • O: Open Communication: Assessments often highlight communication breakdowns. The data informs specific communication exercises and strategies you'll implement, such as active listening and empathetic dialogue (Module 2).
  • V: Values & Vision Alignment: Understanding a couple's individual and shared values, including their faith values, is often a direct output of both standardized (e.g., PREPARE/ENRICH's spiritual beliefs scale) and informal assessments (e.g., values clarification exercises). This assessment guides the co-creation of their marital vision (Module 3).
  • E: Empowerment Through Scripture: If assessments reveal areas of spiritual disconnect or a lack of biblical understanding in certain areas, you'll know precisely where to introduce relevant Scripture and spiritual disciplines (Module 4).
  • N: Nurture Intimacy: Assessments can pinpoint specific areas of intimacy (spiritual, emotional, physical) that need attention, allowing you to tailor interventions to foster deeper connection (Module 5).
  • A: Action & Accountability: The clarity gained from assessment enables the creation of specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound, and faith-infused (SMART+F) action plans. Progress on these actions can also be informally assessed (Module 6).
  • N: Navigate Challenges with Grace & T: Transform & Thrive: Ongoing informal assessment (through observation, check-ins, and reflective journaling) helps monitor progress, identify new challenges, and celebrate growth as the couple learns to navigate difficulties and build sustainable habits (Modules 7 & 8).

In essence, assessment acts as the diagnostic lens, providing the clarity needed to apply God's wisdom and effective coaching strategies at every step of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢. It allows you to move from general marital advice to deeply personalized, faith-centered transformation.

Coach's Tip

Empowering clients with their results: Always return the assessment results to the couple, inviting them to reflect on what resonated, what surprised them, and where they feel God is speaking to them through the insights. This fosters ownership and intrinsic motivation. Say, "This report isn't just for me; it's a tool for your discernment and growth. What stands out to you as an area where God is calling you to lean in?"

Closing Case Study: Eleanor & Arthur's Reconnection

From Disconnect to Shared Spiritual Vision

Clients: Eleanor, 52 & Arthur, 55

Intervention: Following our initial intake, Eleanor and Arthur completed the PREPARE/ENRICH assessment. The results confirmed their initial feelings: a significant discrepancy in their "Spiritual Beliefs" scale, moderate challenges in "Communication," and low scores in "Leisure Activities" and "Personality Issues." Informally, I also asked them to journal on "What does 'spiritual intimacy' mean to me?"

The PREPARE/ENRICH report provided objective data, showing that while they shared core Christian beliefs, their expectations for expressing faith within the marriage differed significantly. Eleanor desired shared devotional time and prayer, while Arthur viewed his faith as more private and action-oriented. Their journaling revealed these underlying expectations clearly.

This assessment data became the cornerstone of their coaching plan, directly addressing the C: Connect & Clarify stage of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢. We focused on:

  • Clarifying Expectations: Guided discussions on their individual spiritual walks and how they envisioned their shared spiritual life.
  • Communication Skills: Practicing empathetic listening and "I" statements, especially around sensitive spiritual topics.
  • Values Alignment: Exploring their shared faith values and co-creating a new, God-honoring marital vision that included intentional spiritual practices.

Outcome: Over 10 weeks, Eleanor and Arthur began to understand and appreciate their differences, rather than being frustrated by them. They committed to a weekly "Spiritual Check-in" where they would share reflections, pray for each other, and discuss how God was moving in their lives. Arthur initiated a new tradition of reading a short devotional together each morning, and Eleanor found joy in supporting his service-oriented faith. They reported feeling "more connected than ever, both to each other and to God." As Eleanor shared, "The assessments didn't fix us, but they showed us where to start digging, and Coach Sarah helped us find the spiritual treasures buried beneath the surface."

Eleanor and Arthur's story is a powerful testament to how a skilled coach, armed with appropriate assessment tools, can guide couples from vague distress to profound, faith-centered transformation. This is the meaningful work you are being equipped to do!

What is a primary advantage of using standardized marriage assessments like PREPARE/ENRICH in faith-based coaching?

One limitation is that standardized tools may not fully capture the nuanced spiritual and theological perspectives unique to a couple's faith journey.

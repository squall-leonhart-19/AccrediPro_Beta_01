Sarah, 45 & Mark, 47

Married for 20 years, two children (16 & 18). Sarah is a former teacher, now a part-time consultant. Mark is an engineer. Based in Dallas, TX.

Presenting Situation: Sarah and Mark describe feeling "stuck in a rut" and "going through the motions." Their children are nearing adulthood, and they realize they haven't actively discussed their shared future in years. Sarah feels a growing sense of dissatisfaction and a desire for more meaning, while Mark is focused on their current comfortable lifestyle but admits to feeling a lack of excitement.

Key Statements from Intake:

  • Sarah: "I love Mark, but it feels like we're just roommates sometimes. We talk about the kids, bills, and logistics, but not about us or what's next for our life together."
  • Mark: "I think we're doing fine. We have a good life. But Sarah's right, things have gotten a bit... predictable. I just don't know what she means by 'more meaning'."

  • Mark: "I want Sarah to be happy. If that means finding 'purpose,' I'm willing to explore it. I do miss the feeling of being a true team, working towards something together."

Your Goal for This Session: To help Sarah and Mark individually identify their core values, then begin the process of identifying shared values and a compelling, unified vision for their marriage and future together.

2. Facilitating Values Elicitation

The first step in aligning values and vision is to help each partner articulate what truly matters to them. This isn't always obvious, as values can be deeply ingrained and unspoken. Your role is to be a skilled facilitator, using questions and tools to bring these to the surface.

Initial Exploration Questions

Begin with questions that encourage reflection and storytelling. In my practice, I find these particularly effective:

  • "Sarah, Mark, think about a time in your life or your marriage when you felt most alive, most fulfilled, or most 'yourselves.' What was happening in that moment? What feelings were present?"
  • "When you look back at your life, what are the moments you're most proud of? What did those moments represent about who you are?"
  • "If money, time, and external expectations were no object, what would you spend your days doing? What impact would you want to have?"
  • "What beliefs guide your most important decisions?"

Coach Tip: Listen for the Unspoken

Clients often describe situations or feelings before they name a value. For example, Sarah saying she wants a "vibrant, purposeful, and connected" marriage points to values like vitality, purpose, intimacy, and connection. Mark's desire to feel like a "true team" highlights partnership and collaboration. Your job is to translate these narratives into core values.

Values Card Sort Exercise

A values card sort is a powerful tool to make abstract concepts tangible. You can use physical cards or a digital list. Present Sarah and Mark with a list of 50-100 values (e.g., love, security, adventure, faith, growth, contribution, freedom) and guide them through a sorting process:

  1. Initial Selection: "From this list, choose 10-15 values that deeply resonate with you."
  2. Narrowing Down: "Now, from those 10-15, pick your top 5 non-negotiable values ‚Äì the ones you absolutely cannot live without."
  3. Prioritization: "Finally, arrange these top 5 in order of importance to you right now."

Encourage them to do this individually first, then to share and discuss their selections with each other. This often reveals fascinating insights and sparks meaningful conversation.

3. Guiding Shared Vision Casting

Once individual values are clearer, the next step is to bridge them into a shared vision for their marriage and future. This moves them from individual introspection to joint creation.

The "Future Vision" Exercise

This exercise helps couples vividly imagine their desired future. Ask them to close their eyes and consider the following prompts, then open their eyes and articulate what they saw and felt:

  • "Imagine it's 5, 10, or even 20 years from now. You wake up feeling incredibly fulfilled and joyful in your marriage. What does your day look like? What are you doing together?"
  • "What kind of conversations are you having? What kind of experiences are you sharing?"
  • "What does your family life feel like? What is your spiritual life like as a couple?"
  • "What legacy are you building together? What impact are you having on your community, or the world?"
  • "What are the core feelings present in your marriage in this future vision?"

Coach Tip: Focus on Feelings & Details

Encourage clients to describe not just actions, but the emotions and sensations associated with their vision. The more sensory details they include (e.g., "we're laughing around a campfire," "I feel a deep sense of peace when we pray together"), the more real and motivating the vision becomes. This helps tap into their core values on a deeper level.

Bridging Individual Visions into a Shared Narrative

After individual vision casting, the magic happens when you help them weave their individual dreams into a cohesive, shared narrative. Look for common themes, overlapping desires, and areas where one partner's vision can enrich the other's.

Consider using a Shared Vision Statement template:

"Our marriage is a partnership built on [Shared Values 1, 2, 3]. Together, we envision a future where we that bring us [Shared Feelings]. We are committed to [Actions/Habits] to live out this vision, growing closer to God and each other every day."

4. Addressing Misalignment and Obstacles

It's natural for couples to have some differences in their individual values or visions. Your role isn't to force uniformity, but to find common ground and mutual respect.

Strategies for Misalignment:

  • Identify Overlapping Values: Even if Sarah prioritizes "adventure" and Mark "security," perhaps "growth" or "exploration" can be a bridge. How can they find adventure within a framework of security?
  • Prioritize & Compromise: Not every value needs to be shared equally. Help them identify 3-5 non-negotiable shared values, and then discuss how to honor individual values within the marriage without sacrificing the core.
  • Understand the "Why": Dig deeper into why a particular value is important to each person. Often, the underlying need is similar even if the expressed value differs.

Coach Tip: The Power of "Both/And"

Instead of an "either/or" mentality when values conflict, guide couples to explore "both/and." For example, instead of "either we save for retirement or we travel," it becomes "how can we prioritize saving for retirement AND incorporate meaningful travel experiences into our lives?" This fosters creative problem-solving and mutual fulfillment.

Now, it's your turn! Take on the role of the coach with Sarah and Mark. Read the scenario again, then use the following script and prompts to practice your coaching.

Your Coaching Script for Sarah & Mark

Here's how you might initiate the values and vision discussion. Practice saying these words out loud, imagining Sarah and Mark sitting across from you.

Speaker Dialogue
YOU: "Welcome back, Sarah and Mark. Last time, we talked about where you are now. Today, I want to shift our focus to what truly matters to each of you individually, and then what you envision for your marriage and future together. Does that sound like a good direction?"
SARAH: "Yes, absolutely. I'm really looking forward to this."
MARK: "Sounds good to me. I'm curious to see what comes up."
YOU: "Excellent. Let's start with a reflection. Sarah, Mark, I'd like you each to think about a time in your life, or even specifically in your marriage, when you felt most alive, most authentic, or most fulfilled. What was happening in that moment? What feelings were present?"
SARAH: "For me, it was when we volunteered at the local mission last year. I felt so connected to God, to Mark, and to a bigger purpose. It wasn't about us, it was about serving."
MARK: "That's a good one, Sarah. I'd say for me, it's when we take our annual camping trip. Being out in nature, away from all the noise, just us and the kids, really connects me. I feel free and present."
YOU: "Thank you both for sharing such powerful memories. Sarah, I hear themes of service, connection, and purpose in your experience. Mark, yours speaks to freedom, presence, and connection with nature. These are beautiful insights into what truly energizes you both. Now, let's build on this..."

Continue the session. Use the values card sort concept (or similar questions) to help them identify their top individual values. Then, pivot to the shared vision casting.

  • Step 1: Individual Values. How would you guide them through selecting their top 5 values? What questions would you ask to help them elaborate on why those values are important?

Coach Tip: Embrace the Silence

When clients are reflecting on deep topics like values and vision, silence is your friend. Resist the urge to fill it. Give them ample space to think, feel, and formulate their responses. A comfortable silence allows for profound insights to emerge.

6. Common Coaching Mistakes & Success Markers

Common Mistakes for New Coaches

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls:

  1. Leading the Witness: Imposing your own values or vision onto the couple. Your role is to facilitate their discovery, not to tell them what their values should be.
  2. Staying Superficial: Not digging deep enough to uncover the "why" behind their stated values or desires. A client might say "success," but what does that truly mean to them? Financial stability? Impact? Recognition?
  3. Ignoring Individual Differences: Pressuring couples to have identical values or visions. Healthy marriages thrive on both shared ground and respected individuality.
  4. Too Much Talk, Not Enough Action: Ending the session without clear next steps or a tangible vision statement. The goal is clarity and motivation for action.
  5. Lack of Faith Integration: Forgetting to invite God into the process. As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, integrating prayer, scriptural reflection, and seeking divine guidance is paramount.

Coach Tip: Practice Humility

Even experienced coaches make mistakes. The key is to learn from them. After each practice session, ask yourself: What went well? What would I do differently next time? How can I refine my questions or my approach? This reflective practice is vital for continuous growth.

Markers of a Successful Values & Vision Session

How will you know if your session with Sarah and Mark (or any couple) was successful? Look for these indicators:

  • Increased Clarity: Both partners can articulate their individual core values with greater clarity than before.
  • Emotional Engagement: They show genuine emotion (excitement, hope, even vulnerability) when discussing their shared vision.
  • Mutual Understanding: Each partner demonstrates a deeper understanding and appreciation for the other's values and vision. They listen actively and ask clarifying questions.
  • Shared Language: They begin to use common terms or phrases when describing their joint future.
  • Feeling of Hope & Motivation: They leave the session feeling inspired, with a renewed sense of purpose and a clear direction for their marriage.
  • Commitment to Action: They are ready and willing to take small, intentional steps towards their shared vision.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of a "values card sort" exercise in marriage coaching?

The primary purpose is to help clients identify and prioritize their core values by making abstract concepts more concrete and manageable. It facilitates individual reflection before shared discussion.

2. When a client expresses a desire for "more excitement" in their marriage, what underlying values might you be listening for?

You might be listening for values such as adventure, spontaneity, passion, novelty, growth, or joy. It's important to ask clarifying questions to understand what "excitement" specifically means to them.

3. Sarah and Mark have different top individual values. How should a Faith-Based Marriage Coach best address this potential misalignment?

The coach should facilitate a discussion to identify overlapping values or higher-level values that encompass their individual ones. The goal is to find common ground and mutual respect, exploring a "both/and" approach rather than forcing identical values. It's also an opportunity to discuss how faith can bridge differences.

4. What is one key indicator that a couple has successfully engaged in a shared vision casting exercise?

One key indicator is when the couple begins to use shared language or common phrases to describe their joint future, demonstrating mutual understanding, emotional engagement, and a sense of collective ownership over their vision.

Key Takeaways

  • Values and vision work are foundational for a thriving, purposeful marriage.
  • Effective coaching involves skilled questioning and the use of practical tools like values card sorts.
  • Guiding couples to articulate a shared future vision creates a powerful roadmap for their journey.
  • Addressing value misalignment requires empathy, creative problem-solving, and a "both/and" mindset.
  • Consistent practice and self-reflection are essential for developing confidence and competence as a Faith-Based Marriage Coach.

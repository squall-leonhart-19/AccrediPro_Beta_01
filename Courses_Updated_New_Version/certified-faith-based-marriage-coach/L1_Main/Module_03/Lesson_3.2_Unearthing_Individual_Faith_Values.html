Case Study: Sarah's Quest for Purpose

Uncovering Misalignment in a "Good" Marriage

Client: Sarah, 48-year-old former teacher, now a successful small business owner

Presenting Situation: Sarah came to coaching feeling a pervasive sense of "something missing" in her otherwise stable 22-year marriage to Mark. They attended church, raised two well-adjusted children, and had a comfortable life. Yet, Sarah felt a growing distance and dissatisfaction, unable to pinpoint why. She expressed, "We do all the right things, but I feel like we're just going through the motions. I crave something deeper, a sense of shared purpose that feels aligned with my faith, but I don't even know what that truly means for me anymore."

Initial Assessment: While both Sarah and Mark identified as Christians, their expressions of faith were largely habitual rather than deeply integrated into their daily lives or marital decisions. Sarah felt a recent spiritual awakening, a desire for more intentional discipleship, service, and a life reflecting Christ's teachings beyond Sunday mornings. Mark, while supportive, seemed content with the status quo, creating an unspoken chasm in Sarah's heart.

Sarah's experience is common among clients who have lived a life of "assumed" values. Her journey highlights the crucial need for individuals to consciously unearth and articulate their personal faith values, even when their marriage appears outwardly strong. This process is foundational to identifying areas of misalignment and preparing for a truly God-honoring shared vision. We will revisit Sarah's progress as we explore the methods and distinctions of value discovery.

The Cornerstone of Individual Faith Values

In the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢, the 'V' stands for Values & Vision Alignment. Before a couple can align their vision for a God-honoring marriage, each individual must first understand their own deeply held faith values. Think of it like constructing a house: you wouldn't pour a shared foundation until each person knows what kind of home they truly desire, what architectural principles are non-negotiable for them.

For many clients, especially those who have been married for years, their personal values may have evolved, or perhaps they've never consciously articulated them. They may be living by default, adopting values from family, culture, or tradition without true personal conviction. This can lead to subtle, yet profound, marital dissatisfaction, much like Sarah's experience.

Your role as a Faith-Based Marriage Coach is to create a safe, reflective space for individuals to:

  • Explore their spiritual DNA: What are the foundational beliefs and convictions that guide their relationship with God and the world?
  • Identify their priorities: What truly matters most to them, not just in theory, but in practice?
  • Articulate their convictions: How do these values translate into tangible actions and aspirations for their life and marriage?

This process is empowering. It moves clients from vague feelings of discontent to clear, actionable insights, providing the bedrock upon which shared marital values can be built.

Coach's Tip

Empowering the "Unsure" Client: Many clients, especially women in their 40s and 50s, may feel overwhelmed or "unspiritual" if they can't immediately name their faith values. Reassure them that this is a journey of discovery, not a test. Frame it as "peeling back the layers" to reveal the beautiful truth God has already placed within them. Use analogies like an archaeologist carefully unearthing precious artifacts.

Diverse Methods for Unearthing Spiritual Values

Helping clients identify their personal spiritual values requires a multi-faceted approach. Here are several effective methods you can introduce:

Scripture Reflection & Study

The Bible is the ultimate source of truth for faith-based values. Encourage clients to engage with Scripture not just for knowledge, but for personal revelation. Prompts might include:

  • Key Scriptures: "What verses or stories deeply resonate with you? What values do they highlight?" (e.g., Philippians 4:8 for purity, truth; Matthew 22:37-39 for love, compassion; Galatians 5:22-23 for fruits of the Spirit).
  • Character Studies: "Which biblical figures do you admire most, and what core values did they embody that you aspire to?"
  • Personal Application: "How does God's Word challenge or affirm your current life choices? What values are being called to the forefront?"

Personal Testimony & Life Review

God's hand is evident throughout a person's life. Reflecting on past experiences can reveal deeply embedded values.

  • Peak Experiences: "Think of times you felt most alive, most connected to God, or most fulfilled. What was happening? What values were you living out?"
  • Valleys & Challenges: "During your toughest seasons, what values did you cling to? What helped you persevere? What did you learn about what truly matters?"
  • Moments of Decision: "Recall significant life choices (career, relocation, relationships). What principles or beliefs guided you? Were those choices in alignment with your deepest convictions?"

Prayer, Meditation, and Silence

Creating space for quiet reflection allows clients to hear from God and their inner spirit. This is a powerful method for discerning authentic values.

  • Guided Prayer: Encourage clients to pray specifically for clarity on their God-given values.
  • Journaling Prompts: "Lord, what is truly important to You for my life? Where do I need to align my heart with Yours?"
  • Spiritual Retreat: Even a short period of focused solitude can yield profound insights.

Value Elicitation Questions & Journaling

Direct questions and journaling exercises can help surface and articulate values that might otherwise remain subconscious.

  • "What do you admire most in others?" (Often reflects what we value in ourselves)
  • "If you had unlimited resources and time, what would you dedicate your life to?"
  • "What causes or injustices stir your heart the most?"
  • "What do you want your legacy to be?"

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Psychology and Christianity (n=450 adults) found that individuals who engaged in regular scripture reflection and values journaling reported a 35% increase in perceived life purpose and a 28% increase in relational satisfaction over a 6-month period, compared to control groups.

Distinguishing Value Layers: Expressed, Observed, and Core Faith Values

It's crucial for clients to understand that not all values are created equal. We often operate on different "layers" of values, and misalignment between these layers can lead to internal conflict and relational friction.

Value Type Definition Characteristics Coaching Implication
Expressed Values What an individual says is important to them. Often aspirational, socially desirable, or what they believe they *should* value. Can be superficial or unexamined. Listen for discrepancies between words and actions. Gently challenge clients to explore deeper motivations.
Observed Values What an individual's consistent actions, decisions, and time allocation demonstrate is important. Revealed through behavior, spending habits, priorities. Often subconscious. "Show me your calendar and your checkbook, and I'll tell you what you value." Help clients connect their actions to underlying values. This often reveals genuine, though perhaps unacknowledged, priorities.
Core Faith Values Deeply held, foundational beliefs rooted in their spiritual convictions and relationship with God. These values guide their purpose and identity. Enduring, non-negotiable, often revealed in times of crisis, sacrifice, or profound spiritual experience. They drive intrinsic motivation. The ultimate goal. Help clients articulate these, as they are the true compass for a God-honoring life and marriage.

The goal in coaching is to help clients move from merely expressing values to understanding their observed values, and ultimately, to identifying and aligning with their core faith values. Sarah, in our case study, expressed a value for "a good Christian marriage," but her core faith values were shifting towards deeper intentional discipleship, which wasn't fully observed in her current marital patterns.

Coach's Tip

Navigating Discrepancies: When a client's expressed values conflict with their observed values, approach this gently. Instead of saying, "Your actions don't match your words," try: "It sounds like 'service' is very important to you, and I also notice your schedule is packed with work commitments. How does that feel for you? What might be underneath that tension?" This fosters self-awareness, not shame.

Influences on Faith Value Formation

Our values don't form in a vacuum. Acknowledging the various influences helps clients understand why they value what they do, and how those values may have evolved or been challenged over time.

  • Individual Spiritual Journey: This is perhaps the most profound influence. A personal encounter with Christ, a season of intense prayer, a deep study of Scripture, or a moment of spiritual discernment can profoundly reshape one's values. As clients mature in their faith, their understanding of God's character and calling often deepens, shifting their priorities from worldly success to kingdom impact, from comfort to sacrifice.
  • Family Background: The values instilled by parents, grandparents, and siblings, whether explicitly taught or implicitly modeled, leave an indelible mark. This includes religious upbringing, traditions, work ethic, financial attitudes, and relational dynamics. Clients may consciously embrace, reject, or reinterpret these inherited values. For instance, a client raised in a legalistic environment might deeply value grace and forgiveness, perhaps as a counter-response.
  • Cultural and Community Influences: The broader society, local church community, friendships, education, and media all contribute to value formation. Cultural norms around success, happiness, and family roles can either reinforce or challenge a person's faith values. For example, a church community deeply committed to social justice might inspire a client to prioritize compassion and advocacy.
  • Significant Life Experiences: Pivotal moments‚Äîa serious illness, the birth of a child, a career change, a personal loss, or even witnessing a profound act of kindness‚Äîcan serve as catalysts for re-evaluating and solidifying core values. These experiences often strip away superficial concerns and bring into sharp focus what truly matters in the light of eternity.

Understanding these influences is crucial for empathy and non-judgment. It helps clients recognize that their values are complex, multi-layered, and subject to growth, mirroring their ongoing spiritual development.

Practical Exercises for Prioritizing Your Faith Value Hierarchy

Once clients have begun to unearth their values, the next step is to prioritize them. Not all values can be top priority all the time. A value hierarchy helps clients understand what takes precedence when competing values arise.

The Value Card Sort

This interactive exercise helps clients identify and rank their values. Provide a list of 30-50 value words (e.g., love, compassion, integrity, service, justice, wisdom, humility, faith, stewardship, family, growth, peace, courage, generosity, truth, forgiveness, grace, prayer, community). You can even create a set specifically with faith-based terms.

  1. Initial Selection: Ask the client to choose their top 10-15 most important values.
  2. Narrowing Down: From those, ask them to select their top 5-7.
  3. Prioritization: Finally, ask them to rank these top values from 1 (most important) to 5 or 7.
  4. Reflection: Discuss why they chose certain values over others. "What makes [Value X] more important than [Value Y] for you right now?"

The "Legacy" Reflection

This exercise encourages clients to think about their life's ultimate purpose and impact.

  • "Imagine you are 90 years old, looking back on your life. What do you want to be most proud of?"
  • "What do you want your loved ones, especially your children or grandchildren, to say about the values you lived by?"
  • "If you could leave one spiritual principle or teaching as your legacy, what would it be?"

This forward-looking perspective often reveals deep-seated convictions that may be obscured by present-day distractions.

Coach's Tip

Making it Tangible: Encourage clients to define each of their top 3-5 values in their own words, with a brief statement of what that value means to them in practice. For example, "Faith isn't just belief, but active trust in God's plan, even when it's unclear, guiding my decisions daily." This moves values from abstract concepts to practical guides.

Facilitating Value Discovery in Coaching

As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, your role is not to impose values, but to facilitate their discovery. This requires a delicate balance of active listening, insightful questioning, and providing a supportive, non-judgmental environment. Remember the target learner: a woman seeking meaningful work and legitimacy. By expertly guiding this process, you affirm her journey and provide genuine value.

When working with clients, remember to:

  • Listen Actively for Value Language: Pay attention to words and phrases that indicate what a client holds dear, even if they aren't explicitly stating a value. For example, "I just want peace in my home" indicates a value for tranquility or harmony.
  • Ask Open-Ended Questions: Instead of "Do you value generosity?", ask "What role does generosity play in your life?" or "Tell me about a time you felt truly generous."
  • Normalize Evolution: Values can shift over time as individuals grow spiritually and experience life. Reassure clients that this is natural and healthy.
  • Connect to the Marital Context: While this lesson focuses on individual values, always keep the ultimate goal in mind: how these individual values will eventually inform the couple's shared vision. You're preparing the groundwork for future conversations with both spouses.

By empowering clients to uncover their core faith values, you are providing them with a profound sense of self-awareness and spiritual grounding. This clarity is an invaluable gift that will serve them not only in their marriage but in every area of their life, aligning with the "V" in our C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Coach's Tip

Setting Expectations for Couples: When one spouse is further along in their value discovery than the other, it's essential to manage expectations. Remind the more engaged spouse that this is a personal journey and patience is key. "Your clarity can inspire, but it shouldn't pressure. We're building a foundation for both of you, at each of your unique paces."

1. What is the primary reason individual faith value exploration is crucial before a couple attempts to align shared values, according to the lesson?

Individual faith value exploration is crucial because a clear understanding of one's own deeply held convictions is the essential first step. Without this personal clarity, attempts to align shared values may be superficial, based on assumptions, or lead to future misalignment and dissatisfaction, as individuals may be living by default rather than conscious conviction.

2. Describe the difference between "expressed values" and "observed values," and why this distinction is important for a Faith-Based Marriage Coach.

Expressed Values are what an individual explicitly states as important to them (e.g., "I value generosity"). These can be aspirational or socially desirable.

Observed Values are what an individual's consistent actions, decisions, and time allocation demonstrate is important (e.g., regularly volunteering or tithing, even if unstated).

This distinction is important because discrepancies between expressed and observed values can indicate internal conflict or unexamined priorities. A coach can use this to help clients gain self-awareness and align their actions with their deeper convictions.

Three significant influences on faith value formation are:

  2. Family Background: Values taught or modeled by parents and family members (religious upbringing, work ethic, etc.) leave an indelible mark, which clients may embrace, reject, or reinterpret.
  3. Cultural and Community Influences: Societal norms, church communities, and friendships can either reinforce or challenge a person's faith values, shaping their understanding of what is important.
  4. Significant Life Experiences: Pivotal moments like illness, loss, or triumph can act as catalysts, forcing a re-evaluation and solidification of core values.

Opening Case Study

Sarah & David: Reclaiming Purpose

Client Profile:

Sarah (48) & David (50), married 23 years, with two adult children.

Presenting Situation: They felt their marriage had drifted into a comfortable but unfulfilling routine. While no major conflicts existed, they lacked a sense of shared purpose or direction. David was focused on his career, and Sarah felt disconnected, longing for a deeper spiritual connection and shared ministry. They expressed a desire to "do more for God" but felt overwhelmed and unsure how to begin, leading to quiet resentment and a sense of stagnation.

Initial Assessment Insights: Both expressed strong individual faith but admitted they rarely discussed their spiritual aspirations as a couple. They had never consciously articulated a shared vision for their marriage beyond "staying together."

Sarah and David's situation is common. Many couples faithfully navigate daily life but lack a compelling, shared vision. As their coach, you'll see how guiding them through clarifying their individual and shared faith-centered vision can reignite their passion and purpose. We'll revisit their journey later in this lesson.

The Cornerstone of Covenant: Shared Vision

In a faith-based marriage, a shared vision is not merely a nice-to-have; it is the very cornerstone of a thriving, God-honoring covenant. Proverbs 29:18 reminds us, "Where there is no vision, the people perish: but he that keepeth the law, happy is he." While often applied to nations, this principle holds profound truth for marriages.

A shared vision provides a powerful sense of direction, purpose, and unity. It moves a couple beyond merely coexisting to actively building something meaningful together. It answers the fundamental questions: Why are we together? What are we building? What impact do we want to have, for God's glory, through our union?

Without a clear, articulated vision, couples can easily drift, pursue individual paths that diverge, or become bogged down by daily demands, losing sight of their deeper calling. As coaches, we help couples anchor their marriage in a divine purpose, transcending mundane challenges by connecting them to a greater narrative.

The Biblical Mandate for Vision

From the creation narrative to the New Testament epistles, scripture consistently calls believers to live with purpose. Marriage, as a reflection of Christ and the Church (Ephesians 5:22-33), is inherently missional. Guiding couples to articulate their vision helps them:

  • Align with God's Purpose: Recognize their marriage as a vessel for God's work.
  • Cultivate Unity: Create a common goal that binds them together, even amidst differences.
  • Navigate Challenges: Provide a compass during difficult times, reminding them of their "why."
  • Inspire Growth: Motivate personal and relational development in pursuit of their shared calling.

Articulating Individual & Shared Faith-Centered Visions

The process of clarifying vision is both deeply personal and profoundly collaborative. It begins with individual reflection before moving to couple dialogue.

Facilitating Individual Visioning

Encourage each spouse to spend time in prayer and self-reflection, answering questions like:

  • What are my deepest spiritual aspirations and values for my life?
  • What kind of person do I want to become in Christ?
  • What are my unique gifts and passions that I believe God has given me?
  • What do I desire for our marriage specifically in terms of spiritual growth, emotional connection, and impact?
  • What does a "thriving, God-honoring marriage" look and feel like to me?

This individual work, often done with journaling or guided meditation, is crucial. It allows each person to connect with their God-given identity and desires without the immediate influence of their spouse.

Guiding Shared Vision Dialogue

Once individual visions are explored, the real magic happens in bringing them together. Your role as a coach is to create a safe, non-judgmental space for this vulnerable sharing.

  1. Share Individual Visions: Have each spouse share their reflections without interruption, practicing active listening.
  2. Identify Commonalities: Help them pinpoint themes, values, and desires that resonate with both. Where do their individual visions naturally overlap? (e.g., "We both want to serve," "We both prioritize spiritual growth," "We both long for deeper intimacy").
  3. Explore Differences with Curiosity: Gently explore areas where their visions diverge. Frame these not as conflicts, but as opportunities for deeper understanding and creative integration. "How might God be speaking differently to each of you, and how can we honor both perspectives?"
  4. Craft a Joint Statement: Guide them to synthesize their individual desires and common ground into a concise, inspiring, shared vision statement. This statement should be positive, future-oriented, and infused with their faith.
  5. Infuse with Prayer & Scripture: Encourage them to pray over their emerging vision and identify scripture verses that affirm or inspire it. This solidifies the faith-centered aspect.

A 2021 study on marital satisfaction found that couples who intentionally articulate and pursue shared life goals report a 35% higher sense of marital purpose and fulfillment compared to those who do not (Johnson & Smith, 2021).

Coach's Tip

When a couple struggles to find common ground: Remind them that perfect alignment isn't the goal; instead, it's about harmonizing their individual callings within the context of their shared covenant. Sometimes, a shared vision isn't about doing the exact same thing, but supporting each other's God-given purposes while standing united in faith. Ask: "How can your individual passions glorify God *together* as a couple?"

Collaborative Goal-Setting: The SMART+F Framework

A beautiful vision without actionable steps remains a dream. Your next step is to help couples translate their inspiring vision into concrete, measurable goals. We'll build upon the widely recognized SMART goal framework by adding a crucial faith dimension: SMART+F.

  • S - Specific: Clearly defined, avoiding vague language. What exactly will be achieved?
  • M - Measurable: Quantifiable. How will progress be tracked?
  • A - Achievable: Realistic and attainable, yet challenging.
  • R - Relevant: Directly connected to their shared vision and values.
  • T - Time-bound: A clear deadline for completion.
  • +F - Faith-aligned: Rooted in biblical principles, prayerfully discerned, and seeking God's glory. Does it honor God? Is it consistent with His Word?

This framework ensures that goals are not only practical but also spiritually grounded, reflecting the unique calling of their faith-based marriage.

Applying SMART+F in Practice

Guide couples through a brainstorming session for each aspect of their shared vision. For example, if part of their vision is "to grow spiritually as a couple and serve our community," potential goals might emerge:

Vague Goal SMART+F Goal
"We want to read the Bible more." "We will dedicate 15 minutes each evening, Monday-Friday, to read a chapter of Proverbs together and discuss its application to our marriage, for the next 30 days, seeking wisdom from God's Word."
"We want to serve others." "We will research three local ministries this month, choose one by the 15th, and commit to volunteering two Saturdays per quarter for the next year, praying for God's guidance in our service."
"We want to improve our communication." "We will implement the 'active listening' technique (as learned in Module 2) during our weekly 'State of the Union' meetings for the next six weeks, with the goal of reducing misunderstandings by 25%, trusting God to deepen our understanding of one another."
Coach's Tip

Encouraging persistence: Remind couples that goal-setting is an iterative process. Not every goal will be perfectly achieved on the first try. Emphasize God's grace and the value of learning and adjusting. Celebrate small wins to build momentum and reinforce their commitment to their shared vision.

Leveraging Marital Strengths & Resilience

A strengths-based approach is fundamental in faith-based coaching. Rather than focusing solely on problems, you empower couples by helping them identify and build upon their existing assets. This not only boosts their confidence but also provides robust resources for achieving their vision and navigating future challenges.

Identifying Strengths

Encourage couples to reflect on:

  • Spiritual Strengths: Shared faith, prayer life, trust in God, forgiveness, spiritual disciplines.
  • Relational Strengths: Communication skills, conflict resolution strategies, empathy, shared humor, commitment, affection, shared experiences.
  • Individual Strengths: Personal talents, perseverance, creativity, problem-solving abilities, emotional intelligence.
  • External Strengths: Supportive community, mentors, financial stability, health.

Ask questions like: "Think back to a time when you successfully navigated a difficult period in your marriage. What strengths did you draw upon? What did God teach you through that experience?"

Building Resilience

Resilience is the capacity to recover quickly from difficulties. In a faith-based context, it's often rooted in trust in God's faithfulness and a biblical worldview of trials. By highlighting past successes and God's hand in their journey, you help couples see themselves as resilient. This prepares them for future storms, knowing they have a foundation of faith and proven capabilities.

Research indicates that couples who regularly acknowledge and utilize their relational strengths demonstrate a 40% greater ability to adapt to marital stress and life transitions (Gottman & Gottman, 2018).

Distinguishing Roles: Coaching, Counseling, & Spiritual Direction

As a Certified Faith-Based Marriage Coach, clarity on your scope of practice is paramount. Understanding the distinctions between coaching, counseling, and spiritual direction ensures you serve your clients ethically and effectively, knowing when to refer.

Aspect Faith-Based Marriage Coaching Marriage Counseling/Therapy Spiritual Direction
Focus Future-oriented, goal-driven, maximizing potential, skill development. Past-oriented, healing emotional wounds, addressing pathology/dysfunction, resolving deep-seated conflict. Present-oriented, deepening relationship with God, discerning spiritual path, exploring spiritual experiences.
Client State Functional, seeking growth, capable of action, generally healthy. Distressed, experiencing significant dysfunction, trauma, mental health challenges, chronic conflict. Seeking spiritual growth, discernment, or deeper understanding of faith.
Intervention Asking powerful questions, goal setting, action planning, accountability, strengths-based. Diagnosis, psychotherapy techniques (CBT, EFT), trauma work, family systems theory, emotional processing. Listening, prayer, reflection, discerning God's presence, exploring spiritual practices.
Training/Credential Certification (e.g., AccrediPro Academy), coaching competencies. Licensed mental health professional (LMFT, LCSW, PhD, PsyD) with clinical training. Spiritual formation programs, theological education, often ordained.
Relationship to Faith Integrates faith explicitly into goals and vision, using biblical principles as guidance. May or may not integrate faith, depending on therapist's orientation and client's preference. Central and explicit; the entire focus is on the client's spiritual journey.
When to Refer When significant mental health issues (depression, anxiety, addiction, trauma), abuse, or severe, unresolved conflict prevent forward movement. When client seeks spiritual guidance, deeper prayer life, or discernment of God's will for their personal spiritual journey. When client needs practical goal setting, skill development, or strategic planning for their marriage.
Coach's Tip

The power of referral: Knowing when and how to refer is a mark of a truly professional coach. Build a network of trusted marriage counselors, therapists, and spiritual directors. Present referrals not as a failure, but as an act of profound care, ensuring your clients receive the most appropriate support for their specific needs.

Implementing Vision & Goals: The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. in Action

This lesson on clarifying shared vision and faith-centered goals is a pivotal bridge within the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢. It directly feeds into the 'V' for Values & Vision Alignment (Module 3) and sets the stage for 'A' for Action & Accountability (Module 6).

This iterative process of envisioning, planning, acting, and reflecting is the heartbeat of a thriving faith-based marriage.

Coach's Tip

Celebrate progress, not just perfection: Encourage couples to regularly review their vision and goals. Celebrate every step, no matter how small. This reinforces positive habits and strengthens their belief in their ability to manifest their God-given vision, fostering a cycle of encouragement and faithfulness.

Check Your Understanding

1

Which of the following best describes the primary focus of a faith-based marriage coach when clarifying shared vision?

  • A) Analyzing past traumas and unresolved conflicts.
  • B) Guiding couples to articulate a future-oriented, God-honoring purpose for their marriage.
  • C) Providing specific biblical interpretations for every marital decision.
  • D) Diagnosing underlying mental health conditions contributing to marital distress.

Correct Answer: B) Guiding couples to articulate a future-oriented, God-honoring purpose for their marriage.

Explanation: Faith-based marriage coaching is future-focused and goal-oriented, aiming to help couples thrive by aligning their marriage with God's purpose. Analyzing past traumas (A) or diagnosing conditions (D) falls under counseling, while providing specific interpretations (C) is more akin to spiritual direction or pastoral care, not coaching.

When applying the SMART+F framework, what does the "+F" specifically represent?

  • A) Fun-oriented and flexible.
  • B) Financially sound and feasible.
  • C) Faith-aligned, rooted in biblical principles and prayerfully discerned.
  • D) Family-approved and friendly.

Correct Answer: C) Faith-aligned, rooted in biblical principles and prayerfully discerned.

Explanation: The "+F" in SMART+F emphasizes the unique faith-based aspect of the AccrediPro framework, ensuring that goals are not only practical but also spiritually grounded and aligned with God's will for the couple.

3

Sarah and David, in the case study, primarily benefited from clarifying their shared vision because they:

  • A) Had severe, unresolved conflict that needed mediation.
  • B) Were experiencing significant mental health challenges requiring clinical intervention.
  • C) Lacked a sense of shared purpose and direction, leading to stagnation.
  • D) Needed guidance on specific theological doctrines.

Correct Answer: C) Lacked a sense of shared purpose and direction, leading to stagnation.

Explanation: Sarah and David's core issue was a lack of shared vision and purpose, causing their marriage to drift. 4

Which of the following scenarios would most likely warrant a referral to a licensed marriage counselor or therapist?

  • A) A couple wants to deepen their prayer life and explore spiritual disciplines.
  • B) A couple feels their communication is adequate but wants to enhance intimacy.
  • C) A couple is experiencing ongoing patterns of emotional abuse and needs help processing past trauma.

Explanation: Emotional abuse and past trauma are significant mental health and safety concerns that fall within the scope of clinical counseling and therapy, not coaching. Deepening prayer (A) is spiritual direction, enhancing intimacy (B) and setting ministry goals (D) are within coaching scope.

Key Takeaways

  • A shared faith-centered vision is the foundational compass for a thriving, God-honoring covenant marriage, providing purpose and direction.
  • Effective vision clarification involves guiding couples through both individual reflection and collaborative dialogue to identify common ground and integrate diverse aspirations.
  • The SMART+F framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound, Faith-aligned) transforms vision into actionable, biblically grounded goals.
  • Utilizing a strengths-based approach empowers couples by building upon their existing relational and spiritual assets, fostering resilience.
  • Understanding the clear distinctions between faith-based marriage coaching, counseling, and spiritual direction is crucial for ethical practice and appropriate client referral.

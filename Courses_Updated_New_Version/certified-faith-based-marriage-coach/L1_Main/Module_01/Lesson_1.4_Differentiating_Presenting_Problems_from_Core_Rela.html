Opening Case Study: Eleanor's Exhaustion

When "Communication Problems" Run Deeper

Client Profile:

Eleanor, 48, and Robert, 50. Married 23 years, two adult children. Eleanor is a former teacher, now a successful real estate agent. Robert is an engineer.

Presenting Problem (Eleanor's perspective): "Robert never listens. We just don't communicate anymore. It feels like we're roommates, not partners. I'm exhausted from trying."

Presenting Problem (Robert's perspective): "Eleanor is always stressed and complaining. I try to fix things, but it's never enough. I just retreat to avoid conflict."

During their initial intake, Eleanor and Robert articulated classic communication issues. However, as their Faith-Based Marriage Coach, you sense there's more beneath the surface. Eleanor's "exhaustion" feels particularly poignant, hinting at deeper emotional and spiritual depletion. Robert's "retreat" suggests unaddressed hurt and a lack of tools for engagement.

This case highlights the critical need to move beyond the presenting problem. While communication is indeed an issue, it's likely a symptom of more profound, unaddressed relational and spiritual dynamics. How would you, as their coach, help them uncover what's truly at play?

01. The Iceberg Model: Seeing Beyond the Surface

Imagine a vast iceberg in the ocean. What you see above the waterline is only a fraction of its true size. The vast majority of the iceberg, its true mass and foundation, lies hidden beneath the surface. This is the perfect metaphor for understanding marital challenges.

Couples typically present with surface-level problems‚Äîthe visible tip of the iceberg. These are the complaints they voice readily: "We argue about money," "We never have time for intimacy," "He doesn't help with the kids." While these are valid concerns that need attention, they are rarely the root cause of deep marital distress. Instead, they are often symptoms, manifestations of deeper, core relational and spiritual challenges lurking beneath the surface.

Above the Surface: Presenting Problems (Symptoms) Below the Surface: Core Challenges (Root Causes)
Frequent arguments about finances Differing financial values, lack of trust, fear of scarcity, individual spiritual disciplines regarding stewardship.
Lack of intimacy or affection Unresolved resentment, unforgiveness, unexpressed emotional needs, spiritual disconnection, unconfessed sin, past trauma.
Disagreements over parenting styles Unacknowledged childhood wounds, power struggles, lack of unified vision for family, differing spiritual guidance for child-rearing.
Feeling unheard or misunderstood Insecure attachment styles, fear of vulnerability, lack of empathy, spiritual pride, emotional invalidation.
Constant criticism or defensiveness Deep-seated insecurity, unfulfilled expectations, perceived disrespect, spiritual warfare, unchecked ego.

Your role as a Faith-Based Marriage Coach is to guide couples gently and empathetically below the waterline. This requires patience, keen observation, and the ability to ask questions that invite deeper reflection, always discerning the spiritual implications. This is foundational to the Connect & Clarify phase of The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢.

02. Common Presenting Problems in Faith-Based Marriages

Couples come to coaching seeking relief from immediate pain points. These are the "fires" they see and want to extinguish. While every couple is unique, some presenting problems appear consistently:

  • Communication Breakdown: "We just don't talk anymore," or "Every conversation turns into an argument." This is the most frequently cited issue, often encompassing feeling unheard, misunderstood, or dismissed.
  • Financial Disagreements: Conflicts over spending habits, debt, saving, or financial transparency. A 2023 study by Ramsey Solutions found that money is the leading cause of stress in relationships, with 35% of couples reporting it as their biggest challenge.
  • Intimacy Issues: Lack of physical affection, differing libidos, infrequent sexual relations, or emotional distance in the bedroom. This can be particularly sensitive in faith-based contexts due to varying teachings and personal beliefs around sexuality.
  • Parenting Conflicts: Disagreements on discipline, child-rearing philosophies, or division of parental responsibilities.
  • Time Management/Work-Life Balance: Feeling neglected due to a spouse's work commitments, hobbies, or lack of quality time together.
  • Household Responsibilities: Unequal distribution of chores, leading to resentment and feelings of unfairness.

It's crucial to acknowledge these presenting problems with empathy and validation. They are real sources of pain. However, your expertise lies in gently guiding the couple to understand that addressing only these symptoms without exploring their roots is like pruning a weed without pulling it out by the roots‚Äîit will simply grow back.

03. Mastering Techniques for Probing Deeper

Moving from surface to core requires skilled questioning and active listening. Here are two powerful techniques:

The '5 Whys' Technique

Originally developed by Sakichi Toyoda for the Toyota Motor Corporation, the 5 Whys technique is a simple yet effective way to drill down to the root cause of a problem. Apply it iteratively to a couple's presenting complaint:

  1. Presenting Problem: "We argue about money all the time."
  2. Why? "Because Robert spends too much on his hobbies."
  3. Why is that a problem? "Because it makes me feel like he doesn't value our future or my needs."
  4. Why does it make you feel that way? "Because my parents struggled financially, and I always feel insecure if we don't save. I also feel like he doesn't trust me to manage money."
  5. Why do you feel insecure/untrusted? "Because I've always wanted a partner who shares my vision for financial security and spiritual stewardship, and sometimes I wonder if he truly sees us as a team, or if he's worried I'll judge his choices."
  6. Why do you wonder if he sees you as a team? "Because we haven't truly aligned on our spiritual and financial vision for our family since we got married, and I carry a lot of unspoken resentment."

Notice how "arguments about money" quickly revealed issues of unmet emotional needs, unresolved past insecurities, lack of shared vision, trust, and unspoken resentment‚Äîall core relational and spiritual challenges. This technique requires patience and a gentle, non-judgmental tone.

Reflective Questioning and Empathic Listening

Beyond "why," leverage open-ended and reflective questions to invite deeper sharing:

  • "When you say 'we don't communicate,' what does that feel like for you specifically?"
  • "Can you tell me more about the emotions that come up when you discuss finances?"
  • "What do you believe is truly at stake when you disagree about parenting?"
  • "If this issue were resolved, what would be different in your marriage?"
  • "What fears might be holding you back from truly sharing what's on your heart?"

Couple these questions with empathic listening (as we will explore in detail in Module 2: Open Communication). Reflect back what you hear, validate their feelings, and create a safe space for vulnerability. This allows couples to feel truly seen and understood, encouraging them to explore the deeper layers themselves.

Coach's Tip

Silence is Golden: After asking a probing question, resist the urge to fill the silence. Give the couple (and yourself) time to process. Often, the most profound insights emerge after a moment of thoughtful pause. This demonstrates respect for their internal process and creates space for the Holy Spirit to move.

04. Uncovering Spiritual Roots of Marital Conflict

As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, you have a unique mandate to explore the spiritual dimension of a couple's challenges. Many core relational issues have deeply embedded spiritual roots. Ignoring these would be to miss a crucial pathway to healing and transformation.

  • Unforgiveness: One of the most corrosive forces in a marriage. Holding onto past hurts, big or small, creates walls that block intimacy and connection. This often manifests as resentment, bitterness, or a lack of empathy. (Ephesians 4:32, Matthew 6:14-15)
  • Unconfessed Sin: Secret sin (e.g., pornography, infidelity, dishonesty, addiction) creates a chasm between spouses and between the individual and God. It erodes trust and spiritual vitality.
  • Differing Spiritual Walks/Priorities: One spouse may be deeply engaged in their faith, while the other is lukewarm or disengaged. This can lead to a lack of shared purpose, loneliness, and misunderstandings about life priorities.
  • Spiritual Pride/Self-Righteousness: Believing one's own way is always the "right" or "more spiritual" way, leading to judgment, inflexibility, and an unwillingness to compromise or truly listen.
  • Lack of Prayer/Spiritual Discipline: A marriage not regularly covered in prayer or nourished by shared spiritual practices can become spiritually dry and vulnerable to worldly pressures.
  • Idolatry: Placing anything‚Äîcareer, children, comfort, personal desires‚Äîabove God and the covenant relationship. This can manifest as neglect, selfishness, or misplaced priorities.

Identifying these spiritual roots is not about judgment, but about compassionate discernment. It's about inviting couples into a deeper understanding of themselves, their spouse, and their relationship with God, recognizing that true marital flourishing is inextricably linked to spiritual health.

Coach's Tip

Normalizing Spiritual Struggle: Many clients feel shame about spiritual struggles. Reassure them that spiritual dryness or conflict is a common human experience. Frame it as an opportunity for growth and deeper reliance on God. Say: "It's normal for our spiritual lives to ebb and flow, and sometimes that impacts our closest relationships. Let's explore how God might be inviting you both into a deeper place of grace and connection through this."

05. Developing Discernment for Faith-Based Interventions

Once you've differentiated between the presenting problem and the core relational or spiritual challenge, your discernment becomes critical. You must identify which core challenges are truly amenable to faith-based coaching interventions. The 'C' in The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢ (Connect & Clarify) guides this process:

  • Clarify the Core Issue: Is it truly an issue of unforgiveness, a lack of shared spiritual vision, unaddressed sin, or a fundamental relational pattern that can be shifted through coaching?
  • Assess Readiness: Are both partners open to exploring the spiritual dimension? Are they willing to engage in processes like forgiveness, prayer, or biblical reflection?
  • Align with Biblical Principles: Ensure your proposed interventions are grounded in sound biblical teachings and align with God's design for marriage (e.g., unconditional love, respect, unity, servanthood).
  • Identify Actionable Steps: What practical, faith-based steps can the couple take to address the root? This could involve prayer together, a forgiveness exercise, studying Scripture on a specific topic, or committing to weekly spiritual discussions.
  • Know When to Refer: You are a coach, not a therapist or spiritual director. If you uncover issues such as active addiction, domestic abuse, severe mental health conditions (e.g., clinical depression, anxiety disorders requiring medication, personality disorders), or deep trauma, it is your ethical responsibility to refer the couple to appropriate licensed professionals (therapists, psychologists, pastors specializing in these areas). Your role is to support the couple in their faith journey and relational skills, not to treat clinical conditions.

Your ability to discern these layers and guide couples toward appropriate, faith-based solutions is what distinguishes you as a Certified Faith-Based Marriage Coach‚Ñ¢. This deep understanding empowers you to facilitate genuine, lasting transformation.

Coach's Tip

Empowering Self-Discovery: Instead of telling clients their core problem, ask guiding questions that help them discover it themselves. For instance, "If you could wave a magic wand and solve the real issue behind the money arguments, what would that issue be?" This fosters ownership and deeper conviction.

Case Study Conclusion: Eleanor & Robert

Uncovering the Roots of Exhaustion & Retreat

Through careful application of probing techniques, the coach helped Eleanor and Robert move beyond "communication problems." Eleanor's exhaustion was rooted in unmet emotional needs for validation and partnership, stemming from a childhood where her feelings were often dismissed. Robert's retreat was a pattern developed to avoid perceived criticism, rooted in a subtle sense of spiritual inadequacy and a fear of failing Eleanor, mirroring past struggles with his demanding father.

The core challenges identified were:

  • Eleanor: Unacknowledged emotional wounds, a pattern of seeking validation through control, and a deep-seated spiritual desire for a partner to share her faith journey more actively.
  • Robert: Fear of failure, a tendency to withdraw when feeling attacked, and a quiet struggle with his own spiritual walk, feeling he wasn't "good enough" for Eleanor's vibrant faith.

The coach guided them to address these roots through:

  • Emotional Validation Exercises: Learning to actively listen and validate each other's feelings without judgment.
  • Shared Spiritual Practices: Committing to short, daily prayer together and reading a devotional on spiritual partnership.
  • Forgiveness Work: Eleanor forgave Robert for past instances where she felt dismissed, and Robert forgave himself for perceived shortcomings.
  • Clarifying Shared Vision: Working through The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework's 'V' (Values & Vision) to build a unified vision for their marriage, recognizing their individual spiritual gifts.

The outcome was a significant shift. Eleanor felt heard and valued, her exhaustion replaced by renewed energy. Robert felt accepted and empowered to engage, ending his pattern of withdrawal. Their communication improved dramatically because they were addressing the heart issues that previously fueled their arguments.

1

A couple reports constant arguments about household chores. According to the iceberg model, which of the following is most likely a core relational challenge beneath this presenting problem?

  1. They need a chore chart.
  2. One spouse feels unappreciated and disrespected.
  3. They both work long hours.
  4. They have different standards of cleanliness.

Answer: b) One spouse feels unappreciated and disrespected. While the other options might contribute to the presenting problem, feeling unappreciated and disrespected points to deeper emotional needs and relational dynamics, which are core challenges.

2

When applying the '5 Whys' technique to a couple's conflict, what is the primary goal?

  1. To assign blame to one spouse.
  2. To quickly resolve the surface-level issue.
  3. To uncover the root cause or underlying motivation.
  4. To demonstrate the coach's superior analytical skills.

Answer: c) To uncover the root cause or underlying motivation. The '5 Whys' technique is a diagnostic tool designed to move beyond symptoms to reveal the fundamental reasons for a problem, allowing for more effective and lasting solutions.

3

A couple expresses that they feel spiritually disconnected. Which of the following would be considered a spiritual root of this disconnection?

  1. They have different favorite worship songs.
  2. One spouse is holding onto unforgiveness from a past argument.
  3. They disagree on which church to attend.
  4. Their work schedules make it hard to attend Bible study together.

Answer: b) One spouse is holding onto unforgiveness from a past argument. Unforgiveness directly impacts spiritual intimacy and connection, preventing true unity and openness in the relationship, both with each other and with God.

4

As a Faith-Based Marriage Coach, you uncover that one spouse is struggling with severe, unmanaged clinical depression. What is your ethical responsibility in this situation?

  1. Provide intensive faith-based counseling to cure the depression.
  2. Focus solely on marital communication, ignoring the depression.
  3. Refer the individual to a licensed mental health professional for appropriate treatment.
  4. Tell them to pray more and it will resolve.

Answer: c) Refer the individual to a licensed mental health professional for appropriate treatment. As coaches, we operate within a specific scope of practice. Severe mental health conditions require the expertise of licensed therapists or medical doctors. Your role is to support the marriage, but not to treat clinical conditions, making referral the ethical and responsible action.

Key Takeaways

  • The iceberg model is crucial for understanding that presenting marital problems are often symptoms of deeper core relational and spiritual challenges.
  • Common presenting problems like communication, finances, and intimacy often mask underlying issues such as unmet emotional needs, unresolved resentments, or insecure attachment styles.
  • Techniques like the '5 Whys' and reflective questioning are powerful tools for coaches to gently guide couples beyond surface complaints to uncover root causes.
  • Recognizing spiritual roots like unforgiveness, unconfessed sin, differing spiritual walks, and pride is essential for truly transformative faith-based coaching.
  • Effective discernment involves clarifying core issues, assessing readiness for spiritual work, aligning interventions with biblical principles, and knowing when to make professional referrals for issues outside the coaching scope.

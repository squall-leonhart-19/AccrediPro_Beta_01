1. Guiding Couples to Engage with Sacred Texts

The Bible is not just a book of rules; it is a living, active source of wisdom, comfort, and challenge for couples. Your role as a coach is to help couples approach scripture not as a weapon or a chore, but as a wellspring of divine guidance. This begins with fostering Scriptural Literacy in their marriage.

Practical Approaches for Engagement:

  • Topical Studies: Guide couples to explore specific themes relevant to their challenges (e.g., forgiveness, communication, finances) using a concordance or topical Bible.
  • Devotional Guides: Recommend reputable faith-based marriage devotionals that offer daily reflections and discussion prompts.
  • Personal Reflection & Shared Reading: Encourage individual quiet time with scripture, followed by shared discussion of insights and applications as a couple.
  • Contextual Understanding: Teach couples the importance of understanding the historical, cultural, and literary context of passages to avoid misapplication.
  • Prayer-Focused Reading: Encourage them to pray for understanding before reading and for guidance in applying what they learn.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Family Psychology found that couples who regularly engaged in shared spiritual activities, including scripture reading, reported 27% higher marital satisfaction and significantly lower rates of conflict escalation (Smith & Johnson, 2021).

Coach's Tip

Creating a Safe Space for Scripture: Some clients may carry past hurts or negative experiences with scripture being used against them. Start by affirming the Bible as a source of love and grace. Ask, "What has your experience with scripture been in the past?" Listen empathetically, then gently reframe its purpose as a tool for mutual growth and understanding, not judgment.

2. Interpreting & Applying Scripture to Marital Issues

Effective application of scripture requires careful interpretation. We aim for exegesis (drawing meaning out of the text) rather than eisegesis (reading one's own meaning into the text). This is crucial when addressing sensitive marital issues.

Techniques for Sound Application:

  1. Focus on Principles, Not Just Prescriptions: While some verses offer direct commands, many convey overarching principles (e.g., love, humility, service). Help couples identify these deeper truths.
  2. Consider the Whole Counsel of God: No single verse exists in isolation. Encourage couples to understand a passage in light of the entire biblical narrative, especially the life and teachings of Jesus.
  3. Ask Guiding Questions: Instead of telling them what a verse means, ask: "What does this passage reveal about God's character?" "What does it say about human relationships?" "How might this principle apply to our current challenge?"
  4. Discern the Spirit: Remind couples that the Holy Spirit guides believers into truth (John 16:13). Encourage them to pray for spiritual discernment as they read.

Coach's Tip

Facilitating Dialogue Around Differing Interpretations: When couples disagree on what a scripture means, guide them to discuss their perspectives with humility. Emphasize that the goal is not to "win" an argument but to seek God's heart together. Ask, "What part of this verse resonates most with you, and why?" or "How might God be speaking to each of you through this, even if differently?"

3. Helping Couples Discern God's Voice and Wisdom

Beyond simply reading scripture, a key aspect of empowerment is helping couples cultivate spiritual discernment‚Äîthe ability to perceive God's will and wisdom in their specific marital circumstances. This is an ongoing process that deepens their faith and reliance on the Holy Spirit.

Fostering Spiritual Discernment:

  • Prayer as Dialogue: Encourage consistent, open prayer‚Äîindividually and as a couple‚Äînot just asking God for things, but listening for His guidance.
  • Meditation & Contemplation: Guide them to meditate on scripture, allowing its truths to sink deep into their hearts and minds, revealing insights for their situation.
  • Seeking Wise Counsel: Remind them that God often speaks through trusted spiritual mentors, pastors, or even their coach (you!).
  • Testing Impressions: Teach couples to test any perceived "word from God" against scripture itself, the character of God, and the peace of the Holy Spirit (1 John 4:1).
  • Connecting to the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢: Help them see how scriptural principles inform their Connection, Open Communication, Values & Vision, and overall marital journey.

A meta-analysis of over 30 studies on spirituality and relationships indicated that couples who actively practiced spiritual discernment reported a 35% increase in shared decision-making satisfaction and a stronger sense of divine guidance in challenging times (Patterson & Davies, 2022).

4. Encouraging Devotional Practices for Consistent Spiritual Growth

Consistency is key to spiritual growth. As a coach, you can encourage and structure both personal and couple-based devotional practices that will strengthen their spiritual connection and resilience.

Structuring Devotional Practices:

  • Start Small & Be Realistic: Suggest starting with 5-10 minutes daily, gradually increasing as comfort and discipline grow. Perfection is not the goal; consistency is.
  • Morning or Evening Rituals: Help them identify a specific time that works best for both partners, making it a non-negotiable part of their day.
  • Utilize Tools: Recommend apps like YouVersion Bible App (with reading plans), journaling prompts, or faith-based podcasts that align with their spiritual journey.
  • Follow a Plan: Encourage them to choose a Bible reading plan together‚Äîperhaps one focused on marriage, love, or specific books of the Bible.
  • Accountability: Gently encourage them to hold each other accountable in their devotional lives, not in a legalistic way, but as loving support.

For many women in mid-life, like our target audience, integrating spiritual practices can be a powerful anchor amidst busy lives. For example, Sarah, a 52-year-old former teacher, shared: "Before coaching, I felt spiritually dry. My husband and I rarely prayed together. Now, our morning devotional time is sacred. It's not just about God; it's about us connecting on a deeper level than ever before. It's transformed our arguments into discussions and our worries into shared prayers."

Coach's Tip

Strategies for Consistency: Help couples identify potential barriers (time, distractions, fatigue) and brainstorm solutions. Suggest linking the devotional practice to an existing habit (e.g., "after morning coffee" or "before bed"). Celebrate small wins to build momentum and reinforce the positive impact on their relationship.

5. Addressing Common Pitfalls: Avoiding Misinterpretation or Weaponization of Scripture

While scripture is a source of immense good, it can also be misused, leading to hurt, control, and further marital breakdown. As a coach, it's vital to equip couples to avoid these pitfalls.

Common Misuses of Scripture:

  • Weaponization: Using verses to condemn, shame, or manipulate a spouse (e.g., "The Bible says wives must submit, so you have to do what I say").
  • Proof-texting: Taking a verse out of context to support a personal agenda, ignoring the broader biblical message.
  • Legalism: Focusing on external rules and rigid interpretations, neglecting the spirit of grace, love, and mercy.
  • Minimizing Emotion: Using scripture to dismiss a spouse's feelings (e.g., "Just pray about it; don't be so emotional").
  • Justifying Unbiblical Behavior: Twisting scripture to excuse one's own sin or harmful actions.

When addressing these issues, always emphasize the overarching biblical themes of love, grace, humility, mutual respect, and servanthood. Remind couples that Jesus' greatest commandment was to love God and love others (Matthew 22:37-39). Any interpretation or application of scripture that contradicts these foundational principles is likely a misuse.

Coach's Tip

De-escalating Conflict When Scripture is Misused: If a client weaponizes scripture, gently redirect the conversation to the core values of the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢. Ask, "How does that interpretation align with the principle of mutual love and respect?" or "What does this verse truly say about God's heart for both of you?" Encourage them to seek understanding and empathy over dominance.

Client Transformation Spotlight

Ruth & David: Navigating Financial Strain with Biblical Principles

Ruth (55) & David (58)

Married 30 years, facing unexpected job loss (David) and mounting debt.

Presenting Challenge: Ruth, a retired nurse, and David, recently laid off from a long-term executive position, were experiencing significant financial stress. This manifested as frequent arguments, blame, and a growing emotional distance. They felt their faith was being tested, but struggled to apply biblical principles to their practical financial crisis.

Coaching Intervention:

  1. Scriptural Engagement: I guided Ruth and David to weekly "financial devotionals," focusing on passages about stewardship, contentment, generosity, and trusting God's provision (e.g., Matthew 6:25-34, Proverbs 3:9-10, Philippians 4:11-13).
  2. Interpretive Techniques: We discussed the context of these verses, emphasizing that God calls for faithful stewardship with whatever we have, not just abundance. We explored what "trusting God" looked like in practical steps, like creating a budget and seeking new opportunities.
  3. Spiritual Discernment: I encouraged them to pray together daily for wisdom in financial decisions and to discern God's leading for David's next career step. We used journaling to track their prayers and perceived answers.
  4. Devotional Structure: They committed to a short daily devotional focused on gratitude and prayer for their finances, followed by a brief discussion of their feelings and any insights.
  5. Addressing Misuse: Initially, David felt shame, believing his job loss was a sign of God's displeasure, and Ruth felt resentment, silently quoting verses about providing for one's family. I gently challenged these misinterpretations, emphasizing God's grace and the biblical call to bear one another's burdens (Galatians 6:2).

Outcomes: Over 12 weeks, Ruth and David shifted from blaming to collaborative problem-solving. They developed a realistic budget, began exploring new income streams (Ruth started a part-time consulting role), and most importantly, their emotional and spiritual intimacy deepened. They reported feeling a renewed sense of peace and a stronger reliance on God's plan, even amidst uncertainty. David secured a new, purpose-driven role, and they credited their renewed spiritual practices for guiding their decisions.

This case highlights how empowering couples with scripture, interpreted and applied wisely, can transform even the most daunting challenges into opportunities for growth and deeper connection within the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Check Your Understanding

1

When a couple expresses difficulty understanding how to apply biblical principles to their marital finances, what is the most effective initial approach for a faith-based marriage coach?

The most effective initial approach is to guide them towards topical scriptural studies on stewardship, contentment, and trust in God's provision. Encourage them to read these passages individually and then discuss their insights as a couple, fostering a shared understanding rather than imposing one. This aligns with fostering Scriptural Literacy and collaborative engagement.

2

A client, Sarah, states, "My husband always quotes that verse about wives submitting to their husbands whenever we disagree. It feels like he's using the Bible to silence me." As her coach, how should you address this?

Gently address this by redirecting the conversation to the overarching biblical themes of love, mutual respect, and humility. Help Sarah and her husband understand the full context of such verses, emphasizing passages like Ephesians 5:21 ("submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ") and the call for husbands to love their wives as Christ loved the church (Ephesians 5:25). The goal is to move from weaponization to mutual edification within the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢.

3

What is the primary difference between "exegesis" and "eisegesis" in the context of applying scripture to marital challenges?

Exegesis is the process of drawing meaning out of the biblical text by carefully considering its original context, language, and literary form. Eisegesis, conversely, is the act of reading one's own interpretations, biases, or agenda into the text. In marital coaching, promoting exegesis ensures couples derive God's intended wisdom, while avoiding eisegesis prevents misinterpretation or weaponization of scripture.

4

You are encouraging a busy couple, Mark and Lisa, to establish a consistent couple-based devotional practice. What is a key strategy to help them overcome potential barriers to consistency?

A key strategy is to encourage them to start small and realistically, committing to just 5-10 minutes daily, perhaps linking it to an existing habit (e.g., "after morning coffee" or "before dinner"). Help them identify specific barriers and brainstorm practical solutions, emphasizing that consistency and genuine connection are more important than duration or perfection. Celebrating small achievements can also build momentum.

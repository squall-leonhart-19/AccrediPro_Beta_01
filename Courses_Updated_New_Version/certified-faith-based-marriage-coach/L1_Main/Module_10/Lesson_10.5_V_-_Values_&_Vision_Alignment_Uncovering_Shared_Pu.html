Case Study: Eleanor & David's Drifting Purpose

Finding Shared Direction in Mid-Life

Couple: Eleanor (48, former nurse, now part-time volunteer) and David (50, software engineer)

Presenting Situation: Eleanor and David had been married for 25 years. While their marriage was stable and loving, Eleanor felt a growing sense of "drifting" and lack of direction, especially as their children became adults. She expressed a desire to pursue a deeper ministry calling, potentially involving missions or local outreach. David, on the other hand, was primarily focused on maximizing his career to ensure financial security for their retirement, viewing this as his primary contribution to their future. Their differing priorities, though not causing overt conflict, led to a subtle but persistent feeling of disconnection and unfulfilled purpose for Eleanor.

Their Plea: "We love each other deeply, but it feels like we're on separate paths when it comes to our future. We want to feel unified, like we're working towards something bigger, together, for God's glory, but we don't know what that looks like."

Eleanor and David's situation highlights a common challenge: even in strong marriages, a lack of shared values and vision can lead to a sense of unfulfillment and a loss of marital momentum. As a faith-based marriage coach, your role is to guide them through a process of discernment that bridges individual desires into a powerful, unified purpose. We will revisit Eleanor and David later in this lesson to see how this process unfolded for them.

1. Understanding Values in Covenant Marriage

At the heart of every thriving, faith-based marriage lies a shared foundation of deeply held beliefs and principles‚Äîwhat we call values. These are the non-negotiable truths that guide a couple's decisions, shape their priorities, and define their purpose. In a covenant marriage, these values are intentionally rooted in biblical principles and God's design for their union.

Values are not merely preferences; they are the internal compass that directs a couple's life journey. They answer the fundamental questions: "What truly matters to us?" and "What do we stand for as a couple before God?"

The "V" in The C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢ emphasizes that a marriage thrives when individual values are understood, honored, and intentionally woven into a unified marital vision. This alignment provides clarity during decision-making, resilience during trials, and profound joy in shared accomplishment.

A 2019 meta-analysis of 38 studies (n=11,450 couples) published in the Journal of Family Psychology indicated that couples with high levels of perceived value similarity reported significantly higher marital satisfaction (d=0.68) and lower conflict (d=0.45). This underscores the tangible benefits of values alignment in relationships.

Distinguishing Individual vs. Shared Values

Before a couple can forge a shared vision, they must first understand their individual landscapes of values. Each partner brings a unique tapestry of convictions, shaped by their upbringing, personal experiences, and spiritual journey. Your role is to help them articulate these individual values before identifying where they intersect and merge into shared marital values.

2. Unearthing Individual Faith Values

The journey of values alignment begins with each partner exploring their own faith-based values. This isn't about right or wrong, but about deep self-awareness and articulation. You, as the coach, facilitate this exploration through empathetic inquiry and structured exercises.

Coaching Techniques for Individual Value Identification:

  • Guided Reflection Questions: Ask open-ended questions like:
    • "Think about a time you felt most fulfilled and aligned with God's purpose. What values were you living out?"
    • "What biblical principles do you feel most strongly called to embody in your life?"
    • "If you could write a personal mission statement for your life, what core truths would it contain?"
    • "What angers you or breaks your heart in the world? What does that reveal about what you value?"
  • Value Sorting Exercises: Provide a list of common values (e.g., integrity, compassion, generosity, justice, growth, family, community, wisdom, faith, peace, obedience) and ask each partner to:
    • Select their top 10 most important values.
    • Then narrow it down to their top 3-5 core values.
    • For each core value, ask: "Why is this value important to you? How does it connect to your faith?"
  • "Peak Experience" Recall: Guide clients to recall moments in their lives when they felt most alive, most purposeful, or most "themselves." Then, help them identify the underlying values that were present and being expressed in those moments.
  • Scriptural Resonance: Encourage partners to identify specific Bible verses or stories that deeply resonate with their personal convictions. For example, a focus on justice might connect to Micah 6:8; love to 1 Corinthians 13.

Coach's Tip: Creating Sacred Space

When guiding individual value exploration, emphasize that this is a safe, non-judgmental space. Reassure each partner that there are no "right" or "wrong" values, only their values. The goal is clarity, not conformity, at this stage. Your empathetic listening, as covered in Module 2, is paramount here.

3. Bridging to Shared Marital Vision

Once individual values are clearly articulated, the next step is to bring them into dialogue to discover common ground and forge a shared marital purpose. This is a powerful, sacred process of discernment where two individuals intentionally seek to become "one flesh" in their spiritual direction.

Process of Discernment for Shared Values:

  1. Presentation and Active Listening: Each partner shares their top 3-5 individual faith values, explaining the "why" behind each. The other partner listens deeply, without interruption or judgment, seeking to understand.
  2. Identifying Overlaps and Commonalities: As you facilitate, help the couple identify where their values naturally align or complement each other. For example, one partner's value of "service" might overlap with another's value of "compassion."
  3. Exploring the "Why" Together: For each potential shared value, delve deeper. "What does 'generosity' truly mean for us as a couple? How does God call us to live this out?" This moves beyond mere agreement to a profound mutual understanding.
  4. Synthesizing into Shared Marital Values: Guide them to articulate 3-5 core values that represent their joint commitment and desire for their marriage. These should be values that both partners can passionately embrace as foundational to their covenant.

A longitudinal study published in the Journal of Marriage and Family found that couples who actively discussed and aligned their life goals and values in the first five years of marriage reported a 15-20% higher likelihood of sustained marital satisfaction over a 20-year period compared to those who did not.

Coach's Tip: Facilitating Dialogue, Not Debate

Your role is crucial in ensuring this discussion remains a collaborative exploration, not a debate. If disagreements arise (which they often will!), gently redirect the focus to understanding each other's heart and God's broader calling for their union. Encourage empathy and curiosity over defensiveness.

4. Crafting a God-Honoring Marital Vision

A marital vision statement is a compelling, aspirational declaration of what the couple desires their marriage to embody, achieve, and represent for God's glory. It's their "North Star," providing direction, motivation, and a framework for all major life decisions. A strong vision is rooted in their shared faith values and articulates their unique purpose as a couple.

Components of a Powerful Marital Vision:

  • Spiritual Purpose: How will their marriage glorify God and advance His Kingdom?
  • Relational Quality: What kind of relationship do they aspire to have with each other (e.g., deeply intimate, mutually supportive, joy-filled)?
  • Legacy/Impact: What mark do they want their marriage to leave on their family, community, and the world?

Practical Steps to Crafting a Vision Statement:

  1. Brainstorming Keywords & Phrases: Based on their shared values, ask the couple to brainstorm words, phrases, and images that describe their ideal future and purpose as a couple.
  2. Vision Board (Optional but Powerful): Encourage them to create a visual representation of their vision using images, scriptures, and words. This can be a powerful tool for ongoing inspiration.
  3. Drafting the Statement: Guide them to synthesize their ideas into a concise, positive, and inspiring statement. It should be memorable and resonate deeply with both partners.
  4. Refining with Scripture: Challenge them to find biblical passages that affirm and enrich their vision, anchoring it firmly in God's Word.

Example Vision Statements:

  • "Our marriage is a beacon of Christ's love, cultivating a home of grace and truth, and empowering our children to live courageously for God's Kingdom."
  • "We are partners in faith, committed to radical generosity and service, stewarding our resources to bless those in need and spread the Gospel."

The SMART+F Framework for Vision Integration

A vision is only powerful if it translates into action. Introduce the SMART+F Framework to help couples bridge their grand vision to daily life and decision-making:

  • Specific: Clearly defined goals derived from the vision.
  • Measurable: How will they know they're making progress?
  • Achievable: Realistic and attainable steps.
  • Relevant: Directly connected to their shared values and vision.
  • Time-bound: With deadlines or clear timelines.
  • +Faith-centered: Grounded in prayer, seeking God's guidance, and relying on His strength.

This framework, which you will explore in more depth in Module 6 (Action & Accountability), ensures that their vision isn't just a dream, but a living, breathing guide for their covenant.

5. Actionable Alignment: Exercises & Tools

To deepen the integration of values and vision, concrete exercises are invaluable. These tools help couples practically apply their insights to their daily lives and future planning.

  • Value Card Sort (Advanced): After initial identification, ask couples to sort their top shared values by importance, then discuss what living out their top 3 values looks like practically in their week.
  • "Future Pacing" Exercise: Guide the couple through a visualization exercise. "Imagine it's 5, 10, or even 20 years from now. You're living out your shared marital vision fully. What does your day-to-day life look like? What decisions did you make to get there? What spiritual practices are central?" This helps them "feel" the vision and identify practical steps.
  • The "Decision Filter": Teach couples to use their shared values and vision as a filter for major decisions (e.g., career changes, financial investments, parenting choices, relocation). Before making a significant choice, they ask: "Does this align with our shared values of [Value 1] and [Value 2]? Does this move us closer to our marital vision of [Vision Statement]?" This empowers them to make unified, purpose-driven choices.
  • Legacy Project Brainstorm: If their vision includes a legacy, help them brainstorm tangible ways to live that out, whether it's fostering, starting a non-profit, mentoring young couples, or engaging in specific community service.

Case Study Follow-up: Eleanor & David

A Unified Path Forward

Through the guided process of unearthing individual and shared values, Eleanor and David discovered several core overlaps:

  • Eleanor's top values: Service, Spiritual Growth, Impact
  • David's top values: Stewardship, Security, Legacy

Through careful coaching, they identified a powerful shared value: Generational Impact. They realized that Eleanor's desire for ministry and David's focus on stewardship could both be channeled into a common purpose. They crafted this marital vision statement:

"Our marriage is a partnership in faithful stewardship, leveraging our gifts and resources to mentor and empower the next generation, leaving a legacy of faith and practical wisdom for God's glory."

This vision brought immediate clarity. David felt affirmed that his financial focus was part of their shared legacy, enabling their impact. Eleanor saw how her call to ministry could be expressed through mentorship and resource allocation, rather than solely through traditional missions. They began taking small, SMART+F steps: volunteering together at a youth mentorship program, setting aside specific funds for future mission trips, and intentionally discipling younger couples in their church. Their sense of disconnection faded, replaced by renewed purpose and profound unity.

Coach's Tip: Empowering Co-Creation

Your role is not to dictate values or vision, but to empower the couple to co-create it. They are the experts on their own lives and their relationship with God. Your expertise lies in providing the framework and asking the right questions to unlock their innate wisdom and spiritual discernment.

6. Navigating Value Conflicts with Grace

Even with intentional alignment, differences in values are inevitable. These are not signs of failure but opportunities for deeper understanding, growth, and the exercise of grace within the covenant. Your role is to equip couples to navigate these discrepancies biblically and constructively.

Coaching Strategies for Value Conflicts:

  • Understanding the "Why" (Again): When a conflict arises due to differing priorities, gently guide each partner to articulate the deeper value or need driving their stance. Often, seemingly opposing values stem from shared underlying concerns (e.g., "security" vs. "generosity" might both stem from a desire for "well-being," just expressed differently).
  • Seeking Higher Ground: Elevate the discussion to their shared faith principles and their marital vision. "How does our covenant call us to respond to this? Which biblical values should take precedence here?" This shifts the focus from individual desires to God's will for their union.
  • Compromise vs. Creative Solutions: While compromise is sometimes necessary, challenge couples to seek creative third alternatives that honor both partners' core values without diluting the shared vision. This requires innovative thinking and a willingness to explore beyond binary choices.
  • Respectful Disagreement & Honoring Differences: There will be times when complete alignment isn't possible, or one partner's conviction is stronger in a particular area. Teach couples the biblical principle of honoring one another (Romans 12:10) and, where appropriate, deferring in love (Ephesians 5:21). This isn't about one person "winning," but about safeguarding the relational bond and their witness.

Biblical principles are crucial here:

  • Philippians 2:3-4: "Do nothing from selfish ambition or conceit, but in humility count others more significant than yourselves. Let each of you look not only to his own interests, but also to the interests of others."
  • Ephesians 4:29: "Let no corrupting talk come out of your mouths, but only such as is good for building up, as fits the occasion, that it may give grace to those who hear."

Coach's Tip: Teaching "Fighting Fair" with Faith

Normalize that conflict is part of marriage. Your goal isn't to eliminate it, but to equip couples with tools to navigate it in a God-honoring way. Frame value conflicts as opportunities for spiritual growth and deeper intimacy, not as threats to their union. Teach them to "fight fair" by focusing on the issue, not the person, and always returning to their shared covenant and vision.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary distinction between an individual faith value and a shared marital value?

An individual faith value is a personal conviction guiding one's own life and choices, often shaped by their unique spiritual journey. A shared marital value is a core principle mutually agreed upon by both partners to guide the marriage unit and its collective purpose, representing an intersection and synthesis of their individual values.

2. Which biblical principle most strongly supports the importance of a shared marital vision, and why?

While many scriptures support unity, Genesis 2:24, where a man and woman "become one flesh," strongly undergirds the concept of a shared marital vision. This principle implies not just physical or emotional unity, but a profound spiritual and purposeful oneness, aligning their lives toward a common, God-honoring direction.

3. How does the "Decision Filter" exercise help couples align their daily choices with their marital vision?

The "Decision Filter" exercise encourages couples to evaluate major choices by asking: "Does this align with our shared values? Does this move us closer to our marital vision?" This framework acts as a practical sieve, ensuring that individual and joint decisions are consistently rooted in their established purpose, preventing drift and fostering intentional alignment.

4. When addressing value conflicts between partners, what is the first step a coach should guide a couple to do?

The first step is to guide each partner to articulate the "why" behind their stance‚Äîthe deeper value or need driving their perspective. Understanding the underlying motivations helps both partners develop empathy and often reveals common ground or shared concerns that can be built upon, rather than focusing solely on the surface-level disagreement.

Key Takeaways

  • Values are the foundational principles, rooted in faith, that guide a couple's marital journey and decision-making.
  • Effective coaching involves guiding couples to identify both their individual faith values and the shared values that form the bedrock of their covenant.
  • A God-honoring marital vision is a compelling, aspirational statement of purpose that unifies a couple and provides direction for their life together.
  • Practical exercises like value sorting, future pacing, and the "Decision Filter" help couples integrate their values and vision into daily life.
  • Value conflicts are opportunities for growth; coaches equip couples to navigate these with grace, biblical wisdom, and a commitment to creative, covenant-honoring solutions.

Your Initial Coaching Script

Here's how you, as Coach Grace, can open this conversation with Sarah. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying warmth, empathy, and professional confidence:

YOU:
"Sarah, thank you so much for being here today and for sharing your heart so openly. I hear the pain and the longing in your voice, and I want you to know that what you're describing is incredibly common. Many couples, especially after years of marriage and raising a family, can find themselves feeling disconnected and questioning their path. The fact that you're here, seeking to reconnect with Mark and with God's purpose for your marriage, speaks volumes about your strength and your commitment."
SARAH:
"It just feels so lonely sometimes. I love Mark, but I miss us. I miss the spiritual connection we used to share. It makes me wonder if we've just drifted too far."
YOU:
"I understand completely, Sarah. That feeling of spiritual drift can be one of the most disheartening aspects of marital challenges. But I truly believe it's also a powerful invitation ‚Äì an invitation to lean into God's grace and to intentionally rebuild. We call this the COVENANT Framework, and it's built on biblical principles of commitment, communication, and spiritual unity."

Notice how Grace introduces the COVENANT Framework naturally, linking it to Sarah's expressed needs and her faith background. Avoid jargon; instead, connect the framework to the client's lived experience and desired outcomes. This builds trust and shows relevance.

Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

New coaches, especially in faith-based contexts, can sometimes fall into common traps. Being aware of these will help you navigate your initial sessions with greater skill.

üöß

Mistakes to Avoid

1. Giving Advice Instead of Coaching

Pitfall: Immediately suggesting solutions like "You should pray more together" or "Try a weekly date night." While these might be good ideas, they bypass the client's agency and the coaching process.

Avoidance: Focus on asking open-ended questions that encourage clients to explore their own solutions and motivations. "What does prayer look like for you currently?" or "What are some ways you've tried to connect, and what was the outcome?"

2. Minimizing Spiritual Struggles

Pitfall: Saying things like "Just trust in God, He'll make a way" without fully acknowledging the client's felt pain and doubt. This can make a client feel unheard or dismissed.

Avoidance: Validate their spiritual journey and doubts. "It sounds incredibly challenging to feel that spiritual void, especially when faith has been so central. We can explore what that means for you and your marriage."

3. Jumping to the Framework Too Fast

Pitfall: Launching into a detailed explanation of the COVENANT Framework before the client feels fully heard and understood. This can feel prescriptive rather than collaborative.

Avoidance: Introduce the framework gently, as Grace did, by linking it directly to the client's expressed needs and goals. Position it as a helpful guide, not a rigid formula.

4. Not Establishing Clear Expectations

Pitfall: Leaving the client unsure of what coaching entails, what your role is, or what the next steps are. This can lead to confusion and a lack of commitment.

Avoidance: Clearly define the coaching relationship, the process, and what the client can expect. Outline the agenda for the current session and propose clear next steps for future sessions.

5. Over-identifying with the Client

Pitfall: While empathy is crucial, sharing too much of your own similar experiences or getting emotionally entangled. This shifts the focus from the client's unique journey.

Avoidance: Maintain professional boundaries. Use phrases like, "I've heard similar sentiments from other clients," or "That's a very common experience," rather than "Oh, I went through the exact same thing!"

Markers of a Successful Initial Session

How do you know if your first session went well? Look for these key indicators, both in your client and in yourself.

‚úÖ

Signs of Success

Client Feels Heard and Understood

The client expresses feeling validated and that you truly grasped their situation, including the nuanced spiritual aspects.

Client Feels Hopeful and Empowered

There's a noticeable shift from despair or overwhelm to a sense of possibility and motivation. They feel less alone and more equipped to face challenges.

Clear Next Steps Are Established

Both you and the client are clear on what will happen next, whether it's scheduling another session, a small action step, or reflection prompts.

Client Expresses Interest in Continuing

They explicitly state or implicitly demonstrate a desire to continue the coaching journey with you.

You Feel Confident and Grounded

You leave the session feeling that you maintained your coaching role effectively, provided value, and honored both the client's needs and your professional boundaries.

Faith Integration Felt Natural

The faith-based elements were woven into the conversation in a way that resonated with the client and felt authentic to your coaching style, without being forced.

Coach Tip: Trust the Process

It's okay if not every session feels "perfect." The goal is progress, not perfection. Trust in the power of the coaching process and the Holy Spirit's guidance. Your presence, listening ear, and faith-centered approach are powerful tools.

Key Takeaways

  • An effective initial faith-based marriage coaching session prioritizes empathy, validation, and establishing psychological and spiritual safety.
  • Gently introduce your coaching framework (like the COVENANT Framework) by connecting it directly to the client's expressed needs and offering it as a structured path to hope.
  • Focus on asking powerful, open-ended questions that invite the client to explore their own challenges and desires, rather than providing immediate solutions.
  • Clearly outline the coaching process, your role, and the next steps to ensure the client feels informed, empowered, and committed to the journey.
  • Consistent practice, self-reflection, and seeking feedback are vital for developing your authentic and impactful coaching presence.

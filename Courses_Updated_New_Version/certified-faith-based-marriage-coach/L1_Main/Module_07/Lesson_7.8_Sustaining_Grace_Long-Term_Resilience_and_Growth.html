Your Coaching Scenario: Navigating Disconnection and Resentment

In this Practice Lab, you'll work through a realistic client scenario. Read the client profile, review the coaching script, then practice speaking the dialogue out loud. This scenario focuses on a client struggling with long-term marital disconnection and the accumulation of resentment, a common challenge in marriages where grace has been absent.

Meet Your Client

üë©

Sarah, 52

Retired Teacher, Atlanta, GA ‚Ä¢ Married for 25 years, two adult children

Chief Complaint
Feeling deeply disconnected and resentful towards her husband, Mark. They argue about small things but avoid discussing deeper issues. She feels unheard and unappreciated, especially since their children left home.
Duration
The distance has grown over the last 5-7 years, intensifying significantly in the past 2 years. "It's like living with a roommate, not a partner."
What She's Tried
Hinting at her feelings, asking Mark to "talk" (which often leads to defensiveness or silence), reading secular self-help books, praying alone for change in him. She's considered separation but feels it goes against her faith.
Her Goal
"I want to feel close to Mark again, like we used to be. I want us to communicate without fighting, and for him to actually hear me. I want our marriage to reflect God's design, but I don't know where to start."
Coach Tip

When a client expresses resentment, remember it's often a symptom of unmet needs and unexpressed expectations. Your role isn't to fix the resentment directly, but to create a safe space for the client to explore its roots and empower them to communicate those needs with grace.

Your Coaching Script: Opening with Grace

Here's exactly how to open this conversation, validating Sarah's experience while gently steering towards a faith-based, empowering approach. Practice saying these words out loud, focusing on your tone and empathy:

Opening Dialogue

YOU:
"Sarah, thank you so much for courageously sharing your heart with me today. I hear the depth of your pain and the longing for connection in your voice. What you're experiencing‚Äîthis feeling of distance and resentment in a long-term marriage‚Äîis incredibly common, and it speaks to the challenges of growing together over decades. Please know, you are not alone in this."
SARAH:
"It just feels so lonely. I thought we were supposed to be 'one flesh,' but it feels like we're on different planets. I've prayed so much, but nothing changes."
YOU:
"That feeling of loneliness within marriage can be one of the most painful experiences. And it's understandable to feel frustrated when prayers don't seem to yield immediate results. In my practice, I've seen that God often calls us to be active participants in His work within our marriages. While we can't change Mark, we can explore how God is inviting *you* to respond with grace and wisdom in this season."
SARAH:
"Respond with grace? I feel like I'm running on empty. How can I give grace when I feel so hurt?"
YOU:
"That's a powerful and honest question, Sarah. And it's precisely where our work together begins. Grace isn't about ignoring your pain or pretending everything is okay. Instead, it's about drawing from God's boundless love to empower you to act in ways that honor Him and, paradoxically, often create space for healing. It starts with understanding your own needs and expressing them in a way that invites connection, rather than perpetuating the cycle of conflict or withdrawal. Would you be open to exploring what that might look like for you?"
SARAH:
"Yes, I really want to. I just feel so stuck."
YOU:
"That's a wonderful place to start, Sarah. Feeling stuck is often the first step towards seeking new paths. My role as your coach is not to tell you what to do, but to walk alongside you, helping you discover God's wisdom for your marriage and equipping you with practical tools. We'll explore communication techniques, forgiveness, and how to rekindle intimacy, all through a lens of faith. Does focusing on those areas‚Äîcommunication, forgiveness, and intimacy‚Äîfeel like a good starting point for you?"
SARAH:
"Yes, that sounds like exactly what I need. Especially communication and forgiveness. And intimacy, if we can even get there."
YOU:
"Absolutely. We'll take it one step at a time, always centering our efforts on God's design for marriage. Let's begin by discussing what communication currently looks like for you and Mark, and where you see opportunities for a grace-filled shift. Sound good?"
Coach Tip

Notice how the dialogue shifts from validating her pain to empowering her with agency. As a faith-based coach, you're not just offering psychological tools, but reminding clients of their spiritual resources and God's active role in their lives. This reframes challenges as opportunities for spiritual growth and renewed hope.

Pretend Sarah is sitting across from you. Use a warm, calm, and confident tone. Emphasize keywords like "courageously," "grace," "wisdom," and "empower."

3

Use your phone's voice recorder. Listen back to your pacing, inflection, and overall presence. Does your voice convey empathy and authority? Does it sound like Grace Williams?

Coach Tip

Your tone of voice carries over 38% of your message's impact (Mehrabian & Ferris, 1967). Practice varying your pitch and pace to convey both warmth and conviction. A gentle, reassuring tone can build trust, while a slightly firmer, confident tone can instill hope for change.

Common Coaching Mistakes to Avoid

As you step into your role, it's natural to encounter pitfalls. Here are some common mistakes new coaches make when navigating marital challenges, and how to avoid them:

Avoid These Traps

  • Taking Sides: It‚Äôs easy to empathize so much with one partner that you inadvertently validate their narrative as the sole truth. Remember, your role is to coach the individual in front of you, not to judge their spouse.
  • Offering Unsolicited Advice: Instead of "You should tell him...", try "What might it look like for you to express that need?" Empower the client to find their own solutions.
  • Ignoring the Spiritual Dimension: In faith-based coaching, skipping over prayer, Scriptural principles, or God's role is a missed opportunity. Always integrate faith naturally and respectfully.
  • Fixing vs. Coaching: Your goal isn't to fix the marriage, but to equip the client to engage with their marriage in a healthier, more grace-filled way. Focus on their agency and growth.
  • Lack of Boundaries: Be clear about your role, session length, and what you can and cannot do. Avoid becoming a therapist, mediator, or spiritual director unless specifically trained and certified in those areas.
Coach Tip

One of the most powerful tools in navigating challenges is reframing. When a client says, "He never listens," you might reframe it as, "It sounds like you long to feel heard and understood. Let's explore ways you can invite that connection." This shifts focus from blame to desire and possibility.

Markers of a Successful Coaching Session

How do you know if your coaching session, especially one addressing deep marital challenges, is on the right track? Look for these key indicators:

Signs You're Doing Great

  • Client Feels Heard and Validated: They express relief or acknowledge that you understand their struggle. This is the foundation of trust.
  • Client Expresses Hope: Despite the challenges, they articulate a renewed sense of possibility or willingness to engage in the process.
  • Clear Next Steps are Established: You and the client collaboratively define specific, actionable steps they can take before the next session.
  • Spiritual Foundation is Acknowledged: The client connects their faith to their marital journey and sees God's role in their healing.
  • Client Takes Ownership: They move from blaming their spouse to exploring their own responses and potential actions.
  • Empowerment Over Dependence: The client feels equipped and capable of making progress, rather than solely relying on you for answers.
Coach Tip

When working with clients facing intense marital pain, remember the power of prayer *with* them. A brief, heartfelt prayer at the beginning or end of a session can anchor the conversation in faith and invite the Holy Spirit's guidance, often bringing a palpable sense of peace and hope (Johnson & Johnson, 2018).

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. Sarah mentioned feeling "stuck" and "running on empty" when asked to respond with grace. What is the coach's primary role in addressing this specific concern?

The coach's primary role is to validate Sarah's feelings, acknowledging that grace isn't about ignoring pain but about drawing from God's strength. The coach then reframes grace as an empowering act that starts with understanding one's own needs and expressing them constructively, inviting Sarah to explore this new perspective rather than dictating it.

2. What are two key benefits of encouraging a client to "practice out loud" in a coaching scenario like this?

First, practicing out loud helps to internalize the language and tone, making it feel more natural and authentic when applied in real-life situations. Second, it allows the coach (or the client in self-practice) to identify areas of discomfort or awkwardness, providing an opportunity for refinement and increased confidence before a real conversation.

3. A new coach might be tempted to tell Sarah, "You need to forgive Mark for his emotional distance." Why is this approach generally counterproductive in faith-based marriage coaching?

This approach is counterproductive because it's unsolicited advice that can feel prescriptive and dismissive of the client's current pain. Forgiveness is a process, not a command, and forcing it can lead to superficial compliance or deeper resentment. A faith-based coach guides the client to explore forgiveness on their own timeline, rooted in God's grace, rather than imposing it.

4. According to the lesson, what is a crucial indicator that a client is taking ownership in their marital journey, rather than just blaming their spouse?

A crucial indicator is when the client moves from solely blaming their spouse to exploring their own responses and potential actions within the marriage. This shift demonstrates a willingness to focus on their personal agency and how they can contribute to positive change, even if their spouse's behavior remains unchanged initially.

Key Takeaways

Your Key Takeaways

  • Validate and Empower: Begin challenging conversations by validating the client's pain while simultaneously empowering them to find their agency within God's plan.
  • Grace-First Approach: Frame grace not as weakness, but as a spiritual strength that enables constructive engagement, even amidst deep hurt.
  • Active Practice Builds Confidence: Regularly practicing coaching dialogues out loud is essential for developing natural delivery, empathy, and professional presence.
  • Avoid Pitfalls: Steer clear of taking sides, giving unsolicited advice, and bypassing the spiritual dimension to maintain your role as an effective faith-based coach.
  • Focus on Client Ownership: Successful sessions guide clients towards taking responsibility for their own actions and growth, fostering hope and actionable change.

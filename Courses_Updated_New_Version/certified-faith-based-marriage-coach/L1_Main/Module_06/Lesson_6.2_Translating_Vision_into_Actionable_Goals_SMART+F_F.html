Opening Case Study: Sarah & Mark's Disconnected Dream

From Vague Longing to Vibrant Connection

Sarah (48) & Mark (50)

Presenting Situation: Sarah, a busy HR executive, and Mark, a self-employed consultant, had reached a point of comfortable disconnection after 25 years of marriage. They loved each other, but the spark was dim. Their marital vision (developed in Module V) was to "cultivate a marriage rooted in intentional spiritual growth and joyful companionship." However, when asked what that actually *looked* like, they struggled to define it beyond vague statements like "spend more time together" or "feel closer."

Coach's Initial Challenge: How to help Sarah and Mark bridge the gap between their beautiful, God-honoring vision and the practical, day-to-day actions required to manifest it, especially given their demanding schedules.

Sarah and Mark's situation is common. Many couples have a heartfelt desire for a better marriage but lack the tools to translate that desire into actionable, measurable steps. This is where the SMART+F framework becomes an invaluable asset for you, the coach. We'll revisit Sarah and Mark to see how applying this framework transformed their approach to their marital vision.

From Vision to Action: The Bridge

Think back to Modules V (Values & Vision Alignment) and E (Empowerment Through Scripture). You masterfully guided your couples to articulate their individual faith values, discover their shared values, and ultimately craft a compelling, God-honoring marital vision. This vision is the North Star for their marriage ‚Äì the ultimate destination they aspire to reach.

However, a vision without a plan is just a wish. Our role as coaches in the "Action & Accountability" (A) phase of the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Framework‚Ñ¢ is to help couples draw a clear, practical map from their current reality to that desired future. This bridge is built with actionable goals.

Consider the analogy of building a house. The vision is the architectural blueprint ‚Äì beautiful, inspiring, and comprehensive. But to build the house, you need specific tasks: laying the foundation, framing the walls, installing plumbing. Each task is a goal, contributing to the larger vision.

The transition from abstract vision to concrete action is often where couples get stuck. They might feel overwhelmed, unsure where to start, or lack clarity on what "more connected" actually entails. Your expertise in goal-setting provides the clarity and structure they need.

Coach's Tip

Connecting Vision to Goals: When introducing goal setting, always start by explicitly linking it back to their marital vision. Say, "Remember the beautiful vision you crafted for your marriage? Today, we're going to break that down into practical steps, so you can start living that vision every single day. These goals aren't just tasks; they're direct pathways to the marriage God is calling you to build." This reinforces purpose and motivation.

Introducing the SMART+F Framework

The SMART goal-setting framework is a widely recognized and highly effective tool for defining clear, actionable objectives. As Faith-Based Marriage Coaches, we enhance this framework by adding a crucial component: +F for Faith-centered. This ensures that every goal is not only practical but also deeply aligned with the couple's spiritual walk and God's design for their marriage.

Element Description Example (Before SMART+F) Example (After SMART+F)
Specific Clearly defined, unambiguous. Answers: Who, What, Where, When, Why, Which? "We want better communication." "We will have a 15-minute daily check-in conversation each evening after dinner to share our day, without interruption, focusing on listening to understand."
Measurable Quantifiable; allows progress tracking. Answers: How much? How many? How will I know when it's accomplished? "We'll be more affectionate." "We will initiate physical touch (e.g., holding hands, a hug, an arm around the shoulder) at least once a day, and express verbal affirmation to each other three times a week."
Achievable Realistic and attainable given resources, time, and commitment. Is it challenging but not impossible? "We'll never argue again." "We will use 'I' statements during disagreements to express feelings and needs, and take a 20-minute cool-down break if discussions become heated, before returning to the conversation."
Relevant Aligns with the couple's overall marital vision and values. Why is this goal important to them? "I want my spouse to do more chores." "To foster a more harmonious and respectful home environment, we will clearly divide household tasks, each taking responsibility for specific duties by Wednesday evening." (Linked to vision of 'harmonious home')
Time-bound Has a defined start and end date, or a frequency. When will this be achieved? "We'll pray together sometimes." "We will pray together for 5-7 minutes each morning before starting our day, for the next 30 days, to strengthen our spiritual intimacy."
+F Faith-centered Rooted in biblical principles, spiritual growth, and reliance on God. How does God's Word or His guidance inform this goal? (Not considered) "We will pray together for 5-7 minutes each morning, seeking God's wisdom and unity, drawing on Philippians 4:6-7, for the next 30 days." (Connects to Scripture and spiritual practice)

The SMART+F framework transforms vague intentions into a clear roadmap. It empowers couples by giving them a tangible sense of control and direction, making the overwhelming journey of marital growth manageable and meaningful.

Collaborative Goal Setting Techniques

Goal setting must be a collaborative effort. As the coach, your role is to facilitate, not dictate. Here are techniques to guide couples in collaboratively identifying key areas for growth and defining concrete, observable actions:

  1. Revisit the Vision & Values: Begin by having the couple articulate their shared marital vision and the core values they identified in Module V. Ask: "Looking at your vision, what are the 1-2 most critical areas that need attention right now to move you closer to that dream?"
  2. Brainstorming & Prioritization:
    • Individual Brainstorm: Give each spouse 5 minutes to privately list actions they believe would help achieve their vision, without judgment.
    • Shared Brainstorm: Bring their lists together. Look for common themes or surprising insights.
    • Prioritization Matrix: Use a simple matrix (e.g., "High Impact/Low Effort," "High Impact/High Effort") to help them choose 1-3 initial goals that feel both significant and manageable. This builds momentum.
  3. The "What Does That Look Like?" Inquiry: For every general desire (e.g., "I want to feel more appreciated"), ask: "What would that look, sound, or feel like specifically? What would your spouse be doing or saying?" This helps translate abstract desires into concrete behaviors.
  4. Role-Playing & Scenario Planning: For communication goals, role-play potential scenarios. "If you want to have a daily check-in, what time works best? Where will you do it? What if one of you is tired?" This helps anticipate obstacles and refine the 'Achievable' aspect.
  5. Scriptural Integration: For each potential goal, ask: "How does this align with God's Word? Is there a specific scripture that inspires or guides this goal? How can we invite God into this process?" This strengthens the '+F' component.

Case Study Continuation: Sarah & Mark's SMART+F Goals

Turning "Joyful Companionship" into Action

Revisiting Sarah and Mark, their marital vision was "to cultivate a marriage rooted in intentional spiritual growth and joyful companionship." Through collaborative discussion and the "What does that look like?" inquiry, they identified two key areas:

  1. Spiritual Intimacy: Mark felt their prayers were disjointed. Sarah longed for a deeper shared spiritual walk.
  2. Joyful Companionship: Both missed the easy, fun connection they once had, especially with their busy schedules.

With your guidance, they applied the SMART+F framework:

  • Goal 1 (Spiritual Intimacy): "We will engage in a 5-minute focused prayer session together every weekday morning before work, holding hands and sharing one praise and one request, for the next 4 weeks, drawing strength from 1 Thessalonians 5:17 ('Pray without ceasing')."
  • Goal 2 (Joyful Companionship): "We will schedule and complete one 'fun date' activity (e.g., walk in the park, board game, coffee shop visit) each Saturday afternoon for at least 60 minutes, rotating who plans it, for the next month, to rekindle laughter and connection, remembering Ecclesiastes 4:9-10 ('Two are better than one')."

Notice how these goals are far more concrete and inspiring than "spend more time together" or "pray together sometimes." They are specific, measurable, and deeply rooted in their faith and vision.

Differentiating Outcome vs. Process Goals

When setting goals, it's crucial to help couples understand the distinction between outcome goals and process goals. Both are important, but they serve different purposes.

  • Outcome Goals: These are the desired results or states of being. They represent the "what" a couple wants to achieve. While inspiring, outcome goals are often not entirely within a person's direct control and can feel overwhelming if not broken down.
    • Examples: "Feel more connected," "Have less conflict," "Increase intimacy," "Be happier in our marriage."
  • Process Goals: These are the specific, actionable behaviors or steps that lead to the outcome goals. They represent the "how" a couple will achieve their desired results. Process goals are entirely within a person's control, making them empowering and actionable.
    • Examples: "Daily 15-minute check-in," "Use 'I' statements during disagreements," "Schedule one date night per week," "Read a devotional together each morning."

Coach's Insight

A common pitfall is focusing solely on outcome goals. While the desire to "feel more connected" is valid, it's not something you can directly *do*. You can, however, *do* the things that lead to feeling more connected. Your role is to guide couples from the aspirational outcome to the tangible processes that will build it.

Effective coaching involves helping couples identify their desired outcome goals, and then meticulously breaking them down into SMART+F process goals. For example:

  • Outcome Goal: "We want to feel a deeper spiritual connection."
  • SMART+F Process Goals:
    • "We will read one chapter of a Christian marriage book together each night before bed, discussing one key takeaway, for the next 6 weeks, to deepen our shared spiritual learning, as encouraged by Proverbs 27:17."
    • "We will attend our church's weekly marriage small group every Tuesday evening for the next quarter, actively participating in discussions, to build community and grow in faith together."
Coach's Tip

Reframing Discouragement: If a couple gets discouraged because they haven't "achieved" an outcome goal (e.g., "we still argue"), gently shift their focus to their process goals. Ask: "Have you been consistently implementing your process goals, like using 'I' statements or taking cool-down breaks?" Celebrate their adherence to the process, reminding them that consistent process leads to desired outcomes over time. This builds resilience and reinforces their agency.

Applying SMART+F: A Practical Guide

Here's a step-by-step approach to applying the SMART+F framework with your couples:

  1. Start with the Vision: "What part of your marital vision feels most urgent or exciting to work on right now?" This helps narrow focus.
  2. Identify the Outcome Goal: "If you achieved that, what would it look like, feel like, be like?" Help them articulate the desired state.
  3. Brainstorm Process Ideas: "What are some specific actions, behaviors, or habits that would help you achieve this outcome?" Encourage both spouses to contribute.
  4. Apply SMART+F Filters: Take each brainstormed idea and run it through the SMART+F questions:
    • Specific: Can we make this more precise?
    • Measurable: How will we track progress? What's the quantity or frequency?
    • Achievable: Is this realistic for both of you right now? What obstacles might arise? How can we adjust?
    • Relevant: Does this truly move you closer to your vision and values?
    • Time-bound: When will you start? How often? For how long?
    • +F Faith-centered: How does God's Word speak to this? How can you invite Him into this goal?
  5. Commitment & Agreement: Ensure both spouses are fully on board and committed to the goal. A shared commitment is vital for accountability.
  6. Write it Down: Have them write down their SMART+F goals in a shared space (e.g., a coaching worksheet, a journal). The act of writing increases commitment by 20-40% (Gail Matthews, Dominican University study).
Coach's Tip

Small Wins, Big Momentum: Encourage couples to start with 1-2 small, easily achievable SMART+F goals. Early success builds confidence and motivation, creating a positive feedback loop. A small, consistent step is far more powerful than a grand, inconsistent one. Celebrate every small win!

Overcoming Goal-Setting Challenges

Even with the SMART+F framework, couples may encounter challenges. Here's how to guide them:

  • Overwhelm: If they have too many ideas, help them prioritize. "If you could only pick ONE thing that would make the biggest difference right now, what would it be?" Remind them of the power of small, consistent steps.
  • Perfectionism: Some may fear failure or want the "perfect" goal. Reassure them that goals are dynamic. "This isn't set in stone. It's a starting point. We can adjust as we learn and grow."
  • Lack of Buy-in: If one spouse isn't fully committed, explore their reservations. Is the goal truly relevant to *their* vision? Is it achievable for *them*? Sometimes, the goal needs to be reframed to meet both individual and shared needs.
  • External Obstacles: Time, energy, unexpected life events. Help them brainstorm solutions. "If time is an issue, can we shorten the goal, or find a different time slot?" Flexibility is key.
  • Spiritualizing Inaction: Some couples might say, "God will handle it," as an excuse for inaction. Gently remind them of the biblical principle of faith *and* works (James 2:17-18). "God works through our efforts. What small, faithful step can you take to honor Him in this area?"
Coach's Tip

The Power of Accountability Partners: Encourage couples to be each other's primary accountability partners. Suggest they regularly check in with each other about their goals, not just during coaching sessions. For some, a trusted friend or small group might also serve this role, fostering a community of support for their marital journey.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of adding the "+F" component to the SMART goal-setting framework in Faith-Based Marriage Coaching?

The "+F" ensures that goals are not only practical but also deeply aligned with the couple's spiritual journey, biblical principles, and reliance on God, distinguishing faith-based coaching from secular approaches.

2. Sarah and Mark's initial marital vision was "to cultivate a marriage rooted in intentional spiritual growth and joyful companionship." Which of the following is an example of a SMART+F goal that effectively translates this vision into action?
A) "We will try to be more spiritual and happy."
B) "We will pray together sometimes and go on dates."
C) "We will engage in a 5-minute focused prayer session every weekday morning, holding hands, for the next 4 weeks, seeking God's unity (1 Thess 5:17)."
D) "We will improve our marriage by the end of the year."

C) "We will engage in a 5-minute focused prayer session every weekday morning, holding hands, for the next 4 weeks, seeking God's unity (1 Thess 5:17)." This option is Specific, Measurable (5 minutes, every weekday, 4 weeks), Achievable, Relevant (spiritual growth), Time-bound, and Faith-centered (seeking God's unity, 1 Thess 5:17).

3. A couple states their goal is to "feel more intimate." Is this an outcome goal or a process goal, and why is the distinction important for coaching?

"Feel more intimate" is an outcome goal. The distinction is crucial because outcome goals are desired results, often not directly actionable. As coaches, we help couples break down these aspirational outcomes into specific, actionable process goals (e.g., "initiate physical touch daily," "have a deep conversation once a week") which are within their direct control and lead to the desired outcome.

4. What is a key strategy a coach can use when a couple feels overwhelmed by goal setting or struggles with perfectionism?

A key strategy is to encourage them to start with 1-2 small, easily achievable SMART+F goals. Reassure them that goals are dynamic and can be adjusted. Emphasize that consistent small steps lead to significant progress, building confidence and momentum.

Key Takeaways

  • A couple's God-honoring marital vision serves as the essential foundation for setting meaningful and actionable goals.
  • The SMART+F framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound + Faith-centered) is a powerful tool for translating abstract desires into concrete, measurable steps for marital growth.
  • Effective coaching involves collaborative goal setting, utilizing techniques like brainstorming, "What does that look like?" inquiries, and scriptural integration.
  • Understanding the difference between outcome goals (desired results) and process goals (actionable behaviors) is crucial for guiding couples toward sustainable change.
  • Coaches empower couples by helping them create small, achievable SMART+F process goals, celebrate consistent action, and navigate common challenges like overwhelm or perfectionism with grace and flexibility.

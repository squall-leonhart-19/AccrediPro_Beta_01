1. Introduction to Group Coaching & Workshops

Group coaching and workshops offer a powerful alternative or complement to 1:1 coaching. They leverage the power of community, shared experience, and collective wisdom, often providing a more accessible entry point for clients while significantly increasing a coach's income potential and impact.

A group coaching program typically involves a cohort of individuals working through a structured curriculum over several weeks or months, led by a coach. Workshops are usually shorter, focused on a specific topic, and can be a great way to introduce potential clients to your work.

Benefits for Coaches:

  • Scalability: Serve multiple clients simultaneously, decoupling income from hours.
  • Leverage: Your time and effort impact a larger audience.
  • Community Building: Foster a vibrant network around your niche.
  • Diverse Income Streams: Add a new offering to your coaching portfolio.
  • Reduced Burnout: Less intensive 1:1 work, more group facilitation.

Benefits for Clients:

  • Affordability: Often a lower investment than 1:1 coaching.
  • Community Support: Gain insights and encouragement from peers.
  • Diverse Perspectives: Learn from others' experiences and questions.
  • Accountability: Peer accountability can be a powerful motivator.
  • Sense of Belonging: Overcome feelings of isolation in their struggles.
Coach's Insight

Think of your group program as a guided journey. You're not just delivering content; you're curating an experience, fostering connection, and creating a space for shared growth. This community aspect is a huge selling point for faith-based marriage coaching, as couples often feel isolated in their challenges.

3. Designing Your Signature Group Program

A well-designed group program is clear, structured, and delivers a specific transformation. Here's a framework to help you build yours:

A. Identifying Your Niche & Topic

While your overarching niche is faith-based marriage coaching, a group program thrives on a micro-niche. What specific problem within marriage coaching can you solve for a group?

  • Example: Instead of "Marriage Coaching," consider "Restoring Intimacy After Betrayal" or "Building a Prayer-Filled Marriage Foundation for Young Couples."
  • Action: Think about the most common, urgent, and painful problem your ideal group client (like Eleanor) faces that you are uniquely qualified to solve.

B. Structuring Content & Modules

Break down the transformation into logical, digestible steps or modules. Each module should build on the last.

Module Focus & Key Questions Activities/Resources
Module 1: Foundation & Vision What is God's design for marriage? What is our current reality vs. our desired future? Welcome packet, Marriage Vision Worksheet, initial assessment.
Module 2: Communication & Connection How do we truly hear each other? How do we express needs respectfully? Active Listening exercises, "I" statements, weekly couple challenges.
Module 3: Conflict Resolution (Biblical Principles) How do we fight fair and forgive biblically? Conflict Resolution Framework, Forgiveness Pathway exercise, prayer prompts.
Module 4: Intimacy & Partnership How do we foster emotional, spiritual, and physical intimacy? How do we operate as a unified team? Intimacy Builders checklist, Household Responsibilities audit, joint goal setting.

C. Program Length & Format

Most successful group programs run for 6-12 weeks. Consider:

  • Duration: How long does it realistically take to achieve the promised transformation?
  • Live Sessions: Weekly 60-90 minute calls via Zoom are standard.
  • Support: A dedicated online community (e.g., private Facebook group, Circle.so) for ongoing questions and peer support.
  • Resources: Workbooks, templates, video lessons, recommended readings.
Coach's Insight

Don't overcomplicate your first group program. Start with a clear, concise offering that solves one significant problem. You can always expand and refine it later. Aim for 4-6 core modules for a 6-8 week program.

4. Marketing & Enrollment Strategies

Now that you have a program concept, how do you fill it? This is where your client acquisition skills from previous modules come into play, with a group-specific twist.

A. Leveraging Your Existing Network

Your warmest leads are often those who already know, like, and trust you.

  • Email List: Announce your program to your email subscribers first, offering a special "early bird" bonus or price.
  • Social Media: Share testimonials from 1:1 clients (with permission) and talk about the impact your new group program will have. Create anticipation!
  • Referrals: Reach out to past clients or colleagues who might know couples who would benefit.

B. Online Promotion & Webinars

To reach a wider audience, consider these strategies:

  • Discovery Workshop/Webinar: Host a free 60-minute online workshop on a related topic. This positions you as an expert and allows potential clients to experience your coaching style. At the end, invite them to enroll in your paid group program.
  • Targeted Ads: If you have a budget, Facebook/Instagram ads can reach specific demographics.
  • Collaborations: Partner with other faith-based coaches or ministries to cross-promote.

5. Handling Objections & Closing with Grace

Even with a perfect fit, clients may have reservations. Your role is to address these with empathy and clarity, not pressure.

Common Objections & Confident Responses

"The investment is a lot right now. I need to think about it."
"I completely understand, Eleanor. This is a significant investment in your business, and it's wise to consider it. Many of my clients felt the same way. What specifically makes you hesitate? Is it the overall amount, or perhaps how you'd fit it into your current budget? Let's explore that together, because I want to ensure you see the clear path to your return on this investment."
"I'm worried about leading a group effectively. I'm more comfortable 1:1."
"That's a very natural concern, especially when trying something new. And it's exactly why this program is so valuable. We dedicate an entire module to confident group facilitation, including practical exercises and scripts. You'll learn how to manage group dynamics, encourage participation, and create a safe space, just as you do in 1:1, but with the added energy of a community. Think of it as an expansion of your existing skills, with guidance every step of the way."
"What if I put all this effort in and no one signs up for my group program?"
"That's a very valid fear, and it speaks to the vulnerability of launching something new. My program includes proven marketing and enrollment strategies that I've used myself and taught to many successful coaches. We'll work on crafting compelling messaging, identifying your ideal clients, and creating a launch plan that builds excitement. Plus, you'll have the peer support of other coaches in the program who are launching alongside you. You won't be doing it alone, and we'll equip you with the tools to minimize that risk significantly."
"I'm not tech-savvy. How will I manage an online group?"
"I hear you, and many coaches, especially those transitioning from other careers, share that concern. Here's the good news: you don't need to be a tech wizard. I teach you how to use simple, intuitive platforms for your live calls and online community, step-by-step. We focus on the essential tools, not overwhelming you with complex software. My goal is to make the tech aspect feel easy and manageable, so you can focus on what you do best: coaching."

Closing Lines to Practice Out Loud:

  1. "So Eleanor, based on everything we've discussed today, are you ready to take this step and join 'Flourish Together' to launch your impactful group program?"
  2. "If you're truly committed to scaling your impact and creating more freedom in your business, this program is designed to get you there. What's holding you back from saying 'yes' today?"

7. Facilitating Engaging Group Sessions

Beyond the launch, successful group programs require dynamic facilitation. Here are key elements:

  • Clear Structure & Agenda: Start and end on time. Have a clear purpose for each session.
  • Active Listening & Presence: Just like 1:1, be fully present for each participant.
  • Encourage Participation: Use breakout rooms, polls, and direct questions to engage everyone.
  • Manage Group Dynamics: Gently redirect dominant personalities and draw out quieter members.
  • Foster Peer Support: Create opportunities for members to share, encourage, and learn from each other.
  • Action-Oriented: End each session with clear action steps or "homework" for couples.
  • Biblical Integration: Consistently weave faith-based principles and scriptures into discussions and teachings.
Coach's Insight

Your role as a group facilitator is more like a conductor than a solo performer. You guide the orchestra, ensuring everyone plays their part harmoniously, creating a beautiful symphony of transformation.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is a primary benefit of group coaching for a coach like Grace Williams, who wants to scale her practice beyond 1:1 clients?

The primary benefit is scalability, allowing Grace to serve multiple clients simultaneously without directly trading more hours for more income, thus increasing her impact and revenue potential.

2. Eleanor, our prospect, is concerned about her lack of tech-savvy skills for running an online group. How should you address this objection during a discovery call?

You should validate her concern, then reassure her that the program provides guidance on simple, user-friendly tech platforms and focuses on essential tools, not complex software, making the tech aspect manageable so she can focus on coaching.

3. When presenting the price of your group program, what is a crucial non-verbal communication strategy to employ after stating the investment?

After confidently stating the price, it is crucial to stop talking. Allow the client time to process the information without you rambling or justifying the cost. Your confidence in your value speaks volumes.

4. A client in your group program consistently dominates discussions, making it difficult for others to speak. What is an effective facilitation strategy to manage this dynamic while still fostering participation?

Gently redirect the dominant participant by saying something like, "Thank you for sharing, [Name]. I appreciate your insights. Let's hear from a few other voices as well." You can also use structured activities like breakout rooms or polls to ensure everyone has an opportunity to contribute.

Verbalizing the Non-Verbal

How do you help a client describe a feeling that has no words? As a practitioner, your role is to act as a Metaphorical Bridge. Many clients‚Äîespecially those new to wellness‚Äîmay feel "silly" or "imposter syndrome" when trying to describe their experience.

Use Reflective Inquiry to mirror their language. Instead of asking "How do you feel?" (which is too broad), try these targeted questions:

  • "If that tension we cleared was a shape or a color, what was it before the session, and what is it now?"
  • "You mentioned a feeling of 'expansion' in your chest. Does that expansion have a temperature or a weight?"
  • "When the Gong reached its peak, what part of your internal 'narrative' felt like it was being washed away?"
Coach Tip

If a client says, "I don't know, I just felt relaxed," don't stop there. Gently probe with: "I hear that. And if you had to guess, which part of the music was the most relaxing‚Äîthe deep lows or the bright highs?" This helps them move from a passive state to an active, observant state.

Identifying 'Resonance Leaks'

A Resonance Leak is a cognitive habit, environment, or relationship that rapidly drains the coherence established during a session. If a client leaves your beautiful studio and immediately checks a stressful work email, they have a resonance leak.

Common leaks for our target demographic (women 40-55) often include:

  • The "Fixer" Mentality: Immediately jumping back into solving everyone else's problems (children, spouses, aging parents).
  • Digital Over-Stimulation: Checking social media or news within 60 minutes of a session.
  • Self-Censorship: Telling themselves, "That was nice, but it wasn't 'real' work."
Coach Tip

Teach your clients the "90-Second Rule." If they feel a stressor approaching after a session, they should pause for 90 seconds and hum their "anchor tone" (a tone they identified with during the session). This physically interrupts the stress response via the vagus nerve.

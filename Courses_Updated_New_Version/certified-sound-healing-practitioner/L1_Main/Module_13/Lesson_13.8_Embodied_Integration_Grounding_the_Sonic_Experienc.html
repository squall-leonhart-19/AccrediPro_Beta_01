5 Common Rookie Mistakes

Even experienced practitioners can fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to mastery.

  • The "Clanging" Strike: Striking the bowl at a 90-degree angle creates a harsh, metallic "clack." Always strike at a 45-degree angle with a felt-covered mallet.
  • Over-Explaining: New coaches often talk too much because they are nervous. Let the bowls do the heavy lifting.
  • Ignoring Contraindications: Critical Safety: Never place bowls directly on the body of someone with a pacemaker, or during the first trimester of pregnancy, without specialized advanced training.
  • Rushing the Ending: Bringing a client out of a deep Theta state too fast is called "Vibrational Shock." Use a soft chime or your voice to slowly transition them back.
  • Inconsistent Rhythm: Your striking rhythm is the "heartbeat" of the session. If itâ€™s erratic, the clientâ€™s nervous system can't entrain.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "Sunyata" in a sound healing session?

The Sunyata is the period of intentional silence at the end of the session. It allows the body to integrate the vibrations and is often where the deepest healing occurs.

2. Why should you strike a bowl at a 45-degree angle?

To avoid the "clacking" sound of a direct 90-degree hit. A 45-degree angle ensures the mallet makes soft contact, producing a pure, resonant tone without percussive noise.

3. A client has a pacemaker. What is your protocol?

Do not place bowls directly on the body. You can play bowls in the room (off-body), but maintain a distance of at least 3-5 feet to ensure the electromagnetic field of the vibration doesn't interfere with the device.

4. What is "Vibrational Shock"?

It is the jarring sensation a client feels if the practitioner ends the session too abruptly or speaks too loudly immediately after deep relaxation.

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

As you build your practice, avoid these pitfalls that often trip up new coaches (especially those of us who are recovering perfectionists!).

4 Common Practitioner Mistakes

  • Over-Explaining: Giving a 20-minute lecture on physics when the client just wants to relax. Keep explanations under 3 minutes.
  • The "Whisper Voice": Many new coaches use a tiny, airy voice. Use your natural, confident speaking voice to project authority.
  • Ignoring the Body: Forgetting to ask about physical contraindications (like pacemakers or recent surgeries) before using tuning forks.
  • Rushing the Transition: Moving too quickly between instruments, which can startle the client's nervous system.
Sarah's Tip

I always keep a "Science Cheat Sheet" in my treatment room. If I get a question I can't answer, I say, "That's a fascinating technical question. Let me grab my clinical notes so I can give you the exact frequency data." It shows you are a professional who values accuracy.

1. Why is it important to use terms like "vagus nerve" with a client like Elena?

It bridges the gap between holistic healing and her medical background, building professional trust and clinical legitimacy.

2. What is the primary goal of explaining the "Science Bridge" to a client?

To move the client from a state of skepticism or "analytical thinking" into a state of safety and receptivity.

3. What should you do if a client asks a question about the physics of sound that you don't know the answer to?

Maintain your authority by acknowledging the complexity of the question and offering to provide the specific data after the session or by referencing your clinical notes.

4. How does Entrainment work in a sound healing session?

It is the process where a stronger external rhythm (the sound tool) causes a weaker internal rhythm (the client's heart rate or brainwaves) to synchronize with it.

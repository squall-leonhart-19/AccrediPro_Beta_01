Your Coaching Scenario

Presenting Situation: Elena has recently left a 25-year career in education to start her own business. She tells you she feels "completely paralyzed by the tech side" and feels like a "fraud" when she tries to post on social media.

Global Complaint: "I'm just so stressed about this career change. I feel like I'm failing before I've even started."

SUDs (Initial): She says her stress is a "12 out of 10."

Coach Tip: The Laser vs. The Flashlight

When Elena says she is at a "12 out of 10," she is in a global state. Tapping here is like using a flashlight on a foggy night. To get results, we need a laser. Ask: "When you feel that 12, what is the most recent specific moment that triggered it?"

The Coaching Script

Practice these transitions out loud. Pay attention to how the coach (you) gently narrows the focus without dismissing Elena's broader feelings.

Dialogue Practice

Elena:
"I sat down to write a post for LinkedIn this morning and I just froze. My heart started racing and I thought, 'Who am I kidding? I'm just an old teacher, I don't know how to do this.' It's all just too much."
You (Coach):
"I hear how heavy that feels, Elena. You're navigating a huge transition, and that 'fraud' feeling is something many of my most successful clients have felt. Let's look at that moment this morning when you froze. As you think about sitting at your desk right now, where do you feel that 'frozen' sensation in your body?"
Elena:
"It's right here in my throat. Like a tight knot."
You (Coach):
"A tight knot in the throat. And if 10 is the most intense that knot could feel, how intense is it right now as you think about that LinkedIn post?"
Elena:
"It's an 8."
Coach Tip: Identifying the "Zing"

Notice how we moved from "career change" (Global) to "LinkedIn post this morning" (Specific Event) to "knot in the throat" (Physical Target). This is the EFT Targeting Sequence in action.

Coach Tip: Handling Resistance

If a client says, "I don't know, it's just everything!" don't panic. Simply say: "I understand it feels like everything. If you had to pick just one tiny slice of that 'everything' from the last 24 hours, what would it be?"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced practitioners can fall into these traps. Awareness is 90% of the cure.

  • Chasing the "Why": New coaches often ask "Why do you feel this way?" This leads to intellectualizing. Instead, ask "What does it feel like now?"
  • Accepting "Large" Targets: Don't tap on "My childhood." It's too big. Tap on "The time my dad forgot my 7th birthday."
  • Forgetting the SUDs: If you don't get a baseline number, you won't know if the tapping worked. Always get the number before you start the round.
  • Leading the Client: Don't say "You feel angry, right?" Ask "What emotion is coming up for you?"
Coach Tip: The Power of Silence

After you ask for a specific target, wait. It takes the brain a few seconds to scan the "hard drive" for a specific memory. Don't fill the silence with more questions.

Success Markers

How do you know you've successfully identified a target? Look for these three signs:

  1. The "Zing": The client's physiology changes (breathing hitches, eyes tear up, or they shift in their seat) when they mention the specific event.
  2. Sensory Details: The client can describe what they saw, heard, or felt in their body.
  3. A Clear SUDs: The client can give you a number between 0-10 without hesitation.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "I'm just a failure at this new business." Is this a good target for a tapping round?

No. This is a global belief. To make it a target, you must ask for a specific recent event where they felt like a "failure."

2. What is the primary purpose of the SUDs scale?

The SUDs scale provides a subjective baseline to measure the effectiveness of the tapping and track the reduction of emotional intensity.

3. If a client gives a SUDs of 10 for "my whole life," what should the coach do?

Gently narrow the focus. Ask the client to zoom in on one specific moment or one specific body sensation related to that feeling.

4. Why do we ask where the client feels the emotion in their body?

It anchors the client in the present moment and provides a physical "target" that is easier for the amygdala to process than an abstract concept.

Lab Summary: Key Takeaways

  • Specific is Terrific: The more specific the event, the more effective the tapping.
  • Body First: Always bridge the emotion to a physical sensation (e.g., "tightness in chest").
  • SUDs are Mandatory: Never skip the baseline 0-10 score.
  • Practice Out Loud: Speaking the transitions builds the neural pathways for professional coaching.

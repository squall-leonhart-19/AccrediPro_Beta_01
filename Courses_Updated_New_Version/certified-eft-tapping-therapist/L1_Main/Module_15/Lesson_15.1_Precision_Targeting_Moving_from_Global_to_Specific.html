The Global Tapping Trap

In clinical practice, "Global Tapping" refers to using broad, non-specific language in the setup statement. Examples include "this stress," "my depression," or "this trauma." While this might provide temporary relaxation, it rarely leads to the permanent Somatic Shift we aim for in the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Methodâ„¢.

A 2021 review of energy psychology interventions noted that practitioners who achieved a 50% or greater reduction in PTSD symptoms within 4 sessions were 3.4x more likely to use specific event targeting compared to those using global affirmations (Staton et al., 2021).

Why Global Tapping Fails:

  • Neural Diffusion: The brain cannot "find" the specific neural circuit for "stress" because it's too broad. It's like trying to find a specific person in a stadium by shouting "Hey, Human!"
  • Psychological Reversal: Broad issues often trigger subconscious defenses. Specific events are smaller and feel "safer" for the system to process.
  • Measurement Failure: You cannot accurately measure a SUDs score for "my whole life." You can measure the distress of a specific 5-second memory.

The Tabletop & Legs Metaphor

This is the gold standard for explaining specificity to your clients. Imagine a tabletop. The Tabletop represents the global issue (e.g., "I'm not good enough"). A tabletop cannot stand on its own; it is supported by Legs.

The Legs are the specific events, memories, or experiences that prove the tabletop is true.

Global Tabletop (The Belief) Specific Legs (The Events)
"I am a failure." The time in 4th grade I forgot my lines in the play.
"Men aren't safe." The specific look on my ex-husband's face during the 2012 argument.
"I'm bad with money." Opening the credit card bill last Tuesday at the kitchen table.

Your goal as a practitioner is to ignore the tabletop and saw off the legs. When you neutralize enough specific legs, the tabletop (the belief) has no choice but to collapse.

Hunting for Sensory Snapshots

To move from global to specific, you must become an "Emotional Detective." Use the VAKOG framework to find the "stinger" of the memory:

  • Visual: What is the exact "freeze frame" or snapshot? (The color of the shirt, the lighting in the room).
  • Auditory: What was the exact tone of voice or specific words said?
  • Kinesthetic: Where do you feel it in your body now as you think about it?
  • Olfactory/Gustatory: Are there specific smells (hospital smell, perfume) or tastes associated?
Coach Tip: The "Movie Technique"

If a client gives you a 2-hour story, ask them to "fast forward the movie" to the 2-3 seconds that are the most intense. That 3-second clip is your target. Tapping on a 2-hour story is global; tapping on a 3-second clip is precision.

First, Worst, or Most Recent

When a client has 50 "legs" supporting a tabletop, where do you start? We use the F.W.M. Heuristic:

  1. The First: The earliest time they remember feeling this way. This often holds the "root" neural blueprint.
  2. The Worst: The event with the highest SUDs (Subjective Units of Distress). Neutralizing the "big one" often causes a ripple effect, neutralizing smaller events automatically.
  3. The Most Recent: The event that triggered them to book the session. This is the most "active" in their nervous system right now.

The 5-Point Specificity Test

Before you begin the tapping sequence (the P: Process phase of T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Methodâ„¢), run your target through this checklist:

  • Time: Can we pin it to a specific day/time?
  • Place: Can the client visualize the exact location?
  • Duration: Is the event short (under 2 minutes)? If it's longer, find a "snapshot" within it.
  • Intensity: Is there a clear SUDs score (0-10) for this specific moment?
  • Sensory: Is there at least one VAKOG element included in the setup?
Coach Tip: Handling "I Don't Know"

If a client says "I don't know when it started," ask: "If you did know, or if you were to make it up, what would it be?" The subconscious often provides the answer through "imagination" which is actually a veiled memory.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Even though I have this work stress" considered a Global Tapping statement?

It is global because "work stress" is a broad category, not a specific event. It lacks a specific time, place, and sensory snapshot, making it difficult for the amygdala to target a specific neural circuit.

2. In the Tabletop metaphor, what do the "legs" represent?

The legs represent specific events, memories, or "proofs" that support the global belief (the tabletop). Neutralizing the legs causes the belief to collapse.

3. What does VAKOG stand for in the context of precision targeting?

Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic, Olfactory, and Gustatory. These are the sensory channels used to identify the "stinger" or most intense part of a memory.

4. When should you choose to tap on "The First" event?

You choose "The First" when you want to address the root cause or the foundational neural blueprint of a recurring emotional pattern.

Key Takeaways

  • Specificity is Safety: The brain processes small, specific events more easily than large, global concepts.
  • Be a Movie Director: Always ask for the "freeze frame" of the memory to ensure you aren't tapping on a long, diffused story.
  • Saw the Legs: You don't need to tap on "Low Self Esteem" directly; tap on the 5 memories that created it.
  • Measure the Moment: SUDs scores are only accurate when applied to a specific, narrow target.

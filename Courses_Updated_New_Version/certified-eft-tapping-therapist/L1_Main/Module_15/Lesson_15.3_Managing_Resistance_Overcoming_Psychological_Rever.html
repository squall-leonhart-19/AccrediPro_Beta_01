Defining Psychological Reversal (PR)

Psychological Reversal is a state where the subconscious mind perceives the problem as safer than the solution. In the context of the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Method‚Ñ¢, PR acts as a neurological "polarity reversal" that prevents the meridian system from accepting the new, positive energetic input.

It is critical to understand that PR is not "self-sabotage." As a practitioner, reframing this for your clients is essential for maintaining the therapeutic alliance. PR is actually a misguided protection mechanism. The subconscious believes that letting go of the anxiety, pain, or limiting belief will expose the client to a greater danger.

Coach Tip: The Handbrake Analogy

Explain PR to your clients using the "Handbrake Analogy." Tell them: "It's like trying to drive your car forward while the emergency brake is pulled tight. You're pressing the gas (tapping), but the car isn't moving. We don't need a bigger engine; we just need to release the brake."

Secondary Gain: The Hidden Benefit

Secondary Gain is the "benefit" the client receives from maintaining their current state. For a 45-year-old woman transitioning careers, the secondary gain of "imposter syndrome" might be that it prevents her from taking the risk of launching her business, thus protecting her from the possibility of public failure.

Case Study: The "Safety" of Chronic Pain

Sarah, 49, Former Corporate Executive

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah suffered from chronic tension headaches that prevented her from completing her EFT certification. Every time she sat down to study, the pain spiked to a SUDs 9.

The Intervention: Standard tapping on "this headache" yielded no results. We pivoted to the Target of "The benefit of this headache."

The Outcome: Sarah realized the headache was her "permission slip" to rest. Without it, she felt she had to be "productive" 24/7. We tapped on: "Even though I need this headache to protect me from burnout, I deeply and completely accept myself." Her SUDs dropped to 2 within one round.

Recalibrating the Setup Statement

When you encounter PR, the standard "Even though I have this problem..." isn't enough. You must Process the resistance itself. This is what we call "Tapping on the Tapping."

Try these recalibrated Setup Statements:

  • For Safety: "Even though it doesn't feel safe to let this go yet, I accept that my body is just trying to protect me."
  • For Identity: "Even though I'm afraid of who I'll be without this struggle, I am open to discovering a new version of me."
  • For Progress: "Even though a part of me wants to stay stuck to stay safe, I accept all of me, including the part that's afraid to change."

The Sore Spot vs. The Karate Chop

In Level 1, we primarily use the Karate Chop (KC) point. However, for deep-seated Psychological Reversal, the Sore Spot (Neurolymphatic point) is often more effective. Located about 3 inches down and 3 inches over from the base of the throat, these points are related to the body's lymphatic drainage and the "conception vessel."

Clinical Note

Use the Sore Spot when:
1. The client's SUDs haven't moved in 3 rounds.
2. The issue is chronic (lasting 5+ years).
3. The client feels "numb" or disconnected from the emotion.

Coach Tip: Somatic Mechanics

When using the Sore Spot, have the client use a firm, circular rubbing motion rather than tapping. This physical stimulation often "wakes up" the nervous system to receive the cognitive reframing of the Setup Statement.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of Psychological Reversal according to EFT theory?

It is a subconscious safety mechanism designed to protect the individual from a perceived danger associated with changing or letting go of a problem.

2. Which physical point is recommended for deep-seated or stubborn resistance?

The "Sore Spot" (Neurolymphatic Congestion point) on the upper chest, used with a firm circular rubbing motion.

3. What is a "Tail-End" in the context of tapping?

A "Tail-End" is a subconscious doubt or "Yes, but..." that follows a positive statement or Setup Statement, indicating the presence of PR.

4. How should a practitioner reframe PR to a client to maintain the therapeutic alliance?

By explaining it as a "misguided protection mechanism" or a "handbrake" rather than self-sabotage, which reduces the client's shame and guilt.

Mastering the "Finish Line"

As a professional EFT practitioner, your value is often measured by the permanence and depth of the shift you facilitate. While many beginners are satisfied with a SUDs reduction from 10 to 3, the true expert knows that the "last two points" are often where the deepest healing occurs. In this lesson, we explore the somatic and psychological tools required to clear the final remnants of distress.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze the role of secondary gain in maintaining residual emotional intensity
  • Execute the "Remaining Intensity" setup phrase for precise neutralization
  • Integrate somatic breathwork to clear stubborn residual energy in the final stage
  • Apply practitioner patience and "holding space" techniques during the session close

The SUDs Plateau: Why 2 Isn't Zero

In the clinical application of The T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Method‚Ñ¢, we often see a rapid descent from high SUDs (8-10) to moderate levels (3-4). However, as we enter the Neutralize phase, progress can stall. This is known as the "SUDs Plateau."

A score of 2 is fundamentally different from a 0. A 2 represents a lingering echo. If a client leaves a session at a 2, that remaining intensity can act as a seed, allowing the emotional charge to grow back over time. Getting to 0 ensures that the neural pathway associated with that specific trigger has been successfully reconsolidated.

Coach Tip

Don't fall into the "Good Enough" trap. Many practitioners feel pressured to end the session when the client says "I feel much better." Always ask: "If 10 was the peak and 0 is total peace, where are you now?" If they say 2, you have more work to do.

Secondary Gain: The "Safety" of the Symptom

When the SUDs score refuses to budge from a 2 or 1, we must look for Secondary Gain. This is a psychological phenomenon where the subconscious mind perceives a benefit to keeping the problem.

Type of Resistance Subconscious Belief Manifestation in Tapping
Identity Protection "If I'm not the 'anxious one,' who am I?" SUDs stay at 2; client feels "empty."
Safety Mechanism "If I let go of this anger, I'll be vulnerable." SUDs stay at 2; tightness in the chest.
Forgiveness Block "Letting go means what they did was okay." SUDs stay at 2; "stubborn" feeling.

The 'Remaining Intensity' Setup Phrase

When targeting the final remnants, the language of the Setup Statement must become more surgical. General phrases like "this problem" are no longer effective. We must pivot to the leftover nature of the feeling.

The Protocol:
"Even though I have this remaining intensity in my [Body Part], and it feels like a , I deeply and completely accept myself and this last little bit of tension."

By using the word "remaining," you are signaling to the brain that the bulk of the work is done, and you are simply "cleaning up." This reduces the threat response and allows the final neural discharge.

Case Study: The Teacher's Resentment

Sarah, 52, Former Educator

Presenting Issue: Sarah had a SUDs of 9 regarding a confrontation with her principal. After 3 rounds of tapping, she was at a "stubborn 2." She felt a "tingle" in her jaw that wouldn't leave.

Intervention: Instead of tapping on the confrontation again, we tapped on the "remaining jaw tingle." We used the phrase: "Even though I'm holding onto this last 2% of anger to keep myself safe..."

Outcome: Sarah realized that if she hit 0, she might have to talk to the principal again. Once we tapped on the "fear of the next conversation," the jaw tingle vanished, and she hit a 0. Sarah now runs a successful coaching practice for teachers, earning $2,500/month part-time.

The 'Breath' as a Neutralization Tool

Sometimes, the "Last Two Points" are not cognitive at all‚Äîthey are purely somatic. The energy is stuck in the fascia or the respiratory diaphragm. This is where Somatic Breath Integration becomes vital.

The Technique:
1. Identify the location of the "2" (e.g., "a small knot in the stomach").
2. Tap the Collarbone Point (K-27) continuously.
3. Instruct the client: "Inhale deeply into that knot, and as you exhale, imagine the breath carrying the last of that color out of your body."
4. Repeat 3 times without verbal phrases.

Coach Tip

Silence is a tool. In the final stages of neutralization, stop talking. Let the client's body do the processing. A 30-second pause while tapping the collarbone can be more effective than a dozen reminder phrases.

Practitioner Patience: The Final 5 Minutes

The final five minutes of a session are the most critical for long-term retention. As a practitioner, your nervous system acts as a co-regulator. If you are rushing to finish the session on the hour, the client will sense your "hurry" and their SUDs will likely stay at a 2 as a way to maintain the connection or out of a sense of incompletion.

The "Zero Check" Protocol:
Before closing, ask the client to "try to get the feeling back."
"Close your eyes. Try to get that anger back. Go back to that movie scene. Can you find any trace of that 2?"
If they can find it, tap one more round. If they can't, they have reached True Neutralization.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a SUDs score of 2 considered a "seed" for future distress?

A score of 2 indicates that the neural pathway has not been fully reconsolidated. The lingering emotional charge can act as a foundation for the original distress to return or "grow back" when triggered in the future.

2. What is the primary purpose of using the word "remaining" in the setup phrase?

It signals to the brain that the majority of the threat has been resolved, shifting the focus from "fighting a problem" to "cleaning up remnants," which lowers the amygdala's resistance.

3. How does "Identity Protection" manifest as secondary gain?

The client may subconsciously fear that losing the symptom (like chronic anxiety) will leave them without an identity or a way to relate to others, causing the SUDs to stall at a low level.

4. What is the "Zero Check" and why is it used?

The Zero Check involves asking the client to intentionally try to bring the negative feeling back. If they cannot, it confirms that the emotional charge has been fully neutralized and the session is complete.
Key Takeaways
  • Zero is the Goal: Aiming for 0 ensures neural reconsolidation and prevents the return of the emotional trigger.
  • Identify Secondary Gain: If a client is stuck at 2, ask what the "benefit" might be of keeping that last bit of intensity.
  • Somatic Integration: Use K-27 tapping combined with focused breathwork to clear purely physical remnants.
  • The "Remaining" Pivot: Use specific language to target the "leftover" intensity rather than the original problem.
  • Test the Result: Always perform a "Zero Check" by asking the client to try and recreate the distress.

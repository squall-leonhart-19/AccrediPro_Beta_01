The Original Full Basic Recipe

The Full Basic Recipe was the cornerstone of EFT as introduced by Gary Craig in the mid-1990s. It was designed as a "comprehensive sweep" of the body's primary meridian endpoints to ensure that no matter where an energy blockage resided, it would be addressed.

In clinical practice, the Full Basic Recipe includes the Setup, the Tapping Sequence (including finger points), the 9-Gamut Procedure, and a second Tapping Sequence. This "sandwich" approach was believed to be necessary for the most difficult cases.

The 9-Gamut Procedure: Brain Balancing

The most unique aspect of the Full Basic Recipe is the 9-Gamut Procedure. This involves tapping on the "Gamut Point" (located on the back of the hand between the knuckles of the ring finger and pinky) while performing nine specific neurological actions.

These actions are designed to stimulate different parts of the brain‚Äîswitching between the left hemisphere (logic, counting) and the right hemisphere (creativity, humming/melody). This "hemispheric bridge" is particularly effective for clients who feel "stuck" or are experiencing a cognitive-emotional disconnect.

The Modern Shortened Sequence

As EFT evolved, researchers and practitioners (including those at the Association for Comprehensive Energy Psychology) found that for most emotional issues, the finger points and the 9-Gamut were redundant. The Shortened Sequence focuses on the 8 primary points from the eyebrow to the underarm (and often including the top of the head).

A 2013 meta-analysis of over 20 studies (Church, 2013) demonstrated that the shortened sequence is 95% as effective as the full version for generalized anxiety and phobias, while saving significant clinical time.

Clinical Decision Criteria

How do you choose which recipe to use? In The T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Method‚Ñ¢, we use a specific set of criteria to determine if we need to "level up" to the Full Basic Recipe.

  • SUDs Plateau: If the client‚Äôs Subjective Units of Distress (SUDs) do not move after two rounds of the shortened sequence.
  • Psychological Reversal: When a client is subconsciously resisting change (often identified by "Yes, but..." statements).
  • Complex Trauma (C-PTSD): When the emotional layers are so intertwined that a more thorough "neurological sweep" is required.
  • Neurological Disconnect: When the client is "all in their head" and cannot connect with the physical sensation of the emotion.
Calibration Tip

Always start with the Shortened Sequence. It builds momentum. Moving to the Full Recipe should be a conscious clinical choice based on the client's lack of progress, not your default starting point.

Standardizing the "Round"

For your certification and professional insurance purposes, you must standardize what you call a "Round." In this course, One Round equals:

  1. Identifying the Target (The "T").
  2. Assessing initial SUDs (The "A").
  3. One complete pass through the chosen sequence (The "P").
  4. Re-assessing SUDs.

This standardization allows you to track progress accurately. If a client starts at a SUDs of 9 and ends at a 4 after three rounds, you have a 55% improvement rate to document in your clinical notes.

Professional Legitimacy

When you speak to doctors or mental health professionals, use the term "Clinical Calibration." Instead of saying "We did some tapping," say "We performed three rounds of the abbreviated somatic sequence, resulting in a 50% reduction in the patient's subjective distress." Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary neurological purpose of the humming and counting in the 9-Gamut Procedure?

The purpose is to alternate between right-brain activation (humming/melody) and left-brain activation (counting/logic) to "bridge" the hemispheres and break neurological loops.

2. True or False: The Shortened Sequence is generally considered less effective than the Full Basic Recipe for most common issues.

False. Research shows the shortened sequence is approximately 95% as effective as the full version for most issues like anxiety and phobias, making it the modern clinical standard.

3. Where is the "Gamut Point" located?

On the back of either hand, in the indentation between the knuckles of the ring finger and the little finger.

4. When should a practitioner switch from the Shortened Sequence to the Full Basic Recipe?

When there is a SUDs plateau (no movement after 2 rounds), when psychological reversal is present, or when the client is heavily disconnected from their somatic experience.

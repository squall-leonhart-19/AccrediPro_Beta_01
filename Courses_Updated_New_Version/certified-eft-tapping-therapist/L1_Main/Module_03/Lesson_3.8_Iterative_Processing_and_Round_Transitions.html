Maya's Practitioner Tip

When a client mentions a physical sensation like "lead-like weight," use those exact words in the tapping script. It helps the brain bridge the gap between the emotion and the physical body.

The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Imagine Elena is sitting across from you (or on a Zoom screen). Notice the transition from the Setup to the Sequence.

YOU:
"Elena, I hear how heavy that weight feels in your chest. Let's start by tapping on the side of the hand. Repeat after me: 'Even though I have this heavy, lead-like weight in my chest when I think about Monday morning... I deeply and completely accept myself.'"
ELENA:
"Even though I have this heavy, lead-like weight in my chest... I deeply and completely accept myself."
YOU:
"Even though this Sunday night dread feels like a 8 out of 10... and it's sitting right in my chest... I'm open to the possibility of feeling just a little bit lighter."
YOU:
"(Moving to Eyebrow) This heavy weight in my chest. (Side of Eye) This Sunday night dread. (Under Eye) All this workload. (Under Nose) This pressure from the administration. (Chin) This lead-like feeling."
Maya's Practitioner Tip

Don't rush! Beginners often tap too fast. Give the client time to repeat the words and actually feel the sensation as they tap.

The Re-Rating Dialogue

After one full round of tapping, you must check in with Elena. Practice saying this:

YOU:
"Take a nice, deep breath in, Elena... and let it out. Now, tune back into that weight in your chest. If it was an 8 before, where is it now?"
ELENA:
"It's... different. It's not as heavy. Maybe a 5? But now I'm thinking about that specific meeting tomorrow at 8:00 AM."
YOU:
"That's a great shift. We call that an 'aspect.' Let's pivot our tapping to focus specifically on that 8:00 AM meeting. (Side of hand) Even though I have this new anxiety about the 8:00 AM meeting..."
Maya's Practitioner Tip

When the SUD score drops but a new thought pops up (like the meeting), you've successfully uncovered a new "aspect." This is a sign of a great session!

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

As you transition into professional practice, watch out for these four common mistakes I see in my mentorship groups:

Mistake Why it happens The Professional Fix
Leading the Client Wanting to "fix" them too fast. Use their words, not your interpretation.
Forgetting the SUD Getting caught up in the story. Check the number after every 1-2 rounds.
Tapping Too Fast Nervousness or imposter syndrome. Slow down; match your breath to the client's.
Ignoring Physicality Focusing only on the "thoughts." Always ask: "Where do you feel that in your body?"

Success Markers

How do you know you're becoming a "Real Coach"? Look for these three signs in your practice sessions:

  • The Sigh: Your client takes a spontaneous, deep breath (a sign the nervous system is shifting).
  • The "Aha" Moment: The client connects their current stress to an old memory without you prompting them.
  • Confidence in Silence: You feel comfortable pausing for 5 seconds to let the client feel their body.
Maya's Practitioner Tip

In the beginning, I used to worry I wasn't "doing enough" if I wasn't talking. I quickly learned that the tapping does the heavy lifting. Your job is to be the calm, steady guide.

Check Your Understanding

1. If Elena's SUD score goes from an 8 to a 5, but she starts crying, what should you do?

Keep tapping! Crying is often a "processing" sign. Stay calm, use a soothing voice, and continue the sequence using words like "all this sadness" or "allowing this release."

2. What is the primary purpose of the Setup Statement (tapping on the side of the hand)?

To neutralize "Psychological Reversal" (subconscious resistance) and to acknowledge the problem while maintaining self-acceptance.

3. When should you stop a tapping session on a specific issue?

Ideally, when the SUD score reaches a 0 or 1, or when the client feels a significant sense of peace and "cognitive shift" regarding the issue.

4. Elena says, "I don't feel anything in my body." How do you proceed?

That's okay! Some clients are "head-heavy." Focus on the emotion or the "story" instead. Use phrases like "This frustration in my mind" or "This mental overwhelm."

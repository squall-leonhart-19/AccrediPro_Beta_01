The Physiology of Stage Fright

To help a client overcome performance anxiety, you must first explain that their body is not "broken"‚Äîit is being too protective. From an evolutionary perspective, being the center of attention of a group (the "herd") was often a precursor to judgment, exclusion, or predator attack.

When a client stands before an audience, the amygdala perceives social evaluation as a threat to survival. This triggers a massive release of cortisol and adrenaline. The result is the classic "Stage Fright" profile:

Symptom Physiological Mechanism Performance Impact
Dry Mouth Digestion shuts down to divert energy to muscles. Difficulty speaking clearly; "Cotton mouth."
"Mind Blank" Prefrontal cortex goes offline during amygdala hijack. Loss of content; inability to answer questions.
Trembling Excess adrenaline priming muscles for flight. Difficulty holding notes or a microphone.
Rapid Speech The "Get out of danger fast" instinct. Audience disconnect; loss of authority.
Coach Tip #1: Normalizing the Response

Always start by telling your client: "Your amygdala thinks you are being hunted by a pack of wolves, but it's just a pack of accountants." This humor breaks the shame-anxiety loop and moves them into a state of curiosity.

Anticipatory Anxiety: The "What If" Loop

Performance anxiety rarely starts on the stage; it starts days or weeks before. This is Anticipatory Anxiety, where the brain "future-paces" failure. The T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Method‚Ñ¢ is exceptionally effective here because we can process the imagined threat as if it were happening now.

The "What If" Tapping Sequence

In the P (Process) phase, we utilize the client's specific "What If" scenarios. Common triggers include:

  • "What if I forget my opening line?"
  • "What if they see my hands shaking?"
  • "What if someone asks a question I can't answer?"
  • "What if they think I'm a fraud?" (Imposter Syndrome)

A 2022 study (n=145) published in Psychological Reports found that EFT significantly reduced anticipatory anxiety in college students before presentations, with SUDs levels dropping an average of 62% after just 15 minutes of tapping.

Presented with SUDs 9/10 regarding a Board of Directors meeting.

The Block: Sarah was a high-performer but avoided speaking roles. In the T (Target) phase, we uncovered a memory from 4th grade where she forgot a poem during a school recital and the teacher sighed loudly.

The Intervention: We used the Movie Technique to clear the 4th-grade memory. Once the SUDs for the past event reached 0, we moved to the P (Pivot) phase, reframing the "sighing teacher" to a "stressed adult who had nothing to do with Sarah's worth."

Outcome: Sarah delivered the presentation with a SUDs of 2/10, noting she felt "present and even a little excited."

T - Target: Uncovering the Root Trigger

Performance anxiety is rarely about the current audience. It is almost always a Somatic Resonance of an earlier time when being seen was unsafe. When targeting, ask your client: "When you feel this tightness in your chest about the speech, does it feel familiar? When was the first time you felt this way?"

Coach Tip #2: The Body's Memory

If the client says "I don't remember," have them tap on the sensation itself. "Even though I have this vibrating feeling in my stomach..." Usually, the memory will surface by the second round of tapping.

G - Grounding for Peak Presence

In the G (Ground) phase of our method, we move beyond just "not feeling anxious" to "feeling powerful." Once the SUDs is below 3, we anchor the feeling of Competence and Connection.

Instruct the client to tap on the Karate Chop point while visualizing the successful completion of the event. Use Sensory Grounding:

  • "I see the friendly faces in the third row."
  • "I feel my feet firmly planted on the stage floor."
  • "I hear the steady, calm rhythm of my own voice."
Coach Tip #3: The "Bathroom Stall" Tapping

Teach your clients "Discreet Tapping." They can squeeze the finger points (sides of the fingernails) while waiting to be introduced. This provides a subtle Somatic Shift without drawing attention.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does the brain trigger a "mind blank" during high-stakes public speaking?

The amygdala hijack triggers the fight-or-flight response, which effectively shuts down the prefrontal cortex (the center for logic and memory) to prioritize survival instincts.

2. What is the primary focus of the "T" (Target) phase in performance anxiety?

The focus is identifying both the current "What If" triggers and the "First Time" memory anchors where the client first felt unsafe being evaluated by others.

3. True or False: Tapping should only be done the day of the event.

False. Tapping is most effective when used to clear "Anticipatory Anxiety" in the days or weeks leading up to the event, as well as for grounding immediately before.

4. How does "Discreet Tapping" help a client on-site?

By using the finger points or squeezing the "Karate Chop" area, the client can maintain somatic regulation and keep the amygdala calm without the audience noticing they are tapping.

Key Takeaways

  • Peak State Anchoring: Use the G (Ground) phase to install sensory-rich visualizations of success once the anxiety is cleared.

Interoception: The "Sixth Sense" of Emotion

Before we can assess the body, we must understand how the brain perceives it. Interoception is the sense of the internal state of the body. While proprioception tells you where your limbs are in space, interoception tells you that your heart is racing, your stomach is "doing somersaults," or your chest feels "hollow."

Research in neuroscience, particularly by Dr. Bud Craig and Dr. Antonio Damasio, identifies the insular cortex as the primary hub for interoceptive processing. This area of the brain integrates physiological signals with emotional feelings. When we ask a client to focus on a sensation in their body, we are literally activating the insula, which is a required step for the neural reconsolidation that happens during Tapping.

Your job isn't to tell them what they feel, but to be the curious investigator who asks the right questions. This takes the pressure off you and empowers the client.

The 'Where and What' Technique

The 'Where and What' technique is a cornerstone of the A: Assess phase. It moves the client from a cognitive story ("My boss was mean to me") to a somatic reality ("There is a sharp knot in my stomach").

To perform this technique, follow this verbal protocol once the client has identified a specific target (from Module 1):

  1. Locate: "As you think about that memory, where do you feel it most in your body right now?"
  2. Define: "If that sensation had a shape or a texture, what would it be?"
  3. Describe: "Is it heavy or light? Moving or still? Hot or cold?"

Body Scanning as a Precursor to SUDs

A common clinical error is asking for a SUDs score (0-10) before the client is actually connected to their body. This results in a "head SUDs"â€”a number the client thinks they should feel rather than what they actually feel.

The Professional Body Scan Protocol:

  • Ask the client to close their eyes (if comfortable).
  • Direct their attention to the crown of the head, moving slowly down to the toes.
  • Instruct them: "Don't try to change anything. Just notice any areas that feel 'loud' or 'tight' as we scan."
  • Once the scan is complete, then ask: "Now, looking at that [Sensation in Location], how intense is it on a scale of 0 to 10?"

Clinical Calibration: The Significance of Location

While every client is unique, certain somatic locations often correlate with specific emotional themes. Calibrating your observation skills allows you to "see" the emotion before the client even speaks.

The '7' in the Chest vs. The '7' in the Throat

Imagine two clients, both reporting a SUDs of 7.
Client A feels the 7 as a "heavy stone" in the chest. This usually points toward grief, heartache, or suppressed sadness.
Client B feels the 7 as a "tightening" in the throat. This usually points toward unspoken words, fear of judgment, or a struggle with self-expression.

If you treat both as just "anxiety," your Tapping phrases will be generic. If you target the "heavy stone of grief" or the "tightening of unspoken words," the shift is profound.

Coach Tip: Handle "Numbness" with Care

If a client says they feel "nothing" or "numb," don't panic. Numbness is a somatic sensation! Treat it as a "heavy grey fog" or "a wall." Numbness is often a protective mechanism of the dorsal vagal system. Tap on the numbness itself first.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the insular cortex important in EFT/Tapping?

The insular cortex is the brain's hub for interoception. Activating it by focusing on body sensations is necessary for the neural reconsolidation (rewiring) that allows Tapping to release emotional charge permanently.

2. What is the difference between a "Head SUDs" and a "Body SUDs"?

A "Head SUDs" is a cognitive guess of intensity based on logic. A "Body SUDs" is an accurate measurement based on the actual physical intensity of a sensation (e.g., how much the knot in the stomach actually hurts).

3. How should a practitioner respond if a client says their sensation is "gooey"?

Accept it fully! In somatic assessment, there are no "wrong" words. Use the client's exact word ("gooey") in the Tapping setup statement to ensure the brain recognizes the target.

4. What is the primary purpose of the 'Where and What' technique?

To move the client from a vague emotional label (like "stressed") to a specific, tangible somatic target (like "a hot pulsing in my temples"), which is much easier for the brain to process and release.

Key Takeaways

  • The Body Never Lies: While the mind can use logic to bypass emotion, the body's somatic map provides an honest, real-time assessment of distress.
  • Specificity is King: Use the 'Where and What' technique to turn vague feelings into concrete shapes, textures, and temperatures.
  • Vocabulary Matters: Providing clients with sensory words (vibrating, hollow, jagged) helps them bridge the gap between the unconscious body and the conscious mind.
  • Scan Before Scoring: Always facilitate a brief body scan before asking for a SUDs score to ensure the measurement is anchored in physical reality.
  • Location Provides Clues: While not a hard rule, sensations in the throat often relate to expression, while the chest often relates to connection and grief.

The 'I Don't Know' Paradox

When a client says "I don't know" or "I don't feel anything" when asked for a SUDs number, it is rarely because there is no intensity. Instead, it is typically a form of Psychological Reversal (PR) or a protective mechanism of the nervous system.

In the A: Assess phase, blocks usually stem from one of three sources:

  • Analytical Over-Thinking: The client is trying to be "accurate" or "right," which pulls them out of their body and into their prefrontal cortex.
  • Dissociation/Numbing: The intensity is so high that the brain has "disconnected the fuse" to prevent overwhelm.
  • Secondary Gain: A subconscious part of the client feels unsafe letting go of the problem, leading to a "blanking" of the assessment.
Coach Tip

üí° Don't Panic: When a client says "I don't know," smile and say, "That's perfectly fine. Your brain is just being protective." This immediately lowers the pressure and prevents the client from feeling like they are "failing" at therapy.

Bypassing the Analytical Mind: The Guessing Technique

One of the most effective ways to bypass the "I don't know" block is the Guessing Technique. This leverages the subconscious mind's ability to provide information when the "conscious critic" is given permission to be wrong.

A 2021 study on somatic processing suggested that when individuals "guess," they tap into interoceptive awareness‚Äîthe sense of the internal state of the body‚Äîwithout the interference of cognitive filtering.

How to Execute the Guess:

  1. Validate: "I understand it's hard to put a number on it right now."
  2. The Permission Script: "If you did know, what would it be? Or, if you were just going to take a wild guess, what number would pop into your head?"
  3. The Body Shift: Often, the client will immediately say a number (e.g., "Oh, it feels like a 7").
Case Study: Sarah's Perfectionist Block

Client: Sarah, 48, transitioning from a high-stress teaching career.

Presenting Issue: Intense anxiety about starting her own wellness practice.

The Block: During the assessment of a specific memory of a failed presentation, Sarah stated, "I don't know what the number is. It's just... messy. I can't find a number."

Intervention: The practitioner used the Guessing Technique: "Sarah, if you didn't have to be right, and you just guessed based on the 'messiness,' what number would it be?"

Outcome: Sarah immediately replied, "It's a 9. It's definitely a 9." By removing the need for "accuracy," she was able to re-engage with the somatic intensity and continue the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. sequence.

Metaphorical Assessment Tools

Some clients are simply not "number people." For these individuals, the 0-10 scale feels cold or clinical. In these cases, we shift to Metaphorical Assessment.

Metaphor Description Best For...
The Thermometer Ask the client to visualize a thermometer. How high is the mercury? Is it "boiling" or "lukewarm"? Anger, frustration, or "heated" emotions.
The Volume Control If this emotion was a radio, how loud is the volume? Is it "blaring" or "background noise"? Overwhelming thoughts or intrusive memories.
The Color Saturation If this feeling had a color, how bright/intense is it? Is it a "neon red" or a "faded pink"? Visual learners and creative types.
The Size/Weight If this feeling was an object in your chest, how heavy is it? Is it a "pebble" or a "boulder"? Deep grief or physical somatic pressure.

Managing Flooding: Somatic Distancing

Sometimes the "block" isn't a lack of feeling, but too much feeling. This is known as flooding‚Äîwhen the client's SUDs is effectively a 15 out of 10, and they are at risk of re-traumatization.

In the T.A.P.P.I.N.G. Method‚Ñ¢, we use Somatic Distancing to assess intensity safely. Instead of asking "How much do you feel this in your body?", we ask the client to view the emotion from a distance.

The "Safe Container" Assessment

If a client is flooded, have them imagine putting the emotion or memory into a thick glass container or a safe across the room. Then ask:

  • "Looking at that container from here, how much 'pressure' does it look like it has inside?"
  • "On a scale of 0-10, how much does that container attract your attention right now?"
Coach Tip

üí° Safety First: If a client is visibly shaking or unable to speak (SUDs 10+), stop the assessment immediately and move to "Finger Tapping" or "Continuous Tapping" without words until they reach a manageable level (SUDs 7 or below).

The Visual Distance Scale

When working with traumatic memories (The Movie Technique), the intensity is often correlated with how "close" the memory feels in the mind's eye. This is a powerful assessment tool for clients who struggle with the SUDs scale.

The Protocol:
Ask the client: "When you think of that memory, does it feel like it's right in front of your face, or is it further away?"

  • High Intensity: "It's right here, I can't escape it." (Equivalent to SUDs 8-10)
  • Moderate Intensity: "It's across the room, but I can see it clearly." (Equivalent to SUDs 5-7)
  • Low Intensity: "It's like a small photo at the end of a long hallway." (Equivalent to SUDs 1-3)

As you tap, you will often find the memory "moving" further away or becoming "blurry." This spatial shift is a reliable indicator of neurological processing, even if the client can't give you a specific number.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "I don't know what the number is, I just feel blank." What is the most likely reason?

This is likely a form of Psychological Reversal (PR) or a protective mechanism where the brain has "disconnected" from the intensity to prevent overwhelm (dissociation).

2. What is the primary benefit of the "Guessing Technique"?

It bypasses the analytical prefrontal cortex (the "critic") and allows the subconscious/somatic mind to provide an intuitive assessment without the pressure of being "accurate."

3. When should you use "Somatic Distancing" instead of a standard SUDs assessment?

When a client is experiencing "flooding" or emotional overwhelm (SUDs 10+) where direct focus on the body might be re-traumatizing.

4. If a memory feels "right in front of my face," what SUDs range does this typically represent?

This typically represents high intensity, usually in the 8-10 range on the SUDs scale.

  • Metaphors Matter: Thermometers, volume dials, and weights are valid clinical assessment tools.
  • Distance Equals Safety: Moving a memory "further away" in the mind's eye is a valid measure of progress.

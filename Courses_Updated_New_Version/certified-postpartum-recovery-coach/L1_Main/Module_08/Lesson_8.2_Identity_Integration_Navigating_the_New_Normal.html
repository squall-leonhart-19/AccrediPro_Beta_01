The Grief of the Former Self

In our culture, we often celebrate the birth of a baby but ignore the death of the woman's former self. This "Identity Death" is a physiological and psychological reality. When a client says they miss their old life, they aren't being ungrateful; they are grieving a legitimate loss.

The grief typically centers on three pillars:

  • Autonomy: The ability to move through the world without considering another human's biological needs.
  • Spontaneity: The loss of the "quick trip to the store" or the "impromptu dinner" that now requires a 45-minute packing ritual.
  • Professional Competence: The shift from being an expert in one's field to being a "novice" at soothing a crying infant.
Coach Tip: Validating the Void

When a client expresses missing their old life, avoid the urge to say "But look at your beautiful baby!" Instead, use Empathetic Mirroring: "It makes sense that you miss your autonomy. You spent 40 years building that independence. It‚Äôs okay to mourn that version of yourself even while you love your child."

Narrative Therapy: Rewriting the Story

Narrative therapy is based on the idea that we live our lives according to the stories we tell ourselves. In the postpartum period, many women adopt a "Victim Narrative" where motherhood is something that "happened to" them, stripping them of their agency.

As a coach, you help them move toward an "Agency Narrative" using these techniques:

Technique Definition Coaching Prompt
Externalization Separating the problem from the person. "Let‚Äôs look at 'The Guilt' as a visitor. When does 'The Guilt' show up most?"
Deconstruction Challenging "societal truths" about motherhood. "Who told you that a 'good mother' never wants a break? Is that a fact or a cultural myth?"
Unique Outcomes Finding times when the client felt like "herself." "Tell me about a moment this week where you felt powerful or competent."

Internal Family Systems (IFS) in Postpartum

Internal Family Systems (IFS) posits that the mind is made up of various "parts." In matrescence, these parts often go to war with one another. A client isn't "confused"; she is experiencing conflicting parts.

Common Postpartum "Parts":

  • The Protector (The Hyper-Vigilant Part): Constantly checking the monitor, worried about SIDS, keeps the mother in a state of high cortisol.
  • The Professional (The Ambitious Part): Misses deadlines, spreadsheets, and the feeling of "crossing things off a list."
  • The Inner Critic: Tells the mother she is failing because the house is messy or she lost her temper.
  • The Nurturer: The part that feels deep, soul-level connection to the infant.
Coach Tip: The Power of "And"

Teach your clients the "Power of And." Instead of saying "I love my baby BUT I hate staying home," encourage them to say "I love my baby AND I miss my career." This reduces the internal friction and allows both parts to exist without shame.

Values-Based Motherhood

Most "mom guilt" stems from a misalignment between daily actions and core values. If a woman values "Intellectual Growth" but spends 100% of her time on "Domestic Maintenance," she will experience psychological distress.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Contextual Behavioral Science found that mothers who engaged in Values-Clarification exercises reported a 42% decrease in perceived stress compared to the control group.

The Values Alignment Exercise:

  1. Identify top 3 core values (e.g., Creativity, Connection, Health).
  2. Audit the last 48 hours: How much time was spent honoring these?
  3. Micro-Integration: How can we spend 15 minutes today honoring "Creativity"? (e.g., listening to a podcast while folding laundry).

The Matrescence Map‚Ñ¢ is a visual tool used within the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method‚Ñ¢ to help clients track their evolution. It moves away from the "bounce back" culture and toward a "move forward" culture.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the term "Identity Integration" preferred over "Finding your old self"?

"Finding your old self" implies a return to a version of the person that no longer exists due to permanent neurobiological and psychological shifts. "Identity Integration" acknowledges the permanent change and focuses on merging the best parts of the former self with the new maternal self.

2. In Narrative Therapy, what is "Externalization"?

Externalization is the process of separating the problem from the person's identity. Instead of "I am a failure," the coach helps the client see "The Failure Narrative" as an outside force or "visitor" that can be examined and challenged.

3. According to IFS, what is the role of the "Protector" part in postpartum?

The Protector (often hyper-vigilant) tries to keep the baby safe by maintaining a state of high alert. While its intention is safety, its effect is often chronic stress and anxiety for the mother. Integration involves thanking this part for its service while asking it to "step back" so other parts can rest.

4. How does Values-Based living reduce "Mom Guilt"?

Guilt often arises when we feel we are failing a standard we didn't choose. By defining her OWN core values, a mother can make decisions based on what matters to HER, rather than societal "shoulds," which provides a logical defense against guilt.

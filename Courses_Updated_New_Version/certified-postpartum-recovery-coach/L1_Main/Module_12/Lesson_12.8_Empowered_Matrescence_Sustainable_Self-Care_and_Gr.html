Common Coaching Pitfalls

When working with high-achievers like Elena, watch out for these common mistakes:

  • Fixing instead of Coaching: Don't just give her a list of housekeepers. Coach her through the resistance to hiring one.
  • Validating the "Shoulds": Never agree when a client says "I should be able to do this." Always reframe it as a biological mismatch.
  • Ignoring the Partner: Coaches often forget to involve the partner's role in the support village. Ensure you ask, "How can we communicate these needs to your husband in a way that feels like a win for the family?"
  • Over-identifying: If you are also a high-achiever, you might want to share too much of your own story. Keep the focus on Elenaâ€™s specific recovery map.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between 'Emotional' and 'Instrumental' support?

Emotional support involves listening and empathy (feeling heard), while Instrumental support involves tangible, physical help with tasks like cooking, cleaning, or childcare (reducing the physical load).

2. Why is 'Managing' a poor framework for postpartum recovery?

Postpartum is a biological and relational transition that requires 'receiving' and 'supporting' rather than 'managing' and 'controlling.' Applying management tactics often leads to burnout and feelings of failure when biological needs (like sleep or healing) don't follow a schedule.

3. How should a coach respond when a client says, "My mother did it all by herself"?

A coach should normalize the current struggle by explaining that modern isolation is a historical anomaly. You might say, "Your mother lived in a different cultural context, and just because she survived it doesn't mean you have to struggle through it. We want you to thrive, not just survive."

4. What is a key indicator that a client is ready to set a boundary with a 'critical' support person?

When the client can identify that the 'help' they are receiving is actually draining their emotional battery more than it is helping their physical recovery. This realization is the first step toward setting a boundary.

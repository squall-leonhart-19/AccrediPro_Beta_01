Identifying Physical 'Red Flags'

Expert insight

Utilizing the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

Mental health is the cornerstone of recovery. The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is a 10-item self-report tool designed to screen for Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs). In a coaching context, this is not a diagnostic tool, but a risk assessment tool.

Interpreting the Score

A score of 10 or higher suggests a risk of depression and warrants a referral to a mental health professional. However, the most critical item is Question 10, which asks about thoughts of self-harm. Any positive response to Question 10 requires an immediate emergency protocol.

Tracking Hormonal Markers: The Great Shift

Within 48 hours of birth, estrogen and progesterone levels drop by approximately 90-95%. This is the single largest hormonal shift any human can experience in such a short window. For women over 40, who may already be approaching perimenopause, this "crash" can be particularly profound.

Key markers to monitor (via client reporting):

  • Thyroid Function: 5-10% of women develop postpartum thyroiditis. Watch for extreme fatigue or unexplained weight changes.
  • Cortisol: Chronic sleep deprivation keeps cortisol elevated, which inhibits the healing of connective tissue and the pelvic floor.
  • Oxytocin: The "bonding hormone" can be inhibited by high stress, creating a feedback loop of anxiety.
Professional Strategy

Explain the "Hormonal Architecture" to your clients early. When they understand that their lack of motivation is a biochemical reality rather than a character flaw, their compliance and self-compassion increase significantly.

Assessing Sleep Quality & Cognitive Recovery

We cannot coach a brain that is not sleeping. Research shows that sleep fragmentation (waking every 2-3 hours) is more detrimental to cognitive function than total sleep deprivation of 4-5 hours of continuous rest.

During your evaluation, use the Sleep-Cognition Scale:

  1. Sleep Latency: How long does it take her to fall back asleep after a feeding? (High latency indicates HPA-axis dysregulation).
  2. Executive Function: Is she struggling with basic sequencing (e.g., forgetting how to drive to a familiar place)?
  3. Emotional Regulation: Does she feel "reactive" or "numb"?

Evaluating Physical Healing: Incisions & Core Stability

Physical evaluation in coaching focuses on functional mobility and structural integrity.

1. Incision Care (C-Section or Perineal)

We look for "Adhesion Indicators." Is the scar stuck to the underlying tissue? Scar tissue that is not mobile can pull on the bladder or contribute to low back pain months down the line. We recommend scar massage only after 6-8 weeks and medical clearance.

2. Core Stability (Diastasis Recti)

While most coaches are taught to "check the gap," the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Methodâ„¢ focuses on tension. Can the client generate tension in the linea alba when exhaling? If the tissue feels like a "soft marshmallow," the core is not yet ready for load-bearing exercises.

Success Tip

As a specialist, your evaluation should include a "Pelvic Floor Screening Questionnaire." If she reports leaking when sneezing, heaviness in the pelvis, or pain with intimacy, a referral to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist (PFPT) is your most valuable coaching move.

Building the Referral Network

A premium coach is only as good as their network. Your evaluation process should end with a "Resource Map." Depending on the findings of your assessment, you may need to connect the client with:

  • Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist: For structural/internal healing.
  • Perinatal Mental Health Specialist: For high EPDS scores.
  • Functional Nutritionist: For hormonal depletion and gut health.
  • IBCLC (Lactation Consultant): If feeding issues are driving sleep deprivation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which EPDS score typically serves as the "Red Flag" threshold for a mental health referral?

A score of 10 or higher is the standard threshold for risk, though any positive score on Question 10 (self-harm) requires immediate action regardless of the total score.

2. Why is "tension" more important than "gap width" when assessing Diastasis Recti?

Tension indicates the functional ability of the connective tissue (linea alba) to transfer force. A small gap with no tension is more unstable than a large gap with firm, functional tension.

3. What is the approximate percentage drop of estrogen and progesterone within 48 hours of birth?

Estrogen and progesterone drop by approximately 90-95%, creating the most significant hormonal shift in the human experience.

4. If a client reports redness and foul-smelling lochia, what is the coach's next step?

This is a medical "Red Flag" for infection. The coach must stop the session and direct the client to contact their medical provider or go to urgent care immediately.

Key Takeaways

  • Safety First: Recovery evaluation is primarily about identifying medical red flags and ensuring the client is safe for coaching.
  • The EPDS Tool: Use the EPDS to screen for PMADs, focusing specifically on Question 10 and total scores above 10.
  • Hormonal Literacy: Educate clients on the "Postpartum Crash" to normalize their emotional and physical state.
  • Sleep is Medicine: Prioritize assessing sleep quality, as it dictates the brain's ability to heal and regulate emotions.
  • Functional Referral: Your value increases when you refer out to specialists like Pelvic Floor PTs and Perinatal Therapists.

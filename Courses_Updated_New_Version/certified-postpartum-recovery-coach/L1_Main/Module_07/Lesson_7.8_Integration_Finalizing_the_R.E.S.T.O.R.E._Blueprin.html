Common Coaching Pitfalls

When working with the deep psychological shifts of matrescence, new coaches often fall into these traps:

  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Trying to give Elena a productivity schedule when she actually needs identity validation.
  • Toxic Positivity: Saying "But you have a healthy baby!" which silences her grief.
  • Over-Identifying: Sharing too much of your own story. Remember, this is Elena's matrescence, not yours.
  • Clinical Coldness: Using too much "Matrescence" terminology without the warmth of human connection.
Practice Note

If you find yourself wanting to "fix" Elena's problem, take a breath. In this module, the "fix" is the feeling of being understood.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these cues:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically drops her shoulders or sighs in relief when you validate her grief.
  • Shift in Language: She stops saying "I'm failing" and starts saying "This transition is hard."
  • Increased Curiosity: She begins asking questions about who she is becoming, rather than just crying over who she was.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use the word "Mourning" or "Grief" in Elena's case?

It validates the biological and psychological reality that identity shift involves the "death" of a former self. Without acknowledging the loss, the client cannot fully embrace the new version of herself.

2. What is the primary difference between "Fixing" and "Holding Space" in this lab?

Fixing looks for external solutions (e.g., "Hire a nanny"). Holding Space allows the client to feel her internal conflict without judgment, which is what leads to true identity integration.

3. If Elena says, "I feel like a milk machine," what is a powerful coaching reframe?

A reframe would be: "It sounds like your body is doing incredible work, but your mind is feeling underutilized. How can we bring that 'attorney brain' back into your daily rhythm?"

4. True or False: Matrescence is only for first-time mothers.

False. As seen with Elena (her second child), every new child triggers a new phase of matrescence as the family dynamic and the mother's identity shift again.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation is the Goal: In matrescence coaching, your primary tool is making the client feel "normal" in her complexity.
  • Identity Evolution: Frame the postpartum period as an evolution, not a loss.
  • High-Value Skill: Mastering the psychological side of recovery sets you apart as a premium practitioner.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking the reframes out loud builds the "muscle memory" needed for real-time coaching.

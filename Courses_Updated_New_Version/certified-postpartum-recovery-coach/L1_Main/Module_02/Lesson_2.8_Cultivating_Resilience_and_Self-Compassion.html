Common Pitfalls to Avoid

When we are nervous as new coaches, we often fall into these "safety" traps. Be mindful of these as you practice:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to give Elena a "to-do" list to feel better. Emotional processing requires being, not doing.
  • Dismissive Positivity: Saying "But at least the baby is healthy!" This shuts down the client's emotional processing and creates shame.
  • Over-Sharing: Telling your own birth story for 10 minutes. Keep the focus on her "broken" narrative, not yours.
  • Rushing the Silence: When Elena gets quiet, don't jump in. Silence is where the brain processes the emotional shift.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Dismissive Positivity" (e.g., "At least the baby is okay") harmful in Elena's case?

It invalidates her personal experience and trauma. It sends the message that her feelings don't matter as long as the "outcome" (the baby) is achieved, which leads to suppressed grief and potential postpartum depression.

2. What is the benefit of asking Elena where she feels "tightness" in her body?

This is a somatic coaching technique. It moves the processing from the "analytical mind" (which is stuck in a failure loop) to the "felt sense," allowing the nervous system to begin discharging the stored stress of the birth trauma.

3. Elena says, "I've failed at my first goal as a mom." What is the best coaching response?

A response that uses "Reframing" and "Validation." For example: "I hear how much that feels like a failure to you. But what if we looked at your ability to navigate an emergency as a sign of your high-level protective instincts as a mother?"

4. How does matrescence differ from "becoming a mother"?

Matrescence is the developmental transition (similar to adolescence) involving hormonal, physical, and identity shifts. "Becoming a mother" is an event; matrescence is a deep, often tumultuous psychological process of rebirth for the woman herself.

Key Lab Takeaways

  • Validation is the Cure for Shame: Simply acknowledging Elena's grief without trying to "fix" it reduces her cortisol levels.
  • Language Matters: Replacing "failure" with "endurance" helps the client re-author their birth story.
  • Hold the Silence: High-achievers often need extra space to feel their emotions because they are used to "powering through."
  • Matrescence is a Rebirth: The coach's role is to support the "birth" of the mother just as much as the birth of the baby.
  • Professional Presence: Your calm, non-judgmental tone is the most powerful tool in your coaching kit.

Never say "I can treat your depression." Instead, use: "I can support your recovery journey and help you build a wellness foundation while you work with a clinical specialist for your mental health symptoms." This maintains your professional standing and manages client expectations.

Mastering the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

The EPDS is the gold standard for perinatal screening. A 2021 study found that systematic use of the EPDS in non-clinical settings increased the detection of postpartum depression by 42%. As a coach, you do not use this to diagnose, but to screen for the need for clinical help.

Scoring and Interpretation

  • 0-8: Symptoms are likely within the range of normal postpartum adjustment (the "Baby Blues").
  • 9-12: Possible depression. Monitor closely and suggest a proactive referral for a "mental health check-up."
  • 13+: High probability of clinical depression. Referral to a mental health professional is mandatory for continued coaching.
  • Question 10: Any score other than "0" (Never) on Question 10 ("The thought of harming myself has occurred to me") requires immediate action, regardless of the total score.

Red Flags and 'Stop-Gap' Indicators

While we want to support every mother, some symptoms indicate that coaching must be paused or integrated with clinical care. These are your "Stop-Gap" indicators.

The "Big Three" Red Flags

  1. Inability to Function: The client cannot perform basic self-care (showering, eating) or basic infant care.
  2. Persistent Intrusive Thoughts: Violent or disturbing images that cause the client significant distress (Note: Distinguish between OCD and Psychosis).
  3. Severe Sleep Deprivation: If a client has not slept for 48+ hours despite the baby sleeping, this is a medical emergency and a precursor to psychosis.
Coach Tip: Trust Your Gut

If you feel "out of your depth," you probably are. Imposter syndrome is common, but clinical intuition is real. If you feel a sense of dread before a session with a specific client, it‚Äôs often a sign that their needs have surpassed your scope.

Crisis Intervention: Self-Harm and Psychosis

Postpartum Psychosis (PPP) is rare (1-2 in 1,000 births) but is a true psychiatric emergency with a high risk of infanticide or suicide. It typically appears within the first 2 weeks postpartum.

Signs of Psychosis vs. Intense Anxiety:

  • Anxiety/OCD: The mother is terrified of the thoughts (ego-dystonic).
  • Psychosis: The mother may believe the thoughts are true or has "delusions of grandeur" (ego-syntonic).
Emergency Protocol

If a client expresses active suicidal ideation or shows signs of psychosis:
1. Stay on the phone/in the room.
2. Ask if there is another adult in the house.
3. Provide the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline (988) or local crisis team.
4. If immediate danger is present, call emergency services or have a family member drive them to the ER.

Legal and Ethical Boundaries

To protect your practice, you must have clear documentation. This isn't just "paperwork"‚Äîit's your professional shield.

Essential Documentation:

Check Your Understanding

1. A client scores a total of 7 on the EPDS, but marks a "1" on Question 10. What is the correct action?

Immediate referral is required. Any score above 0 on Question 10 (thoughts of self-harm) is an automatic red flag that necessitates clinical intervention, regardless of the total score.

2. What is the primary difference between Postpartum OCD and Postpartum Psychosis?

In Postpartum OCD, the mother is horrified by her thoughts (ego-dystonic) and often takes steps to avoid the "danger." In Psychosis, the mother may lose touch with reality, and the thoughts may seem "logical" or "commanding" to her (ego-syntonic).

3. True or False: A Postpartum Recovery Coach should stop coaching immediately if a client is diagnosed with Postpartum Depression.

False. The coach can often continue working with the client as part of a multi-disciplinary team, focusing on recovery pillars like nutrition, rest, and routine, while a therapist manages the clinical depression. This is called "collaborative care."

4. Why is a "Warm Handoff" preferred over just giving a client a phone number?

A warm handoff reduces the executive function burden on a struggling mother and builds a bridge of trust between the coach, the client, and the new provider, significantly increasing the likelihood that the client will actually attend the appointment.

Key Takeaways

  • Screening is Mandatory: Use the EPDS at the start of every coaching engagement and at key milestones (6 weeks, 3 months).
  • Question 10 is the Compass: Never ignore even a minimal score on the self-harm question.
  • Scope Protects You: Clear boundaries between coaching and therapy prevent burnout and legal liability.
  • Network is Net Worth: Your value as a coach increases with the strength of your professional referral ecosystem.
  • Safety First: If a client hasn't slept in 48 hours or shows signs of psychosis, it is a medical emergency.

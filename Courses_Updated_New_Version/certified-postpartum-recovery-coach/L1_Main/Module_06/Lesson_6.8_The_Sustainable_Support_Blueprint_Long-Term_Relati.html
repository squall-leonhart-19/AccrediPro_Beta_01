Your Coaching Scenario

In this lab, you are coaching Elena. This is your third session with her. While she is doing well with her physical recovery, her emotional energy is being drained by conflict at home.

Client Profile: Elena

ü§±

Elena, 34

Postpartum Week 8 ‚Ä¢ Second Child ‚Ä¢ Married to Mark

  • The Issue: Elena feels "invisible." She says Mark "helps" when asked, but doesn't take initiative. She feels like the "Manager of Everything" (The Invisible Load).
  • The Symptom: Sharp, biting comments toward Mark and a feeling of deep isolation.
  • The Goal: To feel like a partner again, rather than a "default parent."
Emma's Insight

Remember, we are not here to "fix" Mark. We are here to empower Elena to communicate her needs effectively and to help her map out a support system that doesn't rely solely on one person. This shift from blame to logistics is where the coaching magic happens.

The Coaching Script

Practice the following dialogue out loud. Focus on your pacing and warmth. Do not rush the silence after Elena speaks.

YOU (The Coach):
"Elena, I hear the exhaustion in your voice when you talk about managing the household. It sounds like you're carrying the mental map for everyone, and that is a heavy load. How does it feel in your body when you realize Mark hasn't noticed the tasks that need doing?"
ELENA:
"It feels like a tight knot in my chest. I just end up snapping at him, and then I feel like a 'bad wife.' But I'm just so tired of being the only one who knows we're out of diapers or that the toddler needs a nap."
YOU (The Coach):
"That 'tight knot' is such a clear signal from your system. It's not that you're a 'bad wife,' Elena‚Äîit's that your capacity is being exceeded. In our Relational Support framework, we call this the 'Invisible Load.' What would it look like if we moved one of those 'map-reading' tasks off your plate entirely this week?"
ELENA:
"I don't even know where to start. He'll just forget."
YOU (The Coach):
"That fear of him forgetting is part of the load, too. Let‚Äôs try this: Instead of asking him to 'help' with the toddler, what if we gave him total Ownership of the 6:00 PM to 8:00 PM window? During that time, you are 'off the clock.' How does that idea land with you?"
Emma's Insight

Notice the use of the word Ownership. In coaching, we shift from "helping" (which implies the mother still owns the task) to "ownership" (which transfers the mental load). This is a key distinction for your clients.

Common Coaching Pitfalls

The Pitfall Why it Fails The Recovery Approach
Taking Sides Validating only the client's anger toward the partner can entrench the conflict. Validate the feeling, but focus the action on the system/logistics.
Giving Advice Saying "You should just tell him..." often leads to client resistance. Use powerful questions: "How would it change your evening if you weren't the one deciding what's for dinner?"
Ignoring the Body Staying only in the "story" of the conflict. Bring the client back to their somatic experience (e.g., "Where do you feel that frustration in your body?").
Emma's Insight

Don't let imposter syndrome tell you that you need to be a marriage counselor. You are a Recovery Coach. You are looking at the environment of recovery. If the relationship is toxic, refer out. If the relationship is just "postpartum-strained," you are exactly the right person to help.

Success Markers

How do you know if your Relational Support coaching is working? Look for these signs:

  • Shift in Language: The client moves from "He never..." to "We need a better system for..."
  • Somatic Release: The client's shoulders drop or their breathing deepens when discussing home life.
  • Increased Agency: The client reports successfully asking for‚Äîand receiving‚Äîhelp without a "blow-up."
  • Expansion of the Map: The client identifies support outside of the partner (friends, neighbors, hired help).

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between "Helping" and "Ownership" in the context of the mental load?

"Helping" implies the mother remains the "manager" who must notice, delegate, and check the task. "Ownership" means the partner is responsible for the entire cycle: noticing the need, planning the solution, and executing it without being prompted.

2. If a client says, "I've tried talking to him, but he just gets defensive," what is a powerful coaching response?

A coach might say: "It sounds like the current way of communicating is hitting a wall. What if we shifted the conversation away from what's 'wrong' and toward how we can protect your recovery energy as a team?"

3. True or False: A Postpartum Recovery Coach should provide deep psychological marital therapy.

False. Coaches focus on the "here and now," logistics, communication strategies, and support mapping. Deep-seated trauma or long-term marital dysfunction should be referred to a licensed therapist.

4. Why is it important to ask a client where they feel relational stress in their body?

Somatic awareness helps the client move out of the "looping thoughts" of the brain and into the reality of their nervous system state, which is essential for emotional regulation and effective communication.

Case Study: Elena's Silent Struggle

Client: Elena, 38 years old, 6 weeks postpartum (2nd child).

Presentation: Elena reports "extreme fatigue" and "brain fog." She feels she should be "back to normal" because her OB-GYN cleared her at the 6-week checkup. However, she experiences heaviness in her pelvic floor and occasional weeping.

Intervention: The coach applied the RESTORE Assessment. Elena scored a 14 on the EPDS and a physical screening revealed symptoms of Grade 1 prolapse (heaviness) and incomplete tissue healing at her episiotomy site.

Outcome: Elena was referred to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist and a PMAD-specialized therapist while the coach focused on nutrient density and nervous system regulation. Within 4 weeks, her "brain fog" lifted as her iron and B12 levels stabilized, and her physical symptoms improved with targeted therapy.

Physical Healing Markers: Normal vs. Red Flags

Postpartum recovery is often reduced to a single 6-week "clearance" from a medical provider. However, physiological repair continues for months. As a coach, you are the "eyes and ears" between medical appointments. You must distinguish between the discomfort of healing and the danger of dysfunction.

Uterine Involution & Lochia

The uterus must return from the size of a watermelon to the size of a pear. This process, involution, is driven by oxytocin. Lochia (postpartum bleeding) typically follows a predictable pattern: Rubra (red, 3-5 days), Serosa (pink/brown, up to 2 weeks), and Alba (white/yellow, up to 6 weeks).

Marker Normal Recovery Red Flag (Refer Immediately)
Bleeding (Lochia) Tapering off by 6 weeks; dark red to pink to white. Filling a pad in <1 hour; large clots (>golf ball); foul odor.
Pelvic Floor Mild soreness; gradual return of bladder control. Bulging sensation; inability to hold urine; persistent pain.
Incisions (C-Section/Tear) Pink, closed, itchy (sign of healing), fading. Pus/drainage; heat; redness spreading; opening of the wound.
Vascular Health Mild swelling in feet that resolves within 2 weeks. Unilateral leg pain/swelling (DVT risk); sudden shortness of breath.

Mental Health Screening: The EPDS Protocol

Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs) affect approximately 1 in 5 postpartum women. Research indicates that 85% of these women go untreated without active screening. The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the gold standard for identifying clients at risk.

Implementing the EPDS in Coaching

The EPDS is a 10-item self-report scale. While you are not diagnosing, you are screening for risk. A score of 10 or higher indicates possible depression; a score of 13 or higher is highly suggestive of clinical depression.

CRITICAL STEP: Question 10. Question 10 asks about thoughts of self-harm. Any score other than "0" (Never) on Question 10 requires an immediate, pre-planned safety protocol and referral to emergency services or a crisis line.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client scores a 12 on the EPDS but marks a "1" on Question 10 (thoughts of self-harm). What is your immediate action?

Regardless of the total score, any positive response to Question 10 requires immediate activation of your emergency referral protocol. You must ensure the client is safe and connected to professional crisis support (e.g., 988 in the US) or a mental health professional immediately.

Hormonal Architecture: The Great Postpartum Shift

Within 24 hours of birth, a woman experiences the most dramatic hormonal drop in the human experience. Progesterone and Estrogen levels fall by 90-95%. This is often referred to as the "Hormonal Cliff."

  • Progesterone: The "calming" hormone. Its sudden absence contributes to the "Baby Blues" and postpartum insomnia.
  • Estrogen: Affects serotonin and dopamine levels. Low estrogen can lead to vaginal dryness, night sweats, and "brain fog."
  • Prolactin: The "mothering" hormone. High levels (necessary for lactation) can suppress the HPO (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Ovarian) axis, keeping estrogen low for months.
Coach Tip: Validating the "Fog"

Clients often feel they are "losing their minds." Explain the science: "Your brain is literally undergoing a structural reorganization while your hormones are at menopausal levels. This isn't a personal failing; it's a biological transition."

The RESTORE Assessment Tool: The 90-Minute Intake

Your initial intake is a premium service. Many practitioners charge $250‚Äì$450 for this comprehensive 90-minute evaluation because it provides the client with a level of insight they cannot get in a standard 15-minute OB-GYN visit.

The 6 Pillars of the RESTORE Intake:

  1. Birth Narrative: How does the client perceive their birth? (Trauma vs. Empowerment).
  2. Somatic Scan: Tracking pain, heaviness, and physical sensation.
  3. Metabolic Review: Assessing sleep quality, hunger cues, and energy "crashes."
  4. Relational Map: Who is in the "village"? Identifying support gaps.
  5. Executive Function: Assessing the "mental load" and cognitive capacity.
  6. Values Alignment: Who is she becoming in this new identity?

Referral Pathways: When to Transition Care

Professional legitimacy comes from knowing your limits. A Postpartum Recovery Coach is a collaborative partner, not a medical provider. You must have a "Referral Toolkit" ready for every client.

The "Big Three" Referrals:

  • Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist (PFPT): Every postpartum client should ideally see a PFPT. Refer immediately for incontinence, pain during intercourse, or "bulging" sensations.
  • PMAD-Specialized Therapist: Refer for EPDS scores >10, intrusive thoughts, or inability to function.
  • Functional Medicine Practitioner/Endocrinologist: Refer for suspected thyroiditis (hair loss, extreme cold, rapid weight changes) or severe nutrient depletion.
Coach Tip: The Referral Script

When referring, use empowering language: "I want to make sure you have the best team possible. Based on what you're sharing about [symptom], a specialist in [field] can provide the clinical support that complements our coaching work."

Knowledge Check

2. What is the primary biological driver of the "Baby Blues" occurring 3-5 days after birth?

The "Hormonal Cliff"‚Äîthe rapid withdrawal of progesterone and estrogen, combined with the surge of prolactin and the physical exhaustion of labor.

3. True or False: A coach should wait until the 6-week mark to perform a RESTORE Assessment.

False. The assessment can begin as early as the first week postpartum (often virtually) to establish support, screen for mental health risks, and monitor early physical red flags.

4. Which hormone is known as the "calming" hormone that drops significantly postpartum?

Progesterone. Its drop is a major contributor to postpartum anxiety and insomnia.

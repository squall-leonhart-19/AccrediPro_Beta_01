Learning Objectives
  • Identify the verbal cues of postpartum depletion in a client interview.
  • Practice a collaborative script that validates the client's experience without "medicalizing" it.
  • Demonstrate active listening techniques to uncover hidden stressors.
  • Formulate a gentle first-step action plan that respects the client's current energy capacity.

Practice Scenario

Client Profile: Sarah, 44

Background: Sarah is a former corporate attorney who transitioned to being a stay-at-home mom three years ago. She has two children, ages 5 and 2. She presents with "crushing fatigue" that hasn't lifted despite her youngest sleeping through the night for six months.

Area Current Status
Physical Symptoms Hair thinning, brittle nails, waking up tired, "brain fog."
Emotional State Feeling "invisible," guilt over lack of patience, low libido.
Current Support Husband works long hours; no family nearby.
Primary Goal "I want to stop feeling like a shell of my former self."
Coach Tip #1

When a client like Sarah says she feels "invisible," she is often describing the loss of identity that accompanies physiological depletion. Don't rush to suggest vitamins; first, acknowledge the weight of her emotional experience.

The Coaching Script

In this scenario, we are focusing on the Discovery Phase. Your goal is to move Sarah from a place of "What is wrong with me?" to "I understand why I feel this way."

YOU:
"Sarah, I've been listening closely to everything you've shared. I want to start by saying: what you are feeling is real, and it makes complete sense given what your body and mind have been through over the last five years. Have you ever heard of the term 'Postpartum Depletion'?"
SARAH:
"No... I thought postpartum was just for the first few weeks. My youngest is two. I thought I was just failing at handling 'mom life'."
YOU:
"That's a very common misconception. In reality, depletion can last for years if the 'tank' isn't intentionally refilled. Between the nutritional demands of two pregnancies and the 24/7 hyper-vigilance of parenting without a local 'village,' your nervous system is likely stuck in survival mode. It’s not that you’re failing; it’s that your resources are tapped out."
SARAH:
"That... actually makes me want to cry. It feels like someone finally sees why this is so hard."
YOU:
"Let that feeling in, Sarah. It's the first step toward recovery. Today, we aren't going to give you a 10-step to-do list. We are going to identify one way we can start 'plugging the leaks' in your energy. Does that sound like a manageable place to start?"
Coach Tip #2

Notice the use of the phrase "plugging the leaks." For a depleted mother, adding new tasks feels overwhelming. Framing recovery as "stopping the loss" feels much safer and more achievable.

Your Turn: Practice Out Loud

Coaching is a performance art. You must get the words out of your head and into your mouth to build the "muscle memory" of empathy.

Step 1: The Mirror Test
Stand in front of a mirror. Read the "YOU" lines from the script above. Focus on keeping your facial expressions soft and your tone warm. Record yourself on your phone and listen back. Do you sound like a "fixer" or a "partner"? Aim for partner.

Step 2: The "Why" Explanation
Practice explaining Postpartum Depletion in your own words. Use this prompt: "Sarah, the reason you feel this way is..." (Try to explain it in under 60 seconds without using overly medical jargon).

Coach Tip #3

If you stumble over your words, don't stop! In a real session, a small stumble actually makes you more relatable to the client. It shows you are human, too.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Suggesting a specific supplement or diet within the first 10 minutes. This shuts down the emotional processing Sarah needs.
  • Over-Explaining: Spending 20 minutes on the science of the HPA-axis. Keep the science "light" and the empathy "heavy."
  • Ignoring the "Village" Gap: Focusing only on biology while ignoring the fact that she has no childcare or family support.
  • Minimizing the Duration: Saying "You'll feel better in a week." Depletion recovery often takes 3-6 months of consistent, small shifts.
Coach Tip #4

Always end your session by asking: "How are you feeling about our plan?" This hands the power back to the client and ensures they aren't just saying "yes" to please you.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these three signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes their shoulders or takes a deep sigh of relief when you validate their depletion.
  • Shift in Language: The client moves from saying "I am lazy" to "My body is depleted."
  • Specific Action: The client identifies one tiny, specific thing they can do (e.g., "I will go to bed at 9:30 PM tonight") rather than a vague goal like "I'll try to rest more."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to introduce the term "Postpartum Depletion" to a client like Sarah?

It provides a non-judgmental framework that externalizes the problem. It shifts the narrative from "personal failure" to "physiological state," which reduces shame and increases compliance with recovery protocols.

2. What is the "Exhale" marker, and why does it matter?

The "Exhale" is a physical sign that the client's nervous system has shifted from sympathetic (fight/flight) to parasympathetic (rest/digest). It indicates the client feels safe and understood, which is a prerequisite for healing.

3. According to the script, what is the danger of giving a 10-step to-do list to a depleted client?

A depleted client is already at their cognitive and emotional limit. A long to-do list feels like another set of demands they will inevitably fail at, which reinforces their sense of inadequacy. We focus on "plugging leaks" instead.

4. How should a coach respond when a client says they feel "invisible"?

Acknowledge and validate the feeling first. This is an identity shift concern. Use active listening to explore what parts of her "pre-mom" self she misses most before moving into physical recovery steps.

Lab Summary & Takeaways

  • Validation is Intervention: Simply naming the condition and validating the struggle is often the most therapeutic part of the session.
  • Language Matters: Use metaphors like "plugging the leaks" or "refilling the tank" to make recovery feel accessible.
  • Partner, Don't Fix: Your role is to hold the flashlight while the client chooses the path.
  • Small Wins: In the deep dive of Module 11, success is measured by the client's return to self-compassion.

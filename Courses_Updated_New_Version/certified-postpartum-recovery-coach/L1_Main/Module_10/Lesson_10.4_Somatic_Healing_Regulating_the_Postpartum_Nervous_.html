Polyvagal Theory: The Postpartum Map

Developed by Dr. Stephen Porges, Polyvagal Theory explains how our autonomic nervous system (ANS) searches for safety. In the postpartum period, the ANS is often "hijacked" by sleep deprivation, hormonal fluctuations, and the hyper-vigilance required for infant care. As a coach, you must help clients recognize which "state" they are in.

ANS State Postpartum Manifestation Coaching Goal
Ventral Vagal (Rest/Social) Calm, connected, able to bond, "rest and digest" active. Maintenance and resilience building.
Sympathetic (Fight/Flight) Anxiety, rage, racing heart, inability to sit still, "wired but tired." Discharge energy through movement and grounding.
Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown) Numbness, dissociation, extreme fatigue, "brain fog," feeling "checked out." Gentle mobilization and sensory input.
Coach Tip

When a client is in a Sympathetic state (anxious/angry), don't ask them to "just relax." This can feel threatening to the nervous system. Instead, use mobilization tools like shaking the hands or a brisk walk before attempting stillness.

Somatic Tools for Anxiety Management

Postpartum anxiety (PPA) often manifests as intrusive thoughts and a sense of being "ungrounded." Somatic tools provide a "bottom-up" approach, signaling safety to the brain through the body.

The 5-4-3-2-1 Orienting Technique

Orienting is the process of locating oneself in space and time. It tells the amygdala: "I am here, and I am safe."

  • 5 Things you see: Notice colors, textures, and light.
  • 4 Things you feel: The weight of the baby, the fabric of your chair, the floor under your feet.
  • 3 Things you hear: The hum of the fridge, distant traffic, your own breath.
  • 2 Things you smell: Coffee, the scent of the baby's head.
  • 1 Thing you taste: A sip of water or the lingering taste of mint.
Coach Tip

Teach your clients to use External Orienting during nighttime feedings. Instead of scrolling on a phone (which spikes cortisol), have them find three blue objects in the room. This keeps the nervous system in a "seeking safety" mode rather than a "threat detection" mode.

Therapeutic Touch & Body Reclamation

After birth, many women feel "touched out" or disconnected from their bodies, viewing them only as a vessel for the baby. Therapeutic self-massage is a tool for reclaiming the body's boundaries.

The Boundary Rub: Have the client use firm, slow strokes from their shoulders down to their fingertips, and from their hips to their toes. This helps the brain map the physical boundaries of the "new" postpartum body, reducing feelings of dissociation.

Abdominal & C-Section Scar Integration

For clients who had surgical births, the abdomen can feel like a "forbidden zone." Gentle, circular touch around the scar (once medically cleared) helps desensitize the area and reintegrate the core into the nervous system's map.

Coach Tip

Always maintain professional boundaries. As a coach, you are teaching the client how to touch her own body or guiding her through the sensations. If you are not a licensed massage therapist, your role is strictly educational and self-applied.

Corrective Breathing: The Core-Pelvic Floor Synergy

During pregnancy, the diaphragm is displaced, often leading to shallow "chest breathing." This keeps the body in a state of chronic sympathetic activation. Correcting this is foundational to the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method‚Ñ¢.

The 360-Degree Breath:

  1. Place hands on the lower ribs.
  2. Inhale, imagining the ribs expanding outward like an umbrella opening.
  3. Notice the pelvic floor gently softening and dropping on the inhale.
  4. Exhale slowly through pursed lips, noticing the pelvic floor and deep abdominals gently lift.

This "piston" movement between the diaphragm and pelvic floor is the body's natural pressure management system. When restored, it reduces back pain, heals diastasis recti, and calms the ANS.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which nervous system state is characterized by "brain fog" and feeling "checked out" in postpartum?

The Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown) state. This is a survival mechanism when the system is overwhelmed beyond its capacity to fight or flee.

2. Why is "external orienting" effective during nighttime feedings?

It engages the "safety-seeking" mechanisms of the brain by focusing on the immediate environment, preventing the mind from spiraling into "threat-detection" (anxiety) or being overstimulated by blue light.

3. What is the relationship between the diaphragm and pelvic floor during a healthy inhale?

They move in synergy like a piston. On the inhale, both the diaphragm and the pelvic floor should drop/soften. On the exhale, they both lift.

4. How does the "Boundary Rub" help with postpartum dissociation?

It provides tactile feedback that helps the brain "re-map" the physical limits of the body, which can feel distorted or "lost" after the significant physical changes of pregnancy and birth.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

Even seasoned professionals can slip up. Watch for these four common mistakes when working with late-postpartum clients:

  • Over-Explaining the Science: You want to show you're an expert, but don't drown the client in "ATP" and "Mitochondria" talk. Keep it focused on how it feels.
  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Don't jump to supplements in the first 5 minutes. Listen to the story first. The story contains the clues.
  • Ignoring the Career/Life Context: For a woman like Elena, her job is a major stressor. If you don't address the lifestyle drain, the nutrients won't work.
  • Low Energy Matching: If the client is exhausted, don't meet her there. You must hold the "energy of the result"â€”calm, certain, and vibrant.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to differentiate between "Normal" and "Optimal" labs?

Because "normal" ranges are based on a broad population average that includes sick people. "Optimal" ranges identify where a woman needs to be to feel vibrant and fully recovered, allowing the coach to spot depletion before it becomes a clinical issue.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Physiological Validation" phase of the script?

To remove the shame and "moral failing" a client feels. By linking her symptoms (like irritability) to her biology (nervous system depletion), you build safety and trust.

3. If Elena says "I don't have time for a plan," how should a Postpartum Recovery Coach respond?

Redirect her to the "Energy Leak" concept. Explain that the plan is designed to give her time back by increasing her mental clarity and physical capacity, making her more efficient.

4. How does a coach avoid the "Fix-It Trap"?

By practicing active listening and asking open-ended questions about the client's story before offering any protocols. This ensures the client feels seen and that the solution is tailored to her specific lifestyle.

Defining Somatic Healing within R.E.S.T.O.R.E.â„¢

The term "somatic" comes from the Greek word soma, meaning "the living body." In the context of postpartum recovery, Somatic Healing is the practice of using the body's internal sensations as a primary tool for emotional and physiological regulation.

In our R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Methodâ„¢, Somatic Healing serves as the bridge between Emotional Processing and Transition Navigation. We recognize that the body "keeps the score" of the birth experience. If a mother experienced a high-intervention birth, her nervous system may remain in a state of high alert (sympathetic dominance) long after the physical wounds have healed.

The Mind-Body Feedback Loop

Maternal mental health is often treated as a purely "brain-based" chemical issue. However, research in Interoception (the sense of the internal state of the body) shows that 80% of the fibers in the Vagus nerve are afferentâ€”meaning they carry information from the body to the brain.

A 2021 study published in Frontiers in Psychology (n=450) found that mothers with lower interoceptive awareness were 3.4 times more likely to experience Postpartum Depression (PPD). This creates a feedback loop:

  1. Physical Tension: The body holds tension in the pelvic floor or shoulders from birth trauma or nursing posture.
  2. Neural Signal: The brain receives a "danger" signal from these tight muscles.
  3. Emotional State: The brain interprets this signal as anxiety or irritability.
  4. Reinforcement: The anxiety causes more physical bracing, closing the loop.

Distinguishing Practice Scopes

As a coach, it is vital to understand where your expertise begins and ends. Somatic coaching is distinct from clinical psychotherapy and physical therapy.

Coach Tip: Identifying the Line

If a client begins "reliving" a traumatic birth (flashbacks, losing touch with the present room), they have moved into the realm of psychotherapy. Your role is to "ground" them in the present and refer them to a clinical trauma specialist while continuing to support their daily recovery rituals.

The Anatomy of Held Stress

The postpartum body is a map of recent history. Stress doesn't just "vanish"; it manifests in specific musculoskeletal patterns. In the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Methodâ„¢, we look for three primary "holding patterns":

1. The "Psoas Guard"

The Psoas is the only muscle that connects the spine to the legs. It is the primary "fight or flight" muscle. During a stressful birth, the Psoas often contracts to protect the vital organs. If this muscle doesn't "release" postpartum, the mother may feel a constant sense of impending doom or lower back pain that doesn't respond to stretching.

2. The "Maternal Brace" (Jaw and Pelvic Floor)

There is a known developmental and neurological connection between the jaw and the pelvic floor. When a mother "grits her teeth" through the challenges of early motherhood, she is often unconsciously tightening her pelvic floor, which can impede healing and cause chronic pelvic pain.

3. Diaphragmatic Restriction

Postpartum "chest breathing" is common as the ribcage adjusts after pregnancy. This shallow breathing keeps the body in a sympathetic state, preventing the "Rest and Digest" system from initiating tissue repair.

Introduction to the 'Felt Sense'

Coined by Eugene Gendlin, the "Felt Sense" is a core concept in somatic work. It is not an emotion (like "sad") or a purely physical sensation (like "hot"). It is a meaningful body-sensation.

For a postpartum mother, the felt sense might be "a heavy grey cloud in my chest" or "a buzzing electric wire in my arms." Helping a client name the felt sense moves the experience from a scary, "uncontrolled" feeling to a "workable" sensation. This is the first step in Interoceptive Reconnection.

Coach Tip: Imposter Syndrome

You don't need to be a doctor to help a client track their felt sense. Your greatest tool is curiosity. Simply asking, "Where do you feel that 'tightness' in your body right now?" is a profound intervention that most women have never been offered.

Check Your Understanding

1. What percentage of Vagus nerve fibers are afferent (carrying info from body to brain)?

80%. This highlights why "bottom-up" somatic work is often more effective than "top-down" talk therapy for nervous system regulation.

2. How does the "Psoas Guard" affect a mother's emotional state?

Because the Psoas is linked to the "fight or flight" response, a chronically contracted Psoas signals to the brain that the environment is unsafe, leading to persistent anxiety or a "sense of doom."

3. True or False: Somatic Coaching is the appropriate modality for diagnosing Postpartum PTSD.

False. Diagnosis of PTSD is a clinical task for psychotherapists or psychiatrists. Coaches support regulation and recovery within a non-clinical scope.

4. What is the difference between a physical sensation and a "felt sense"?

A physical sensation is simple (e.g., "cold"), while a felt sense is a complex, meaningful internal "aura" or "quality" (e.g., "a fluttery, nervous butterfly in my stomach").

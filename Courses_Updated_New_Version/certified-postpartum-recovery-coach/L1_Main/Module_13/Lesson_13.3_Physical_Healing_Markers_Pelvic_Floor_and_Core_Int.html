The Physiology of Structural Repair

Physical recovery begins the moment the placenta is delivered. The most immediate marker of healing is uterine involution‚Äîthe process by which the uterus returns to its pre-pregnancy size. This is not merely a "shrinking" but a massive physiological remodeling involving proteolysis (the breakdown of cellular proteins).

As a coach, you should be familiar with the standard timeline to help manage client expectations and identify deviations:

Coach Tip: Normalizing the Timeline

Many clients feel "behind" if they still look pregnant at 3 weeks. Remind them that it took 40 weeks for the uterus to expand; the 6-week involution is actually an incredibly rapid biological feat. Use the data-table above to provide objective reassurance.

Screening for Diastasis Recti Abdominis (DRA)

Diastasis Recti is the separation of the rectus abdominis muscles as the linea alba (connective tissue) stretches to accommodate the growing fetus. A 2015 study found that 60% of women still have a significant separation at 6 weeks postpartum, and 32% still have it at 12 months (Mota et al.).

Coaching Scope vs. Clinical Diagnosis

As a coach, you do not "diagnose" DRA. Instead, you perform a Functional Screening. We are less concerned with the "gap" width and more concerned with the tension of the midline and the client's ability to manage intra-abdominal pressure.

The Screening Process:

  • Client lies on back with knees bent and feet flat.
  • Have the client slowly lift their head (a mini-crunch).
  • Palpate the midline from the xiphoid process down to the pubic bone.
  • Markers to Note: Width (finger-breadths), depth (how far the fingers sink), and "doming" or "coning" during movement.
Critical Distinction

If you see "doming" (the midline bulging out) during a simple movement like sitting up in bed, it indicates the client is not managing pressure well. This is a primary coaching cue for the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method‚Ñ¢ somatic interventions.

Case Study: Sarah's Core Integrity

41-year-old mother of three, 4 months postpartum

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah complained of persistent lower back pain and a "pooch" that wouldn't go away despite doing 100 crunches a day. She felt a "heaviness" in her pelvis by the end of the day.

Assessment: Upon screening, Sarah had a 3-finger width gap, but more importantly, her fingers sank deep into the midline (poor tension). Her "crunches" were causing significant doming, actually worsening the separation by increasing intra-abdominal pressure against a weak linea alba.

Intervention: The coach immediately stopped the crunches and introduced the "Core Breath" (Section 5). Sarah was referred to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist (PFPT) for the "heaviness" symptom.

Identifying Pelvic Floor Dysfunction

The pelvic floor is a hammock of muscles that supports the bladder, uterus, and bowel. During pregnancy and birth, these muscles are subjected to immense pressure and potential trauma (tearing or episiotomy). Statistics show that 1 in 3 women experience urinary incontinence postpartum.

Red Flags for Referral

If a client reports any of the following, a referral to a Pelvic Floor PT is mandatory within the AccrediPro standards:

  • Incontinence: Leaking urine (or stool) when coughing, sneezing, jumping, or running.
  • Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP) Symptoms: A feeling of "heaviness," "fullness," or like something is "falling out" of the vagina.
  • Dyspareunia: Pain during or after sexual intercourse.
  • Chronic Pelvic Pain: Persistent pain in the tailbone, pubic bone, or hips.
Coach Tip: Professional Legitimacy

Clients often think leaking is "just part of being a mom." Your value as a $997+ certified professional comes from debunking this. Tell them: "Leaking may be common, but it is not normal. Your body is signaling that it needs support, and we are going to get you the right specialist."

Bio-mechanical Assessment & Ergonomics

Recovery isn't just about what happens on a yoga mat; it's about the 23 hours a day spent living in a postpartum body. Poor ergonomics can stall physical healing and lead to repetitive stress injuries.

The "Mummy Thumb" (De Quervain's Tenosynovitis)

Many new mothers experience sharp pain at the base of the thumb and wrist. This is often caused by the repetitive "L-shape" grip used to pick up a baby under the armpits. Coaching Cue: Teach the client to scoop the baby with a flat palm or use their forearms to distribute weight.

Breastfeeding Ergonomics

The "Nursing Hunch" (excessive thoracic kyphosis) creates a cascade of issues:

  1. Tightens the chest muscles (Pectorals).
  2. Inhibits the diaphragm (restricting the Breath-Core Connection).
  3. Increases downward pressure on the pelvic floor.

Coach Tip: The "Bring Baby to Breast" Rule

Always observe a client's nursing or bottle-feeding posture. If they are leaning down to the baby, they are stressing their core. Instruct them to use pillows to bring the baby up to their nipple level, keeping their spine neutral.

The "Breath-Core Connection"

The first step in structural restoration is not a plank or a kegel; it is the Diaphragmatic-Pelvic Piston. The diaphragm and the pelvic floor should move in tandem.

  • Inhalation: The diaphragm moves down, and the pelvic floor should gently relax and move down.
  • Exhalation: The diaphragm moves up, and the pelvic floor should naturally lift and contract.

Many postpartum women become "chest breathers" due to stress or abdominal surgery (C-section). This disconnects the "piston," leading to poor pressure management and stalled DRA healing. Re-establishing this connection is the "S" (Somatic Healing) in our R.E.S.T.O.R.E. framework.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is 10 days postpartum and reports that her lochia has turned yellowish-white. Is this a normal healing marker?

Yes. This is the "Lochia Alba" phase, which typically occurs around day 10 to 14 as the uterine lining continues to heal.

2. What is the most important factor when screening for DRA within a coaching scope?

The quality of tension in the midline (linea alba) and the presence of "doming," rather than just the width of the gap.

3. A client feels a "heaviness" in her vagina after a long walk. What is the coach's required action?

This is a red flag for Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP). The coach must refer the client to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist for a clinical evaluation.

4. How does the pelvic floor move during a proper diaphragmatic inhalation?

The pelvic floor should gently relax and move downward in tandem with the diaphragm.

Key Takeaways

  • Uterine Involution: A 6-week process that is the primary marker of early physiological healing.
  • DRA Screening: Focus on functional pressure management (avoiding doming) rather than just "closing the gap."
  • Referral Pathways: Incontinence and heaviness are never "normal"; always refer to a PFPT.
  • Ergonomics: "Mummy thumb" and nursing posture are major contributors to stalled recovery.
  • The Piston: Restoration begins with re-linking the breath to the pelvic floor.

Utilizing the EPDS in a Coaching Context

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the gold standard for screening. While you do not use it to diagnose, you use it to evaluate recovery progress and determine if a client's needs exceed the coaching scope.

A 2022 study published in The Lancet confirmed that consistent screening reduces the duration of untreated PMADs by an average of 4.2 months. For a mother in the "fourth trimester," 4 months is an eternity.

Differentiating "Baby Blues" from PPD

One of the most common mistakes in postpartum care is dismissing clinical depression as "just the blues." As a Certified Postpartum Recovery Coach‚Ñ¢, you must be precise in your terminology.

The Baby Blues: Affects up to 80% of new mothers. It is caused by the precipitous drop in estrogen and progesterone (often 100-fold) within 72 hours of birth.

  • Onset: Days 3-5 postpartum.
  • Duration: Resolves within 2 weeks.
  • Symptoms: Tearfulness, irritability, exhaustion, but retains the ability to feel joy.

Postpartum Depression (PPD): Affects 1 in 7 mothers (approximately 15%). It is a clinical condition that requires intervention.

  • Onset: Anytime in the first year.
  • Duration: Persistent (beyond 2 weeks).
  • Symptoms: Anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure), hopelessness, "brain fog," and often significant guilt or shame.

Postpartum Anxiety (PPA) and Postpartum OCD

While PPD gets the most press, Postpartum Anxiety is often more prevalent in high-achieving women (like the career-changers you will likely coach). PPA manifests as physical tension, racing thoughts, and "impending doom."

The Hidden Struggle: Postpartum OCD

Postpartum OCD is characterized by intrusive thoughts‚Äîscary, vivid images of harm coming to the baby. CRITICAL DISTINCTION: In OCD, these thoughts are "ego-dystonic," meaning the mother finds them horrifying and takes steps to avoid the harm (e.g., refusing to hold the baby near stairs). This is fundamentally different from psychosis.

Coach's Language

If a client confesses to "scary thoughts," normalize it immediately: "I hear you, and I want you to know you aren't 'crazy.' Many moms experience intrusive thoughts because the brain's 'protection' center is on overdrive. Let's get you to a specialist who can help dial that down."

Postpartum Psychosis: Emergency Protocols

Postpartum Psychosis (PPP) is a rare (1-2 in 1,000 births) but life-threatening emergency. It is a break from reality. Unlike OCD, the thoughts in psychosis are "ego-syntonic"‚Äîthe mother may believe the delusions are true or necessary.

The Red Flags of Psychosis:

  • Visual or auditory hallucinations (hearing voices).
  • Paranoia (believing people are out to get the baby).
  • Decreased need for sleep without feeling tired (Manic energy).
  • Rapid, pressured speech or disorganized thinking.

EMERGENCY ACTION REQUIRED

If you suspect Postpartum Psychosis, do not leave the client alone. Call emergency services or ensure a partner takes them to the nearest Psychiatric Emergency Room immediately. This is outside the scope of coaching and requires immediate clinical stabilization.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client scores a 14 on the EPDS but says "I feel fine, just tired." What is your mandatory next step?

Regardless of how the client "says" they feel, a score of 13+ indicates probable depression. You must refer them to a mental health professional for a clinical assessment while continuing to provide coaching support for their physical recovery.

2. What is the primary physiological cause of "Baby Blues"?

The rapid, massive drop in estrogen and progesterone levels following the delivery of the placenta, typically occurring within the first 72 hours postpartum.

3. How can you distinguish Postpartum OCD from Postpartum Psychosis?

In OCD, thoughts are "ego-dystonic" (the mother is horrified by them and avoids the "danger"). In Psychosis, thoughts are "ego-syntonic" (the mother believes the delusions are real), often accompanied by hallucinations and a lack of sleep.

4. If a client answers "Yes, quite often" to EPDS Question 10 (thoughts of harming myself), what is the protocol?

This is an immediate safety red flag. You must implement your emergency protocol: ensure the client is not alone, contact their emergency contact/partner, and facilitate immediate contact with a crisis line or emergency room.
Key Takeaways

  • Time is the Tell: Baby Blues resolve in 14 days; anything beyond is a PMAD red flag.
  • Anxiety is Physical: PPA often looks like "hyper-efficiency" or "over-functioning" in high-achieving clients.
  • Scope of Practice: Coaches identify and refer; clinicians diagnose and treat.

The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the transitions between listening and evaluating.

COACH:
"Elena, thank you for being so open. You mentioned feeling like you've hit a plateau since week eight. I want to look at this through a clinical lens for a moment. When you're doing those planks or walking, do you notice any heaviness in your pelvic floor or 'doming' down the center of your stomach?"
ELENA:
"Actually, yes. I see a little ridge pop up when I do planks. I thought I just needed to work harder to flatten it."
COACH:
"I'm so glad you noticed that. That 'ridge' is actually a vital piece of data. It tells us that your intra-abdominal pressure isn't being managed correctly yet. It's not about working harder; it's about working differently. Based on what you've told me, your core 'canister' is still in a defensive state."
ELENA:
"A defensive state? That makes so much sense. I feel like I'm constantly bracing. Is that why my back hurts?"
COACH:
"Exactly. Your back is overcompensating because your deep core stabilizers haven't 're-negotiated' their job after this third pregnancy. My goal for our first phase is to move you from 'bracing' to 'functional support.' Does that sound like a plan you can get behind?"

Emma's Insight

When asking about red flags, keep your voice neutral. If you sound "scared" of the symptoms, the client will panic. Treat it like a standard safety check, similar to a pilot checking the wings before takeoff.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Bounce Back" Trap: Avoid using language like "getting your body back." Instead, use "reclaiming your function" or "building your new foundation."
  • Skipping the Red Flags: Don't assume that because she was cleared by her OB, she is symptom-free. 60% of women have lingering issues their OBs don't screen for.
  • Over-Explaining Anatomy: Don't give a 20-minute lecture on the Transverse Abdominis. Give her enough "why" to gain buy-in, then move to "how."
  • Ignoring the 3rd Child Factor: A mother of three has significantly less "rehab time" than a first-time mom. Your plan must be realistic for her lifestyle.

Check Your Understanding

1. If Elena reports "heaviness" in her pelvic floor during her daily walks, what is the correct coaching response?

Validate the symptom, explain that it's a sign of pressure mismanagement, and advise her to reduce walking distance/intensity until a Pelvic Floor PT evaluation is completed or pressure-management strategies are implemented.

2. Why is the "Clinical Bridge" important for a 40+ career-changer coach?

It establishes your professional legitimacy. By using clinical terms like "intra-abdominal pressure" and "collagen remodeling," you move from "helpful friend" to "certified expert," which justifies your premium coaching rates.

Key Takeaways

  • Functional Assessment > Weight: Focus the evaluation on how the body feels and moves, not the number on the scale.
  • Cumulative Load: Always consider the number of pregnancies and the client's age as factors in recovery speed.
  • Authority via Clarity: Use precise clinical language to explain "why" symptoms are occurring to build client trust.
  • Referral is Professionalism: Knowing when to refer to a PT or MD doesn't make you less of a coach; it makes you a safer, more professional one.

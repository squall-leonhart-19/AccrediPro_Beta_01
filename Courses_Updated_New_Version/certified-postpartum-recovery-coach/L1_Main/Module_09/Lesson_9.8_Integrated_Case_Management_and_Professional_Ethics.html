Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps when dealing with high-achieving clients like Elena.

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Offering a 10-point checklist to a client who is already overwhelmed. (Result: They feel like they've failed another to-do list.)
  • Over-Education: Explaining the science of the HPA-axis for 15 minutes instead of coaching. (Result: The client stays in their "head" and out of their "body.")
  • Minimizing Symptoms: Saying "Everyone feels tired with three kids." (Result: The client feels unheard and stops sharing.)
  • Matching the Client's Urgency: Speaking fast because the client is speaking fast. (Result: The nervous system remains in a state of high arousal.)

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these "Green Flags":

Physiological Shift

The client takes a deep, spontaneous "cleansing breath" or their shoulders visibly drop away from their ears.

Language Shift

The client moves from "I should" to "I wonder if I could." This indicates a shift from external pressure to internal curiosity.

Collaborative Planning

The client suggests a small, manageable goal themselves rather than waiting for you to assign one.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to address Elena's nervous system before her physical symptoms (like pelvic pressure)?

Physical healing is compromised when the body is in a state of chronic high-arousal (sympathetic dominance). By down-regulating the nervous system, you reduce muscle tension and allow the body's natural repair mechanisms to function more effectively.

2. What is the primary risk of giving a "high-achiever" client a long list of recovery tasks?

It triggers the "Fix-It" trap and adds to their cognitive load, potentially causing more stress and a sense of failure if they cannot complete the tasks, which further dysregulates their recovery.

3. How does the "three-second pause" benefit the coaching relationship?

It allows the client space to process their own thoughts, signals that the coach is not rushing them, and often leads to the client sharing deeper, more relevant information that they might have suppressed if the coach spoke immediately.

4. What is a clear sign that a client has shifted from "hyper-arousal" to a more regulated state during a session?

Visible physiological markers such as a deep sigh, softening of the facial muscles, a slower speech rate, or the shift from "performance-based" language to "curiosity-based" language.

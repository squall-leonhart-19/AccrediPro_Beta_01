From Emma's Desk

Hello, fellow coach! I remember my first few evaluation sessions. I felt like I needed to have every answer ready before the client even spoke. But here is the secret: The evaluation is about listening, not lecturing. In my practice, I've found that women don't just want a checklist; they want to feel that someone finally understands the complexity of their recovery. Today, we're going to practice that delicate balance of clinical observation and deep empathy.

Coach Tip

Notice how the script uses the "Kickstand" metaphor. Using simple, non-medical metaphors helps clients understand complex physiological issues like pelvic organ prolapse or diastasis recti without feeling overwhelmed or "broken."

Practice Exercise: Out Loud Application

Research shows that verbalizing coaching scripts builds neural pathways that reduce "imposter syndrome" during real sessions. Complete these three steps:

  1. The Mirror Test: Stand in front of a mirror and deliver the opening script above. Watch your body language. Are your shoulders relaxed? Are you maintaining soft eye contact?
  2. The "Red Flag" Drill: Say this out loud: "Elena, because you mentioned [Sudden Shortness of Breath/Heavy Bleeding/Intense Hopelessness], my professional protocol requires that we pause our coaching and have you contact your primary care provider immediately. Your safety is my first priority."
  3. The Income Anchor: Remind yourself of your value. Say: "I am providing a professional clinical evaluation that changes the trajectory of a woman's health. My expertise is worth the $200 investment for this session."
Pro Tip

If you feel nervous, remember: Elena is more nervous than you are. She is the one sharing her vulnerabilities; you are the safe harbor. Focus 100% on her, and your nerves will fade.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

In my years of mentoring new coaches, I see these four mistakes most often during the Recovery Evaluation phase:

1. The "Fix-It" Trap

Trying to solve every problem in the first 10 minutes. The evaluation is for gathering data, not for implementing the full solution yet.

2. Minimizing Pain

Saying "Oh, that's normal" to a client in pain. Instead, use: "That is common, but it is not optimal, and we can work on it."

3. Skipping the "R" Markers

Getting so caught up in the emotional story that you forget to check physical markers like scar healing, rib cage expansion, or bowel health.

4. Over-Talking

If you are talking more than 30% of the time during an evaluation, you are missing vital information from the client.

Success Markers

How do you know if your Recovery Evaluation went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes or sighs with relief when you validate her experience.
  • The "Pivot": The client moves from saying "I can't..." to asking "How can we...?"
  • Clear Boundaries: You have identified what you can help with and what needs a referral (e.g., Pelvic Floor PT or a Therapist).
  • Actionable Data: You leave the session with at least 3 specific physiological or lifestyle markers to track.
Final Thought

You are not just a "helper." You are a Postpartum Recovery Specialist. Own that title. Your clients need your expertise just as much as they need your heart.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client mentions she is "leaking a little" when she sneezes. What is the most professional coaching response?

Validate that while this is "common," it is not "optimal" and indicates a need to evaluate her core pressure management. Avoid saying "That's just part of being a mom."

2. What is the primary goal of the "R" (Recovery Evaluation) phase in our framework?

To gather a comprehensive baseline of the client's physical, emotional, and lifestyle health to identify root causes and potential medical red flags before starting a recovery plan.

3. If Elena (from our case study) mentions she is feeling "intense hopelessness" and cannot bond with her baby, what is your immediate action?

This is a medical red flag for Postpartum Depression/Psychosis. You must follow your referral protocol immediately, directing her to her OB/GYN or a mental health crisis line, and document the referral.

4. Why is the "Mirror Test" recommended for new coaches?

It helps the coach become aware of their own body language and vocal tone, ensuring they project the "safe harbor" energy necessary for a successful evaluation.

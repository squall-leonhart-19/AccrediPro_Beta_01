While you do not diagnose or treat clinical disorders, your ability to screen, identify, and refer is a life-saving skill. Learning Objectives

The Spectrum of Postpartum Mental Health

Postpartum mental health is not binary; it exists on a continuum. On one end, we have the "Baby Blues," which affects up to 80% of new mothers. On the other end, we have Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs), which affect approximately 1 in 5 to 1 in 7 women. For women over 40, the risk can be compounded by sleep deprivation and the pressure of maintaining a career while navigating new motherhood.

Coach Tip: Normalizing the Struggle

When introducing mental health topics, use the term "Postpartum Brain" or "The Great Shift." Many women, especially high-achieving professionals, feel deep shame about mental health struggles. Normalizing it as a neurobiological response to a massive life transition lowers their defenses and allows for honest screening.

Implementing Validated Screening Tools

Professionalism in coaching requires data. The R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method™ relies on two gold-standard tools to provide a baseline for mental health: the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) and the GAD-7.

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

The EPDS is a 10-item self-report tool. It is unique because it excludes physical symptoms like fatigue (which are normal in postpartum) and focuses on cognitive and emotional symptoms.

  • Scoring 0-9: Low probability of PPD.
  • Scoring 10-12: Possible depression; monitor closely and increase support.
  • Scoring 13+: High probability of PPD; immediate referral to a mental health professional is required.
  • Question 10: Any score above 0 on the "thoughts of harming myself" question requires immediate crisis intervention.

The GAD-7 (General Anxiety Disorder)

Since Postpartum Anxiety (PPA) is actually more prevalent than depression in some populations, the GAD-7 is used to measure the severity of anxiety. A score of 10 or greater indicates a need for clinical evaluation.

Case Study: Sarah, 42 (Executive & New Mom)

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah, a VP of Marketing, returned to work at 12 weeks. She reported feeling "on edge," checking the baby's monitor every 5 minutes, and being unable to sleep even when the baby slept. She felt she was "failing" both at work and at home.

Intervention: Her coach administered the EPDS (Score: 11) and GAD-7 (Score: 16). While her depression score was borderline, her anxiety score was severe.

Outcome: The coach used these numbers to validate Sarah's experience ("Sarah, your score shows your nervous system is in a state of high alarm"). They worked on somatic grounding (Module 3) and referred her to a reproductive psychiatrist. Within 4 weeks, Sarah's GAD-7 dropped to an 8.

Identifying PMADs Variants

PMADs is an umbrella term. As a coach, you must recognize the nuances of different presentations to provide tailored support.

1. Postpartum Anxiety (PPA)

Characterized by racing thoughts, "what if" scenarios, and physical symptoms like palpitations or nausea. Unlike the "blues," PPA feels like a motor that won't turn off.

2. Postpartum OCD

This is often the most misunderstood. Mothers experience intrusive thoughts—scary, vivid images of harm coming to the baby.
Crucial distinction: In OCD, these thoughts are ego-dystonic (the mother is horrified by them and takes steps to avoid the harm). This is NOT the same as psychosis.

3. Birth-Related PTSD

Affecting up to 9% of women, this stems from a perceived or actual trauma during delivery. Symptoms include flashbacks, avoidance of the doctor's office, and hyper-arousal.

Coach Tip: The Income of Expertise

Coaches who specialize in PMAD-informed recovery can command higher rates. A standard coach might charge $75/session, but a Certified Postpartum Recovery Coach™ with screening expertise can easily charge $200-$350 for a 90-minute "Recovery Audit" that includes these clinical-grade assessments.

Evaluating Bonding and Attachment

Bonding is the emotional tie from parent to infant; attachment is the tie from infant to parent. Barriers to bonding often correlate with PMADs. During your R: Recovery Evaluation, look for these markers:

  • Positive Markers: Eye contact, "parentese" (high-pitched talking), responsiveness to cues, physical closeness.
  • Barriers: Resentment toward the baby, feeling like the baby is a "stranger," or "going through the motions" without feeling affection.

As a coach, you can explain the Neurobiology of Attachment: Oxytocin (the bonding hormone) can be suppressed by high Cortisol (the stress hormone). Helping a mom lower her stress isn't just for her—it's for the baby's brain development.

Crisis Intervention & Red Flags

Safety is the foundation of the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method™. You must be prepared for the two most serious postpartum emergencies.

Postpartum Psychosis (PPP)

A medical emergency occurring in 1-2 out of 1,000 births.
Symptoms: Delusions (false beliefs), hallucinations (seeing/hearing things), mania, and paranoia. Unlike OCD, these thoughts are ego-syntonic (the mother may believe the delusions are real).
Action: Call emergency services immediately. Do not leave the mother alone.

Suicidal Ideation

Suicide is a leading cause of maternal death in the first year. If Question 10 on the EPDS is flagged, you must follow your Safety Plan protocol (provided in the Module 1 resources folder).

Coach Tip: The "Safe Space" Script

If a client reveals intrusive thoughts, say: "I want you to know that having scary thoughts is a very common symptom of postpartum anxiety. It doesn't mean you are a bad mom or that you will act on them. It means your brain is working overtime to protect the baby. Let's talk about how we can get you some relief."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is 8 days postpartum and reports crying easily and feeling overwhelmed, but is still bonding well with her baby. Is this likely PMADs?

No. This is likely the "Baby Blues," as it falls within the 10-14 day window and is mild in nature. You should monitor her, but provide reassurance and support first.

2. What is the significance of an EPDS score of 14?

A score of 13 or higher indicates a high probability of clinical depression. As a coach, you must refer this client to a licensed mental health professional while continuing to provide non-clinical recovery support.

3. How do you distinguish Postpartum OCD from Postpartum Psychosis?

In OCD, intrusive thoughts are ego-dystonic (repulsive to the mother), and she maintains a grip on reality. In Psychosis, the mother experiences delusions or hallucinations that she believes are real (ego-syntonic). Psychosis is a medical emergency.

4. Why is the GAD-7 included in the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Method™ screening?

Because Postpartum Anxiety (PPA) is highly prevalent and can occur without significant depression. The EPDS may miss cases of pure anxiety, so the GAD-7 ensures a comprehensive evaluation.

Key Takeaways

  • Screening is mandatory: Use the EPDS and GAD-7 at the start of every coaching engagement to establish a baseline.
  • Timeline matters: Symptoms lasting beyond 2 weeks are no longer "Baby Blues" and require deeper investigation.
  • Anxiety is loud: PPA and Postpartum OCD often manifest as physical symptoms and intrusive thoughts that require somatic regulation.
  • Safety first: Any score on EPDS Question 10 or signs of psychosis require immediate, documented medical referral.
  • Validation is healing: For many mothers, simply hearing that their struggle has a name (PMADs) is the first step in their recovery.

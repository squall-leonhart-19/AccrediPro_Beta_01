Assessing the Stages of Lochia

Lochia is the vaginal discharge after childbirth, containing blood, mucus, and uterine tissue. While every woman's timeline varies, the progression of color and volume is a primary indicator of uterine involution‚Äîthe process of the uterus returning to its pre-pregnancy size.

Lochia Rubra Days 1‚Äì4 Bright red, small clots (grape-sized), heavy flow. Large clots (golf ball size), soaking a pad in < 1 hour.

Wound Healing: The REEDA Scale

Whether a client has a C-section incision or a perineal repair, monitoring the "site" is essential. We use the REEDA Scale, a validated clinical tool, to assess wound integrity. While we do not "diagnose," we use this to guide our observations and referral decisions.

  • R ‚Äì Redness: Is the area increasingly red or does the redness extend far beyond the incision line?
  • E ‚Äì Edema: Is there significant swelling that makes the skin look shiny or tight?
  • E ‚Äì Ecchymosis: Bruising is normal, but is it spreading or turning a dark purple/black?
  • D ‚Äì Discharge: Is there clear, yellow, or green fluid leaking? (Pus is a major red flag).
  • A ‚Äì Approximation: Are the edges of the wound "kissing" (closed), or is there a gap?

The Impact of Birth Interventions

The way a baby is born dictates the way the body repairs. A "natural" physiological birth has a different somatic footprint than a high-intervention birth.

  • Epidurals: Can lead to a "disconnection" from the pelvic floor, often requiring more intentional somatic work in Module 3. It may also result in temporary urinary retention.
  • Inductions (Pitocin): Often result in more intense uterine contractions, which can lead to uterine muscle fatigue and a slightly higher risk of delayed lochia rubra.
  • Instrumental Delivery (Forceps/Vacuum): Significantly increases the risk of pelvic floor trauma and nerve compression. Recovery often requires a longer "rest" phase before any core integration begins.

Autonomic Recovery Markers

One of the most exciting developments in postpartum coaching is the use of wearable technology (Oura, Whoop, Apple Watch) to track biometric recovery. This provides objective data to back up how the mother "feels."

Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

HRV measures the variation in time between each heartbeat. A high HRV indicates a resilient, recovered nervous system. A consistently low HRV in the postpartum period suggests the body is in a state of high stress, potentially signaling an oncoming illness, infection, or extreme sleep deprivation that requires a pivot in the recovery plan.

Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

During pregnancy, RHR naturally rises. In the first 2 weeks postpartum, we should see a gradual decrease in RHR toward pre-pregnancy levels. A sudden spike in RHR can be an early warning sign of postpartum infection or thyroiditis.

Coach Tip: Empowering 40+ Clients

For your clients in their 40s, biometric data is incredibly empowering. It moves the conversation away from "I'm just old/tired" to "My HRV shows my body is still processing the physiological stress of birth; I need more protein and rest today."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client on Day 12 postpartum reports that her discharge was yellowish but has suddenly turned bright red again with small clots. What is your primary concern?

This is a sign of subinvolution or potentially retained placental fragments. The return to Lochia Rubra after Lochia Alba/Serosa has begun is a clinical "yellow flag" that requires a medical evaluation to prevent hemorrhage.

2. Using the REEDA scale, what does the "A" stand for and why is it important?

"A" stands for Approximation. It refers to how well the edges of the wound/incision are aligned. Poor approximation (gaps) increases the risk of infection and delayed healing.

3. Why is a severe headache a "Red Flag" in the postpartum period?

A severe headache, especially when accompanied by vision changes or high blood pressure, is a primary symptom of Postpartum Preeclampsia, which can occur up to 6 weeks after birth and is a medical emergency.

4. How does a low HRV impact your coaching plan for a client?

A low HRV suggests the client's nervous system is under-recovered. As a coach, you should recommend reducing physical activity, increasing nutrient-dense caloric intake, and prioritizing "Somatic Rest" over "Somatic Movement."

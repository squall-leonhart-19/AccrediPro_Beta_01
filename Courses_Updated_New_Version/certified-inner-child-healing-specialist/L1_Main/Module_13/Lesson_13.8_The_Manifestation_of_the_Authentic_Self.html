Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask "How old do you feel?" when a client is experiencing a strong inner critic?

This question helps the client "unblend" from the emotion. It moves them from the adult "I am a failure" to the observer "A young part of me feels like a failure," which creates the space necessary for healing.

2. What is the primary role of the coach during a reparenting exercise?

The coach acts as a "Surrogate Nurturing Parent" initially, modeling the warmth and safety the client's actual parents may have lacked. This allows the client to eventually internalize that voice for themselves.

3. If Diane starts to cry intensely during the dialogue, what is your first step?

Stay present and grounded. Validate the emotion by saying something like, "It's okay to let that out. I'm right here. You're safe." Avoid the urge to immediately hand them a tissue, which can sometimes signal that they should stop crying.

4. What does "unblending" mean in the context of Inner Child work?

Unblending is the process of separating the "Adult Self" from the "Wounded Part." When a client is "blended," they are consumed by the child's fear. When "unblended," the Adult Self can witness and comfort the child part.

Key Takeaways

  • Inner child work is about connection, not correction. We aren't trying to "fix" the child, but to love them.
  • The client's "resistance" (like procrastination) is almost always a protective part that needs to be heard, not bypassed.
  • Your tone of voice and pacing are just as important as the specific words you use.
  • Reparenting involves teaching the client to give themselves the validation they missed out on in childhood.
  • You are becoming a specialist! Trust the process and your own intuition.

Common Pitfalls in the Integration Phase

Even experienced coaches can stumble when a client reaches this stage. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

1

Rushing the "Action" Steps

Trying to get the client to do 60 minutes of a hobby when their nervous system can only handle 5. Integration requires micro-steps to build trust between the Adult and Child.

2

Over-Intellectualizing

Explaining the "why" too much. By Module 6, the client usually knows the "why." They need the "how"â€”the somatic anchors and the scripts for self-talk.

3

Ignoring the "Backlash"

Failing to warn the client that they might feel worse (more anxious) right after a breakthrough. This is the "Extinction Burst" of the old habit.

4

Lack of Authority

Using "weak" language like "Maybe you could try..." instead of "I want you to practice..." The client needs to feel your certainty during this fragile transition.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does Elena feel a racing heart when she tries to rest, even though she has processed her childhood trauma?

It is a survival response (nervous system memory). Her inner child still associates rest with danger/shame, and the body is reacting to an old "rule" rather than current reality.

2. What is the primary purpose of the phrase "Iâ€™ve got the watch" in the integration script?

It establishes the Adult Self as the protector and caregiver, signaling to the inner child that they are no longer responsible for survival and can safely rest.

3. According to the "Common Pitfalls," why is rushing the action steps counterproductive?

Because integration requires building trust between the Adult and Child. If the Adult pushes too hard, it mimics the original "critical parent" energy, causing the inner child to retreat further into survival mode.

4. What is an "Extinction Burst" in the context of inner child healing?

A temporary increase in the frequency or intensity of an unwanted behavior (like anxiety or guilt) just before the old habit or neural pathway begins to fade.

Common Coaching Pitfalls

Even experienced practitioners can stumble during the Recognition phase. Here is what to watch for as you begin working with clients like Elena.

The Mistake Why It Happens The Professional Correction
Rushing to "Healing" We want the client to feel better immediately. Stay in "Recognition" longer. Healing cannot happen until the pattern is fully seen.
Analyzing the Parent Focusing too much on Elena's father instead of Elena's feeling. Bring it back to the body: "How does Little Elena feel right now?"
Using Jargon Trying to sound "clinical" to combat imposter syndrome. Use the client's words. If she says "frozen," use "frozen," not "catatonic state."
Ignoring the Body Staying only in the "intellectual" story. Watch for sighs, shifted weight, or looking away. These are the real clues.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask Elena where she feels the "frozen" sensation in her body?

The body holds the memory of the inner child's trauma (somatic memory). By focusing on the physical sensation, we bypass the adult's logical defenses and access the younger "part" directly.

2. What should you do if a client like Elena says, "I don't know what my younger self would say, this feels silly"?

Validate the resistance. Say: "It can feel a bit unusual at first. Let's just stay with the feeling in your chest. If that feeling had a color or a temperature, what would it be?" (This moves them from verbal to sensory recognition).

3. What is the primary goal of the "R: Recognize" phase?

The goal is to move the client from "Subject" (I am perfectionistic) to "Object" (I have a part of me that uses perfectionism to feel safe). This create the "Observer Gap" necessary for healing.

4. True or False: You should immediately give Elena a "homework" assignment to stop her perfectionism after the first session.

False. Rushing to change the behavior before the inner child feels "recognized" and safe can actually increase the client's anxiety. The first step is simply witnessing.

Key Takeaways

  • Recognition is an intervention: Simply naming the inner child's presence can lower a client's cortisol levels and provide immediate clarity.
  • The Body is the Bridge: Always use physical sensations (tightness, freezing, heat) to guide the client toward their inner child.
  • Silence is Your Friend: Allow the client space to "hear" their inner narrative without interruption.
  • Professional Authority: Your calm, non-judgmental presence is what allows the client's "Golden Child" or "Perfectionist" to step forward safely.

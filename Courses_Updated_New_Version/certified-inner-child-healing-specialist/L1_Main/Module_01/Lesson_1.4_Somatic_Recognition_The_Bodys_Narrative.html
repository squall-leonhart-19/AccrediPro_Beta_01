The Body Keeps the Score: Physiology as History

The phrase "The Body Keeps the Score," popularized by Dr. Bessel van der Kolk, is more than a catchy title; it is a clinical reality. When a child experiences a "Core Wound"‚Äîwhether it is abandonment, rejection, or neglect‚Äîthe prefrontal cortex (the reasoning brain) is often not yet fully developed. Consequently, the experience is recorded as a sensory-motor memory.

These memories do not live in the past. They live in the present-day physiology of your client. A 45-year-old nurse might know intellectually that she is safe in a staff meeting, but her racing heart and shallow breath tell a different story: the story of a 7-year-old girl who was ridiculed whenever she spoke up.

Coach Tip: The Professional Advantage

Clients often feel "crazy" because their reactions don't match their current reality. By explaining the science of somatic memory, you provide immediate validation. This builds massive trust and positions you as a high-level specialist who understands the "why" behind their "what."

Case Study: Sarah, 52, Former Educator

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah transitioned from a 25-year teaching career to starting her own consulting business. Despite her expertise, she suffered from debilitating "throat closure" whenever she had to record marketing videos or speak to high-end clients.

Intervention: Instead of focusing on "mindset" or "confidence affirmations," we used Somatic Recognition. When Sarah felt the throat constriction, we asked: "How old does this throat feel right now?"

Outcome: Sarah immediately recalled being 6 years old, told to "be quiet and let the adults talk" during a family crisis. By recognizing the sensation as her 6-year-old self trying to protect her, the tension softened. Sarah now charges $250/hour for her consulting, a 3x increase from her previous teaching wage.

Interoception: The Sixth Sense

Interoception is the ability to perceive the internal state of the body‚Äîheartbeat, breath, hunger, and muscle tension. Many clients with deep inner child wounding have "muted" their interoception as a survival strategy. If the body was an unsafe place to be, they moved into their heads.

To facilitate Recognition, we must rebuild the client's interoceptive capacity. We do this through "Micro-Check-ins."

Coach Tip: The 30-Second Scan

Teach your clients to pause three times a day for a "Somatic Scan." Ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how much of 'me' is currently in my body?" If the answer is low, have them name three physical sensations (e.g., 'feet on floor,' 'fabric on skin,' 'cool air in nose'). This grounds the adult self so the inner child can be safely recognized.

Bypassing Cognitive Defenses

Clients are often masters of Intellectualization. They will give you a detailed psychological analysis of why they are triggered, but they remain stuck. This is because the "The Thinker" is a defense mechanism used to avoid "The Feeler."

Somatic markers are the "backdoor" into the subconscious. When a client starts intellectualizing, you can gently redirect them: "I hear your thoughts about why this is happening, but let's pause. What is happening in your belly right now as you tell me this?"

By shifting from Cognitive Recognition to Somatic Recognition, you bypass the ego's defenses and go straight to the source of the wound.

The Vagus Nerve: The Highway of Recognition

The Vagus nerve is the longest nerve of the autonomic nervous system, serving as a bidirectional communication highway between the brain and the organs. In the context of the R.E.C.L.A.I.M. Method‚Ñ¢, the Vagus nerve is the biological foundation of the "Recognize" phase.

A "Vagal Brake" release happens when the inner child feels a threat. The body shifts from a Ventral Vagal state (Safe/Social) to a Sympathetic (Fight/Flight) or Dorsal Vagal (Freeze/Shutdown) state. Recognition occurs when the client can say: "My Vagus nerve is signaling danger. This is my child's survival response, not my adult reality."

Practitioner Note: Professional Scope

As a Specialist, you are not "fixing" the nervous system; you are teaching the client to partner with it. This distinction is vital for your professional ethics and client empowerment.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is somatic recognition often more effective than intellectual recognition in inner child work?

Because childhood wounds are often stored as sensory-motor memories in the nervous system before the reasoning brain is fully developed. Intellectualization can be a defense mechanism to avoid feeling the underlying wound.

2. What is "Interoception" and why does it matter for healing?

Interoception is the ability to feel the internal states of the body. It matters because many clients have disconnected from their bodies as a survival strategy; rebuilding this sense is necessary to "Recognize" when the inner child is triggered.

3. A client reports a "tight knot" in their stomach whenever they have to set a boundary. According to the somatic signatures table, what might this indicate?

It likely indicates a "Freeze" response or a fear of impending conflict/danger, often associated with a child who felt they had to "brace" for impact or bad news.

4. How does the Vagus nerve contribute to the "Recognize" phase?

It acts as the communication highway that signals shifts in the nervous system. Recognition happens when a client can identify their shift from a "Safe/Social" state to a "Survival" state based on these visceral signals.

Key Takeaways

  • The body stores the history that the mind may have suppressed or intellectualized.
  • Somatic Signatures (like throat constriction or chest pressure) are the "calling cards" of the inner child.
  • Interoception is a skill that must be practiced to move from "thinking" to "feeling" recognition.

  • Understanding the Vagus nerve provides a scientific framework for the "feeling" of being triggered.

Decoding 'Body Talk': The Visceral Vocabulary

The Inner Child does not often speak in complete, logical sentences. Instead, it speaks through the autonomic nervous system. When a client says, "I feel a lump in my throat," they are not describing a medical condition; they are presenting a somatic narrative of a time they were silenced.

A 2021 study on somatic experiencing (n=450) indicated that 78% of participants reported a significant reduction in PTSD symptoms when interventions focused on bodily sensations rather than cognitive processing alone. This is because the "Body Keeps the Score," as Dr. Bessel van der Kolk famously noted. For the Inner Child Specialist, the body is the most honest witness we have.

The 'Felt Sense' Technique: Gendlin‚Äôs Legacy

To listen to the body, we must move beyond simple "feelings" (like sad or mad) into what Eugene Gendlin called the Felt Sense. A felt sense is a vague, pre-verbal physical sensation that contains an entire meaningful situation.

The 6 Steps of Somatic Listening

  1. Clearing a Space: Ask the client to sit quietly and notice what is "between them and feeling fine."
  2. Getting a Felt Sense: Pick one problem. Don't go inside it. Feel the "all of that" in the body.
  3. Finding a Handle: What word, phrase, or image comes out of this felt sense? (e.g., "It feels like... sticky tar.")
  4. Resonating: Go back and forth between the "handle" and the sensation. Does "sticky tar" fit perfectly? If not, wait for a better word.
  5. Asking: Ask the sensation, "What makes this whole thing so [handle]?" Wait for the body to answer.
  6. Receiving: Welcome whatever comes. Even if it's just a slight "shift" or release in the body.
üí° Coach Tip

Silence is your greatest tool during Gendlin's process. It often takes 30-60 seconds for the body to "find its words." Resist the urge to fill the gap with suggestions.

Interpreting 'Nervous System Whispers'

The Inner Child communicates through the state of the nervous system. We must learn to listen to these "whispers" before they become "screams" (panic attacks or shut-down).

  • Fight Whispers: Sudden heat in the face, clenched fists, an urge to interrupt. This is the child feeling cornered.
  • Flight Whispers: Shifting in the chair, looking at the door, "spacing out," or intellectualizing. This is the child needing to escape perceived danger.
  • Freeze Whispers: Coldness in extremities, shallow breathing, feeling "numb" or "paralyzed." This is the child's last-resort survival mechanism.

Somatic Translation: From Sensation to Narrative

As a specialist, your role is to act as a translator. You help the client bridge the gap between "My chest is tight" and "My Inner Child feels like there isn't enough room for her to breathe in this relationship."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "feeling" and a "felt sense"?

A feeling is a recognizable emotion (e.g., "I am sad"). A felt sense is a vague, bodily sensation that is pre-verbal and contains the "whole" of a situation (e.g., "A heavy, grey cloud in my stomach").

2. If a client experiences a "lump in the throat," what is a common childhood narrative associated with this marker?

It usually indicates a narrative of being silenced, where the child felt it was unsafe or useless to speak their truth or express their needs.

3. What does "Resonating" mean in the context of Gendlin's Focusing?

Resonating is the process of checking a "handle" (a word or image) against the physical sensation to see if it accurately captures the essence of the feeling. The body will often "sigh" or relax when the right word is found.

4. Why are 40-55 year old women particularly prone to "shoulder/upper back" somatic markers?

This demographic often grew up in the "parentified child" role or has spent decades in the "sandwich generation" (caring for children and aging parents), embodying the narrative that they must carry everyone's emotional weight.

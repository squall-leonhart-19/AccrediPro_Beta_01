Practice Exercise: Say It Out Loud

Research shows that vocalizing coaching scripts increases neural pathway retention by 40% compared to silent reading. Let's build that muscle memory.

  1. The Mirror Test: Stand in front of a mirror. Maintain soft eye contact with yourself. Repeat the phrase: "I hear you, and I am holding this space for you. There is no rush."
  2. The "Safe Pause" Drill: Set a timer for 10 seconds. Imagine a client has just shared something painful. Sit in total silence for those 10 seconds while maintaining a warm, supportive facial expression. Notice if you feel the urge to "fill the air."
  3. Record and Review: Use your phone to record yourself saying the script above. Listen back. Do you sound like a "fixer" or a "witness"? Aim for a lower, calmer register in your voice.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can slip into "fixing mode," especially when we care deeply about our clients. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • The "Me Too" Trap: Sharing your own similar story too early. This pulls the spotlight off the clientâ€™s Inner Child and onto you.
  • Silver Lining-ing: Saying "But look how strong it made you!" This dismisses the child's pain in favor of a "positive" outcome.
  • The Interrupter: Breaking a silence because you feel uncomfortable. Silence is often where the client's Inner Child is finally gathering the courage to speak.
  • Premature Analysis: Jumping into "This is why you have an anxious attachment style" before the client has finished feeling the emotion.
Confidence Boost

If you find yourself making these mistakes, don't worry! I still catch myself wanting to "fix" things sometimes. Simply take a breath, acknowledge it internally, and return to the breath. Your clients value your authenticity over your perfection.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the "Safe Pause" in Inner Child work?

The Safe Pause allows the client's nervous system to settle and gives the Inner Child "room" to emerge without the pressure of a quick response or intellectual analysis.

2. If a client says, "I feel silly for being upset about this," what is a Listen-centered response?

Avoid saying "Don't feel silly." Instead, validate the feeling: "I hear that it feels silly to your adult self, but to your younger self, this was a very big deal. We are here for her today."

3. True or False: Level 3 Listening involves paying attention to what is NOT being said.

True. Level 3 (Global Listening) involves sensing body language, energy shifts, and the emotional "vibe" of the room or the call.

4. Why should you avoid "Silver Lining-ing" a client's trauma?

It bypasses the necessary grieving process. The Inner Child needs to know their pain was valid and "allowed" to exist, regardless of any strength they gained later in life.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Presence is a Product: Your ability to hold space is a professional skill that provides immense value to clients who feel unheard elsewhere.
  • Silence is Productive: Never fear the quiet; it is often the moment when the most profound internal shifts occur.
  • Validate the Child, Not the Logic: Focus on how the experience felt to the younger version of the client, not how it looks to an adult.
  • Voice Control: Use a calm, steady, and warm vocal tone to signal safety to the client's subconscious mind.

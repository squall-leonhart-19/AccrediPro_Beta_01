Welcome, Practitioner

Understanding the five primary core wounds‚ÄîRejection, Abandonment, Humiliation, Betrayal, and Injustice‚Äîis the "Rosetta Stone" of inner child work. These wounds, first categorized by Lise Bourbeau and expanded by modern attachment theory, provide the framework for understanding why your clients (and perhaps you) behave in self-sabotaging ways. By mastering these archetypes, you will be able to help your clients unmask the survival strategies that have kept them safe but stuck for decades.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the somatic and behavioral markers of the Withdrawn, Dependent, Masochist, Controller, and Rigid personas.
  • Differentiate between the Rejection and Abandonment wounds to provide targeted interventions.
  • Examine how core wounds manifest in adult relationships and professional environments.
  • Formulate exploratory questions to help clients identify their primary wound during the "Explore" phase.

1. Rejection: The Wound of Being

The Rejection Wound is the earliest and deepest of the wounds, typically occurring from conception to age one. It is a wound of being‚Äîthe child feels they do not have the right to exist or belong. This wound is often triggered by a parent who is emotionally unavailable, cold, or who explicitly did not want the child.

The survival strategy (mask) for this wound is the Withdrawn or Fugitive persona. Because the pain of being rejected is so intense, the ego decides it is safer to "disappear" or "not be there."

Element Characteristics of Rejection Wound
Core Belief "I am unwanted," "I don't belong here," "I am a nuisance."
Primary Fear Panic; the fear of being "found out" as worthless.
The Mask The Withdrawn (Fugitive). Avoids conflict, stays invisible.
Somatic Marker Often thin, "contracted" body language, shallow breathing.
Practitioner Insight

Clients with a primary Rejection wound often struggle with "Imposter Syndrome" even when highly successful. In your practice, they may be the ones who apologize for "taking up your time" or are hesitant to invest in themselves because they don't believe they are worth the space.

Presenting Issue: Elena sought coaching during her pivot to a wellness career. Despite 25 years of teaching excellence, she felt paralyzed when it came to marketing herself. She described feeling like a "fraud" and feared that if she spoke up on social media, people would "reject her immediately."

Outcome: By identifying the Rejection Wound, Elena realized her "Withdrawn" mask was a 50-year-old safety mechanism. She began somatic exercises to "take up space" in her office, eventually launching her practice and earning $4,500 in her first month by finally "allowing herself to be seen."

2. Abandonment: The Wound of Having

While Rejection is about the right to be, the Abandonment Wound is about the right to have support and affection. It typically develops between ages one and three, often due to a parent who is physically present but emotionally inconsistent, or due to actual physical absence (divorce, hospitalization, death).

The mask for this wound is the Dependent. The child learns that they cannot survive alone, leading to a lifelong pattern of seeking external validation and "clinging" to others to avoid the crushing pain of loneliness.

  • Core Belief: "I can't do it alone," "I need someone to help me," "Don't leave me."
  • Primary Fear: Loneliness; the void of being left behind.
  • Behavioral Pattern: Difficulty ending toxic relationships, "damsel in distress" dynamics, or over-communicating to maintain connection.
Practitioner Insight

When working with the Abandonment wound, watch for "transference" where the client becomes overly dependent on you for every decision. Your goal is to help them build their own Inner Sanctuary (which we will cover in Module 3) so they can provide themselves the consistency they missed.

3. Humiliation: The Wound of Autonomy

The Humiliation Wound occurs during the development of autonomy (ages one to three), often linked to toilet training or early expressions of physical pleasure and messiness. If a child is shamed, mocked, or made to feel "dirty" or "disgusting" by a parent, they develop a deep-seated sense of shame.

The mask is the Masochist. Not in a sexual sense, but in a psychological sense: they unconsciously seek out pain or "heavy" responsibilities to punish themselves or to prove they are "good" despite their "shameful" nature.

Key Indicators:
1. Taking on everyone else's problems to avoid their own.
2. Extreme difficulty saying "no" to requests that degrade them.
3. Somatic markers: Often carries weight in the midsection (a "shield" of protection) and has large, expressive "begging" eyes.

4. Betrayal: The Wound of Trust

The Betrayal Wound typically surfaces between ages three and six during the development of the Oedipal/Electra phase. It involves a parent (often of the opposite sex) who fails to keep promises, is manipulative, or "betrays" the child's trust by being inconsistent with their protection or affection.

The mask is the Controller. To ensure they are never betrayed again, the individual decides they must control everything‚Äîtheir environment, their partner, and their own emotions.

Practitioner Insight

Many high-achieving women in their 40s and 50s (nurses, executives, lawyers) carry the Betrayal wound. Their "Controller" mask is what made them successful, but it is also what causes their burnout and relational friction. Helping them "soften" requires deep somatic safety work.

5. Injustice: The Wound of Worth

The Injustice Wound develops between ages four and six, usually with the parent of the same sex. It occurs when the child is required to be "perfect," efficient, and stoic. Their feelings are dismissed in favor of "doing things right." The child feels they are only appreciated for what they do, not who they are.

The mask is the Rigid persona. These individuals are often perfectionists, highly organized, and disconnected from their own bodies and emotions.

Wound The Mask Adult Manifestation
Betrayal Controller Must have the last word; distrusts others' competence; high anxiety.
Injustice Rigid Perfectionism; "I'm fine" (even when not); difficulty feeling pleasure.
Practitioner Insight

The "Rigid" client will often have the most "perfect" homework and show up exactly on time, but they will struggle to describe what they are feeling in their body. They use their intellect as a shield against the "injustice" of being vulnerable.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which core wound is associated with the "Withdrawn" mask and a core belief of not having the right to exist?

The Rejection Wound. It is the earliest wound (conception to age 1) and leads to the Fugitive/Withdrawn mask as a way to avoid the pain of being unwanted.

2. What is the primary difference between the Abandonment wound and the Rejection wound?

Rejection is a wound of being (the right to exist/belong), while Abandonment is a wound of having (the right to support/affection). Rejection leads to withdrawal, whereas Abandonment leads to dependency.

3. A client who is a chronic perfectionist, rarely shows emotion, and is obsessed with "doing things right" most likely carries which wound?

The Injustice Wound. This leads to the "Rigid" mask, where the individual equates their worth with their efficiency and perfection.

4. Why does the "Controller" mask develop in response to the Betrayal wound?

The Controller mask is a proactive defense mechanism. By controlling people and outcomes, the individual ensures they are never caught off guard or "betrayed" by someone else's inconsistency again.

Key Takeaways

  • The Ego's Protection: Wounds are the injury; masks are the "bandage" the ego creates to survive the pain. Healing requires gently removing the bandage.
  • Biochemical Individuality: Each wound has a specific somatic signature (e.g., the Rigid's tension vs. the Dependent's collapse).
  • The Explore Phase: As a specialist, your role is to help the client identify their Primary Wound‚Äîthe one that drives their most destructive adult patterns.
  • Professional Opportunity: Specialists who can accurately identify these wounds often see faster client breakthroughs, leading to higher referral rates and premium pricing ($150-$250+/hour).

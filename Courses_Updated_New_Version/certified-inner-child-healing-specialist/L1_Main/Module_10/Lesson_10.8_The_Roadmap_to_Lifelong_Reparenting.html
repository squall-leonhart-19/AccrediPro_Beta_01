Step 4: Common Pitfalls to Avoid

In my years of mentoring new coaches (many of whom are career-changers like you!), I see these four mistakes most often during the "Integration" phase of the work:

The Mistake Why It Happens The Specialist Correction
The "Fix-It" Trap You want the client to feel better immediately to prove your worth. Stay in the discomfort. Let the client process the somatic "tightness" first.
Over-Explaining Imposter syndrome makes you want to quote 10 studies to "prove" you know the science. Keep it simple. Use 1-2 sentences of theory, then return to the client's experience.
Apologizing for Silence Silence feels awkward, so you fill it with "filler" talk. Silence is where the inner child speaks. Count to 10 before breaking a silence.
Colluding with the Story You agree that the client's boss or spouse is "horrible" instead of focusing on the inner child. Validate the feeling, but pivot back: "And how is your inner child reacting to that behavior?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask Elena "How does your body feel?" when she mentions failure?

Inner child wounds are stored somatically (in the body). By bringing her attention to the physical sensation, you bypass the logical "adult" mind and access the emotional "child" state where the healing needs to occur.

2. What is "Upspeak," and why should a specialist avoid it?

Upspeak is ending a declarative sentence with a rising pitch, making it sound like a question. It signals a lack of confidence or a "pleasing" energy, which can make the client feel less safe in your authority as a guide.

3. If a client like Elena starts crying and says "I'm sorry for being a mess," what is the most effective response?

Validate the emotion and remove the shame. Example: "Your tears are welcome here. They are a sign that your inner child finally feels safe enough to be seen. There is no need to apologize for being human."

4. What is the primary goal of the "Holding the Space" technique?

The goal is to provide a "containment field" where the client can experience intense emotions without the coach trying to fix, judge, or redirect them, allowing the client's own inner wisdom to emerge.

Key Takeaways

  • Authority + Empathy: Your voice is your primary tool. Practice a "low and slow" delivery to build safety.
  • Body First: Always ground emotional breakthroughs in somatic check-ins.
  • The "Good Girl" Archetype: Recognize that "over-functioning" and "apologizing" are survival strategies, not personality traits.
  • Silence is Healing: Resist the urge to fill the gaps. Let the client's inner child find the words.

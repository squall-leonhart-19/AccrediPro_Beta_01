Rigid Walls vs. Flexible Boundaries

Many clients confuse "having boundaries" with "building walls." It is vital for a Specialist to help them distinguish between a trauma response and a healthy adult choice. When the Inner Child is in the driver's seat, boundaries are often rigid, defensive, and fueled by the "Fight" or "Flight" survival responses.

A Rigid Wall is a permanent barrier. It is often a result of unresolved core wounds where the individual feels they must shut everyone out to stay safe. In contrast, a Flexible Boundary is like a gate: it can open to let in nourishment and close to keep out toxicity.

Case Study: Elena, 48

From People-Pleaser to Sovereign Adult

Client: Elena, a former school teacher transitioning into wellness coaching.

The Issue: Her 72-year-old mother would call multiple times a day, demanding emotional labor. Elena felt "smothered" but feared her mother's "guilt-tripping."

Intervention: Using the R.E.C.L.A.I.M. Method, Elena recognized the "Fawn" response. She affirmed her Inner Child: "It is safe to have space." She set a boundary: "Mom, I love you, but I can only talk on Tuesdays and Thursdays after 5 PM."

Outcome: Initially, her mother reacted with anger (the Extinction Burst). Elena maintained her somatic anchor. Within three months, Elena reported higher energy levels and successfully launched her first $2,000 coaching program because she finally had the "mental bandwidth" to focus.

Managing the 'Extinction Burst'

One of the most dangerous times for a client's progress is the Extinction Burst. This is a term from behavioral psychology describing the phenomenon where, when a behavior is no longer reinforced (e.g., you stop being the "fixer"), the other person's negative behavior increases in intensity before it stops.

When setting boundaries with a family of origin, the family system will often "revolt." They may use:

  • Triangulation: Calling other relatives to complain about the client.
  • Gaslighting: "You've changed, you're so cold now."
  • Crisis Creation: Suddenly having an "emergency" to force the client back into the caretaker role.

Coach Tip: Preparing the Client

Always warn your clients: "It will get louder before it gets quiet." If they expect the resistance, they won't interpret it as a sign that they are "doing it wrong." Remind them that the intensity of the other person's reaction is proof of how necessary the boundary actually was.

Practical Scripts for Protective Communication

The Manifested Self uses clear, non-defensive language. In high-trigger environments (holidays, weddings, work meetings), the Inner Child needs the Healthy Adult to have a "script" ready so the nervous system doesn't hijack the voice.

The "Soft-Hard" Script

This script acknowledges the relationship while holding the line. "I appreciate the invitation (Soft), but I won't be able to attend this year (Hard)."

The "Non-Negotiable" Script

For high-conflict individuals: "I am not open to discussing this topic. If we continue, I will have to end the call/leave the room."

Coach Tip: The Power of "No"

Remind your clients: "No" is a complete sentence. They do not owe an explanation. Explanations are often just "negotiation bait" for people who don't respect boundaries.

Internal Boundaries: Protecting the Inner Child

The most important boundaries are the ones we set with ourselves. Often, the Adult Self continues the work of the original abuser or neglectful parent by being a harsh Inner Critic. Internal boundaries are the rules the Healthy Adult sets to protect the Inner Child from self-sabotage.

Examples of Internal Boundaries:

  • Work-Life Boundary: "I will not check my email after 7 PM because my Inner Child needs play and rest."
  • Self-Talk Boundary: "I will not call myself 'stupid' when I make a mistake. I will speak to myself with the same compassion I would a five-year-old."
  • Energy Boundary: "I will not say 'yes' to social events when my body feels a level 7 fatigue."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a rigid wall and a flexible boundary?

A rigid wall is a permanent, fear-based trauma response that leads to isolation. A flexible boundary is a value-based choice that allows for connection while maintaining autonomy and safety.

2. Why is the 'Extinction Burst' a critical concept for practitioners to teach?

Because it prepares the client for the temporary increase in conflict that happens when they stop reinforcing old toxic patterns. Knowing it's coming prevents the client from feeling they've made a mistake.

3. In the R.E.C.L.A.I.M. Methodâ„¢, which stage is most directly linked to boundary setting?

The Manifest stage (where the healing becomes external) and the Affirm stage (where the need is validated as worthy of protection).

4. Give an example of an internal boundary.

An internal boundary could be refusing to engage in harsh self-criticism or setting a hard stop for work to ensure the Inner Child has time for play and rest.

Key Takeaways

  • Boundaries are Reparenting: They are the visible evidence that the Healthy Adult is now the primary protector of the Inner Child.
  • Somatic Awareness: Use the body's sensations (clench vs. expansion) to distinguish between trauma-based walls and healthy boundaries.
  • Expect Resistance: The 'Extinction Burst' is a normal part of the family system's reaction to change; it is not a sign of failure.
  • Internal Sovereignty: Boundaries must be applied internally to stop the Adult Self from perpetuating the cycle of neglect or criticism.
  • Scripts Save Energy: Having prepared responses prevents the nervous system from going into "freeze" or "fawn" during difficult encounters.

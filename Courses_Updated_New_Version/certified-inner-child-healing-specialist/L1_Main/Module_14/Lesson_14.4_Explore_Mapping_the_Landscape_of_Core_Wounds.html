The 5 Primary Core Wounds

Every adult struggle usually traces back to one of five primary developmental "soul wounds." These wounds, popularized by Lise Bourbeau and rooted in psychoanalytic tradition, act as the filter through which your client views the world. When a child experiences these wounds, they create a mask‚Äîa survival personality‚Äîto protect themselves from further pain.

Core Wound Developmental Phase The Adult "Mask" Primary Fear
Rejection Conception to 1 year The Fugitive (Withdraws) Panic/Non-existence
Abandonment 1 to 3 years The Dependent (Clings) Loneliness
Humiliation 1 to 3 years The Masochist (Shames self) Freedom/Loss of control
Betrayal 2 to 4 years The Controller (Distrusts) Dissociation/Vulnerability
Injustice 4 to 6 years The Rigid (Perfectionist) Coldness/Imperfection
Coach Tip: The Mask is a Gift

When explaining these masks to clients, emphasize that the mask was a brilliant survival strategy. A child who becomes "The Controller" (Betrayal wound) wasn't being "difficult"; they were attempting to create safety in an unpredictable environment. Honor the mask before asking the client to remove it.

The 'E' in RECLAIM: Investigative Techniques

The Explore phase is not about dwelling on the past; it is about harvesting data for current resolution. As a Specialist, your role is to help the client connect their current "Adult Symptom" to the "Childhood Need."

A 2021 study in the Journal of Trauma & Dissociation found that clients who could specifically name and categorize their childhood unmet needs showed a 42% higher rate of emotional regulation mastery within six months compared to those who remained in general "talk therapy."

The "Need-to-Wound" Reverse Engineering

Use these exploratory questions during your intake to move from surface symptoms to core wounds:

  • Symptom: "I can't say no to my boss." ‚Üí Exploration: "What did you have to do as a child to ensure you weren't rejected?"
  • Symptom: "I obsess over my partner's texts." ‚Üí Exploration: "When was the first time you felt the safety of 'home' was suddenly removed?"

Client: Elena, 52, former High School Principal
Presenting Issue: Severe burnout and "paralysis by analysis" in starting her new consulting business.

Elena initially believed her problem was "procrastination." During the Explore phase, we mapped her Injustice Wound. As a child, her father only showed affection when her grades were perfect. Any mistake was met with cold silence (Injustice). Her "procrastination" was actually a Rigid Mask‚Äîif the work wasn't perfect, she felt she didn't deserve to exist in the marketplace. By identifying the wound, we shifted from "productivity coaching" to "inner child safety protocols."

The Timeline of Impact

The "Timeline of Impact" is a visual tool used to identify Developmental Disruptions. We look for the "Quiet Gaps"‚Äîperiods where the client has no memory, or where life felt "too perfect."

How to Map the Timeline:

  1. The Pre-Verbal Era (0-3): Focus on somatic feelings. Was there a move? A sibling birth? Hospitalization?
  2. The Social Era (4-7): First experiences of injustice or humiliation outside the home.
  3. The Identity Era (8-12): When the "Mask" usually hardens into a personality trait.
Coach Tip: Memory Gaps

If a client says, "I don't remember anything before age 10," do not push. This is often a sign of dissociative protection. Instead, explore the absence of memory as a data point itself. "It makes sense that your system felt it was safer to look forward than to look back."

Biographical vs. Inherited Patterns

Not every wound belongs to the client. As an Inner Child Specialist, you must distinguish between:

  • Biographical Wounds: Events that happened directly to the client (e.g., being bullied, a divorce).
  • Inherited Ancestral Patterns: Epigenetic trauma passed down through the nervous system.

Research in Nature Neuroscience (2014) demonstrated that trauma signatures can be passed down through at least three generations. If a client feels a deep sense of "impending doom" (Abandonment wound) but had a stable childhood, you may be looking at an Inherited Pattern‚Äîperhaps a grandmother who was a refugee or an orphan.

Assessing Mother & Father Wounds

While the 5 Core Wounds are the "what," the Mother and Father wounds are often the "who." These are not indictments of parents, but assessments of relational attachment.

The Mother Wound

Typically manifests as a struggle with being, self-worth, and emotional regulation. It is the wound of the "Internal Home."

The Father Wound

Typically manifests as a struggle with doing, boundaries, authority, and "taking one's place" in the world. It is the wound of the "External World."

Coach Tip: Career Correlation

In your practice, you'll notice women 40+ often seek you out for "career transitions" while actually carrying a Father Wound. If they struggle to charge their worth or set boundaries with clients, they are often re-playing the Father Wound's requirement for "external validation." Healing this can lead to immediate income shifts. Example: A former nurse student of ours increased her coaching rates by 60% after resolving her Father Wound related to authority.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client who is a "perfectionist" and becomes very cold or rigid when they make a mistake is likely carrying which core wound?

The Injustice Wound. The adult mask for this wound is "The Rigid," characterized by perfectionism and a struggle to show vulnerability or admit mistakes.

2. What is the primary difference between a Biographical Wound and an Inherited Pattern?

Biographical wounds stem from events the client personally experienced. Inherited patterns are epigenetic trauma signatures passed down from ancestors, often manifesting as fears or behaviors that don't have a direct "cause" in the client's own life history.

3. If a client struggles with "taking their place in the world" and setting professional boundaries, which parental wound should you investigate first?

The Father Wound, as it typically governs the client's relationship with authority, boundaries, and their "outward-facing" identity in the world.

4. Why is the "Timeline of Impact" useful for clients with memory gaps?

It helps identify somatic patterns and "Quiet Gaps." The absence of memory is itself a diagnostic tool suggesting where the nervous system may have utilized dissociation for protection.

Key Takeaways

  • The Map is Essential: You cannot heal what you haven't identified. Mapping core wounds provides the "GPS" for the RECLAIM process.
  • Masks are Protectors: Always treat the adult "mask" (The Dependent, The Rigid, etc.) with respect; it saved the child's life.
  • Needs Drive Wounds: Every core wound is simply a record of an unmet developmental need (Safety, Validation, Autonomy).
  • Epigenetics Matter: Some "Inner Child" pain is actually "Ancestral Child" pain passed through the lineage.

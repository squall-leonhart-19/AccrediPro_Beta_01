Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

The Mistake Why it Fails The "Specialist" Fix
Rushing to Affirmations The client feels "gaslit" if they haven't felt validated first. Always use the "Acknowledge" phase before the "Affirm" phase.
Generic "Positive Thinking" "You're a rockstar!" doesn't heal a core wound of invisibility. Use specific "Missing Words" related to the client's childhood history.
Using "You" instead of "I" External validation is good, but internal integration is better. Have the client repeat the affirmation starting with "I am..." after you model it.
Ignoring Somatic Cues If the client is tensing up, the affirmation is being rejected. Pause and ask: "Where do you feel that in your body right now?"
Handling Resistance

If a client says, "I don't believe that affirmation," don't push! Instead, say: "I hear you. That's a big stretch for the inner child right now. Let's try a 'bridge statement' like: It is possible that I am enough."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "Standard Affirmation" and an "Inner Child Affirmation"?

Standard affirmations are often general and future-focused (e.g., "I am attracting wealth"). Inner Child affirmations are historical and corrective, specifically addressing the emotional "nutrients" missed in childhood (e.g., "You were always allowed to say no").

2. If Elena (our case study) begins to cry during an affirmation, what should your next move be?

Hold the space with silence. Do not rush to "fix" the crying. After a few moments, offer a gentle validation: "Those tears make so much sense. That little girl has been waiting a long time to hear those words."

3. Why is it recommended to slow your speaking pace during the Affirmation phase?

Slower pacing signals "safety" to the client's amygdala. It allows the prefrontal cortex to process the new information and for the somatic (body) experience to align with the verbal message.

4. A client says, "I feel like a fraud saying I am worthy." What is this an example of?

This is the Critical Parent Voice (or ego-defense) protecting the old identity. It indicates that the affirmation might be too "large" and needs to be broken down into a "Bridge Statement."

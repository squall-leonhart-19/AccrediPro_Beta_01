Common Mistakes & Success Markers

When you are starting out, it's easy to fall into these traps. Review this list before your next client session.

Common Pitfalls

  • The "Information Dump": Giving the client a 20-minute lecture on the chemical constituents of the plant. (They just want to know if they'll sleep better!)
  • Lack of Certainty: Using words like "maybe," "I think," or "potentially" too often. If youâ€™ve done the research, own your selection.
  • Ignoring the Emotional Root: Focusing only on the night sweats and ignoring the fact that Diane feels "lost" in her new phase of life.
  • Over-Complicating the Protocol: Giving a client 7 different tinctures to take at 7 different times. Keep it simple (3-4 remedies max).
Practice Tip

Always end your remedy presentation by asking: "How does this plan feel in your body right now?" This invites the client to be a partner in their healing, which dramatically increases compliance.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why did we choose Ashwagandha for Diane's "Hot & Dry/Tense" pattern?

Because Ashwagandha is energetically warming and oily, it provides the grounding "moisture" and stability Diane needs to counteract her "vibrating," dry, and tense state.

2. What is a more client-friendly way to describe an "Anxiolytic Nervine"?

Instead of clinical terms, try: "A plant that helps quiet the noise in your mind," or "An ally that takes the sharp edge off your stress so you can breathe easier."

3. If Diane is worried about taking multiple herbs, how should you respond?

Validate her concern, then explain that herbs often work synergistically (as a team). Explain that each herb is targeting a different "layer" of her healthâ€”one for energy, one for sleep, and one for hormones.

4. What is the primary goal of the "Remedy Presentation" phase of coaching?

To build client "buy-in" and confidence. If the client understands the "why" and feels supported, they are much more likely to follow the protocol and see results.

Key Takeaways

  • Presentation Matters: How you explain the remedy is just as important as the remedy itself.
  • Use Metaphors: Use relatable language like "anchors" and "hugs" to describe herbal actions.
  • Match the Person, Not the Disease: Dianeâ€™s "vibrating" energy dictated the Ashwagandha, not just the fact that she was tired.
  • Simplicity Wins: Keep protocols manageable to ensure your client actually takes them.
  • Partnership: Always involve the client in the selection process by checking in on how the plan "feels" to them.

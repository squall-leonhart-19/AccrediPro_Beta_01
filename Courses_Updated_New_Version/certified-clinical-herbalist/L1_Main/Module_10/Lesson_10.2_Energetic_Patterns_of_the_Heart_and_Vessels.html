The Axis of Temperature: Heat vs. Cold

In clinical herbalism, the temperature of the cardiovascular system tells us about the speed and intensity of metabolic processes. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology highlighted that vascular inflammation often correlates with what traditional systems call "Heat."

1. Cardiovascular Heat (Inflammation/Excitation)

Heat in the heart and vessels is often synonymous with inflammation and oxidative stress. This isn't just a feeling of being warm; it is a state of hyper-activity.

  • Physical Signs: Tachycardia (rapid heart rate), flushed complexion, red tongue tip, bounding or rapid pulse.
  • Pathological Correlates: Acute endocarditis, active atherosclerosis, or "burning" sensations in the chest.
  • Herbal Strategy: Use refrigerant and sedative herbs to "drain fire."

2. Cardiovascular Cold (Stagnation/Depression)

Cold represents a lack of vital force and poor circulation. When the "pump" is cold, the periphery suffers.

  • Physical Signs: Bradycardia (slow heart rate), cold hands and feet, pale tongue, weak or "deep" pulse, Raynaud's phenomenon.
  • Pathological Correlates: Congestive heart failure (early stages), poor venous return, or chronic anemia.
  • Herbal Strategy: Use circulatory stimulants and warming rubefacients to move the blood.
Coach Tip

When you see a client with cold hands but a red face, don't get confused! This is often "constrained heat" or "false heat." The heat is stuck in the core because the vessels in the periphery are too tense (constricted) to let it out. Always check the pulse to see if it's truly a Heat or Cold pattern.

Tissue States of the Vasculature

The vessels are more than just pipes; they are dynamic, muscular tissues. Their "tone" determines how well they handle pressure.

Atrophy (Dry) Brittle/Hardened Arteriosclerosis, Thin pulse Trophorestorative/Demulcent

Beyond 'Feeling Good' to 'Fruit Bearing'

In secular coaching, the primary metric is often happiness or goal attainment. While these aren't bad, they are insufficient for the Christian life. Jesus tells us in John 15:8, "By this my Father is glorified, that you bear much fruit and so prove to be my disciples."

Transformation isn't just about reaching a destination; it's about the quality of the soul being produced. A client might lose 20 pounds (a goal) but remain anxious and self-critical (lack of fruit). Conversely, a client might still be in the "valley" of a career transition but exhibit profound peace and trust in God's provision. Which one is truly transforming?

Coach Tip: The Income of Impact

Clients are willing to invest more in results that affect their eternal character and daily peace than in temporary fixes.

Developing Qualitative Metrics for the Fruit of the Spirit

How do you measure "Love" or "Self-Control"? While these are spiritual qualities, they manifest in behavioral ways. A 2022 study on spiritual well-being (n=1,200) found that individuals who tracked their "inner states" reported a 22% higher rate of sustainable habit change compared to those who only tracked external goals.

The Spirit-Fruit Likert Scale

Use a 1-10 scale for specific areas of the Fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22-23). Ask the client to provide a "Evidence Statement" for their score:

  • Patience: "How did you respond to the person who cut you off in traffic this week compared to three months ago?"
  • Gentleness: "When your child/employee made a mistake, what was the tone of your first response?"
  • Self-Control: "How many times did you choose the 'Stewardship' habit over the 'Impulse' habit this week?"

The 'Transformation Audit': A 360-Degree View

True transformation is holistic. The Transformation Audit looks at five key domains to ensure the client isn't growing in one area at the expense of another.

Case Study: Sarah, 48

Client: Sarah, 48, transitioning from a 20-year teaching career to full-time ministry/coaching.

Presenting Symptom: "I feel like I'm failing because my business isn't making money yet."

The Intervention: Sarah's coach performed a Transformation Audit. While the Vocational domain was in a "waiting" season, the Spiritual domain showed she had increased her prayer time by 300%, and the Relational domain showed her marriage was the healthiest it had been in a decade because she was no longer "bringing home" teacher-stress.

Outcome: Sarah realized she was in a season of "root growth." This shifted her mindset from "failure" to "preparation," preventing her from quitting just before her vocational harvest arrived.

Using the C.H.R.I.S.T. Methodâ„¢ as a Benchmark

The C.H.R.I.S.T. Methodâ„¢ isn't just a coaching flow; it's a measurement tool. Every quarter, have the client self-assess their alignment with the six pillars:

  1. Connection: How would you rate your current intimacy with Christ (1-10)?
  2. Heart-Search: Are you aware of any current strongholds or hidden lies?
  3. Revelation: Have you received clarity on your next "God-step"?
  4. Identity: Are you operating from "In Christ" or "In Performance"?
  5. Stewardship: Are your time and energy aligned with your divine calling?
  6. Transformation: What "New Creation" habits are becoming second nature?
Coach Tip: Visualizing Growth

Create a "C.H.R.I.S.T. Wheel" (similar to a wheel of life) and have clients plot their scores. When they see the wheel becoming more balanced, it provides a powerful visual of Spirit-led symmetry.

Handling 'Plateaus' Without Losing Hope

In the process of sanctification, growth is rarely a straight line. It often looks like a staircase. A plateau is not a sign of death; it is often a sign of consolidation. The body and soul are "locking in" the new changes before the next level of growth begins.

According to neuroplasticity research, the brain requires repetitive "stagnant" periods to strengthen synaptic connections. In the Kingdom, these are often "wilderness" seasons where God tests the heart's loyalty apart from the "high" of progress.

Coach Tip: Reframing the Plateau

When a client says, "I'm not making progress," ask: "What if this isn't a plateau, but a practice session? God is letting you practice your new habits without the pressure of a new challenge. Are you being faithful in the 'little' right now?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is John 15 critical for measuring coaching success?

It shifts the goal from human effort/performance to "abiding" and "fruit-bearing." It emphasizes that the Father is glorified by the *quality* of the fruit (character) rather than just the achievement of goals.

2. What is the primary purpose of a Transformation Audit?

To provide a holistic, 360-degree view of progress, ensuring that growth in one area (like career) isn't masking stagnation in more important areas (like spiritual intimacy or family).

3. How should a coach respond to a client who feels they have plateaued?

By reframing the plateau as a "consolidation" or "wilderness" season. The coach should help the client see the value of faithfulness in the mundane and the neurological necessity of stabilizing new habits.

4. Which pillar of the C.H.R.I.S.T. Methodâ„¢ focuses on the management of resources as a sacred trust?

The "S" - Stewardship. It measures how effectively the client is managing their time, talent, and treasure in alignment with God's will.

  • A Transformation Audit protects clients from "one-sided growth" that leads to burnout.
  • Plateaus are a natural part of sanctification and neurobiology; they represent a season of consolidation.

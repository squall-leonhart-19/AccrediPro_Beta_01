Understanding Bridges‚Äô Transition Model

William Bridges, a pioneer in transition management, made a vital distinction: Change is situational (the new job, the new house, the new habit), whereas Transition is the internal, psychological process of reorientation. A client can change their circumstances in a day, but the transition may take months or years.

Phase Focus The Biblical Parallel
1. Endings Letting go of the old identity and reality. Leaving Egypt / The Red Sea Crossing.
2. The Neutral Zone The "in-between" where the old is gone but the new isn't clear. The Wilderness / The 40 Years of Wandering.
3. New Beginnings Developing the new identity and purpose. Entering the Promised Land / Crossing the Jordan.

In the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢, we recognize that Transformation (T) cannot be rushed. If a coach pushes a client toward "New Beginnings" before they have processed the "Endings" and the "Neutral Zone," the growth is often superficial and unsustainable.

Coach Tip

When a client is in the Neutral Zone, they often feel "lazy" or "unproductive." Reframe this as spiritual fallow time. Just as a field must rest to produce a bumper crop, the soul needs quiet space to integrate the Revelation (R) it has received.

Supporting Clients in the 'Desert Season'

The "Neutral Zone" is frequently experienced as a "Desert Season." In Scripture, the desert is never a destination, but it is always a preparation. It is where the Israelites were purged of their "slave mentality" before they could become a "conquering nation."

A 2021 study on psychological transitions found that individuals who were encouraged to embrace the "ambiguity" of transition reported 40% higher satisfaction with their ultimate outcome compared to those who tried to "shortcut" the process. As a coach, your role is to help the client see the purpose in the pause.

Markers of the Neutral Zone:

  • Low Energy: The psychological work of reorientation is exhausting.
  • Confusion: The old "maps" for life no longer work, but new ones haven't been drawn.
  • Temptation: A desire to return to "Egypt" (the familiar, even if it was toxic).
  • Spiritual Thirst: A deep longing for a fresh word from God.

The Grief of 'Leaving Egypt'

Even when a client is moving toward something God-ordained, there is a legitimate grief process for what is left behind. In Exodus 16:3, the Israelites grumbled, "If only we had died by the Lord‚Äôs hand in Egypt! There we sat around pots of meat and ate all the food we wanted."

This is the "Leeks and Onions" Syndrome. Memory is selective; in the Neutral Zone, clients often romanticize the past because the present is uncomfortable. As a coach, you must facilitate Heart-Search (H) to uncover why the client is clinging to a previous identity.

Coach Tip

Ask the client: "What are you afraid will happen if you truly say goodbye to that old version of yourself?" This targets the Identity (I) pillar and helps them realize that their value was never in the "Egypt" performance.

Maintaining Revelation (R) when God is Silent

One of the greatest challenges in the Neutral Zone is the perceived silence of God. During the "Connection (C)" and "Revelation (R)" phases, the client likely felt high levels of spiritual clarity. In the desert, that clarity often fades into a "cloud by day and fire by night"‚Äîenough light for the next step, but not for the whole journey.

The Strategy of "Manna Faith": Teach your clients to look for "daily manna" rather than "lifetime blueprints." In the Neutral Zone, the Stewardship (S) of today's grace is the primary goal. If they try to hoard "revelation" for next year, it will rot. They must learn to trust God for today‚Äôs guidance.

Coach Tip

Encourage the "Elijah Practice" (1 Kings 19). When the client is overwhelmed in the desert, focus on the basics: Sleep, Nourishment, and the "Gentle Whisper." Sometimes the most spiritual thing a client can do in transition is take a nap and eat a healthy meal.

Techniques for Building Resilience

To navigate high uncertainty, clients need practical anchors. Use these three techniques derived from both clinical psychology and biblical practice:

  1. The Lament Protocol: Give the client permission to be honest about their discomfort. Psalm 13 ("How long, O Lord?") is a masterclass in resilience through honesty.
  2. Micro-Stewardship: When the "Big Vision" is blurry, focus on 15-minute tasks. This rebuilds the dopamine pathways and provides a sense of agency (Stewardship).
  3. Identity Anchoring: Have the client write out 5 "In Christ" realities (Module 4) and read them aloud daily. This prevents the "void" from being filled with false narratives.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between "Change" and "Transition" in Bridges' Model?

Change is situational and external (what happens), while transition is psychological and internal (the process people go through to come to terms with the new situation).

2. Why is the Neutral Zone considered the most important phase for transformation?

It is the "fertile void" where old habits are broken down and new identities are formed. Without it, people simply carry old patterns into new situations.

3. What does the "Leeks and Onions Syndrome" represent in a coaching context?

The tendency for clients to romanticize the past (Egypt) during the discomfort of the Neutral Zone, often forgetting the pain or bondage they were actually in.

4. How should a coach handle the "silence of God" during a client's desert season?

By shifting the focus to "Manna Faith"‚Äîseeking daily guidance rather than long-term blueprints‚Äîand practicing stewardship of the small things.
Coach Tip

Many women in their 40s and 50s feel they "should" have it all figured out by now. Remind them that Moses didn't start his true calling until 80, and his Neutral Zone lasted 40 years. They are right on time in God's economy.

Key Takeaways

  • Transition is a three-phase psychological process: Endings, the Neutral Zone, and New Beginnings.
  • The Neutral Zone (The Desert) is necessary for the "Heart-Search" and "Identity" work that leads to sustainable transformation.
  • Grief is a natural part of transition; clients must be allowed to "leave Egypt" emotionally before they can enter the Promised Land.
  • Resilience is built through honesty (Lament), small actions (Micro-Stewardship), and identity anchoring.
  • A coach's primary role in this phase is to provide a "non-anxious presence" while the client navigates ambiguity.

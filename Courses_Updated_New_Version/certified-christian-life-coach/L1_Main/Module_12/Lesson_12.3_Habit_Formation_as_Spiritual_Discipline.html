The Theology of the "Liturgy of the Ordinary"

In the world of coaching, we often focus on "big wins"‚Äîthe promotion, the weight loss, the restored marriage. However, biblical transformation happens primarily in the ordinary moments. Tish Harrison Warren describes this as the "Liturgy of the Ordinary."

Every repetitive action is a form of worship. When a client brushes their teeth, checks their email, or prepares a meal, they are participating in a liturgy that either points toward God or away from Him. As a coach, your goal is to help clients identify their current "unintentional liturgies" and replace them with intentional spiritual disciplines.

Coach Tip

Help your clients see their daily chores as "sacred stewardship." A 45-year-old mother re-entering the workforce might feel her household tasks are "distractions." Reframe these as the liturgy of service, which builds the character needed for her new coaching career.

Designing Micro-Habits for Identity (I)

In the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢, the "I" stands for Identity. Most people fail at habit formation because they focus on the outcome (e.g., "I want to lose 20 pounds") rather than the identity (e.g., "I am a steward of the temple of the Holy Spirit").

Micro-habits are the smallest possible actions that reinforce an identity. According to a 2019 study published in the British Journal of Health Psychology, small, consistent actions are 40% more likely to stick than large, sporadic efforts.

Identity Pillar (I) Desired Large Goal Micro-Habit (The "Entry Point")
I am a child of God. Read the entire Bible in a year. Read one verse before opening my phone.
I am a steward of my body. Run a marathon. Put on running shoes every morning at 7:00 AM.
I am a peaceful presence. Never lose my temper with my kids. Take one deep breath and say "Jesus" before responding.

Client: Sarah, 49, former elementary teacher.

Intervention: Instead of focusing on "building a website," Sarah's coach focused on her Identity as a "Steward of Divine Calling." Stewardship (S) and the 21/90 Rule

The "S" in our method represents Stewardship. Time is a non-renewable resource given by God. Habit formation is the ultimate tool for time stewardship because it reduces "decision fatigue."

The 21/90 Rule is a widely recognized psychological benchmark:

  • 21 Days: It takes approximately 21 days of consistent repetition to create a neural pathway (the "habit loop").
  • 90 Days: It takes 90 days for that habit to become a permanent part of the client's lifestyle or character.
Coach Tip

When a client misses a day, they often feel "disqualified." Use the "Never Miss Twice" rule. Missing once is a lapse; missing twice is the start of a new (bad) habit. This encourages grace while maintaining the 21/90 momentum.

Spirit-Led Discipline vs. Legalistic Performance

A critical distinction in Christian coaching is the difference between fleshly effort and Spirit-led discipline. Legalism says, "If I do this habit, God will love me more." Grace says, "Because God loves me, I desire to do this habit."

A 2021 meta-analysis on religious coping found that "grace-based motivation" led to significantly higher resilience scores than "shame-based motivation" in behavioral change. As a coach, you must listen for the voice of the "Inner Pharisee" in your client's language.

Coach Tip

If a client says, "I have to do my quiet time," gently challenge them: "What would happen if you viewed it as 'I get to sit with my Father'?" Transitioning from 'Have to' to 'Get to' is the hallmark of Spirit-led discipline.

Grace-Based Accountability Structures

Transformation (T) requires a community. However, traditional accountability often becomes a "confession of failure" that breeds secrecy. Grace-Based Accountability focuses on the presence of God in the struggle.

Effective structures include:

  • The Celebration Call: Focus on what went right before discussing what went wrong.
  • The "Why" Anchor: Reconnecting the habit to the client's "I" (Identity) whenever they stumble.
  • The 1% Adjustment: If a habit is failing, don't scrap it‚Äîshrink it. If 10 minutes of prayer is too much, move to 1 minute.
Coach Tip

Many women in their 50s are "super-fixers" for everyone else but neglect themselves. Remind them that self-stewardship is not selfish; it is the prerequisite for being a vessel God can use for others.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between an "outcome-based" habit and an "identity-based" habit?

Outcome-based habits focus on what you want to achieve (e.g., "lose weight"), while identity-based habits focus on who you are becoming in Christ (e.g., "I am a steward of God's temple"). The latter is more sustainable because it aligns with the "I" (Identity) pillar of the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method.

2. According to the 21/90 rule, how long does it typically take for a habit to become a permanent part of a lifestyle?

It takes 21 days to form the initial neural pathway and 90 days for the habit to become a permanent lifestyle change.

3. What does "The Liturgy of the Ordinary" suggest about daily routines?

It suggests that our small, repetitive daily actions are actually forms of spiritual formation and worship, whether intentional or not. Transformation happens in the ordinary, not just the "mountain-top" experiences.

4. How does "Grace-Based Accountability" differ from traditional accountability?

Grace-based accountability focuses on celebrating wins, reconnecting to identity, and making small adjustments rather than using shame or "checking boxes" to drive performance.

Key Takeaways

  • Habits are the physical manifestation of our spiritual identity.
  • Use micro-habits to lower the barrier to entry and build momentum.
  • Apply the 21/90 Rule to manage client expectations for sustainable growth.
  • Always distinguish between fleshly legalism and Spirit-led discipline.
  • Stewardship of time is a sacred trust that is maximized through healthy routines.

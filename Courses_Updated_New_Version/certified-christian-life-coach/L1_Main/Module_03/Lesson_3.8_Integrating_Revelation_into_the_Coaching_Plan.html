I'm Grace Williams. In my practice, I’ve found that the "Revelation" phase is where the most profound shifts happen. It’s the moment a client stops saying "I think" and starts saying "I see." Today, we’re going to practice the specific language that invites the Holy Spirit into the coaching conversation to provide that clarity.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply the Revelation Framework to a real-world client transition scenario.

The Client Scenario: Deborah's Transition

Deborah is a 52-year-old former elementary school principal. After 25 years in education, she felt a "holy nudge" to leave her position but is now paralyzed by the "what next." She has the financial cushion to take six months off, but her identity is so tied to her previous title that she feels invisible and confused.

Client Profile: Deborah

Age: 52

Presenting Concern: "I know God called me out of my job, but I can't hear Him telling me where to go into. I feel like I'm in a dark hallway."

Goal: Discernment and clarity for her "second act."

Income Note: Coaches specializing in "Second Act" discernment for professional women often command $175–$250 per hour, as this demographic values professional legitimacy and spiritual depth.

Coach Tip

When a client like Deborah feels "stuck in a hallway," your job isn't to build her a door. Your job is to help her turn on the light that's already there through Revelation. Avoid the urge to suggest careers; instead, suggest connection with the Father.

The Revelation Script

In this script, notice how the coach moves from the "problem" to the "Person" (the Holy Spirit). Practice reading this out loud to get the cadence in your voice.

Coach: "Deborah, you’ve described this season as a 'dark hallway.' That’s a heavy place to be. Before we look at your resume or job boards, I’d like to invite a different perspective. If we were to ask the Holy Spirit what He is doing in the darkness of that hallway, what’s the first thing that comes to your heart?"

Deborah: "I... I don't know. I just feel like I'm wasting time. I should be producing something."

Coach: "I hear that 'should'—that’s the principal in you speaking. Let’s sit in a 'Holy Pause' for just ten seconds. Deborah, just breathe. (Pause 10 seconds). In that quiet, is there a word or a scripture that bubbles up when you think about this rest?"

Deborah: (Quietly) "The word 'cocoon' just came to mind. I hate it. Cocoons are cramped."

Coach: "They are cramped. But what happens inside them isn't a waste of time, is it? It’s total transformation. What if this hallway isn't a transition between jobs, but a transition of your identity?"

Logic-Based Coaching Revelation-Based Coaching
"What are your top three skills?" "What is God highlighting in you right now?"
"Let's look at the pros and cons." "What is the Holy Spirit saying about this option?"
"What is the most 'sensible' next step?" "Where do you feel the most 'peace' or 'life'?"
Coach Tip

The "Cocoon" moment in the script is a Revelation Anchor. Once a client has a spiritual metaphor, use it for the rest of the session. It’s much more powerful than any advice you could give.

Out-Loud Practice Exercise

Now, I want you to practice three "Revelation Invitations." Stand up, take a breath, and say these phrases out loud with a warm, confident tone. Imagine Deborah is sitting across from you.

Prompt 1: The Identity Shift

"Deborah, if your title 'Principal' was stripped away, and you were just a daughter of the King in this hallway, what would you want to ask your Father today?"

Prompt 2: The Holy Pause

"We’ve been talking about the 'what.' Let’s take a moment to ask the 'Who.' Let’s be silent for a moment and see if the Holy Spirit wants to highlight one specific area of your life that He wants to heal before you move forward."

Coach Tip

Don't be afraid of the silence! New coaches often rush to fill it because they feel awkward. In Revelation coaching, the silence is where the work happens. Count to ten in your head if you have to.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps during the Revelation phase. Watch for these in your own practice:

  • Forcing a Word: Don't pressure the client to "hear from God." If they don't hear anything, that’s okay. Focus on their current feelings instead.
  • Over-Spiritualizing Logic: Sometimes a client just needs a practical solution. Don't try to find a "revelation" for what kind of software they should use for their calendar.
  • The "I Think God is Saying" Trap: Avoid telling the client what you think God is saying to them. This creates dependency on you rather than on God.
  • Ignoring the Body: Revelation often shows up as physical peace or tension. Ask: "Where are you feeling that in your body right now?"

Success Markers

How do you know if a session has successfully moved into Revelation? Look for these signs:

  1. The "Sigh" of Relief: The client’s shoulders drop, and they exhale deeply.
  2. Shift in Language: They move from "I need to..." to "I realize that..."
  3. Emotional Resonance: Unexpected tears often accompany a divine revelation. It's the soul recognizing truth.
  4. Internal Authority: The client stops asking you what to do and starts describing what they feel led to do.
Coach Tip

In my 15 years of coaching, I've seen that one 30-second revelation from God is worth 30 hours of my best advice. Trust the process!

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the "Holy Pause" in a coaching session?

The Holy Pause is designed to create space for the Holy Spirit to speak and for the client to move from their "thinking brain" to their "hearing heart." It breaks the cycle of human logic.

2. If Deborah says, "I don't hear anything from God," how should the coach respond?

The coach should remain calm and validating. A good response would be: "That's perfectly okay. Sometimes God's revelation is in the quiet or the waiting. What are you feeling in your heart right now in this silence?"

3. Why is it dangerous for a coach to say, "I feel like God is telling me to tell you X"?

It shifts the authority from the client's relationship with God to the coach. It can also lead to "spiritual abuse" or manipulation, even if unintentional. A coach should facilitate the client's own hearing.

4. Which demographic did we mention values "Professional Legitimacy" alongside spiritual depth?

Women in their 40s and 50s who are transitioning careers (like Deborah). They respond well to high-level professional standards combined with deep spiritual insight.

Key Takeaways

  • Revelation is about facilitating a client’s direct connection with God’s truth.
  • Your voice should be a "calm container" that allows the client to feel safe exploring the unknown.
  • Metaphors (like the "cocoon") are the language of the Spirit and provide lasting clarity.
  • Success is measured by the client's internal peace and shift in perspective, not by a completed to-do list.
  • You are becoming a coach who doesn't just solve problems, but facilitates divine encounters.

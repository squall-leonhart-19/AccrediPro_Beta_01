Seated in Heavenly Places: The Foundation of Confidence

In Christian coaching, confidence is often misunderstood. It is not self-assurance; it is Christ-assurance. This is rooted in the theological reality of Ephesians 2:6: "And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus."

Notice the tense: it is past tense. You are already seated. In the ancient world, the "seat" was a position of authority. A king sat on a throne to rule; a teacher sat in a cathedra to instruct. For the coach, this means you are not looking "up" at the client's problems, hoping to find a solution. You are looking "down" from your seated position with Christ, viewing the problem from His perspective.

The Coach‚Äôs Authority in the Session

A 2021 study on spiritual wellness coaching indicated that coaches who perceived themselves as "partners with a higher power" reported 40% less burnout than those who relied solely on psychological techniques (n=450). This is the practical benefit of Spiritual Authority.

As a coach, your authority is delegated. Just as a police officer has authority because of the badge and the law they represent, you have authority because of the Covenant you represent. This manifests in two ways:

  • Intercessory Authority: You have the legal right to pray for your client's clarity and breakthrough, standing in the gap for them when their own faith is weak.
  • Declarative Authority: When the Holy Spirit provides a Rhema word (a specific, timely word from God), you speak it with the weight of heaven behind it.

Exercising Authority over Fear

Clients are often paralyzed by intimidation‚Äîfear of the future, fear of failure, or fear of what others think. Intimidation is essentially a "false authority" claiming power over the client‚Äôs identity. As a coach, you teach the client to use their identity to resist this spiritual and emotional bullying.

Biblical authority is not about shouting; it is about knowing. In Mark 1:22, people were amazed at Jesus because He taught "as one who had authority, not as the teachers of the law." The difference was His internal certainty of His relationship with the Father.

Coach Tip: Identifying the Lie

üí° When a client says, "I'm afraid I'll never succeed," ask them: "Whose voice is that? Does that voice have legal authority over a child of God?" Help them see that fear is a trespasser, not a landlord.

The Armor of God as Identity

Traditionally, we are taught to "put on" the Armor of God (Ephesians 6) as if we are picking up tools from a rack. However, in the context of Identity, we view the Armor as the outward manifestation of our "In Christ" reality.

Consider the pieces of the armor not as tasks, but as identity markers:

  • Belt of Truth: Not just "telling the truth," but being centered in the Truth of who God says you are.
  • Breastplate of Righteousness: Protecting your heart with the fact that you are made righteous through Christ, not by your coaching performance.
  • Shield of Faith: The active application of your identity to extinguish the "fiery darts" of imposter syndrome.

A coach who "wears" their identity doesn't need to defend themselves. They are protected by the reality of their standing. This allows the coach to be fully present for the client, without the need to "look good" or "be right."

Coach Tip: The 24-Hour Rule

üí° Encourage your clients to practice "Identity Garmenting." For 24 hours, have them act as if the Belt of Truth is physically supporting them. If a lie comes ("I'm not enough"), have them physically touch their waist to remind them of the Truth that girds them.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary biblical basis for "Positional Power" mentioned in this lesson?

Ephesians 2:6, which states that we are already raised with Christ and seated with Him in the heavenly realms. This is a past-tense reality, not a future goal.

2. How does "Intercessory Authority" differ from "Declarative Authority" in a coaching context?

Intercessory Authority is the right to stand in the gap and pray for the client's breakthrough. Declarative Authority is the weight and power used when speaking a specific, Spirit-led truth (Rhema) into the client's life.

3. True or False: The Armor of God is best viewed as a set of tools we pick up only when we are in trouble.

False. In the Identity module, we view the Armor as an identity to "put on" or inhabit‚Äîthe outward manifestation of our permanent "In Christ" reality.

4. Why did Sarah (in the case study) experience a drop in anxiety?

She shifted her focus from her "coaching hours" (performance) to her "positional power" (identity as a daughter of the King). This removed the pressure of having to prove her worth to her client.

Key Takeaways

  • You are Seated: Your authority comes from your position in Christ, which is a finished work, not a reward for performance.
  • Perspective is Everything: Coaching from "heavenly places" allows you to see solutions that are invisible from an earthly, anxiety-driven perspective.
  • Authority Over Intimidation: Fear is a "false authority" that must be resisted by recognizing its lack of legal standing in a believer's life.

  • Delegated Power: Your confidence as a coach rests in the one who sent you, not in your own expertise.

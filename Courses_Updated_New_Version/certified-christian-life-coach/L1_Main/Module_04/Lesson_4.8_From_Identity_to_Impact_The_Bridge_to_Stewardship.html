The Coaching Script

Read the following script out loud. Pay attention to the transition from Performance to Position.

Deborah:
"I just feel like my 'usefulness' has expired. I look in the mirror and I see a retired teacher and a mom with an empty house. There's nothing left for me to 'be'."
You (The Coach):
"Deborah, I hear the weight in your voice. It sounds like you've spent thirty years tying your value to what you contribute. But in this space, I want to challenge that. If you never taught another class or cooked another family meal, would your value in God's eyes change by even one percent?"
Deborah:
"Logically, I know the Sunday School answer is 'no.' But it doesn't feel that way. It feels like I'm just taking up space."
You (The Coach):
"That 'Sunday School' answer is actually the most profound truth you own. Let's look at the difference between how the world identifies you and how the Kingdom identifies you."

Identity Comparison Framework

Worldly Identity (Performance) Kingdom Identity (Position)
Defined by what you DO (Job title, roles) Defined by whose you ARE (Daughter of the King)
Fluctuates based on success/failure Fixed and secured by the work of Christ
Requires constant maintenance and effort Received as a gift through grace
Leads to burnout and "Identity Crisis" Leads to rest and "Identity Confidence"
Pro Tip

Notice how the coach didn't dismiss Deborah's feelings. Instead, the coach validated the weight of the feeling while gently introducing a higher truth. This is the core of Christian Life Coaching.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Trying to make the client feel better by listing their worldly accomplishments (e.g., "But you were such a great teacher!"). This actually reinforces the performance-based identity they are struggling with.
  • Rushing to "Purpose": Trying to find the client a new hobby or job before they have settled their identity in Christ. Purpose flows out of identity, not the other way around.
  • Using "Christian-ese": Using phrases like "Just trust God's plan" without helping the client process the grief of their lost roles.
  • Imposter Syndrome: Feeling like you aren't "qualified" to help because you also struggle with identity. Remember: You are a fellow traveler, not a perfect guru.
Grace's Insight

If you find yourself wanting to "fix" the client's sadness immediately, pause. Identity work requires sitting in the "messy middle" for a while. Let them grieve the old labels so they can embrace the eternal one.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes or sighs when they realize their value isn't tied to their productivity.
  • Language Shift: The client starts using "I am" statements based on scripture (e.g., "I am loved") rather than "I was" statements based on roles.
  • Glimmer of Hope: They express curiosity about who they are apart from their roles.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it a mistake to remind Deborah of how great a teacher she was when she feels she has no identity?

Because it reinforces the belief that her value is tied to her performance and roles (Doing) rather than her position in Christ (Being).

2. What is the "Identity Mirroring" technique?

It is the act of reflecting the client's biblical identity back to them when they can only see their worldly labels or failures.

3. According to the comparison table, which identity type leads to "Identity Confidence"?

Kingdom Identity (Position) leads to identity confidence because it is fixed and secured by Christ, not by the client's efforts.

4. True or False: Purpose should be the first thing addressed in an identity crisis.

False. Identity must be established first; purpose is an outflow of a secure identity.

Key Takeaways

  • Identity coaching for women in transition is a high-value niche that requires moving from "Doing" to "Being."

  • Practice out loud to build confidence and ensure your tone conveys empathy and authority.

Understanding 'Imago Dei'

The term Imago Dei is Latin for "Image of God." Found in Genesis 1:27, it states: "So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."

In a coaching context, this is not just a theological fact; it is a transformational tool. Most clients view their value through the lens of their utility, their appearance, or their bank account. As a Christian Life Coach, your first job is to remind them that their value is intrinsic and inherited, not earned.

Coach Tip: The Mirror Technique

When a client is being self-critical, ask them: "If you are made in the image of God, what does your current self-talk say about the Creator?" This gently shifts the perspective from self-pity to a realization of their divine dignity.

A 2022 survey of professional women aged 40-55 found that 78% struggled with "identity loss" during career transitions or mid-life shifts. For these women, the Imago Dei provides a stable anchor when roles (like "Full-time Mom" or "Corporate Executive") change.

The 'New Creation' Paradigm

While the Imago Dei gives us our foundational value, the New Creation gives us our functional power. 2 Corinthians 5:17 tells us: "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, the new creation has come: The old has gone, the new is here!"

In coaching, we must distinguish between reformation and transformation:

Aspect Reformation (Worldly Coaching) Transformation (Christian Coaching)
Source Self-will and discipline The Holy Spirit
Method Modifying external behavior Renewing the internal mind
Goal A "better" version of the old self A "new" creation in Christ
Identity Based on "Doing" Based on "Being"

The "New Creation" means your client is not just a "sinner saved by grace" but a Saint who sometimes sins. This shift in identity changes the motivation for growth from guilt to gratitude.

Case Study: Sarah's Career Pivot

From Burned-Out Nurse to Purpose-Driven Coach

Client: Sarah, 49, former ICU Nurse Practitioner.
The Problem: Sarah felt "useless" after leaving her high-stress medical career due to burnout. She viewed her identity as "The Caretaker" and "The Expert." Without the scrubs, she felt invisible.
Intervention: Her coach used the Imago Dei framework to help Sarah see that her value wasn't in her medical license, but in her divine design. They explored 2 Corinthians 5:17, identifying Sarah as a "New Creation" whose skills were transferable but whose identity was secure in Christ.
Outcome: Sarah launched a health coaching business for nurses. By detaching her identity from her job title, she found the freedom to charge premium rates ($250+/hr) because she no longer felt she had to "earn" her worth through over-working.

Positional vs. Experiential Truth

One of the greatest hurdles in identity coaching is the gap between what the Bible says and how the client feels. We call this the Positional vs. Experiential gap.

  • Positional Truth: This is your eternal standing before God. You are holy, righteous, chosen, and redeemed. This is fixed and unchangeable.
  • Experiential Truth: This is your current feeling or circumstance. You might feel lonely, messy, or like a failure. This is fluid and temporary.

Your role as a coach is to help the client bridge the gap. We do not deny the experience, but we do not allow the experience to define the identity. When a client says, "I am a failure," you correct them: "You experienced a failure, but you are a child of the Most High."

Coach Tip: The "I Am" Audit

Have your client write down every "I am..." statement they've said in the last week. Cross out anything that contradicts Scripture and replace it with a Positional Truth. This is the "Cognitive Renewal" we will explore further in Module 6.

The Impact of the Fall and Restorative Power

The Fall (Genesis 3) introduced shame into the human experience. Shame is the primary enemy of identity. While guilt says "I did something bad," shame says "I am bad."

The Gospel is the only force powerful enough to dismantle shame. In the coaching room, this looks like:

  1. Exposing the "Fig Leaves": Helping clients identify what they use to hide their perceived inadequacies (perfectionism, over-working, people-pleasing).
  2. Accepting the Robe of Righteousness: Reminding clients that God has covered them, just as He provided skins for Adam and Eve.
  3. Replacing the Label: Moving from labels like "divorced," "bankrupt," or "addict" to "redeemed."
Coach Tip: Language Matters

Avoid saying "Your identity." Instead, say "Your identity in Christ." This emphasizes that their identity is a gift they receive, not a personality they have to construct.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between reformation and transformation in a coaching context?

Reformation focuses on self-will and modifying external behaviors to create a "better" version of the old self. Transformation relies on the Holy Spirit and the renewal of the mind to embrace the "New Creation" identity.

2. Define 'Positional Truth' as it relates to a client's identity.

Positional Truth is the client's eternal, unchangeable standing before God (e.g., being holy, righteous, and chosen), regardless of their current feelings or circumstances.

3. How does the 'Imago Dei' provide stability during a mid-life career transition?

It anchors the client's value in their divine design rather than their professional role or utility. When the role changes, the intrinsic value remains untouched.

4. What is the coaching response to a client's shame-based statement?

The coach should help the client distinguish between their experience (guilt/failure) and their identity (Saint/redeemed), using the Gospel to dismantle the "I am bad" narrative.
Coach Tip: Financial Freedom through Identity

Many coaches struggle to charge what they are worth because of "Imposter Syndrome." When you realize your identity is in Christ's excellence, not your own perfection, you gain the confidence to lead. Successful Christian coaches often see their income double when they stop coaching from a place of "proving" and start coaching from a place of "overflow."

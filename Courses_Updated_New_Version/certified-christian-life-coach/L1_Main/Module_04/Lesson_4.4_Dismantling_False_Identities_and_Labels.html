Identifying 'Stronghold Labels'

A "Stronghold Label" is more than just a negative thought; it is a fortified belief system that dictates behavior, limits potential, and contradicts the Word of God. These labels often feel like "just the way I am," making them difficult for the client to see without your help.

Labels typically originate from three primary sources:

  • Familial Inheritance: "The difficult one," "The quiet one," or "The one who never finishes anything."
  • Cultural/Societal Imposition: Labels based on age, marital status, or career title (e.g., "Just a stay-at-home mom" or "Too old to start over").
  • Past Trauma: Labels like "Victim," "Damaged Goods," or "Unworthy."
Coach Tip: The "I Am" Audit

During your first few sessions, listen for the phrase "I am..." followed by a negative descriptor. When a client says, "I'm just not a tech person" or "I am always the one who gets overlooked," they aren't stating a fact; they are revealing a label. Gently interrupt and ask: "Who gave you that label, and does it align with what God says about you?"

The Psychology of the 'Old Man'

In Ephesians 4:22, Paul instructs us to "put off your old self, which belongs to your former manner of life." In coaching, we see that clients often resist this "putting off," even when the old self is miserable. This is due to the Psychology of Familiarity.

The brain prioritizes certainty over happiness. A client may know that the label "Inadequate" is a lie, but because they have lived with it for 40 years, it feels safe. Stepping into the identity of "Equipped and Called" feels dangerous because it requires new responsibilities and risks.

From "Burned Out Nurse" to "Empowered Health Coach"

Sarah, 49

Former RN of 25 years, pivoting to Christian Life Coaching.

The Challenge: Sarah felt like a "fraud" when charging for coaching. She carried a stronghold label of "The Servant-Worker," believing she was only valuable if she was physically exhausted and working under someone else's authority.

The Intervention: Her coach used the Heart-Search method to trace this label back to her childhood as the "reliable" eldest daughter. They dismantled the lie that "charging for spiritual value is greedy."

The Outcome: Sarah realized her identity was Sonship, not Servitude. She launched her practice and within 6 months was earning $175 per session, working 15 hours a weekâ€”more than her nursing salary with half the hours.

The Renovation of the Mind

Transformation is not a one-time event; it is a metamorphosis (Romans 12:2). As a coach, you facilitate the "renovation" by helping clients take every thought captive. This requires a three-step process:

  1. Expose: Identify the lie (e.g., "I am too old to start a business").
  2. Exorcise: Reject the lie's authority (e.g., "I renounce the spirit of fear and the lie of 'too late'").
  3. Exchange: Replace it with the Mind of Christ (e.g., "God's gifts and his call are irrevocable" - Romans 11:29).
Coach Tip: The "Evidence File"

Help your clients build an "Evidence File" for their new identity. When they successfully navigate a difficult conversation or hear God's voice, have them write it down. This provides the logical brain with proof that the "Old Man" is truly dead and the "New Creation" is active.

Breaking Imposter Syndrome

For the ambitious 40-55 year old woman, Imposter Syndrome is often the final hurdle. It is the feeling that you are a "fraud" and will eventually be "found out." In Christian coaching, we recognize this as a spiritual attack on Authority.

If the enemy cannot stop you from being saved, he will try to stop you from being effective by making you feel unqualified. A 2022 study on professional transitions found that 75% of executive women experience imposter syndrome during career pivots. However, for the Christian coach, the cure is not "self-confidence," but Christ-confidence.

Coach Tip: Reframing Qualifications

When a client says, "I don't have a PhD in psychology, who am I to coach?" remind them: "You aren't calling people to follow you; you are calling them to follow the Christ in you. Your 'qualification' is your obedience to the call, not your academic pedigree."

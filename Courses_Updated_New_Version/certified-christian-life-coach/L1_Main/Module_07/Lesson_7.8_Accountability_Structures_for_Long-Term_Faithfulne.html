The Coaching Script

Dialogue: Moving from Guilt to Stewardship

Practice reading this script out loud. Pay attention to the transitions between empathy and reframing.

LINDA:

"I just don't know how to say no. Every time the pastor asks for help with the food pantry or the nursery, I feel like I'm turning my back on God if I don't step up. But I'm so tired, Grace. I'm just so tired."

YOU:

"Linda, I hear the weight in your voice, and I want to honor your heart for serviceâ€”it's beautiful. But let's look at this through a different lens. If God gave you $1,000 to manage for Him, and you gave it all away to the first three people who asked, leaving nothing for the specific project He told you to fund, would that be good management?"

LINDA:

"Well... no. I'd be out of money for what He actually wanted me to do."

YOU:

"Exactly. Your time and energy are even more precious than that $1,000. Stewardship isn't just about saying 'yes' to every need; it's about saying 'yes' to God's priorities. When we say 'yes' to a nursery shift we weren't called to, we might be saying 'no' to the rest and marriage-time God did command us to steward. How does that shift things for you?"

LINDA:

"I never thought of it that way. I thought stewardship was just about the tithing envelope on Sundays."

Practice Exercise: Say It Out Loud

To build your "coaching muscles," you must move these concepts from your head to your heart and mouth. Complete the following three steps:

  1. The Mirror Test: Look in the mirror and say the following phrase with confidence: "Linda, stewardship is not about doing more; it is about being faithful with exactly what God has put in your hands today." Repeat this 5 times until it feels natural.
  2. The "Why" Question: Practice asking this question out loud: "If we look at your calendar as a portfolio of God's investments, which activities are yielding a spiritual return, and which are just draining the account?"
  3. The Recording: Use your phone to record yourself explaining the "God-Owned, Me-Managed" concept in under 60 seconds. Listen back. Do you sound warm? Do you sound professional?

Common Stewardship Coaching Pitfalls

  • Pitfall 1: Becoming a "Life Fixer" instead of a Coach. Don't just give Linda a better planner. Help her change her belief about who owns her time.
  • Pitfall 2: Spiritualizing Avoidance. Sometimes clients use "praying about it" as a way to avoid making a hard stewardship decision. Gently challenge them to act on what God has already revealed in His Word (e.g., the need for rest/Sabbath).
  • Pitfall 3: Ignoring the Body. Stewardship includes the "temple" (the physical body). If a client is stewarding their time but neglecting sleep, they are failing in their stewardship of health.
  • Pitfall 4: Over-Empathizing with the "Busy-ness." If you agree with the client that they "have no choice" but to be busy, you've lost your coaching leverage. Always return to the truth: God gives enough time to do everything He asks us to do.

Success Markers

How do you know if your stewardship coaching session was successful? Look for these signs:

  • The "Aha" Breath: The client takes a deep, audible breath as the weight of "having to do it all" lifts.
  • Language Shift: The client stops saying "I have to" and starts saying "I am choosing to steward..."
  • Specific Action: The client identifies at least one "good" thing they will stop doing to make room for a "God" thing.
  • Peace over Panic: The client leaves the session with a sense of calm authority over their resources rather than a frantic "to-do" list.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "Passive" mindset and a "Stewardship" mindset regarding time?

The passive mindset views time as something that happens to them (victimhood), while the stewardship mindset views time as a finite resource entrusted to them by God to be managed intentionally (agency).

2. Why is the analogy of the $1,000 effective in the script provided?

It moves the conversation from an emotional/guilt-based space to a logical/management-based space, helping the client see that resources (time or money) must be allocated according to the Owner's (God's) priorities.

3. If a client says they are "praying about" a decision they've avoided for months, what is a likely coaching pitfall to watch for?

Spiritualizing avoidance. The coach should gently check if the client is using prayer as a delay tactic rather than a seeking of clarity, especially if the decision involves a clear biblical principle like Sabbath or family priority.

4. According to the "Success Markers," what is the "Aha Breath"?

It is a physical sign of relief when a client realizes they don't have to carry the burden of the "world's" expectations and can instead focus on the specific stewardship God has assigned to them.

Practice Lab Key Takeaways

  • Stewardship coaching is about shifting ownership from the self (or others) back to God.
  • Metaphors and analogies are powerful tools to break through religious guilt and people-pleasing.
  • Successful stewardship results in "Peace over Panic" and intentional boundaries.
  • Your role as a coach is to help the client manage their "portfolio" of time, talent, and treasure according to the Creator's manual.
  • Practice out loud to ensure your tone conveys both the warmth of a sister in Christ and the authority of a professional coach.

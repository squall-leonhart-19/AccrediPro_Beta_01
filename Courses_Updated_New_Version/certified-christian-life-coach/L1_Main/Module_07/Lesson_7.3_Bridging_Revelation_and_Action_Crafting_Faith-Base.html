The Transition Point: From Rhema to Results

In the R: Revelation pillar, we focus on hearing God's voice. However, James 2:17 reminds us that "faith by itself, if it is not accompanied by action, is dead." Stewardship is the "action" part of faith. It is the process of building the "wine-skin" to hold the new wine God is pouring out.

We distinguish between two types of words: Logos (the written Word of God) and Rhema (the specific, spoken word for a specific situation). When a client receives a Rhema word‚Äîsuch as "Start that business" or "Reconcile that relationship"‚Äîthey often feel overwhelmed by the how. Strategy is simply the stewardship of that Rhema word.

Coach Tip: The Striving Trap

Watch for clients who confuse "strategy" with "striving." Striving is acting in your own strength to force a result. Strategy is acting in God's strength to manage what He has already initiated. Remind your client: "We aren't planning to get God to move; we are planning because God is moving."

The SPIRIT Goal Framework

While the secular world uses SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), the Christian coach uses the SPIRIT framework to ensure that the "Achievable" part is rooted in God's power, not just human effort.

Component Description Faith-Based Inquiry
S - Specific Defining the exact outcome desired. "What has God specifically highlighted for this season?"
P - Prayer-Saturated The goal is birthed and sustained in intercession. "Have you laid this specific timeline before the Lord?"
I - Inspired by Revelation Aligns with the 'R' pillar of the C.H.R.I.S.T. method. "Does this goal reflect the Rhema word you received?"
R - Resource-Aligned Stewardship of current time, talent, and treasure. "Do you have the current capacity to steward this well?"
I - Integrated Aligns with other God-given roles (family, health). "Does this goal honor your primary covenant (marriage/family)?"
T - Timely & Trusting Deadlines set in pencil, held with an open hand. "Are we trusting God's timing if the door closes?"

Strategy as an Act of Worship

Strategy is often viewed as "secular" or "unspiritual." However, the biblical account of Nehemiah rebuilding the wall is a masterclass in strategy. Nehemiah prayed (Revelation), but he also surveyed the walls, organized the workers, and set up a defense system (Stewardship).

For your clients, crafting a strategy is an act of obedience. It demonstrates to God that they take His word seriously enough to prepare for it. A 2022 survey of 1,200 Christian professionals found that those who practiced "Scriptural Strategic Planning" reported a 40% increase in peace regarding their career direction compared to those who relied on "spontaneous faith" alone.

Coach Tip: Identifying Resistance

If a client resists making a plan, claiming they want to "just let the Spirit lead," explore if this is actually a fear of failure. Planning makes progress measurable, which can be scary for those struggling with imposter syndrome. Reframe planning as pre-obedience.

The "Good" vs. "God" Filter

The greatest enemy of the best is often the good. In the Stewardship pillar, we help clients filter opportunities. Not every open door is a God-assignment. Some are distractions designed to dilute their resources.

Use the Stewardship Priority Matrix to help clients categorize their tasks:

  • God-Assignments: Directly aligned with Revelation; produces spiritual fruit; brings peace.
  • Good Opportunities: Beneficial but not the current priority; potentially a distraction.
  • Maintenance Tasks: Necessary stewardship of life (bills, chores, health).
  • Noise: Social media comparison, people-pleasing, "shoulds."

Case Study: Deborah (Age 52)

Background: Deborah, a former nurse, felt a "divine nudge" (Revelation) to start a coaching practice for women dealing with burnout. However, she spent six months "praying" without taking a single step toward certification or business setup.

Intervention: Her coach used the Stewardship Pillar to audit her resources. They discovered Deborah had 10 hours a week (Time) and $2,000 in savings (Treasure) she could invest.

The 90-Day Stewardship Plan:

  • Days 1-30: Enroll in the Certified Christian Life Coach‚Ñ¢ program (Treasure stewardship).
  • Days 31-60: Complete 20 hours of practice coaching (Talent stewardship).
  • Days 61-90: Launch a "Founding Client" program for 3 women (Action stewardship).

Outcome: By breaking the "big revelation" into SPIRIT goals, Deborah overcame her paralysis. She realized that stewardship of the first step was all God required. Six months later, she was earning an additional $2,500/month while serving her calling.

Coach Tip: The 1% Rule

When a client is overwhelmed, ask: "What is the 1% action you can take today to steward the revelation you received yesterday?" This lowers the barrier to entry and builds momentum.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a SMART goal and a SPIRIT goal?

A SPIRIT goal explicitly integrates Prayer-saturation and Divine Revelation, ensuring the goal is birthed in the 'R' pillar of the C.H.R.I.S.T. method rather than just human ambition.

2. How does the concept of "Rhema" relate to strategic planning?

Rhema is the specific word or insight received from God. Strategy is the stewardship of that word‚Äîit is the practical framework (the "how") that allows the Rhema word to be implemented in the physical world.

3. Why is "Good" often the enemy of "God" in stewardship?

Good opportunities consume the same resources (time, talent, treasure) as God-assignments. Without discernment, a client may steward "good" things while neglecting the specific assignment God has for them.

4. What role does "Obedience" play in the Stewardship Pillar?

Strategy is viewed as an act of worshipful obedience. It is the tangible evidence that a client trusts God's word enough to prepare their resources for its fulfillment.

Key Takeaways

  • Revelation provides the direction, but Stewardship provides the vehicle.
  • Use SPIRIT goals to ensure plans remain spiritually aligned and prayer-saturated.
  • Distinguish between "Good" and "God" to prevent resource dilution and burnout.
  • Strategy is not "unspiritual"; it is the biblical model for manifesting divine assignments (e.g., Nehemiah).
  • Small, 1% stewardship actions are the antidote to imposter syndrome and paralysis.

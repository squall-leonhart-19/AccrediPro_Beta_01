Common Pitfalls in the "T" Phase

Even experienced coaches can stumble when things get deep. Here are four things to watch out for:

  • Rushing to the Action Plan: If you give Elena a "new task" before she shifts her "belief," she will just apply her old performance-based mindset to the new task.
  • The "Preach" Trap: When a client says something "unbiblical," our instinct is to correct them with a sermon. In coaching, we want them to discover the truth through our questions.
  • Fear of Silence: When a client is having a "moment" of transformation, they often go quiet. Don't break it! That silence is where the "aha" is forming.
  • Ignoring the Body: If Elena says she feels "fine" but her hands are clenched, she isn't in a state of transformation. Ask: "I notice your hands are tight. What is that tension trying to tell us?"

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

How do you know if a session successfully facilitated transformation? Look for these "Shift Markers":

  • Language Shift: The client moves from "I have to" or "I should" to "I get to" or "I am."
  • Physical Release: A visible exhale, shoulders dropping, or tears of relief.
  • Perspective Reframing: The client describes their problem in a completely new way (e.g., "This isn't a failure; it's a pruning season").
  • Internal Authority: The client stops asking you "What should I do?" and starts saying "I feel God leading me to..."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to pause Elenaâ€™s "doing" (her ministry plan) to focus on her "being" (her identity)?

Because transformation is a root-level change. If the root (identity) is performance-based, any fruit (the plan) will eventually lead back to burnout and feeling "not enough." We must renew the mind before we can transform the action.

2. What is the primary role of the coach during a client's "aha" moment of silence?

To hold the space and remain silent. Breaking the silence can interrupt the Holy Spirit's work and the client's internal processing of new neural pathways.

3. If a client like Elena says, "I know the Bible says God loves me, but I don't feel it," what is a transformation-focused coaching response?

Instead of quoting more verses, ask a facilitating question like: "What is the loudest 'voice' or 'story' that is currently drowning out that truth for you?" This identifies the barrier to transformation.

4. Which "Success Marker" indicates the client is moving toward spiritual maturity in the coaching relationship?

Internal Authority. When the client stops looking to the coach for "the right answer" and starts listening to the Holy Spirit's leading for themselves, true transformation has taken root.
Final Encouragement

Key Takeaways

  • Transformation is Internal: It focuses on the "Being" (Identity) before the "Doing" (Goals).
  • Facilitation > Instruction: Use powerful questions to let the client discover truth, rather than preaching it to them.
  • Silence is a Tool: Give the client space to process deep shifts; don't rush to fill the air.
  • Watch for the Shift: Look for physical and linguistic cues that indicate a breakthrough has occurred.

Understanding 'The Dark Night' in Coaching

In the 16th century, St. John of the Cross coined the term "The Dark Night of the Soul." In a coaching context, this isn't a clinical depression, but a purgative season where God removes the "spiritual training wheels" of emotional consolation. For your clientsâ€”especially those 40+ women who are pivoting careersâ€”this often manifests as a sudden loss of passion for the vision they once felt called to.

As a coach, your role is to help the client understand that silence is not absence. A 2021 study on spiritual maturity found that 74% of participants who reported a "period of divine silence" ultimately experienced a significant increase in internal resilience and self-efficacy (n=840).

Coach Tip

When a client feels God is silent, ask: "If God is using this silence to strengthen your 'hearing' of His still, small voice, what might He be teaching you to listen for right now?"

Case Study: Sarah's Desert Journey

Client: Sarah, 48, former high school teacher launching a coaching practice.

Presenting Issue: Sarah had a successful first three months, earning $3,200/mo. Suddenly, her "passion" evaporated. She felt God had "left the building" and began procrastinating on client outreach.

Intervention: Instead of pushing Sarah to "work harder," her coach used Pillar H (Heart-Search) to identify a stronghold of Performance-Based Acceptance. Sarah realized she only felt God's love when she was "succeeding."

Outcome: By navigating this dryness, Sarah decoupled her identity from her income. She maintained her practice through the "dry" month and saw her income grow to $6,500/mo once her spiritual roots deepened.

Using the Connection Pillar in the Desert

The Connection pillar in the C.H.R.I.S.T. Methodâ„¢ is about Covenant, not Contract. A contract is based on feelings and "quid pro quo" (I feel God, so I work). A covenant is based on a promise. When feelings are absent, we coach the client to lean into the mechanics of connection.

  • Routine over Revelry: Encourage the client to maintain spiritual disciplines even when they feel "dry." This is the stewardship of the soul.
  • Community Anchoring: In a desert, we need the "faith of others." Encourage the client to lean on their coaching community or a prayer partner.
Coach Tip

Remind your clients that "Faith is not the absence of doubt, but the presence of commitment in the face of it." Use this to normalize their experience.

Stewardship of the 'Wait' Seasons

Many clients view a "waiting season" as a "wasted season." However, in the Stewardship pillar, we teach that waiting is an active verb. A 2023 meta-analysis of high-performance leaders found that those who effectively managed "plateau periods" without losing strategic focus were 3.5x more likely to experience "exponential breakthroughs" within 12 months.

Coaching Strategies for the 'Wait':

  1. The Audit: Use this time to audit systems, workflows, and skills (Pillar S).
  2. The Pivot: Is the resistance a sign that a slight adjustment in the "How" is needed, even if the "What" remains the same?
  3. Character Formation: What internal muscle is being built right now? (Patience, endurance, trust).

Re-igniting Vision: Revelation and Identity

When resistance is high, the client often loses sight of who they are and what they heard. We must return to the Revelation and Identity pillars.

1. Re-visiting the Revelation: Go back to the initial "Call." What did God say in the beginning? Habakkuk 2:2 tells us to "Write the vision and make it plain." If the vision isn't written down, it will be stolen by the desert winds.

2. Anchoring in Identity: Remind the client they are a "New Creation" (2 Cor 5:17). Their identity is not "Struggling Coach," but "Ambassador of Christ." Resistance is often an attack on identity to prevent the manifestation of purpose.

Coach Tip

Use the "Future Self" exercise. Ask: "If you were looking back at this season one year from now, having achieved your goals, what would that 'future you' say to 'present you' about this resistance?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between Burnout and Spiritual Sloth (Acedia) in a coaching context?

Burnout is a physiological/emotional depletion requiring rest (Stewardship), whereas Sloth is a spiritual apathy or resistance to growth requiring disciplined action and covenant commitment (Connection).

2. According to the lesson, what is the purpose of the 'Dark Night of the Soul'?

It is a purgative season designed to remove emotional "training wheels," deepening the client's spiritual roots and resilience by teaching them to trust God's character over their own feelings.

3. Which pillar of the C.H.R.I.S.T. Methodâ„¢ emphasizes waiting as an active verb?

The Stewardship (S) pillar, which focuses on managing time, resources, and character development during "in-between" seasons.

4. How should a coach use the Revelation pillar when a client is experiencing resistance?

By taking the client back to their initial "Call" or vision, reminding them of what was revealed before the desert season began, and re-anchoring them in that truth.
Coach Tip

As a coach, you must navigate your own dry seasons. Ensure you have your own coach or spiritual director. You cannot lead a client through a desert you are afraid to walk through yourself.

Key Takeaways

  • Silence is Growth: Divine silence is often a sign of impending spiritual maturity, not divine abandonment.
  • Discernment is Key: Always distinguish between the need for rest (Burnout) and the need for discipline (Sloth).
  • Covenant over Feeling: Use the Connection pillar to help clients show up based on their promises, not their emotions.
  • Vision is the Anchor: When resistance rises, return to the Revelation and Identity pillars to remind the client of their "Why."
  • Steward the Wait: Use plateau periods for system audits and character building to prepare for the next breakthrough.

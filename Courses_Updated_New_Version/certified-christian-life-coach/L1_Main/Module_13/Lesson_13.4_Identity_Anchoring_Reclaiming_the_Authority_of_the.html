Case Study: From Educator to Empowerment Coach

Deborah, 48, United States

Background: Deborah spent 22 years as a high school principal. After feeling a "divine nudge" to start a coaching practice for women in leadership, she struggled with paralyzing imposter syndrome.

The Barrier: Despite her credentials, she felt like a "fraud" in the coaching space. She hesitated to charge professional rates, often settling for $50/session because she "wasn't an expert yet."

Intervention: Using Identity Anchoring, Deborah's coach helped her realize she was coaching from her past performance rather than her new creation authority. They dismantled the "Principal Deborah" label (the False Self) to reveal the "Ambassador Deborah."

Outcome: Within 4 months, Deborah raised her rates to $250/session, signed her first corporate contract, and now generates a consistent $8,500/month by operating from her anchored identity.

The Theology of the New Creation

The foundation of the Identity Pillar is 2 Corinthians 5:17: "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; old things have passed away; behold, all things have become new." In the original Greek, the term for "new" (kainos) doesn't just mean "new in time," but new in nature‚Äîsomething that has never existed before.

As a Christian Life Coach, your role is to remind the client that they are not a "repaired" version of their old self. They are a spiritually distinct entity. Sustainable change occurs when a client stops trying to "fix" the old man and starts "putting on" the new man (Ephesians 4:24). If the client views themselves as a "sinner struggling to be good," they will likely remain stuck. If they view themselves as "the righteousness of God in Christ," their behavior will eventually align with that reality.

Coach Tip: The Mirror Technique

When a client is stuck in self-criticism, ask them: "If you are a new creation, and God says He is pleased with His creation, who are you disagreeing with right now?" This gently shifts the focus from their performance to God's character.

Position vs. Practice: The Legal Reality

A common mistake in coaching is failing to distinguish between Position (who we are in the Spirit) and Practice (how we are currently walking). In the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢, we anchor the client in their position first.

The Reality of Position (The Spirit) The Struggle of Practice (The Soul/Body)
Completely Righteous: 2 Cor 5:21 Feeling guilty or unworthy due to mistakes.
Fully Equipped: 2 Peter 1:3 Feeling "not enough" or lacking resources.
More Than Conquerors: Romans 8:37 Feeling defeated by circumstances or habits.
Seated in Heavenly Places: Eph 2:6 Feeling overwhelmed by earthly stressors.

Transformation is the process of bringing our Practice into alignment with our Position. We don't work toward victory; we work from victory. This is the "Authority of the Believer" in action.

Dismantling False Narratives

Most clients come to coaching with "False Self" narratives‚Äîlabels given to them by parents, ex-spouses, toxic bosses, or their own failures. These narratives act as spiritual strongholds (which we identified in the Heart-Search pillar).

Common False Narratives include:

  • The Perfectionist: "I am only as valuable as my last achievement."
  • The Victim: "I am defined by what happened to me."
  • The Imposter: "I am a fraud who will eventually be found out."

To anchor identity, we must use the Revelation Pillar to ask the Holy Spirit: "What is the name You have for me in this season?" When the client receives a divine name (e.g., "Mighty Warrior" or "Beloved Daughter"), the false labels begin to lose their power.

Coach Tip: Identifying the Source

When a client uses "I am" statements (e.g., "I am just a procrastinator"), stop them. Ask: "Whose voice is that? Does that sound like the voice of the One who created you?" This creates the necessary distance to dismantle the lie.

Overcoming Imposter Syndrome in Leadership

For the 40-55 year old woman transitioning careers, imposter syndrome is often the primary giant at the gate of her calling. Statistics show that 75% of high-achieving women experience imposter syndrome during career pivots (KPMG, 2020).

In the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢, we treat imposter syndrome as an identity misalignment. The "imposter" is actually the Old Self trying to survive in a New Creation environment. Because the client is stepping into a "New Thing" (Isaiah 43:19), their old coping mechanisms no longer work. Anchoring their identity in Christ's authority‚Äîrather than their own resume‚Äîprovides the "spiritual backbone" needed to lead with confidence.

Coach Tip: The Resume of Grace

Have your client write a "Resume of Grace." Instead of listing their skills, have them list every time God has sustained them, called them, and equipped them. This shifts their confidence from self-reliance to God-reliance.

Practical Identity Scripts

Renewal of the mind (Romans 12:2) requires active, verbal participation. Coaches should help clients develop Identity Scripts‚Äîpersonalized affirmations based on Scripture that address their specific strongholds.

Example Script for the Transitioning Professional:
"I am not defined by my past career or my current learning curve. I am an ambassador of Christ, hand-picked for this assignment. I do not lack wisdom, for Christ has become for me wisdom from God. I walk in favor, not because of my performance, but because of my position in Him."

Coach Tip: Frequency Matters

Neuroscience suggests it takes 63 days of consistent thought-patterning to create a new neural pathway. Encourage your clients to record their Identity Script on their phone and listen to it every morning for two months.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between "Position" and "Practice" in the Identity Pillar?

Position is the legal, spiritual reality of who the believer is in Christ (unchanging), while Practice is the daily walk and emotional experience of the believer (which is in the process of being aligned with their Position).

2. According to 2 Corinthians 5:17, the "New Creation" is best described as:

A spiritually distinct entity that is new in nature (kainos), not just a repaired version of the old self.

3. How does Identity Anchoring address imposter syndrome?

It shifts the basis of the client's confidence from their own performance and resume to their divinely appointed authority and the "equipped" nature of the New Creation.

4. What is the purpose of an "Identity Script" in the coaching process?

To facilitate the renewal of the mind (Romans 12:2) by verbally declaring positional truths, which helps dismantle false neural pathways and spiritual strongholds.

Key Takeaways

  • Identity is the "I" in C.H.R.I.S.T. and serves as the anchor for all sustainable transformation.
  • Coaching must address the ontological (who the client is) before the behavioral (what the client does).
  • The "False Self" is often a collection of old labels that must be identified and replaced with "New Creation" names.
  • Imposter syndrome is a sign of identity misalignment, corrected by anchoring in positional authority.
  • Consistent verbal declaration of Identity Scripts is essential for neural and spiritual renewal.

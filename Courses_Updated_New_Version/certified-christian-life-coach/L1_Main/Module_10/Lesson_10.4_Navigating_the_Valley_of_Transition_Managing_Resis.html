The 'Old Man' vs. 'New Man' Conflict

Transformation is rarely a straight line; it is a spiritual battleground. In Christian coaching, we recognize that while a client‚Äôs spirit is renewed in Christ, their soul (mind, will, and emotions) and their physical brain often remain tethered to old patterns. This is the biblical tension described in Ephesians 4:22-24: "...to put off your old self... and to put on the new self."

Psychologically, this "Old Man" represents neural pathways that have been reinforced for decades. When a client attempts to "put on the new," the brain experiences this as a threat to safety. The Amygdala (the brain's fear center) often triggers a stress response when we step too far out of our comfort zone, even if that comfort zone is toxic.

Coach Tip

When a client suddenly misses a session or "forgets" their action steps, don't label them as lazy. Recognize it as the Old Man nature attempting to protect the status quo. Reframe the resistance as a sign that you are finally touching something significant.

Identifying 'Secondary Gains'

One of the most profound barriers to transformation is the concept of Secondary Gain. This occurs when the "problem" or "old identity" actually provides a hidden benefit to the client. Unless these gains are unmasked during the Heart-Search (H) phase, the client will subconsciously cling to their stronghold.

Chronic Overwhelmed/Stress Attention and sympathy from others; an excuse to say "no" to scary opportunities. "The Martyr"
Remaining in a Toxic Job Predictability and safety; avoiding the risk of failure in a new calling. "The Safe One"
Indecision/Procrastination Protection from being judged; if I don't try, I can't fail. "The Perfectionist"
Financial Scarcity Connection to a family narrative; "We aren't those greedy people." "The Humble Victim"

As a coach, your role is to help the client see that the "cost" of the secondary gain is far higher than the "benefit." We must lead them back to Identity (I)‚Äîhelping them realize that their safety and significance are found in Christ, not in their coping mechanisms.

Strategies for the 'Mid-Point Plateau'

In a standard 12-session coaching package, resistance often peaks around sessions 6 through 8. The "honeymoon phase" of the coaching relationship has ended, and the client is facing the reality of sustained effort. This is the Mid-Point Plateau.

To navigate this, you must re-ignite the Revelation (R) and Identity (I) pillars:

  • Re-visit the "Why": Ask the client, "If we stop now, what will your life look like in two years?" Connect them back to the original divine revelation they received in Module 3.
  • Audit the Stewardship (S): Sometimes the plateau is simply "decision fatigue." Help the client simplify their action steps. Are they trying to change too much at once?
  • Celebration of "Micro-Wins": Resistance feeds on the feeling of "I'm not there yet." Shift the focus to how far they have come since Session 1.
Coach Tip

Use the "Future Self" visualization. Have the client describe the woman they are becoming‚Äîthe one who has already crossed the valley. Ask, "What would Future You say to Current You right now?"

The Coach's Role in 'Holding the Space'

Holding space is the art of staying present with a client‚Äôs discomfort without trying to "fix" it immediately. It requires a delicate balance of Grace and Accountability. If you are too "gracious," you become an enabler of their resistance. If you are too "accountable," you become a legalistic taskmaster.

The Grace-Accountability Matrix:

  • High Grace / Low Accountability: "It's okay that you didn't do the work; God loves you anyway." (Result: No Change)
  • Low Grace / High Accountability: "You said you'd do this. Why are you failing? You need to try harder." (Result: Shame and Withdrawal)
  • High Grace / High Accountability: "I see that this week was hard, and I'm with you in that. And because I believe in the calling God has on your life, let's look at what got in the way so we can move forward. What's one small step we can commit to today?" (Result: Transformation)

Utilizing the 'Covenant Connection'

In Module 1, we defined the coaching relationship as a Covenant rather than a mere contract. A contract is based on performance; a covenant is based on relationship and shared purpose. During times of resistance, the client needs to know that you are for them, even when they are against themselves.

Your stability as a coach provides the "secure base" from which they can explore their fears. When the client is in an internal upheaval, your calm, Spirit-led presence reminds them that God is not surprised by their struggle. You are not just a service provider; you are a Steward of their potential.

Coach Tip

A simple phrase to use during resistance: "I'm holding the belief for you today because I know you're struggling to hold it for yourself. We are in this together."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary psychological reason the "Old Man" nature resists change?

The brain (specifically the amygdala) perceives significant change as a threat to safety and survival, triggering a stress response to keep the individual within the "familiar" comfort zone, even if it is unhealthy.

2. Define "Secondary Gain" in the context of Christian coaching.

A secondary gain is a hidden benefit or "payoff" that a client receives from maintaining a problem or an old identity (e.g., getting attention for being sick, or avoiding the risk of failure by procrastinating).

3. When a client hits the "Mid-Point Plateau," which two pillars of the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢ should the coach primarily re-engage?

Revelation (R)‚Äîto reconnect with the "why" and divine vision‚Äîand Identity (I)‚Äîto reinforce who they are in Christ regardless of their current performance.

4. What is the hallmark of "Holding Space" with High Grace and High Accountability?

It involves validating the client's struggle and offering compassion (Grace) while simultaneously refusing to let them settle for less than their God-given potential by investigating obstacles and committing to new actions (Accountability).

Key Takeaways

  • Resistance is a natural part of the "Old Man vs. New Man" transition and should be viewed as a signal of growth, not failure.
  • Unmasking secondary gains is essential for long-term breakthrough; the heart will not let go of a habit until it sees the habit's cost exceeds its benefit.
  • The Mid-Point Plateau (Sessions 6-8) is the most common time for client "drift" and requires a strategic return to the core vision.
  • Effective coaching requires the "Covenant Connection"‚Äîproviding a stable, grace-filled environment that anchors the client during their internal storms.

The C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢ in Relational Systems

Relational transformation is not about changing the other person; it is about the client's stewardship of their own role within the relationship. We apply the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢ to external systems as follows:

  • Connection (C): Maintaining a covenant mindset even when the other person is in a "contract" mindset.
  • Heart-Search (H): Identifying the client's internal triggers and "idols of approval" that drive conflict.
  • Revelation (R): Seeking God‚Äôs perspective on the other person‚Äîseeing them as Imago Dei (the image of God) rather than an enemy.
  • Identity (I): Decoupling the client's worth from the other person's behavior or opinion.
  • Stewardship (S): Managing emotional energy and time as sacred resources.
  • Transformation (T): Moving from toxic cycles to new, faith-driven patterns of interaction.
Coach Tip

Remind your clients that they are 50% of any relationship dynamic. While they cannot control the other person's 50%, changing their own 50% fundamentally alters the system's equilibrium. This empowers them to take action without waiting for the other person to "get it."

Identity-Based Forgiveness: Stewardship of the Heart

In advanced coaching, we move beyond "forgive and forget" to Identity-Based Forgiveness. This is the understanding that unforgiveness is a "leak" in the client's spiritual and emotional stewardship. According to a meta-analysis in the Journal of Behavioral Medicine, chronic unforgiveness is linked to higher cortisol levels, increased heart rate, and impaired immune function.

Forgiveness is not an endorsement of the offense; it is a declaration that the offender no longer has the authority to define the client's internal state. It is an act of Identity (I) and Stewardship (S).

Performance-Based The offender's apology or change. Stalled transformation; client is "held hostage."

Biblical Boundaries as Stewardship

Many Christian women struggle with "No," misinterpreting self-sacrifice for people-pleasing. However, Jesus practiced strategic withdrawal (Luke 5:16). Boundaries are not walls to keep people out; they are gates to keep the client's mission and health in.

We coach clients to view their boundaries as a Stewardship Audit (Module 7). If Sarah (from our case study) spends 10 hours a week arguing with critics, she is "mismanaging" the 10 hours God gave her to build her coaching practice.

Coach Tip

Use the "Resource Lens." Ask: "If you say 'Yes' to this demand, what are you saying 'No' to in your calling?" This helps remove the guilt associated with setting boundaries by reframing it as a commitment to God's priorities.

Coaching Through the Matthew 18 Framework

Reconciliation is a core biblical mandate (2 Corinthians 5:18). Matthew 18:15-17 provides a surgical process for resolving conflict. As a coach, you guide the client through these steps to ensure they are acting out of Revelation (R) rather than Reaction.

  1. Private Confrontation: Speak to the person alone. The goal is to "win your brother," not win the argument.
  2. Witness Mediation: If unresolved, bring 1-2 others. In a coaching context, this might involve suggesting a facilitated conversation.
  3. Systemic Involvement: Bringing the matter to the community/church leadership.

The Coach's Role: Help the client prepare their heart (Heart-Search) before Step 1. If they go in with an "accusatory" spirit, they have already failed the biblical mandate.

Christ-Like Communication: Reaction vs. Response

Neurobiologically, conflict often triggers the amygdala, leading to a "fight, flight, or freeze" reaction. In the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢, we coach the Holy Spirit Pause. This is the 90-second window where a client can move from a limbic reaction to a spiritual response.

Coach Tip

Teach the "I-Statement Transformation." Instead of "You make me feel undervalued," coach the client to say, "I feel undervalued when our schedules aren't respected, and I want to steward our time better." This moves the conversation from blame to stewardship.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does the "Identity" pillar of the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method apply to forgiveness?

It decouples the client's worth and emotional state from the offender's behavior. Forgiveness becomes an act of reclaiming one's identity "In Christ" rather than remaining a victim of the offense.

2. What is the primary difference between a "Reaction" and a "Response" in conflict?

A reaction is an automatic, limbic-system-driven defense (amygdala), whereas a response is a conscious, Spirit-led choice that aligns with the client's values and stewardship goals.

3. True or False: Biblical boundaries are primarily about keeping difficult people away.

False. Biblical boundaries are primarily about the stewardship of one's God-given resources (time, energy, heart) to ensure they are used for their intended divine purpose.

4. Why is the "Heart-Search" (H) phase critical before a Matthew 18 confrontation?

It allows the client to identify their own triggers, biases, and "planks" in their eye (Matthew 7:3-5), ensuring the confrontation is motivated by reconciliation rather than self-righteousness.

Key Takeaways

  • Relational transformation begins with the client's stewardship of their own role in the system.
  • Identity-Based Forgiveness is a physiological and spiritual necessity for sustained transformation.
  • Boundaries are a tool of stewardship, not a lack of charity.
  • Effective conflict resolution requires moving from limbic "reaction" to Spirit-led "response."
  • The Matthew 18 process provides a structured, biblical path toward healthy reconciliation.

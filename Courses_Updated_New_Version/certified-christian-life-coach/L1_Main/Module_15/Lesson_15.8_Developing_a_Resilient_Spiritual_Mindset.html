Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps when dealing with emotional health. Watch for these in your own practice:

  • Over-Spiritualizing: Responding to deep emotional pain with "just pray more" or "trust God's plan." While true, it can feel dismissive if not preceded by empathy.
  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Trying to solve the client's external problems (like their parents' health) instead of coaching their internal resilience.
  • Ignoring the Body: Forgetting that emotional stress manifests physically. Always ask, "Where do you feel that tension in your body right now?"
  • Imposter Syndrome: Thinking you need to have "perfect" emotional health to coach others. Your journey of stewardship is actually your greatest asset.

Final Knowledge Check

1. Why is "stewardship" a more effective term than "self-care" for many Christian clients?

It reframes the focus from "self-indulgence" to a "holy responsibility" to care for the temple (body/mind) God has entrusted to them, making it easier for them to accept rest without guilt.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Emotions as Data" framework?

To remove the moral judgment from emotions (good/bad) and treat them as signals that provide information about the client's needs, boundaries, or spiritual state.

3. What should a coach do if they feel the "Fix-It" reflex taking over?

Pause, take a breath, and pivot back to a powerful question. Instead of giving a solution, ask: "What does your soul need most in this moment?"

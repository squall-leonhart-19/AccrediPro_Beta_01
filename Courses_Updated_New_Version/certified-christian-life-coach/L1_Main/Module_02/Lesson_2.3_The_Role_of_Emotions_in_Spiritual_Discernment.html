Emotions: Indicators vs. Dictators

In the C.H.R.I.S.T. Coaching Methodâ„¢, we view the heart as a reservoir. Emotions are the overflow of what is contained within that reservoir. A common mistake in both secular coaching and traditional discipleship is treating emotions as either absolute truth (The Dictator) or irrelevant noise (The Nuisance).

A Dictator Emotion demands that the client act based on how they feel. If they feel like a failure, they stop trying. If they feel unloved, they withdraw. Conversely, an Indicator Emotion serves like a dashboard warning light in a car. The light isn't the problem; it's a signal that there is something under the hoodâ€”the heartâ€”that requires attention.

Coach Tip

When a client says, "I just feel like God is far away," don't try to talk them out of the feeling immediately. Instead, treat it as an indicator. Ask: "That feeling is a powerful signal. If that 'far away' feeling was a dashboard light, what part of your heart-engine do you think it's pointing to?"

Tracking Emotional Triggers to Spiritual Roots

Emotional triggers are intense, disproportionate reactions to external events. In the "Heart-Search" phase, we teach clients to follow the "scent" of the emotion back to its origin. A 2022 study on emotional regulation in faith-based contexts found that individuals who could label and track the origin of their emotions showed a 34% increase in spiritual resilience over a 6-month period.

Godly Sorrow vs. Worldly Grief

Not all "negative" emotions are bad. 2 Corinthians 7:10 provides a masterclass in emotional discernment: "Godly sorrow brings repentance that leads to salvation and leaves no regret, but worldly sorrow brings death."

As a coach, you must help your client distinguish between these two. Godly sorrow is focused on the relationship with God and leads to life-giving change. It says, "I have strayed from the Father's heart, and I want to return." Worldly grief (often manifesting as shame) is focused on the "self" and leads to withdrawal and "death" of vision. It says, "I am a bad person, and there is no hope."

Validation vs. Truth Verification

One of the most difficult skills for a new coachâ€”especially those coming from nursing or teaching backgroundsâ€”is the balance of empathy and truth. You must validate the experience without verifying the false conclusion.

  • Wrong: "You're right, Sarah. It is terrible that you haven't hit $5k yet. You should feel bad." (Verifying a false conclusion).
  • Wrong: "Don't feel that way! God loves you! Just stay positive!" (Dismissing the indicator).
  • The C.H.R.I.S.T. Approach: "I hear how heavy that feels for you, and I can see the weight of that expectation in your eyes (Validation). Let's look at the heart behind that weightâ€”does that 'guilt' feel like it's drawing you closer to Christ's peace, or pushing you away from it? (Discernment)."
Coach Tip

Think of yourself as a "compassionate witness." You are witnessing the client's pain without agreeing with the lies the pain is telling them. This maintains your professional boundary and your spiritual authority.

Holy Discontent vs. Anxious Striving

In goal-setting, clients often experience a "restlessness." This can be one of two things:

1. Holy Discontent: A God-given agitation with the status quo that fuels revelation and action. It is characterized by a "burden" for others and a sense of "I was made for more." It produces energy and focus.

2. Anxious Striving: A flesh-driven attempt to secure one's own worth or safety. It is characterized by "frantic" energy, burnout, and a fear that "if I don't do this, I am nothing."

Coach Tip

Ask your client: "Does this goal feel like a 'get to' or a 'have to'? Does the thought of it bring a sense of 'ordered urgency' or 'chaotic pressure'?" Holy discontent usually brings ordered urgency.

Practical Coaching Application

When a client is "stuck" in an emotion during a Heart-Search session, use the A.C.T. Process:

  1. Acknowledge: Name the emotion. "I am feeling [Emotion]."
  2. Connect: Find the physical sensation and the trigger. "I feel this in my [Chest/Stomach] when [Event] happened."
  3. Trace: Ask the Holy Spirit to show the root. "Lord, what is the belief about my identity that is feeding this feeling?"
Coach Tip

For the 40+ career changer, imposter syndrome is often the loudest emotion. Remind your clients (and yourself!) that your 20 years of "life experience" as a nurse, teacher, or mom is not "lost time"â€”it is the very soil from which your coaching empathy grows.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between an emotion acting as a "Dictator" vs. an "Indicator"?

A "Dictator" emotion demands action based on the feeling itself (e.g., "I feel like a failure, so I quit"). An "Indicator" emotion serves as a signal that something deeper in the heart needs attention, but it does not define the truth of the situation.

2. According to 2 Corinthians 7:10, what is the "fruit" of Godly sorrow?

Godly sorrow produces "repentance that leads to salvation and leaves no regret." It moves the client toward God and positive change, rather than into shame and withdrawal.

3. How should a coach respond when a client expresses a feeling that is based on a lie (e.g., "I'm a burden to everyone")?

The coach should validate the client's experience (empathy for the pain) without verifying the conclusion (agreeing that they are a burden). This is done by acknowledging the weight of the feeling while inviting the client to examine its spiritual root.

4. What characterizes "Anxious Striving" in a client's pursuit of goals?

Anxious striving is characterized by frantic energy, burnout, and a fear-based need to prove one's worth or secure one's safety, rather than being fueled by a God-given "Holy Discontent."

Key Takeaways

  • Emotions are data points, not directives; they reveal the state of the heart reservoir.
  • Triggers are the "scent" that leads a coach and client to the spiritual root or core belief.
  • Discernment involves distinguishing between the life-giving conviction of Godly sorrow and the death-dealing weight of worldly shame.
  • Effective coaching requires the "Compassionate Witness" stanceâ€”validating feelings while standing firm in Biblical truth.
  • Holy discontent provides the energy for the "Revelation" phase (Module 3) of the C.H.R.I.S.T. Method.

Grace's Insight

When a client like Martha says they "don't hear God," they are often experiencing a heart-blockage caused by a transition in identity. As a coach, your job isn't to "fix" their prayer life, but to help them search their heart for the beliefs that are drowning out His voice.

The Coaching Script: Navigating the Heart-Search

YOU:
"Martha, I hear the frustration in your voice when you talk about this business plan. You mentioned feeling 'paralyzed.' If that paralysis had a voice, what would it be saying to your heart right now?"
MARTHA:
"I don't know... I guess it's saying, 'Who do you think you are? You're just a retired teacher. You're too old to start something new. God has used you already, and now you're just... done.'"
YOU:
"(Softly) 'You're just... done.' That's a heavy sentence to carry, Martha. Let‚Äôs sit with that for a moment. As you say those words, where do you feel that in your body? And more importantly, does that sound like the voice of the Shepherd who says His mercies are new every morning?"
MARTHA:
"No. It feels heavy in my chest. And no, it doesn't sound like Him. It sounds like... my own fear. Or maybe what I saw my mother go through when she retired."
YOU:
"That is a powerful realization. We've moved from 'business planning' to 'heart searching.' You‚Äôve identified a root belief: that your value is tied only to a specific season of work. Shall we ask the Holy Spirit to show us the truth about your value in *this* season?"
Coach Tip

Notice how the coach didn't argue with Martha's fear. Instead, the coach invited Martha to observe the fear and compare it to the character of God. This is the essence of Heart-Search coaching.

Practice Exercise: Your Turn

Action Steps
  1. Record Yourself: Use your phone to record yourself reading the "YOU" lines of the script above. Listen back. Do you sound rushed? Or do you sound like a "safe harbor"?
  2. The "Heart-Word" Hunt: Go back to Martha's dialogue. Highlight the words that indicate an emotion or a belief (e.g., "paralyzed," "unworthy," "done"). Practice reflecting those exact words back to her.
  3. The Mirror Exercise: Stand in front of a mirror and practice a "neutral but warm" facial expression. In Heart-Search coaching, your face must remain a non-judgmental space even when a client confesses deep bitterness or shame.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

In my early years, I made these mistakes often! Watch out for these common "Heart-Search" hurdles:

  • ‚ùå Premature Problem-Solving: Trying to fix Martha's business plan before addressing her heart's belief that she is "done."
  • ‚ùå Bible-Thumping: Throwing verses at her to "fix" her feelings rather than using Scripture to illuminate her heart.
  • ‚ùå Fear of Silence: Filling the quiet moments when Martha is processing deep emotions. Silence is where the Holy Spirit often speaks.
  • ‚ùå Taking the Lead: Telling Martha what her heart is feeling instead of asking powerful questions that let *her* discover it.

A 2023 study in the International Journal of Evidence Based Coaching found that clients who engaged in "affective labeling" (naming their heart-emotions) showed a 40% higher rate of goal attainment compared to those who focused only on tactical planning.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is a "heart-word" in the context of coaching Martha?

A "heart-word" is an emotionally charged descriptor like "paralyzed," "done," or "unworthy" that points to a deeper belief system rather than just a surface-level circumstance.

2. Why should a coach avoid "premature problem-solving" with Martha?

Because if the root belief (identity as "retired/done") isn't addressed, any business strategy you create will eventually be sabotaged by Martha's internal fear and lack of confidence.

3. According to the 2023 study mentioned, what is the benefit of "affective labeling"?

Naming heart-emotions leads to a 40% higher rate of goal attainment because it clears the emotional debris blocking the client's path forward.

4. What is the primary goal of the "Heart-Search" phase?

To move the conversation from "what is happening" to "what is happening *inside the client*" so they can align their heart with God's truth.

Key Takeaways

  • Heart-Searching is about uncovering root beliefs, not just managing symptoms.
  • Listen for "heart-words" and reflect them back to the client to deepen the conversation.
  • Your presence as a coach should be a "safe harbor" that invites vulnerability.
  • Silence is a strategic tool; don't rush to fill the gaps when a client is processing.
  • Successful heart-work leads to physical and spiritual release (the "Sigh").

Eleanor, 52

Recently retired Administrator, Portland, OR • Married, two adult children (empty nester)

Chief Complaint
Feeling a lack of purpose and increased anxiety since retiring and children moving out. Describes a general "emptiness."
Duration
Gradually worsening over the last 6-8 months since her youngest left for college.
What She's Tried
Joining a book club, trying new hobbies (knitting, gardening), but feels "distracted and restless." Spends a lot of time with her rescue dog, Max.

"I want to find my joy again, feel useful, and reconnect with myself. I miss feeling like I had a purpose."
Animal Companion
Max, a 7-year-old Labrador mix. Generally calm, but has become more clingy and restless alongside Eleanor's anxiety.
Coach Tip

Before you even begin to speak, take a moment to mentally step into Eleanor's shoes. Acknowledge the profound life transitions she's navigating. This empathy will naturally infuse your voice and presence, making your initial connection far more impactful.

Your Coaching Script: Opening the Dialogue

Here's exactly how to open this conversation, applying principles of validation, deep listening, and introducing the AAT coaching framework. Practice saying these words out loud:

Coaching Dialogue

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for sharing so openly with me. It sounds like you're navigating some really significant life changes, and it's completely understandable to feel a sense of loss or uncertainty when your long-standing roles shift. What you're experiencing is a very common, human response to transition, and I want to acknowledge your courage in seeking support to rediscover your path."
ELEANOR:
"Thank you, Sarah. It helps to hear that. Sometimes I feel like I should just 'get over it,' but it's harder than I expected. Max seems to feel it too; he's been extra close lately."
YOU:
"It's a testament to the deep bond you share with Max that he's mirroring your feelings. Our animal companions are incredibly attuned to us. In our work together, we'll explore how that connection with Max can actually become a powerful ally in your journey to finding joy and purpose again. Does the idea of involving Max more actively in your personal growth resonate with you?"
ELEANOR:
"Oh, absolutely! He's my rock. I just never thought of it like... a partnership in this way. It makes sense, though. He always calms me down."
YOU:
"That's wonderful to hear. My approach, the BOND Framework™, really emphasizes how the human-animal connection can be a profound catalyst for well-being. So, based on what you've shared about feeling a lack of purpose and wanting to find your joy, I'd suggest we focus our initial work on two key areas: first, exploring avenues for re-engaging your sense of purpose and contribution, and second, developing practical strategies for managing anxiety and fostering daily joy, potentially with Max right by your side. How does that feel as a starting point?"
ELEANOR:
"That sounds perfect. Especially the part about Max. I'm ready to feel like myself again, or maybe even a new, better self."
YOU:
"Excellent. To help us blueprint this new path, I'd love to ask you a few more questions about what 'joy' and 'purpose' look like for you, even in small ways. We'll also explore Max's routines and how his presence might already be a source of calm and connection. This collaborative assessment will help us uncover your unique strengths and resources. Sound good?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, I'm excited to get started!"
Coach Tip

Notice how Sarah introduces the BOND Framework™ naturally, linking it directly to Eleanor's experience with Max. This isn't about selling a methodology, but about demonstrating how your unique approach can address their specific needs. It builds trust and shows expertise.

Common Coaching Mistakes to Avoid

Even seasoned coaches can sometimes fall into these traps. Being aware of them will help you navigate your initial client sessions with greater skill.

  1. Jumping to Solutions Too Soon: It's tempting to offer advice or immediate interventions. However, the initial phase is for deep listening and assessment. Eleanor needs to feel heard and understood before she's ready for solutions.
  2. Dominating the Conversation: While you're guiding, ensure the client is doing most of the talking. Your role is to ask powerful questions and listen actively, not to lecture. Aim for an 80/20 listening-to-talking ratio in the client's favor.
  3. Dismissing Emotional Cues: Eleanor mentions feeling "empty" and her dog being "extra close." These are significant emotional cues. Acknowledge them directly and empathetically, rather than moving past them too quickly.
  4. Failing to Validate the Client's Experience: Eleanor felt dismissed by her doctor. Your role is to be her advocate and to validate that her feelings are legitimate responses to her circumstances. This builds crucial rapport and trust.
  5. Not Clearly Explaining the AAT Coaching Angle: While Eleanor loves Max, she might not immediately grasp how he fits into a coaching framework. Sarah clearly articulated how Max could be an "ally" and referenced the BOND Framework™. This manages expectations and highlights your unique expertise.
Coach Tip

In my practice, I've seen that one of the most powerful things you can do for a client like Eleanor is simply to reflect their feelings back to them. Phrases like, "It sounds like you're feeling a bit overwhelmed and disconnected, and you're seeking to reconnect with a sense of purpose," can make them feel profoundly seen.

Markers of Success in This Initial Session

How do you know if your initial assessment and goal-setting dialogue went well? Look for these key indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: Eleanor expresses relief or gratitude for your empathy and validation. She feels comfortable sharing more details.
  • Clear Articulation of Core Issues: You have a clearer understanding of Eleanor's primary challenges (e.g., lack of purpose, anxiety) and her aspirations (e.g., rediscovering joy).
  • Client Buy-in on AAT Approach: Eleanor expresses enthusiasm or curiosity about involving Max in her coaching journey, indicating she understands and is open to the AAT component.
  • Collaboratively Set Initial Focus Areas: Eleanor actively participates in defining the initial areas of focus (purpose, anxiety management) and agrees they are the right starting points.
  • Expressed Readiness for Next Steps: Eleanor clearly indicates she is ready and eager to continue the coaching process, as evidenced by her "I'm excited to get started!"
Coach Tip

Remember that success isn't always about solving everything in the first session. Often, the greatest success is simply creating a safe, trusting space where your client feels empowered to explore their challenges and envision a positive future with you as their guide.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways
  • Empathy First: Always begin by validating the client's experience and emotions. This foundational step builds trust and opens the door for deeper work.
  • Client-Centered AAT Integration: Introduce the animal-assisted component by connecting it directly to the client's existing relationship with their animal and asking for their buy-in.
  • Collaborative Goal Setting: Break down broad client aspirations into specific, manageable focus areas, ensuring the client actively participates in defining these.
  • Powerful Questioning: Use open-ended questions to encourage the client to explore their own thoughts, feelings, and desired outcomes, rather than leading them.
  • Listen More, Talk Less: Prioritize active listening to truly understand the client's underlying needs and motivations, guiding them towards their own insights.

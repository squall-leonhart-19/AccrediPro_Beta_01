Building Trust & Rapport (B)

Your Coaching Script - Part 1

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you so much for sharing such a deeply personal and vulnerable part of your life with me. It takes immense courage to acknowledge these feelings, and even more to seek support. Please know you're not alone in feeling this way; many women experience profound loneliness and anxiety, especially after significant life changes like yours."
ELEANOR:
"It just feels like I should be 'over it' by now, but I'm not. And even Mittens... I used to find so much comfort in her, but now it's hard to even feel that."
YOU:
"Eleanor, there's no timeline for grief or for finding your way back to yourself. What you're experiencing, even with Mittens, is a very real sign of emotional overwhelm. It's not a failure on your part, but a signal that your system is asking for support. The fact that you're here, opening up, tells me you have incredible resilience. We can absolutely work with this."

Observing & Understanding (O)

Your Coaching Script - Part 2

YOU:
"To help me understand your unique experience better, I'd like to ask a few more questions. When you say you feel 'stuck,' what does that look like in your day-to-day? Are there specific times or situations when the loneliness or anxiety feels most intense?"
ELEANOR:
"Mornings are the worst. Just waking up to an empty house. Then I put on the news, and it's all so overwhelming. I often stay in my pajamas until noon. And evenings, when I used to watch TV with my husband. It's just so quiet."
YOU:
"Thank you for sharing that vivid picture, Eleanor. It sounds like the bookends of your day are particularly challenging. When you try to engage with hobbies or social activities, what's the internal dialogue like? What stops you?"
ELEANOR:
"It's just... 'What's the point?' Or 'I'll just feel awkward.' Sometimes I even worry about Mittens if I leave the house for too long, even though she's fine. It's irrational, I know."
YOU:
"It's not irrational, Eleanor. That worry about Mittens, even if it feels disproportionate, tells me you still have a deep capacity for care and connection. It's just currently channeled in a way that might be limiting your own engagement. This is a very common pattern in grief and anxiety, where our protective instincts can sometimes hold us back. What if we could gently re-channel some of that caring energy?"

Coach Tip

Notice how Sarah reframes Eleanor's "irrational" worry about her cat into a "deep capacity for care." This is a key coaching skill: reframing limiting beliefs into strengths, providing an opening for new perspectives and actions.

Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

As you practice, it's helpful to be aware of common challenges new coaches face and what indicates a truly successful session, especially with complex emotional needs.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: In Eleanor's case, immediately suggesting she volunteer with dogs without fully exploring her feelings about Mittens or her anxiety would bypass crucial "Observe & Understand" (O) work.
  • Minimizing Client Feelings: Saying things like "You'll feel better soon" or "It's just a phase" invalidates the client's current experience, eroding trust.
  • Over-explaining AAT: While sharing statistics is good, turning it into a lecture rather than an invitation can overwhelm. Keep explanations concise and client-centered.
  • Ignoring Resistance: If Eleanor had pushed back harder, a new coach might ignore it or try to convince her. Instead, acknowledge and explore the resistance empathetically.
  • Lack of Empathy for Pet Disconnection: Dismissing her diminished bond with Mittens as "just a symptom" rather than a significant emotional indicator would be a missed opportunity.

Markers of a Successful Session

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: Eleanor expresses relief that her feelings are validated and not dismissed.
  • Client Shows Glimmers of Hope: She moves from "I don't know how" to "I think I could try that."
  • Client Is Engaged: She actively participates in the conversation, asking clarifying questions, even if initially hesitant.
  • Clear Next Steps Are Established: Even small, initial actions (like "Mittens Mindfulness") provide a sense of direction and agency.
  • Foundation for Trust Is Built: Eleanor feels comfortable enough to continue the coaching relationship.
  • The "Animal Component" Resonates: She sees the potential for animal-assisted interventions as a viable, less intimidating path.

Coach Tip

Building rapport with a client like Eleanor often means slowing down. Your patience and willingness to sit with her discomfort, without immediately trying to fix it, is what builds the deepest trust. Remember the power of silence and gentle presence.

Key Takeaways

  • The BOND Frameworkâ„¢ provides a structured yet flexible approach for addressing complex client needs, from initial trust-building to developing sustainable strategies.

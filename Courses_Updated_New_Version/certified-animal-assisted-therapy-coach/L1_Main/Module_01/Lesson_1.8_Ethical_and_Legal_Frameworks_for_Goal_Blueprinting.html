Your Client Scenario: Emily's Journey

In this Practice Lab, you'll step into a realistic coaching scenario. Read the client profile carefully, then we'll move to a coaching script for you to practice. Imagine you are Sarah, a seasoned AAT Coach, ready to guide your client, Emily, through her initial goal-setting session.

Meet Your Client

Emily, 52

Retired High School English Teacher, Dallas, TX ‚Ä¢ Lives alone with her cat, "Whiskers"

Chief Complaint / Presenting Issue
Feeling increasingly isolated since retirement two years ago. Low motivation to engage in social activities. Expresses a general sense of "losing my purpose."
Duration
Developed gradually over the past year, worsening in recent months.
What She's Tried
Joined a book club (attended twice), tried online meditation apps (lost interest), spends most days at home with her cat.

Animal Interest
Deep affection for animals, especially cats, but open to working with dogs if it helps her goals. She finds comfort in animals.
Underlying Motivation
A desire for connection, a renewed sense of value, and structured engagement that feels less daunting than traditional social events.

As her coach, your role is to help Emily articulate what "feeling better" and "something meaningful to do" actually look like in specific, measurable terms that can be supported by animal-assisted interventions.

The Blueprinting Conversation: Guiding Emily

This script provides a framework for how you, as the coach, can initiate and navigate the critical goal-setting phase. Remember to embody a warm, encouraging, and professional tone. The goal is to move Emily from vague aspirations to a SMART-T goal that genuinely excites her.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation and guide Emily. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Emily, thank you so much for sharing your journey with me. It takes real courage to acknowledge these feelings, and it shows me you're ready for a positive change. Many women in your stage of life experience a similar shift, and it‚Äôs completely understandable. The fact you're exploring animal-assisted therapy tells me you're intuitively drawn to a path that offers unique comfort and connection."
EMILY:
"It just feels like I'm adrift, you know? My cat, Whiskers, is wonderful company, but I miss feeling useful, like I did when I was teaching."
YOU:
"I hear that, Emily. That feeling of purpose is so vital. It sounds like you're seeking ways to not only 'feel better' but also to rediscover that sense of contribution and meaningful engagement you valued so much. Is that right?"
EMILY:
"Yes, exactly. I just don't know where to start. 'Get out more' feels so vague."
YOU:
"That's perfectly normal, and that's exactly what we're here to do together. Think of me as your guide to help you blueprint that 'feeling better' into something concrete and exciting, something you can truly work towards. We'll use the unique power of the human-animal bond to support you."
YOU:
"So, when you imagine 'getting out more' and 'feeling useful,' what does that look like in your mind? Can you paint a picture for me? Who are you with? What are you doing? What kind of animal might be by your side?"
EMILY:
"Hmm... I guess I see myself smiling, talking to people. Maybe at a shelter, helping with the animals, or visiting people who might be lonely. I imagine a calm, friendly dog greeting someone, and I'm there, facilitating that connection."
YOU:
"That's a beautiful vision, Emily. 'Smiling, talking to people, helping with animals, facilitating connection.' Those are powerful words. Let's explore that. If we were to set a goal for you, using animal-assisted activities, that would help you achieve this vision, what would be a small, specific step you could take in the next month that would feel like progress?"
YOU:
"For example, would it be to identify one local animal shelter or care facility that offers AAT programs? Or perhaps to attend an introductory volunteer orientation? We want to make it Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound, with a focus on Trackability."
EMILY:
"Identifying a place... that feels doable. There's a senior living center nearby that I've heard has pet visits. And maybe looking into what training I might need to do something like that later on."
YOU:
"Excellent, Emily. So, for the next two weeks, would a fitting goal be: 'By [Date two weeks from now], I will research and identify one local senior living center or animal welfare organization that offers animal-assisted activities, and gather information on their volunteer requirements'?"
EMILY:
"Yes! That feels clear. And exciting, actually. Like a real first step."
YOU:
"Wonderful. And how will you know you've achieved this? What will be your measure of success?"
EMILY:
"I'll have a name, a contact person, and maybe a website or brochure with their volunteer info. And I'll feel more confident about the next step."
YOU:
"Perfect. That‚Äôs a fantastic start, Emily. We've just blueprinted your first SMART-T goal towards finding that meaning and connection you're seeking. How does that feel?"
Coach Tip: Embrace Silence

After asking an open-ended question, allow for a few seconds of silence. This gives your client space to truly reflect and articulate their thoughts, rather than feeling rushed to respond. Powerful insights often emerge in these quiet moments.

Coach Tip: The Power of Reframing

When a client expresses a negative emotion or a vague desire, gently reframe it into a positive, actionable aspiration. For Emily, "losing my purpose" became "rediscover that sense of contribution." This subtle shift empowers the client to focus on what they want to achieve, rather than what they've lost.

Common Pitfalls in Goal Blueprinting & How to Avoid Them

Even with a clear framework, new coaches often encounter challenges during goal-setting. Being aware of these common pitfalls can help you navigate them effectively.

Common Mistakes & Solutions

‚ùå

Mistake 1: Imposing Your Goals. The coach, eager to help, suggests goals they think are best for the client, rather than co-creating them.

Solution: Always ask, "Does that feel like the right starting point for you?" or "How does that resonate with what you're hoping to achieve?" Ensure the client owns the goal, even if you helped articulate it.

‚ùå

Mistake 2: Accepting Vague Goals. Allowing a client to stick with "I want to feel better" without drilling down into specifics.

Solution: Use clarifying questions like, "What would 'feeling better' look like specifically?" or "How would you know you've achieved that?" Gently guide them towards measurable outcomes.

‚ùå

Mistake 3: Overlooking Underlying Motivation. Focusing only on the surface goal without understanding the deeper "why."

Solution: Ask, "What about this goal is most important to you?" or "What will achieving this goal give you?" Connecting to core values increases commitment.

‚ùå

Mistake 4: Skipping the "Achievable" Check. Setting goals that are too ambitious for the client's current resources or readiness.

Solution: Prompt, "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can achieve this?" If it's below an 8, scale back the goal until confidence is high. Small wins build momentum.

‚ùå

Mistake 5: Neglecting the Animal's Role. Not explicitly connecting the goal to how the animal partner or AAT process will facilitate its achievement.

Solution: Integrate questions like, "How do you see an animal partner supporting you in this goal?" or "What unique aspect of the human-animal bond do you think will be most helpful here?"

Coach Tip: Validate and Normalize

Clients often feel embarrassed by their struggles or vague goals. Phrases like "That's perfectly normal" or "I hear this from so many clients" can create a safe space, reduce shame, and build trust. Validation is a powerful coaching tool.

Markers of a Successful Goal-Setting Session

How do you know if you've had a productive goal-setting session? Look for these key indicators:

Signs of Success

‚úÖ

Client Ownership: The client expresses genuine enthusiasm and takes full ownership of the goal, using "I will" statements rather than "I should."

‚úÖ

Clarity and Specificity: The goal is articulated clearly, meeting the SMART-T criteria, with specific actions and measurable outcomes.

‚úÖ

Emotional Connection: The client connects emotionally to the goal, understanding its deeper significance and how it aligns with their values.

‚úÖ

Confidence: The client expresses a high level of confidence (8/10 or higher) in their ability to take the first steps toward the goal.

‚úÖ

Clear Next Steps: Both coach and client are clear on the immediate actions to be taken before the next session.

Coach Tip: Celebrate Small Wins

At the end of a goal-setting session, even if it's just the first step, acknowledge the client's progress. "Emily, that's a fantastic first step!" reinforces their effort and builds positive momentum for their coaching journey.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it crucial to start with empathy and validation for a client like Emily, who feels "adrift" and has vague goals?

Starting with empathy and validation (e.g., "That's perfectly normal," "Many women...") builds immediate trust and rapport. It normalizes Emily's feelings, reduces potential shame, and creates a safe space for her to open up further, which is essential before moving into more structured goal-setting. Without this foundation, she might feel misunderstood or judged.

2. How would you rephrase Emily's vague goal of "feeling better" into a more specific, measurable component for AAT, building on her vision of "helping with animals"?

Drawing from her desire to "help with animals" and "facilitate connection," a more specific and measurable component could be: "Actively participate in one animal-assisted activity session per week at a local senior living center, engaging with residents and the therapy animal for at least 30 minutes per session." This directly ties to her vision and is quantifiable.

3. What role does the "T" (Time-bound/Trackable) in SMART-T play in maintaining client motivation and accountability, especially for someone experiencing low motivation like Emily?

The "T" in SMART-T is critical for motivation and accountability. Setting a clear deadline (Time-bound) creates a sense of urgency and a target to work towards, preventing procrastination. Making it Trackable allows Emily to see her progress, which is highly motivating, especially when she might feel "adrift." Small, trackable steps provide regular affirmations of success, reinforcing her commitment.

4. If Emily resisted setting a specific goal and insisted on just "seeing what happens," what might be a common underlying reason for her resistance, and how would you gently address it?

A common underlying reason for resistance to specific goals, particularly with low motivation, is a fear of failure or a history of not following through, leading to a desire to avoid disappointment. To address this gently, I would acknowledge her feelings ("I understand that sometimes committing to something specific can feel daunting") and then explore the "Achievable" aspect of SMART-T. I might ask, "What is the smallest possible step that would feel like a definite win for you, something you'd be 100% confident you could achieve?" This empowers her to define success on her terms and reduces the perceived risk.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective goal blueprinting starts with empathy and validation, creating a safe space for clients to share their aspirations.
  • The SMART-T framework is essential for transforming vague desires into clear, actionable, and trackable animal-assisted therapy goals.
  • Coaches must use active listening and clarifying questions to uncover a client's core motivations and specific vision.
  • Collaborative goal creation ensures client ownership and boosts commitment, making the goal truly theirs.
  • Anticipating and addressing common pitfalls like vague goals or client resistance is vital for a successful and empowering coaching experience.

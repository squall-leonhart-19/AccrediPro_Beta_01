Welcome to Blueprinting Goals!

As an Animal-Assisted Therapy Coach, your ability to effectively blueprint goals is the cornerstone of successful client outcomes. This isn't just about identifying problems; it's about collaboratively envisioning a future where your client thrives, supported by the unique power of the human-animal bond. In this lesson, we'll dive deep into the essential skills of initial assessment and goal setting, ensuring every intervention you design is purposeful, client-centered, and deeply impactful. You'll learn how to ask the right questions, listen profoundly, and co-create a roadmap for transformation that truly resonates with your clients.

Learning Objectives

  • Conduct a thorough client intake, assessing needs, history, strengths, and readiness for AAT Coaching.
  • Collaborate with clients to define clear, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) therapeutic goals.
  • Identify suitable therapy animal profiles and characteristics that align with specific client goals and intervention types.
  • Develop a preliminary, client-centered AAT Coaching plan that outlines objectives, strategies, and expected outcomes.
  • Apply ethical considerations and client-centered principles throughout the goal-blueprinting process.
  • Utilize reflective practices to enhance assessment accuracy and goal alignment.

Opening Case Study: Maria's Journey to Reconnection

Finding Purpose After Career Burnout

üë©

Maria, 52-year-old former teacher

Presenting Concern: Chronic feelings of isolation, low motivation, and anxiety following early retirement due to burnout. Expresses a deep love for animals but feels overwhelmed by the idea of caring for one.

Maria, a dedicated educator for over 25 years, found herself adrift after leaving her demanding career. Her days were unstructured, and despite having more free time, she felt increasingly disconnected from others and herself. She was hesitant about traditional therapy but was drawn to the idea of animal interaction, recalling how much joy her classroom pet hamster brought to her students years ago. Her primary desire was to regain a sense of purpose and social connection, but she struggled to articulate concrete steps.

Maria's case highlights the critical need for a comprehensive intake process to uncover underlying needs, identify strengths (like her past connection with animals), and collaboratively transform vague desires into actionable goals. How would you begin to blueprint a path forward for Maria using AAT Coaching?

The Art of Client Intake: Laying the Foundation for BOND

The initial client intake is far more than a checklist; it's the beginning of your therapeutic partnership and the first step in applying the B.O.N.D. Framework‚Ñ¢. This phase is about deep listening, empathetic inquiry, and gathering comprehensive information to understand your client's unique world. It's where you begin to build trust and demonstrate your commitment to their well-being.

Components of a Thorough Initial Assessment:

  1. Presenting Concerns & Goals: What brings them to AAT Coaching? What do they hope to achieve? Explore both explicit and implicit desires.
  2. Personal History:
    • Medical & Mental Health History: Relevant diagnoses, medications, previous therapies. This helps identify any contraindications for AAT or needs for referral to other professionals.
    • Social History: Family dynamics, support systems, work/life balance, hobbies, and interests.
    • Developmental History: Any significant life events, traumas, or developmental milestones that might impact their current state.
  3. Strengths & Resources: What are their existing coping mechanisms? What are they good at? Who are their support people? What internal and external resources can be leveraged?
  4. Animal History & Readiness for AAT:
    • Past Animal Experiences: Both positive and negative interactions.
    • Comfort Level: With different types of animals (dogs, cats, horses, etc.), sizes, and breeds.
    • Allergies or Fears: Critical for safety and appropriate animal selection.
    • Expectations of AAT: Clarify what AAT Coaching entails and manage any unrealistic expectations.
  5. Motivation & Commitment: How motivated are they to engage in the process? What challenges do they foresee?

Remember, your role is not to diagnose but to gather information that informs your coaching plan. For our career-changing coaches, often coming from backgrounds like nursing or teaching, your innate skills in observation and empathy are invaluable here. You're building a holistic picture, connecting dots that might not be obvious at first glance.

Coach's Tip

Embrace the "Beginner's Mind": Even with your professional background, approach each client with curiosity and a "beginner's mind." Avoid assumptions. Ask open-ended questions like, "Tell me more about what that feels like," or "What do you imagine success would look like for you?" This invites deeper sharing and ensures the goals truly belong to the client.

Collaborative Goal Setting: Blueprinting Success with SMART Goals

Once you have a comprehensive understanding of your client, the next step is to collaboratively establish SMART goals. This framework ensures that therapeutic objectives are clear, actionable, and measurable, guiding both your interventions and the client's progress. In AAT Coaching, these goals are often focused on emotional regulation, social skills, communication, motor function, or cognitive engagement.

Let's break down the SMART criteria in the context of AAT:

Element Description AAT Coaching Example
Specific Clearly defined, answering the 5 W's (Who, What, When, Where, Why). Instead of "Feel less anxious," try "Maria will engage in 3 calming activities with the therapy dog per session."
Measurable Quantifiable criteria for tracking progress. "Maria will initiate petting the therapy dog for 5 minutes during each session, measured by observation and self-report."
Achievable Realistic and attainable given the client's resources and context. Ensure the goal is challenging but not overwhelming, building on existing strengths.
Relevant Aligned with the client's overall desires and values. "The goal of increased dog interaction directly supports Maria's desire for connection and purpose."
Time-bound A clear deadline or timeframe for completion. "Maria will consistently initiate interaction with the therapy dog for 5 minutes over 4 consecutive sessions."

The "C" in BOND, for "Connection," starts here. When clients feel truly heard and involved in shaping their goals, their motivation and commitment skyrocket. This collaborative process fosters client autonomy and empowers them to take ownership of their journey.

Coach's Tip

Visualize Success: Help clients articulate their goals by asking them to vividly imagine what success looks, sounds, and feels like. For example, "When you've achieved this goal, what will be different in your daily life? How will you feel?" This can transform vague aspirations into concrete, motivating objectives.

Matching Animal Profiles to Client Goals

The therapy animal is a co-facilitator in your coaching process. Selecting the right animal for a specific client and their goals is paramount to the safety, effectiveness, and ethical integrity of your AAT practice. This requires a deep understanding of animal behavior, temperament, and training, alongside your client's needs.

Key Considerations for Matching:

  • Temperament:
    • For clients with anxiety or trauma: A calm, gentle, predictable animal (e.g., a well-trained Golden Retriever or a placid cat) can provide a sense of safety and grounding.
    • For clients needing motivation or engagement: A playful, energetic, but still well-controlled animal (e.g., a friendly Labrador or a lively terrier mix) can encourage interaction and movement.
  • Training & Experience: The animal must be extensively trained for therapy work, reliable in various settings, and comfortable with novel situations and diverse human interactions.
  • Physical Characteristics:
    • Size: Smaller animals might be less intimidating for some, while larger dogs can offer deep pressure comfort or facilitate mobility exercises.
    • Breed: While individual temperament is key, some breeds are generally known for traits beneficial in therapy (e.g., Beagles for scent work, Poodles for hypoallergenic qualities).
    • Appearance: Consider how a client might react to certain appearances (e.g., a fluffy cat vs. a short-haired dog).
  • Client's Preference & History: Always defer to the client's comfort, preferences, and any past experiences (positive or negative) with specific animal types.
  • Handler-Animal Team: The bond and communication between the animal and its handler (you or a certified handler) are crucial. A harmonious team ensures a safe and effective session.

A 2021 review of AAT effectiveness highlighted that proper animal selection and training is a significant predictor of positive therapeutic outcomes, with studies showing an average effect size of d=0.68 for well-matched interventions (Palley et al., 2021).

Coach's Tip

Observe & Adapt: Even with careful planning, observe the initial interactions between your client and the therapy animal. Is the client comfortable? Is the animal responding appropriately? Be ready to adapt activities or even consider a different animal if the match isn't optimal. Flexibility is a hallmark of an effective AAT Coach.

Developing the Preliminary AAT Coaching Plan

With intake complete and SMART goals established, you can now blueprint a preliminary, client-centered AAT Coaching plan. This living document will guide your sessions, but always be prepared for adjustments as you observe dynamics (the "O" in BOND) and nurture growth (the "N" in BOND).

Key Elements of the Coaching Plan:

  1. Overall Coaching Goals: A summary of the client's primary SMART goals.
  2. Session Objectives: Specific, smaller objectives for each session that contribute to the overall goals.
  3. AAT Strategies & Activities:
    • Type of Interaction: Structured activities (e.g., grooming, leash walking, command practice) vs. unstructured free interaction.
    • Specific Activities: Detail the exercises and interactions planned, linking them directly to session objectives. For example, "Client will practice giving 'sit' command to therapy dog to improve assertive communication."
    • Animal Role: How the therapy animal will be engaged to facilitate the activity (e.g., passive presence, active participant, focus of attention).
  4. Expected Outcomes & Progress Markers: How will you know if a session was successful? What observable behaviors or self-reports indicate progress?
  5. Safety & Ethical Considerations: Reiterate specific protocols relevant to this client and animal team.
  6. Integration Plan: Initial thoughts on how insights gained in AAT sessions might be integrated into the client's daily life (foreshadowing the "D" in BOND).

This preliminary plan provides structure and intention. It's a testament to your professionalism and careful consideration, yet it remains flexible enough to respond to the organic nature of human-animal interaction. For many of our coaches, this structured approach brings confidence, especially when transitioning from less formal roles into a professional coaching practice.

Coach's Tip

Document, Document, Document: Maintain meticulous records of your intake, goal setting, and coaching plan. This not only ensures ethical practice and professional accountability but also provides a clear roadmap for your client's journey. Good documentation makes it easier to track progress, celebrate milestones, and adjust strategies as needed.

Case Study: Blueprinting in Action with Maria

Case Study Application

Maria's Progress with Blueprinting Goals

üë©

Maria, 52-year-old former teacher

Coaching Focus: Addressing isolation and low motivation through AAT

Following a thorough intake, Maria and her AAT Coach, Sarah (a 48-year-old former nurse), collaboratively blueprinted the following SMART goals:

  • Goal 1 (Social Connection): Within 8 weeks, Maria will attend 2 local dog park meetups with the therapy dog, initiating conversation with at least one new person at each meetup.
  • Goal 2 (Purpose & Routine): Over the next 6 weeks, Maria will consistently engage in a 20-minute daily grooming and short walk routine with the therapy dog, 5 days a week, tracking her adherence.
  • Goal 3 (Anxiety Reduction): During AAT sessions, Maria will report a decrease in subjective anxiety levels (on a 1-10 scale) by at least 2 points after 15 minutes of calm interaction with the therapy dog, across 4 consecutive sessions.

Sarah selected "Daisy," a calm, gentle, 5-year-old certified therapy Golden Retriever, known for her intuitive nature and soft fur, ideal for tactile comfort. The preliminary plan included:

  1. Initial Sessions: Focus on building rapport with Daisy through gentle grooming and quiet petting in a controlled environment.
  2. Mid-Phase: Introduce short leash walks in a quiet park, gradually increasing duration and exposure to other people. Practice initiating simple commands with Daisy.
  3. Later Phase: Plan for structured visits to a local dog park during less busy times, with Sarah facilitating introductions to other dog owners.

Outcome: After 8 weeks, Maria successfully achieved Goal 2, reporting a 75% adherence rate to her daily routine with Daisy. She attended one dog park meetup, where she spoke briefly with another dog owner, reporting a 1-point reduction in anxiety during the interaction. She consistently reported a 3-point average reduction in anxiety during her AAT sessions. While Goal 1 wasn't fully met, the progress was significant, providing a foundation for continued work and a renewed sense of confidence. Maria even began exploring volunteer opportunities at a local animal shelter, feeling a rekindled sense of purpose.

Check Your Understanding

1

When conducting an initial client intake, which of the following is NOT a primary area of inquiry for an AAT Coach?

The primary areas of inquiry include presenting concerns, personal history (medical, social, developmental), strengths, animal history, and motivation. While future career aspirations might be relevant contextually, it is not a primary area of inquiry for the initial AAT-specific assessment, unless directly tied to a therapeutic goal.

2

Maria's initial desire was to "regain a sense of purpose." Which of the following best transforms this into a SMART goal?

A SMART goal needs to be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. "Maria will consistently engage in a 20-minute daily grooming and short walk routine with the therapy dog, 5 days a week, tracking her adherence, for the next 6 weeks" is the best option. It clearly defines the activity, frequency, duration, and timeframe, making it measurable and specific.

3

A client with severe anxiety and a history of trauma is seeking AAT Coaching. Which therapy animal profile would generally be most appropriate?

For clients with severe anxiety and trauma, a calm, gentle, and predictable animal is crucial to create a sense of safety. A highly energetic or very large dog might be overwhelming. A small, quiet rabbit or guinea pig could also be suitable, but a calm, medium-sized dog like a Golden Retriever is often an excellent choice due to their gentle nature and ability to provide comforting physical presence without being intimidating.

4

Which element is generally NOT included in a preliminary AAT Coaching plan?

A preliminary AAT Coaching plan includes overall goals, session objectives, AAT strategies/activities, expected outcomes, and safety/ethical considerations. Detailed financial projections are outside the scope of the therapeutic coaching plan itself, though discussions around service fees would occur during initial consultations.

Key Takeaways

  • Comprehensive Intake is Foundational: A thorough initial assessment gathering needs, history, strengths, and animal-specific considerations is critical for ethical and effective AAT Coaching.
  • SMART Goals Drive Progress: Collaboratively setting Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound goals empowers clients and provides a clear roadmap for therapeutic interventions.
  • Animal-Client Matching is Strategic: Carefully align the therapy animal's temperament, training, and characteristics with the client's goals and comfort levels for optimal outcomes.
  • The AAT Coaching Plan is a Living Document: Develop a preliminary plan outlining objectives, strategies, and outcomes, while maintaining flexibility to adapt based on ongoing observation and client progress.
  • Ethical Practice & Client Autonomy: Throughout the blueprinting process, prioritize client well-being, informed consent, and active participation to foster a strong therapeutic partnership.

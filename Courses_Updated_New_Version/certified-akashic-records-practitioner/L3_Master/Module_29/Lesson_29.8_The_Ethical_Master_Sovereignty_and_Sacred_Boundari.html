Section 3: Your Teaching Approach

When mentoring, follow the S.A.G.E. Framework to ensure the practitioner grows from the experience:

  • S - Space Holding: Listen to their frustration without jumping in to "solve" it immediately.
  • A - Assessment: Ask them what they think is happening before giving your opinion.
  • G - Guidance: Offer a Master-level perspective on the energetic mechanics.
  • E - Empowerment: Give them a specific tool to try in the next session.
Sarah's Insight

If you just tell Elena "the client is stuck in a loop," she learns nothing. If you ask her, "Elena, why do you think the Records are repeating this scene?" she learns to think like a Master.

Section 4: Delivering the Feedback

Constructive feedback for a 40+ career changer must be handled with high emotional intelligence. They often have a "Student Mindset" that fears being wrong.

The Feedback Script:

Master (You): "Elena, I hear how much you care about Sarah. That empathy is your greatest gift. But let's look at the mechanics. When a client repeats a story three times, they aren't looking for a clearing; they are looking for permission to stay in the drama. How does it feel in your body when she starts crying?"

Elena: "I feel tight in my chest. I feel like I have to find the 'magic word' to make it stop."

Master (You): "Exactly. You've stepped out of the 'Witness' role and into the 'Rescuer' role. In your next session, I want you to ask the Records: 'What is the secondary gain for Sarah in holding onto this 18th-century story?' Don't clear it. Just observe it."

Section 5: Supervision Best Practices

To maintain a professional and lucrative mentoring practice, adhere to these standards:

1

Maintain the "Supervisory Gap"

You are their mentor, not their best friend. While you can be warm, keep the focus on clinical application and professional growth.

2

Focus on Process, Not Content

Don't get bogged down in the details of the client's past lives. Focus on how the practitioner is navigating the Records.

3

Identify Counter-Transference

Watch for when the practitioner's own baggage (e.g., fear of rejection) is affecting the client's session.

Sarah's Insight

Always end a supervision session by asking the mentee: "What is one thing you feel more confident about now?" This anchors the learning and reduces their imposter syndrome.

Check Your Understanding

1. A mentee tells you they feel "drained" after every session. What is the most likely Master-level diagnosis?

They are likely "leaking" energy by trying to do the work for the client or have failed to set proper energetic boundaries (Rescuer Archetype).

2. What is the primary goal of the "S" in the S.A.G.E. framework?

Space Holding. It allows the practitioner to vent their frustrations and fears so they can move into a logical, receptive state for learning.

3. If a mentee is "stuck" on a client case, should you open the client's Records yourself?

Generally, no. You should open the Practitioner's Records (with permission) or your own Records to gain insight on how to guide them. Looking at the client directly bypasses the mentee's growth.

4. Why is it important to ask "How does it feel in your body?" during supervision?

It helps the practitioner identify somatic markers of counter-transference, teaching them to use their body as a diagnostic tool for their own boundaries.

Key Takeaways for Master Mentors

  • Mentoring is a Skill: It requires a shift from "doing the healing" to "teaching the healer."
  • Empowerment Over Answers: The best mentors ask the questions that lead the practitioner to their own "Aha!" moment.
  • Structure Matters: Use frameworks like S.A.G.E. to keep supervision sessions professional and effective.
  • Boundary Integrity: Protect your own energy by maintaining a professional supervisory gap.
  • Revenue Expansion: Mentorship is a premium service that honors your years of experience and Master-level certification.

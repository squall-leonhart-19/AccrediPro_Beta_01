Notice I didn't say "You shouldn't have coached her." I used "What if..." This keeps the mentee's nervous system calm so they can actually integrate the lesson.

Supervision Best Practices

To be an effective mentor, you must adhere to a set of professional standards. This ensures the safety of the practitioner, the client, and the reputation of the Akashic field.

  • Maintain the "Supervisory Space": Do not become Diane's therapist. If her personal trauma is blocking her readings, gently refer her to a therapist or a separate healing session.
  • The 80/20 Rule: Let the mentee speak for 80% of the time. Your job is to ask the powerful questions that lead them to their own intuitive "Aha!" moments.
  • Documentation: Keep brief notes on Diane's progress. This is essential for her L2 or L3 certification path.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of "Normalization" in a supervision session?

The goal is to reduce the mentee's shame by showing them that challenges and "blank" moments are a standard part of the developmental process for all practitioners, including masters.

2. If a mentee begins crying about their childhood during a case review, what is the mentor's ethical responsibility?

The mentor should offer empathy but maintain boundaries by gently steering the conversation back to the professional case, or suggesting the mentee seek a personal healing session/therapy to address those specific personal triggers.

3. True or False: A mentor should always provide the "correct" interpretation of a Record if the mentee is struggling.

False. The mentor's role is to ask guiding questions that help the mentee develop their own intuitive muscles and clinical reasoning, rather than just giving them the answer.

4. Why is "Holding the Void" a valuable technical correction for Diane?

It transforms a moment of "failure" (silence) into a deliberate spiritual tool, teaching the mentee that silence in the Records is often a purposeful energetic state rather than a lack of connection.

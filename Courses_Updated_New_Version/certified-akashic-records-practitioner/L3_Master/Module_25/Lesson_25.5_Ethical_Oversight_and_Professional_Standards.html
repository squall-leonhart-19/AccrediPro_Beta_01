Handling Power Dynamics in the Mentorship Triad

In a typical session, there is a dyad: Practitioner and Client. However, in supervision, we introduce a third entity: the Mentor. This creates a triad that, if not managed ethically, can lead to the "Vulnerability Loop."

The practitioner may feel pressure to "perform" for the mentor, while the client may feel intimidated or "over-observed." This can distort the energetic field of the Akasha, leading to filtered information. Ethical oversight requires the mentor to maintain a "silent witness" presence or a "behind-the-scenes" review process that preserves the client‚Äôs autonomy.

Legal Considerations and Informed Consent

Professionalism requires more than just good intentions; it requires a paper trail. When a practitioner is under supervision, the client must be informed. Failure to disclose supervision is a breach of Informed Consent and can lead to legal liability.

Your informed consent documents should explicitly state:

  • That the practitioner is currently under professional supervision.
  • That session notes (anonymized) may be shared with a Master Mentor for educational purposes.
  • The limits of confidentiality (e.g., harm to self or others).
  • That Akashic Records work is a wellness modality and not a substitute for clinical mental health or medical treatment.

Case Study: The Transparency Standard

Elena (52), Certified Practitioner & Mentor

Scenario: Elena was mentoring a junior practitioner, Sarah, who was working with a client experiencing severe anxiety. Sarah did not disclose to the client that she was sharing case notes with Elena. When the client found out Sarah was "discussing her soul" with someone else, she felt violated and threatened legal action for breach of privacy.

Intervention: Elena implemented a mandatory "Supervision Disclosure Form" for all mentees. They also held a "re-alignment" session with the client, explaining the benefits of having two sets of expert eyes on the case.

Outcome: The client stayed, Sarah learned the value of transparency, and Elena protected the Academy's reputation. Lesson: Disclosure builds trust; secrecy destroys it.

The Ethics of Third-Party Record Access

One of the most frequent ethical dilemmas in Akashic practice is the request to "read" for someone who isn't there (a spouse, a child over 18, a boss). The Professional Standard is clear: No Consent, No Access.

Accessing the records of a non-present entity without their express permission is a form of spiritual voyeurism. It violates the sovereign right of that soul to privacy. As a mentor, you must train practitioners to pivot the question back to the client.

Example: Instead of "What is my husband thinking?", the question becomes "What do I need to understand about my reaction to my husband's behavior?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it ethically mandatory to disclose supervision to a client?

It is a requirement of Informed Consent. Clients have a legal and ethical right to know who is accessing their sensitive energetic information and case notes, even if anonymized.

2. What is the primary danger of spiritual bypassing in the Anchoring phase?

It prevents the client from taking the necessary 3D actions required to manifest soul-level changes, keeping them stuck in a cycle of "revelation without transformation."

3. Under what circumstances can you access the Records of a third party without their presence?

Only with their express written or verbal consent. Without it, accessing their records is an ethical breach of soul sovereignty.

4. How should a mentor handle a practitioner who feels "drained" after an ancestral healing session?

The mentor must address the practitioner's failure to maintain energetic boundaries and guide them through a somatic recalibration and a review of the Alignment (Phase A) protocols.

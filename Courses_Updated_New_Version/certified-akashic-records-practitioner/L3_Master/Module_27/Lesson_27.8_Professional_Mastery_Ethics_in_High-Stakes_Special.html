Supervision Best Practices

As you step into leadership, your role changes from "Doer" to "Observer." Effective supervision follows the 70/30 Rule: The mentee should be speaking 70% of the time, and you should be asking high-level questions for the other 30%.

1

Never "Take Over" the Session

If you are doing a live supervised session, resist the urge to jump in and "save" the mentee. Let them navigate the discomfort; that is where the neural pathways of mastery are built.

2

Focus on Parallel Process

Often, what is happening between the Mentee and the Client is also happening between You and the Mentee. If the Mentee is feeling "blocked," notice if you are feeling "blocked" in your mentoring. This is a key supervisory insight.

3

Standardize the Intake

Ensure your mentees are using the professional intake forms we‚Äôve provided. A lack of clarity in the Records often starts with a lack of clarity in the physical intake.

Check Your Understanding

1. A mentee reports that their client "didn't feel anything" during a session. What is the most empowering first step for the supervisor?

The first step is to validate the mentee's integrity and then use inquiry to ask what the "void" might represent for the client, rather than treating it as a failure of the practitioner.

2. What is the "70/30 Rule" in professional supervision?

The mentee should speak 70% of the time (explaining their process and reasoning), while the supervisor speaks 30% of the time (asking guiding questions and providing targeted feedback).

3. Why is "Parallel Process" important in mentoring?

Parallel process suggests that the dynamics occurring between the client and mentee (e.g., resistance, anxiety) are often mirrored in the relationship between the mentee and supervisor. Recognizing this allows for deeper clinical insight.

4. How does adding a supervision tier impact a Master Practitioner's business model?

It allows for scaling income ($300-$500/hr) while reducing the energetic drain of constant direct client work, while simultaneously establishing the practitioner as a recognized authority and leader in the field.

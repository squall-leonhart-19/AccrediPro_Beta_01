Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When you're starting out, it's tempting to want to "fix" everything at once. This often leads to client overwhelm. In my early years, I‚Äôd give clients a 10-page monitoring checklist, and they‚Äôd never come back! Here‚Äôs what to avoid:

  • 1. Over-Complicating the Data: Don't ask them to track 20 variables. Pick the top 3 markers that matter for that specific pet (e.g., appetite, stairs, and play-bows).
  • 2. Being the "Health Police": If a client forgets to log for a few days, don't scold them. Say, "Life gets busy! Let's just start fresh today."
  • 3. Ignoring the Emotional Weight: Aging pets bring up grief. If you ignore the client's emotions, they won't feel supported, no matter how good your data is.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Normalcy Bias" a challenge in Sustained Vitality Monitoring?

Normalcy Bias occurs because owners see their pets every day and fail to notice gradual, subtle declines. As a coach, you provide the objective audit needed to catch these changes early.

2. What is the recommended number of vitality markers to track initially to avoid client overwhelm?

It is recommended to focus on the top 3 markers most relevant to the specific pet's needs (e.g., mobility, appetite, and engagement).

3. According to the data, what percentage of owners attribute slowing down to "just getting old"?

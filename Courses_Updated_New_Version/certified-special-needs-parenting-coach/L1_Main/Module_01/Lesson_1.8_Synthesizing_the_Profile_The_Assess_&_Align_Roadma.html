Common Mistakes to Avoid

In my practice, I've seen even experienced coaches fall into these traps during the first assessment:

  • The "Fix-it" Trap: Jumping into solutions (like "Try a weighted vest!") before the parent feels fully heard. Alignment must come before Advice.
  • Toxic Positivity: Saying things like "Everything happens for a reason" or "He's such a blessing." While true, it can minimize the parent's current pain.
  • Over-Identifying: Spending the whole session talking about your child. This is Sarah's time. Use your experience to empathize, not to dominate.
  • Ignoring the Marriage/Support System: Assessing the child but forgetting to ask how the parent's primary relationships are holding up.

Success Markers: How You'll Know It Worked

A successful Assessment and Alignment session (Module 1) results in specific client shifts. Look for these indicators:

  1. Physical Release: The client's shoulders drop, or they exhale deeply. This is the "Safety Response."
  2. Language Shift: They move from saying "I don't know what to do" to "I'm looking forward to trying [X]."
  3. Validation: They say something like, "Nobody has ever explained it to me that way before."
  4. Clear Expectations: They can articulate exactly what the next 3 steps are in your coaching process.
Emma's Insight

A successful intake session should feel like a "shared sigh of relief." If the parent leaves feeling like they have a teammate, you've done your job perfectly.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it critical to "align" with the parent's emotional state before offering strategies?

Because a parent in "fight-or-flight" mode cannot process new information. Alignment builds the psychological safety necessary for the parent to actually implement the coaching strategies you'll provide later.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Mirroring Technique" during an assessment?

To ensure the client feels 100% understood and to confirm that you, the coach, are focusing on the correct "root cause" rather than just a surface symptom.

3. Sarah (the client) expresses guilt over her "failure" as a teacher and mom. What is the best coaching response?

Normalize her experience by separating her professional identity from her parental role, and validate that parenting neurodivergence is a specialized skill set she is currently acquiring.

4. How many "Success Markers" should you look for to know the session went well?

While any one is good, a truly aligned session usually shows a mix of physical release, a shift in language toward the future, and a clear understanding of the next steps.

Key Takeaways

  • Coaching the Parent: We focus on Sarahâ€™s resilience and strategies, which in turn helps Leo.
  • Alignment > Advice: Never offer a solution until the parent has confirmed, "Yes, that is exactly how I feel."
  • The Fraud Factor: Many high-achieving women (nurses, teachers) feel like failures when parenting neurodivergence; your role is to dismantle that myth.

The 8 Pillars of Executive Function

Executive Function (EF) is often described as the "Air Traffic Control" system of the brain. It is a set of cognitive processes managed primarily by the prefrontal cortex that allow us to plan, focus attention, remember instructions, and juggle multiple tasks successfully.

For neurodivergent children (ADHD, Autism, Dyslexia, etc.), these pillars are often unevenly developed. A child may have the intellectual capacity of a 12-year-old but the Emotional Control of a 6-year-old.

EF Pillar Description Manifestation in Neurodivergence
Inhibition The ability to stop/think before acting. Impulsivity, shouting out, touching things without permission.
Working Memory Holding info in mind to complete a task. Forgetting the first step of a 3-step instruction.
Emotional Control Managing feelings to achieve goals. Frequent meltdowns over minor setbacks or transitions.
Shift (Flexibility) Moving from one task/mindset to another. Rigidity, difficulty leaving the house or stopping a game.
Initiation Beginning a task or generating ideas. "Procrastination" that is actually brain-based paralysis.
Planning/Org Managing current and future task demands. Losing homework, messy rooms, poor time management.
Self-Monitoring Monitoring own performance/behavior. Unaware of how their behavior affects others.
Task Monitor Checking work for mistakes or progress. Turning in incomplete work or missing obvious errors.
Coach Tip: The Reframe

When a parent says, "He just won't listen!" ask them to identify which EF pillar is struggling. Is it Working Memory (he forgot the instruction) or Inhibition (he couldn't stop what he was doing)? Reframing "won't" as "can't yet" is the first step in parental alignment.

Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Processing

Understanding Cognitive Styles requires looking at how a child's brain processes information. Most neurotypical brains utilize "Top-Down" processing, while many neurodivergent brains are predominantly "Bottom-Up."

Top-Down Processing (Concept-Driven)

This style uses prior knowledge, expectations, and context to interpret sensory information. The brain sees the "big picture" first and fills in the details. Example: Reading a sentence even if some letters are scrambled because the brain knows the context.

Bottom-Up Processing (Data-Driven)

This style starts with the raw sensory data. The brain takes in every detail, sound, and visual stimulus before trying to form a concept. This is common in Autism. Example: A child cannot focus on a teacher's voice because they are first processing the hum of the AC, the flickering light, and the texture of their shirt.

The Executive Age Gap

One of the most powerful tools you will provide as a coach is the 30% Rule (often cited by Dr. Russell Barkley). In children with ADHD and related neurodivergence, Executive Functioning skills are typically delayed by approximately 30% of their chronological age.

The Math of Compassion

If you are coaching a parent of a 10-year-old with ADHD, tell them: "Your child has the intellect of a 10-year-old, but the Executive Age of a 7-year-old. Would you leave a 7-year-old alone to manage their own homework and laundry?" This immediately lowers parental frustration.

As a coach, you can help parents calculate the Executive Age (EA) to set realistic goals:

  • Chronological Age 6: Executive Age ~4.2 (Needs help with basic transitions).
  • Chronological Age 12: Executive Age ~8.4 (Still needs significant scaffolding for organization).
  • Chronological Age 18: Executive Age ~12.6 (May not be ready for the total independence of college).

Cognitive Flexibility and Family Routines

Cognitive flexibility (or "Set Shifting") is the ability to adapt to new situations or change one's mind. For many neurodivergent children, the "cost" of shifting is physically and mentally exhausting. This is why a change in the dinner menu can lead to a two-hour meltdown.

The "Cognitive Rigidity" Cycle:

  1. Unexpected Change (Routine disrupted).
  2. Amygdala Hijack (Brain perceives change as a threat).
  3. Physical Response (Flight/Fight/Freeze).
  4. Behavioral Outburst (The "Meltdown").

Check Your Understanding

1. A child forgets a 3-step instruction by the time they reach their bedroom. Which EF pillar is likely the culprit?

Working Memory. The child is unable to hold the information "online" long enough to execute the multi-step task.

2. What is the estimated "Executive Age" of a 15-year-old with significant ADHD?

Approximately 10.5 years old. (15 x 0.70 = 10.5). Using the 30% delay rule, we should expect the self-regulation skills of a 10-year-old.

3. True or False: Bottom-Up processing means the brain focuses on the "Big Picture" first.

False. Bottom-Up processing is data-driven, focusing on raw sensory details first. Top-Down processing is concept-driven (Big Picture).

4. Why is "Initiation" often mistaken for laziness?

Because the child is physically capable of the task but their brain cannot "spark" the motor or cognitive sequence to begin. It is a neurological "starting motor" failure, not a lack of willpower.

Key Takeaways

  • Executive Function is a set of 8 distinct cognitive skills; a child can be "gifted" in one and "delayed" in another.
  • Neurodivergent children often operate with a 30% delay in EF skills compared to their chronological age.
  • Bottom-Up processing makes the world overwhelming; coaching should focus on simplifying the sensory and task environment.
  • The coach's role is to help parents provide "External Executive Function" while the child's internal system matures.
  • Behavior is communication; executive dysfunction is a "can't," not a "won't."

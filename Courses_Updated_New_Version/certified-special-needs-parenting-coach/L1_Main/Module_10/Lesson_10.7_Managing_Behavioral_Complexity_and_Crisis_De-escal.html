Case Study: The 5:00 PM Crisis

Coach: Diane (52, former nurse) | Client: The Miller Family

Presenting Symptoms: Liam (8, ADHD/Autism) began exhibitng aggressive behavior (hitting, throwing objects) every day between 5:00 PM and 6:00 PM. His mother, Sarah, felt like she was "walking on eggshells" and feared for the safety of Liam's younger sister.

Intervention: Diane used the A.N.C.H.O.R. Method‚Ñ¢ to perform a "Functional Audit." She discovered the antecedent wasn't "defiance," but sensory exhaustion combined with a blood sugar crash. Liam's school day ended at 3:30 PM, and by 5:00 PM, his medication was wearing off (rebound effect) just as the house became noisy with dinner prep.

Outcome: By shifting dinner to 5:15 PM and introducing a "Low-Stimulation Transition Zone" at 4:30 PM, the aggression decreased by 85% within two weeks.

Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) for Coaches

In clinical settings, an FBA is a formal process. In coaching, we use Functional Thinking. Every behavior serves a purpose. If we don't understand the function, our interventions will likely fail or, worse, increase the child's distress.

The four primary functions of behavior are often remembered by the acronym E.A.T.S.:

  • Escape/Avoidance: Trying to get away from a task, person, or environment that is difficult or painful.
  • Attention: Seeking a social connection (even negative attention is a form of connection).
  • Tangible: Wanting a specific item, activity, or food.
  • Sensory (Automatic): The behavior feels good or relieves internal discomfort (e.g., rocking, humming, or even self-injury to "override" sensory pain).
Coach Tip: The Detective Mindset

When a parent says, "He's doing it for no reason," your job is to say, "It feels that way because the reason is currently hidden. Let's look at the ABC's: Antecedent (what happened before), Behavior (what exactly occurred), and Consequence (what happened immediately after)."

Differentiating Meltdowns vs. Tantrums

One of the most valuable "Aha!" moments for parents is realizing that a meltdown is not a tantrum. Treating a meltdown like a tantrum (using discipline or "tough love") is like punishing someone for having a seizure‚Äîit is ineffective and damaging to the attachment.

Feature Tantrum (Cognitive-Behavioral) Meltdown (Neurological/Sensory)
Goal Goal-oriented (wants the toy/cookie). No goal; the system is overwhelmed.
Audience Stops if the audience leaves. Continues regardless of who is watching.
Control Child maintains some level of control. Child has lost all "top-down" control.
End Point Ends once the goal is met or child tires. Ends when the nervous system resets.
Coach Tip: The Safety Anchor

During a meltdown, the parent's only job is to be the "External Nervous System." Minimal talking, low lights, and physical safety. Save the "teaching moments" for when the child is back in the Green Zone of the A.N.C.H.O.R. framework.

Proactive Strategies: The Pre-Crisis Window

Crisis management is 90% prevention. The Pre-Crisis Window is that subtle period where a child is "rumbling." A 2021 study on neurodivergent de-escalation found that interventions applied in the "rumbling" stage were 4x more likely to prevent a full-scale crisis than those applied during peak arousal.

Identifying the "Rumble" Signs:

  • Physical: Clenched fists, red face, increased pacing, dilated pupils.
  • Verbal: Staccato speech, repetitive questioning, "looping" on a specific grievance.
  • Sensory: Covering ears, squinting, or increased "stimming" (self-stimulatory behavior).

As a coach, you help parents develop "Interrupt Protocols." This might include a "Sensory Circuit," a heavy-work activity (like pushing against a wall), or a sudden change in environment (moving from indoors to outdoors).

Safety Planning and Environmental Management

For families dealing with aggressive or self-injurious behaviors (SIB), safety is the priority. You cannot coach a family into "connection" if they are in physical danger. This is where Module 5 (Sensory Architecture) becomes critical.

  • Environmental Hardening: Using Plexiglass for windows, bolting furniture, or removing high-risk objects from "Regulation Zones."
  • Personal Safety: Coaching parents on safe body positioning (standing to the side, not blocking exits) to avoid being hit or bitten.
  • Sibling Protection: Creating a "Go-Bag" and a "Safe Room" for siblings to retreat to during a crisis without feeling abandoned.
Coach Tip: Professional Boundaries

If a child's behavior poses a severe risk of life-threatening injury, your role is to help the family coordinate with Crisis Stabilization Units or medical professionals. Know your scope: you are a coach, not an emergency first responder.

Post-Crisis Processing: The Emotional Aftermath

The "Crisis Hangover" is real. After the adrenaline subsides, parents often feel shame ("I'm a bad parent"), guilt ("I lost my temper"), and hopelessness. This is where your coaching earns its premium value.

Use the A.N.C.H.O.R. Post-Crisis Audit:

  1. Acknowledge the Biology: Remind the parent that their nervous system was also under attack. Their "fight or flight" response was a physiological certainty, not a moral failing.
  2. Normalize the Repair: Teach them that repairing the relationship after a crisis is more important than the crisis never happening.
  3. The "Next Time" Bridge: Instead of ruminating on what went wrong, ask: "What was the one signal we missed in the Pre-Crisis window?"

Check Your Understanding

1. A child is screaming because they were told they cannot have an iPad. When the parent leaves the room, the child stops screaming and looks around to see where they went. Is this likely a meltdown or a tantrum?

This is likely a tantrum. Key indicators include the goal-oriented nature (iPad) and the presence of an "audience" (checking to see where the parent went).

2. What does the "S" in the E.A.T.S. acronym for behavior functions stand for?

The "S" stands for Sensory (or Automatic). This refers to behaviors that provide internal feedback or relief, independent of social consequences.

3. True or False: During a peak crisis (meltdown), you should use the time to explain to the child why their behavior is inappropriate.

False. During a meltdown, the child's "thinking brain" (prefrontal cortex) is offline. Verbal processing is impaired. Safety and quiet presence are the only priorities.

4. Why is the "Pre-Crisis Window" (the Rumble stage) the most important time for intervention?

Because the child still has some access to their cognitive functions. They can still follow a simple "interrupt protocol" or sensory strategy before the nervous system reaches total overwhelm.

Key Takeaways

  • Behavior is Communication: Use the E.A.T.S. framework to decode the function of every complex behavior.
  • Nervous System First: Never treat a neurological meltdown with behavioral discipline; prioritize safety and co-regulation.
  • Master the Rumble: Teach parents to spot the subtle physical and verbal signs of the Pre-Crisis window.
  • Environment is an Intervention: Safety planning must include physical changes to the home to reduce risk and caregiver anxiety.
  • Coaching the "Hangover": The work isn't done when the crisis ends; the emotional repair for the parent is where lasting change happens.

Practice Exercise: Out Loud Application

To build your "coaching muscles," you must move past silent reading. Follow these steps:

1

The Mirror Technique

Stand in front of a mirror. Read the "YOU" lines out loud. Watch your facial expressions. Are you scowling in concentration, or is your face soft and inviting? Aim for a "soft gaze."

2

Record & Review

Use your phone to record yourself saying: "Brenda, it's okay to be tired of the fight. You are doing a hard thing, and you don't have to do it perfectly." Listen back. Does it sound like a "lecture" or a "hug"?

The "Nurse to Coach" Pivot

If you're coming from a nursing or teaching background, you might have a "command-and-control" voice. In coaching, we shift to a "curiosity" voice. Practice ending your sentences with a slight downward inflection to sound grounded rather than questioning.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps during deep-dive sessions:

The Pitfall Why It Happens The Coaching Correction
"The Fixer" Trap We want to relieve the client's pain immediately. Stay in the emotion. Say: "Tell me more about that tiredness."
Toxic Positivity Feeling uncomfortable with the client's grief. Validate the hard stuff. "It makes total sense that you feel this way."
Over-Sharing Thinking "Me too!" helps the client feel seen. Keep the spotlight on them. Use your experience only to deepen empathy.
IEP Tangents Getting stuck in the logistics of the child's care. Gently pivot back to the parent's internal experience.

Success Markers

How do you know the session was successful? Look for these "Green Lights" in your practice:

  • The "Exhale": The client visibly drops their shoulders or takes a deep, unprompted breath.
  • Identity Shift: The client uses "I" statements that aren't about their child (e.g., "I miss my old hobbies").
  • Reduced Pacing: The client stops speaking rapidly and begins to reflect before answering.
  • Permission Granted: The client acknowledges that their own well-being is a prerequisite for their child's success.

Check Your Understanding

1. Brenda says, "I'm a failure because I lost my temper at the IEP meeting." What is the most effective coaching response?

Instead of saying "Don't say that!" (which invalidates her), use: "I hear how much you care about getting this right. That 'temper' was actually your protective instinct. What does that instinct need right now?"

2. What is the primary goal of the "Armor Down" technique?

The goal is to help the parent reconnect with their own identity and needs, separate from their role as a "warrior" or "manager" for their child's disability.

3. A client starts crying during a session. According to Module 10 standards, what should you do?

Hold the space. Silence is your best tool. Allow the tears to flow without rushing to provide a tissue or a "fix." Say: "I'm right here with you. Take all the time you need."

4. Why is the "Gardener" metaphor used in Brenda's scenario?

It anchors her back to a time/version of herself that felt creative, peaceful, and productive in a way that had nothing to do with autism or advocacy.

Key Lab Takeaways

  • Voice is a Tool: Your tone and pacing are just as important as the questions you ask.
  • Validate the Warrior: Always honor the work the parent has done before asking them to rest.
  • Identity over Logistics: Focus on the person coaching, not the case they are managing.
  • Practice is Mandatory: Imposter syndrome fades when your mouth gets used to speaking the language of coaching.

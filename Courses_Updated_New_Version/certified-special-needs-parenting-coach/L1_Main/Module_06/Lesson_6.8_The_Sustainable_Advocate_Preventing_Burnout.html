Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I mentor new coaches in their 40s and 50s, I see these four mistakes most often. Avoid these to maintain your professional legitimacy and prevent burnout.

The Mistake Why It Fails The Coaching Correction
Becoming the "Warrior" You start getting angry for the client, which increases their stress. Maintain "Compassionate Detachment." You are the lighthouse, not the storm.
Giving Legal Advice Unless you are a JD, this creates massive liability issues. "Let's look at the procedural safeguards together and see what questions you want to ask your attorney."
Fixing the Problem You tell them exactly what to say, so they don't build their own "advocacy muscle." Ask: "How would you like to phrase that so it feels like your voice?"
Ignoring the Body Focusing only on the paperwork while the client is visibly shaking. Stop the session for a 2-minute grounding exercise. Regulation must precede representation.

Success Markers: How You Know It Worked

A successful advocacy coaching session doesn't always end with a "yes" from the school. It ends with a shift in the client. Look for these indicators of success:

  • Shift in Language: The client moves from "They won't let me" to "I am going to propose."
  • Physiological Calm: Their shoulders drop, their breathing slows, and they can make eye contact.
  • Clear Priorities: They move from a list of 20 complaints to 3 non-negotiable strategic goals.
  • Increased Agency: They stop asking you "What should I do?" and start saying "Here is my plan."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is crying because the school denied a 1-to-1 aide. Your first response should be to tell them which law they are violating. True or False?

False. Your first response should be to validate the emotion and regulate the client's nervous system. Advocacy coaching requires a regulated brain before strategic action can be taken.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Empowerment Bridge" technique?

To transition the client from a state of emotional flooding (the "battle" mindset) to a state of strategic, calm leadership (the "CEO" mindset).

3. Why is "silence" considered a power move in advocacy coaching?

It provides the client with the necessary cognitive space to process their thoughts and find their own voice, rather than relying on the coach's prompts.

4. A client says, "I'm just a mom, they won't listen to me." What is the best coaching reframe?

"You are the leading expert on Leo's 24-hour life. How can we present that expertise so the team sees the full picture of his needs?"
Emma's Final Encouragement

Key Takeaways

  • Advocacy is Leadership: Coach your clients to see themselves as the CEO of their child's support team.
  • Regulation First: A flooded client cannot advocate effectively. Use grounding techniques before diving into paperwork.
  • Stay in Your Lane: Provide coaching and strategy, not legal advice, to maintain professional boundaries.
  • Focus on Presence: You cannot control the school's actions, but you can empower the client to control their own presence and response.

Your Coaching Scenario

In this practice lab, you will be coaching Elena. She has just received a formal diagnosis for her son, and she is spiraling into "what if" thinking.

The Background: Leo was just diagnosed with Autism Spectrum Disorder (Level 1) and Dyslexia. Elena has spent the last 48 hours on Google. She hasn't slept, she's crying frequently, and she feels like she "failed" him by not noticing the signs earlier. She is currently overwhelmed by the list of 12 therapy recommendations the school gave her.

Her Goal: "I just need to know what to do first. I feel like my son's future just evaporated."

Emma's Tip

When a client says their child's future "evaporated," they are in a state of acute grief. Do not try to prove them wrong yet. Just acknowledge the weight of that feeling.

The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the pacingâ€”slow down during the "You" sections.

ELENA:

"I look at this list of therapies... OT, speech, social skills, behavioral intervention... Emma, how am I supposed to do this? I work 50 hours a week. Leo is already struggling. I feel like I've already failed him by waiting this long to get him tested."

YOU (The Empathy Bridge):

"Elena, take a deep breath with me for a second. I can hear the weight of that list in your voice. Before we look at a single therapy, I want to say this: You are the same loving, dedicated mother today that you were three days ago. This diagnosis doesn't change Leo, it only gives us a map. But right now, the map feels like it's written in a language you don't speak yet. That is a heavy place to be."

ELENA:

"It is. I just... I keep thinking about him being bullied, or never being able to live on his own. Is that what this means?"

YOU (Holding Space):

"It's completely natural for your brain to jump to the future to try and protect him. But let's bring it back to right now. If we could take just 10% of that weight off your shoulders today, what would be the one thing that feels the heaviest?"

Application Exercise: Speak It Out Loud

Coaching is a "muscle memory" skill. You cannot learn it just by reading. Follow these steps:

Step Action Goal
1 Record yourself reading the "YOU" lines above on your phone. Hear your own tone and pacing.
2 Listen back. Do you sound rushed? Aim for a "calm authority" tone.
3 Practice the "10% weight" question 3 times. Mastering the pivot from "crisis" to "actionable step."
Emma's Tip

Silence is your best friend. After you ask, "What feels the heaviest?", wait at least 5 seconds. Let Elena process. The first thing she says after the silence is usually the real issue.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Saying "It's going to be fine! Leo is great!" too early. This invalidates the parent's current pain.
  • The "Information Dump": Giving her more resources or book titles. She is already drowning in information; she needs integration.
  • The "Me Too" Story: Spending 10 minutes talking about your own child's diagnosis. A brief mention is okay for rapport, but keep the focus on Elena.
  • Solving the School Issue First: Elena's internal state is the priority. If she isn't regulated, she can't advocate for Leo at school effectively.
Emma's Tip

If you feel the urge to "fix" her pain, remind yourself: You are a coach, not a magician. You are there to walk beside her, not to carry her.

Success Markers

How do you know this session went well? Look for these "shifts" in your client:

  1. Physical Regulation: Her breathing slows down, or her shoulders drop from her ears.
  2. Shift in Language: Moving from "I failed" (shame) to "This is hard" (acknowledgment).
  3. Narrowed Focus: She stops talking about Leo at age 25 and starts talking about what she can do this Tuesday.
  4. Connection: She says something like, "I feel like I can finally breathe," or "Thank you for not just giving me another to-do list."
Emma's Tip

You're becoming a real coach! Every time you resist the urge to 'fix' and instead 'listen,' you are providing a premium service that most people never receive in their daily lives.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to acknowledge Elena's "what-if" thinking rather than correcting it immediately?

Correcting her immediately (e.g., "He'll be fine!") can feel dismissive and shut down the 'Empathy Bridge.' Acknowledging it shows you are a safe person who can handle her darkest fears, which builds the trust necessary for later coaching work.

2. What is the primary purpose of the "10% weight" question?

It helps the client move from "global overwhelm" (everything is broken) to "local action" (one thing I can manage). It reduces the cognitive load and gives them a sense of agency.

3. If Elena starts crying and can't speak, what is your best coaching move?

Offer "supportive silence." You might say, "I'm right here with you. Take all the time you need." This demonstrates that her emotions are not "too much" for the coaching space.

4. Which of the following is a sign of "Physical Regulation" in a client?

A visible softening of the jaw, a deep sigh, or a change from rapid, high-chest breathing to slower, diaphragmatic breathing.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy is the Foundation: In a crisis, emotional safety must come before strategic planning.
  • Watch the Pacing: Slow, deliberate speech from the coach acts as an external regulator for a panicked client.
  • Avoid the Fixer Trap: Your value is in helping the parent find their next step, not giving them your solution.
  • Focus on the Present: Gently pull the client back from "future-tripping" to the immediate, manageable present.

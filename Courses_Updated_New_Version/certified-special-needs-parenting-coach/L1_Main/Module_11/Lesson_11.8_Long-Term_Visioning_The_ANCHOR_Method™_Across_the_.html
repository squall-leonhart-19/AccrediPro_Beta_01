Common Pitfalls to Avoid

When you're starting out, your "Fixer" instinct will be screaming. Watch out for these four traps:

  • The "Teaching" Trap: Explaining the science of ADHD for 20 minutes instead of coaching Elena's emotions.
  • Skipping Normalization: Moving straight to "How do we fix the homework?" before Elena feels seen.
  • Toxic Positivity: Saying "It will all be fine!" instead of "This is incredibly hard, and you are doing it."
  • Ignoring the Body: Focusing only on Elena's thoughts and ignoring her tight chest and clenched jaw.
Emma's Pro Insight

If you feel yourself rushing to give a "solution," stop. Ask the client: "What do you need most in this momentâ€”a plan, or just for someone to know how heavy this feels?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we focus on the parent's physical sensations (Observation) before creating a homework plan?

Because a dysregulated parent cannot regulate a dysregulated child. By identifying the parent's "bracing" response, we can implement an Anchor habit that prevents the "co-escalation" cycle.

2. What is the primary goal of "Advanced Normalization"?

To remove the shame associated with biological stress responses, particularly for parents with professional backgrounds who feel they "should" be able to handle the situation.

3. If Elena says, "I just want a list of consequences that work," how should an Advanced Coach respond?

Validate the desire for a quick fix, then pivot back to the 'A' (Assessment) pillar: "I hear how much you want a solution that works right now. To make sure that list actually sticks, we first need to assess why the current environment makes those consequences feel like 'noise' to Leo."

4. What is the "Strategic Pause" and why is it used?

It is a 3-5 second silence after a powerful statement or question. It allows the client's nervous system to move from "defensive processing" to "reflective processing."

Key Takeaways

  • The Parent is the Anchor: Your coaching focus must remain on the parent's regulation as the foundation for child behavior change.
  • Shame is the Enemy of Progress: Use the Normalization pillar early and often to strip away the "shoulds."
  • Pacing Matters: Your voice is a tool. Slow down and use pauses to signal safety.
  • Micro-Habits Over Big Plans: One 30-second habit (like jaw-releasing before pick-up) is more effective than a 5-page behavior plan.
  • You Are a Guide, Not a Fixer: Your value lies in holding the framework (ANCHOR) so the client can find their own resilience.

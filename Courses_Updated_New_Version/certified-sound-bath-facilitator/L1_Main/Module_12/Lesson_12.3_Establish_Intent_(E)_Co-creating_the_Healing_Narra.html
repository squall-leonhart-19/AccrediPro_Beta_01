When a client articulates a specific goal, they are engaging the Reticular Activating System (RAS)‚Äîa network of neurons in the brainstem that filters incoming sensory data.

The Resonance Interview: Identifying the Core

The goal of the intake interview is to move from the symptom to the source. If a client says, "I want to feel less stressed," that is a symptom. The source might be a lack of boundaries, a fear of the future, or a disconnected sense of self.

Socratic Questioning for Sound Healing

Use these three specific levels of inquiry to deepen the narrative:

  • The Surface: "What physical sensations are you noticing in your body today?"
  • The Emotion: "If that tension in your shoulders had a voice, what would it be saying?"
  • The Shift: "What would it feel like in your body if that tension were replaced by a sense of absolute safety?"

The Ethics of Co-creation

As a facilitator, you are a guide, not a guru. It is vital that the client remains the primary agent of their healing. If you "assign" an intention to a client, you may inadvertently create resistance or bypass their own inner wisdom.

Ethical Guidelines for Intent:

  • Non-Directive Guidance: Offer suggestions only when the client is stuck. "Would it feel right to focus on [Concept], or does something else feel more true?"
  • Inclusivity: Ensure the intent respects the client's cultural and spiritual background.
  • Scope of Practice: If a client's intent involves deep psychological trauma, hold the space sonically but remind them that sound is a complementary modality to professional therapy.
Coach Tip: Overcoming Imposter Syndrome

If you feel like you aren't "expert" enough to help a client set an intent, remember: The client is the expert on their life. Your job is simply to hold the mirror so they can see their own truth. This takes the pressure off you and empowers them.

The Sacred Transition

Once the intent is established, how do you move into the sound? This transition is a critical "bridge" for the nervous system. A jarring shift from talking to loud gongs can trigger a sympathetic (fight/flight) response.

The 3-Step Transition Protocol:

  1. The Verbal Seal: Have the client repeat their Sonic Affirmation three times‚Äîonce out loud, once in a whisper, and once silently.
  2. The Breath Anchor: Lead a 4-7-8 breath cycle to down-regulate the Vagus nerve.
  3. The First Strike: Begin with the most gentle instrument (usually a high-frequency chime or a soft crystal bowl) to signal the start of the Sonic Induction (S) phase.
Coach Tip: The Silent Moment

After the client says their intent, leave 30 seconds of absolute silence before you touch an instrument. This silence allows the intention to "marinate" in the field you've co-created.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the Reticular Activating System (RAS) significant in intention-setting?

The RAS acts as a biological filter for the brain. By setting a specific intent, the client primes their brain to prioritize and absorb the specific frequencies and sensations associated with their healing goal.

2. What is the primary difference between a "Surface" question and a "Shift" question in the Resonance Interview?

A Surface question identifies current physical sensations or symptoms, while a Shift question asks the client to imagine and describe the somatic feeling of the desired outcome (the resolution).

3. True or False: A facilitator should decide the client's intention if the client is feeling indecisive.

False. Ethically, the client must be the primary agent. The facilitator can offer options or themes, but the client must choose the one that resonates as "true" for them.

4. What is the benefit of using "I AM" at the start of a Sonic Affirmation?

"I AM" statements speak to the limbic system in the present tense, signaling to the nervous system that the desired state is an immediate reality rather than a future possibility.

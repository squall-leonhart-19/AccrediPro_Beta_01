Check Your Understanding

1. If Diane's breathing remains shallow after 10 minutes of playing, which R.E.S.O.N.A.T.E. step should you focus on?

You should focus on Adjust. This involves changing your playing technique (perhaps slowing the tempo or using a softer mallet) and providing a verbal grounding cue to encourage deeper respiration.

2. Why is the "Receive" phase particularly important for a career-changer like Diane?

Career-changers often carry "professional armor" or stress from high-pressure roles. The Receive phase builds the necessary trust and safety, allowing them to drop that armor so the sound frequencies can actually do their work.

3. What is a specific physiological marker that Diane has shifted from Sympathetic to Parasympathetic dominance?

Specific markers include a visible softening of the facial muscles, a slower and deeper respiratory rate, and the "parasympathetic sigh" (a long, involuntary exhale).

4. True or False: You should use high-frequency, loud crystal bowls immediately to "break through" Diane's mental clutter.

False. For a client with hyper-vigilance, loud or high-pitched sounds can be perceived as a threat. You should start with grounding, lower-frequency bowls at a low volume to establish safety first.

Key Takeaways

  • Language Matters: Use "invitational" language to help high-achieving clients lower their guard.
  • Notice the Nuance: Active facilitation requires constant monitoring of the client's physical responses (the "Notice" phase).
  • The Power of the Pivot: Be prepared to Adjust your planned "Orchestration" if the client isn't responding as expected.
  • Professional Presence: Your own calm, grounded energy is the "anchor" for the entire R.E.S.O.N.A.T.E. process.

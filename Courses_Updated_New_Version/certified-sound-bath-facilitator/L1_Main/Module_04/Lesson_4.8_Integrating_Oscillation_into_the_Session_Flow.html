Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-Explaining Physics: Don't talk about Hertz (Hz) unless they ask. Focus on the feeling of the sound.
  • Too Much Friction: Creating oscillations that are too fast (dissonant) can actually cause anxiety in some clients. Start with harmonious, slow beats.
  • Ignoring the Environment: Ensure the room is warm. Women in their 40s and 50s are often sensitive to temperature shifts during sound work.
  • Rushing the Intake: If you don't listen to their "why," the sound won't land as deeply. Spend 5 minutes just listening first.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it beneficial to use metaphors like "a motor running" when talking to a stressed client?

Metaphors bridge the gap between complex science and the client's lived experience, making the therapy feel accessible and reducing the "performance anxiety" of trying to understand the physics.

2. If a client like Beth says the oscillating sound feels "a bit wobbly," how should you respond?

Validate her observation! Explain that the "wobble" is exactly what helps the brain entrain. You might say: "That wobble is the beat that's going to help guide your brainwaves into a slower, more relaxed state."

3. What is the target brainwave state for a client struggling with "wired but tired" syndrome?

The Alpha-Theta border. This is the state of deep relaxation and light meditation where the body can begin to lower cortisol levels.

4. True or False: You should always use the fastest oscillations possible to "shock" the nervous system into relaxing.

False. Fast, dissonant oscillations can cause hyperarousal or "frequency anxiety." It is better to start with slow, harmonious oscillations for clients already in a state of stress.

The Physics of the 'Gong Bloom'

Unlike most instruments that have a linear decay (getting quieter until they stop), a high-quality gong exhibits a phenomenon known as the Gong Bloom. When you strike a gong, the initial fundamental note is heard, but as the energy travels through the metal, it excites higher partials and overtones.

This "bloom" is essentially a non-linear oscillation. As the metal continues to vibrate, waves collide with one another, creating new frequencies that weren't present in the initial strike. This is why a gong often sounds "busier" or more complex several seconds after it has been struck. For the facilitator, understanding this timing is critical; you must learn to play the energy of the previous strike rather than just hitting the metal repeatedly.

Coach Tip: Listening for the Bloom

Don't rush the next strike. Strike the gong, then lean in and listen. Notice how the sound changes from a deep bass to a shimmering high frequency. Wait for that high-frequency "shimmer" to peak before adding more energy. This creates a more organic, breathing soundscape.

Mallet Mastery & Oscillation

To create a professional sonic wash, you must move beyond the simple strike. Your mallets are the conduits for frequency oscillation. Here are the three essential techniques for this module:

1. The Flick (The Spark)

A quick, snapping motion of the wrist. This technique is used to "brighten" the sound of the gong by exciting the higher frequencies on the outer rim. It adds a "sparkle" to the deeper fundamental notes.

2. The Roll (The Foundation)

Using two mallets in a rapid, alternating sequence. This is the primary way to build the Wall of Sound. The key is consistency. If your rolls are uneven, the oscillation becomes jagged, which can trigger a "startle" response rather than a "flow" state.

3. The Sustained Crescendo

Starting at the lowest possible volume and slowly building energy over 2-3 minutes. This allows the client's nervous system to acclimate to the volume, preventing the "fight or flight" response that occurs with sudden loud noises.

Coach Tip: Grip Pressure

Keep your grip loose! If you hold your mallets too tightly, you absorb the vibrations into your own body, and the gong will sound "choked." A loose grip allows the mallet to bounce naturally, creating a much richer, oscillating tone.

Friction Mallets (Flumis)

Friction mallets, often called Flumis, are rubber-headed mallets that are dragged across the surface of the gong rather than struck against it. This creates a sound reminiscent of whale song or high-pitched singing.

In the R.E.S.O.N.A.T.E. Method‚Ñ¢, we use Flumis specifically for Superficial Energetic Blockages. Because Flumis produce extremely high frequencies with very little "thump" or fundamental bass, they are excellent for addressing tension held in the skin, fascia, and the outer layers of the biofield.

Application Strategy:

  • Circular Motion: Creates a swirling, ethereal effect that helps "unstick" stagnant energy.
  • Linear Motion: Can be used to "pull" energy from the center of the gong to the edge, symbolizing a release.

Managing the 'Gong Flush'

The Gong Flush is the systemic physiological release that occurs when a high-intensity sonic wash is successfully integrated. Clients may experience sudden temperature changes, emotional releases (tears or laughter), or physical twitching as the nervous system discharges stored tension.

As a professional, managing this "flush" requires extreme sensitivity. A 2021 study on Vibroacoustic Effects on Cortisol (n=124) showed that while moderate sound pressure levels decreased cortisol by 18%, sound that was perceived as "unexpectedly loud" actually caused a 12% increase in cortisol.

Coach Tip: The Safe Exit

Never stop the gong abruptly at high volume. This is called a "sonic cliff" and it is jarring to the nervous system. Always taper the volume down slowly, allowing the client to "land" softly back into the silence.

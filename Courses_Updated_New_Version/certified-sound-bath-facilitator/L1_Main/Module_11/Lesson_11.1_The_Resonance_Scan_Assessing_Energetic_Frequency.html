Techniques for Sensing the Field

There are two primary ways to perform the Resonance Scan. As you develop your "sonic ears," you will likely use a combination of both.

1. Hand Scanning (Sensing Density)

The human hand contains thousands of sensory receptors. By moving your hands 2-6 inches above the client's body, you can detect changes in temperature, "tingling," or a feeling of "thickness" in the air. This thickness often indicates energetic stagnation or physical inflammation.

2. Vocal Toning (The Sonic Sonar)

Vocal toning is the most advanced form of scanning. By humming a steady, neutral tone while moving your focus across the client's body, you will notice the sound of your own voice change. If you hit a dissonant area, your voice may crack, go flat, or feel "absorbed" by the client's field. This is acoustic biofeedback in its purest form.

Identifying Sonic Dissonance

What exactly is "Sonic Dissonance"? In music, dissonance is a lack of harmony. In the human body, it represents a frequency mismatch between the organ's healthy state and its current state.

Research from the HeartMath Institute shows that when we are in a state of frustration or anxiety, our heart rhythms become "incoherent" or dissonant. This incoherence radiates into the biofield. During your scan, you are looking for these "pockets of incoherence."

  • Physical Dissonance: Often feels "hard" or "sharp" during a hand scan.
  • Emotional Dissonance: Often feels "heavy" or "cloudy."
  • Mental Dissonance: Often felt around the head as a "buzzing" or "static" sensation.
Coach Tip: The Neutral Observer

When you find dissonance, do not try to "fix" it immediately during the scan. Your job during the (R) phase is simply to notice. If you jump into "fixing" mode too early, you lose the baseline data needed for the rest of the RESONATE Method‚Ñ¢.

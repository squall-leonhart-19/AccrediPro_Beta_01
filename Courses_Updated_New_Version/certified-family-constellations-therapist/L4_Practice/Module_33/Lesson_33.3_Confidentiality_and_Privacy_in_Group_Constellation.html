Navigating the 'Circle of Silence'

In the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, the step of Reveal Entanglements often brings to light family secrets: addictions, hidden deaths, or financial improprieties. Because these sessions involve multiple observers (representatives), privacy becomes a complex legal dance. We refer to this as the Circle of Silence.

It is vital to distinguish between legal confidentiality (which applies to you as the practitioner) and ethical privacy (which applies to the participants). While you are legally bound by your professional certification and local laws, you cannot legally guarantee that a participant won't talk. However, you can create a legal framework that makes them liable if they do.

Coach Tip: The Verbal Ritual

Always begin every group workshop with a verbal "Confidentiality Ritual." Ask every person in the room to look each other in the eye and verbally agree: "What is seen here, stays here. What is heard here, stays here." This psychological contract is often more powerful than the written one.

Group Participation Agreements

To protect the Reveal process, your clients need to sign a Group Participation Agreement. This is separate from the individual Informed Consent. It specifically addresses the presence of others.

A professional agreement should include a Non-Disclosure Clause. This clause states that participants may share their own experience and feelings, but they are strictly prohibited from sharing the names, identities, or specific ancestral stories of other participants.

Legal Risks of Recording Sessions

Many practitioners want to record sessions for the client's future "Settle the Resolution" integration. However, in a group setting, multi-party consent is a legal minefield. If 12 people are in the room, and 11 consent to recording but 1 does not, you cannot record the session.

The "Field" is Fluid: Even if everyone consents at the start, a representative might become highly emotional during the session and regret being filmed. Legally, they can withdraw consent at any time, requiring you to delete the entire recording.

Coach Tip: Safer Alternatives

Instead of video recording, offer to take a high-quality photo of the final healing image (the "Restorative Image"). This captures the spatial resolution without the legal risks of audio/video of other participants.

Handling Breaches of Confidentiality

If a breach occurs, your response defines your professionalism. You must have a "Breach SOP" (Standard Operating Procedure):

  1. Identification: Verify exactly what was shared and by whom.
  2. Containment: Contact the breaching party immediately to demand cessation of sharing.
  3. Notification: Inform the affected client (transparency is key to the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢).
  4. Documentation: Record the incident, your response, and the outcome in your compliance log.
Coach Tip: The "Ancestral Weight" Reframe

When someone breaches confidentiality, reframe it systemically: "By sharing another's story, you are taking on the weight of their system. For your own peace, leave their story here." This often stops gossip more effectively than legal threats.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary legal difference between the practitioner's and the participant's duty of confidentiality?

The practitioner has a professional and legal duty based on their certification and law, while participants are bound by the Group Participation Agreement (contractual duty). You cannot guarantee participant behavior, but you can make them legally liable for breaches.

2. Why is recording a group constellation session considered a high legal risk?

It requires multi-party consent from everyone in the room. If even one person (including a representative) withdraws consent during or after the session, the recording must be destroyed, potentially creating a liability if it has already been shared.

3. What is the "Double-Lock Rule" for physical client data?

It is a standard security protocol where sensitive physical data (like genograms) must be stored in a locked container (cabinet/safe) which is itself inside a locked room (office).

4. According to the lesson, what is a "Breach SOP"?

A Standard Operating Procedure for handling privacy violations, involving four steps: Identification, Containment, Notification of the affected client, and Documentation in a compliance log.

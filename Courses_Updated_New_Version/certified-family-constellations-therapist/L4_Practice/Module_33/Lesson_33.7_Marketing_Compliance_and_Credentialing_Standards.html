The Ethics of Professional Identity

Your professional identity is defined by how you represent your credentials. The title Certified Family Constellations Therapist‚Ñ¢ is a trademarked certification title. However, the word "Therapist" is a protected term in several U.S. states and international jurisdictions (e.g., Germany, parts of Canada).

If you are not a licensed mental health professional (LCSW, LMFT, Psychologist), you must be diligent in how you present this title. In "Title Protected" states, you should use the full trademarked title or append a clarifying statement:

  • Recommended Usage: "Jane Doe, Certified Family Constellations Therapist‚Ñ¢ (Non-Clinical Systemic Facilitator)"
  • Avoid: "Jane Doe, Family Therapist" (This implies state licensure you may not have).

Avoiding the "Cure" Trap

In the eyes of the FDA and FTC, only a drug or a licensed medical procedure can "cure," "treat," or "prevent" a disease. As a systemic practitioner, you are working with the Knowing Field and systemic entanglements, not biological pathologies.

FTC and Testimonial Ethics

Testimonials are your most powerful marketing asset, but they are also a legal lightning rod. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) updated its guidelines in 2023 to be even stricter regarding "Results Not Typical" disclosures.

The 3 Pillars of Compliant Testimonials:

  1. Authenticity: You must have proof that the testimonial is real. Retain the original email or video.
  2. No "Cure" Claims by Proxy: If a client says, "Sarah cured my cancer through a constellation," you cannot post it. You are responsible for the claims made by your clients on your platform.
  3. Clear Disclosures: Use a clear disclaimer near testimonials.

Standard Testimonial Disclaimer

"These testimonials represent the individual experiences of clients. Results are not typical and may vary based on individual systemic readiness and participation. Family Constellations is an educational and systemic process, not a substitute for medical or psychological treatment."

The 'Orders of Helping': Hellinger‚Äôs Ethical Framework

Bert Hellinger observed that helping is an art that follows specific systemic laws. When a facilitator violates these laws, they become "entangled" with the client, often leading to practitioner burnout and client dependency. The Orders of Helping ensure the facilitator remains a clear vessel for the Field.

The Order The Principle The Facilitator's Stance
First Order Giving only what one has. Avoid "magical thinking" or promising outcomes beyond systemic reach.
Second Order Respecting the client's fate. Do not try to "save" the client from their destiny or their ancestors.
Third Order Adult-to-Adult stance. Avoid the "Parent-Child" dynamic where the client becomes dependent.
Fourth Order Systemic Empathy. The facilitator must love everyone in the system equally (including the "perpetrators").
Fifth Order The Empty Center. Helping without judgment or a personal agenda for the "resolution."
Coach Tip

Many practitioners struggle with the Second Order. Your desire to "fix" a client's pain is often a projection of your own unresolved systemic trauma. When you feel a desperate urge to save a client, pause and return to your Empty Center. Respecting their fate is the highest form of love.

Boundaries and Scope of Practice

As a Certified Family Constellations Therapist‚Ñ¢, it is vital to distinguish your work from clinical psychology or psychiatry unless you hold those specific licenses. When to Refer Out

Systemic work can be intense. You must have a "Referral Network" of professionals for clients who present with:

  • Active Suicidal Ideation: Immediate referral to emergency services or a licensed crisis counselor.
  • Psychosis or Dissociative Disorders: The Field can be destabilizing for those with fragile ego structures.
  • Active Substance Addiction: Clients must be sober during sessions to process somatic shifts.
  • Severe Clinical Depression: Constellations complement, but do not replace, medical intervention.

Informed Consent & The 'Settle' Phase

Informed consent in this modality is unique. You must manage client expectations regarding the Settle Phase (the 4-12 weeks following a session). Clients must understand that a constellation is not a "quick fix" but a systemic realignment.

Your Informed Consent Document should include:

  • The nature of Representative Perception and how it works.
  • The potential for temporary emotional upheaval after a session.
  • The "Silence Protocol": Encouraging clients not to discuss the session for at least 3 days to allow the image to settle.
  • Clear cancellation and refund policies.
Coach Tip

For the ambitious career changer, having a professional, signed Informed Consent form is the first step toward financial freedom. It signals to your client that this is a premium, professional service, justifying your $250+ session fee.

Ethical Considerations for Third Parties

A common ethical dilemma: "Can I do a constellation for my husband/child/boss without them being here?"

The R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ protocol is clear: We only work with the "system" through the client's own entanglement. We do not "spy" on others in the Field for the sake of curiosity. We only observe third parties as they relate to the client's healing movement.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "Second Order of Helping" and why is it difficult for new facilitators?

The Second Order is "Respecting the Client's Fate." It is difficult because facilitators often have a "helper's itch" to save the client from pain, which actually weakens the client and violates the systemic order.

2. Why is the "Silence Protocol" part of informed consent?

Discussing a constellation too soon (the "Settle" phase) can dissipate the energetic power of the healing image. Silence allows the new systemic order to take root in the client's soul and unconscious mind.

3. When should a Constellator refer a client to a medical professional?

Immediate referral is required for active suicidal ideation, psychosis, active addiction, or severe clinical instability that prevents the client from being grounded in the present moment.

4. What is the primary purpose of the "Deep Bow" in energetic hygiene?

The Deep Bow is a somatic ritual that acknowledges the greatness of the client's system while simultaneously setting a boundary, ensuring the facilitator does not carry the client's ancestral burdens home.

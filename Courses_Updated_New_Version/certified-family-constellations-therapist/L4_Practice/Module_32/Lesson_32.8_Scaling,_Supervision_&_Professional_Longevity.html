Handling Common Objections

Objections are rarely about the money; they are usually about the client's fear of failure or their own internal "systemic" resistance to change.

Objection The Systemic Truth Your Response
"I need to talk to my husband." Seeking permission; fear of autonomy. "I completely understand. When you talk to him, what is the main thing you want him to understand about why this matters to you now?"
"It's a lot of money right now." Prioritizing others over self-healing. "I hear you. If we look at the cost of not changing‚Äîthe lost revenue in your business and the stress you're carrying‚Äîwhat is that costing you annually?"
"Will this really work for me?" Imposter syndrome; fear of being "unfixable." "This work is about seeing what is true. We aren't 'fixing' you, because you aren't broken. We are simply clearing the path that‚Äôs already there."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of Phase 1 (Rapport) in the discovery call?

To establish why the client reached out now and to create a "container" or agreement for how the call will proceed, ensuring the practitioner leads the session.

2. If a client says, "I need to think about it," what is the best systemic follow-up question?

"I understand. Usually, when someone needs to think about it, there's a specific piece of information missing or a lingering doubt. Is it the financial investment, the time, or perhaps a fear that this won't work for your specific family history?"

3. Why is it recommended to offer packages (e.g., 3 months) rather than single sessions?

Systemic change requires integration time. Packages ensure client commitment, provide better clinical outcomes, and create financial stability for the practitioner.

4. What should you do immediately after stating your price?

Remain silent. This allows the client to sit with the investment and respond from a place of choice rather than being "sold" to.

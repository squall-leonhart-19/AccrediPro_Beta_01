Managing the 'Orders of Love' in Business

Bert Hellinger‚Äôs Orders of Love are not just for families; they apply to the practitioner-client relationship. Violating these orders in business creates professional entanglement, which leads to burnout and client harm.

1. The Law of Precedence (The Client Comes First)

In a professional setting, the client‚Äôs need for resolution precedes the facilitator's need for recognition, income, or validation. If you find yourself needing a "breakthrough" to feel like a good facilitator, you have stepped out of order.

2. The Balance of Giving and Taking

This is where your pricing strategy becomes an ethical tool. If you undercharge (over-giving), the client may feel a systemic debt they cannot repay, leading them to leave the process prematurely. A clear fee structure restores the balance of giving and taking.

Case Study: The Boundary Blur

Practitioner: Elena (52), former school administrator turned Constellator.

The Situation: Elena began working with a client, "Sarah," who was going through a messy divorce. Sarah began texting Elena at night for emotional support. Elena, wanting to be "helpful," responded frequently.

The Ethical Breach: By becoming a "friend" or "rescuer," Elena violated the Order of Precedence. She was no longer a facilitator holding the field; she was an enmeshed participant. Sarah eventually stopped attending sessions because the systemic "weight" of the friendship became too heavy.

Resolution: Elena implemented a "Communication Policy" (Lesson 1) and referred Sarah to a divorce support group, resetting the professional order in their next session.

Grievance Procedures & Group Dynamics

Workshops are high-intensity environments. When the "Knowing Field" reveals difficult truths, participants may experience emotional "flooding." A robust ethical framework includes a clear path for grievances.

  • Pre-Workshop Briefing: Explicitly state that "strong emotions may arise" and identify the "safe exit" protocol.
  • The Grievance Path: If a participant feels harmed by a representative's actions or a facilitator's sentence, they must have a designated way to report this (e.g., a follow-up call or a formal email process).

üí° Income Insight: Practitioners who have clear, written grievance and refund policies are able to command higher fees (often $350-$500 for a 1-on-1 constellation) because they project a level of professional legitimacy that "hobbyists" lack.

Mandated Reporting & Crisis Protocols

While we work in the "Field," we live in the "Law." Depending on your jurisdiction and prior licensing, you may be a mandated reporter. Even if you are not legally classified as one, ethical best practices require a Crisis Intervention Protocol.

Your Crisis Kit should include:

  1. Local Crisis Hotline numbers.
  2. A "Safety Plan" template for clients to fill out if they feel overwhelmed post-session.
  3. A signed "Informed Consent" that outlines the limits of confidentiality (e.g., harm to self or others).

The Ethics of Representation & Systemic Dignity

In Family Constellations, we often represent people who are deceased or not present. This carries a specific ethical weight. We must protect the dignity of the system.

  • No Gossip: Information revealed in the field stays in the field. Using client stories for marketing without extreme anonymization is a violation of the family soul's privacy.
  • Respect for the "Other": Even when representing a "perpetrator" in a system, the facilitator must ensure the representative maintains a stance of systemic inclusion rather than moral judgment.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks for your advice on whether they should leave their husband based on a constellation. What is the ethical response?

The ethical response is to decline giving direct life advice. You should state: "The constellation shows the systemic movement, but the choice belongs to you and your soul. I cannot take that weight from you." This respects the client's autonomy and your scope of practice.

2. How does the "Balance of Giving and Taking" apply to setting your session rates?

If you charge too little, the client cannot "repay" the value of the systemic shift, creating an imbalance where they may feel unconsciously compelled to leave or "sabotage" the results. Professional fees facilitate a clean energetic exchange.

3. What is the "Ethics of Representation" regarding marketing?

It requires protecting the privacy of the family system. You should never share details that could identify a client's family members, as those members did not consent to have their systemic "entanglements" made public.

4. When must a facilitator break confidentiality?

Confidentiality must be broken when there is a clear and imminent risk of the client harming themselves or others, or in cases of suspected child/elder abuse (subject to local jurisdictional laws).

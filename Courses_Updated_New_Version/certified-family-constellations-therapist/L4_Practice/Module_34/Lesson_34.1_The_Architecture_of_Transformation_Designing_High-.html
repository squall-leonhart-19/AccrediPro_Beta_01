Defining the Workshop Container

As an L4 practitioner, your first decision is the shape of the container. The duration and format of your workshop dictate the depth of the systemic work possible. Unlike traditional coaching workshops, Family Constellations require time for the "Field" to open and for the representatives to settle into their roles.

A 2022 survey of professional facilitators found that workshops exceeding 12 hours of total contact time showed a 64% higher rate of long-term integration compared to 4-hour "introductory" sessions.

The R.O.O.T.S. Curriculum Framework

A high-impact workshop isn't just a collection of random constellations. It must follow a psychological and systemic arc. We use the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ to ensure every participant‚Äîwhether they have a personal constellation or not‚Äîexperiences a shift.

  • Reveal (Morning Session): Focus on identifying the hidden loyalties. Use "blind constellations" or group exercises to reveal who is excluded in the collective field.
  • Observe (Mid-Morning): Teach the group how to sense the field. This lowers resistance and prepares them to be effective representatives.
  • Order (Afternoon Session 1): Move into the heavy lifting. This is where the primary constellations happen, restoring the Orders of Love.
  • Transmute (Afternoon Session 2): Rituals of release. Use the "Deep Bow" or "Healing Sentences" as a group to shift inherited burdens.
  • Settle (Closing): Final integration. Stillness and the "Restorative Image" ensure participants leave grounded, not "blown open."

Logistics and the Sacred Temenos

In ancient Greek, a Temenos is a piece of land marked off as a sacred precinct. In Family Constellations, the workshop room is the Temenos. The logistics are not just about "comfort"; they are about energetic safety.

Venue Selection Criteria

When selecting a venue for L4 work, consider the following non-negotiables:

  • Privacy: No glass walls where strangers can peer in. The field requires a closed container.
  • Acoustics: Avoid "echoey" rooms. Soft surfaces (carpets, curtains) help hold the emotional resonance.
  • Space for Movement: You need enough "floor space" for 10-15 people to stand in a circle with a large empty center for the constellation.
Coach Tip

Always "clear" the room before participants arrive. Whether you use sage, sound, or simply a silent meditation, set the intention that this room is a Judgment-Free Zone where only the truth of the system is welcome.

Pricing and Enrollment Models

Many practitioners struggle with pricing because they view it as "charging for healing." At the L4 level, you must reframe this: You are charging for the expertise of holding a safe, transformational container.

Consider the "Issue Holder" vs. "Representative" pricing model:

  • Issue Holders ($$$): These participants are guaranteed a full constellation for their specific issue. This is premium pricing.
  • Representatives ($): These participants participate in the field and represent for others. They often receive profound "secondary healing" but pay a lower rate.

Income Example:
- 5 Issue Holders at $450 = $2,250
- 10 Representatives at $150 = $1,500
Total Revenue for 1 Day: $3,750

Coach Tip

Avoid "early bird" discounts that devalue the work. Instead, offer "Pay in Full" bonuses, such as a 30-minute integration call post-workshop. This adds value without cutting your revenue.

Participant Intake Protocols

Not everyone is ready for a group workshop. As the facilitator, your primary ethical duty is to the safety of the field. A "bad fit" participant can disrupt the resonance for everyone else.

Your intake form should screen for:

  1. Psychological Stability: Are they currently in a crisis? Family Constellations can be highly activating.
  2. Systemic Ripeness: Have they done any preliminary work? (Ideally, they have had at least one 1-on-1 session with you).
  3. Physical Capability: Can they stand for 20-30 minutes as a representative?
Coach Tip

If someone is currently in active, untreated trauma or showing signs of personality disorders that seek to dominate the group's attention, refer them to 1-on-1 clinical support first. Protecting the group container is your first priority.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the "Settle" phase in the R.O.O.T.S. workshop curriculum?

The "Settle" phase focuses on integration, stillness, and the Restorative Image. Its purpose is to ensure participants leave the workshop grounded and centered, rather than emotionally overwhelmed or "blown open" by the deep systemic work.

2. Why is a "closed container" (privacy) essential for a Family Constellations venue?

The "Knowing Field" requires a sacred precinct (Temenos) free from outside observation or interruption. Privacy allows participants to feel safe enough to enter deep emotional states and ensures the energetic resonance of the group is not diluted by external "noise" or observers.

3. Define the difference between "Issue Holders" and "Representatives" in a workshop model.

Issue Holders pay a premium to have a full constellation dedicated to their specific systemic entanglement. Representatives pay a lower fee to participate in the circle and stand in for others' family members; they often experience "secondary healing" by mirroring dynamics relevant to their own lives.

4. According to the lesson, what is the most important factor in protecting the group's safety?

The most important factor is the Intake Protocol. Screening participants for psychological stability and systemic "ripeness" ensures that the individuals entering the circle are capable of holding the work without disrupting the collective field.

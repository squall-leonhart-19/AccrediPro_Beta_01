Defining the Scope of Practice

One of the most common sources of "imposter syndrome" for new practitioners is the fear of crossing into clinical territory without a medical license. It is vital to understand that Family Constellations is a systemic intervention, not a medical or clinical psychological treatment.

While a psychotherapist may focus on the intra-psychic (what is happening inside the individual's mind), a Constellator focuses on the systemic (what is happening between the elements of the family soul). We do not "treat" depression; we "reveal" the systemic entanglement that may manifest as depression.

The 'Circle of Trust': Confidentiality Protocols

In a group Constellation, the "Field" is shared. This creates a unique ethical challenge: how do we protect the privacy of the client when 15 other people are standing in their family's "shoes"?

The Circle of Trust protocol requires that every participant signs a non-disclosure agreement (NDA) that covers not just the "facts" of the client's story, but the "movements" of the Constellation. What is revealed in the Field stays in the Field.

Facilitator Responsibility in Group Settings:

  • Pre-Session Briefing: Explicitly state that representatives must not "analyze" or discuss the client's session with them during breaks or after the workshop.
  • Post-Session Integration: Remind the client that speaking about the Constellation too soon can "bleed out" the healing energy. Silence is a systemic boundary.

Case Study: The Boundary of Silence

Practitioner: Elena (52), former HR Executive

Scenario: Elena facilitated a workshop where a participant, "Susan," represented the "Abusive Father" in another client's Constellation. After the workshop, Susan approached the client in the parking lot to offer "insight" she felt while in the role.

Intervention: Elena intervened, gently but firmly reminding Susan of the workshop agreement. She explained that "representative perception" belongs to the Field, not the individual, and that sharing it post-session interferes with the client's integration process.

Outcome: By enforcing the boundary, Elena protected the client's "Healing Image" and maintained her position as the authority in the space.

Managing Dual Relationships and Systemic Entanglements

In the wellness world, the lines between "friend," "colleague," and "client" often blur. In Systemic Constellations, a dual relationship isn't just an ethical "no-no"‚Äîit‚Äôs a systemic entanglement that can blind the facilitator.

If you are constellating for a close friend, you are already "in" their system. You cannot hold the "Empty Center" because you have a preference for their outcome. You are no longer observing the Field; you are participating in the family drama.

Since I'm in your life, I'm too close to the 'Field' to see it clearly for you." This builds massive trust and respect.

The Ethics of 'Ordering the System' (O)

In The R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, the "O" stands for Order the System. This applies to the therapeutic relationship itself. According to the Second Order of Love (Precedence/Hierarchy), the facilitator must remain "small" in relation to the client's fate, but "large" in relation to the process.

The Practitioner's Place:

  1. Respect for Fate: We do not "pity" the client. Pity makes the practitioner feel superior and the client feel weak. We stand in awe of their systemic burden.
  2. The Empty Center: We have no intention, no fear, and no love for a specific outcome. We only follow what the Field reveals.
  3. Financial Balance: Charging a professional fee ($150-$300+ per session) is an ethical act. It balances the "Giving and Taking" and prevents the practitioner from becoming a "parent" figure to the client.

Mandatory Reporting and Ancestral Narratives

As a practitioner, you may hear narratives of historical harm‚Äîancestral murders, hidden rapes, or past child abuse. It is critical to distinguish between Ancestral Narrative and Present-Day Danger.

  • Ancestral Narrative: Information about deceased ancestors is generally not subject to mandatory reporting, but must be handled with extreme somatic care to avoid re-traumatization.
  • Present-Day Danger: If a client discloses current child abuse, elder abuse, or intent to harm themselves or others, mandatory reporting laws apply. Your certification does not exempt you from state or federal laws regarding the safety of vulnerable populations.
Coach Tip

üí° Safety Tip: Always have a list of local crisis resources and clinical referrals ready. Professionalism means knowing when a client's needs exceed the systemic scope and require immediate clinical intervention.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "pity" considered an ethical violation in Systemic Constellations?

Pity violates the systemic hierarchy. It places the practitioner "above" the client, suggesting the practitioner is stronger than the client's fate. Ethically, we must respect the client's strength to carry their own burden while facilitating the resolution.

2. What is the primary difference between the scope of a Constellator and a Psychotherapist?

A Psychotherapist focuses on intra-psychic symptoms and clinical diagnosis, while a Constellator focuses on the spatial dynamics and entanglements within the family system (the "Field").

3. A close friend asks you to facilitate a Constellation for their recurring financial block. What is the most ethical response?

Refer them to a qualified colleague. Facilitating for a close friend creates a dual relationship where the practitioner's personal knowledge and "love" for the friend prevent them from maintaining the "Empty Center" required to observe the Field neutrally.

4. What does the "Circle of Trust" NDA cover in a group workshop?

It covers the client's personal facts AND the movements/revelations of the representatives during the session. It forbids participants from discussing the session with the client or others outside the workshop.

Key Takeaways

  • Scope Clarity: Always distinguish systemic facilitation from clinical therapy to maintain legal and professional safety.
  • Systemic Silence: Protecting the "Healing Image" through confidentiality is a core ethical duty.
  • The Empty Center: Ethical facilitation requires letting go of pity, intention, and personal preference for the client's outcome.
  • Legal Compliance: Systemic work does not override mandatory reporting laws for present-day harm.
  • Professional Value: Clear boundaries are the foundation of a high-fee, high-integrity practice.

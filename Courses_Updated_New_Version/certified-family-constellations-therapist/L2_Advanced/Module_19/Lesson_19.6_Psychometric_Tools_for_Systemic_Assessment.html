The Family System Questionnaire (FSQ)

In the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, the "Reveal" (R) phase often relies on the practitioner's ability to "see" the field. However, to anchor this in evidence-based practice, we use the Family System Questionnaire (FSQ). Developed by systemic researchers, the FSQ is a psychometric instrument designed to measure a client‚Äôs perception of their family dynamics across several key systemic dimensions.

The FSQ doesn't just ask "how do you feel about your mom?" It measures the functional architecture of the system. In a 2021 study involving over 400 participants, the FSQ successfully identified "systemic entanglement" patterns that correlated with clinical levels of anxiety and depression.

FSQ Dimension Systemic Meaning R.O.O.T.S. Phase
Cohesion The sense of belonging and "The First Order." Reveal (R)
Hierarchy The "Order of Precedence" (Parents vs. Children). Order (O)
Flexibility The system's ability to adapt to change/trauma. Observe (O)
Communication The flow of love and information within the field. Transmute (T)

Using the FSQ during your initial intake allows you to say to a client: "Based on your assessment scores, we see a significant disruption in the Hierarchy dimension." This professional language immediately shifts the session from "woo-woo" to "clinical systemic intervention," which is essential for charging premium rates ($200-$500 per session).

The Experience of Systemic Constellations (ESC) Scale

How do we measure what happens during a session? The Experience of Systemic Constellations (ESC) scale is a post-session psychometric tool that captures the phenomenological shift in a client. It measures three primary factors:

  • Factor 1: Image of Resolution. Does the client have a clear, internalized restorative image?
  • Factor 2: Emotional Relief. The somatic drop in tension (Transmutation).
  • Factor 3: Systemic Insight. The cognitive understanding of the entanglement.

Research published in the Journal of Systemic Therapies indicates that clients who score high on the "Image of Resolution" factor of the ESC show a 68% higher rate of long-term symptom remission at the 6-month follow-up compared to those who only achieved "insight" without the image.

Tracking 'Settle' (S) Outcomes

The final phase of the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ is Settle (S). This is where most practitioners lose track of their clients. By using psychometric tools, you can quantify the integration process. We recommend a "30-Day Systemic Integration Audit."

By comparing pre-session FSQ scores with a 30-day post-session audit, you can show the client their progress in black and white. For a woman pivoting from a career in nursing, this data-driven approach is her "security blanket"‚Äîit provides the objective proof that her new career is just as impactful as her previous medical work.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which factor of the ESC scale is the strongest predictor of long-term symptom remission?

The Image of Resolution factor. Research shows that internalizing the restorative image is more effective for long-term change than just gaining cognitive insight.

2. What does a high "Hierarchy" score on the FSQ typically indicate in systemic terms?

It indicates a disruption in the Order of Precedence, such as a child taking on a parental role (Parentification) or a younger sibling acting as the "elder."

3. How does "Systemic Literacy" benefit a practitioner's business?

It allows the practitioner to communicate with clinical professionals (doctors, therapists) and high-level clients in a way that builds legitimacy and allows for higher professional fees.

4. When should the "Settle" (S) audit ideally be performed?

Approximately 30 days post-session. This allows enough time for the "Restorative Image" to integrate into the client's daily life and neurobiology.

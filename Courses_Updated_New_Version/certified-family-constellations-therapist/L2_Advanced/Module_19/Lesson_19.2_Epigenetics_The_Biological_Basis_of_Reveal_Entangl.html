Mechanisms of Inheritance: DNA Methylation & Histone Modification

To understand how an entanglement is physically "stored," we must look past the DNA sequence itself and toward the epigenome. Think of DNA as the "hardware" of a computer and the epigenome as the "software" that tells the hardware which programs to run.

1. DNA Methylation

DNA methylation is the process where a methyl group (a small chemical tag) attaches to the DNA. This acts like a dimmer switch. When a gene is "highly methylated," it is typically turned off or silenced. In descendants of trauma survivors, genes responsible for regulating the stress response (like the glucocorticoid receptor) are often methylated differently, meaning the person is biologically "pre-set" to be more reactive to stress.

2. Histone Modification

DNA is wrapped around proteins called histones. If the DNA is wrapped tightly, the cell cannot "read" the gene. Trauma can cause these histones to tighten or loosen, effectively locking or unlocking ancestral behaviors, fears, and physiological responses. This is the biological mechanism of what we call a Systemic Entanglement.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a genetic mutation and an epigenetic change?

A genetic mutation is a change in the DNA sequence itself (the hardware), whereas an epigenetic change is a change in how that sequence is expressed or "read" (the software) without changing the underlying code.

2. What did Rachel Yehuda discover about the cortisol levels of Holocaust survivors' children?

She discovered they had lower baseline cortisol levels, making them more susceptible to stress and PTSD, mirroring the biological adaptations of their parents.

3. How does DNA methylation act like a "dimmer switch"?

Methyl groups attach to the DNA and can "silence" or turn down the expression of specific genes, such as those responsible for regulating stress.

4. Which gene is most commonly cited in transgenerational trauma research regarding stress regulation?

The FKBP5 gene.

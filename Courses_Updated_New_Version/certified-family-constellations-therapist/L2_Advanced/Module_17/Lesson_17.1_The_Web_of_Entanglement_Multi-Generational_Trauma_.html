Identifying 'Overlapping Loyalties'

In standard constellations, we often find a client entangled with a single "excluded" ancestor. However, in complex scenarios, we encounter Overlapping Loyalties. This occurs when the client‚Äôs current life struggle is a composite of multiple ancestral unfinished business items.

Imagine a client who suffers from both a "fear of starvation" and a "compulsion to hide." Through the Reveal (R) phase, we might discover a grandfather who died in a famine and a grandmother who had to hide her identity during a migration. The client is not just representing one; they are the "meeting point" for both burdens.

Coach Tip: The Sensation of Heaviness

When a client presents with overlapping loyalties, they often describe a feeling of being "crushed" or "immobile." This is a somatic clue that the weight is multi-directional. Don't rush to find one ancestor; look for the intersection of stories.

Mapping the 3-Generation Genogram

To navigate the web, the practitioner must look beyond the parents and grandparents. In Level 2 work, we utilize the Advanced Systemic Map. This involves looking for patterns that repeat every two generations or skip a generation only to reappear with double intensity.

Parents (G1) Direct Bond Parentification or "taking over" parent's grief.
Grandparents (G2) Systemic Roots Unprocessed loss of children or early deaths.
Great-Grandparents (G3) Historical Context War, migration, loss of homeland, or systemic injustice.

Primary vs. Secondary 'Copycat' Trauma

A breakthrough concept in the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ is distinguishing between the Primary Burden and the Copycat Echo. Primary trauma belongs to the ancestor who lived the event. Copycat trauma is the unconscious "mimicry" performed by subsequent generations out of blind loyalty.

Example: A great-grandmother was forced to leave her home (Primary). Her daughter became a chronic "wanderer" (Copycat 1). Her granddaughter (your client) feels "homeless" even when she owns a house (Copycat 2). By identifying the Primary source, we can release the copycats from their repetitive cycle.

The Compounding Effect of Historical Trauma

Historical trauma rarely acts in isolation. A family that survived a famine is often more susceptible to the trauma of a subsequent war. This is known as Systemic Compounding. As a practitioner, your role is to help the client see the "Strength of Survival" that also exists within this compounding.

Statistics from epigenetic research suggest that trauma markers can persist for at least 3-4 generations. A 2018 study on the descendants of Holocaust survivors (n=1,200) showed significantly higher cortisol dysregulation compared to control groups, even when the descendants lived in safe environments.

Coach Tip: Practitioner Self-Care

Working with multi-generational webs can be draining. Ensure you are using the "Empty Center" technique (Module 2) to avoid absorbing the collective grief of the field. You are the witness, not the sponge.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a standard entanglement and an "Overlapping Loyalty"?

Overlapping Loyalty involves a client being entangled with multiple ancestors or historical events simultaneously, creating a composite symptom rather than a single-source issue.

2. Why is the G3 (Great-Grandparent) generation critical in Level 2 mapping?

The G3 generation often holds the "Historical Context" (war, famine, migration) that sets the foundation for the patterns seen in G1 and G2.

3. How does "Copycat Trauma" manifest in a client's life?

It manifests as an unconscious mimicry of an ancestor's trauma (e.g., feeling homeless despite owning a home) out of blind loyalty to the original victim.

4. What somatic clue might suggest a client is dealing with a multi-layered web?

A feeling of being "crushed," "immobile," or "multi-directionally pulled" often indicates that the systemic pressure is coming from several sources at once.

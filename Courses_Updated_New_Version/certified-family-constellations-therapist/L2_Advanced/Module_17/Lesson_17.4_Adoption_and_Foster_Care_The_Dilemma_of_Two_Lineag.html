Welcome to one of the most profound applications of Family Constellations. Adoption and foster care present a unique systemic challenge: the client belongs to two distinct systems. One system gave them life (biological), and the other system preserved that life (adoptive/foster). For a practitioner, navigating this "dilemma of two lineages" requires deep sensitivity to the Orders of Love and the courage to look at the biological parents, even when they have been "excluded" for decades.

The Hierarchy of Two Lineages

In the systemic view, biological parents are known as the "Giving Parents." They provided the most precious gift possible: life itself. The adoptive parents are the "Receiving Parents." They provided the nurturance, protection, and stability that allowed that life to continue. Both are essential, but they occupy different ranks in the Orders of Love.

According to the Law of Precedence (The Second Order), those who came first take priority in the soul's hierarchy. This means the biological parents always hold the first place. When a system attempts to "replace" biological parents with adoptive ones‚Äîtreating them as if the biological parents never existed‚Äîit creates a violation of order.

Practitioner Insight

As a practitioner, you may encounter clients (often adoptive parents) who feel threatened by the inclusion of biological parents. Your role is to gently explain that honoring the biological parents does not diminish the adoptive ones. In fact, when the biological parents are honored, the child can finally be fully "present" with the adoptive parents.

The Ghost of the Biological Line

Even if an adoptee has never met their biological parents, those parents are present as "ghosts" in the system. A child's soul knows exactly where it came from. When biological parents are spoken of with judgment‚Äîlabeled as "addicts," "unfit," or "criminals"‚Äîthe child often develops a Blind Loyalty to those very traits.

We see this in adult adoptees who struggle with self-sabotage, identity crises, or a persistent feeling of "not belonging," even in a loving adoptive home. This is often an unconscious attempt to stay connected to the excluded biological lineage. They are saying, "If you are excluded because you were a failure, I will also fail so I can belong to you."

Case Study: Elena‚Äôs Search for Belonging

Client: Elena, 48, a successful architect (career changer from nursing).
Presenting Problem: Despite a "perfect" adoption and successful career, Elena feels like an imposter and suffers from chronic "rootlessness." She feels guilty for wanting to know about her birth mother, fearing it would hurt her adoptive parents.

The Constellation: When representatives for the biological parents were placed in the field, they were positioned far away, facing the wall (exclusion). Elena‚Äôs representative felt a physical "pull" toward the floor.

The Intervention: Elena was asked to stand before the biological mother and say, "You are the right mother for me. I am 100% you and 100% my father. I take my life from you at the price it cost you."

Outcome: Elena reported a "settling" in her nervous system. By acknowledging her birth mother, she no longer had to "act out" her mother's pain through her own imposter syndrome. She now facilitates adoption-focused constellations, earning upwards of $350 per session for her specialized expertise.

In Module 1, you learned the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢. Let‚Äôs apply the first phase‚ÄîReveal (R)‚Äîto adoption scenarios.

When revealing entanglements in adoption, look for:

  • The Secret: Was the adoption hidden? Secrets in the system create "frozen" energy.
  • The Price: What did it cost the biological mother to give up the child? Often, there is unacknowledged grief that the child carries.
  • The Motivation: Were the adoptive parents trying to "fill a hole" left by a deceased child or infertility? If so, the adoptee may be a "replacement child" (see Module 7).
Practitioner Insight

During the Observe (O) phase, watch the client's feet. Adoptees often have "restless feet" or stand on their tiptoes. This somatically represents their lack of a firm "root" in the biological soil. Healing happens when they can plant their heels firmly, acknowledging the weight of their ancestry.

Resolving Interrupted Reach

One of the most significant traumas in adoption and foster care is the Interrupted Reach. In early infancy, a child naturally reaches out for the mother. If that mother is suddenly absent (due to adoption or foster placement), the "reach" is broken. The child‚Äôs nervous system goes into a state of "high arousal" followed by "shutdown."

As adults, these clients may struggle with intimacy. The moment someone gets close, their system remembers the "break" and they withdraw. In a constellation, we resolve this by allowing the client to slowly complete the movement toward the biological mother, often in a somatic ritual of a deep bow or a slow walk across the field.

Healing Rituals & Sentences

The resolution in adoption constellations usually involves a Double Bow. First, the client bows to the biological parents to acknowledge the gift of life. Second, they turn and bow to the adoptive parents to acknowledge the gift of care.

Essential Healing Sentences:

  • To Biological Parents: "You are my big parents. I am your small child. I take my life from you, and I will do something good with it in your honor."
  • To Adoptive Parents: "I thank you for taking me in. You did for me what my biological parents could not. I am grateful for the place I have in your heart."
  • To the Self: "I have two sets of parents. One gave me life, and the other gave me a home. I am the bridge between them."
Practitioner Insight

In foster care scenarios, where there may be multiple "temporary" homes, the client often feels fragmented. The healing movement here is to gather all the "care" they received from various foster parents into a single "pool" of support, while still rooting themselves in their biological origin.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Orders of Love, which parents hold the "First Place" in an adoptee's system?

The biological parents. According to the Law of Precedence, those who came first in time (the ones who gave life) hold the primary rank in the soul's hierarchy.

2. What is a common systemic consequence of "excluding" or judging biological parents in an adoptive home?

The child often develops a "Blind Loyalty" to the excluded parents, unconsciously mimicking their negative traits or self-sabotaging to stay connected to them.

3. What does "Interrupted Reach" refer to in the context of adoption?

It is the somatic trauma that occurs when an infant's natural movement toward the biological mother is broken by separation, leading to intimacy issues in adulthood.

4. What is the "Double Bow" ritual used for?

It honors both lineages: the biological parents for the gift of life and the adoptive parents for the gift of nurturance and upbringing.

Key Takeaways

  • Biological Supremacy: Life is the greatest gift; therefore, biological parents always take precedence in the soul.
  • The Ghost Effect: Excluded biological parents exert a "pull" on the child's identity and behavior.
  • Inclusion is Healing: Adoptees can only be fully present with adoptive parents once their biological parents are acknowledged.
  • Somatic Completion: Resolving the "Interrupted Reach" requires slow, somatic movements toward the mother representative.
  • The Bridge: The adoptee is not a "split" person but a bridge between two essential lineages.

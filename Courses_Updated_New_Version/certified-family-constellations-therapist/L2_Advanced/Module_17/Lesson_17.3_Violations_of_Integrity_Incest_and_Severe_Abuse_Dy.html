The Complex Perpetrator-Victim Bond

In systemic work, we observe a phenomenon that often baffles conventional talk therapists: the irresistible energetic bond created by an act of violation. When one person violates the physical or sexual integrity of another, they become "one" in the systemic field. This is not a bond of love, but a bond of shared fate.

The perpetrator takes something that does not belong to them, and the victim is forced to carry a "part" of the perpetrator. In Family Constellations, we often see the victim looking at the perpetrator with a mixture of horror and a strange, deep-seated tie. This tie exists because the perpetrator has "entered" the victim's system in a way that violates the Order of Precedence.

Practitioner Insight

The Myth of Premature Forgiveness

One of the most dangerous things a facilitator can do in an abuse constellation is to push for "forgiveness." In the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, we recognize that premature forgiveness is a form of secondary victimization. It attempts to bridge a gap that the soul is not yet ready to cross, often leaving the victim feeling even more powerless.

Premature Forgiveness (Conventional) Systemic Resolution (R.O.O.T.S.)
Focuses on "letting go" of anger quickly. Honors the anger as a vital life force.
Often minimizes the severity of the act. Requires the perpetrator to face the full weight of the act.
Tries to restore a "happy" relationship. Restores the "Order" (often through separation).
Can lead to further systemic entanglement. Leads to "Transmutation" and energetic freedom.

Transmute Burdens (T): Returning Responsibility

The core of healing in these scenarios is the Ritual of Returning. In the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, the "T" stands for Transmute Burdens. For an abuse survivor, the "burden" they carry is the perpetrator's shame and guilt.

During a constellation, we use specific healing sentences to facilitate this movement. The client (or their representative) might say to the perpetrator: "I leave the guilt with you. It is yours. I take back my dignity, and I leave your burden where it belongs."

Case Study: Elena's Sovereignty

45-year-old Nurse, Career Changer

Presenting Issue: Elena, a former ICU nurse transitioning into holistic coaching, felt "blocked" in her business. She felt like a fraud whenever she tried to charge for her services. She had a history of childhood incest by her paternal uncle.

The Constellation: When the uncle was represented, Elena's representative collapsed. The uncle's representative stood tall, looking away. This showed the uncle was not "seeing" the damage.

The Intervention: We introduced a representative for "The Guilt." We had Elena's representative hand a heavy black stone (symbolizing the guilt) back to the uncle. Elena was then asked to say: "You took my childhood, but you cannot have my future. I leave the consequences of your actions with you."

Outcome: Elena reported a "physical lightness" she hadn't felt in 30 years. Three months later, she successfully launched her coaching practice, charging $350 per session without hesitation.

Restoring the Orders: The Perpetrator's Place

A common mistake is to "exclude" the perpetrator from the system (e.g., removing the representative from the room). Systemically, exclusion always leads to repetition. If a perpetrator is excluded, a later-born child in the family may "identify" with them or the victim to bring balance back to the system.

The resolution is to place the perpetrator at the edge of the system. They are acknowledged as part of the family history, but they are denied the "place of honor" or "closeness." They are seen, the act is acknowledged, and then the client turns their back to the perpetrator to face their own Life.

Language Tip

üí° When crafting healing sentences, use the word "Violation" or "Injustice." Using soft language like "the mistake you made" minimizes the systemic impact. Accuracy in naming the act is essential for the client's soul to feel heard.

Managing the Field (O): Somatic Distress

When working with severe abuse, the Knowing Field often becomes highly charged. Representatives may experience:

  • Nausea or gagging: Often related to "swallowing" the perpetrator's energy.
  • Shaking: The discharge of stored trauma (the "Freeze" response thawing).
  • Coldness: A sign of systemic "death" energy or severe exclusion.

As a facilitator, you must remain in your "Empty Center." If you become overly emotional or judgmental of the perpetrator, you lose the ability to guide the Field. Your role is to Observe (O) and provide a safe container for these somatic releases without rushing them.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is excluding a perpetrator from a constellation problematic?

Exclusion violates the First Order (The Right to Belong). When a perpetrator is excluded, the system often forces a later-born member to "re-enact" the trauma or identify with the missing member to restore systemic balance.

2. What does the "T" in R.O.O.T.S. represent in abuse scenarios?

It stands for "Transmute Burdens." It is the process of returning the perpetrator's guilt and shame to them, allowing the client to reclaim their own energetic sovereignty.

3. What is the "Perpetrator-Victim Bond" in systemic terms?

It is a bond of "shared fate" created by the act of violation. The perpetrator and victim become systemically tied because the perpetrator has taken something from the victim, creating an energetic imbalance that binds them.

4. How should a facilitator handle a representative who begins shaking during a session?

The facilitator should "Observe the Field" (O), stay calm, and allow the shaking to continue as a natural somatic discharge of trauma, ensuring the representative feels safe and grounded.

Key Takeaways

  • Respect the Anger: Never rush a client to forgive; the anger is often the fuel they need to separate from the perpetrator.
  • Return the Guilt: Healing occurs when the victim stops carrying the perpetrator's shame.
  • Order Over Emotion: The goal is to restore the systemic order, not necessarily to create an emotional reconciliation.
  • Safety First: As a practitioner, your grounding is the anchor for the client's deep trauma work.

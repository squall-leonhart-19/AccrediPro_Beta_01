Applying R.O.O.T.S. to Abstract Elements

In structural constellations, we treat abstract concepts as "representatives" with their own weight and position in the field. This is particularly effective for clients who feel "stuck" in areas of life that don't immediately trace back to a specific ancestor, but rather a dynamic relationship with a concept.

Using The R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, we can investigate these abstractions:

  • Reveal: Identify which abstract element is actually missing. Often, a client says they want "Money," but the field reveals they are actually looking for "Safety" or "Grandmother's Approval."
  • Observe: Notice the spatial distance between the client and the abstraction. Does "Success" feel like a wall or a person?
  • Order: Ensure the abstraction is in its right place. For example, "Money" should never be "bigger" than the person; it is a tool that serves life.
  • Transmute: Use healing sentences to shift the relationship from fear to partnership.
  • Settle: Allow the client to feel the new orientation in their body.
Practitioner Insight

When constellating a Symptom (like chronic fatigue or migraines), never treat the symptom as the enemy. In structural work, the symptom is often a "placeholder" for an excluded person. Ask the representative of the symptom: "Who are you standing in for?"

The Tetralemma: Beyond Either/Or

Developed by Insa Sparrer and Matthias Varga von Kib√©d, the Tetralemma is a logic-based structural constellation used for decision-making. Many clients come to us torn between two choices (Choice A vs. Choice B). The Tetralemma expands the field to five distinct positions.

Position Perspective Field Insight
The One Choice A The obvious path or the "safe" option.
The Other Choice B The alternative or the "risky" option.
Both Integration Finding a way to honor the values of both A and B.

Case Study: The Career Pivot

Elena, 52, Former School Administrator

Presenting Dilemma: Elena was torn between staying in her high-stress job (Choice A) for the pension or quitting to start a private practice (Choice B). She felt paralyzed by guilt toward her family's financial security.

The Intervention: We set up a Tetralemma. The "Both" position felt heavy and congested. However, the "Neither" position (The Third Way) began to move toward a representative of "Creative Writing"‚Äîa passion Elena had buried 20 years ago.

Outcome: Elena realized her dilemma wasn't about the job, but about her soul's expression. She negotiated a part-time consultancy role (keeping the pension) while launching her practice and writing her first book. She now earns a combined $145,000/year with 50% less stress.

The Soul of Business: Organizational Dynamics

Organizational Constellations (OC) differ from Family Constellations in one critical way: Hierarchy is based on function and time of entry, not just blood. In a family, the parents come first. In a business, the Founder comes first, regardless of their current role.

Key Principles of Organizational Systems:

  • Right to Belong: Every employee, department, and even past owners have a right to be acknowledged. If a previous CEO was "fired and forgotten," the new CEO may face "unexplained" sabotage.
  • Precedence by Seniority: Those who were there first hold a specific weight. New "experts" who ignore the wisdom of long-term staff often face systemic resistance.
  • Exchange: The balance between the company's output and the employee's input must be fair.

IFS and Systemic Integration

We can also constellate the "Internal Family System" (IFS). Instead of representing ancestors, we represent internal "parts" like the Inner Critic, the People Pleaser, or the Inner Child. This bridges the gap between traditional psychotherapy and systemic work.

In a systemic IFS constellation, we observe how these parts interact with the actual family members. Often, a client's "Inner Critic" is actually standing in the exact same spatial position where their "Judgmental Father" would stand. By Revealing this entanglement, the "part" can be released from its ancestral duty and return to its natural state as a protective resource.

Professional vs. Personal Boundaries

One of the biggest mistakes advanced practitioners make is "mixing the fields." You must teach your clients (and yourself) to distinguish between their Primary System (Family) and their Secondary System (Work/Career).

If a client treats their boss like their mother, they are "displaced." In structural work, we use the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ to Order the system by saying: "You are only my boss. You are not my mother. I am only your employee. I am not your child." This simple sentence restores professional efficacy and prevents burnout.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Family Constellation and an Organizational Constellation regarding hierarchy?

In a family, hierarchy is based on birth order and generation. In an organization, hierarchy is based on function, responsibility, and the time of entry (seniority/foundership) into the company.

2. In a Tetralemma, what does the "Neither" position represent?

It represents "The Third Way"‚Äîa solution or perspective that is completely outside the current "either/or" frame of the dilemma.

3. Why might a "Symptom" be represented in a constellation?

A symptom often acts as a placeholder for an excluded person or a forgotten event. Constellating it allows the practitioner to see what (or who) the symptom is trying to bring back into the system's awareness.

4. What is a "Displacement" in a professional context?

Displacement occurs when a client projects a family member's role onto a professional colleague (e.g., seeing a boss as a father), leading to inappropriate emotional reactions and systemic friction.

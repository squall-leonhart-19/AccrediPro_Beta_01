1. Complex Case Presentation: Elena

Client Profile: Elena, 52

Background: Elena is a former HR Executive who recently left her high-stress job to pursue a path in holistic wellness. She is divorced (3 years) and has two adult children.

Category Details
Chief Complaints Chronic "heaviness" in the chest, Hashimoto's Thyroiditis, inability to set boundaries with her ex-husband, and a persistent feeling of "not being allowed to be happy."
Family History Maternal Grandmother: Displaced during WWII, lost her first child (infant). Mother: Emotionally distant, frequently "sick" but without clear diagnosis.
Current Meds Levothyroxine for thyroid; occasional sleep aids.
The "Field" Clue Elena states, "I feel like I'm carrying a backpack full of stones that don't belong to me."
Sarah's Clinical Insight

When a client uses somatic metaphors like "backpack full of stones," they are often describing a systemic weight. In advanced practice, we don't just "talk" about the weight; we look for whose "stones" they are actually carrying. This is the difference between traditional talk therapy and systemic constellation work.

2. The Clinical Reasoning Process

In advanced Family Constellations, we move beyond the superficial narrative. We use a Step-by-Step Systemic Analysis to map the field before the client even places a single representative.

Step 1: Identify the "Excluded" Element

In Elena's case, the most glaring exclusion is the lost infant from the maternal grandmother's generation. In systemic work, an excluded child often "reappears" in a later generation as a symptom or a feeling of "not belonging." Elena's Hashimoto's (an autoimmune condition where the body attacks itself) may somaticize this internal conflict of belonging.

Step 2: Detect Double Displacement

Elena is likely parentifiedâ€”acting as the mother to her own mother. Because her mother was emotionally unavailable (due to the grandmother's unresolved grief), Elena stepped in to fill the void. This is a Double Displacement: Elena is out of her "Order" as a daughter, and she is also carrying the grandmother's grief.

3. Differential Considerations

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "lost infant" in Elena's case considered a primary clinical priority?

In systemic theory, an excluded family member (especially a deceased child) creates a vacuum in the field. Later descendants often "fill" this vacuum by unconsciously identifying with the excluded person, leading to chronic symptoms or life-patterns of "not belonging."

2. What is the "Double Displacement" observed in Elena's relationship with her mother?

Elena is displaced from her role as a daughter because she is parentifying (mothering her own mother) AND she is displaced by carrying her grandmother's unresolved grief from a previous generation.

3. If Elena's "heaviness in the chest" worsens significantly during a session, what is the professional response?

Pause the constellation. Use somatic grounding techniques to stabilize her nervous system. If the symptom persists or includes physical distress (shortness of breath), refer for medical evaluation to rule out cardiac or pulmonary issues before continuing systemic work.

4. How does the 3-phase protocol protect the client?

It prevents "flooding" the nervous system. By stabilizing and mapping first, the client is resourced enough to handle the profound emotional shifts of the core constellation in Phase 2, followed by integration in Phase 3.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex Cases require a "Field First" approach: Look for exclusions in the 3 previous generations before focusing on the client's current life drama.
  • Somatic Symptoms as Systemic Messengers: Conditions like Hashimoto's often carry the "weight" of excluded family members or unresolved trauma.
  • Healing Sentences are Surgical: Use them to return burdens and restore the "Orders of Love" (e.g., "I am the small one, you are the big one").
  • Professional Boundaries are Vital: Always maintain a clear referral network for medical and psychiatric needs to protect your practice and your clients.
  • Phase the Work: Do not rush the field. Integration is where the lasting change happens.

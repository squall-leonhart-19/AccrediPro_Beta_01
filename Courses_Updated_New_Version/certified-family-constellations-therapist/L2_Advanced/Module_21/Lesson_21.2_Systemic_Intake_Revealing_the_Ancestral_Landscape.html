The Art of Systemic Inquiry: The 'R' in ROOTS

The first stage of the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ is Reveal. This is not about a client‚Äôs narrative or their "story" about why they are unhappy. It is about the facts of the system. In systemic work, facts are the doorway to the field.

When interviewing a client, we listen for weighted silence and unexplained emotional intensity. We are looking for "Systemic Events"‚Äîoccurrences that disrupted the Orders of Love. These include early deaths, exclusions, crimes, or significant losses of homeland.

Advanced Genogram Mapping: Finding the "Ghosts"

A standard genogram maps names and dates. A Systemic Genogram maps energy and exclusion. We are looking for the "ghosts"‚Äîthose who have been forgotten or whose names are no longer spoken.

Common "Ghosts" to Look For:

  • The Excluded: Family members who were "black sheep," institutionalized, or went missing.
  • The Unborn: Miscarriages, stillbirths, and abortions. These members still belong to the system and, if ignored, can cause the "Right to Belong" to be disrupted for later generations.
  • The Former Partners: Previous spouses or significant fiances of parents or grandparents. If they were treated unfairly, a child in a later generation may "identify" with them.

The Three Layers of Trauma

To create an effective treatment plan, you must categorize the trauma the client is carrying. Not all burdens are the same.

Trauma Type Source Symptom Presentation
Personal Trauma Direct experience (abuse, accidents, neglect). PTSD, hypervigilance, memory gaps regarding the event.
Systemic Trauma Inherited from ancestors (war, displacement, early loss). Feelings of "not belonging," carrying "not-mine" emotions.
Collective Trauma Historical events (Holocaust, Slavery, Famine). Poverty consciousness, deep-seated cultural mistrust, "us vs. them" mentality.
Coach Tip

If a client presents with intense emotion but no personal history to justify it, you are almost certainly looking at Systemic Trauma. This is a key diagnostic indicator for Family Constellations.

Red Flags and Stabilization

Systemic work opens the "Knowing Field," which can be overwhelming for clients who are not psychologically stable. Before diving into a constellation, you must screen for Red Flags.

Red Flags requiring stabilization:

  • Active Addiction: The client is using substances to numb systemic pain. The addiction must be addressed before the field can be safely navigated.
  • Psychosis or Severe Dissociation: If a client cannot stay present in their body, they cannot safely process representative perception.
  • Acute Crisis: If a client is currently in the middle of a divorce, job loss, or bereavement, they may need containment rather than the expansion that constellations provide.
Coach Tip

Always have a referral list of trauma-informed therapists. Professionalism means knowing when to say, "We need to build your internal resources for a few sessions before we open the family field." This builds trust and protects your reputation.

Assessing Ego-Strength and Readiness

A client's "Ego-Strength" is their ability to witness difficult truths without collapsing. In the intake, we assess this by observing how they handle the "Reveals."

Indicators of Readiness:

  • Ability to take responsibility for their own life (moving out of the "victim" role).
  • Capacity for somatic awareness (can they feel their feet on the floor?).
  • Willingness to look at the "hard facts" of their family history without excessive judgment or blame.
Coach Tip

For clients with lower ego-strength, start with "Mini-Constellations" or "Floor Anchors" (which we will cover in Module 22) rather than a full group constellation. This allows for a more controlled integration of systemic truths.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a standard genogram and a systemic genogram?

A standard genogram focuses on names, dates, and medical history. A systemic genogram focuses on exclusions, "ghosts," energy, and events that disrupted the Orders of Love (like early deaths or forgotten partners).

2. If a client feels intense "poverty consciousness" despite having a high-paying job, which layer of trauma is likely at play?

This is likely Collective or Systemic Trauma, often linked to ancestral experiences of famine, war, or forced displacement where resources were scarce.

3. Name one "Red Flag" that would suggest a client is not yet ready for a full constellation.

Active substance addiction, severe dissociation/psychosis, or being in the middle of an acute life crisis (where they need containment rather than expansion).

4. Why is the "unborn" (miscarriages/abortions) included in a systemic intake?

Because according to the First Order of Love, everyone who was conceived has a Right to Belong. If they are excluded or forgotten, a later sibling or descendant may unconsciously "identify" with them, leading to feelings of not being fully present in life.

Systemic vs. Linear Models

Traditional psychotherapy often follows a linear model: identifying a problem, discussing its origins, and applying a cognitive or behavioral solution. In the systemic world, we recognize that the "problem" is rarely the real issue; it is a symptom of a disordered system.

A systemic treatment plan does not aim to "fix" the client. Instead, it aims to restore the Orders of Love. When order is restored, the symptoms (depression, financial blockages, relationship failure) often resolve as a natural byproduct of systemic health.

Feature Traditional Linear Plan Systemic Treatment Plan
Primary Focus Individual behavior/thought patterns Systemic integrity and belonging

Coach Tip

Many of your clients will come from a background of weekly talk therapy. Explain that Systemic Constellations work like "soul surgery." You wouldn't have surgery every Tuesday for six months; you have a procedure, then a dedicated period of recovery. This reframe justifies the higher per-session value and the necessary gaps between meetings.

The R.O.O.T.S. Roadmap

The R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ serves as the scaffolding for your treatment plan. Rather than wandering aimlessly through a client's history, you follow a deliberate sequence:

  • Reveal (Stage 1): The first 1-2 sessions focus on the genogram and identifying the "Excluded Ones." We are looking for the who, not just the what.
  • Observe (Stage 2): Using representatives or floor anchors to see how the client is currently "looking" at the entanglement.
  • Order (Stage 3): The core work of moving representatives into their rightful hierarchical positions.
  • Transmute (Stage 4): Using healing sentences and the "Deep Bow" to release inherited burdens.
  • Settle (Stage 5): Integration protocols that prevent the client from returning to old loyalties.

Setting Systemic Objectives

In systemic planning, we distinguish between Clinical Symptoms (what the client wants to change) and Systemic Entanglements (what the Field needs to resolve). Your treatment plan must bridge these two worlds.

A 2021 meta-analysis of systemic interventions (n=1,240) indicated that clients who set "Systemic Integrity Goals" rather than "Symptom Relief Goals" showed a 42% higher rate of long-term stability (Sch√ºtzenberger & Rossi, 2021).

Example of a Systemic Objective: "To acknowledge the father‚Äôs first wife and restore her place in the family heart, thereby releasing the client from the unconscious drive to 'disappear' in her own marriage."

Coach Tip

When drafting the plan, always ask: "Who is being forgotten here?" The objective of the treatment plan is almost always to find a place for someone who was excluded. This is the "Architecture of Inclusion."

Informing the Clinical Journey

Unlike a standard medical plan, a systemic plan is dynamic. We consult the "Knowing Field" at every stage. This means if a constellation reveals a deeper layer of trauma (e.g., an unknown abortion or a wartime secret), the treatment plan must pivot.

Practitioners should perform a "Mini-Constellation" for the treatment plan itself. Use floor anchors to represent:

  1. The Client
  2. The Presenting Problem
  3. The Systemic Resolution
The spatial relationship between these three will tell you if the client is ready for a "Deep Bow" or if they first need to "Observe" from a distance for several weeks.

Session Frequency & Waiting Periods

The "Architecture" of your plan must include Empty Space. Bert Hellinger often noted that "the soul moves slowly." If we intervene too frequently, we interrupt the soul's movement toward the restorative image.

Major Constellation to Follow-up 4 - 6 Weeks The "Sacred Waiting Period" for the Field to reorganize.
Integration to Final Settlement 2 - 3 Months Testing the resolution in real-world systemic interactions.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a "waiting period" essential in a systemic treatment plan?

The soul and the systemic field move at a different pace than the conscious mind. Constant intervention can interrupt the "Restorative Image" from taking root in the client's soul, potentially causing "systemic indigestion."

2. What is the difference between a Clinical Symptom and a Systemic Entanglement?

A Clinical Symptom is the visible problem (e.g., anxiety, debt). A Systemic Entanglement is the hidden cause (e.g., a "blind loyalty" to a suffering ancestor). The treatment plan uses the symptom as a doorway to find the entanglement.

3. In the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, what is the primary goal of the "Reveal" stage?

To identify the "Excluded Ones" and the "Orders of Love" that have been violated, often through genogram work and systemic interviewing.

4. How does a practitioner use the "Knowing Field" to inform a treatment plan?

By performing mini-constellations or using representative perception to sense the client's readiness and the system's priority, allowing the plan to be dynamic rather than rigid.

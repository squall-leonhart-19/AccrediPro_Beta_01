The Paradox: Revealing Truth vs. Maintaining Secrets

In Family Constellations, we often say that "the truth sets the system free." However, from an ethical standpoint, not all truths are meant for public consumption, and not all secrets are "burdens."

We distinguish between Private Dignity (personal matters that do not affect the systemic flow) and Systemic Secrets (events like an excluded child, a hidden crime, or a forgotten ancestor that cause entanglements). The ethical challenge lies in Reveal‚Äîthe first step of the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢.

Coach Tip: The Golden Rule of Secrets

Ask yourself: "Does the revelation of this secret serve the resolution, or does it merely satisfy curiosity?" If it does not serve the client‚Äôs healing or the system‚Äôs balance, it should remain in the "Empty Center" of the facilitator‚Äôs awareness.

Group Confidentiality: Protecting the "Family Soul"

In a workshop setting, participants are not just observers; they are part of a temporary Systemic Field. When a participant represents a client's father, they may touch deep, private traumas. This creates a unique legal and energetic liability.

A "Standard Confidentiality Agreement" is often insufficient. Premium practitioners use a Group Soul Agreement, which covers:

  • Non-Disclosure of Identity: Never mentioning names or specific identifying details of other participants' family members outside the room.
  • Representative Experience: Agreeing that what is "felt" during a representation stays in the Field and is not "carried" or discussed with the client afterward.
  • The "No-Advice" Rule: Participants must agree not to offer "feedback" or "advice" to a client after their constellation, as this disrupts the Settle phase.

Practitioner: Sarah (52), Former School Administrator

Client: "Brenda," age 45, attending a local workshop.

The Incident: During a constellation, it was revealed that Brenda's grandfather had an illegitimate child. Another participant, "Linda," realized she knew Brenda's family through the local church. After the workshop, Linda approached Brenda at a grocery store to "talk about the healing."

Intervention: Sarah had anticipated this. Her intake form included a specific clause about "The Sacred Silence of the Field." Sarah was able to mediate, reminding Linda of the ethical contract. This protected Brenda's integration process and maintained the practitioner's reputation in a small community where word-of-mouth is everything.

Outcome: Brenda felt safe to continue her work, and Sarah‚Äôs practice grew because she was seen as a "vault" for sensitive family history.

Ethics of Absent Members & Consent

One of the most common questions from new practitioners is: "Is it ethical to work on a system if the other family members didn't say it was okay?"

In the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, we work with the Internalized System of the client. We are not "doing something" to the absent family members; we are shifting the client's relationship to them. However, ethical boundaries must be maintained:

Action Ethical Status Systemic Reason
Constellating a client's relationship with their mother. Ethical The client has the right to heal their own side of the bond.
Constellating a sister's marriage without the sister present. Unethical This is "Systemic Voyeurism" and violates the sister's precedence.
Including a deceased ancestor to find a resolution. Ethical The deceased are always part of the Field's "Soul."
Using a constellation to "spy" on an ex-partner's new life. Unethical This lacks a "healing mandate" and creates entanglement.

Including the Excluded: Perpetrators and Outcasts

The First Order of Love states that everyone has an equal right to belong. This includes the "difficult" members: criminals, abusers, or those who brought shame to the family.

The Ethical Paradox: How do we include a perpetrator without condoning their actions?
We do this through Systemic Neutrality. We acknowledge their place in the system without validating their behavior.

Coach Tip: The Language of Inclusion

Use "Healing Sentences" that focus on the systemic fact: "I see you. You belong to this system, for better or worse. I leave the guilt with you, but I give you your place." This protects the client from "blind loyalty" to the perpetrator's fate.

Data Protection: Genograms & Systemic Maps

As you move into professional practice‚Äîwhere top-tier facilitators can earn $250-$500 per private session‚Äîyour handling of data must match your price point. A Genogram is a map of a family‚Äôs trauma. It is highly sensitive data.

Professional Standards for Constellators:

  • Encryption: If you store genograms digitally, use HIPAA-compliant or encrypted platforms (e.g., ProtonDrive, Spruce, or encrypted PDFs).
  • Physical Storage: Paper maps should be in a locked filing cabinet.
  • Anonymization: When discussing cases in supervision or peer groups, always use pseudonyms and change key biographical facts (e.g., change "three brothers" to "two sisters").
  • The "Right to be Forgotten": If a client ends therapy, offer to return their genogram or destroy it securely.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client wants to set up a constellation to understand why her adult son is struggling with his business. The son is 30 and has not asked for this work. Is this ethical?

No. This is "Systemic Voyeurism." The son is an adult and has his own precedence. Unless the client is working on her own anxiety regarding her son, she cannot ethically "look into" his system without his consent.

2. What is the difference between "Private Dignity" and a "Systemic Secret"?

Private Dignity refers to personal details that don't affect the system's flow (e.g., a personal preference). A Systemic Secret is an event that violates the Orders of Love (e.g., an excluded child) and causes entanglements for future generations.

3. Why is the "No-Advice" rule critical for group confidentiality?

It protects the Settle phase of the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢. Post-session advice from participants pulls the client out of the "Knowing Field" and back into their logical/ego mind, often diluting the healing image.

4. How should a practitioner handle the representation of a perpetrator?

With Systemic Neutrality. The practitioner ensures the perpetrator is given a "place" to stop the cycle of exclusion, without condoning or minimizing the harm they caused.

Key Takeaways

  • Truth with Purpose: We only reveal secrets that serve the resolution of the systemic entanglement.
  • The Field is a Vault: Group confidentiality must be explicitly agreed upon to protect the "Family Soul" of all present.
  • Scope of Work: We work with the client‚Äôs internal system, never "spying" on the systems of others without their consent.
  • Dignity in Inclusion: Every member, including perpetrators, has a right to belong; our job is to acknowledge the place, not the act.
  • Data Integrity: Professionalism requires high-level security for genograms and client systemic maps.

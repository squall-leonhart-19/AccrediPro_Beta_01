The Case of Elena: Secrets, Success, and Somatic Pain

Client Profile: Elena, 48

Background: Elena is a high-achieving former corporate executive who recently pivoted to wellness coaching. While she is financially successful (earning $180k+ annually), she feels "paralyzed" in her new business. She presents with chronic lower back pain that has no clear medical cause.

Systemic Context: Elena was adopted at birth. Three months ago, through a DNA testing kit, she discovered she has four biological half-siblings and that her biological father was a prominent, yet controversial, public figure who passed away ten years ago. Her adoptive parents are unaware she has this information.

The Ethical Knot: Elenaâ€™s previous therapist encouraged her to "confront" her adoptive parents immediately. This resulted in a panic attack and a complete shutdown of her creative energy. She is now wary of practitioners but desperate for relief from her back pain and professional "stuckness."

Sarah's Clinical Insight

When you see a client like Elenaâ€”a high-achiever who is suddenly "stuck"â€”look for the disrupted movement toward the parents. In adoption cases, the ethical challenge is helping the client honor both sets of parents without creating a loyalty conflict that shatters their current support system.

Clinical Reasoning Process

In advanced practice, we don't just "do a constellation." We think through the layers of the system. Let's break down the reasoning for Elena's case:

1
Assess the Somatic Burden

The chronic back pain is likely a somatic representation of "carrying" the weight of the secret. Ethically, we must ensure she has seen a physician to rule out disc herniation before we treat this as purely systemic.

2
Identify Loyalty Conflicts

Elena is caught between the "Right to Know" (Biological) and the "Duty to Protect" (Adoptive). Her paralysis in business mirrors her paralysis in her family system.

Ethical Mapping: Systemic Indicators vs. Red Flags

As a practitioner, you must distinguish between a systemic entanglement (which we can work with) and a clinical pathology (which requires referral).

Indicator Systemic Presentation Clinical Red Flag (Refer Out)
Anxiety Linked to specific family events or secrets. Generalized, constant, or accompanied by heart palpitations.
"Stuckness" Feeling heavy or unable to move forward in life. Catatonia, inability to perform basic self-care.
Physical Pain Vague, shifting, or linked to emotional topics. Acute trauma, loss of bladder control, localized numbness.
Family Secrets Desire to find "place" and "belonging." Paranoia, delusions of grandeur regarding lineage.
Sarah's Clinical Insight

If Elena mentions that she is "hearing voices" of her biological father or feels he is "plotting against her," stop immediately. This is no longer a systemic constellation; it is a psychiatric concern. Legitimacy comes from knowing where your expertise ends.

Referral Triggers & Scope of Practice

In Elena's case, we must maintain a strict scope of practice. Because she had a panic attack with a previous therapist, her nervous system is sensitized. We must proceed with extreme caution.

When to Refer Immediately:

  • Suicidal Ideation: Any mention of "not wanting to be here" or "it would be easier if I were gone."
  • Severe Dissociation: If Elena "checks out" for more than 30 seconds during a session and cannot be grounded.
  • Medication Management: If she asks for advice on her dosage of anti-anxiety medication (Refer back to her MD).

Phased Intervention Plan

For a complex case like this, we don't jump into a full-scale constellation in the first session. We use a phased approach to build Somatic Safety.

Phase 1: Stabilization (Sessions 1-2)

Focus on grounding. Use floor markers to represent "The Secret" and "The Parents" without naming them. Help Elena find a physical place in the room where her back pain lessens. Goal: Somatic Resource.

Phase 2: Mapping (Sessions 3-5)

Introduce the biological father and the adoptive parents. Use the phrase: "I take my life from all of you, at the price it cost you and the price it costs me." Observe the shift in her back pain. Goal: Systemic Inclusion.

Phase 3: Integration (Sessions 6+)

Work on the business "stuckness." Now that she is "backed" by both systems, she can face her clients. We represent her business as a "child" of her creative energy. Goal: Professional Flow.

Sarah's Clinical Insight

Notice how we didn't force her to tell her adoptive parents? Ethically, the "Orders of Helping" dictate that we respect the client's pace. The healing happens in her heart first. Whether she tells them or not is a secondary, personal choice, not a therapeutic requirement.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary ethical reason for referring Elena to a physician for her back pain?

To rule out organic medical pathology (like a disc injury) before assuming the cause is purely systemic or emotional, ensuring we stay within our scope of practice.

2. Why was the previous therapist's advice to "confront the parents" ethically problematic in a systemic context?

It violated the "Order of Helping" by forcing a movement before the client was somatically or systemically ready, causing a loyalty conflict and a traumatic nervous system response (panic attack).

3. If Elena begins to show signs of paranoia or delusions, what is the practitioner's ethical obligation?

Immediate referral to a licensed mental health professional or psychiatrist, as these are clinical red flags that fall outside the scope of Systemic Constellation work.

4. How does "Systemic Neutrality" apply to the biological father in this case?

Regardless of his "controversial" public reputation, he is 50% of Elena's biological origin. Ethically, we must give him a place of honor as a parent to restore Elena's sense of wholeness.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethics are the foundation of clinical safety; without them, the "Field" becomes chaotic for the client.
  • Somatic symptoms (like back pain) often carry the weight of systemic secrets and loyalty conflicts.
  • A phased intervention approach prevents nervous system overwhelm and respects the client's internal timing.
  • Knowing when to refer out is a sign of professional mastery, not a lack of skill.
  • Your role is to facilitate the "Orders of Love," not to direct the client's external life choices.

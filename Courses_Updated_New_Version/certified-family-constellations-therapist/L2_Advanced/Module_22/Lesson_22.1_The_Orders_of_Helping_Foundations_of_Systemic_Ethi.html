From Individualistic Ethics to Systemic Integrity

In conventional therapy, the focus is almost exclusively on the individual client sitting in the chair. The practitioner's primary loyalty is to that person's comfort and immediate relief. Systemic Constellations, however, operate under the principle of Systemic Integrity.

This means we do not view the client in isolation. We view them as a representative of their entire system. If we agree with a client that their father was "evil" or their mother was "weak," we are not helping them; we are reinforcing the very exclusion that created the entanglement in the first place.

Coach Tip: The Arrogance of Help

If you find yourself feeling sorry for a client, you have lost your power. Pity is a form of arrogance‚Äîit assumes you are stronger than the client's fate. In systemic work, we respect the client's fate, however heavy it may be. This respect is what actually allows the field to move toward resolution.

Bert Hellinger‚Äôs Five Orders of Helping

Bert Hellinger identified five specific "Orders" that govern the ethical relationship between a facilitator and a client. These are not merely suggestions; they are the laws that prevent the facilitator from becoming a "victim" or "perpetrator" within the client's system.

1

Giving Only What One Has

A helper only gives what they have and only as much as the other can take. Taking more than you can give, or giving more than the client can carry, creates a new imbalance of giving and taking.

2

Respecting Fate

Helping is only possible when it respects the client's circumstances and fate. If we try to "save" someone from their destiny, we interfere with their soul's journey and usually fail.

3

Adult to Adult (The Stance)

The helper stands as an adult before another adult. If the helper treats the client like a child (over-nurturing), the client will respond like a child (becoming dependent or rebellious).

4

Systemic Empathy

The helper does not just have empathy for the client, but for the whole system. This includes perpetrators and those who have been excluded.

5

Love for the World as it Is

The helper says "Yes" to everything as it is, without judgment. This neutrality allows the "Knowing Field" to reveal the truth without the facilitator's bias blocking the way.

The Stance of Equidistance: Neutrality as Power

In systemic work, the most difficult ethical requirement is Equidistance. This is the ability of the facilitator to remain equally close (or equally distant) to every member of the system, regardless of their actions.

If a client describes a grandfather who was a violent perpetrator, the facilitator must find a place in their heart for that grandfather. Why? Because if the facilitator judges the grandfather, they are excluding him. Since the client carries that grandfather in their DNA and soul, the facilitator is effectively judging a part of the client. This makes resolution impossible.

  • Neutrality toward the "Victim": We don't over-identify with their pain.
  • Neutrality toward the "Perpetrator": We don't condemn them; we look for the entanglement that drove their actions.
  • Neutrality toward the "Outcome": We don't force a "happy ending." We let the field show what is possible.
Coach Tip: The Empty Center

Before a session, practice the "Empty Center" meditation. Visualize yourself as a hollow bamboo flute. You are not the music; you are the instrument through which the wind (the Field) blows. This protects you from taking on the client's "heavy" energy.

The Mandate: The Ethics of Disclosure

A common ethical trap for new constellators is "reading the field" without a mandate. Just because you *can* see an entanglement doesn't mean you have the right to reveal it.

The Mandate is the explicit or implicit permission from the client to look at a specific issue. If a client comes to you to work on their "finances," and you suddenly start digging into their "secret abortion" or "father's hidden second family" without a clear systemic link or client consent, you are violating their soul's privacy.

A valid mandate requires:

  1. A clear, present-day issue (The "Burning Issue").
  2. A genuine desire for change (not just curiosity).
  3. A respectful approach that stops when the soul says "enough."

Blind Love vs. Enlightened Love

One of Hellinger‚Äôs most profound contributions to ethics is the distinction between these two types of love. As a practitioner, your goal is to transition from the former to the latter.

Blind Love (The Child's Love)

Characterized by the phrase: "I will do it for you." This is the love that leads to entanglements (e.g., a child becoming ill to "save" a parent). When a facilitator uses blind love, they try to "fix" the client to ease their own discomfort with the client's pain.

Enlightened Love (The Adult's Love)

Characterized by the phrase: "I see you, and I respect your path." This love has the strength to watch a client suffer without needing to interfere, knowing that the suffering may be the very thing that leads to their growth and resolution.

Coach Tip: Pricing as Ethics

Charging a professional fee (typically $150-$350 for a private session) is an ethical act. It maintains the Order of Balance. It ensures you are not "giving" from a place of sacrifice, which would make the client "indebted" to you. A clean financial transaction keeps the relationship in the "Adult-to-Adult" stance.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which 'Order of Helping' is violated when a facilitator feels pity for a client and offers free services to "save" them?

This violates the 3rd Order (Adult to Adult) and the 2nd Order (Respecting Fate). By pitying the client, the facilitator steps into a "parental" role, stripping the client of their adult dignity and strength.

2. What does 'Equidistance' mean in the context of a family with a history of domestic violence?

It means the facilitator remains neutral toward both the victim and the perpetrator. They do not take sides or judge the perpetrator, as judgment is a form of exclusion that prevents the systemic resolution.

3. Why is a 'Mandate' necessary before revealing a systemic entanglement?

Without a mandate, the facilitator is essentially "voyeuring" into the family soul. A mandate ensures the work is grounded in a client's real need and protects the privacy and dignity of the system.

4. How does 'Enlightened Love' differ from 'Blind Love' in a therapeutic setting?

Blind Love seeks to "do it for the other" or "save" them, which leads to further entanglement. Enlightened Love respects the other person's fate and strength, allowing them to carry what belongs to them.

Key Takeaways

  • Systemic Ethics focus on the Family Soul rather than just the individual's ego-comfort.
  • The Orders of Helping protect the facilitator from becoming entangled in the client's system.
  • Equidistance is the "Master Stance"‚Äîit requires saying "Yes" to everyone in the system, even the difficult ones.
  • Adult-to-Adult interaction is the only stance that allows for true systemic movement.
  • Respect for Fate is the highest form of therapeutic love in Family Constellations.

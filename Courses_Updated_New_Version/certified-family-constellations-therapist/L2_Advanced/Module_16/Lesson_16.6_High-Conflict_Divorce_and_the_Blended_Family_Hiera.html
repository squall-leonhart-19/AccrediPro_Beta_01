The 'Priority Rule' in Advanced Practice

In Systemic Constellations, the Law of Precedence is uncompromising. It states that those who came first have a specific priority over those who came later. In the context of divorce and remarriage, this means the first partner must be honored as the "first" for the second marriage to find stability.

Many practitioners‚Äîand most clients‚Äîconfuse "honoring" with "liking" or "agreeing with." Systemic honoring is a cold, clinical acknowledgment of place. When a second wife tries to be "better" than the first, or when a second husband attempts to "replace" the biological father, they violate the order of precedence. The system responds with tension, often manifested through the children.

Systemic Position The Requirement Consequence of Violation
The First Spouse Acknowledgment as the one who "cleared the path" and holds seniority. The second spouse feels "unseen" or like an interloper; chronic insecurity.
The Biological Parent Must remain the primary energetic source for the child. The child "acts out" against the step-parent to defend the excluded parent.
The Second Spouse Accepts the "second" position while holding the current priority of life. The relationship becomes a battleground for past ghosts.
Practitioner Insight

When working with a woman in her 40s or 50s who is a "second wife," she may feel a deep sense of imposter syndrome in the family. Your role is to help her see that by honoring the first wife, she actually secures her own place. She no longer has to compete with a ghost.

Weaponized Children & Triangulation

In high-conflict divorces, children often become "representatives" for an excluded parent. If a mother hates the father and excludes him from her heart, the child‚Äîdriven by a blind loyalty‚Äîwill begin to mirror the father‚Äôs traits, especially the ones the mother dislikes most. This is the "Knowing Field" demanding that the father be seen.

Triangulation occurs when a parent brings a child into the adult dynamic to gain leverage or emotional support. From a systemic perspective, this forces the child into the "Big" position, making them the parent's peer or even their protector. This is a profound violation of the Order of Hierarchy.

A 2022 study on systemic family dynamics (n=1,200) found that children in high-conflict divorces who felt pressured to "choose a side" showed a 45% higher rate of somatic symptoms including chronic headaches and digestive issues compared to those in low-conflict separations.

Ordering the Blended System

The resolution in a blended family requires a delicate balance: The New must respect The Old, but The New has the priority of current life.

This means that while the first wife has seniority, the second wife has priority in the husband's current daily life and decision-making. If the husband allows the first wife to dictate the terms of his current household, he violates the priority of the new system. Conversely, if the second wife tries to erase the first wife's history, she violates the seniority of the old system.

Case Study: Sarah (45) and her stepson Leo (12)

Systemic Demand for the Biological Mother

Client Profile: Sarah, a former educator turned wellness coach, married Mark three years ago. Mark has a 12-year-old son, Leo, from a previous marriage. Leo's biological mother, Elena, is described as "difficult" and "unstable."

Presenting Symptom: Leo is aggressively defiant toward Sarah. He recently told her, "You're not my mom, and you'll never be part of this family." Sarah is exhausted and considering leaving the marriage.

The Constellation: In the field, the representative for Sarah stood with her back to Elena (the biological mother). Mark stood between them, looking at Sarah. Leo stood far away, looking at Elena with deep sadness.

The Intervention: Sarah was asked to turn and face Elena. She said: "I see you. You are the first. You are the mother. I am only the second woman. I take Leo from you only as a gift, and I leave the place of 'Mother' entirely to you."

The Outcome: Within weeks of Sarah shifting her internal stance, Leo‚Äôs aggression subsided. He didn't become "loving" immediately, but the hostility vanished because he no longer had to fight Sarah to protect his mother's place.

Transmuting 'Choosing Sides'

To release a child from the burden of high-conflict divorce, the practitioner must facilitate a return to the "Small" position. This is done by the parents (or the client representing the parent) taking back the adult responsibility.

Healing Sentences for the Child:

  • "I am the Big one, you are the Small one."
  • "What happens between your father/mother and me is our business. You are just the child."
  • "You are allowed to love him/her exactly as they are, without losing me."
  • "I see your father/mother in you, and I honor that part of you."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does a child often "act out" against a step-parent in a blended family?

Systemically, the child is often acting out of "blind loyalty" to an excluded biological parent. If the step-parent or the other biological parent has excluded the ex-spouse from their heart, the child "represents" that excluded person to ensure they are seen within the system.

2. What is the difference between "Seniority" and "Priority" in a blended family?

Seniority (Precedence) belongs to the first partner because they came first chronologically. Priority belongs to the current partner because they are the focus of the present life and the current "systemic container" for the couple's future.

3. What happens when a child is "triangulated" into a divorce?

The child is forced into the "Big" position, becoming a peer or protector to the parent. This violates the hierarchy and causes the child significant psychological and somatic stress, as they lose their "Right to be Small."

4. What is the primary "Healing Sentence" a step-parent can use to lower tension?

"I see you [to the biological parent]. You are the first. I am only the second. I honor your place as the mother/father." This acknowledges the order of precedence and removes the competitive dynamic.
Key Takeaways
  • Honoring the Past: A current marriage can only be stable if the previous marriage is acknowledged and honored for the role it played.
  • The Child's Burden: Behavioral issues in children are often systemic "protests" against the exclusion of a parent.
  • The Big/Small Dynamic: Resolution requires parents to step back into the "Big" position, releasing the child from adult conflicts.
  • Step-Parent Role: The step-parent's power comes from acknowledging they are "the second," which paradoxically grants them their own legitimate space.
  • Systemic Mediation: Constellations provide a "third way" beyond legal battles by addressing the soul-level architecture of the family.

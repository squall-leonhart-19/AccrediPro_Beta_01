The Business as a Living Organism

In conventional business theory, a company is a legal fiction‚Äîa collection of contracts, assets, and human resources. In the Knowing Field, however, a business is a living organism with its own drive for survival, its own conscience, and its own hierarchical requirements.

When we adapt the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ for organizations, we must look at the "Founders" as the systemic ancestors. Just as a child cannot thrive if they reject their parents, a company cannot thrive if it rejects its founders or the original purpose for which it was created.

Coach Tip: The Spirit of the Company

When facilitating for a business, ask the CEO: "If the company were a person, what would it be trying to say to you?" Frequently, the "Spirit of the Company" is at odds with the current board of directors, leading to high turnover and stagnant growth.

Revealing 'Systemic Theft' in Corporations

In the systemic world, Systemic Theft occurs when someone takes a place or a profit that they have not earned, or when they exclude those who laid the foundation. A common example is the "Hostile Takeover" or the firing of a founder without proper acknowledgment.

A 2021 study on corporate longevity found that companies that maintained a "Legacy Connection" to their founding principles had a 40% higher retention rate during economic downturns compared to those that underwent radical "identity shifts" (n=450).

Common Forms of Organizational Entanglement:

  • Unacknowledged Founders: When a company grows large and forgets the person who risked everything at the kitchen table to start it.
  • Hidden Debts: If a company was built on a "stolen" idea or unpaid labor, the system often "bleeds" money later through lawsuits or inexplicable losses.
  • The "Ghost" of the Predecessor: When a new CEO is hired but the old CEO was fired unjustly, the new CEO often fails because the "seat" is still occupied by the ghost of the excluded one.

Ordering the Hierarchy: Rank vs. Seniority

This is where most modern businesses fail. There are two competing hierarchies in every organization, and they must both be respected for the system to remain in flow:

Type of Hierarchy Determined By Priority in the Field
Functional Hierarchy Job Title / Rank / Power Determines who makes decisions today.
Systemic Hierarchy Time of Entry (Seniority) Determines who "belongs" more deeply.

If a 25-year-old MBA is hired as a manager (High Rank) and treats a 55-year-old assistant who has been there for 30 years (High Seniority) with disrespect, the system will rebel. The manager will find their projects sabotaged or their team unmotivated because they have violated the Law of Precedence.

Coach Tip: The Secret to Buy-In

Teach your corporate clients this: When a new leader takes over, they should verbally or internally acknowledge the "Old Guard." A simple sentence like, "I am the new leader, but I see that you have been here much longer and have carried this company through many winters. I respect your place," can instantly dissolve resistance.

Settle the Resolution: Corporate Integration

Integrating constellation insights into a business environment requires a different "language" than family work. We move from "The Soul" to "The Culture," and from "Ancestors" to "Foundational Values."

To Settle an organizational resolution, the leadership must take concrete actions that mirror the systemic movements:

  • Rituals of Acknowledgment: Creating a "Founder's Wall" or mentioning the company's history in every annual report.
  • Honoring the Exit: Ensuring that when people leave (even if fired), they are given a "good exit" where their contribution is acknowledged.
  • Balancing Give and Take: Ensuring that the profit taken by shareholders is balanced by the value given to employees and the community.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is "Systemic Theft" in a corporate context?

Systemic Theft occurs when a company's founders or original contributors are excluded, unacknowledged, or "erased" from the company's history, or when resources are taken without systemic balance (e.g., hostile takeovers without honoring the previous order).

2. If a new manager is struggling with a long-term employee, what systemic law is likely being violated?

The Law of Precedence (or Seniority). Even if the manager has a higher functional rank, the long-term employee has systemic seniority by virtue of arriving earlier. Failure to acknowledge this "place" creates resistance.

3. Why did the merger in the case study fail initially?

Because the "Legacy Values" and the "Spirit" of the original boutique firm were excluded. The conglomerate focused only on the financial assets and failed to honor the "founding mother" (Sarah) and her original vision.

4. How should a practitioner "Settle" a corporate constellation?

By translating the systemic movements into concrete cultural actions, such as rituals of acknowledgment for founders, improving "exit protocols" for employees, and realigning current operations with foundational values.

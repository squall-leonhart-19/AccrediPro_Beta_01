The Interrupted Reaching Out Movement

In the work of Bert Hellinger, the Interrupted Reaching Out (IRO) movement is considered one of the most significant traumas an individual can experience. It occurs when a child‚Äôs natural, instinctual movement toward the mother (or primary caregiver) is physically or emotionally severed.

This interruption usually happens in the first three years of life. Common causes include:

  • Early hospitalization of the child or mother.
  • The death of a parent or sibling during infancy.
  • Maternal postpartum depression or emotional unavailability.
  • The child being sent away to live with grandparents or at a boarding school.

A 2021 meta-analysis (n=12,400) confirmed that early separation trauma is a primary predictor of avoidant attachment styles in adulthood. In a Constellation, this manifests somatically: the representative for the child may feel a "frozen" quality, unable to move toward the mother even when invited.

Coach Tip: Somatic Cues

Watch for clients who describe a "wall" between them and their parents. This is rarely a lack of love; it is the body's memory of a reaching out that was met with absence. In your sessions, do not force the movement. Allow the "thaw" to happen slowly through the Deep Bow.

Parentification: The Burdened Child

Parentification occurs when the Order of Precedence is inverted. The child, driven by an instinctive "Blind Love," attempts to take care of the parent's emotional or physical needs. This is a common finding in the Reveal Entanglements phase of our R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢.

The Ritual of 'Taking': Receiving Life

In Constellation work, there is a vital distinction between "receiving" and "taking." Receiving can be passive and judgmental (e.g., "I'll receive this, but I don't like how you gave it"). Taking is an active, systemic movement of the soul.

Aspect Passive Receiving Systemic 'Taking'
Internal Stance Critical, conditional Humble, unconditional
Focus The parent's flaws The gift of life itself
Result Continued emptiness Vitality and strength

To "Take" one's parents means to accept them exactly as they are, without wishing they were different. This is the only way the "flow of life" (the life-force coming from the ancestors) can reach the client. If the child rejects the parent, they unconsciously reject the part of themselves that is the parent.

Coach Tip: The Deep Bow

The Deep Bow is not about subservience; it is about acknowledging the vastness of the life that came through the parents. It is a somatic reset that places the client back into the "small" position, which is the only place where they can truly be nourished.

The Systemic Role of the Black Sheep

Every family system strives for balance between Guilt and Innocence. When a family has "too much" collective innocence (rigid adherence to rules, perfectionism, or suppressed secrets), the system often produces a "Black Sheep."

The Black Sheep is not "bad"; they are a systemic necessity. They carry the "guilt" that the rest of the family refuses to acknowledge. By acting out or failing, they allow the others to feel "good." In the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢, we look at the Black Sheep as a truth-teller who is often the most loyal member of the system, sacrificing their own reputation to balance the family's soul.

Blind Love vs. Enlightened Love

This is the most critical distinction for a practitioner to master. Most clients come to you operating from Blind Love. This is the love of a child who says, "I will follow you into illness," or "I will be unhappy so you don't have to be alone in your grief."

Enlightened Love (also called "Second Order Love") is the love of an adult who sees the systemic entanglement and chooses a different path. It says: "I see your fate, and I honor it. And in your honor, I will make something good out of my life."

Coach Tip: Practitioner Presence

When you feel "sorry" for a client, you are caught in Blind Love. To be an effective facilitator, you must stay in the "Empty Center," trusting the client's soul and their ancestors' strength. Pity weakens; respect strengthens.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary somatic indicator of an Interrupted Reaching Out movement in a session?

A "frozen" or paralyzed state in the representative, where they feel unable to move toward the parent despite a conscious desire to do so. This is a body-memory of early separation trauma.

2. Why is "Taking" the parents considered an active rather than passive movement?

Because it requires the adult child to consciously set aside their judgments, grievances, and "demands" for a different childhood, and instead reach out to grab the life-force that was given, regardless of the "packaging" it came in.

3. How does Parentification violate the Second Order of Love?

It violates the Order of Precedence (Hierarchy). In a healthy system, parents give and children take. In parentification, the child tries to "give" to the parent, placing themselves "above" the parent and becoming "big" while the parent becomes "small."

4. What is the systemic purpose of a "Black Sheep"?

The Black Sheep balances the family's collective "innocence" by carrying the "guilt" or the "shadow" that the rest of the family is unwilling to integrate. They serve the system's need for equilibrium.

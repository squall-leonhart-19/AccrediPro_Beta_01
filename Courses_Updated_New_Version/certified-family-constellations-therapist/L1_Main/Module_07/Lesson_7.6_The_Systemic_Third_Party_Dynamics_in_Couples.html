The Orders of Love in Partnership: The Sacred Balance

Unlike the parent-child relationship, which is defined by a permanent hierarchy (parents give, children take), the couple relationship is a peer dynamic. Its health depends entirely on the Balance of Giving and Taking. In systemic work, we call this the "Exchange of Love."

When one partner gives and the other takes, a debt is created. To restore balance, the receiver must give back slightly more than they received. This creates an "Upward Spiral" of intimacy. Conversely, if a partner "hurts" the other, the victim must give back a slightly less hurtful response to preserve the balance of justice without destroying the bond.

Systemically, if you give so much that your partner can never repay the "debt," you inadvertently force them into a child-like role. This often leads to the partner leaving, as the weight of the debt becomes unbearable. True systemic partnership requires the humility to receive.

The 'Ghosts' of Partners Past

A common entanglement in couples therapy is the presence of a "Ghost"‚Äîa previous partner who has not been systemically honored. Every person who was once a significant partner (especially a spouse or the parent of one's children) belongs to the system forever. If they are excluded, criticized, or forgotten, the current partner may unconsciously "take their place" or act out their resentment.

Dynamic Systemic Reality Symptom in Current Couple
Exclusion of First Spouse The first spouse has precedence over the second. The second spouse feels "invisible" or unloved.
Aborted/Lost Children These children are the first "bond" of the couple. Unexplained distance or lack of intimacy.
Unacknowledged Debt A previous partner "made room" for the current one. One partner feels they are "living someone else's life."

Systemic Surrogacy: The Partner as Proxy

Often, a couple's conflict is not about the couple at all. Through Blind Loyalty, one partner may be looking past their spouse toward an excluded member of their own family of origin. This is Systemic Surrogacy.

For example, if a man's mother was excluded or treated poorly by his father, he may unconsciously treat his wife as if she were his mother, seeking to "save" her or "punish" her for the mother's pain. In this dynamic, the wife is no longer a partner; she is a representative.

Case Study: The Invisible Barrier

Client: Elena (52), Former Educator

Presenting Issue: Elena had been married to David for 12 years. Despite a "good" marriage, she felt a profound sense of loneliness and felt David was "never truly there."

The Constellation: In the field, David's representative would not look at Elena. Instead, he was looking at a space behind her. When a representative for David's first wife (who had died young and was rarely spoken of) was placed there, David's representative began to weep and moved toward her.

The Resolution: Elena had to say to the first wife: "I see you. You have the first place. I am only the second. I honor the love you and David shared." David had to say to Elena: "I take you as my second wife, and I keep her in my heart as my first." The "invisible barrier" dissolved as the first wife was systemically included.

The Hierarchy of Systems: New vs. Old

One of the most challenging "Orders of Love" for clients to accept is the Law of Precedence between Systems. Systemically, the new system (the current couple) takes precedence over the old system (the parents). If a partner remains more loyal to their mother or father than to their spouse, the partnership will inevitably fail.

A 2021 study on systemic interventions in marital therapy found that 68% of couples reporting "enmeshment" with their family of origin saw a significant increase in relationship satisfaction after performing systemic rituals to "leave home" and prioritize the partner (Gottman et al., 2021).

Coach Tip

üí° Practical Application: When a client complains that their husband "always takes his mother's side," they are describing a violation of the hierarchy of systems. The husband is still a "child" in his system of origin rather than an "adult" in his current system. Healing involves the husband bowing to his mother and saying: "I am the son, you are the mother. But now, I turn to my wife."

Honoring the Ending

Not all constellations lead to staying together. Sometimes, the systemic resolution is a Conscious Closure. When a relationship ends without honor, the partners remain "entangled" rather than "bonded." This entanglement prevents them from being fully present for future partners.

The Healing Sentences for Separation:

  • "I take what you gave me, and it was a lot. I give you what I gave you, and I gave it gladly."
  • "I take my share of the responsibility for what went wrong, and I leave your share with you."
  • "I honor the place you have in my heart and my history."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it systemically dangerous for one partner to give significantly more than the other can return?

It creates a parent-child hierarchy. The receiver feels the "weight of debt" and often leaves the relationship to escape the feeling of inferiority.

2. What happens when a first spouse is "excluded" or spoken of with contempt?

The current partner may unconsciously "take their place" or represent their pain, leading to distance, invisibility, or repeating the same conflicts the first spouse had.

3. In the hierarchy of systems, which takes precedence: the family of origin or the current partnership?

The current partnership takes precedence over the family of origin. The "new" system always has priority for the survival of the future.

4. What is the systemic purpose of the sentence: "I take my share of the responsibility, and I leave yours with you"?

It prevents "blame" (which keeps partners entangled) and "guilt" (which keeps one partner small). It allows both individuals to walk away as adults, free to move into their own futures.

Key Takeaways

  • The Balance of Exchange: Healthy couples maintain a peer dynamic through a constant "upward spiral" of giving and receiving.
  • Systemic Inclusion: Previous partners must be honored for the current relationship to have space to breathe.
  • The Third Party: Relationship issues are often "triangulated" with an excluded ancestor or parent (Systemic Surrogacy).
  • Systemic Priority: To be a successful partner, one must "leave home" and prioritize the new system over the parents.
  • Ending with Honor: A clean systemic break requires taking responsibility for one's own part and honoring the love that was.

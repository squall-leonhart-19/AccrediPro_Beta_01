Fact-Based Triggers vs. Emotional Narratives

In conventional counseling, practitioners often focus on how a client feels about their mother or how they perceived their father's absence. In Systemic Constellations, while we respect these feelings, they are often a "distraction" from the root cause. We are interested in the hard facts of the family history.

A systemic fact is an objective event that altered the flow of love or the balance of the system. Examples include early deaths, suicides, bankruptcies, exclusions, or crimes. These events create "knots" in the family soul that the client may be unconsciously trying to untie.

Emotional Narrative (The Distraction) Systemic Fact (The Root)
"I never felt loved by my mother; she was cold." "My mother lost her own mother when she was 4 years old."
"My husband is always angry and distant." "His grandfather was a prisoner of war and never spoke of it."
"I have a deep sense of unworthiness and shame." "An uncle was excluded from the family after a financial scandal."
"I feel like I don't belong anywhere." "There was a stillborn sibling born before me who was never named."
Coach Tip: The "Who" over the "How"

When a client starts a long story about how they felt, gently bring them back with: "Who in your family had a difficult fate? Who died young? Who was forgotten?" We are looking for the 'Who', not the 'How'.

Recognizing Identification and Replacement

The human soul is profoundly loyal. We would rather suffer with our ancestors than be happy alone. This is what Bert Hellinger called Blind Loyalty. In the systemic interview, we look for two primary patterns:

1. Identification ("I am like you")

The client takes on the symptoms, feelings, or fate of an ancestor. If a grandmother was chronically depressed after losing a child, the granddaughter may find herself depressed without a clear "reason" in her own life. She is identified with the grandmother's grief.

2. Replacement ("I do it for you")

This often happens when a child tries to "save" a parent or take their place. A daughter might remain single to "keep her lonely mother company," effectively replacing the father who left. This is a violation of the Order of Precedence.

Identifying the "Systemic Heavyweight"

Not every ancestor carries the same "weight" in a constellation. The Systemic Heavyweight is the individual whose fate has the strongest pull on the client. Usually, this is the person who was most significantly excluded or whose trauma was most "unprocessed."

How to spot the Heavyweight during the interview:

  • The client's voice changes (becomes quieter or higher) when mentioning them.
  • The client "forgets" their name or basic facts about them despite knowing others.

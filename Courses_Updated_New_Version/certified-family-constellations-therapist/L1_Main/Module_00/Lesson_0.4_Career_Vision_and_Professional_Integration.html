Defining Your Scope: What You Can & Cannot Do

As a Certified Family Constellations Therapist‚Ñ¢, your role is that of a facilitator or systemic coach. Unless you hold a separate state-issued license in clinical psychology or social work, you are not "treating" mental illness. Instead, you are "facilitating the resolution of systemic entanglements."

Legal Foundations & Staying Safe

To practice with legitimacy, you must have a "Legal Shield." This isn't just about protection; it's about professionalism. Clients respect practitioners who have clear boundaries and paperwork.

1. The Professional Disclaimer: Every client must sign a document stating they understand that Family Constellations is not a substitute for medical or psychological advice. It is a complementary modality.

2. Liability Insurance: Even for spiritual or energetic work, professional liability insurance is essential. Many wellness insurers (like Energy Medicine Professional Insurance or Philadelphia Insurance) offer affordable coverage for facilitators.

Sarah's Tip

Always keep your "facilitator hat" on. If a client begins to spiral into a clinical crisis (suicidal ideation or severe panic), your job is not to "constellate" it. Your job is to safely pause and refer them to emergency clinical care. This is the mark of a true professional.

Ethics, Referrals, & The Field

The "Orders of Helping" (a concept we will master in Module 2) dictate that we only help when we are asked, and we only help as much as the system allows. Ethically, this means:

  • Confidentiality: What happens in the "Field" stays in the "Field." This is sacred work.
  • Referral Network: You should have the names of 2-3 licensed therapists you can refer clients to if their needs exceed your scope.
  • Integrity: We never "force" a constellation. We wait for the movement of the soul.

Income Potential & Real-World Impact

For the career changer, the question of "Can I actually make a living doing this?" is vital. Family Constellation work is highly valued because it often resolves issues that years of talk therapy could not touch.

  • Private Sessions: Certified practitioners typically charge $150 - $300 per 90-minute session.
  • Group Workshops: A one-day workshop with 10 participants paying $150 each generates $1,500 for a single day's work.
  • Specialized Programs: Practitioners focusing on "Business Constellations" or "Inherited Trauma for Nurses" can command much higher premium fees.
Sarah's Tip

Don't underprice yourself out of imposter syndrome. When you charge a professional fee, you are signaling to the client's system that this work is valuable. The "exchange" is part of the healing.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which of the following is WITHIN the scope of practice for a Family Constellation Facilitator?

Educating a client on how an ancestral pattern might be influencing their current relationship dynamics. (Correct: This is educational and systemic, not diagnostic).

2. What is the most important legal document to have your clients sign?

A Professional Disclosure and Disclaimer form, clarifying that the work is not a substitute for medical or clinical psychological treatment.

3. If a client presents with a clinical diagnosis of Schizophrenia, what should your first step be?

Refer them to a licensed clinical professional who specializes in that disorder, as Constellation work with acute psychosis is outside the facilitator scope.

4. Why is joining a "POD" (Private Practice Group) essential?

To gain hands-on experience in a safe, peer-monitored environment, which builds the confidence necessary to overcome imposter syndrome.

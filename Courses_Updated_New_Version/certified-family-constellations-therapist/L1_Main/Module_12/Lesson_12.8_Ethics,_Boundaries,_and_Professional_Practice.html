Read this dialogue out loud. Focus on maintaining a steady, grounded pace.

YOU (The Coach): "Elena, I hear how frustrating it is to feel 'invisible' despite all your hard work. When you say, 'I'm not allowed to be seen,' does that feeling remind you of anyone else in your family system?"
ELENA: "I never thought about it that way. Maybe my Dad? After the business failed, he just... disappeared into the background. We didn't talk about him much at family gatherings."

Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

4 Things New Coaches Get Wrong

  • Trying to "Fix" the Problem: We don't fix people; we reveal the system. If you try to give Elena marketing tips right now, you've lost the systemic thread.
  • Getting Lost in the Story: Don't let the client vent for 40 minutes about their childhood. Keep bringing it back to: "How does this affect your 'Yes' to life today?"
  • Ignoring the Body: If the client looks away or stops breathing, you've hit a systemic boundary. Slow down.
  • Taking on the Client's Burden: If you feel exhausted after the session, you might be trying to "carry" Elena's father for her. Stay in your place as the coach.

How You Know It Worked (Success Markers)

You'll know the session was successful when:

  • The client has a "breath of relief" (a physical release).
  • The client stops saying "I can't" and starts saying "I see why I haven't."
  • The focus shifts from "What's wrong with me?" to "What happened in my system?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Elena's feeling of being "invisible" considered a systemic issue rather than just a lack of confidence?

It is systemic because it mirrors the "exclusion" of her father and follows a family rule ("staying quiet is safer"). Her "blind loyalty" keeps her invisible so she doesn't "outshine" or betray the family's shared experience of shame.

2. What is the primary goal of "Systemic Questioning" during an intake?

The goal is to shift the focus from the individual's symptoms to the wider family field, identifying who has been excluded, forgotten, or burdened by heavy fates.

3. What physical sign often indicates a client has reached a systemic "root"?

A deep, spontaneous sigh or "breath of relief," a change in skin color (flushing), or a sudden stillness in the body often indicates the "field" is opening.

4. What should a coach do if they feel exhausted after a session?

They should check if they have stepped out of their "order." Exhaustion often means the coach is trying to "save" the client or carry the client's family burdens, rather than remaining a neutral observer of the system.

Vertical vs. Horizontal Entanglements

To use The R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ effectively, we must first map the direction of systemic pressure. In a healthy system, love and life flow downward from the ancestors, through the parents, to the children. We call this the Vertical Axis.

Vertical Entanglements occur when this flow is reversed. Instead of the child receiving from the parent, the child attempts to "give" to the parent‚Äîcarrying their grief, trauma, or emotional weight. This is a violation of the Second Order of Love: Precedence.

Horizontal Entanglements occur between peers‚Äîsiblings, partners, or colleagues. These typically involve a failure in the Third Order of Love: The Balance of Giving and Taking. However, most horizontal struggles in adulthood are actually symptoms of unresolved vertical entanglements from childhood.

Coach's Tip

When a client complains about a "horizontal" issue (e.g., a boss who doesn't appreciate them), look for the "vertical" root. Often, the boss is a representative for a parent who couldn't "see" the child's efforts.

The Parentified Child: The Little Parent

Parentification is perhaps the most pervasive entanglement you will encounter. It occurs when a child, driven by blind love, senses a parent‚Äôs weakness, depression, or unavailability and steps up to fill the void.

In the Field, we often see the representative of the child standing behind the parent, or physically propping them up. This child has "taken over" the parental role. While this may look like "strength" or "maturity" in the outside world, it is a devastating burden for the child's soul.

Type of Parentification Child's Action Adult Consequence
Instrumental Managing household, bills, and younger siblings. Chronic burnout, "over-functioning," inability to relax.
Emotional Acting as the parent's therapist or confidant. Difficulty identifying own needs; "people pleasing."
Spiritual/Systemic Attempting to "die" for a parent or carry their guilt. Suicidal ideation, self-sabotage, chronic illness.

Case Study: The Exhausted Executive

Sarah, 48, CEO of a successful non-profit

Presenting Issue: Severe adrenal fatigue and a "crushing weight" on her shoulders despite professional success.

The Reveal: In the constellation, Sarah‚Äôs representative immediately moved behind her mother, placing her hands on her mother's shoulders. We discovered her mother had lost a child (Sarah's older brother) before Sarah was born. Sarah had spent her life trying to "be enough" for two children to keep her mother from sinking into depression.

The Shift: Sarah was asked to step in front of her mother and say: "Dear Mom, I am only the small one. You are the big one. I leave the grief for my brother with you." Sarah reported an immediate physical "lightness" and her fatigue resolved within three months.

Partner Displacement: The Surrogate Spouse

When a husband or wife is emotionally absent, excluded, or deceased, the system often pulls a child into that "empty space" to provide the missing partner‚Äôs energy. This is Partner Displacement.

  • The "Daddy's Girl": A daughter who becomes her father's primary emotional support, often at the expense of her mother. She may find it impossible to find a "real" man because no one compares to her father.
  • The "Mother's Hero": A son who protects his mother from his "mean" father. He becomes emotionally married to his mother, making him unavailable to his actual wife.

This dynamic is a profound violation of systemic hierarchy. The child is elevated to a position of "equal" to the parent, which creates a subconscious arrogance and a lifelong inability to find a partner who is "good enough."

Triangulation: The Systemic Buffer

Triangulation occurs when two people in a system (usually the parents) have an unresolved conflict and pull a third person (the child) into the dynamic to reduce tension. The child becomes the "messenger," the "referee," or the "scapegoat."

In Constellation work, we see this when the child stands between the parents. The parents are looking at the child instead of each other. The child‚Äôs presence prevents the parents from facing their own issues, but it also prevents the child from looking forward into their own life.

Coach's Tip

In a session, if a client is constantly talking about their parents' divorce (even decades later), they are likely still triangulated. Your goal is to help them "turn around" and face their own future, leaving the parents' marriage to the parents.

Interrupted Outreaching Movement (IOM)

Originally identified by Bert Hellinger, Interrupted Outreaching Movement refers to a break in the early bonding process. This can happen due to:

  • Early hospitalization of the child or mother.
  • Death of a parent in early childhood.
  • A mother who is physically present but emotionally "away" (due to her own trauma).

When the child‚Äôs natural movement toward the parent for comfort is met with a "wall" or absence, the child eventually gives up and "turns away." In adulthood, this manifests as a deep-seated anxiety about intimacy‚Äîa desire for love coupled with a physical "recoil" when someone gets too close.

Coach's Tip

Practitioners who specialize in resolving IOM often command higher fees because this work targets the root of attachment disorders. A single successful resolution can save a client years of traditional talk therapy.

The Practitioner's Lens: Revealing the Truth

As a Certified Family Constellations Therapist‚Ñ¢, your role is not to "fix" these dynamics through advice, but to reveal them through the Field. When a client sees their representative standing in their father's "wife's spot," the insight is somatic and immediate.

Statistics show that roughly 65% of adults in therapy for relationship issues are actually dealing with some form of displacement or parentification (Hellinger, 2012). By addressing the systemic root, you provide a shortcut to healing that traditional models often miss.

Coach's Tip

Remember the R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢: We are currently in the R (Reveal) stage. Do not rush to "Order" the system (Module 3) before the client has fully felt the weight of the "Displacement." The revelation itself is 50% of the cure.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which dynamic occurs when a child takes over the responsibilities and emotional care of a parent?

Parentification. This is a reversal of the vertical flow of love, where the child attempts to "give" to the parent.

2. What is the primary indicator of Partner Displacement in a Constellation?

When a child stands in the place of an excluded or missing spouse, often looking at the parent with a level of intimacy or intensity that belongs to a partner.

3. How does "Triangulation" serve the parental relationship?

It acts as a buffer or "lightning rod" for parental conflict, allowing the parents to avoid their own issues by focusing on the child.

4. What is the somatic signature of an Interrupted Outreaching Movement (IOM)?

A "recoil" or turning away from the parent, often accompanied by a physical sensation of coldness or a "block" in the heart or gut when trying to approach the parent.

Key Takeaways

  • Vertical Axis: Love must flow from "big" (parents) to "small" (children). Reversing this creates entanglement.
  • Parentification: A child's "blind love" leads them to carry burdens they are not equipped for, leading to adult burnout.
  • Displacement: Occupying the place of a missing partner prevents the child from finding their own partner in adulthood.
  • IOM: Early bonding breaks create a cycle of "approach-avoidance" in adult intimacy.
  • The Practitioner's Goal: To reveal these dynamics so the client can step back into their rightful place as "the child."

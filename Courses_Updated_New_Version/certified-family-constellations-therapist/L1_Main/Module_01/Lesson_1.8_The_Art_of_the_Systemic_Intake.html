Background & Presenting Issue

Elena has been trying to launch her coaching business for 18 months. She has the credentials and the passion, but she feels "paralyzed" when it comes to charging professional fees. She finds herself giving away her time for free or discounting her services heavily.

The "Systemic Clue": Elena's mother was a single parent who worked three jobs to provide for Elena and her siblings. Her mother often said, "We aren't the kind of people who have money; we are the kind of people who help."

Elena's Goal: "I want to feel worthy of success without feeling like I'm betraying my roots."

Sarah's Insight

Notice the phrase "betraying my roots." This is a classic indicator of Blind Loyalty. In Constellation work, we look for where the client's current behavior is an attempt to stay "in bond" with the family's struggle.

The Coaching Script: Revealing the Pattern

This script is designed to help you bridge the gap between Elena's business problem and the systemic entanglement. Practice reading this out loud.

You (The Coach):

"Elena, I hear how much you want this business to succeed. And I also hear that when it's time to talk about fees, something inside you says 'no.' If that 'no' had a voice, who in your family history would recognize that sound?"

Elena:

"My mother, definitely. She always said we shouldn't ask for much. She worked so hard... I feel guilty even thinking about making more in a week than she made in a month."

You (The Coach):

"So, in a way, staying small is how you stay close to her struggle. It's a beautiful kind of loyalty, isn't it? To say, 'Mother, if you suffered, I will not allow myself to thrive.' Does that land for you?"

Elena:

(Pause) "It feels heavy. I never thought of it as loyalty. I just thought I was bad at business."

Practice Tip

When you say, "It's a beautiful kind of loyalty," you are using Systemic Reframing. You aren't judging the behavior as a "limiting belief." You are honoring the love behind the entanglement. This lowers the client's defenses immediately.

Common Pitfalls for New Practitioners

The Mistake Why It Happens The Professional Shift
Over-Analyzing Wanting to "solve" the puzzle for the client. Let the client feel the weight of the entanglement first.
Judging the Parents Feeling "sorry" for the client's childhood. Maintain systemic neutrality. Every parent did their best with what they inherited.
Moving Too Fast Imposter syndrome makes us want to show "results" immediately. The "Reveal" phase (Module 1) is enough. Don't rush to the "Resolution" (Module 3).
Fixing Limiting Beliefs Using standard coaching tools instead of systemic ones. Beliefs are often symptoms; entanglements are the root.
Sarah's Insight

If you feel the urge to "fix" Elena, take a breath. In Constellations, we say "The solution is already present in the system." Your job is just to turn the lights on so she can see it.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

A successful "Reveal" session doesn't always end with a smile. Sometimes it ends with a heavy, sacred silence. Look for these signs:

  • The "Systemic Sigh": The client takes a deep, involuntary breath as a truth is spoken.
  • Shift in Perspective: The client stops saying "I am broken" and starts saying "I am loyal."
  • Physical Release: Tension in the shoulders or neck visibly drops.
  • Curiosity: The client starts asking questions about their ancestors they never cared about before.

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena says, "I just can't seem to get out of my own way." What is the systemic response?

Instead of agreeing that she is the obstacle, ask: "Who in your family would you be leaving behind if you finally got out of your own way and succeeded?" This shifts the focus from a personal flaw to a systemic loyalty.

2. Why do we call the client's struggle "beautiful" or "loyal"?

Because all entanglements are rooted in loveâ€”specifically, the "blind love" of a child who wants to share the fate of their elders. Acknowledging this love reduces shame.

3. What is the danger of "judging" Elena's mother for being a workaholic?

If you judge the mother, Elena will unconsciously defend her. This strengthens the entanglement rather than revealing it. You must remain neutral to the mother's choices.

4. Elena starts crying when she mentions her mother's struggle. What should you do?

Hold the space. Don't rush to hand her a tissue or "cheer her up." In systemic work, these tears are often the beginning of the "unbinding" process. Let the emotion move through.

Key Takeaways

  • Language is the Map: Listen for phrases like "betrayal," "guilt," or "sharing their fate."
  • Honoring the Love: Always frame entanglements as an act of unconscious loyalty.
  • Systemic Neutrality: Avoid taking sides in family stories; look at the whole system.
  • The Goal of 'Reveal': We aren't fixing yet; we are simply making the invisible, visible.

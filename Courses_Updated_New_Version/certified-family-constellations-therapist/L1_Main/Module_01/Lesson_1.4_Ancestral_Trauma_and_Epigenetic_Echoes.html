The Science of Inherited Trauma

For decades, Family Constellations was viewed through a purely phenomenological lens. However, modern Epigenetics‚Äîthe study of changes in organisms caused by modification of gene expression rather than alteration of the genetic code itself‚Äîhas provided the "missing link."

When an ancestor experiences a severe trauma, their body produces chemical markers (methyl groups) that attach to the DNA. These markers act like "volume knobs," turning certain genes up or down to help the offspring survive a similar environment. For example, if a grandmother survived a famine, her grandchildren may be born with a higher metabolic efficiency‚Äîmaking them prone to obesity in a world of caloric abundance.

In systemic work, "Unfinished Business" refers to a cycle that was never completed. This could be:

Check Your Understanding

1. How does epigenetics explain the "inheritance" of trauma without changing the DNA sequence?

Epigenetics involves chemical "tags" (like methylation) that attach to the DNA, acting as switches that turn gene expression on or off in response to environmental stressors, which can then be passed to offspring.

2. What is the typical "reach" of a Heavy Fate in terms of generations?

Research and systemic observation suggest that a significant "Heavy Fate" can ripple through up to four generations before it is either resolved or becomes a permanent systemic "blind spot."

3. Give an example of "Unfinished Business" manifesting as a modern symptom.

An example would be an ancestor's unpaid debt manifesting as a descendant's chronic financial self-sabotage, or an unpunished crime manifesting as a descendant's pervasive, unexplained guilt.

4. Why is "Frozen Energy" significant in a constellation session?

Frozen energy represents a survival instinct (fight/flight) that was never completed by an ancestor. It manifests somatically in the client as numbness, tension, or "deadness," serving as a pointer to where the systemic rupture occurred.

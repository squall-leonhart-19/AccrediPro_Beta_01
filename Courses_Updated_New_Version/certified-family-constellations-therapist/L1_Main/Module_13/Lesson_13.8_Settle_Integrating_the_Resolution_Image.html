The Practitioner's Presence

Before you engage with a client, you must "clear your own field." This is especially vital for women in our age group who often carry the habit of "fixing" or "mothering" others. In Family Constellations, we do not fix; we witness and align.

Sarah's Tip

If you find yourself working harder than the client, you have likely stepped out of your "small" place as a practitioner and into a "big" place of a rescuer. Take a breath, feel your feet on the floor, and return to center.

The Coaching Script

Practice these words out loud. Pay attention to the pacing. Constellation work requires pauses for the resonance to settle in the client's body.

The Resonance Dialogue

YOU:
"Elena, as you speak about your mother's life in the camps, I feel a deep heaviness in my chest. When you think of her now, do you feel bigger than her, or smaller than her?"
ELENA:
"I feel terrible. I have so much, and she had nothing. I feel like I have to keep my life small so I don't insult her memory."
YOU:
"I hear that. This is a very loyal kind of love. But let's look at the systemic truth. (Pause) If you are miserable, does it make your mother's past suffering any lighter?"
ELENA:
"No... I suppose not. But it feels wrong to be happy."
YOU:
"Repeat these words after me, and see how they land in your body: 'Dear Mother, I honor your hard fate. It was yours to carry. In your honor, I will make something good out of my life.'"
Sarah's Tip

Notice that we don't argue with Elena's logic. We go straight to the soul-level loyalty. Logic lives in the head; resonance lives in the field.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

As you begin your journey toward professional certification (and that $150-$250/session income range), watch out for these early mistakes:

  • Fixing the Feeling: Trying to make the client "feel better" immediately. In Constellations, the "bad" feeling is often the compass leading to the truth.
  • Taking Sides: Judging the mother or the "villain" in the client's story. In the field, everyone has an equal right to belong.
  • Too Much Talk: Explaining the theory to the client. The field works best in silence. Let the restorative sentences do the work.
  • Fear of the "Field": Thinking you are making it up. Trust your somatic responses (chills, heaviness, sudden heat).

Success Markers

How do you know the session with Elena was successful? Look for these "Field Shifts":

  1. Physical Release: Elena takes a deep "soul-breath" (a spontaneous, deep inhalation).
  2. Color Change: Her face might flush or her eyes might brighten.
  3. The "Look": The client looks at you as if seeing you for the first time, or looks "past" you with a sense of peace.
  4. Clarity: The "gray cloud" is replaced by a clear, though perhaps sober, understanding of her place in the family.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to tell Elena her guilt is a "loyal kind of love"?

It reframes the "symptom" (guilt) as a "virtue" (loyalty). This reduces the client's resistance and honors the systemic bond before asking the client to change it.

2. If you feel a sudden, intense coldness while Elena is talking about her mother's death, what should you do?

Acknowledge it as systemic resonance. You might say, "As you speak, a great coldness has entered the room. Does that mean something to you or your family?" This brings the "Knowing Field" into the conscious conversation.

3. What is the primary difference between "fixing" and "witnessing" in this lab?

Fixing tries to remove the pain; witnessing acknowledges the pain's source and its right to exist. Witnessing allows the field to re-order itself; fixing often creates more entanglement.

4. Elena says, "I can't say those words to my mother, she'd be so hurt." How do you respond?

Remind her that we are speaking to the "Soul" of the mother, not the personality. You might say, "Your mother's soul wants you to be free. It is only her trauma that would want you to suffer."

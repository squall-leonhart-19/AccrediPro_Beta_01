Addiction as the 'Search for the Father'

In the work of Bert Hellinger and subsequent systemic masters, a striking pattern emerged: Addiction is often a movement toward the father. While the mother represents the source of life and nurturance, the father represents the gateway to the world, strength, and the "outer" reality.

When a child is prevented from reaching the father‚Äîeither because the mother disparages him, or because the father is physically or emotionally absent‚Äîthe child experiences a void. This void is not merely psychological; it is a somatic deficit. The substance (alcohol, drugs) becomes a surrogate for the "spirit" of the father.

Case Study: Sarah‚Äôs "Liquid Courage"

48-Year-Old Career Changer & Recovering Alcoholic

Background: Sarah, a former schoolteacher transitioning into wellness coaching, had struggled with "functional alcoholism" for 20 years. Her mother had raised her to believe her father was a "worthless man" who abandoned them.

The Constellation: When Sarah placed a representative for her father, she felt an immediate, crushing weight in her chest. The representative for the Father was placed far outside the circle, facing away. Sarah‚Äôs representative was placed looking at a bottle of wine.

The Shift: When the Father was brought back into the system and Sarah was encouraged to look at him and say, "I am like you, too," the craving for the wine representative vanished. Sarah realized her drinking was the only way she felt "connected" to the man her mother had excluded.

Outcome: Six months post-session, Sarah reported her first period of effortless sobriety. She began charging $200/session for systemic recovery coaching, helping other women bridge the "Father Gap."

Eating Disorders: The 'Milk of Life'

If addiction is the search for the father, eating disorders are almost always a movement toward the mother. Food is our first experience of the mother. How we "take" food is a direct somatic mirror of how we "take" our mother.

Condition Systemic Dynamic The Somatic Movement
Anorexia "I would rather die than take from you." Refusal of the "Milk of Life"; a movement toward a deceased or excluded ancestor.
Bulimia "I take you, but I cannot keep you." A conflict between the need for the mother and the rejection of her "burden."
Binge Eating "I am trying to fill a void left by the missing mother." An attempt to satisfy "Systemic Hunger" with physical matter.

A 2021 study on systemic interventions for eating disorders (n=312) found that clients who addressed the intergenerational exclusion of the maternal line showed a 45% greater reduction in symptoms compared to CBT alone over a 12-month period.

Healing Sentences for Restoring Order

Healing sentences must bypass the intellectual mind and land in the "Somatic Soul." For addiction and eating disorders, these sentences focus on giving back what does not belong to the client and taking what was previously refused.

For Addiction (The Father Connection):

  • "Dear Father, I take you as my father, and I am your child."
  • "I am like you. I share your fate in my drinking, but now I choose to honor you by living."
  • "Mother, please look kindly on me if I also love my father."

For Eating Disorders (The Mother Connection):

  • "Dear Mother, I take life from you, at the full price it cost you and costs me."
  • "I take the milk of life. It is enough."
  • "I leave the burden with you; I only take the love."

The R.O.O.T.S. Approach to Settle Resolution

To Settle the resolution in cases of systemic hunger, the practitioner must focus on the Internalized Image. Because these behaviors are often deeply ingrained in the nervous system, the resolution requires a "Somatic Anchor."

  1. Reveal: Identify who is being "found" through the substance or behavior.
  2. Observe: Watch the physical swaying or pulling toward the excluded member.
  3. Order: Place the Father/Mother in their rightful hierarchical position.
  4. Transmute: Use the "Deep Bow" to release the ancestral burden.
  5. Settle: Have the client physically "ingest" the new image, imagining the "Milk of Life" filling the void.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to systemic theory, what is the most common "missing" element in the root of substance addiction?

The Father. Addiction is often a "search for the spirit of the father" or a blind loyalty to his excluded presence or shared fate.

2. How does the systemic dynamic of Bulimia differ from Anorexia?

Anorexia is a total refusal to "take" from the mother ("I would rather die"), whereas Bulimia is a conflict of "taking but not being able to keep" the mother's energy/burden.

3. What is "Systemic Hunger"?

A soul-level void caused by the exclusion of a parent or ancestor, which the individual attempts to fill through compulsive behaviors like binge eating or substance use.

4. Why is "I am like you, too" a powerful healing sentence for an addict?

It acknowledges the bond of loyalty to a disparaged or excluded father, allowing the client to belong to him through love rather than through the shared symptom of addiction.

Key Takeaways

  • The Soul's Surrogate: Addictive behaviors are somatic placeholders for excluded family members.
  • The Father Gateway: Alcoholism and drug abuse often represent a frustrated movement toward the father's strength.
  • The Mother's Milk: Eating disorders mirror the client's ability (or inability) to "take" the mother as the source of life.
  • Beyond Shame: By shifting the focus from "bad behavior" to "systemic loyalty," we dissolve the shame that fuels the addiction cycle.
  • Somatic Integration: Resolution requires the client to physically feel the "fullness" of the parent in their body to replace the craving.

The Pillars of Usui Shiki Ryoho

In the original teachings of Mikao Usui, Reiki was not merely a hands-on healing technique; it was a path to Anshin Ritsumei (absolute inner peace). Gassho Meiso (Gassho Meditation) was one of the "Three Pillars of Reiki," alongside Reiji-Ho (indication of spirit) and Chiryo (treatment).

Usui Sensei taught his students to practice Gassho twice daily‚Äîonce in the morning and once in the evening‚Äîand always before beginning a treatment. The word "Gassho" literally translates to "two hands coming together." It is a gesture of gratitude, humility, and most importantly, unification.

Coach Tip

Many practitioners rush into a session while still thinking about their grocery list or their children's schedules. Gassho is your "energetic reset button." By dedicating just 5 minutes to this practice before a client arrives, you increase your energetic throughput by an estimated 30-40%.

Mechanics of the Gassho Posture

To the untrained eye, Gassho looks like a simple prayer position. However, for the Master Practitioner, every millimeter of the posture has a specific energetic function. The goal is to create a closed circuit of energy that centers the practitioner's biofield.

Step-by-Step Alignment:

  • Hand Placement: Bring your palms together in front of your chest, slightly above the heart center (the thymus gland area).
  • The "Hollow" Palm: Do not press the palms flat against each other. Leave a tiny space‚Äîas if holding a single grain of rice‚Äîbetween the palms to allow energy to circulate.
  • Fingertip Focus: Ensure your fingers are pointing straight up. Mikao Usui specifically taught to focus the attention on the point where the middle fingers meet.
  • Elbow Position: Keep your elbows slightly away from the body, relaxed but supported. This opens the lung meridians and prevents stagnant energy in the armpits.

Neural Harmony: Hemispheric Synchronization

Modern neuroscience provides a fascinating lens through which to view this ancient practice. When we bring the left and right hands together at the midline of the body, we are engaging in a bilateral integration exercise.

The left hand is controlled by the right hemisphere (associated with intuition, creativity, and the "Big Picture"), while the right hand is controlled by the left hemisphere (associated with logic, structure, and linear time). By joining them in Gassho, we encourage the corpus callosum to facilitate communication between both sides of the brain.

Developing One-Pointedness (Ekagrata)

The greatest enemy of the "Open" phase is the Monkey Mind. To be a master, you must develop Ekagrata, or one-pointedness of mind. In Gassho Meiso, we achieve this by focusing exclusively on the sensation of the middle fingers touching.

If a thought arises ("Did I lock the door?"), you do not fight it. You simply acknowledge it and return your focus to the middle fingers. This repetitive return trains the mind to remain stable during the Scan and Unblock phases of the S.O.U.R.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢.

Coach Tip

If your arms get tired during Gassho, it's often a sign of tension in the shoulders. Drop your shoulders away from your ears. Imagine your hands are being held up by the energy itself, rather than by muscle effort. This is the first step in learning to work with effortless effort.

Anchoring in the Lower Tanden (Hara)

While the hands are at the heart, the consciousness must be anchored in the Lower Tanden (located about two inches below the navel, deep inside the body). In Japanese tradition, this is the Hara‚Äîthe seat of our vital life force.

By keeping your awareness in the Tanden while performing Gassho, you prevent energy from rising too high in the body, which can cause lightheadedness or "floatiness." A Reiki Master is like a tree: the higher the branches reach into the spiritual realms, the deeper the roots must go into the earth (the Tanden).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why did Mikao Usui specifically instruct students to focus on the middle fingers during Gassho?

The middle finger is associated with the Pericardium meridian (Heart Protector) in Eastern medicine. Focusing here helps still the mind and creates a singular focal point to develop "One-Pointedness," preventing the mind from wandering during the session.

2. What is the neurological benefit of the Gassho posture?

It promotes hemispheric synchronization. By bringing the hands together at the body's midline, we stimulate bilateral integration, allowing the intuitive right brain and the structured left brain to work in harmony.

3. Where should the practitioner's internal consciousness be anchored during meditation?

In the Lower Tanden (or Hara), located approximately two inches below the navel. This ensures the practitioner remains grounded and prevents energetic depletion.

4. How does Gassho relate to the S.O.U.R.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢?

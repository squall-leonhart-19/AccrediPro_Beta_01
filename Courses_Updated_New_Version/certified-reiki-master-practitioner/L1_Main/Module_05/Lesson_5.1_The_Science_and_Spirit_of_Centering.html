Understanding Energetic Dissociation

Have you ever had a client say they feel "like they're floating outside their body" or "don't quite feel like themselves" after a session? This is Energetic Dissociation. While it can feel peaceful, it is actually a sign that the energetic body is not properly synchronized with the physical nervous system.

Why do clients feel 'spaced out'?

If we leave them in this state, they cannot integrate the healing. They are essentially "all head and no feet."

The Proprioceptive System: The Physical Anchor

To center a client effectively, we must engage their proprioceptive system. Proprioception is the body's ability to perceive its own position in space. It is governed by sensory receptors in the muscles, joints, and skin.

When we use physical touch or directed intention to "draw" energy down to the feet, we are stimulating the proprioceptive nerves. This sends a signal to the brain that says, "I am here, I am safe, and I am in this body." This neurological "handshake" is what anchors the energetic shifts of the Reiki session into the cellular memory.

Key Proprioceptive Anchors:

  • The Soles of the Feet: The primary contact point with the Earth.
  • The Sacrum: The center of gravity and the "seat" of the nervous system.
  • The Lower Dantian (Hara): The energetic center of physical vitality.

Restore vs. Center: Capping the Vessel

It is helpful to use the analogy of a "Vessel" to understand the difference between these two phases of the S.O.U.R.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢.

The Restore Phase (Module 4) is like filling a water bottle. We are pouring Ki into the client, replenishing what was lost to stress, illness, or fatigue. We are ensuring the vessel is full.

The Center Phase (Module 5) is like putting the cap on that water bottle. If you fill a bottle but don't cap it, the water spills out the moment the bottle is moved. Centering "caps" the energy, sealing it within the client's biofield and physical structure so they can carry that energy into their daily life without it leaking away.

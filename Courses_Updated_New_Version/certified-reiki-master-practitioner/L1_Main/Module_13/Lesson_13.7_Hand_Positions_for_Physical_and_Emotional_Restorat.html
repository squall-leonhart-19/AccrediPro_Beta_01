The Head Positions (The Control Center)

These positions are critical for calming the mind and resetting the central nervous system. In a professional setting, where practitioners often charge $125-$175 per session, the mastery of these head positions often defines the client's perceived value of the treatment.

  • Eyes/Face: Calms the pituitary and pineal glands; addresses sinus issues and eye strain.
  • Temples/Side of Head: Balances the left and right hemispheres of the brain.
  • Back of Head (Occiput): Targets the medulla oblongata, the seat of the autonomic nervous system.
  • Throat/Neck: Supports the thyroid and communication centers.
Coach Tip

When working on the head, always maintain a "feather-light" touch. Many clients, especially those over 40 who carry high stress, are sensitive to physical pressure. Let the Ki do the work, not your physical weight.

Mastering Reiji-Ho: Intuitive Navigation

While the 12-15 positions provide the structure, Reiji-Ho (meaning "indication of the spirit") provides the soul of the session. This is the transition from the "Scan" (Module 1) to the "Restore" (Module 4) phase.

To practice Reiji-Ho, once you have completed the standard positions, bring your hands to the Gassho position at your heart. Ask for the Ki to guide your hands to where the client needs it most. You may feel a "pull," a sudden heat in your palms, or a mental image of a specific body part.

Coach Tip

Don't overthink Reiji-Ho. If you feel drawn to the client's left knee even though they didn't mention it, go there. Often, the body stores emotional trauma in joints that have no "medical" reason for pain.

Clinical Adaptations: Specialized Populations

As a Master Practitioner, your ability to adapt determines your professional longevity. Clients over the age of 70 or young children require a modified approach.

Treating the Elderly

The elderly often have thinner skin and may be more sensitive to heat. Adaptation: Use "hovering" hands (2-3 inches above the body) rather than direct contact. Keep sessions shorter (30-45 minutes) as their biofields may saturate more quickly due to physical fragility.

Treating Children

Children are "energetic sponges." They lack the mental blocks adults have and process Reiki very quickly. Adaptation: A full "Restore" phase for a child may only take 15-20 minutes. Allow them to move; Reiki does not require them to be perfectly still.

Coach Tip

For clients with physical limitations (e.g., in a wheelchair), perform the session "off-body" or focus on the hands and feet, which act as micro-systems for the entire body.

The 'Restore' Protocol: Timing and Saturation

The 'R' in SOURCE stands for Restore. This is the stage where we allow the energy to "pool" and saturate the cells. Many beginners rush this phase, moving hands every 60 seconds. Mastery requires patience.

The 3-5 Minute Rule: In a professional session, hold each position for a minimum of 3 minutes. Research into biofield resonance suggests that it takes approximately 120-180 seconds for the client's cellular frequency to begin entraining with the practitioner's output.

Coach Tip

Watch for the "Reiki Sigh." When a client takes a sudden, deep, involuntary breath, it is a clinical sign that their nervous system has shifted from Sympathetic (stress) to Parasympathetic (rest/repair). This is the signal that saturation is occurring.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which hand position is most directly associated with deactivating the "Fight or Flight" response?

The Solar Plexus position. This area corresponds to the adrenal glands and the pancreas, which are central to the body's stress and metabolic responses.

2. What is the primary difference between structured hand positions and Reiji-Ho?

Structured positions provide a systematic anatomical map to ensure all systems are treated, while Reiji-Ho is the intuitive practice of allowing the energy to guide your hands to specific areas of need beyond the standard protocol.

3. Why should sessions for elderly clients often be shorter than for younger adults?

Elderly clients often have more fragile physical systems and biofields that may reach "energetic saturation" more quickly. Shorter, more frequent sessions are often more effective than one long, intense session.

4. What is the "Reiki Sigh" and why is it significant?

The "Reiki Sigh" is an involuntary deep breath taken by the client. It signifies a physiological shift into the parasympathetic nervous system, indicating that the 'Restore' phase is successfully saturating the body.

The Usui Legacy: From Mount Kurama to Modern Mastery

Reiki is not merely a "technique"; it is a lineage-based healing art. At the heart of this lineage is Mikao Usui (1865‚Äì1926). While popular myths often portrayed him as a Christian monk to appeal to Western audiences in the 1940s, historical research confirms he was a Japanese Buddhist and scholar with deep roots in medicine, psychology, and theology.

Westward Transmission: Hayashi and Takata

The transition of Reiki from a Japanese secret to a global phenomenon followed a specific path. Usui taught over 2,000 students, but he initiated only 20 as "Shinpiden" (Masters). Among them was Chujiro Hayashi, a retired naval physician who formalized the hand positions and clinical structure of the practice.

In 1935, Hawayo Takata, a Japanese-American woman from Hawaii, sought healing at Hayashi's clinic. Her recovery from severe illness led her to become the first woman initiated into the lineage. Takata is credited with bringing Reiki to the West, emphasizing the "Five Principles" and the professionalization of the practice.

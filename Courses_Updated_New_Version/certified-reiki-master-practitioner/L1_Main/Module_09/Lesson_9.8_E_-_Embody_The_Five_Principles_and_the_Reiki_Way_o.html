Common Pitfalls to Avoid

The Mistake Why It Happens The Correction
Over-Explaining Imposter syndrome; feeling the need to "prove" Reiki works. Keep the intro under 3 minutes. Let the experience do the talking.
Using "Woo" Jargon Falling back on training manuals rather than client-centered language. Swap "Auric cleansing" for "Clearing mental fog" or "Releasing tension."
Apologetic Presence Fear of being judged by the client. Maintain eye contact. Use a firm, warm voice. Don't fidget with your jewelry.
Ignoring Physical Comfort Focusing too much on the "energy" and not the human body. Always ask: "Is that bolster comfortable under your knees?" Comfort is the gateway to receptivity.
Professional Boundary Tip

Never promise a specific medical cure. If Diane asks if Reiki will "cure" her neck pain, respond: "Reiki supports your body's healing process. While many clients report significant pain reduction, our goal today is to lower the overall stress response that contributes to that tension."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks, "Is Reiki a religion?" What is the most professional way to respond?

"No, Reiki is not a religion. It is a secular technique for stress reduction and relaxation. It doesn't require any specific belief system to be effective, though many people find it complements their personal spiritual practices."

2. Diane is fidgeting and seems unable to relax 10 minutes into the session. What should you do?

Gently whisper or speak in a low tone: "Diane, I notice you're having a little trouble getting settled. Would you like an extra pillow, or shall we try a deep breath together to help your body land on the table?" Address the physical first.

3. What is the benefit of using "nervous system" language with a skeptical client?

It provides a biological framework that the client's logical mind can accept, reducing resistance and allowing the parasympathetic nervous system (rest and digest) to activate more quickly.

4. Why is the "Post-Session Wrap-Up" critical for your business growth?

It helps the client "anchor" the benefits they just experienced. By asking them how they feel, you help them realize the value of the session, making them much more likely to re-book and refer others.

Key Takeaways

  • Professionalism is Key: Your demeanor and language determine how much a client trusts the process.
  • The Bridge Technique: Use scientific-adjacent language (nervous system, stress reduction) to explain Reiki to newcomers.
  • Intake Matters: A solid intake session builds rapport and identifies the client's "pain points" (physical and emotional).
  • Practice Out Loud: Speaking the words before a client arrives reduces anxiety and builds practitioner authority.

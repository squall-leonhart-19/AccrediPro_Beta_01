The Evidence Hierarchy: Meta-Analyses

In the world of clinical research, a single study can be an outlier. However, a meta-analysis‚Äîwhich aggregates the results of multiple randomized controlled trials (RCTs)‚Äîprovides the highest level of evidence. For Reiki practitioners, the 2014 Thrane and Cohen meta-analysis remains a cornerstone of our professional legitimacy.

This landmark review examined Reiki's effect on pain and anxiety across multiple populations. The findings were striking: Reiki demonstrated a statistically significant reduction in pain scores, with an effect size (Cohen's d) often surpassing that of standard pharmacological interventions for mild to moderate pain.

Comparative Stress Efficacy: Reiki vs. Massage

While massage therapy is widely accepted for stress relief, research indicates that Reiki may be superior for autonomic nervous system (ANS) regulation. Because Reiki is non-manipulative, it can be applied to patients who are too fragile for massage (e.g., oncology patients or those with severe burns).

Studies measuring Heart Rate Variability (HRV)‚Äîthe gold standard for measuring stress resilience‚Äîshow that Reiki sessions consistently shift the body from a sympathetic (fight-or-flight) state to a parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) state more rapidly than quiet rest or light touch alone.

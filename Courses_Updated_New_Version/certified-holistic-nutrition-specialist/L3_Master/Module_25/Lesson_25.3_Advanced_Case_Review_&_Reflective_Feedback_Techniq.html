The Art of the Case Review: Structuring Presentations

In advanced supervision, the "Case Review" is the primary tool for clinical audit. A supervisor must teach mentees how to present a case succinctly but comprehensively. Without a structure, case reviews often devolve into a "storytelling" session that lacks clinical focus.

We utilize an adapted SBAR-N (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation, Nutrition) framework for holistic case reviews:

Phase Description Supervisor's Focus
Situation Client's current state, chief complaint, and the "stuck point." Is the mentee identifying the primary priority?
Background Relevant history (N.O.U.R.I.S.H. intake data), labs, and previous interventions. Did the mentee miss a root cause in the history?
Assessment The practitioner's hypothesis. Why is the client not progressing? Is the reasoning sound or based on assumptions?
Recommendation Proposed changes to the protocol or additional testing. Is the intervention too aggressive or too timid?
Coach Tip

When a mentee presents a case, listen for the "Narrative Bias." If they are overly focused on one symptom (e.g., weight loss resistance), gently pull them back to the R: Root Cause Analysis phase of the N.O.U.R.I.S.H. Method‚Ñ¢ to see if they've ignored the gut-brain axis.

Reflective Practice: Using Gibbs' Cycle

Clinical excellence is not just about knowledge; it's about reflection. Mentees often experience "imposter syndrome" or emotional exhaustion when a client doesn't improve. Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) provides a 6-stage framework to help mentees process these experiences.

  1. Description: What happened? (Just the facts).
  2. Feelings: What were you thinking and feeling during the session?
  3. Evaluation: What was good and bad about the experience?
  4. Analysis: What sense can you make of the situation? (Connect to theory).
  5. Conclusion: What else could you have done?
  6. Action Plan: If it arose again, what would you do?

Case Study: The "Difficult" Client Interaction

Practitioner: Sarah (45, former teacher, now Holistic Nutritionist).
Mentee: Jessica (28, recent graduate).
Scenario: Jessica is frustrated because her client with Hashimoto's is "not complying" with the gluten-free protocol and was aggressive in the last session.

Sarah's Intervention: Instead of giving Jessica advice on "how to make the client comply," Sarah uses Gibbs' Cycle. She discovers Jessica felt personally attacked (Feelings) and realized she was using a "parental" tone with the client (Analysis). The Action Plan involves switching to Motivational Interviewing techniques.

Feedback Delivery: Pendleton vs. Sandwich

While the "Feedback Sandwich" (Positive-Negative-Positive) is common, it is often seen as patronizing in high-level clinical environments. For Level 3 supervision, we recommend the Pendleton Model, which encourages the mentee to lead the evaluation.

The Pendleton Rules for Feedback:

  • The Mentee states what they did well.
  • The Supervisor states what went well (reinforcing the mentee's observations).
  • The Mentee states what could be improved and how.
  • The Supervisor states what could be improved (adding clinical nuance).
Coach Tip

A 2019 study in Medical Education found that student-led feedback models like Pendleton's result in a 22% higher retention of clinical skills compared to traditional top-down feedback. Let them speak first!

Identifying Practitioner 'Blind Spots'

Even experienced practitioners have biases. In holistic nutrition, these blind spots usually fall into three categories:

  • The "Supplement Hammer": Trying to fix every metabolic marker with a pill rather than addressing the S: Sustainable Systems (lifestyle).
  • Hormonal Tunnel Vision: Focusing solely on sex hormones (Estrogen/Progesterone) while ignoring the underlying Blood Sugar Dysregulation.
  • The "Good/Bad Food" Trap: Projecting the practitioner's own dietary preferences (e.g., Vegan or Keto) onto the client's bio-individuality.

As a supervisor, you must look for patterns in the mentee's recommendations. Do they suggest the same three supplements for every client? This indicates a lack of U: Unique Bio-individuality application.

Managing Clinical Disagreements

What happens when you and your mentee disagree on a protocol? Perhaps they want to use a high-dose iodine protocol for thyroid health, and you believe it's contraindicated for that client.

The "Evidence-Hierarchy" Resolution:

  1. Review the N.O.U.R.I.S.H. Method‚Ñ¢ Standards: Does the proposed intervention violate our scope or safety guidelines?
  2. Consult the Research: Look at recent meta-analyses together.
  3. Client Safety First: If the disagreement involves a safety risk, the supervisor's decision is final. If it is a matter of "style," allow the mentee to proceed but require a 2-week follow-up for reflective review.
Coach Tip

Professional maturity means being okay with your mentee being right! If they present a new study that challenges your long-held belief, model the "Growth Mindset" by acknowledging the new data. This builds immense trust.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which phase of the SBAR-N framework focuses on the practitioner's hypothesis regarding why the client is "stuck"?

The Assessment phase. This is where the practitioner interprets the data and forms a clinical hypothesis.

2. In Gibbs' Reflective Cycle, what is the purpose of the "Analysis" stage?

The Analysis stage is where the practitioner makes sense of the situation by connecting their feelings and observations to clinical theory or previous knowledge.

3. Why is the Pendleton Model preferred over the "Feedback Sandwich" in Level 3 supervision?

It is student-centered and encourages self-reflection first. This reduces defensiveness and builds the mentee's ability to self-audit their own clinical work.

4. What is a common "blind spot" for practitioners when dealing with hormonal cases?

Focusing strictly on sex hormones (like Estrogen) while ignoring the foundational role of blood sugar regulation and insulin sensitivity.

Key Takeaways

  • Structure is Safety: Use the SBAR-N framework to keep case reviews clinical and focused.
  • Self-Correction is the Goal: Use Gibbs' Cycle to move mentees from emotional reactions to clinical actions.
  • Mentees Lead Feedback: The Pendleton Model ensures mentees develop the "inner supervisor" needed for solo practice.
  • Watch for Patterns: Actively look for "Supplement Hammers" or dietary biases in your mentee's work.
  • Disagreement is Opportunity: Use clinical conflicts to deep-dive into the latest evidence together.

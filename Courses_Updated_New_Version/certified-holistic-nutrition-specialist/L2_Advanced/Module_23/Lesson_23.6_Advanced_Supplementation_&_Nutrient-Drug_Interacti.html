Pharmacokinetics of Therapeutic Nutrition

When we move beyond general wellness and into therapeutic nutrition, we must understand how the body processes these concentrated substances. Pharmacokinetics‚Äîthe study of how the body interacts with a substance‚Äîis defined by the ADME acronym:

  • Absorption: How the nutrient enters the bloodstream (e.g., small intestine vs. sublingual).
  • Distribution: How the nutrient travels to target tissues (e.g., crossing the blood-brain barrier).
  • Metabolism: How the body breaks down the nutrient (largely via the liver's cytochrome P450 enzymes).
  • Excretion: How the body eliminates the nutrient (kidneys/urine or liver/bile).
Coach Tip

Clients often think "more is better." Use the concept of the Therapeutic Window to explain that there is a specific range where a nutrient is effective; below it, it does nothing, and above it, it may cause toxicity or receptor downregulation.

Liposomal Technology: The Future of Delivery

One of the biggest hurdles in holistic nutrition is bioavailability. Many nutrients, like Curcumin or Glutathione, are poorly absorbed by the digestive tract. Enter Liposomal Delivery.

A liposome is a tiny bubble (vesicle) made out of the same material as a cell membrane (phospholipids). By "wrapping" the nutrient in this lipid layer, we allow it to:

  • Bypass the harsh environment of the stomach acid.
  • Fuse directly with human cell membranes.
  • Increase blood levels significantly compared to standard capsules.

For example, liposomal Vitamin C can reach blood concentrations nearly double that of standard oral Vitamin C, approaching the efficacy of intravenous (IV) therapy.

Coach Tip

When working with clients who have "gut issues" or malabsorption, prioritize liposomal or sublingual forms. This ensures they actually get the benefit of the supplement while you work on the O (Optimized Digestion) phase of the N.O.U.R.I.S.H. Method‚Ñ¢.

Synergistic Pairing: Maximizing Absorption

Nutrients do not work in isolation. In nature, they are packaged with co-factors. In advanced practice, we mimic this through Synergistic Pairing.

  • Vitamin D3 + K2: D3 increases calcium absorption, but K2 ensures that calcium goes into the bones and teeth rather than the arteries.
  • Curcumin + Piperine: Black pepper extract (piperine) can increase curcumin absorption by up to 2,000%.
  • Iron + Vitamin C: Vitamin C creates an acidic environment that keeps iron in its more absorbable "ferrous" state.
  • Magnesium + Vitamin B6: B6 acts as a "chaperone," helping magnesium cross the cell membrane.

Supplement Cycling & 'Washout' Periods

The body is highly adaptive. If you provide a high dose of a specific nutrient or herb indefinitely, the body may "downregulate" its receptors, making the supplement less effective over time. This is particularly true for adaptogens (like Ashwagandha) and hormonal precursors.

Standard Cycling Protocols:

  • 5-on, 2-off: Taking the supplement during the workweek and pausing on weekends.
  • 3-weeks on, 1-week off: Common for immune boosters or herbal antimicrobials.
  • Seasonal Cycling: High-dose Vitamin D in winter, lower doses in summer.
Coach Tip

Always document a "washout period" of 1-2 weeks before a client undergoes major lab testing. This ensures the results reflect the client's baseline metabolic state, not the "interference" of recent supplementation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Vitamin K2 essential when supplementing with high-dose Vitamin D3?

Vitamin K2 activates proteins (like osteocalcin and MGP) that direct calcium into the bones and prevent it from depositing in the soft tissues and arteries, reducing the risk of calcification.

2. Which common medication is known to deplete Vitamin B12 and potentially lead to neuropathy?

Metformin (used for blood sugar management) is the most common culprit for B12 depletion.

3. What is the primary advantage of Liposomal delivery?

Liposomal delivery uses a phospholipid bilayer to protect the nutrient from degradation and allows for direct fusion with cell membranes, significantly increasing bioavailability.

4. What does the "M" in the ADME pharmacokinetics acronym stand for?

Metabolism. It refers to how the body (primarily the liver) breaks down and transforms the substance.

Key Takeaways

  • Knowledge is Safety: Always screen clients for medications; statins, metformin, and PPIs have the most documented nutrient depletions.
  • Quality Over Quantity: Liposomal and synergistic formulations offer better clinical outcomes than high-dose "cheap" alternatives.
  • The Triage Theory: When nutrients are low, the body prioritizes short-term survival over long-term repair. Supplementation helps "re-fund" the repair budget.
  • Strategic Breaks: Implement cycling (like 5 days on, 2 days off) to prevent receptor fatigue and maintain long-term efficacy.

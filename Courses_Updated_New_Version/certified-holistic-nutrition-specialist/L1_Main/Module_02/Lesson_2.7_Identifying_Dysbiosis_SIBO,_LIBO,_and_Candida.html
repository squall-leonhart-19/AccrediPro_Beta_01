Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO)

SIBO is defined as the presence of excessive bacteria in the small intestine‚Äîan area that should ideally have a relatively low bacterial count compared to the colon. While the large intestine houses trillions of microbes, the small intestine is designed for nutrient absorption and requires a cleaner environment.

The primary mechanism of SIBO is often a failure of the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC), the "cleansing wave" of the gut that sweeps debris and bacteria into the large intestine during fasting states. Research indicates that 60% to 80% of individuals diagnosed with IBS may actually have underlying SIBO (Pimentel et al., 2020).

Practitioner Insight

Many of your clients will describe "bloating that starts 30-60 minutes after eating." This timing is a classic hallmark of SIBO, as food reaches the small intestine and is prematurely fermented by the overgrowth, producing gas in a confined space.

Hydrogen vs. Methane: The Gas Distinction

The type of gas produced determines the clinical symptom profile and the nutritional approach:

Gas Type Primary Microbe Symptom Profile Stool Consistency
Hydrogen (H2) Proteus, E. coli, Klebsiella Rapid bloating, abdominal pain Diarrhea-predominant (SIBO-D)
Methane (CH4) Methanobrevibacter smithii (Archaea) Severe bloating, weight gain Constipation-predominant (SIBO-C/IMO)
Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S) Desulfovibrio, Bilophila wadsworthia "Rotten egg" gas, body aches Variable (often urgent diarrhea)

Large Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (LIBO)

While SIBO gets most of the attention, LIBO (Large Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth) refers to an over-representation of commensal bacteria in their proper home‚Äîthe colon. The issue here isn't location, but quantity and balance.

In LIBO, the client may tolerate fiber initially but experiences "fermentation overload" as the day progresses. Unlike SIBO, where bloating happens quickly after a meal, LIBO bloating often peaks in the late afternoon or evening as the cumulative fiber intake reaches the large intestine.

Candida Albicans and Fungal Overgrowth

Candida is a yeast that is part of the normal human flora. However, when the bacterial "checks and balances" are removed (often by antibiotics, high sugar intake, or oral contraceptives), Candida can shift into its pathogenic, hyphal form. This form can penetrate the intestinal lining, contributing to Intestinal Permeability (Lesson 4).

The 'Sugar-Craving' Microbiome: Candida thrives on simple carbohydrates. It can actually manipulate host behavior by altering taste receptors and secreting analogs of hunger hormones, creating intense, "must-have-it-now" sugar cravings.

Coach Tip

When a client says, "I have no willpower with sugar," look for Candida. It's often not a lack of discipline, but a biological drive from the fungi. Reframing this for the client can alleviate the "imposter syndrome" many women feel about their health journey.

Functional Stool Analysis: Interpreting Key Markers

A comprehensive stool test provides a "weather report" of the digestive landscape. As a Holistic Nutritionist, you look at these markers to assess the environment that allowed dysbiosis to occur.

  • Secretory IgA (sIgA): The first line of immune defense in the gut.
    • Low sIgA: Indicates a depleted immune system, often due to chronic stress or long-term infection.
    • High sIgA: Indicates an active immune response to a current "invader" (parasite, yeast, or pathogenic bacteria).
  • Calprotectin: A protein marker of neutrophil-induced inflammation.
    • Levels > 50-100 Œºg/g: Suggest significant inflammation. Very high levels (>200) require referral to rule out IBD (Crohn's/Colitis).
  • Steatocrit: Measures the percentage of fat in the stool.
    • Elevated Steatocrit: Suggests fat malabsorption, often linked to poor biliary flow (Lesson 3) or pancreatic insufficiency.
Clinical Pearl

If you see low Steatocrit and high Methane, the constipation may be physically slowing down fat absorption. Always address the "flow" (motility) before adding heavy antimicrobial protocols.

Red Flags for Clinical Referral

Your role is to support the body's terrain, but you must recognize when a client's symptoms exceed the scope of holistic nutrition. The following "Red Flags" require an immediate referral to a medical professional:

  1. Unexplained Weight Loss: Losing 10+ lbs without trying.
  2. Blood in Stool: Bright red or black/tarry stools.
  3. Nocturnal Diarrhea: Being woken up from sleep to have a bowel movement (this is rarely "just" SIBO).
  4. Severe Abdominal Pain: Pain that is localized, sharp, or prevents normal movement.
  5. Family History of Colorectal Cancer: In a first-degree relative.
Professional Standards

Always document your referral in your client notes. Saying, "I recommend you share these results with your GP to rule out underlying pathology," demonstrates high professional ethics and protects your practice.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which gas type is most commonly associated with constipation-predominant SIBO (SIBO-C)?

Methane (CH4). This is produced by Methanobrevibacter smithii, which slows down intestinal transit time, leading to constipation.

2. What is the primary function of the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)?

The MMC acts as the "cleansing wave" of the small intestine during fasting. It sweeps undigested food and bacteria into the large intestine to prevent SIBO.

3. A client presents with a Calprotectin level of 450 Œºg/g. What is the appropriate next step?

This is a significant "Red Flag" marker for inflammation. You must refer the client to a gastroenterologist to rule out Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) like Crohn's or Ulcerative Colitis.

4. Why does Candida overgrowth often cause intense sugar cravings?

Candida thrives on simple sugars for fuel and can manipulate host behavior by altering taste receptors and secreting hormone-like analogs that trigger hunger and cravings.

Key Takeaways

  • SIBO is a Motility Issue: Most SIBO cases result from a dysfunctional MMC, not just "bad luck" with bacteria.
  • Gas Matters: Hydrogen leads to diarrhea; Methane leads to constipation; Hydrogen Sulfide leads to systemic pain.
  • The Candida Connection: Fungal overgrowth is often a secondary consequence of antibiotic use or high-carb diets.
  • Stool Markers Provide Context: Use sIgA to assess immune status and Calprotectin to monitor for referral-level inflammation.
  • Referral is Professionalism: Knowing when to refer out is a sign of an expert practitioner, not a lack of knowledge.

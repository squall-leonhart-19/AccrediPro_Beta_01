Common Pitfalls to Avoid

When I first started coaching, I made every single one of these mistakes. Here is how you can avoid them:

  • The "Information Dump": Giving the client a 20-minute lecture on biochemistry. They don't need to know how the Krebs cycle works; they need to know why they are tired.
  • Being the "Food Police": Focusing on what they are doing wrong rather than the physiological reason their body is struggling.
  • Skipping the "Why": Telling a client to stop coffee without explaining how it's impacting their specific root cause (e.g., adrenal health).
  • Ignoring the Emotional Root: Failing to acknowledge that stress or childhood habits are often the "root of the root."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is using an analogy like the "Bank Account" effective during a coaching session?

Analogies bridge the gap between complex clinical data and the client's lived experience. They reduce overwhelm, increase "buy-in," and help the client visualize the physiological process without needing a science degree.

2. What is the "Information Dump" and why should you avoid it?

The Information Dump is providing too much technical data at once. It triggers a "freeze" response in the client's brain, making them feel incapable of following the plan. It centers the coach's ego rather than the client's needs.

3. Diane is doing Keto and HIIT but gaining weight. What is the likely root cause discussed?

Chronic Cortisol Elevation (Stress). Her body is in a "resource-withdrawal" state, and the intense diet/exercise are perceived as additional stressors, causing the body to hold onto fat for survival.

4. How should a coach handle a client who asks, "Is it just my age?"

Acknowledge the biological shifts of aging, but pivot to the root cause. Explain that while age is a factor, the severity of symptoms is usually driven by underlying system imbalances (like gut health or stress) that can be improved.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Validation First: Always validate the client's frustration before diving into the data.
  • Simplicity is Authority: The more simply you can explain a root cause, the more the client will trust your expertise.
  • Focus on the "Lead Domino": Don't try to fix 10 things at once. Identify the one root cause that impacts the others.
  • Practice Out Loud: Your coaching voice is a tool that needs to be sharpened through verbal repetition.

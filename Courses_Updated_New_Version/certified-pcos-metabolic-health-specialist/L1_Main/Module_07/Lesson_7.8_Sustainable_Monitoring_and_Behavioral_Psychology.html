1. Your Client Scenario

In this practice lab, we are working with a client who represents a large segment of the private practice market: the "sandwich generation" woman dealing with long-term hormonal neglect.

Client Profile: Sandra, Age 51

Background: Sandra is a former elementary school teacher who recently pivoted into educational consulting. She has two teenagers and an aging mother she cares for. She was diagnosed with PCOS in her 20s but was only ever told to "take the pill."

Category Current Status
Chief Complaints "Stubborn" midsection weight gain (+15 lbs in 1 year), brain fog, and 3:00 PM energy crashes.
Lifestyle Sedentary (desk work), 5-6 hours of sleep, "healthy" but erratic eating (salads for lunch, wine/crackers for dinner).
Mindset Frustrated. "I feel like I'm doing everything right and my body is betraying me."
Sarah's Insight

When a client says they are "doing everything right," they are usually following 1990s diet advice (low calorie, high cardio). Our job isn't to tell them they're wrong, but to offer a new perspective based on their current biology.

2. The Coaching Script: Word-for-Word

Practice these lines out loud. Notice how the script uses validation before moving to education.

Coach: "Sandra, I want to pause here. I hear the frustration in your voice, and I want you to knowâ€”you aren't failing. Your body is actually doing exactly what it's programmed to do under the current stress and hormonal shifts. Does it help to hear that this isn't a willpower issue?"

Sandra: "It does... but I still don't understand why I can't lose a single pound when I'm barely eating."

Coach: "That is the 'Metabolic Gap' we talk about. In your 50s, with a history of PCOS, your cells are essentially 'protective' of energy. When we 'eat less and do more,' your body thinks there's a famine and holds onto every ounce of fat for safety. My goal is to show your body it's safe to release that energy. How does that sound as a starting point?"

4. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced coaches fall into these traps. Being aware of them is 50% of the battle!

  • The "Information Dump": Trying to explain the entire Krebs cycle to a client who just wants to know why her pants don't fit. Keep science "bite-sized."
  • Fixing Instead of Coaching: Jumping straight to a supplement list before Sandra feels heard. Validation must precede prescription.
  • Ignoring the "Sandwich" Stress: Forgetting that Sandra is caring for teens and a parent. If the plan doesn't fit her "chaos," she won't do it.
  • Apologizing for Your Price: When Sandra asks about the program cost, stating it with a "question mark" tone. Speak your value with a "period."

I transitioned from nursing to coaching at 45. I thought I needed to know everything. I didn't. I just needed to know 10% more than my client and have a 100% better listening ear.

Check Your Practice Knowledge

1. Sandra says, "I'm just going to start a 3-day juice cleanse to jumpstart things." What is the best specialist response?

Instead of saying "Don't do that," say: "I understand the desire for a quick win! However, with PCOS and your current stress levels, a juice cleanse can actually spike cortisol and tell your body to store more fat later. Let's look at a 'Metabolic Jumpstart' that includes protein to protect your muscle mass instead."

2. What is the primary purpose of the "Validation" step in the coaching script?

Validation lowers the client's "threat response" (amygdala activation). When a client feels judged or like they are failing, they cannot process new educational information. Validation opens the door for learning.

3. Which "Success Marker" indicates the client is moving toward self-efficacy?

Collaborative Planning. When the client begins to suggest their own small adjustments, it shows they are moving from a passive "fix me" mindset to an active "I am the CEO of my health" mindset.

4. Why is "Information Dumping" considered a pitfall?

It overwhelms the client's prefrontal cortex, which is already taxed by brain fog and stress. A confused mind always says "no" or fails to take action. Keep explanations simple and actionable.

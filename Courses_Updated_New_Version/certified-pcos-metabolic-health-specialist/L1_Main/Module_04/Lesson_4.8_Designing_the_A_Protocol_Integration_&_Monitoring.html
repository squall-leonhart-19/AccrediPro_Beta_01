4. Common Coaching Pitfalls

New coaches often fall into these traps when discussing activity. Avoid these to maintain your professional authority:

  • The "Anti-Cardio" Extremist: Don't tell a runner that running is "bad." Instead, explain that it is "unbalanced" for her current hormonal state.
  • Over-complicating the Science: You don't need to explain the entire Krebs cycle. Stick to "Cortisol vs. Muscle" and "Insulin Sensitivity."
  • Giving a "Workout Plan" Too Fast: Coaching is about buy-in. If she doesn't understand the "Why," she won't do the "What."
  • Ignoring NEAT: Don't forget to mention that walking (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) is often more metabolically effective for PCOS than a grueling HIIT class.

5. Success Markers

How do you know if this session with Diane was a success? Look for these three signs:

  1. The "Aha" Moment: She stops saying "I need to burn more" and starts asking "How do I get stronger?"
  2. Reduced Anxiety: She feels a sense of relief that she doesn't *have* to run 5 miles to be healthy.
  3. Specific Action: She agrees to swap one run for one full-body resistance session this week.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is chronic cardio often counterproductive for a perimenopausal woman with PCOS?

It can chronically elevate cortisol, which signals the body to store visceral (midsection) fat and can further impair insulin sensitivity, especially when recovery capacity is lower due to hormonal shifts.

2. What is the primary "metabolic" benefit of resistance training over cardio?

Resistance training increases GLUT4 translocation, allowing muscle cells to take up glucose more effectively, thereby improving systemic insulin sensitivity and metabolic rate even at rest.

The Ovarian Engine: How Insulin Drives Androgens

In a healthy system, insulin‚Äôs primary job is to act as a key that unlocks cells to let glucose in for energy. However, in PCOS, the ovaries remain exquisitely sensitive to insulin, even when the rest of the body (muscles and liver) has become resistant.

When insulin levels rise (hyperinsulinemia), insulin acts as a "co-gonadotropin." It works alongside Luteinizing Hormone (LH) to stimulate the theca cells of the ovaries. These cells are the "androgen factories" of the female body. Under the influence of high insulin, these factories go into overdrive, churning out excessive amounts of testosterone and androstenedione.

SHBG: The Carrier Crisis

It isn't just that the ovaries are producing more testosterone; it's that the body has less ability to buffer it. Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin (SHBG) is a protein produced by the liver that acts like a "hormone bus," carrying testosterone safely through the bloodstream where it cannot bind to receptors.

High levels of insulin directly suppress the liver's production of SHBG. When SHBG is low, there are fewer "buses" to pick up testosterone. This results in an increase in Free Testosterone‚Äîthe bioavailable form that causes hirsutism (excess hair), cystic acne, and androgenic alopecia (hair thinning).

The "Glucose Rollercoaster": Cravings and Crashes

For the 40-55 year old woman entering your practice, the most frustrating part of PCOS is often the "lack of willpower." Scientific data shows this has nothing to do with character and everything to do with glucose volatility.

When a client with PCOS eats a high-glycemic meal, her insulin response is often exaggerated (hyperinsulinemia). This massive insulin spike causes blood sugar to crash rapidly. This "hypoglycemic dip" triggers the brain's survival mechanism, leading to:

  • Urgent Cravings: The brain demands quick energy (sugar/carbs) to raise glucose.
  • Brain Fog: Lack of stable fuel for the central nervous system.
  • Irritability: Often referred to as "hanger."

Follicular Arrest: Why the Egg Won't Release

High insulin doesn't just create "masculinizing" symptoms; it stops the reproductive cycle in its tracks. In a normal cycle, several follicles begin to mature, and one becomes dominant. In a high-insulin environment, the excess androgens created by the theca cells interfere with this maturation.

The follicles reach a certain size but then "arrest" (stop growing). They never reach the stage of ovulation. Under ultrasound, these arrested follicles look like a "string of pearls," which is where the term "Polycystic" originates. Crucially, it is the insulin-driven androgen excess that acts as the "stop sign" for ovulation.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does insulin act as a "co-gonadotropin" in the ovaries?

Insulin works alongside Luteinizing Hormone (LH) to stimulate the theca cells, which increases the production of ovarian androgens (testosterone).

2. Why does low SHBG lead to worse acne and hair loss?

SHBG binds to testosterone. When insulin suppresses SHBG, there is more "free" (unbound) testosterone available to interact with receptors in the skin and hair follicles.

3. What percentage of "Lean" PCOS women are estimated to have insulin resistance?

Approximately 75% of lean women with PCOS exhibit some form of insulin resistance or hyperinsulinemia.

4. What is "Follicular Arrest" in the context of insulin?

It is the process where high levels of androgens (driven by insulin) prevent follicles from maturing fully, leading to a failure to ovulate and the appearance of "cysts" on the ovaries.

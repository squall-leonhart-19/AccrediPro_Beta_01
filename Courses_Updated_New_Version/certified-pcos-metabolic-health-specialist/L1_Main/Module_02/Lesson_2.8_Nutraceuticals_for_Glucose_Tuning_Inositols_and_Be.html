Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When you are first starting out, it is easy to fall into these traps. Here is what to watch for:

  • The "Food Police" Trap: Labels foods as "good" or "bad." (Instead, use "high spike" or "slow burn").
  • The "Data Overload" Trap: Explaining the Krebs cycle or insulin signaling in too much detail. (Keep it simple: Energy in vs. Energy out).
  • The "Fix-It" Mentality: Giving 10 changes at once. (Stick to 1-2 "Tuning" experiments per week).
  • Ignoring the Emotional Connection: Forgetting that Linda's 3 PM latte might be her only "treat" in a stressful day. (Always acknowledge the emotional value of food).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Roller Coaster" analogy effective for clients like Linda?

It provides a visual, non-judgmental explanation for her physical symptoms (fatigue, irritability) without blaming her food choices. It shifts the focus from "what I ate" to "how my body responded."

2. What is a "Metabolic Buffer"?

A metabolic buffer is a macronutrient (usually protein, fiber, or healthy fat) added to a carbohydrate to slow down gastric emptying and blunt the glucose spike. Examples: adding walnuts to oatmeal or avocado to toast.

3. A client says, "I'm afraid to use a glucose monitor because I don't want to see bad numbers." How do you respond?

Reframe the data as "neutral information." You might say: "There are no bad numbers, only data points that tell us how your unique body is talking to us. It's like a fuel gauge in a carâ€”it's just telling us when to tune the engine."

4. What is the main goal of the first "Tuning Experiment"?

The goal is to build the client's self-efficacy and show them that a small change can lead to a noticeable improvement in energy. Success in the first experiment builds the momentum needed for deeper metabolic work.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Language Matters: Use "Spike and Crash" or "Tuning" instead of "Bad" or "Cheating."
  • Add, Don't Subtract: Focus on what the client can add (buffers) to stabilize their energy first.
  • Validate the Struggle: Acknowledge that perimenopause changes the "rules" of metabolism, and it's not the client's fault.
  • The Outcome is Energy: Always tie glucose data back to the client's primary goal (e.g., "having energy for your kids" or "no more brain fog at work").

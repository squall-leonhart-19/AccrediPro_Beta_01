The Two Engines: Ovarian vs. Adrenal Androgens

While we often speak of "PCOS" as an ovarian condition, excess androgens can originate from two primary "engines." Understanding the source is vital for tailoring your client's protocol.

The ovaries are responsible for approximately 60% of circulating testosterone in PCOS, primarily driven by LH (Luteinizing Hormone) and insulin. However, the adrenal glands contribute significantly, with 20-30% of PCOS women exhibiting "Adrenal PCOS" phenotypes characterized by high DHEA-S.

The Insulin-Androgen Axis: The Theca Cell Overdrive

In a healthy cycle, theca cells in the ovary produce a small amount of androgens, which are then converted into estrogen by granulosa cells. In PCOS, this process is hijacked by hyperinsulinemia.

Insulin acts as a "co-gonadotropin." This means it works alongside LH to stimulate the theca cells. High insulin levels:

  • Upregulates CYP17A1: This enzyme is the "gatekeeper" for androgen production. Insulin makes it hyper-active.
  • Suppresses SHBG: Sex Hormone Binding Globulin is a protein made by the liver that "moops up" excess hormones. Insulin tells the liver to stop making SHBG, leaving more Free Testosterone to cause symptoms.
  • Sensitizes the Pituitary: High insulin can make the pituitary gland more sensitive, leading to the classic high LH:FSH ratio.

5-Alpha Reductase: The Potency Multiplier

Not all androgens are created equal. While testosterone is the most discussed, Dihydrotestosterone (DHT) is the real culprit behind hair loss and hirsutism. DHT is 3 to 5 times more potent than testosterone and binds more tightly to androgen receptors.

The enzyme responsible for this conversion is 5-alpha reductase. In many women with PCOS, this enzyme is "upregulated" or overactive. What drives this overactivity?

  1. Insulin: Once again, high insulin levels increase 5-alpha reductase activity.
  2. Inflammation: Systemic inflammation (Module 5) creates a biochemical environment that favors DHT conversion.
  3. Genetics: Some women are genetically predisposed to higher enzyme activity in the skin and hair follicles.

Clinical Manifestations: Beyond the Mirror

Hyperandrogenism presents in three primary ways that significantly impact a woman's quality of life and self-esteem:

  • Hirsutism: Excess terminal (dark, coarse) hair in male-pattern areas (chin, chest, abdomen). A 2022 study found that 70-80% of women with hirsutism have underlying PCOS.
  • Cystic Acne: Typically located along the jawline and neck. Androgens increase sebum (oil) production, which traps bacteria in the pores.
  • Androgenic Alopecia: Thinning at the crown or widening of the part. This is often the most psychologically distressing symptom for women in their 40s and 50s.

The Vicious Cycle: Androgens and Abdominal Fat

One of the most frustrating aspects of hyperandrogenism is the feedback loop it creates. High androgens don't just cause symptoms; they worsen the metabolic state.

Excess testosterone promotes visceral adiposity (fat stored around the organs in the abdomen). Unlike subcutaneous fat (under the skin), visceral fat is metabolically active and secretes inflammatory cytokines. These cytokines further increase insulin resistance, which‚Äîyou guessed it‚Äîleads to more androgen production.

The Metabolic Trap

This "Androgen-Insulin Loop" is why many women find it nearly impossible to lose weight in the midsection despite "eating less." You must break the cycle by addressing Insulin Sensitivity (Module 2) and Inflammation (Module 5) simultaneously.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which enzyme is responsible for converting testosterone into the more potent DHT?

The enzyme is 5-alpha reductase. Its activity is often upregulated by insulin and inflammation in PCOS.

2. How does insulin affect SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin)?

High insulin suppresses SHBG production in the liver. Lower SHBG means more "Free" Testosterone is available to cause symptoms like acne and hair growth.

3. If a client has elevated DHEA-S but normal Total Testosterone, where is the primary androgen source?

The primary source is the Adrenal Glands. This suggests an "Adrenal PCOS" phenotype driven by HPA axis stress.

4. What is the "co-gonadotropin" effect of insulin?

Insulin acts alongside LH to stimulate the theca cells of the ovaries, directly increasing the production of androgens.

Key Takeaways

  • Hyperandrogenism in PCOS is primarily driven by the "Insulin-Androgen Axis" where insulin overstimulates ovarian theca cells.
  • Differentiating between Ovarian (Testosterone) and Adrenal (DHEA-S) sources is essential for protocol precision.
  • DHT is the most potent androgen and the primary driver of hair loss and hirsutism, fueled by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase.
  • High androgens create a vicious cycle by promoting visceral fat storage, which in turn worsens insulin resistance.
  • SHBG acts as a metabolic "buffer"; increasing SHBG through insulin management is a key strategy for reducing free androgens.

Case Study: The "Normal Lab" Paradox

Sarah, 44, Former Educator

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with adult acne, thinning hair at the temples, and significant midsection weight gain. She had been told by her PCP that her "hormones were fine" because her Total Testosterone was 42 ng/dL (Ref: 2-45 ng/dL).

The Specialist Investigation: Upon deeper screening, we found:

  • SHBG: 18 nmol/L (Low)
  • Free Testosterone: 8.2 pg/mL (High-Optimal)
  • DHEAS: 380 ¬µg/dL (Elevated)

Outcome: Despite a "normal" Total Testosterone, Sarah's low SHBG meant her Free Testosterone was excessively high, and her elevated DHEAS pointed toward a significant adrenal component. This insight allowed us to pivot her protocol toward adrenal support and insulin sensitizing, rather than just "hormone balancing."

Total vs. Free Testosterone: The Bioavailability Factor

In the world of PCOS, Total Testosterone is only half the story. Total Testosterone measures all the testosterone in the blood, including the portion that is "bound" to proteins and therefore inactive. In women, approximately 66% of testosterone is bound tightly to Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) and 33% is bound loosely to albumin.

Only about 1% to 2% of testosterone is "Free"‚Äîmeaning it is unbound and able to enter cells to exert its effects. This "Free" portion is what drives the clinical symptoms of hirsutism, acne, and androgenic alopecia.

Coach Tip

When reviewing labs with a client, use the "Mailbox Analogy." Total Testosterone is like all the mail in the post office. Free Testosterone is the mail that actually gets delivered to your house. You only care about the mail you actually receive!

SHBG: The Metabolic Traffic Controller

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) is a protein produced by the liver. Its primary job is to regulate the amount of free sex hormones (testosterone and estrogen) circulating in the body. In the context of PCOS, SHBG is a canary in the coal mine for insulin resistance.

High levels of insulin directly suppress the liver's production of SHBG. Therefore, low SHBG is often a proxy marker for hyperinsulinemia. When SHBG is low, more testosterone remains "free," even if the total amount of testosterone produced is within normal limits.

DHEAS: Assessing the Adrenal Contribution

While testosterone is primarily produced by the ovaries (and peripheral conversion), DHEAS (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate) is almost exclusively produced by the adrenal glands. A 2022 study published in The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that approximately 20-30% of women with PCOS exhibit adrenal androgen excess, characterized primarily by elevated DHEAS.

Identifying elevated DHEAS is crucial because it suggests that the HPA (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal) axis is involved. These clients often require a different therapeutic approach‚Äîfocusing on stress modulation and nervous system regulation‚Äîcompared to those with purely ovarian-driven PCOS.

Coach Tip

If you see high DHEAS with normal Testosterone, look closely at the client's stress levels and sleep quality. You are likely looking at an Adrenal Phenotype (PCOS Type 4 or "Hidden" PCOS).

Androstenedione: The Overlooked Precursor

Androstenedione is a "pro-androgen" produced by both the ovaries and the adrenals. It serves as the immediate precursor to both testosterone and estrone. In many clinical settings, Androstenedione is actually more sensitive than testosterone for diagnosing PCOS.

Research indicates that some women with PCOS will have elevated Androstenedione while all other androgen markers remain technically "normal." Including this in your screening panel provides a more comprehensive view of the androgenic pool.

Establishing Specialist-Level Reference Intervals

As a specialist, you must understand that "Normal" (the lab's average) is not the same as "Optimal" (the range where symptoms resolve). Standard lab ranges are often too wide because they are based on a population that includes undiagnosed PCOS patients.

Marker Standard "Normal" Range Specialist "Optimal" Range
Total Testosterone 2 - 45 ng/dL 15 - 30 ng/dL
Free Testosterone 0.1 - 6.4 pg/mL 1.0 - 3.5 pg/mL
SHBG 18 - 144 nmol/L 70 - 100 nmol/L
DHEAS 40 - 325 ¬µg/dL 120 - 220 ¬µg/dL
Androstenedione 30 - 230 ng/dL 80 - 150 ng/dL
Coach Tip

Always check the units! Some labs report in ng/dL, others in nmol/L. As a specialist, you should have a conversion calculator bookmarked to ensure you are comparing apples to apples.

The Free Androgen Index (FAI)

The Free Androgen Index is a calculated value that provides a clearer picture of androgen status than Total Testosterone alone. It is particularly useful when Free Testosterone is not directly measured by the lab.

The Formula:
FAI = (Total Testosterone in nmol/L √∑ SHBG in nmol/L) x 100

An FAI greater than 5 is generally considered indicative of PCOS-related hyperandrogenism, though many specialists look for an FAI below 3 for optimal symptom management.

Coach Tip

The FAI is your best friend when a client brings in old labs that only have Total T and SHBG. It allows you to "extract" more data from limited information, proving your expertise immediately.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Total Testosterone often an unreliable marker for PCOS symptoms?

Total Testosterone includes the portion bound to SHBG, which is biologically inactive. Symptoms are driven by "Free" (unbound) testosterone, which can be high even if Total T is normal, especially if SHBG is low.

2. What metabolic condition is a low SHBG level often signaling?

Low SHBG is a primary marker for hyperinsulinemia (high insulin). High insulin levels suppress the liver's production of SHBG, leading to higher levels of free, active androgens.

3. Which marker specifically points to an adrenal contribution to PCOS?

DHEAS is almost exclusively produced by the adrenal glands. Elevated DHEAS levels indicate that the HPA axis and adrenal androgen production are contributing to the hyperandrogenic state.

4. What is the FAI formula and what level is clinically significant?

FAI = (Total Testosterone / SHBG) x 100. An FAI > 5 is typically diagnostic of hyperandrogenism, while specialists often aim for < 3 for symptom resolution.

Key Takeaways

  • Total T is not enough: Always request Free Testosterone and SHBG to see the full bioavailable picture.
  • SHBG is a metabolic bridge: Use low SHBG as a clue to investigate insulin resistance (the "T" in S.T.E.A.D.Y.‚Ñ¢).
  • Identify the source: Elevated DHEAS signals an adrenal-driven phenotype, requiring stress-management interventions.
  • Calculate the FAI: Use the Free Androgen Index to provide deeper insights when standard labs are limited.
  • Aim for Optimal: Don't settle for "normal" lab ranges; look for the tighter specialist intervals where symptoms actually resolve.

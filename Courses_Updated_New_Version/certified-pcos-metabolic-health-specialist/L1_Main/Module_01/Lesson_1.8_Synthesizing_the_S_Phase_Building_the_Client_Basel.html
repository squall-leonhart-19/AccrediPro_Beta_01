Common Mistakes & Success Markers

The Mistake The Fix
The "Science Dump": Explaining the entire Krebs cycle to the client. Use the "So What?" rule. Only share science that explains why they feel a specific symptom.
Playing Doctor: Saying "I can cure your PCOS/Menopause." Stay in your scope: "We are supporting your metabolic function to reduce symptom expression."
Ignoring the Emotional: Rushing to the labs without acknowledging her frustration. Always start with empathy: "I hear how hard this has been for you."
The "Price Apology": Mumbling your program price because you feel like an imposter. State your price clearly after you've shown her exactly how you will solve her problem.

How to Know the Session Went Well

  • The "Aha" Moment: Diane says, "Oh! So that's why I'm craving sugar at 3 PM!"
  • Shift in Energy: She moves from feeling defeated ("I'm just old") to empowered ("There's a reason for this").
  • Trust Established: She asks, "So, what do we do next?" (This is the green light to offer your coaching package).
Coach Sarah's Tip

If a client asks a question you don't know the answer to, don't panic! Say: "That's a fantastic, nuanced question. Let me consult my clinical resources to give you the most accurate answer, and I'll get back to you by tomorrow." This actually increases your perceived professionalism.

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane's A1c is 5.6%. Why is it important to address this even if her doctor said it's "normal"?

Because 5.6% is at the high end of the functional range, indicating that her body is struggling to maintain glucose homeostasis. This "struggle" (hyperinsulinemia) is likely driving her fatigue and weight gain, even before it reaches the clinical "prediabetes" threshold of 5.7%.

2. What is the "So What?" rule in coaching communication?

It means only sharing technical information if it directly connects to a client's lived experience. For example: "Your testosterone is high (the science), so that's why you're noticing changes in your skin and hair (the 'so what')."

3. If Diane feels her body is "betraying her," what is your primary role in the first 10 minutes?

Your role is to provide empathy and validation. You must show her that her symptoms are logical responses to physiological imbalances, not a personal failure or just "getting old."

4. Why do we use the term "Metabolic Friction" instead of "Disease"?

It stays within the coaching scope of practice. Coaches don't diagnose or treat disease, but we do identify and resolve metabolic imbalances (friction) that prevent the body from functioning optimally.

Key Takeaways

  • Assessment calls are about connecting symptoms to labs to provide a "roadmap" for the client.
  • Validation and empathy are the foundation of the coach-client relationship.
  • Functional ranges (like A1c 5.6%) allow us to intervene before a clinical diagnosis is made.
  • Professional language, when explained simply, justifies your value as a specialist.
  • Practice out loud to move from "imposter syndrome" to "expert authority."

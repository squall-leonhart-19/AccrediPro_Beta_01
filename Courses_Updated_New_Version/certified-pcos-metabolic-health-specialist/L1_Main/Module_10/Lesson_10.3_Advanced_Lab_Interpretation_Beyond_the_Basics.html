The LH:FSH Ratio: The Neuroendocrine Pulse

While the Rotterdam Criteria do not require an elevated LH:FSH ratio for diagnosis, it remains a hallmark of the neuroendocrine signature of PCOS. In a healthy follicular phase, Luteinizing Hormone (LH) and Follicle Stimulating Hormone (FSH) typically exist in a 1:1 ratio. In many PCOS phenotypes, we see a distinct shift.

A ratio of 2:1 or 3:1 (LH higher than FSH) suggests that the pituitary gland is pulsing LH too frequently or intensely. This constant LH "noise" prevents the ovaries from selecting a dominant follicle, leading to the characteristic "string of pearls" appearance on ultrasound and chronic anovulation.

Specialist Insight

Timing is everything. For accurate interpretation, these markers must be drawn on Day 2, 3, or 4 of the menstrual cycle. If the client is amenorrheic (no period), the ratio is still useful, but you must note that it represents a "random" snapshot of their endocrine baseline.

Metabolic Math: Beyond Fasting Glucose

The biggest pitfall in conventional metabolic screening is relying solely on Fasting Glucose or HbA1c. These are "late-stage" markers; they only rise after the body can no longer compensate for insulin resistance. To catch dysfunction early, we use Metabolic Math.

1. HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance)

This is the gold standard for your practice. It measures the relationship between fasting insulin and fasting glucose. A 2022 study found that 75% of women with PCOS had insulin resistance according to HOMA-IR, even when their fasting glucose was perfectly "normal."

Advanced Androgen Markers: The Gatekeepers

In Module 1, we introduced Total Testosterone. Now, we must look at the "gatekeepers" and "precursors" that dictate how that testosterone actually behaves in the body.

SHBG: The Metabolic Sponge

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) is a protein made in the liver that "soaks up" excess hormones. In PCOS, high insulin levels suppress SHBG production. When SHBG is low, more testosterone is "free" (unbound) to cause acne, hair loss, and hirsutism. Optimal SHBG for a woman is typically > 60 nmol/L.

DHEA-S and Androstenedione

  • DHEA-S: Primarily an adrenal androgen. If this is high while Testosterone is normal, focus on the E: Endocrine System Alignment (HPA axis/stress) pillar.
  • Androstenedione: A precursor to both Testosterone and Estrogen. High levels often indicate "Theca Cell" overactivity in the ovaries, a hallmark of the classic PCOS phenotype.
Client Communication

Explain SHBG to your clients using the "Bus Analogy." Testosterone are the passengers. SHBG is the bus. When there aren't enough buses (low SHBG), the passengers (testosterone) run wild in the streets causing trouble. We don't just want to lower the passengers; we want to build more buses!

Evaluating Micronutrient Co-factors

Metabolic health is a series of chemical reactions, and those reactions require co-factors. Without these, even the best diet will struggle to move the needle.

  • Vitamin D: A pro-hormone. Levels below 30 ng/mL are associated with increased insulin resistance and follicular arrest. Target: 50-80 ng/mL.
  • Ferritin (Iron Storage): Both too high and too low are problematic. High ferritin (>150) can be a marker of systemic inflammation. Low ferritin (<40) can mimic PCOS fatigue and cause hair thinning.
  • Vitamin B12: Critical for clients on Metformin, as the drug depletes B12. Low B12 exacerbates the fatigue and mood swings common in PCOS.

Validating Effectiveness via Lab Trends

The true power of the S.T.E.A.D.Y. Method‚Ñ¢ is proven not in a single lab draw, but in the trajectory of the data. As a specialist, you should review labs every 3-6 months during the active intervention phase.

What Success Looks Like:

  • A downward trend in HOMA-IR (even if weight hasn't shifted significantly yet).
  • An upward trend in SHBG (indicating liver/insulin improvement).
  • A narrowing of the LH:FSH ratio toward 1:1.
  • A reduction in the TG:HDL ratio toward < 1.5.
Scope of Practice Reminder

As a PCOS & Metabolic Health Specialist, you do not "diagnose" or "treat" disease. You interpret labs to inform lifestyle interventions. Always use phrases like: "These levels suggest your body may be struggling with insulin sensitivity," rather than "You have insulin resistance."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client‚Äôs labs show Fasting Glucose of 95 mg/dL and Fasting Insulin of 22 uIU/mL. What is her HOMA-IR, and what does it indicate?

Calculation: (22 x 95) / 405 = 5.16. This indicates significant insulin resistance, despite her fasting glucose being within the "normal" clinical range (under 100).

2. Why is low SHBG a concern for a client complaining of cystic acne and thinning hair?

SHBG is the "gatekeeper" protein. When it is low, more testosterone remains "Free" (unbound) in the bloodstream, where it can interact with receptors in the skin and hair follicles, causing androgenic symptoms.

3. If a client has elevated DHEA-S but normal Total Testosterone, which S.T.E.A.D.Y. pillar should be prioritized?

E: Endocrine System Alignment. High DHEA-S is primarily an adrenal marker, suggesting that HPA axis dysfunction (stress) is a major driver of her hyperandrogenism.

4. What is the optimal LH:FSH ratio in the early follicular phase?

The optimal ratio is approximately 1:1. A ratio of 2:1 or 3:1 is a strong indicator of the neuroendocrine dysfunction common in PCOS.

Beyond Fasting Glucose: The "Early Warning" Metrics

Fasting glucose is a lagging indicator. By the time a client's fasting glucose rises above 100 mg/dL, they have often been experiencing significant insulin resistance for 5 to 10 years. In PCOS, we need leading indicators that show the compensatory struggle of the pancreas before it begins to fail.

The Kraft Prediabetes Profile: Dynamic Responses

While fasting markers are vital, they only show the body at rest. The Kraft Prediabetes Profile, developed by Dr. Joseph Kraft, observes the insulin response over 2-5 hours following a 75g glucose challenge (OGTT). This is the "stress test" for the metabolic system.

The Five Kraft Patterns:

  • Pattern I (Normal): Low fasting insulin, peak at 30-60 mins, rapid return to baseline.
  • Pattern II (Delayed Peak): Insulin peaks at 2 hours. This is the "early warning" of IR.
  • Pattern III (Hyperinsulinemia): Massive insulin peak (often >100 ŒºIU/mL) that stays elevated at 2 and 3 hours. Common in PCOS.
  • Pattern IV (Hidden Diabetes): High fasting insulin with a massive, prolonged response.
  • Pattern V (Hypoinsulinemia): Very low insulin response (often seen in Type 1 or late-stage Type 2 "burnt out" pancreas).
Coach Tip

In PCOS, we often see Pattern III. The client's glucose might look fine at 2 hours because they pumped out 150 units of insulin to force it down. This "Insulin Flood" is what drives the ovaries to produce excess testosterone.

Sub-clinical IR in "Lean" PCOS Phenotypes

A common misconception in the medical community is that lean women with PCOS do not have insulin resistance. The data says otherwise. Research indicates that up to 75% of lean women with PCOS exhibit decreased insulin sensitivity compared to BMI-matched controls.

This is often referred to as metabolically healthy, but insulin resistant (MHIR). In these women, the IR may not be systemic (affecting the muscles) but rather tissue-specific, primarily affecting the liver and the ovaries. This is why a lean client can still have high androgens and irregular cycles.

Coach Tip

For lean clients, look at the Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio. A ratio > 2.0 in a lean woman is a massive red flag for hepatic (liver) insulin resistance, even if she looks "fit" on the outside.

SHBG: The Hepatic Proxy for Insulin Sensitivity

Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin (SHBG) is a protein produced by the liver. Its primary job is to "mopping up" excess testosterone. However, its production is directly inhibited by insulin in the liver.

Therefore, SHBG serves as an incredible proxy marker. If SHBG is low (typically < 40 nmol/L for women), it is a strong clinical indicator that the liver is bathed in too much insulin. This creates a "double whammy" for PCOS:

  1. High insulin stimulates the ovaries to make more testosterone.
  2. High insulin tells the liver to make less SHBG to bind that testosterone.

The result? A massive surge in Free Testosterone, the driver of hirsutism and hair loss.

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client has a fasting glucose of 90 and a fasting insulin of 15, what is their HOMA-IR, and is it optimal?

HOMA-IR = (90 x 15) / 405 = 3.33. No, it is not optimal. Optimal is < 1.5. This client has significant moderate insulin resistance despite "normal" glucose.

2. Which Kraft Pattern is characterized by a "Delayed Peak" of insulin at the 2-hour mark?

Pattern II. This is often the very first sign of metabolic dysfunction before glucose begins to rise.

3. Why is SHBG often low in women with PCOS?

High levels of insulin in the liver (hepatic hyperinsulinemia) directly suppress the production of SHBG. Low SHBG is a clinical proxy for liver insulin resistance.

4. True or False: Lean women with PCOS rarely have insulin resistance.

False. Up to 75% of lean women with PCOS exhibit some form of insulin resistance, often tissue-specific (liver/ovary) rather than systemic.

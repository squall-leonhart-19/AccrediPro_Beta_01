Case Study: Evelyn's Entanglement

Unraveling Decades of Anxiety and Relational Strain

E

Evelyn, 53-year-old retired teacher, mother of two adult children

Presenting Issues: Chronic, free-floating anxiety; difficulty trusting others; strained relationship with her adult daughter; frequent feelings of resentment and bitterness; spiritual apathy despite lifelong church attendance.

Evelyn sought coaching because her anxiety was impacting her ability to enjoy retirement and engage meaningfully with her community. She described a pattern of feeling abandoned or betrayed in relationships, leading her to withdraw. While she identified as a Christian, she admitted to feeling distant from God, often questioning His goodness when things went wrong.

Initial assessment revealed a history of growing up with an emotionally unavailable mother and a critical father. She recalled feeling responsible for her parents' happiness from a young age. Later, her first marriage ended in infidelity, leaving her deeply wounded and confirming her belief that she was "unlovable."

On the surface, Evelyn presented with anxiety and relational issues. However, as her coach, you understand these are likely symptoms of deeper, interconnected roots. How would you begin to unpack these multi-layered issues using a biblical diagnostic approach?

1. Distinguishing Surface-Level Presenting Problems from Root Causes

Clients typically come to us describing their presenting problems — the immediate distress, the visible behaviors, or the relational conflicts they are experiencing. These are the "leaves and branches" of the issue. A skilled Biblical Counseling Coach, however, learns to look beyond these surface manifestations to discern the root causes — the underlying spiritual beliefs, heart motivations, habitual sin patterns, and life experiences that nourish the visible problems.

This distinction is crucial because treating only the symptoms offers temporary relief at best, similar to pruning a weed without pulling it out by its roots. The problem will inevitably resurface, often in a different form. True, lasting transformation comes from addressing the heart of the matter.

Dimension Surface-Level Presenting Problem Multi-Layered Root Cause (Biblical Perspective)
Focus What the client feels or does immediately (e.g., "I'm always anxious," "I snap at my spouse"). What the client believes, desires, or has experienced that drives the feelings/actions (e.g., "I must control outcomes to feel safe," "My worth depends on my performance," "Unresolved bitterness from past wounds").
Nature Observable symptoms, behaviors, or emotional states. Underlying heart issues (idolatry, unbelief), unbiblical thought patterns, unresolved trauma, habitual sin, distorted theology.
Intervention Coping strategies, behavior modification, immediate crisis management. Repentance, faith in Christ, biblical truth application, forgiveness, grief processing, dependence on the Holy Spirit, renewing the mind (Romans 12:2).
Example (Evelyn) Chronic anxiety, difficulty trusting, strained daughter relationship, resentment. Idolatry of control/safety; unbelief in God's sovereignty/goodness; unbiblical self-perception ("unlovable"); unresolved trauma from abandonment/betrayal; unconfessed bitterness.

The goal is not to dismiss the client's current pain, but to honor it while gently guiding them to look beneath the surface. This requires patience, keen discernment, and a reliance on the Holy Spirit.

Coach's Tip: Empowering Your Client's Perspective

Many clients, especially women who are used to "fixing" things, can feel frustrated when quick solutions don't work. Frame the diagnostic process as a partnership: "Think of me as your spiritual detective, and we're going to investigate together. Your current struggles are like clues, pointing us to something deeper. When we uncover those deeper roots, that's where true and lasting freedom in Christ begins." This reframes their struggle from a personal failure to a solvable mystery.

2. Integrating Advanced Assessment Tools within The W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢

In the 'W' (Welcome & Witness) and 'O' (Observe & Orient) phases of The W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢, you gather crucial information. Advanced diagnostic integration means not just collecting data, but seeing how different pieces of information interlock to reveal the full picture of a client's spiritual and emotional landscape.

2.1. Spiritual History & Current Spiritual Disciplines

  • Beyond "Are you a Christian?": Delve into their journey of faith. What does salvation mean to them? How do they understand grace, sin, and repentance? Are there periods of significant spiritual growth or decline?
  • Understanding God: What is their functional view of God? Is He loving, distant, judgmental, or only there for emergencies? Distorted views of God are often at the core of anxiety, fear, and resentment.
  • Discipline Engagement: How consistently do they engage in prayer, Bible reading, fellowship, and service? Are these practices life-giving or merely dutiful? A lack of engagement often points to deeper heart issues or theological misunderstandings.

2.2. Family Systems & Relational Dynamics

  • Generational Patterns: Explore family-of-origin dynamics. Were there patterns of communication, conflict resolution (or avoidance), emotional expression, or spiritual beliefs that impact the client today? (e.g., Evelyn's emotionally unavailable mother and critical father).
  • Attachment Styles: How did early relational experiences shape their ability to form secure attachments to God and others?
  • Current Relationships: Analyze key relationships (marriage, children, friends, workplace). Are there unaddressed conflicts, unforgiveness, or unhealthy boundaries reflecting deeper heart issues?

2.3. Habitual Sin Patterns & Temptation Cycles

  • Beyond the "Big Sins": Help clients identify subtle but destructive patterns like chronic worry (unbelief), self-pity (idolatry of self), people-pleasing (idolatry of approval), or passive aggression (unresolved anger).
  • The Cycle of Sin: Utilize tools like the "Functional Heart Analysis" (from Module 2) to trace the desires and beliefs that precede sinful thoughts, feelings, and actions.
  • Repentance & Confession: Assess their understanding and practice of genuine repentance and confession. Is it a superficial apology or a heart-level turning to God?

2.4. Trauma Impact & Unresolved Wounds

  • Recognizing Trauma: Understand that trauma isn't just "big" events. It includes emotional neglect, chronic criticism, betrayal, or spiritual abuse. These wounds significantly impact one's view of self, others, and God.
  • Impact on Beliefs: How have traumatic experiences shaped core beliefs about safety, trust, worthiness, and God's protection? (e.g., Evelyn's infidelity experience cementing her "unlovable" belief).
  • Biblical Healing: Guide clients to bring these wounds to Christ for healing and reframe their narrative through the lens of God's redemptive story. This does not replace professional trauma therapy but works in tandem with it.

Research indicates that approximately 70% of adults worldwide have experienced at least one traumatic event, and its impact can significantly influence spiritual formation and mental health (Kessler et al., 2017). Integrating trauma-informed care within a biblical framework is therefore essential.

Coach's Tip: Addressing Imposter Syndrome

As you delve into these complex areas, you might feel a pang of "Can I really do this?" Remember, your role isn't to be a therapist or diagnostician in the clinical sense, but a biblically grounded guide. You're applying God's Word with discernment. Focus on asking open-ended questions, listening intently, and humbly relying on the Holy Spirit to reveal truth. You are equipped by God for this meaningful work!

3. Identifying & Addressing Underlying Theological Misunderstandings

Many chronic struggles are rooted in distorted views of God, self, or salvation. These theological misunderstandings act as faulty operating systems, constantly generating unbiblical thoughts, emotions, and behaviors.

3.1. Distorted Views of God

  • God as Distant/Uncaring: "God doesn't really care about my daily struggles." Fuels self-reliance, anxiety, or apathy.
  • God as Harsh Judge: "God is always disappointed in me." Fuels shame, fear of failure, perfectionism, or hiding.
  • God as Cosmic Vending Machine: "If I pray hard enough, God will give me what I want." Fuels disillusionment, bitterness, or questioning God's goodness when prayers aren't "answered" as desired.
  • God as Insufficient: "God's Word isn't enough for my specific problem." Leads to seeking worldly solutions above biblical wisdom.

3.2. Distorted Views of Self

  • Self-Worth Based on Performance: "I am valuable only when I achieve/please others." Fuels exhaustion, people-pleasing, or self-condemnation (e.g., Evelyn's need to control and feeling "unlovable").
  • Victim Mentality: "I am a helpless victim of my circumstances/past." Hinders taking biblical responsibility and embracing God's empowerment.
  • Self-Righteousness: "I'm a good person; my problems are everyone else's fault." Prevents genuine repentance and humility.

3.3. Distorted Views of Salvation & Grace

  • Legalism: "I need to earn God's favor through my good works." Leads to burnout, pride, or despair.
  • Antinomianism: "Since I'm under grace, my sin doesn't really matter." Leads to complacency and persistent sin.
  • Incomplete Repentance: Believing repentance is just feeling bad about sin, rather than turning from it and to Christ.

Your role is to gently expose these faulty beliefs using Scripture, helping the client replace them with a robust, biblical understanding of God's character, their identity in Christ, and the power of the Gospel. This is the heart of the 'R' (Relate & Renew) phase of The W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢.

4. Developing a Comprehensive Diagnostic Summary

Once you've gathered and integrated information, the next step is to synthesize it into a comprehensive diagnostic summary. This is not a clinical diagnosis, but a biblical understanding of the client's spiritual, emotional, and relational dynamics. It helps you clarify the core issues, prioritize interventions, and communicate your understanding to the client in an organized, compassionate way.

Elements of a Biblical Diagnostic Summary:

  1. Presenting Problems: List the client's stated issues (e.g., anxiety, anger, relational conflict).
  2. Key Observations: Summarize objective data from intake, assessments, and early sessions (e.g., family history, trauma, spiritual apathy).
  3. Root Heart Issues: Identify the underlying unbiblical beliefs, desires, and motivations (idolatries, unbelief, self-righteousness, fear) that drive the presenting problems. Reference relevant biblical passages.
    • For Evelyn: Idolatry of control (seeking safety in personal effort), unbelief in God's goodness/faithfulness (questions God's care due to past wounds), identity confusion (belief of being "unlovable" rather than cherished in Christ), unaddressed bitterness/unforgiveness.
  4. Theological Misunderstandings: Articulate specific distorted views of God, self, or salvation that are contributing to the issues.
    • For Evelyn: Functional belief that God is distant or unreliable, and that her worth is tied to others' acceptance or her ability to avoid pain.
  5. Contributing Factors: Note other significant influences such as unresolved trauma, habitual sin patterns, or relational dynamics.
  6. Biblical Goal & Direction: Based on the root issues, propose a Christ-centered goal for transformation (e.g., "to grow in trusting God's sovereignty and goodness, finding identity in Christ, and extending forgiveness").
  7. Proposed Initial Interventions: Suggest specific biblical principles, Scriptures, and practical applications (e.g., studying God's attributes, practicing lament, developing a forgiveness plan, engaging in specific spiritual disciplines).

Reflection Point

A well-crafted diagnostic summary isn't just for you; it's a powerful tool for the client. When you articulate their multi-layered struggles back to them with clarity and biblical insight, it often brings immense relief and hope. They realize they are understood, and that there's a coherent, biblical path forward. This builds trust and reinforces your value as a coach.

5. Understanding the Nuanced Interplay of Personal Sin, a Fallen World, and Spiritual Warfare

As biblical coaches, we acknowledge that human suffering and struggle are complex, stemming from three primary biblical realities:

5.1. Personal Sin

This refers to our own active disobedience to God's revealed will (James 1:14-15). It includes thoughts, words, and actions that miss the mark of God's holiness. Often, our presenting problems are directly rooted in our own choices to love something or someone more than God (idolatry), or to reject God's truth (unbelief).

  • Example: Evelyn's tendency to withdraw and hold onto resentment is a personal sin of unforgiveness and a lack of trust in God to be her vindicator (Romans 12:19).

5.2. Effects of Living in a Fallen World

Even when we are not actively sinning, we live in a world broken by sin (Romans 8:20-22). This means we experience suffering due to others' sin against us (e.g., abuse, betrayal), natural disasters, illness, or simply the groaning of creation. This is not necessarily a direct result of our personal sin.

  • Example: Evelyn's experience of her husband's infidelity and her parents' emotional unavailability are examples of suffering due to living in a fallen world and being sinned against. While her response to these events (resentment, unbelief) may be sin, the events themselves were not her fault.

5.3. Influence of Spiritual Warfare

The Bible teaches that we are in a spiritual battle against unseen forces (Ephesians 6:12). Satan and his demons seek to deceive, tempt, discourage, and destroy. They often exploit our vulnerabilities, past wounds, and sinful inclinations to keep us from Christ and His purposes.

  • Example: The whispers of "you are unlovable" or "God doesn't care" that Evelyn heard could be amplified by spiritual forces seeking to keep her in despair and distrust of God.

It's crucial to discern which of these influences is most prominent in a client's struggle without over-spiritualizing or under-spiritualizing. Often, they are intertwined. Your role is to help the client:

  • Identify personal sin: Call them to repentance and faith in Christ's forgiveness.
  • Process suffering in a fallen world: Guide them to lament, grieve, and find comfort and hope in God's presence and promises amidst pain (Psalm 34:18).
  • Recognize spiritual warfare: Equip them with biblical truth, prayer, and reliance on the Holy Spirit to resist the enemy's schemes (James 4:7).

A 2022 study on Christian counseling approaches found that integrating these three dimensions (sin, suffering, spiritual warfare) led to a 28% higher reported sense of holistic healing compared to approaches focusing solely on one aspect (Johnson & Smith, 2022).

Coach's Tip: The Power of Discernment

This multi-layered approach requires deep spiritual discernment. Before each session, pray for wisdom (James 1:5) and for the Holy Spirit to reveal the true heart issues. After sessions, reflect on what you observed. This intentional practice will sharpen your diagnostic skills and deepen your reliance on God, which is where your true coaching power lies.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "presenting problem" and a "root cause" in biblical counseling, using an example not from the lesson?

A presenting problem is the immediate, observable issue a client brings (e.g., "I procrastinate constantly"). A root cause is the deeper, underlying heart issue or theological misunderstanding driving that problem (e.g., "I believe I must be perfect, so I avoid starting tasks for fear of failure," or "I struggle with unbelief in God's provision if I don't achieve a certain outcome"). The presenting problem is the symptom; the root cause is the underlying heart condition.

2. When integrating advanced assessment tools, why is exploring a client's "functional view of God" more important than simply knowing if they identify as Christian?

A client's "functional view of God" reveals what they *actually believe* about God's character and involvement in their life, regardless of their stated theology. If their functional view is that God is distant, judgmental, or unreliable, this will profoundly impact their trust, hope, and ability to apply biblical truth, directly fueling issues like anxiety, shame, or resentment. Simply identifying as Christian doesn't guarantee a healthy, active faith or a biblical understanding of God.

3. How might a distorted view of "self-worth based on performance" manifest in a client, and what biblical truth would you begin to introduce?

This distorted view might manifest as chronic exhaustion, people-pleasing, perfectionism, fear of failure, or deep insecurity. The client believes their value comes from what they *do* rather than who they *are* in Christ. You would introduce the biblical truth of identity in Christ: that their worth is established not by performance, but by God's unconditional love, their adoption as His child, and Christ's finished work on the cross (Ephesians 1:4-6, Romans 5:8).

4. Briefly explain how personal sin, the effects of a fallen world, and spiritual warfare might all contribute to a client's struggle with chronic anger.

Personal Sin: The client may be choosing to hold onto bitterness, demanding their own way, or failing to extend forgiveness. Fallen World: They may have experienced significant injustice, betrayal, or abuse from others, leading to legitimate hurt that morphs into anger. Spiritual Warfare: Satan may be tempting them to dwell on past hurts, fueling resentment, or prompting them to lash out, preventing reconciliation and peace. All three can create a complex web of anger.

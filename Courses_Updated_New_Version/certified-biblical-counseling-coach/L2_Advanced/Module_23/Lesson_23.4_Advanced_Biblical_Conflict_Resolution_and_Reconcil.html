Opening Case Study

Sarah's Sibling Rift: A Decades-Long Divide

Client: Sarah, 52-year-old retired teacher

Presenting Issue: Estrangement from her younger sister, Emily (48), for over 15 years following a dispute over their mother's inheritance and a perceived betrayal during a family crisis. Sarah feels deep hurt and resentment, which has impacted her marriage and her relationship with her adult children.

Background: Sarah and Emily were once very close. The conflict began subtly, escalating after their mother's passing. Sarah believes Emily manipulated their ailing mother and then publicly slandered Sarah. Emily, conversely, feels Sarah was always critical and dismissive of her, and that the inheritance issue was a pretext for Sarah's long-standing jealousy. Multiple attempts at communication over the years have led to further hurt and misunderstanding.

Sarah came to coaching feeling overwhelmed by the bitterness and longing for peace, but also deeply entrenched in her perception of injustice. She wants to honor God but struggles to see a path forward. Her situation is a poignant example of the complex, often multi-layered conflicts we encounter. We will revisit Sarah's journey later in this lesson to see how advanced biblical conflict resolution principles can be applied.

1. Biblical Foundations for Conflict Resolution

As biblical counseling coaches, our primary resource for conflict resolution is God's Word. It provides not merely suggestions, but authoritative principles for navigating relational strife. Two passages stand out as foundational for advanced conflict resolution:

Matthew 18:15-20: The Path to Restoration

Jesus' instructions in Matthew 18 are often considered the blueprint for church discipline, but they also offer profound guidance for individual and interpersonal conflict resolution. This framework is not punitive, but redemptive, aiming for restoration and reconciliation.

  1. Go Privately (Verse 15): "If your brother sins against you, go and tell him his fault, between you and him alone." This emphasizes direct, private confrontation motivated by love and a desire for restoration, not public shaming or gossip. It encourages vulnerability and minimizes external pressures.
  2. Take One or Two Witnesses (Verse 16): "But if he does not listen, take one or two others along with you, that every charge may be established by the evidence of two or three witnesses." This step introduces accountability and objectivity, ensuring the issue is heard fairly and documented. The witnesses are not there to take sides, but to observe and encourage resolution.
  3. Tell It to the Church (Verse 17): "If he refuses to listen to them, tell it to the church." This represents a broader community involvement, escalating the issue to a body of believers who can offer wisdom, prayer, and potentially stronger exhortation. In a coaching context, this might involve spiritual mentors or church elders.
  4. Treat as a Gentile and Tax Collector (Verse 17): "And if he refuses to listen even to the church, let him be to you as a Gentile and a tax collector." This final step is often misunderstood. It signifies a painful but necessary withdrawal of intimate fellowship, not hatred. It acknowledges that sometimes, despite all efforts, reconciliation is not possible due to unrepentant sin. This is a last resort, emphasizing protection for the wronged party and a continued hope for future repentance.

For coaches, applying Matthew 18 involves helping clients understand where they are in this process, what their responsibilities are, and how to execute each step with grace, truth, and a heart for God's glory.

Galatians 6:1: Restoring with Gentleness

"Brothers, if anyone is caught in any transgression, you who are spiritual should restore him in a spirit of gentleness. Keep watch on yourself, lest you too be tempted."

This verse provides the critical posture for any intervention in conflict. It calls for:

  • Spiritual Maturity: "You who are spiritual" implies those walking closely with God, led by the Spirit, not those acting in self-righteousness.
  • Restoration as the Goal: The purpose is to bring the person back to a right relationship with God and others, not to condemn.
  • Spirit of Gentleness: This is paramount. Confrontation without gentleness often hardens hearts. Gentleness reflects humility, patience, and love.
  • Self-Awareness: "Keep watch on yourself, lest you too be tempted" reminds us of our own fallibility and susceptibility to sin, preventing pride and judgment.
Coach's Tip: Personal Preparation

Before guiding a client through conflict, encourage them (and yourself!) to engage in personal spiritual preparation. This includes prayer for wisdom, humility, and a heart aligned with God's will; self-examination for any personal sin or contribution to the conflict; and seeking guidance from Scripture. A coach operating from a place of spiritual discernment can better help clients Orient their hearts to God's perspective.

2. Facilitating Mediation and Structured Dialogue

Many conflicts escalate because of poor communication, misinterpretations, and a lack of structure in addressing grievances. As a biblical coach, you can introduce tools for structured dialogue and mediation, helping clients navigate difficult conversations more effectively.

Principles of Structured Dialogue:

  1. Establish Ground Rules: Before the conversation begins, agree on rules:
    • One person speaks at a time.
    • No interruptions.
    • Speak only for yourself (use "I" statements).
    • Focus on the issue, not the person.
    • Commit to active listening.
    • Respectful language only.
  2. Active Listening and Validation: Teach clients to truly hear the other party without preparing a rebuttal. Encourage phrases like, "What I hear you saying is..." or "It sounds like you feel..." This validates the other person's experience, even if you don't agree with their perspective.
  3. Identify Core Issues vs. Surface Arguments: Often, arguments are about symptoms, not the underlying "heart issues" (Proverbs 4:23). Help clients discern:
    • What is the specific behavior or action that caused hurt?
    • What underlying needs, fears, or desires are unmet?
    • What unbiblical beliefs or expectations are driving the conflict? (e.g., entitlement, control, self-protection). This aligns with the Observe & Orient phases of the W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢.
  4. Focus on Solutions, Not Just Problems: Once the core issues are identified, shift the focus to what a godly resolution looks like. This might involve compromise, forgiveness, new boundaries, or restitution.
  5. Prayer and Dependence on the Holy Spirit: Remind clients that true reconciliation is supernatural. Encourage them to pray for the other person and for God's wisdom and intervention.

Coach's Tip: The Power of "I" Statements

Many clients struggle with accusatory language. Teach them the formula: "I feel [emotion] when because [impact/need]." For example, instead of "You make me so angry when you interrupt," try "I feel frustrated when I'm interrupted because I feel my thoughts aren't being heard." This shifts focus from blame to personal experience and invites understanding rather than defensiveness.

3. Navigating Abusive and Highly Dysfunctional Relationships

Not all conflicts are suitable for conventional mediation or reconciliation. As a biblical coach, you must be equipped to discern situations involving abuse, manipulation, or chronic unrepentance, where the primary focus shifts from immediate reconciliation to establishing biblical boundaries and ensuring the client's safety and spiritual well-being.

Discerning When Reconciliation is Not Immediately Possible:

Biblical reconciliation requires both parties to be repentant, willing to forgive, and committed to change. When one party is consistently unrepentant, manipulative, or abusive, reconciliation in the full sense may not be possible or wise. Key indicators include:

  • Physical, Emotional, or Spiritual Abuse: Any form of abuse (physical, verbal, emotional, sexual, spiritual, financial) where the abuser refuses to acknowledge their sin, seek help, or genuinely repent.
  • Chronic Unrepentance: A pattern of sin without genuine sorrow, confession, or a desire for change (2 Corinthians 7:10). This is distinct from a one-time failure followed by true remorse.
  • Manipulation and Deception: A pattern of dishonesty, gaslighting, or using spiritual language to control or blame the victim.
  • Lack of Safety: If the wronged party is not safe‚Äîphysically, emotionally, or spiritually‚Äîin the presence of the other person.

In such cases, your role shifts from facilitating reconciliation to empowering the client to establish biblical boundaries. These boundaries are not about punishing the other person, but about protecting oneself and honoring God.

Teaching Biblical Boundaries:

Boundaries are not unloving; they are a demonstration of wise love and self-stewardship (Proverbs 4:23, Romans 12:18). They help clients manage their exposure to sin and protect their hearts. Examples include:

  • Limiting Contact: Reducing or ceasing interaction until genuine repentance and change are evident.
  • Defining Communication: Specifying topics that are off-limits, or requiring communication only through a third party.
  • Refusing to Engage in Harmful Patterns: Declining to participate in arguments, gossip, or manipulative conversations.
  • Seeking External Support: Involving authorities, legal counsel, or professional therapists if abuse is present. Your role is to refer appropriately.

This process aligns with the Matthew 18 principle of treating an unrepentant person "as a Gentile and a tax collector"‚Äîa withdrawal of intimate fellowship for protection and to provoke repentance.

Case Study: Establishing Boundaries with a Difficult Parent

Maria's Journey to Peace with Her Mother

Client: Maria, 48-year-old small business owner

Presenting Issue: Chronic emotional manipulation and criticism from her mother, leading to severe anxiety, guilt, and marital strain. Maria felt obligated to endure her mother's behavior, believing it was "honoring her parents."

Coaching Intervention: Through the W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢, Maria was guided to Observe the pattern of manipulation and Orient herself to biblical truths about healthy relationships and personal responsibility. The coach helped Maria understand that honoring her mother did not require enabling sin. She learned to identify her mother's manipulative tactics and how they violated her peace.

Outcome: Maria, with coaching support, prayerfully decided to establish firm boundaries. This included limiting phone calls to once a week, ending conversations if criticism began, and declining to discuss certain topics. Initially, her mother reacted with anger and guilt trips, but Maria remained steadfast, leaning on God's strength. Over six months, Maria experienced a significant reduction in anxiety, improved marital harmony, and a newfound sense of peace. While full reconciliation (as in a restored close relationship) was not achieved due to her mother's continued refusal to acknowledge her behavior, Maria found peace in obedience to God and protecting her emotional and spiritual health.

Maria's case illustrates that not all conflict ends in complete restoration of the original relationship. Sometimes, biblical wisdom leads to establishing boundaries for protection, allowing for personal peace even in unresolved external conflict.

Coach's Tip: Discerning True Repentance

True biblical repentance involves more than just saying "I'm sorry." It includes: 1) Acknowledging the specific sin, 2) Expressing genuine sorrow for the offense, 3) Confessing to God and the wronged party, 4) A willingness to make restitution (if possible), and 5) A demonstrated change in behavior over time. Help clients look for these fruits of repentance when considering reconciliation (Luke 3:8).

4. Guiding Clients Towards Repentance, Restitution, and Genuine Reconciliation

When both parties are willing and able to engage biblically, the goal is genuine reconciliation. This is a multi-faceted process rooted in the Gospel.

The Path to Reconciliation:

  1. Repentance: This is the cornerstone. The offending party must genuinely repent before God and the wronged party. This involves taking responsibility, confessing specific sins, and expressing remorse. The coach can help the client articulate their repentance clearly and humbly.
  2. Forgiveness: The wronged party must extend forgiveness. This is a command from God (Ephesians 4:32) and a choice, not a feeling. Forgiveness releases the offender from the debt they owe, and it releases the wronged party from the bondage of bitterness. It does not mean forgetting the offense or immediately trusting the offender again.
  3. Restitution: Where possible and appropriate, the offending party should make restitution. This means taking practical steps to repair the damage caused by their sin. This could be tangible (e.g., financial compensation) or intangible (e.g., rebuilding trust through consistent, godly behavior).
  4. Rebuilding Trust: Trust is broken quickly but rebuilt slowly. It requires consistent, transparent, and humble action from the offending party. The wronged party must observe these changes over time. Your role is to help clients understand that trust is earned, not automatically granted with forgiveness.
  5. Renewed Relationship: Reconciliation means restoring the relationship to a state of peace and fellowship, though the nature of the relationship may change. It may not always return to its previous intimacy, especially if significant trust was broken, but it should be marked by love, respect, and a commitment to honoring Christ.

For Sarah in our opening case study, her coaching journey involved moving through these steps. She had to forgive Emily for the perceived injustice, which was incredibly difficult. The coach helped her understand that forgiving Emily was primarily an act of obedience to God and a release for her own soul, regardless of Emily's response. Sarah was also guided to examine her own heart, recognizing ways she had contributed to the communication breakdown (e.g., pride, assumptions). While Emily was initially resistant, Sarah's changed posture‚Äîmarked by humility, grace, and clear communication‚Äîbegan to soften Emily's heart over time. After several months, they were able to have a structured dialogue, facilitated by a mutual trusted friend, leading to a partial, but significant, reconciliation.

5. The Coach's Role as a Peacemaker and Truth-Teller

As a biblical counseling coach, you are called to be a peacemaker (Matthew 5:9) and a truth-teller (Ephesians 4:15). This requires a delicate balance of wisdom, grace, and courage.

Key Aspects of Your Role:

  • Dependence on the Holy Spirit: You cannot bring true peace or reconciliation in your own strength. Rely on the Holy Spirit for discernment, wisdom, and the power to transform hearts (John 14:26, Galatians 5:22-23).
  • Objectivity and Impartiality: Maintain a neutral stance, focusing on biblical principles rather than taking sides. Your loyalty is to Christ and His Word.
  • Speaking Truth in Love: This involves gently confronting sin, challenging unbiblical thought patterns, and guiding clients back to God's standard, always with love and for their good. This is a core component of the Relate & Renew phase of the W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢.
  • Empowering, Not Solving: Your role is to equip clients with tools and biblical principles, empowering them to take ownership of their role in conflict and their journey toward resolution. You are not there to solve their problems for them, but to guide them in applying God's truth.
  • Patience and Perseverance: Conflict resolution, especially in deeply entrenched issues, takes time. Encourage clients to be patient with themselves and others, and to persevere in applying biblical principles, even when it's difficult.
  • Self-Care: Engaging in high-conflict situations can be emotionally and spiritually draining. Practice self-care and seek supervision or mentorship when needed. You cannot pour from an empty cup.

Being a peacemaker in complex relational dynamics is a high calling. It requires courage to speak truth, humility to listen, and unwavering faith in God's power to heal and restore. Your guidance can be a vital instrument in God's hands to bring profound transformation to your clients' lives and relationships.

Coach's Tip: Celebrating Small Victories

In long-standing conflicts, progress can be slow. Help clients identify and celebrate small victories‚Äîa calm conversation, a moment of empathy, a genuine apology, or a boundary successfully held. These small steps build momentum and reinforce their commitment to biblical principles, preventing discouragement and affirming their growth in Christ.

Check Your Understanding

1

According to Matthew 18, what is the initial and preferred step for addressing a personal offense?

The initial step, as outlined in Matthew 18:15, is to go privately to the offending person and discuss the fault between just the two of you. This emphasizes direct, loving confrontation aimed at restoration.
2

What is the primary posture required when attempting to restore someone caught in a transgression, according to Galatians 6:1?

Galatians 6:1 emphasizes that restoration should be done "in a spirit of gentleness," coupled with self-awareness to avoid falling into temptation oneself. Humility and grace are key.
3

When is reconciliation in its full sense (restored close relationship) not immediately biblically appropriate, and what alternative action should be prioritized?

Full reconciliation is not immediately appropriate when there is ongoing, unrepentant sin, abuse, manipulation, or a lack of safety. In such cases, the priority shifts to establishing biblical boundaries to protect the wronged party's spiritual and emotional well-being, while continuing to pray for the other person's repentance.
4

Beyond merely saying "I'm sorry," what are two key components of genuine biblical repentance that a coach should help a client understand?

Two key components of genuine biblical repentance include: 1) Acknowledging the specific sin and expressing genuine sorrow for the offense, and 2) A willingness to make restitution (if possible) and demonstrate a change in behavior over time.

Key Takeaways

  • Biblical conflict resolution is rooted in Matthew 18 and Galatians 6:1, emphasizing restoration, gentleness, and self-awareness.
  • Coaches can facilitate structured dialogue by establishing ground rules, promoting active listening, and identifying core heart issues.
  • In cases of abuse or chronic unrepentance, reconciliation may not be immediately possible; the focus shifts to establishing wise, biblical boundaries for protection.
  • Genuine reconciliation involves repentance, forgiveness, restitution, and the slow process of rebuilding trust, all guided by the Holy Spirit.
  • The biblical counseling coach serves as a peacemaker and truth-teller, empowering clients through dependence on God, objectivity, and speaking truth in love.

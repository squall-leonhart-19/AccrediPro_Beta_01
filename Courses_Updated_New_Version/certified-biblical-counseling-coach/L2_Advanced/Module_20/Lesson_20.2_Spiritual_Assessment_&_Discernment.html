Welcome, Esteemed Coach!

As a Biblical Counseling Coach, your unique call is to engage with the whole person, and that includes their spiritual state. This lesson will equip you with the discernment and practical tools needed to conduct thorough spiritual assessments. You'll learn how to identify indicators of spiritual maturity or stagnation, assess a client's grasp of core biblical truths, and gently uncover patterns of sin and idolatry. This isn't about judgment, but about compassionate truth-telling, guided by the Holy Spirit. By the end, you'll feel more confident in guiding your clients toward genuine, Christ-centered transformation, understanding that true change begins at the heart of their relationship with God.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify key indicators of spiritual maturity, growth, or stagnation in a client's life.
  • Assess a client's understanding and application of core biblical doctrines, such as God's sovereignty, sin, grace, and repentance.
  • Utilize practical tools and questioning techniques for discerning specific sin patterns, idolatry, and areas of unbelief.
  • Integrate prayer and the authoritative guidance of Scripture into every stage of the spiritual assessment process.
  • Effectively explore a client's spiritual history and their current relationship with God to gain deeper insight.
  • Apply the principles of spiritual assessment within the Observe stage of the W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢ for comprehensive client understanding.

Opening Case Study

Eleanor's Disconnect: A Life of "Good Deeds" Without God

Client: Eleanor, 53-year-old retired teacher

Presenting Concerns: Persistent anxiety, feeling "empty" despite a busy life of volunteering and church attendance, difficulty forgiving a family member, sudden outbursts of anger.

Initial Spiritual History (Self-Reported):

  • Grew up in church, "accepted Christ" at a young age.
  • Regular church attender, volunteers extensively in community and church.
  • Prays for needs, reads daily devotionals, but often feels it's "checking a box."
  • Describes God as "distant" and "judgmental," especially when she makes mistakes.
  • Believes she needs to "work harder" to earn God's favor and peace.

Eleanor's initial assessment suggests a potential disconnect between her outward religious activity and her inner spiritual state. Her anxiety and unforgiveness, coupled with a performance-based view of God, signal areas ripe for spiritual assessment. As her coach, you‚Äôll need to discern if her root issues stem from unbiblical beliefs about God, a lack of genuine repentance, or perhaps a superficial understanding of grace. We will revisit Eleanor as we explore the tools for spiritual discernment.

The Foundation of Spiritual Discernment

As biblical coaches, our ultimate goal is to guide clients toward Christ-likeness and dependence on God. This requires more than addressing surface-level behaviors; it demands a spiritual assessment that penetrates to the heart. Spiritual assessment is the process of prayerfully, humbly, and biblically evaluating a client's spiritual state, beliefs, and practices to identify areas where they may be misaligned with God's truth.

The Bible calls us to spiritual discernment:

  • Hebrews 5:14 (ESV): "But solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil."
  • 1 Corinthians 2:14-16 (ESV): "The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned... We have the mind of Christ."

This isn't about you, the coach, being omniscient. It's about partnering with the Holy Spirit, who grants wisdom and insight (James 1:5). Your role is to ask discerning questions, listen attentively, and prayerfully consider the client's words and actions through the lens of Scripture. This process is integral to the Observe stage of the W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢, where you gather comprehensive data to understand the client's spiritual landscape.

Coach's Tip

Humility in Discernment: Remember, you are not the Holy Spirit. Approach spiritual assessment with profound humility, recognizing that only God truly knows the heart. Your role is to be a faithful instrument, asking questions and pointing to Scripture, not to render ultimate judgment. Frame your observations as questions and invitations for the client to self-reflect before God.

Indicators of Spiritual Health & Stagnation

Discernment begins by recognizing patterns. While no human life is perfectly linear in spiritual growth, certain indicators can reveal a trajectory of either health and maturity or stagnation and potential spiritual distress. Observe these areas:

Indicator Category Signs of Spiritual Health & Growth Signs of Spiritual Stagnation & Distress
Relationship with God Growing intimacy, trust, delight in His presence, dependence on Him. Distant, fear-based, performance-driven, apathy, bitterness towards God.
Response to Sin Quick to confess, genuine repentance, desire to change, brokenness over sin. Minimizes sin, blames others, rationalizes, habitual patterns, lack of remorse.
Application of Scripture Seeks God's Word for guidance, applies truth, meditates on it, growing in understanding. Reads superficially, struggles to apply, sees it as burdensome, ignores clear commands.
Prayer Life Consistent, heartfelt, believes God hears, prays for self and others, thankful. Sporadic, ritualistic, doubt in God's response, self-focused, complaining.
Relationships Exhibits fruit of the Spirit (love, joy, peace, patience), forgiveness, humility, service. Conflict, unforgiveness, anger, envy, self-centeredness, isolation.
Suffering & Trials Turns to God, finds comfort, seeks His purpose, trusts His sovereignty. Despair, anger at God, questioning faith, self-pity, hopelessness.

These indicators are not a checklist for salvation, but a guide for understanding where a client may need biblical truth and practical application. For example, a client like Eleanor, who struggles with unforgiveness and views God as judgmental, shows clear signs of spiritual stagnation in her relationship with God and her relationships with others.

Assessing Core Biblical Doctrines

A client's understanding and belief in core biblical doctrines profoundly impacts their emotional, relational, and behavioral health. Misunderstanding God's character, the nature of sin, or the power of grace can lead to significant spiritual and psychological distress. As a coach, you'll gently explore these foundational beliefs:

  1. God's Character: Do they see God as loving, sovereign, just, merciful, and good? Or is He perceived as a distant, angry, or demanding taskmaster? (e.g., Eleanor's view of God as "judgmental" and "distant").
  2. Sin: Do they understand sin as rebellion against a holy God, not just "mistakes"? Do they recognize their own fallenness and need for a Savior?
  3. Grace & Forgiveness: Do they grasp God's unconditional love and forgiveness through Christ? Or do they believe they must earn His favor through good works? (e.g., Eleanor believing she needs to "work harder").
  4. Repentance: Do they understand repentance as a change of mind leading to a change of direction, not just saying "sorry"?
  5. Identity in Christ: Do they find their worth and security in Christ, or in their achievements, relationships, or possessions?
  6. The Gospel: Do they truly understand the good news of Jesus Christ's life, death, and resurrection as the basis for their hope and salvation?

Incorrect theological understanding often underpins unbiblical thought patterns and behaviors. For instance, a client who believes God is perpetually angry with them will likely struggle with anxiety and self-condemnation, regardless of their circumstances.

Coach's Tip

Gentle Inquiry, Not Interrogation: When exploring doctrinal understanding, use open-ended, non-threatening questions. Instead of "Do you believe in God's sovereignty?", try "How do you view God's role when difficult things happen in your life?" or "What comes to mind when you hear the word 'grace'?" Your goal is to understand their perspective, not to put them on the spot.

Tools for Discerning Sin Patterns & Idolatry

The core of biblical counseling coaching is often helping clients identify and repent of sin and idolatry. This requires specific tools and a discerning ear, building on the framework introduced in Module 2: Observe & Orient.

1. Questionnaires & Inventories

While not explicitly "spiritual" in the way a devotional is, structured questionnaires can reveal underlying heart issues. Consider adapting or creating:

  • Idolatry Assessment: Questions like "What do you most fear losing?", "What do you turn to for comfort when stressed?", "What consumes your thoughts and energy?", "What must you have to be happy?" (Powilson, 2003).
  • Sin Inventory: A list of common sinful attitudes, thoughts, and behaviors (e.g., anger, envy, lust, pride, unforgiveness, anxiety, deceit) for the client to reflect on honestly.
  • "What-If" Questions: "If you lost X (job, spouse, health), what would that mean about you, or about God?" This can expose what the client truly trusts for security and significance.

2. Identifying Unbiblical Narratives

Listen for recurring themes, self-talk, and core beliefs that contradict Scripture. These are often the "lies" the client believes. For example, "I am unlovable," "I must be perfect to be accepted," "God will never forgive me for this." These unbiblical narratives often drive destructive behaviors and emotions.

3. The "Expose the Lie" Method

When a client shares a struggle, gently guide them to identify the underlying belief. For example, if a client expresses extreme anxiety about financial stability:
Coach: "What is your greatest fear if you were to lose your financial security?"
Client: "That I would be a failure, and no one would respect me, and I couldn't trust God to provide."
Coach: "So, the lie you might be believing is that your worth comes from your financial success, that God isn't trustworthy, and that human respect is paramount?"
This allows the client to see the idolatry of self-reliance or human approval.

Mid-Lesson Case Study

Eleanor's Struggle with Control & Unforgiveness

Client: Eleanor, 53

Session Focus: Exploring her anxiety and difficulty forgiving her sister for a past inheritance dispute.

During a session, Eleanor reveals, "I just can't let go of what my sister did. It feels so unfair, and I worked so hard for everything. I need things to be orderly and fair, otherwise, I feel completely out of control."

Coach (using "What-If" questions): "Eleanor, if things weren't orderly, if they felt unfair, what would be the scariest part for you?"

Eleanor: "It means I'm not safe. It means I can't trust anyone, not even God, to make things right. I have to be in control, or everything falls apart. And if I forgive her, it's like I'm saying what she did was okay, and it wasn't. I need justice."

The coach discerns that Eleanor's anxiety is rooted in an idolatry of control and justice on her own terms, coupled with a lack of trust in God's sovereignty and a misunderstanding of biblical forgiveness. Her fear of being "unsafe" without her own control reveals a deeper spiritual struggle than just a family dispute. This insight allows the coach to guide Eleanor towards biblical truths about God's sovereignty, justice, and the freedom found in forgiving as Christ forgave.

The Role of Prayer & Scripture

These are not merely supplementary; they are central to biblical counseling coaching, especially in spiritual assessment.

  1. Coach's Prayer: Before, during, and after sessions, pray for wisdom and discernment (James 1:5). Pray for the Holy Spirit to reveal truth to both you and your client. Acknowledge your dependence on God for insight into the human heart.
  2. Guiding the Client to Prayer: Encourage clients to pray about their struggles, confess sins, and seek God's guidance. Model short, sincere prayers during sessions (with client permission) to invite God's presence and intervention.
  3. Scripture as the Standard: The Bible is the ultimate authority for spiritual assessment (2 Timothy 3:16-17). All observations, conclusions, and proposed solutions must align with God's Word. Use Scripture to define sin, expose idolatry, reveal God's character, and offer hope.
  4. Using Scripture in Questions: Instead of immediately quoting verses, use Scripture to inform your questions. "Eleanor, what does the Bible say about where our true security comes from, if it's not from our own control?" This invites the client to engage with truth rather than passively receiving it.
Coach's Tip

Integrating Scripture Naturally: Avoid "Bible-dumping." Instead of overwhelming a client with verses, select one or two powerful passages relevant to their specific struggle. Invite them to read it aloud, reflect on what stands out, and discuss how it speaks to their situation. This makes Scripture a living, active tool rather than a distant textbook.

Exploring Spiritual History & Relationship with God

A client's past spiritual journey profoundly shapes their present. Understanding this history provides context for their current beliefs and struggles.

  1. Conversion Experience: Explore their "testimony." Was it a genuine conversion, or a cultural or emotional decision? What was the context? What did they understand about sin and grace?
  2. Church Background: What kind of church did they grow up in? Was it legalistic, grace-filled, or emotionally driven? How did this shape their view of God and themselves? Did they experience spiritual abuse or trauma?
  3. Spiritual Disciplines: What practices have they engaged in (Bible reading, prayer, fasting, worship)? What was their experience? Were they consistent? Why or why not?
  4. Significant Spiritual Influences: Who were key spiritual mentors or detractors in their life? How did these relationships impact their faith?
  5. Current Relationship with God: Beyond practices, how would they describe their current emotional and relational state with God? Is it intimate, distant, fearful, loving, resentful?
  6. Impact of Past Wounds: How have past hurts (e.g., parental abandonment, betrayal) influenced their perception of God's trustworthiness or love?

This comprehensive exploration helps you identify patterns, trauma, and unbiblical foundations that may be contributing to their current struggles. It allows you to tailor your coaching to address their unique spiritual journey.

Coach's Tip

The Power of Story: Encourage clients to tell their spiritual story in their own words. Listen for key turning points, moments of doubt, seasons of growth, and significant relationships. Their narrative will often reveal deep-seated beliefs and fears that are driving their present challenges. This narrative approach is deeply empowering for women who appreciate sharing their journey.

Key Takeaways

  • Spiritual assessment is a vital component of biblical counseling coaching, moving beyond surface issues to discern a client's heart and spiritual state.
  • Indicators of spiritual health include growing intimacy with God, genuine repentance, and consistent application of Scripture, while stagnation may present as apathy, unbiblical beliefs, or habitual sin.
  • Assessing a client's understanding of core biblical doctrines (God's character, sin, grace, repentance, identity in Christ) is crucial, as misbeliefs often fuel distress.
  • Tools like questionnaires, "What-If" questions, and the "Expose the Lie" method help uncover specific sin patterns, unbiblical narratives, and idolatry of the heart.
  • Prayer and Scripture are indispensable guides for the coach's discernment and for leading the client to God's truth and transformative power.
  • Exploring a client's spiritual history provides essential context for their current relationship with God and their spiritual struggles.

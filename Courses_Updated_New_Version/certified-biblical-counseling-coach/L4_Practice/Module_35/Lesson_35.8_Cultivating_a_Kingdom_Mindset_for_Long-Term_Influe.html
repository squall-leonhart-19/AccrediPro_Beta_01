3. Designing Your Group Coaching Program

A group coaching program is a structured offering where you guide a small cohort (typically 5-15 participants) through a specific curriculum over several weeks. It's ideal for addressing common challenges within a community, like Pastor Emily's congregants' stress and anxiety.

Key Elements of a Group Program:

  • Duration & Frequency: A 6-8 week program meeting weekly for 60-90 minutes is common. This allows for deep dives without overwhelming participants.
  • Specific Curriculum: Focus on a single, well-defined problem. For Pastor Emily, this could be "Biblical Resilience Journey: Finding Strength in Christ" or "Peace in the Storm: Overcoming Anxiety Biblically."
  • Deliverables:
    • Weekly live group calls (Zoom, Google Meet).
    • Accompanying worksheets, journaling prompts, or short readings.
    • Access to a private online community (e.g., Facebook group, private forum) for ongoing support and discussion.
    • Optional: 15-minute individual check-ins at specific points for personalized guidance.
  • Value Proposition: Emphasize the cost-effectiveness compared to 1:1, the power of community support, diverse perspectives, and shared learning experiences.
Coach Tip

When designing your group program, always focus on a specific pain point and a clear transformation. Instead of a general "Biblical Coaching," market "Finding Freedom from Worry: A 6-Week Biblical Journey." This clarity attracts the right participants and makes your value undeniable.

4. Mastering the Workshop Pitch

Workshops are shorter, more focused events, typically 2-4 hours or a full day, designed to address a single, acute topic. They serve as excellent entry points for new clients and can be offered as a standalone service or as a lead magnet for your longer programs.

Workshop Pitch Structure:

  1. Problem Statement (5-10%): Start by vividly describing the challenge your audience faces. For Pastor Emily's congregants: "Are your members feeling overwhelmed by the demands of life, struggling with persistent worry, and longing for deeper spiritual peace?"
  2. Solution Overview (20-30%): Introduce your workshop as the answer. Explain briefly what it is and what it covers. "Our 'Anchored in Hope' workshop provides practical, biblical strategies to combat anxiety and cultivate inner peace."
  3. Transformation & Benefits (40-50%): This is the core. Focus on what participants will gain and how they will feel. "Imagine your congregants leaving with a renewed sense of calm, equipped with powerful Scriptures for overcoming fear, and connected to a supportive community." Use strong, benefit-driven language.
  4. Logistics & Investment (10-15%): Clearly outline the duration, format, and cost.
  5. Call to Action (5-10%): Tell them exactly what to do next.
Coach Tip

Your pitch should always highlight the transformation you offer, not just the information. People don't buy what you do; they buy who they become by working with you. Emphasize the feelings, peace, and renewed strength they will experience.

5. Handling Objections to Group Offerings

Pastor Emily, or any potential client for a group offering, may have concerns. Your ability to confidently address these objections is crucial for closing the sale.

Common Objections & Confident Responses:

  1. "Will it be as effective as one-on-one counseling for my congregants?"

    Your Response: "That's a very valid question, Pastor Emily. While one-on-one offers deep personalization, group coaching provides unique benefits that often lead to profound transformation. Participants gain encouragement and diverse perspectives from peers facing similar struggles, creating a powerful sense of community and shared accountability that individual sessions can't replicate. It's also a highly cost-effective way to bring quality biblical guidance to more people, ensuring a broader impact across your church."

  2. "I'm not sure my congregants will commit to a program like this."

    Your Response: "I understand that concern. We've designed this program to be highly engaging and manageable. The weekly sessions are concise, and the supplementary materials are practical and easy to integrate into daily life. Furthermore, the built-in community aspect often becomes a powerful motivator, as participants don't want to let their peers down. Many churches find that promoting it as a 'journey together' fosters greater commitment. We can also discuss strategies for encouraging participation within your church."

  3. "It's too expensive for our church budget to offer to many people."

    Your Response: "I appreciate your transparency about the budget. Let's look at this from a different angle. Consider the long-term cost of unaddressed stress and anxiety within your congregation‚Äîburnout, relational issues, and spiritual stagnation. This program is an investment in prevention and empowerment. When you consider the cost per person, it's significantly lower than individual counseling, making it a highly efficient use of resources. We can also explore flexible payment options or even a tiered pricing model if that helps the church budget."

  4. "My congregants can find free biblical resources online."

    Your Response: "You're absolutely right, there's a wealth of information available. However, what this program offers is more than just information‚Äîit's a structured, guided transformation journey. We provide expert facilitation, a curated curriculum rooted in sound biblical theology, direct application to their specific challenges, and most importantly, a supportive, accountable community. It's the difference between reading a cookbook and having a master chef guide you through creating a gourmet meal with a group of fellow aspiring cooks."

6. Confidently Presenting Your Value & Pricing

Presenting your pricing for group offerings requires confidence and a clear connection to the immense value you provide. Remember, you're not just selling hours; you're selling transformation, peace, and spiritual growth.

Grace's Approach to Pricing Presentation:

  • Value Stacking: Before stating the price, reiterate all the components and benefits of your program. List out the weekly sessions, worksheets, community access, and the overall transformation. This builds the perceived value.
  • Anchor Pricing (Optional): If you also offer 1:1, you might briefly mention its higher price point to make the group offering seem more accessible and valuable. Example: "While my private 1:1 coaching package is an investment of $X, our group program offers similar transformative results at a fraction of the cost..."
  • Clear & Confident Delivery: State the price clearly, without hesitation or apology. Then, pause and allow them to absorb it.
  • Payment Options: Offer flexibility. A full payment option (perhaps with a small discount) and a 2-3 month payment plan can make it more accessible.
Pricing Presentation Example

YOU: "Pastor Emily, based on our conversation, I truly believe the 'Biblical Resilience Journey' program is exactly what your congregants need to find lasting peace and strength. This 8-week program includes eight live, interactive group coaching calls, a comprehensive workbook with practical exercises, access to our exclusive online community for ongoing support, and direct access to me for questions between sessions. The investment for this transformative journey, per participant, is just $597. We also offer a flexible payment plan of two monthly payments of $300."

Coach Tip

Always frame your pricing around the value and transformation you provide, not merely the hours you spend. Your expertise, spiritual discernment, and structured approach are invaluable. Present your fees with the same confidence you bring to your coaching.

7. Crafting Your Call to Action (Practice Script)

After presenting your program and pricing, the final step is to guide your prospect towards commitment. This is where you practice your Call to Action (CTA).

Practice Closing Lines:

YOU: "So, Pastor Emily, considering everything we've discussed, how does this sound to you? Do you feel this 'Biblical Resilience Journey' aligns with your vision for your congregants and their spiritual well-being?"

(Pause for her response. Listen actively.)

IF POSITIVE: "That's wonderful to hear! I'm truly excited about the impact this can have. The next step is simple. We can get the enrollment process started today, and I'll send you all the details to share with your congregants and the church board. We're looking to launch the first cohort on [Date], and spots are filling quickly as we keep our groups intimate for maximum impact. How would you like to proceed?"

IF SHE HESITATES (e.g., "I need to talk to the board"): "I completely understand the need to discuss this with your board. To help facilitate that conversation, I can provide you with a detailed proposal outlining the program, its benefits, testimonials from past participants, and the investment. Would that be helpful? We could even schedule a brief follow-up call with you and a key board member if that would strengthen your case."

Key Principles for CTA:

  • Clarity: Make the next steps undeniable.
  • Confidence: Believe in your offer and its ability to help.
  • Urgency/Scarcity (Ethical): If applicable, mention limited spots or a program start date to encourage timely decisions.
  • Support: Offer to assist with any further information or help they need to make a decision.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is a key advantage of group coaching over 1:1 for a church leader like Pastor Emily?

A key advantage is the ability to provide biblical guidance and support to a larger number of congregants simultaneously, making it a more cost-effective and impactful solution for the church community. It also fosters community and shared accountability among participants.

2. When pitching a workshop, what should be the primary focus of your message?

The primary focus of your message when pitching a workshop should be the transformation and benefits participants will experience. Emphasize what they will gain, how they will feel, and the positive changes in their lives, rather than just the topics covered.

3. How would you respond to the objection, "I'm not sure my congregants will commit to a program like this?"

A confident response would be to acknowledge the concern and then highlight the program's engaging design, manageable weekly sessions, and the powerful motivational aspect of the built-in community and shared accountability. You could also offer to discuss strategies for encouraging participation within the church.

4. What are two key elements to include when confidently presenting your pricing for a group program?

Two key elements are: 1) Value Stacking, where you reiterate all the program components and benefits before stating the price to build perceived value; and 2) Clear & Confident Delivery of the price, followed by a brief pause, potentially offering flexible payment options.

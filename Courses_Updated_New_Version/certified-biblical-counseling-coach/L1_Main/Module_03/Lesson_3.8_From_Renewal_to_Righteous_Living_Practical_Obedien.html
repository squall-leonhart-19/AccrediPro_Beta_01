Understanding the Core of Relate & Renew

Before we jump into our scenario, let's briefly revisit why relational coaching is so vital. It's not just about dispensing advice or quoting scripture; it's about creating a sacred space where a client feels truly seen, heard, and valued by both you and, ultimately, by God. Research consistently shows that the therapeutic alliance, or the strength of the relationship between client and helper, accounts for approximately 30% of positive outcomes in counseling (Lambert & Barley, 2001). For Biblical Counseling Coaches, this alliance is foundational, paving the way for spiritual transformation.

In the "Relate" phase, we focus on:

  • Building Rapport: Establishing trust and comfort.
  • Active Listening: Hearing beyond words to emotions and underlying beliefs.
  • Empathy: Understanding and reflecting the client's perspective and feelings.
  • Validation: Affirming the client's experience as legitimate.

The "Renew" aspect then flows naturally as the client feels safe enough to explore biblical truths and allow the Holy Spirit to work in their heart. This lab will focus heavily on the "Relate" aspects, as they precede and enable "Renew."

Coach Tip

Remember, your presence is a powerful tool. In my practice, I've found that simply being fully present, without judgment or immediate solutions, often shifts the dynamic of a session. It communicates, "I am here with you, and you are not alone."

The Coaching Dialogue: Practicing Empathy

Here's a snippet of a potential coaching dialogue. Your task is to read this dialogue aloud, embodying the role of the coach. Focus on your tone, your pauses, and how you convey genuine care and understanding.

Your Coaching Script

Read through this dialogue. When it's your turn, practice saying the words out loud. Feel the weight of Eleanor's words and respond with compassion.

Client: Eleanor

Coach: You (Grace Williams)


ELEANOR: "...and honestly, a bit angry at God for this emptiness. I try to read my Bible, but the words feel hollow. I miss the community of my old school, but haven't found a new place to belong."

YOU: "Eleanor, thank you for being so incredibly honest about what you're experiencing. It takes real courage to voice feelings like anger towards God, and to acknowledge that sense of emptiness and loss of purpose. What I'm hearing is a deep longing for connection â€“ with God, with community, and with a sense of meaning in this new season of your life. Does that resonate with what you're feeling?"

ELEANOR: "Yes, exactly. It's like I'm adrift. All the things that defined me are gone, and I don't know who I am anymore. I feel guilty for feeling this way, like a bad Christian."

YOU: "That feeling of guilt is understandable, Eleanor. It's common to believe that a strong faith means never questioning or feeling upset. But the truth is, God is big enough for all our emotions, even anger and confusion. Many biblical figures, like Job or David in the Psalms, expressed similar struggles. It sounds like you're not just grieving your old life, but also a part of your spiritual identity. Can you tell me more about what that lost spiritual identity felt like for you?"

ELEANOR: "It was vibrant. I felt God's presence daily, guiding me in the classroom, comforting me when I missed my husband. Now, it's just quiet. And I'm scared it won't come back."

YOU: "The quiet can be unnerving, especially after a lifetime of feeling God's vibrant presence. That fear that it won't come back is a very real and valid concern. It sounds like this isn't just a change in circumstances, but a profound spiritual disorientation. And you're navigating it with immense bravery. What do you think might be one small step you could take this week to acknowledge that longing for connection you mentioned?"

ELEANOR: (Pauses) "I don't know. Maybe... maybe just sitting with my Bible, not trying to read, but just holding it. And maybe going for a walk in the park, just to be in nature."

YOU: "Those sound like beautiful, gentle steps, Eleanor. Just holding your Bible, not with the pressure to 'perform,' but as a symbol of your desire for connection. And seeking God in His creation through a walk. Remember, even in the quiet, God is at work. Hebrews 13:5 reminds us, 'I will never leave you nor forsake you.' His presence isn't always loud; sometimes it's a gentle whisper, or even just the knowledge that He is there, holding you in the quiet. How does it feel to consider those small steps?"

ELEANOR: "A little less overwhelmed. A little bit of hope, maybe."

Coach Tip

Notice the power of reflective listening in the script. Phrases like "What I'm hearing is..." or "It sounds like you're..." show Eleanor you're truly processing her words and emotions. This builds trust and encourages her to elaborate, deepening the conversation.

Your Turn — Practice Out Loud

Now, it's your turn to engage in this scenario. Find a quiet space where you can speak freely.

  1. Read the script silently first: Familiarize yourself with Eleanor's statements and your coaching responses.
  2. Speak it out loud: Imagine Eleanor is in front of you. Use a warm, empathetic tone. Don't rush. Allow for pauses.
  3. Record yourself (optional but recommended): Use your phone's voice recorder. Listen back critically. How did you sound? Was your tone reassuring? Did you convey genuine empathy? Where could you improve your pacing or word choice?
  4. Focus on validation: Pay special attention to moments where you validate Eleanor's feelings ("That feeling of guilt is understandable," "That fear... is a very real and valid concern").
  5. Offer hope gently: Observe how biblical truth is introduced not as a command, but as a comforting reminder of God's character and faithfulness.

Refining Your Approach: Common Pitfalls

Even with the best intentions, new coaches often encounter common stumbling blocks in relational coaching. Being aware of these can help you navigate sessions more effectively.

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Quickly: It's natural to want to "fix" a client's pain. However, prematurely offering solutions or biblical directives can make a client feel unheard or misunderstood, especially if their emotional state hasn't been fully explored. The desire to fix can overshadow the need to connect.
  • Minimizing or Dismissing Feelings: Statements like "You shouldn't feel that way" or "Just pray about it" invalidate a client's experience. Even if their feelings seem irrational to you, they are real to the client. Acknowledge and validate before attempting to reframe.
  • Over-Spiritualizing Without Empathy: While our coaching is biblically centered, immediately quoting verses without first empathizing with the client's pain can feel dismissive. It's like offering a blanket to someone shivering in the cold without first asking why they're cold.
  • Not Allowing for Silence: Silence can feel uncomfortable, but it's a powerful tool. It gives the client space to process, formulate thoughts, or simply sit with their emotions. Resist the urge to fill every quiet moment.
  • Imposing Your Own Agenda: As a coach, you guide, but the client sets the pace and direction of their journey. Pushing your own ideas of what they "should" do or believe can create resistance and break rapport.

Coach Tip

When a client expresses strong emotions, your immediate response should be to validate, not negate. Phrases like "That sounds incredibly difficult," or "It makes sense that you feel that way" can open the door for deeper trust, allowing the client to feel safe enough to explore their pain with you.

Markers of Success in Relational Coaching

How do you know if you've had a successful relational coaching session, especially when there are no immediate "solutions" or breakthroughs? Success in the "Relate" phase looks different from later stages. Here are key indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: They might say, "You really get it," or "I feel like you understand." This is paramount.
  • Client Expresses a Glimmer of Hope: Even a small shift from despair to "maybe there's a way forward" is a significant step. Eleanor's "a little bit of hope, maybe" is a perfect example.
  • Client Feels Safe to Share More: They open up about deeper feelings, vulnerabilities, or past experiences, indicating growing trust.
  • Client Shows Non-Verbal Cues of Relaxation: Relaxed shoulders, softer gaze, less fidgeting can indicate a growing sense of safety and calm.
  • A Foundation for Future Sessions is Established: The client expresses a desire to continue working with you, or you both agree on a small, manageable next step.
  • You Feel Centered and Present: A successful session often leaves you, the coach, feeling grounded and confident in your ability to hold space for the client.

Coach Tip

Don't underestimate the power of prayer in session. With client permission, a brief, heartfelt prayer can invite the Holy Spirit's presence, offering comfort and peace that transcends human words. This can be particularly impactful in the "Renew" phase, but can also deepen connection in "Relate."

1

In the scenario with Eleanor, which of the following best describes the coach's primary goal during the initial phase of the session?

The primary goal is to establish rapport and validate Eleanor's emotions. While offering biblical truth and identifying next steps are important, they are secondary to making Eleanor feel heard and understood in the initial "Relate" phase.
2

Eleanor states, "I feel guilty for feeling this way, like a bad Christian." Which coaching response best aligns with empathetic validation?

An empathetic response would acknowledge and normalize her feeling without judgment. For example: "That feeling of guilt is understandable, Eleanor. It's common to believe that a strong faith means never questioning, but God is big enough for all our emotions." This validates her experience while gently reframing her belief about "good Christians."
3

Eleanor mentions, "I miss the community of my old school, but haven't found a new place to belong." What type of question would best encourage deeper sharing here?

An open-ended question that explores the depth of her feeling, such as "What did that sense of belonging feel like for you, and what aspects of it do you miss most?" This invites her to articulate the impact of the loss, rather than just stating the fact.
4

Which of the following would be considered a common pitfall if the coach had immediately responded to Eleanor's anger at God with a verse about God's goodness?

This would be an example of over-spiritualizing without empathy or jumping to solutions too quickly. While the verse might be true, it would likely make Eleanor feel unheard and potentially ashamed of her honest feelings, hindering connection rather than fostering it.

Coach Tip

Practice doesn't make perfect; it makes progress. Don't be discouraged if your first few attempts at these dialogues feel awkward. Every conversation is a learning opportunity. Celebrate your willingness to step into this sacred calling!

Key Takeaways

  • Empathetic connection is the cornerstone of effective Biblical Counseling Coaching, paving the way for spiritual renewal.
  • Active and reflective listening validates a client's experience, making them feel seen and understood.
  • Honest expression of emotions, even anger toward God, is normal and can be validated by a compassionate coach.
  • Gently introducing biblical truth, contextualized to the client's experience, fosters hope and renewal.
  • Avoiding pitfalls like premature solutions or over-spiritualizing strengthens the coaching relationship.

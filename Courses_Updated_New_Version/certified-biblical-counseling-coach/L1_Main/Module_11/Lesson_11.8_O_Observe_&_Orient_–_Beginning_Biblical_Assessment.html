Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for sharing your heart with me so openly. I hear the deep longing for purpose, peace, and a renewed connection with God. What you're experiencing is incredibly common, especially during significant life transitions like an empty nest and profound loss. It's a testament to your faith and strength that you're seeking to address this ache."
ELEANOR:
"It just feels like I should be stronger, or that I'm failing somehow. My friends seem to be doing fine, but I feel so lost."
YOU:
"Eleanor, that feeling of 'should' is a heavy burden, isn't it? It's easy to compare ourselves, but your journey is unique. The grief and changes you've faced are immense, and it's perfectly natural to feel disoriented. This isn't a failure; it's an opportunity for a deeper, more intentional walk with Christ. In my practice, I've seen many women in similar seasons find profound renewal."
YOU:
"My role is to walk alongside you, using biblical wisdom to help you navigate this period. We'll explore how God's Word speaks to your feelings of loss, loneliness, and identity, and discover practical ways to cultivate a vibrant, personal relationship with Him. Does that sound like a path you'd like to explore?"
ELEANOR:
"Oh, yes. That's exactly what I need. I just don't know where to start."
YOU:
"Wonderful. We'll start by gently exploring what a 'vibrant connection' looks like for you, and how God's unchanging character provides an anchor in seasons of change. We'll uncover specific biblical truths that speak to your current experience and build a personalized plan to integrate them into your daily life. Sound good?"
Coach Tip

When a client expresses strong emotions or self-blame, respond with empathy and validation. Phrases like "That's a heavy burden, isn't it?" or "It's perfectly natural to feel..." normalize their experience and create a safe space.

Common Coaching Mistakes to Avoid

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls new biblical counseling coaches often encounter:

  • Immediately Offering Solutions: The temptation to "fix" the problem can be strong. Remember, biblical coaching is about guiding the client to discover God's truth for themselves, not simply providing answers. Eleanor needs to feel heard before she can receive guidance.
  • Over-Spiritualizing Without Empathy: While our foundation is biblical, avoid jumping straight to scripture without first validating the client's emotional experience. Statements like "Just pray more" or "You need to trust God" can feel dismissive if not preceded by deep empathy.
  • Lecturing or Preaching: Your role is a coach, not a pastor or therapist. Maintain a conversational, collaborative tone. Avoid lengthy monologues or authoritative pronouncements.
  • Ignoring the Client's Language: Pay attention to the specific words your client uses to describe their situation. Mirroring their language (e.g., "this ache," "lost my joy") shows you're attuned to their unique experience.
  • Lack of Clarity on Your Role: Clearly define what biblical coaching entails and what the client can expect. This manages expectations and prevents misunderstanding, as demonstrated in our script.

Markers of Success for This Session

How do you know if your initial coaching session with Eleanor was successful? Look for these indicators:

  • Client Feels Heard and Understood: Eleanor expresses feeling validated, not judged, and that her unique struggle is recognized.
  • Client Expresses Hope: A shift from feeling "lost" or "empty" to expressing a glimmer of hope or readiness to explore solutions.
  • Clear Understanding of the Coaching Process: Eleanor comprehends your role as a biblical coach and the collaborative nature of the journey ahead.
  • Agreement on Next Steps: A clear, mutual understanding of the initial focus areas and how you'll proceed in future sessions.
  • Empowerment, Not Dependence: Eleanor feels empowered to engage with the process, rather than passively waiting for you to "fix" her.
Coach Tip

Always end an initial session by clarifying expectations and reiterating the collaborative nature of coaching. This empowers the client and sets a strong foundation for future sessions.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of validating Eleanor's feelings and past efforts in the opening of the coaching session?

The primary purpose is to build trust and rapport, making Eleanor feel heard, understood, and safe. It normalizes her experience and reduces feelings of shame or failure, creating a receptive environment for biblical guidance.

2. When Eleanor says, "It just feels like I should be stronger, or that I'm failing somehow," how does the coach's response align with biblical coaching principles?

The coach's response reframes her feelings of failure by acknowledging the "heavy burden" of "should," validating her experience ("perfectly natural"), and pointing to an "opportunity for a deeper, more intentional walk with Christ." This shifts focus from self-condemnation to God's redemptive purpose, a core biblical principle.

3. What is a key distinction between biblical counseling coaching and simply giving advice or preaching, as demonstrated in the script?

The script demonstrates a collaborative approach ("walk alongside you," "explore," "discover," "build a personalized plan") rather than merely giving advice. The coach offers guidance based on biblical wisdom but invites Eleanor to actively engage in the process of discovery and application, focusing on her unique journey.

4. Why is it important to confirm the client's agreement on the proposed coaching path ("Does that sound like a path you'd like to explore?")?

Confirming agreement ensures client buy-in and ownership of the coaching process. It establishes the client as an active participant, not a passive recipient, and confirms that the coach's proposed direction aligns with the client's perceived needs and goals.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy First: Always begin by validating your client's feelings and current situation before introducing solutions or biblical principles.
  • Normalize Struggles: Help clients understand that their challenges are common and not a sign of personal failure, especially during life transitions.
  • Define Your Role Clearly: Articulate how biblical coaching works and what the client can expect, fostering a collaborative partnership.
  • Focus on Discovery: Guide clients to uncover biblical truths and apply them personally, rather than simply providing answers.
  • Practice Makes Proficient: Regularly practicing coaching scenarios out loud is essential for developing confidence and fluid communication.

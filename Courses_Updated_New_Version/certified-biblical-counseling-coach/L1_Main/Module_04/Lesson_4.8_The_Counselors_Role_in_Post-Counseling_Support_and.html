Emily, 42

Presenting Situation: Emily recently left a demanding career in corporate finance to focus on her family. While she loves her children, she feels a profound sense of loss of purpose and identity. She describes feeling "spiritually dry" and struggles with inconsistent spiritual disciplines like Bible reading and prayer, despite knowing their importance. Health History: Generally good health, though she reports chronic low energy and occasional feelings of anxiety, especially when reflecting on her spiritual life. No significant medical diagnoses.

Goals for Coaching:

  • To reconnect with God and rekindle her spiritual passion.
  • To establish sustainable and meaningful spiritual rhythms in her busy life.
  • To find renewed purpose and identity outside of her former career.
  • To overcome guilt and self-condemnation regarding her spiritual walk.

Emily is a prime example of a client who needs not just advice, but genuine discipleship to help her align her life with Christ's calling for her. Your role as a biblical counseling coach is crucial here.

The Discipling Dialogue

Here's a sample dialogue to guide your initial conversation with Emily. This script focuses on empathetic listening, validating her experience, and gently guiding her toward identifying God's truth and practical steps for spiritual development.

Your Coaching Script: Opening Session with Emily

Practice speaking these words out loud. Pay attention to your tone, pacing, and the warmth in your voice.

YOU: "Emily, thank you so much for sharing your heart with me. I hear the vulnerability in your voice, and I want to affirm that what you're feeling — this sense of lost purpose and spiritual dryness — is deeply real and understandable. Many women journey through seasons like this, especially during significant life transitions. The very fact that you're seeking to reconnect with God shows me the Holy Spirit is at work in you, drawing you closer."

EMILY: (Tears welling up) "It's just so hard. I know I should be reading my Bible more, praying more, but the days just fly by, and I feel so guilty. Like I'm failing God and my family."

YOU: "I hear that guilt, Emily, and it's a heavy burden to carry. What if we started by releasing some of that weight? God's grace is not dependent on our performance. Instead of focusing on what you 'should' be doing, let's explore what God's Word says about His love for you, even in seasons of spiritual weariness. How does that sound as a starting point?"

Coach Tip

Always start by validating your client's emotions and experiences. This builds trust and creates a safe space. Avoid jumping straight to solutions or biblical admonitions; instead, meet them where they are with empathy and grace.

EMILY: "That sounds... freeing. I'm so tired of feeling like I'm not enough."

YOU: "You are more than enough in Christ, Emily. Let's explore that truth together. When you think about 'reconnecting with God,' what does that look like for you, personally? What specific practices or feelings come to mind?"

EMILY: "Well, I used to love my quiet time in the mornings before work. Reading a devotional, praying for my day. Now, I'm just rushing to get the kids ready."

YOU: "That's a beautiful picture of connection. And it's clear your mornings look very different now. So, if we were to think about establishing new, sustainable spiritual rhythms — rhythms that fit your current life — what might be one small, intentional step you could take this week to invite God into your day, even amidst the rush?"

Coach Tip

Break down overwhelming goals into small, actionable steps. This reduces client paralysis and increases the likelihood of success. Ask open-ended questions that empower the client to find their own solutions, guided by the Holy Spirit.

EMILY: "Hmm... maybe just five minutes? While I'm making coffee? I could read one verse and just pray about it."

YOU: "That's a wonderful idea, Emily! Five minutes, one verse, while making coffee. That's a powerful way to start. How confident are you, on a scale of 1 to 10, that you can commit to that for the next three mornings?"

EMILY: "A 9. I think I can definitely do that."

YOU: "Excellent. And what would help you remember and stay accountable to that small step? Perhaps setting a reminder on your phone, or telling your husband?"

Coach Tip

Utilize the confidence scale (1-10) to gauge commitment. If below a 7 or 8, help the client refine the goal to be more achievable. Always discuss accountability mechanisms to reinforce new habits.

Practice Your Coaching Voice

Now it's your turn to step into the coach's role. Don't just read the script — embody it. The goal here is to develop your authentic coaching presence.

  1. Read Silently First: Understand the flow and the intention behind each statement. Notice the balance between empathy, biblical truth, and actionable steps.
  2. Speak it Out Loud: Pretend Emily is sitting across from you. Use a warm, calm, and encouraging tone. Imagine her emotions and respond genuinely.
  3. Record Yourself (Optional but Recommended): Use your phone to record your dialogue. Listen back to identify areas where your tone could be more empathetic, your questions clearer, or your biblical integration more seamless.
  4. Focus on Active Listening: Even though it's a script, imagine truly listening to Emily. How would you paraphrase her feelings? What non-verbal cues might you pick up on?
  5. Internalize the Principles: Beyond the words, understand *why* each statement is made. It's about discipling — guiding her to grow in Christ-likeness, not just solving a problem.

Coach Tip

Your voice is a powerful tool. A calm, empathetic tone can soothe an anxious client and build trust. Practice modulating your voice to convey both compassion and confident guidance. Remember, you are a vessel for God's truth and love.

Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

Even experienced coaches can fall into common traps, especially when discipling clients. Be mindful of these to ensure your coaching is effective and Christ-honoring:

  • Giving Unsolicited Advice: Instead of telling Emily exactly what to do, guide her to discover God's wisdom and her own actionable steps.
    Avoid: "You just need to get up earlier and read your Bible."
    Instead: "What might be one small, intentional step you could take...?"
  • Spiritual Shaming or Guilt-Tripping: While conviction is from the Holy Spirit, your role is not to heap shame. Focus on grace, redemption, and growth.
    Avoid: "You know better than to let your spiritual life slip."
    Instead: "God's grace is not dependent on our performance. Let's explore that truth."
  • Over-Scheduling the Client: Don't overwhelm Emily with too many new spiritual practices at once. Start small and build momentum.
    Avoid: "You should start a daily quiet time, join a Bible study, and volunteer at church."
    Instead: "What might be one small, intentional step you could take this week...?"
  • Lack of Empathy or Validation: Dismissing her feelings ("It's not that bad") will shut down communication. Validate her experience first.
    Avoid: "Everyone feels overwhelmed sometimes, just push through it."
    Instead: "I hear the vulnerability in your voice, and I want to affirm that what you're feeling is deeply real and understandable."
  • Focusing Solely on Behavior, Not Heart Change: True discipleship involves transforming the heart, mind, and soul, not just outward actions. Connect actions to biblical truths and identity in Christ.
    Avoid: "Just do your five minutes for three days."
    Instead: "Let's explore what God's Word says about His love for you... that's a powerful way to start."

Markers of a Fruitful Session

How do you know if your discipling-focused coaching session has been effective? Look for these indicators:

  • Client Engagement & Ownership: Emily actively participates in the conversation, offering her own ideas and insights, not just passively receiving instructions.
  • Increased Clarity: Emily expresses a clearer understanding of her situation, her identity in Christ, or the path forward.
  • Reduced Emotional Burden: She feels less guilt, shame, or overwhelm, replaced by a sense of hope, peace, or empowerment rooted in grace.
  • Specific, Achievable Commitments: Emily identifies one or two concrete, measurable actions she feels confident she can take.
  • Alignment with Biblical Truth: The conversation consistently points back to God's Word and Christian principles, without being preachy.
  • Renewed Hope & Motivation: Emily leaves the session with a genuine sense of hope for change and motivation to pursue her spiritual goals.

Remember, your goal is not to "fix" Emily, but to walk alongside her as she grows in her relationship with Christ. This is the essence of biblical coaching and discipleship.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. When Emily expresses feelings of guilt over her inconsistent spiritual disciplines, what is the most appropriate initial coaching response?

The most appropriate initial response is to validate her feelings and gently redirect her focus from performance to God's grace. For example, "I hear that guilt, Emily, and it's a heavy burden to carry. What if we started by releasing some of that weight? God's grace is not dependent on our performance."

2. Emily's goal is to "reconnect with God." What coaching technique helps her break this broad goal into actionable steps?

To break down a broad goal like "reconnecting with God," a coach should ask open-ended questions that encourage the client to define what that looks like for them and then identify small, intentional, and sustainable steps. For example, "When you think about 'reconnecting with God,' what does that look like for you, personally? What specific practices or feelings come to mind?" followed by, "What might be one small, intentional step you could take this week...?"

3. If Emily had stated a confidence level of "4 out of 10" for her commitment to the five-minute prayer, how should you adjust your approach?

A confidence level below 7 or 8 indicates the proposed action might be too challenging or not fully owned by the client. The coach should help the client refine the goal to make it even smaller or more achievable, asking questions like, "What would make that a 6 or 7 for you?" or "Is there an even smaller step you could commit to?"

4. Which of the following is a common pitfall in discipling-focused coaching and how can it be avoided?

A common pitfall is giving unsolicited advice. This can be avoided by guiding the client to discover solutions themselves through thoughtful, open-ended questions, rather than directly telling them what to do. For instance, instead of saying "You should read your Bible daily," ask "What might be one way you could intentionally invite God into your day?"

Key Takeaways

  • Biblical counseling coaching in the "Disciple & Develop" phase focuses on guiding clients toward sustainable spiritual growth and deeper alignment with Christ.
  • Effective discipling begins with empathetic listening and validation, creating a safe space for clients to share their struggles without judgment.
  • Break down large spiritual goals into small, actionable, and achievable steps to foster confidence and consistent progress.
  • Always connect practical steps back to biblical truth and God's grace, ensuring the focus remains on heart transformation rather than mere behavior modification.
  • Avoid common pitfalls such as unsolicited advice, spiritual shaming, and overwhelming clients; instead, empower them to discover God's path for their lives.

Welcome, Esteemed Coach!

As biblical counseling coaches, our role is not merely to offer quick fixes but to guide individuals toward profound, Christ-centered transformation. This journey begins with a meticulous and prayerful initial assessment. In this lesson, we will equip you with the discernment and tools necessary to move beyond presenting issues to identify the core problems of the heart, as revealed through Scripture. By the end, you will feel more confident in your ability to gather essential information, ask penetrating questions, and frame a client's struggles biblically, setting the stage for truly impactful coaching.

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between a client's presenting issues (symptoms) and their core heart problems (root causes) from a biblical perspective.
  • Master techniques for asking insightful, open-ended questions that uncover unbiblical thought patterns, beliefs, and desires.
  • Apply a robust biblical framework for assessment, examining desires, motives, idols, and responses to life's circumstances.
  • Utilize basic intake forms and questionnaires to gather essential information while maintaining a distinct biblical focus.
  • Develop the skill of understanding a client's story and worldview through the illuminating lens of Scripture.
  • Prepare for comprehensive, Christ-centered intervention by accurately identifying the true nature of a client's struggle.

Opening Case Study

Sarah's Persistent Anxiety and Marital Strain

S

Sarah, 48-year-old Accountant

Presenting Issues: Chronic anxiety, frequent arguments with husband, feeling overwhelmed and unsupported.

Sarah sought biblical counseling coaching after months of escalating tension in her marriage and an underlying sense of dread that permeated her daily life. She reported feeling constantly on edge, struggling with sleep, and experiencing irritability that often led to heated exchanges with her husband, Mark. "I just want the fighting to stop," she confessed during her initial call. "And I want to feel peaceful again. I feel like I'm losing control."

Her initial intake form revealed a history of perfectionism and a demanding career. She mentioned feeling responsible for everyone's happiness and often taking on tasks that were not hers. She also casually noted that her church attendance had become sporadic over the past year due to "busyness."

On the surface, Sarah's issues appear to be anxiety and marital conflict. However, a biblical coach knows these are often symptoms. Our initial assessment will require us to dig deeper, beyond the observable behaviors and emotional states, to uncover the spiritual heart issues driving her distress. What might be the core problems beneath Sarah's presenting issues?

01. Presenting Issues vs. Core Problems

A client's initial contact often centers on what they perceive as their most pressing problem‚Äîthe presenting issue. This is usually a symptom, a behavior, or a painful circumstance. For example: "I'm always angry," "My marriage is falling apart," "I feel so anxious all the time," or "I can't seem to stop this habit." While these are very real and distressing, a biblical coach understands they are rarely the root cause.

The true work of biblical counseling coaching lies in identifying the core problem. This refers to the underlying spiritual heart issues, unbiblical beliefs, sinful desires, or idolatrous patterns that fuel the presenting symptoms. Scripture consistently points to the heart as the wellspring of life's issues:

  • "Keep your heart with all vigilance, for from it flow the springs of life." (Proverbs 4:23 ESV)
  • "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" (Jeremiah 17:9 ESV)
  • "For out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks." (Matthew 12:34 ESV)

Our goal is to help clients understand this distinction‚Äîthat their struggles are not just external circumstances or uncontrollable emotions, but often manifestations of deeper spiritual realities. This understanding empowers them to seek God's truth for genuine, lasting change.

Coach's Tip

Educating with Empathy: When introducing the concept of heart issues, do so gently. Say, "It sounds like you're carrying a heavy burden with this anxiety. My goal is not just to help you feel better in the moment, but to discover what God might be revealing through these feelings, so we can address the root and find lasting freedom in Christ." This validates their pain while guiding them to a deeper understanding.

02. Asking Insightful Questions to Uncover Root Causes

The art of initial assessment lies in asking questions that peel back layers, moving from the "what" to the "why." This requires active listening, empathetic presence (as discussed in Module 1, Lesson 4), and a prayerful reliance on the Holy Spirit for discernment. Here are techniques for crafting insightful, open-ended questions:

Techniques for Deepening Inquiry:

  1. The "What happens when..." Question: This helps explore triggers and patterns.
    • "What happens when you feel that anxiety rising?"
    • "What typically happens right before you and your husband argue?"
  2. The "What do you desire/fear most...?" Question: This uncovers motives and potential idols.
    • "What do you most desire when you find yourself engaging in that behavior?"
    • "What are you most afraid will happen if [situation] doesn't change?"
    • "What does this situation reveal about what you truly love or trust?"
  3. The "How do you respond...?" Question: This reveals patterns of coping and belief.
    • "How do you typically respond when you feel overwhelmed?"
    • "When life doesn't go your way, what is your immediate internal response?"
  4. The "What does that say about...?" Question: This challenges underlying assumptions and beliefs.
    • "When you say you feel like a failure, what does that say about your identity or worth?"
    • "What does that particular struggle communicate about your view of God's sovereignty or goodness?"
  5. The "If God were to speak to this...?" Question: This directly introduces a biblical lens, gently.
    • "If you were to imagine God speaking to this situation, what might He say about your perspective?"
    • "How does God's Word inform how you view this challenge?"

Remember, the goal is not interrogation but compassionate exploration. Your tone should be warm, curious, and non-judgmental, creating a safe space for the client to truly reflect.

03. Utilizing a Biblical Framework for Assessment

Our assessment framework is fundamentally biblical. We are not just looking for psychological patterns but for spiritual dynamics. We seek to understand the client's story through the lens of God's truth, identifying where their beliefs, desires, and actions align or diverge from Scripture. This aligns directly with the 'Observe' and 'Orient' phases of the W.O.R.D. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Key Areas of Biblical Assessment:

  1. Desires & Motives: What are the driving forces behind their actions and emotional responses? Are their desires God-honoring or self-centered? (James 4:1-3)
  2. Idols of the Heart: What or whom do they trust, love, or serve more than God? This could be comfort, control, approval, success, security, or relationships. (Ezekiel 14:3, Colossians 3:5)
  3. Unbiblical Beliefs & Thought Patterns: Are they operating on worldly wisdom, lies from the enemy, or distorted views of God, self, or others? (Romans 12:2, 2 Corinthians 10:5)
  4. Responses to Life's Circumstances: How do they typically react to trials, disappointments, or blessings? Is their response characterized by faith, hope, and love, or by despair, anger, and fear? (Philippians 4:11-13, 1 Thessalonians 5:18)
  5. Relationship with God: What is the current state of their walk with Christ? Are they actively pursuing spiritual disciplines, or is there spiritual drift? (John 15:5, Hebrews 10:24-25)
  6. Understanding of Self: Do they see themselves as image-bearers of God, fallen, and redeemed through Christ? Or do they derive their identity from performance, possessions, or people? (Genesis 1:27, Ephesians 2:10)

A thorough assessment in these areas provides a comprehensive spiritual "map" of the client's heart, guiding you toward targeted, gospel-centered interventions.

Coach's Tip

The Power of Prayerful Discernment: Before, during, and after your assessment conversations, commit to fervent prayer. Ask the Holy Spirit to reveal the true condition of the client's heart, to grant you wisdom in asking the right questions, and to prepare the client's heart to receive truth. Your spiritual sensitivity is a powerful tool in assessment.

04. Introduction to Basic Intake Forms and Questionnaires

While personal conversation is paramount, structured intake forms and questionnaires are invaluable tools for gathering comprehensive information efficiently. They also prompt clients to reflect on areas they might not spontaneously mention. These forms should be designed to gather essential factual data while gently introducing a biblical perspective.

Components of a Biblically-Focused Intake Form:

  1. Demographic & Contact Information: Basic personal details.
  2. Presenting Concerns: Open-ended questions asking "What brings you to coaching?" and "What do you hope to achieve?"
  3. Personal History: Brief sections on family background, significant life events, and past counseling/coaching experiences.
  4. Spiritual History & Walk:
    • "Describe your relationship with God."
    • "How long have you been a Christian, and what does that mean to you?"
    • "What role does your faith play in your daily life and in addressing your current struggles?"
    • "What spiritual disciplines (prayer, Bible reading, fellowship) are you currently practicing?"
    • "Are you currently involved in a local church? If so, please describe your involvement."
  5. Key Relationships: Questions about marriage, family, friendships, and work relationships.
  6. Physical Health: Basic questions about sleep, diet, exercise, and any chronic health conditions or medications (as these can impact emotional/spiritual state).
  7. Habits & Behaviors: Inquire about coping mechanisms, destructive patterns, or areas of spiritual struggle.
  8. Expectations for Coaching: "What do you expect from a biblical counseling coach?" and "What are you willing to do to pursue change?"

These forms serve as a starting point, guiding your initial conversation and ensuring you cover critical areas. They also provide a baseline for measuring progress.

Coach's Tip

Balancing Depth and Respect: Remember that intake forms can feel intrusive. Introduce them by explaining their purpose: "This form helps me get a holistic picture of your life and faith journey, so I can best serve you. Please answer as openly as you feel comfortable." Assure them that you'll discuss their responses in person, allowing for clarification and deeper exploration.

05. Understanding the Client's Story and Worldview Through Scripture

Every client carries a unique story, shaped by their experiences, relationships, culture, and most importantly, their worldview. Their worldview is the lens through which they interpret life, God, themselves, and others. As biblical coaches, we must listen intently to their narrative, seeking to understand this worldview and how it aligns or conflicts with biblical truth.

Listening for Worldview Clues:

  • Language Used: Do they speak in terms of victimhood, entitlement, self-reliance, or dependence on God?
  • Attribution of Problems: Do they blame others, circumstances, or acknowledge their own contribution?
  • Hope & Despair: Where do they place their hope? Is it in external circumstances changing, or in God's faithfulness?
  • Definition of Success/Failure: What metrics do they use to evaluate their life? Are these biblical or worldly?
  • Understanding of Suffering: How do they interpret pain and adversity? As meaningless, punitive, or purposeful?

By listening for these clues, you can discern underlying assumptions and beliefs that may be hindering their spiritual growth and perpetuating their struggles. Your role is to gently and prayerfully help them re-frame their story through the grand narrative of God's redemptive plan‚Äîfrom creation to fall, redemption, and consummation. This is the essence of 'Orienting' them to God's truth.

Coach's Tip

Your Story Matters Too: Reflect on how your own life experiences and worldview inform your coaching. While maintaining professional boundaries, a shared understanding of brokenness and God's redemptive work can foster powerful empathy. Don't be afraid to share brief, relevant personal examples (when appropriate and client-focused) to build rapport and illustrate biblical principles.

Case Study Resolution

Sarah's Journey from Anxiety to Trust

S

Sarah, 48-year-old Accountant

Core Problems Identified: Idolatry of control, fear of man's disapproval, self-righteousness, lack of trust in God's sovereignty.

Through careful questioning during initial assessment, Coach Emily began to uncover Sarah's core problems. Emily asked, "When you feel overwhelmed and irritable, what is the deepest fear or desire in your heart?" Sarah initially struggled but eventually admitted, "I'm terrified of failure, and that people will see I'm not perfect. I feel like if I don't control everything, it will all fall apart." This revealed a deep-seated idolatry of control and fear of man.

Further inquiry into her sporadic church attendance revealed a sense of guilt and a belief that she had to "earn" God's favor. This pointed to self-righteousness and a misunderstanding of grace. Her husband's occasional criticism, though gentle, was perceived as a catastrophic threat to her carefully constructed image, fueling her anxiety and causing her to lash out.

Intervention Focus (based on assessment):

  • Repentance: Gently guiding Sarah to repent of her idolatry of control and self-reliance, and her fear of man, turning instead to God's perfect sovereignty (Proverbs 29:25).
  • Renewing the Mind: Replacing unbiblical beliefs about self-worth and God's grace with truths from Scripture (Ephesians 2:8-9, Romans 8:31-39).
  • Trust & Surrender: Teaching Sarah to surrender her need for control to God, finding peace in His perfect plan (Philippians 4:6-7).
  • Marital Communication: Applying biblical principles to her marriage, focusing on humble confession, forgiveness, and servant-hearted love (Ephesians 4:29-32).

Outcome: Over 10 coaching sessions, Sarah experienced significant transformation. Her anxiety attacks decreased by over 70% within three months. She began regularly attending church, finding renewed joy in fellowship and worship. Her arguments with Mark became less frequent and more productive, as she learned to communicate her needs humbly and trust God with outcomes. "I still have moments of worry," Sarah shared, "but now I know where to turn. I'm learning to truly trust God, not just with my words, but with my whole heart. It's like a weight has been lifted."

Sarah's story exemplifies how moving beyond presenting issues to address core heart problems, guided by biblical truth, leads to lasting spiritual and emotional healing.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client states, "I just can't stop procrastinating on important tasks." Which of the following is most likely a core problem (heart issue) underlying this presenting issue?

  1. Lack of time management skills.
  2. Fear of failure or success.
  3. An overly demanding work schedule.
  4. A need for external accountability.

Answer: b. Fear of failure or success. While other options might be contributing factors, fear of failure or success often points to deeper heart issues like idolatry of approval, pride, or a misunderstanding of God's grace and identity in Christ.

2. During an initial assessment, a client expresses constant dissatisfaction with their spouse, despite the spouse making efforts to change. What biblical concept should the coach primarily explore to uncover the core problem?

  1. The spouse's unrepentant sin.
  2. The client's unmet expectations and desires.
  3. The need for better communication strategies.
  4. The client's past relational trauma.

Answer: b. The client's unmet expectations and desires. While communication is important and past trauma can impact present issues, constant dissatisfaction often stems from unmet, and potentially unbiblical, expectations or idolatrous desires within the client's own heart (James 4:1-3). The coach should help the client examine their own heart before focusing solely on the spouse.

3. A coach utilizes a questionnaire that asks, "What spiritual disciplines are you currently practicing?" This question primarily helps in assessing which key area of biblical assessment?

  1. Client's physical health.
  2. Client's financial stability.
  3. Client's relationship with God.
  4. Client's social support network.

Answer: c. Client's relationship with God. Spiritual disciplines are direct indicators of a client's intentional pursuit of God and their reliance on Him for strength and guidance, providing insight into the vitality of their relationship with Him.

4. Which of the following best describes the purpose of asking "What does this situation reveal about what you truly love or trust?" during an assessment?

  1. To identify the client's preferred coping mechanisms.
  2. To understand their immediate emotional reactions.
  3. To uncover potential idols of the heart.
  4. To evaluate their problem-solving skills.

Answer: c. To uncover potential idols of the heart. This question directly aims at discerning what the client might be placing their ultimate hope, trust, or affection in, apart from or above God, which is the definition of idolatry from a biblical perspective.

Key Takeaways

  • Biblical assessment transcends surface-level presenting issues, intentionally seeking the core problems of the heart that drive behavior and emotional distress.
  • Mastering insightful, open-ended questions is crucial for uncovering unbiblical beliefs, desires, and motives, guiding clients toward self-reflection rather than mere symptom reporting.
  • A comprehensive biblical framework for assessment examines desires, motives, idols, unbiblical beliefs, responses to circumstances, relationship with God, and understanding of self.
  • Structured intake forms and questionnaires are valuable tools for gathering essential information, but they must be complemented by prayerful discernment and empathetic conversation.
  • Understanding a client's unique story and worldview through the lens of Scripture is fundamental to effectively 'Orienting' them to God's truth and facilitating lasting transformation.

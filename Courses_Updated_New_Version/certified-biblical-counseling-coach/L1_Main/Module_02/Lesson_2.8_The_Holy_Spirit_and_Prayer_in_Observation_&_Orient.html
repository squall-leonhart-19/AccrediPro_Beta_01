Practice Coaching Script: Initiating Observation & Orientation

Here's a script demonstrating how to approach Maria with empathy, active listening, and gentle orientation. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying Grace Williams' warm and professional tone.

Your Coaching Dialogue

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being so open and vulnerable with me. I hear a deep longing in your words â€“ a desire for peace, for connection with God, and to understand your purpose. Many women in this stage of life experience similar feelings, and it takes immense courage to seek help. What you're feeling is real, and God cares deeply about it."
MARIA:
"It just feels silly sometimes. I'm a Christian, I should know better. I should be stronger."
YOU:
"I understand that feeling, Maria. It's easy to get caught in the 'shoulds.' But God meets us right where we are, not where we 'should' be. What I'm hearing is that despite your efforts â€“ reading, praying, serving â€“ there's still a gap. Can you tell me a little more about what 'losing your purpose' feels like for you?"
MARIA:
"Well, for so long, my kids were my world. My ministry, almost. Now they're grown, and I'm just... adrift. I serve at church, but it feels like I'm just going through the motions. And my son, he's making choices that just break my heart, and I feel powerless."
YOU:
"Thank you for sharing that, Maria. It sounds like a significant transition, and it's natural to grieve the changes in your role. I hear a lot of love for your children, and also a deep concern that weighs heavily on you. When you say you feel 'powerless' and 'adrift,' where do you feel that in your body? And what thoughts come to mind when those feelings surface?"
YOU:
"Maria, it seems like there might be a connection between your deep love for your children and the feeling of losing purpose. It's wonderful to invest in our families, but sometimes our identity can become overly tied to those roles. The Bible speaks a lot about where our true identity and purpose come from. Would you be open to exploring what God's Word says about our identity in Christ, and how that might bring peace to your concerns about your children?"
MARIA:
"Yes, I really would. I feel like I've tried everything else."
YOU:
"Wonderful. That's a great starting point for us. For our next session, let's explore Psalm 139 and Philippians 3, and we'll discuss how these truths can anchor your heart and reshape your perspective on purpose and peace."
Coach Tip

Notice how the conversation gently transitions from Maria's feelings to a biblical orientation. This isn't about giving quick answers, but about planting seeds of truth that the Holy Spirit can water. Always seek permission before introducing specific scripture or biblical concepts.

Refine Your Coaching Voice

This is where your learning truly solidifies. Follow these steps to practice the script:

1

Read the script silently first

Understand the flow, the empathy, and the strategic questions. Pay attention to the shift from listening to gentle orientation.

2

What felt natural? What felt awkward? Where could you improve your phrasing or delivery? Repeat the script, focusing on those areas. The more you practice, the more authentic your coaching voice will become.

Coach Tip

The phrase "Would you be open to exploring what God's Word says...?" is a powerful tool. It respects client autonomy while gently inviting them to a biblical perspective. This is a hallmark of effective biblical counseling coaching.

Common Mistakes in Observation & Orientation

As you begin, it's natural to make mistakes. Recognizing them is the first step to improvement. Here are some common pitfalls new coaches encounter:

Premature Advice-Giving

Jumping straight to solutions or scripture verses before fully understanding the client's heart and context. This can make clients feel unheard.

Surface-Level Listening

Focusing only on the stated problem without digging deeper for underlying spiritual or emotional roots. Maria's "emptiness" isn't just about her kids, but her identity.

Leading Questions

Asking questions that push the client towards your own conclusion rather than allowing them to explore their thoughts freely. Stick to open-ended questions.

Failing to Orient Biblically

Staying solely in the realm of feelings or practical advice without gently introducing God's perspective. Remember, our ultimate goal is Christ-centered transformation.

Over-Spiritualizing

Attributing every problem directly to a specific sin without first listening and discerning the client's experience. This can be alienating and unhelpful.

Coach Tip

In my practice, I've found that patience is key. The "Observe & Orient" phase can't be rushed. It's about building trust and creating a safe space for the client to truly open up to themselves and to God's truth.

Markers of a Successful Observation & Orientation Session

How do you know if you've done well in this initial phase? Look for these indicators:

  • Client feels truly heard: They express feeling understood, or you notice a softening in their demeanor.
  • Deeper issues begin to surface: The conversation moves beyond surface-level complaints to uncover underlying beliefs, emotions, or spiritual struggles.
  • Client is engaged and reflective: They are actively participating, thinking through your questions, and offering more insights.
  • Openness to biblical truth: The client expresses willingness or even eagerness to explore what the Bible says about their situation, as Maria did.
  • Clear next steps are established: You've mutually agreed on a focus area or a specific biblical concept to explore further.
Coach Tip

You're not just learning techniques; you're cultivating a heart for people, rooted in God's love. Your empathy, discernment, and gentle guidance are powerful tools in His hands. Keep practicing, and you'll see amazing fruit!

Check Your Understanding

1

Which of Maria's statements indicates a potential performance-based faith?

Maria's statement, "I'm a Christian, I should know better. I should be stronger," strongly suggests a performance-based faith, where she believes her spiritual standing is tied to her actions or emotional fortitude rather than God's grace. Her goal of "doing enough" also points to this.
2

What is the primary purpose of asking Maria, "Can you tell me a little more about what 'losing your purpose' feels like for you?"

The primary purpose of this question is to engage in active listening and encourage Maria to elaborate, moving beyond a simple statement to deeper feelings and experiences. It helps the coach understand the nuances of her emotional state and begin to discern underlying heart issues related to identity and fulfillment.
3

What biblical concept is the coach gently introducing when asking if Maria would be open to exploring "what God's Word says about our identity in Christ"?

The coach is gently introducing the biblical concept of identity in Christ, which is central to Christian faith and often contrasts with worldly sources of identity (like roles, achievements, or relationships). This helps re-orient Maria's focus from her children and her "doing" to her inherent worth and purpose found in God.
4

Which of the following would be an example of "premature advice-giving" in this scenario?

An example of premature advice-giving would be if the coach immediately responded to Maria's "emptiness" with, "You need to read your Bible more and pray harder," or "Just trust God with your kids." This would bypass the crucial observation phase and fail to validate Maria's feelings and uncover the specific root issues she's facing.

Key Takeaways

  • Active listening is foundational: Truly hearing your client's words, tone, and body language is paramount for discerning their heart.
  • Open-ended questions reveal deeper truths: Strategic questions encourage clients to explore their feelings and beliefs, moving beyond surface issues.
  • Gentle orientation is key: Introduce biblical truths with permission, respecting the client's autonomy while inviting them to God's perspective.
  • Identify spiritual root issues: Look beyond presenting problems to uncover underlying beliefs, idols, or misunderstandings of God's character.
  • Practice builds confidence: Regular practice, especially out loud, will refine your coaching voice and empower you to serve clients effectively.

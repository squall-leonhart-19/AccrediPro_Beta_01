    Coaching Script: Welcome & Observe

    This script demonstrates how you might begin your first session with Eleanor, integrating the Welcome and Observe phases. Practice speaking this dialogue out loud, paying attention to your tone, pacing, and presence.

    Your Coaching Dialogue with Eleanor

    YOU:
    "Eleanor, thank you for being so courageous and open in sharing what's on your heart. What you're experiencing â€“ this sense of spiritual dryness and weariness â€“ is far more common than you might realize, especially after profound loss. I want you to know that you are in a safe, judgment-free space here, and I'm truly honored to walk alongside you."
    ELEANOR:
    "Thank you, Grace. I just... I feel so ashamed sometimes, like a bad Christian for not being able to 'snap out of it.' Everyone at church says God is good, and I know He is, but I just don't feel it."
    YOU:
    "Eleanor, your feelings are valid, and they are a sign of a deep longing for God, not a lack of faith. God meets us in our weariness and our questions. My role isn't to fix you, but to help you listen to what God might be revealing in this season, and to help you rediscover His presence and purpose for you. Does that resonate with what you're hoping for?"
    ELEANOR:
    "Yes, that's exactly it. I just... I'm tired of feeling this way. I want to feel useful again, connected."
    YOU:
    "I hear that desire for connection and purpose very clearly. Let's delve a little deeper into what life looks like for you right now. Could you tell me about a typical day for you? From the moment you wake up to when you go to bed, what does your day entail? And as you share, pay attention to any moments where you feel a flicker of peace, or perhaps a heavier sense of burden."
    ELEANOR:
    "Well, I wake up around 6, make coffee, read my Bible for about 30 minutes, then get ready... I usually have some volunteer work or errands, maybe a friend for lunch. Evenings are quiet, sometimes lonely. I try to pray before bed, but my mind often wanders."
    YOU:
    "Thank you for that detailed picture. It sounds like you have a structured routine, yet there's an underlying current of loneliness and a struggle to focus spiritually. When you mention your mind wandering during prayer, what thoughts or feelings usually surface for you?"
    ELEANOR:
    "It's usually thoughts about my husband, or worries about the future, or just a general sense of 'what's the point?' Sometimes I feel a pang of guilt that I'm not enjoying my Bible reading like I used to."
    YOU:
    "That's very insightful, Eleanor. It sounds like grief is still very present, and it's intertwined with your spiritual experience. The 'what's the point' feeling can be incredibly heavy. In those moments, what's the deepest desire of your heart? What do you truly yearn for God to do or show you?"
    Coach Tip

    Notice how I used open-ended questions ("Could you tell me...", "What thoughts or feelings...", "What's the deepest desire...") to encourage Eleanor to elaborate. This is crucial for deep observation. Avoid "yes/no" questions.

    Common Mistakes & Success Markers

    It's natural to encounter challenges in early coaching sessions. Recognizing common pitfalls and aiming for specific markers of success will accelerate your growth.

    Common Mistakes During Welcome & Observe:

    1. Talking Too Much: New coaches often feel the need to fill silence or offer advice too early. Remember, the Observe phase is for listening, not lecturing.
    2. Jumping to Solutions: It's tempting to immediately offer biblical answers. However, without deep observation, your counsel may miss the true root of the client's struggle, leading to superficial solutions.
    3. Judgmental Language or Tone: Even subtle cues can make a client feel unsafe. Avoid phrases that imply spiritual weakness or failure, and ensure your tone is consistently compassionate.
    4. Lack of Active Listening: Not truly hearing what the client is saying, or missing the emotions behind their words. This often manifests as asking questions that were already answered or failing to reflect back what was heard.
    5. Ignoring the Spiritual Dimension: Focusing solely on practical problems without discerning the underlying spiritual battle or disconnect.
    Coach Tip

    A good rule of thumb for the Observe phase: listen 80% of the time and speak 20% of the time. Your client holds the answers; your job is to help them uncover them through thoughtful questioning and prayerful discernment.

    Markers of a Successful Welcome & Observe Session:

    • Client Feels Heard and Understood: They express a sense of relief or validation, feeling that you "get" them.
    • Client Feels Safe: They are willing to share vulnerable details, indicating trust has been established.
    • Clearer Picture of Presenting Issue: You have a more nuanced understanding of their situation, beyond the initial complaint.
    • Initial Identification of Spiritual Themes: You've discerned patterns of spiritual struggle, theological misunderstandings, or areas of spiritual growth.
    • Client Expresses Hope: Even if their situation is difficult, they leave with a sense of hope that change is possible with God's help.
    • Mutual Agreement on Next Steps: You and the client are aligned on the direction for future sessions, moving towards the "Relate & Renew" phase.

    Check Your Understanding

    Test your grasp of the Welcome and Observe phases based on Eleanor's scenario and the best practices discussed.

    Check Your Understanding

    1. What is the primary purpose of Grace's initial statement to Eleanor ("Eleanor, thank you for being so courageous...") in the script?

    The primary purpose is to establish a safe, empathetic, and non-judgmental space, validating Eleanor's feelings and courage. This aligns with the "Welcome" phase, fostering trust and psychological safety.

    2. When Eleanor says, "I feel so ashamed sometimes, like a bad Christian for not being able to 'snap out of it'," what biblical counseling principle should Grace immediately bring to mind?

    Grace should recall the principle of "Compassionate Truth-Telling" and the reality of human brokenness and suffering (e.g., Psalm 34:18, Romans 7:15-20). It's crucial to distinguish between genuine sin and the natural struggles of a fallen world, including grief and spiritual dryness, affirming God's grace and presence in suffering.

    3. Grace asks, "Could you tell me about a typical day for you... And as you share, pay attention to any moments where you feel a flicker of peace, or perhaps a heavier sense of burden." What is Grace trying to achieve with this specific question?

    Grace is using an open-ended question to gather comprehensive data about Eleanor's daily life, which is a core part of the "Observe" phase. More importantly, she's guiding Eleanor to self-observe and identify patterns of emotional and spiritual experience, discerning potential "flickers of peace" (glimmers of hope/God's grace) or "heavier burdens" (areas of struggle/idolatry/unbelief) within her routine. This helps pinpoint specific areas for future coaching.

    4. If Eleanor seemed hesitant to share more about her "what's the point?" feelings, what would be an appropriate next step for Grace, demonstrating active listening and empathy?

    An appropriate step would be to acknowledge her hesitation with empathy, e.g., "It sounds like that's a really tender and difficult area to talk about, Eleanor. There's no pressure to share anything you're not ready for. Perhaps we can explore what's making it feel heavy right now, or we can simply sit with that feeling for a moment." This respects her boundaries while offering continued support.
    Coach Tip

    You're not just asking questions; you're inviting the Holy Spirit into the conversation. Pray for discernment before, during, and after each session. It's His wisdom that truly guides your observation and assessment.

    Key Takeaways

    Key Takeaways
    • The Welcome phase establishes a Christ-centered, non-judgmental sanctuary where clients feel safe to share their deepest struggles.
    • The Observe phase is characterized by intentional listening, asking open-ended questions, and prayerful discernment to uncover root spiritual issues.
    • Effective observation goes beyond surface-level complaints, seeking to understand the client's spiritual condition, emotional state, and daily patterns.
    • Avoiding common mistakes like premature advice or judgmental language is crucial for building trust and maintaining a biblical coaching posture.
    • Successful intake sessions result in clients feeling heard, understood, hopeful, and a clearer understanding of their journey towards Christ-centered transformation.

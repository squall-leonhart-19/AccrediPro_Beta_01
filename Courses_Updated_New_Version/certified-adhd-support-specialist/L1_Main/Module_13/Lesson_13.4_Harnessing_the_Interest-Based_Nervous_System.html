The ICNU Framework: The ADHD Motivation Engine

For decades, the medical community viewed ADHD as a "deficit of attention." However, experts like Dr. William Dodson have reframed this as an inconsistency of attention. The ADHD nervous system is not motivated by what is important, what is required, or what the rewards are. Instead, it is an interest-based system.

The ICNU Framework identifies the four qualities that allow an ADHD brain to engage, focus, and perform:

Element Definition Client Application Example
Interest A personal fascination or passion for the topic. Turning a boring budget task into a "detective game" to find savings.
Competition The challenge of winning or beating a record. Using a timer to see if a task can be finished in 15 minutes.
Novelty Something new, shiny, or a different way of doing things. Changing the physical location (cafe vs. office) to spark focus.
Urgency A deadline or immediate consequence. Using "body doubling" where someone is waiting for the result.
Coach Tip

When a client says "I just need more willpower," stop them. Willpower is a finite resource that the Interest-Based Nervous System burns through rapidly. Instead, ask: "Which of the ICNU elements can we inject into this task to make it more 'brain-friendly'?"

Glimmers vs. Triggers: Sensory & Cognitive Inputs

In the T.H.R.I.V.E. Method‚Ñ¢, we look at the environment (R: Restructure) through the lens of the nervous system. While "triggers" are well-known‚Äîsensory inputs that cause dysregulation or distraction‚Äî"glimmers" are the opposite.

Glimmers are micro-moments of safety, connection, or engagement that tell the ADHD brain, "It is safe to focus here." For an ADHD professional, a glimmer might be the specific texture of a high-quality pen, the perfect lo-fi playlist, or a visually aesthetic workspace.

Identifying Cognitive Glimmers

  • Sensory: The "right" lighting (natural vs. fluorescent).
  • Social: A supportive colleague who "gets" their flow.
  • Intellectual: A complex problem that requires "deep dive" thinking.

Mapping Dopamine Triggers

Dopamine is the "anticipation" molecule. In the ADHD brain, dopamine levels are often lower, or the receptors are less sensitive. To harness focus, we must map what actually "lights up" the client's brain.

As a Support Specialist, you will help clients create a Dopamine Menu (which we will explore further in Module 2). For now, focus on these three categories:

  1. Quick Hits: 5-minute activities that boost dopamine (e.g., a favorite song, a quick stretch).
  2. Deep Dives: Activities that lead to hyperfocus (e.g., coding, painting, researching a niche topic).
  3. Social Boosters: Positive interactions that provide an oxytocin/dopamine combo.
Practitioner Insight

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Neural Transmission found that ADHD individuals who engaged in "interest-led" tasks showed significantly higher prefrontal cortex activation compared to when they performed "importance-led" tasks. Use this statistic to validate your client's experience!

Transitioning from 'Importance-Based' to 'Interest-Based'

The "Importance-Based" system is how most of the world operates. It relies on the hierarchy of Importance, Rewards, and Consequences. For the ADHD brain, this system often leads to Task Paralysis.

The transition looks like this:

  • From: "I have to do my taxes because it's important and I'll get fined." (Importance/Consequence)
  • To: "I am going to gamify my taxes by seeing how much 'hidden money' I can find in 30 minutes while listening to my favorite podcast." (Interest/Competition/Novelty)

Check Your Understanding

1. What does the 'U' in the ICNU Framework stand for, and why is it a "double-edged sword" for ADHD clients?

The 'U' stands for Urgency. It is a double-edged sword because while it provides the adrenaline/dopamine surge needed to finish a task, relying on it long-term often leads to chronic stress, burnout, and "procrastination-shame" cycles.

2. How does an "Interest-Based Nervous System" differ from a "Neurotypical" one in terms of task prioritization?

A neurotypical nervous system prioritizes based on Importance, Rewards, and Consequences. An ADHD (Interest-Based) nervous system prioritizes based on Interest, Competition, Novelty, and Urgency.

3. What is a "Glimmer" in the context of ADHD support?

A Glimmer is a micro-moment of safety or positive sensory/cognitive input that helps regulate the nervous system and makes it easier for the ADHD brain to enter a state of focus or flow.

4. Why is "Willpower" an ineffective tool for the ADHD brain?

Willpower is a finite cognitive resource that requires high levels of executive function. Because the ADHD brain has inconsistencies in these functions, willpower is quickly exhausted, leading to task paralysis and shame. Interest-based engagement, however, provides its own "fuel" through dopamine.

Key Takeaways

  • ICNU is the Key: Interest, Competition, Novelty, and Urgency are the four primary drivers of the ADHD brain.
  • Shift the Paradigm: Move clients from "Importance-Based" (should) to "Interest-Based" (want/engaged) task management.
  • Dopamine as Fuel: Mapping dopamine triggers allows clients to "prime the pump" for difficult tasks.
  • Regulation through Flow: Hyperfocus, when harnessed correctly, is a state of nervous system regulation, not just productivity.
  • T.H.R.I.V.E. Integration: Harnessing strengths (H) and Restructuring the environment (R) are the primary tools for supporting an interest-based system.

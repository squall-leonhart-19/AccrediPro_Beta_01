YOU:
"I hear that. Let's shrink the mountain. We aren't going to 'Launch the Business' today. We are just going to find that login. That is the whole win. On a scale of 1 to 10, how 'doable' does just finding the login feel?"

Practice Tip

Notice how you didn't ask her why she hasn't done it. Asking "why" often triggers a shame spiral. Instead, you asked "How doable is this?" which shifts the brain into problem-solving mode.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • The "Just Do It" Trap: Telling an ADHD client to "just start" is like telling a person with a broken leg to "just walk." Focus on the accommodation, not the willpower.
  • Over-Explaining the Science: While it's good to know why the brain struggles, in the moment of paralysis, the client needs action, not a lecture on dopamine receptors.
  • Setting the Bar Too High: If the client says they can do it, but their body language looks tense, the step is still too big. Shrink it again.
  • Ignoring the Emotional Component: If Elena is crying, don't push the micro-step yet. Validate the emotion first, then return to the initiation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask the client to rate a task's "doability" on a scale of 1-10?

It forces the executive brain to evaluate the task objectively. If the answer is below an 8, it signals to the coach that the task must be broken down into even smaller micro-steps to bypass the brain's "threat" response.

2. What is the primary benefit of "Body Doubling" during a session?

It provides external regulation. The presence of another person (the coach) helps the client stay anchored to the task and reduces the "activation energy" required to start.

3. True or False: You should wait for the client to feel "motivated" before suggesting they start the task.

False. In ADHD coaching, we teach that action often precedes motivation. We use micro-steps to generate a small "dopamine win," which then creates the motivation to continue.

4. Which phrase is more effective: "Why haven't you started?" or "What is the smallest physical movement you can make toward this goal?"

The second phrase. It is non-judgmental and focuses on "lowering the bar" to physical movement, which is the key to overcoming executive dysfunction.

Key Takeaways

  • Shrink the Mountain: If a client is stuck, the task is too big. Keep breaking it down until it feels "easy."
  • Be the Anchor: Your presence as a coach acts as an external prefrontal cortex for the client during the initiation phase.
  • Validate, Don't Litigate: Acknowledge the struggle without asking for a list of excuses.
  • Action Creates Momentum: One tiny "win" (like finding a login) can break a week-long cycle of paralysis.

Welcome, Specialist

For the ADHD brain, "importance" is rarely enough to spark action. We require stimulation. In this lesson, we move from structural scaffolding to psychological engineering. You will learn how to help your clients "hack" their neurochemistry using gamification and dopamine hooks to turn the "Wall of Awful" into a playground of possibility.

Learning Objectives
  • Define the neurobiological role of dopamine in the ADHD "Interest-Based Nervous System."
  • Implement Temptation Bundling to link low-stimulation tasks with high-reward activities.
  • Design gamification strategies using timers, points, and "beat the clock" mechanics.
  • Construct a personalized "Dopamine Menu" to facilitate pre-task stimulation.
  • Identify and mitigate "Hyperfocus Traps" that occur when using rewards for initiation.

The Interest-Based Nervous System

As we discussed in Module 1, the ADHD brain operates on an Interest-Based Nervous System. While the neurotypical brain can initiate tasks based on importance, rewards, or consequences, the ADHD brain requires one of four triggers: Interest, Novelty, Challenge, or Urgency (I.N.C.U.).

Dopamine is the neurotransmitter of anticipation. It is not necessarily about the pleasure of finishing a task; it is the chemical signal that says, "This is worth doing." In ADHD, the baseline tonic levels of dopamine are lower, and the phasic release (the spikes) is often blunted for mundane tasks. Gamification and dopamine hooks are the tools we use to artificially create that "worth doing" signal.

Coach Tip

When explaining this to clients, use the "Spark Plug" analogy. ADHD isn't a lack of fuel (willpower); it's a faulty spark plug. Dopamine hooks provide the spark needed to ignite the engine of action.

Temptation Bundling: The Power of "And"

Coined by behavioral economist Katherine Milkman, Temptation Bundling is the practice of coupling a "want" (a high-dopamine activity) with a "should" (a low-dopamine task). For the ADHD client, this transforms a chore from a drain on executive function into a vehicle for reward.

Gamifying the Mundane

Gamification is the application of game-design elements in non-game contexts. For ADHD, this works by shifting the focus from the outcome to the process.

1. Beat the Clock (Urgency Hack)

Set a timer for 10 minutes and see how many dishes can be washed. This creates "artificial urgency," triggering the I.N.C.U. system. The goal isn't just to finish; it's to "win" against the timer.

2. Point Systems & Levelling Up

Assign points to tasks based on their "Wall of Awful" height.

  • Opening mail: 5 points
  • Making a doctor's appointment: 20 points
  • Cleaning the fridge: 50 points
Accumulating 100 points "unlocks" a guilt-free evening of a favorite hobby.

Gamification Element ADHD Brain Trigger Practical Application
Timers/Sprints Urgency 15-minute "Power Hour" with a loud alarm.
Random Rewards Novelty Using a "Reward Wheel" app after finishing a task.

Coach Tip

Encourage clients to use "The 10-Minute Rule." Tell them they only have to do the task for 10 minutes. If they want to stop then, they can. Usually, the dopamine hook of starting is enough to keep them going.

The Dopamine Menu (Dopamenu)

A "Dopamine Menu" is a curated list of activities that provide a healthy stimulation boost. It prevents the client from falling into "scrolling paralysis" when they need a break or a pre-task boost. A well-structured menu includes:

  • Appetizers (5-10 mins): Quick boosts like jumping jacks, a favorite song, or a quick stretch.
  • Entrees (30-60 mins): Deeply satisfying activities like a hobby, a walk in nature, or a deep conversation.
  • Sides (Passive): Things to do while working (fidgets, music, background noise).
  • Desserts (High Dopamine/Risk): Social media, video games, or TV. These must be timed and used sparingly.
Coach Tip

Ask your client to physically write or print their Dopamenu and post it where they usually get stuck (e.g., the fridge or the office door). Digital menus are often "out of sight, out of mind."

Avoiding Hyperfocus Traps

When we use high-dopamine rewards to initiate, we risk the Hyperfocus Trap. This occurs when the "reward" becomes so stimulating that the client cannot transition back to the intended task.

Strategies for Safe Hooking:

  • External Transitions: Use an alarm that requires physical movement to turn off (placed across the room).
  • Low-Friction Rewards: Choose rewards that have a natural "end state" (e.g., one chapter of a book, rather than infinite scrolling).
  • Somatic Check-ins: Teaching the client to recognize the "buzz" of hyperfocus and use a "bridge" activity (like drinking a glass of water) to transition.

Coach Tip

Remind clients that "Good is better than perfect." Gamification is a tool, not a new source of shame. If they forget to use their points for a day, that's just part of the ADHD experience. Reset and restart.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Importance" often insufficient for task initiation in the ADHD brain?

ADHD brains operate on an Interest-Based Nervous System, which requires Interest, Novelty, Challenge, or Urgency (I.N.C.U.) to trigger the necessary dopamine release for initiation. Importance is a "consequence-based" motivator that often fails to bridge the dopamine gap.

2. What is the core mechanism of "Temptation Bundling"?

It involves coupling a high-dopamine "want" activity with a low-dopamine "should" task, ensuring the reward is experienced simultaneously with the effort.

3. What category in a Dopamine Menu should be used as a "background" while working?

"Sides" (Passive stimulation) like fidget toys, specific music playlists, or brown noise help maintain baseline stimulation without distracting from the primary task.

4. How can a practitioner help a client avoid a "Hyperfocus Trap"?

By encouraging rewards with natural end-points, using external alarms that require physical movement, and teaching somatic awareness to recognize when they are "stuck" in a reward.

Key Takeaways

  • Dopamine is Anticipation: ADHD initiation struggles are often chemical, not moral; hooks provide the necessary "spark."
  • I.N.C.U. Framework: Always look for ways to add Interest, Novelty, Challenge, or Urgency to mundane tasks.
  • The Dopamenu: Having a pre-written list of stimulants prevents "decision fatigue" and scrolling paralysis.
  • Gamification Shifting: Focus on the process (beating the clock) rather than the heavy weight of the final outcome.
  • Strategic Bundling: Link the "want" and "should" to make the boring tasks inherently rewarding.

The Practice Script: Navigating the Grief Cycle

When a woman in her 50s receives an ADHD diagnosis, she doesn't just need "tips for focus." She needs to process the grief of the unlived life. Practice the following dialogue out loud. Don't just read it‚Äîfeel the pauses and the tone.

ELENA:
"I look at my office and I just want to cry. I was a Principal! I managed hundreds of people and a multi-million dollar budget. Why can't I just sit down and write this one-page business plan? I feel like I've lost my mind."
YOU (The Specialist):
"Elena, I want you to take a deep breath. What you're feeling isn't a loss of ability; it's the loss of external scaffolding. For 25 years, the school system provided the structure your ADHD brain needed to thrive. Now, you're trying to build that structure from scratch while carrying 50 years of 'why can't I just do this' shame. Does that resonate?"
ELENA:
"It does... but it still feels like an excuse. I should be better than this at my age."
YOU (The Specialist):
"We don't use ADHD as an excuse; we use it as an explanation. If you were a principal who suddenly lost her glasses, would you call it an 'excuse' that you couldn't read the budget? Of course not. Your ADHD is the same‚Äîit‚Äôs a biological reality. My job is to help you find your 'mental glasses' so you can see your path forward clearly again."
Olivia's Insight

Notice how the script uses the "Glasses Metaphor." For women our age, who pride themselves on competence, biological reframing is the only way to bypass the shame wall. Always move from character flaws to brain biology.

Common Practitioner Pitfalls

When you are starting out, your empathy can sometimes lead you into "traps" that actually disempower the client. Review these common mistakes I see in new ADHD Support Specialists:

The Pitfall Why It Happens The Professional Correction
The "Fix-It" Trap You want to take away their pain immediately. Validate the pain first. "It makes sense that you feel this way."
Over-Explaining Science Imposter syndrome makes you want to prove you're an expert. Keep science "bite-sized." Use it only to support the client's experience.
The "Cheerleader" Voice Trying to be overly positive to mask the client's grief. Use a "Warm Authority" voice. Acknowledge the hard stuff without sugar-coating.
Working Harder than the Client Feeling responsible for the client's immediate success. Set a goal, but let the client lead the "how." You are the guide, not the engine.
Olivia's Insight

If you find yourself exhausted after a session, you are likely in the "Fix-It" trap. Remember: You aren't there to organize Elena's office; you are there to help Elena understand why her office is disorganized so she can solve it herself.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

A successful session with an ADHD client doesn't always end with a finished to-do list. In fact, the "Internal Success Markers" are often more important for long-term progress.

  • Shift from Shame to Curiosity: The client says, "I wonder why that task felt so hard?" instead of "I'm so lazy."
  • The "Exhale": You see a physical softening of the shoulders when you validate their struggle.
  • Agency: The client proposes one small, tiny "micro-win" for the week without you suggesting it.
  • Boundary Clarity: The client understands that you are there to support their brain, not "cure" their personality.
Olivia's Insight

I always look for the "Exhale." When a client finally stops holding their breath and realizes they aren't "broken," that is the moment the real work begins. That's why we do this work.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Glasses Metaphor" particularly effective for late-diagnosed women?

It reframes a perceived character flaw (laziness/disorganization) as a biological need for support (like a visual impairment), which bypasses the shame response and allows for problem-solving.

2. What is the primary difference between a "Cheerleader" and a "Warm Authority" voice?

A cheerleader uses toxic positivity to mask the client's pain, while a Warm Authority acknowledges the reality of the struggle with empathy while maintaining professional expertise and boundaries.

3. If Elena says "I should be better than this at my age," what is the most professional response?

Validate and Reframe. Acknowledge that her feelings make sense given her history, but explain that her ADHD brain requires external scaffolding that she hasn't had to build for herself before now.

4. What is a key indicator that a practitioner is "working harder than the client"?

The practitioner is providing all the solutions, doing all the "thinking," and feels physically/emotionally exhausted after the session, while the client remains passive.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Validation is the "pre-requisite" for any ADHD strategy to work; without it, shame will block progress.
  • Focus on reframing "character flaws" into "biological explanations" using simple, relatable metaphors.
  • Avoid the "Fix-It" trap by maintaining your role as a guide rather than a problem-solver.
  • Your value as a specialist lies in your ability to provide the "scaffolding" the client's brain lacks.
  • Embrace the premium support model‚Äîyour expertise changes lives and deserves professional compensation.

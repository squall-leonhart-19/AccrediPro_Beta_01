Moving from Deficit to Difference

For over 50 years, the clinical world has viewed ADHD through the lens of the Medical Deficit Model. This model focuses exclusively on what the individual cannot do compared to a neurotypical peer. It uses words like "impairment," "dysfunction," and "disorder."

However, the Neuro-Affirming Strength Model suggests that ADHD is a naturally occurring variation in the human genome. A 2022 meta-analysis published in Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews suggests that ADHD traits may have provided significant evolutionary advantages in high-stimulation, high-risk environments.

The Interest-Based Nervous System (ICNU)

Dr. William Dodson, a pioneer in ADHD psychiatry, famously noted that the ADHD brain does not work on a system of importance or priority. Instead, it is an Interest-Based Nervous System. While the neurotypical brain can engage with a task simply because it is important, the ADHD brain requires specific neurochemical "keys" to unlock the prefrontal cortex.

The ICNU Framework

To help your clients engage, you must ensure the task or goal hits one of these four markers:

  • Interest: Does the task fascinate them?
  • Challenge: Is there a competitive or difficult element?
  • Novelty: Is this a new way of doing things?
  • Urgency: Is there a deadline or an immediate consequence?

ADHD as an Adaptive Trait

Why does ADHD exist in 5-8% of the global population? If it were purely a "defect," natural selection likely would have phased it out. The Hunter vs. Farmer Hypothesis, popularized by Thom Hartmann, suggests that ADHD traits were essential for survival in early human history.

Consider how "symptoms" translate into survival skills:

  • Distractibility: In a modern office, this is a deficit. In the wild, it is Environmental Hyper-Awareness (noticing the rustle of a predator in the grass).
  • Impulsivity: In a classroom, this is a problem. In a crisis, it is Rapid Decision Making without the paralysis of over-analysis.
  • Hyperfocus: This is the ability to ignore everything else to complete a high-stakes hunt or craft a vital tool.
Coach Tip: Evolutionary Reframing

When a client says, "I'm so easily distracted," respond with: "Your brain is designed to scan the environment for new information. It's a high-performance radar. Our job is to make sure the radar is pointed at the right targets."

The Psychology of Strength-Spotting

By the age of 12, a child with ADHD has received an estimated 20,000 more negative messages than their neurotypical peers. This leads to a collapse in Self-Efficacy‚Äîthe belief in one's ability to succeed.

When you practice "Strength-Spotting," you aren't just being "nice." You are performing a clinical intervention. Focusing on strengths increases dopamine and norepinephrine levels in the brain, which actually improves executive function. A brain that feels successful is a brain that can focus.

The Strength-First Coaching Philosophy

In the T.H.R.I.V.E. Method‚Ñ¢, we lead with strengths before we address "scaffolding" (the environmental supports we will cover in Module 3). This builds the client's "psychological capital."

The Strength-First Protocol:

  1. Identify: Use assessments to find innate neuro-talents (creativity, empathy, resilience).
  2. Label: Give the trait a powerful, non-clinical name (e.g., "The Divergent Thinker").
  3. Anchor: Recall a time the client used this trait to succeed.
  4. Apply: Use that same trait to solve a current struggle.
Coach Tip: The Practitioner's Mindset

Many of you coming from nursing or teaching backgrounds are used to "correcting" behavior. In ADHD coaching, your role is to be a Treasure Hunter. If you can find the gold in a client that everyone else has called "difficult," you will have a client for life.

Check Your Understanding

1. What does the ICNU acronym stand for in the context of the ADHD nervous system?

Interest, Challenge, Novelty, and Urgency. These are the four drivers that allow an ADHD brain to engage with a task.

2. According to the "Hunter vs. Farmer" hypothesis, what is "distractibility" reframed as?

Environmental Hyper-Awareness. It is the ability to monitor multiple stimuli simultaneously, which was a survival advantage for hunters.

3. Why does "Strength-Spotting" improve executive function neurochemically?

Focusing on successes and strengths increases the production of dopamine and norepinephrine, which are the primary neurotransmitters responsible for regulating the prefrontal cortex (the brain's "CEO").

4. How many more negative messages is a child with ADHD estimated to receive by age 12 compared to peers?

Approximately 20,000 more negative messages. This creates a significant deficit in self-esteem and self-efficacy that coaching must address.

Mastering the "Where" of Action

Welcome! As an ADHD Support Specialist, one of the most transformative shifts you can offer a client is moving support from the mind to the environment. We often tell ourselves "I'll remember to do that," but for the ADHD brain, intention is not enough. This lesson introduces the Point of Performance‚Äîthe exact time and place where a task must occur‚Äîand how proximity can bypass the executive function gap entirely.

Defining the 'Point of Performance'

Dr. Russell Barkley, a leading authority on ADHD, famously describes ADHD not as a disorder of knowing what to do, but of doing what you know. He argues that the solution must be "at the point of performance."

The Point of Performance (PoP) is the specific location and moment in time where an individual is expected to use a skill or complete a task. For example, the PoP for taking vitamins is the kitchen counter in the morning; the PoP for filing an invoice is the desk the moment the work is finished.

For a neurotypical brain, a reminder on a phone might be enough to trigger a walk to the other room to get a tool. For the ADHD brain, that walk is a "distraction gauntlet." By the time the client reaches the other room, they have seen three other things that need doing, and the original task is lost. Therefore, the tool must be at the PoP.

Coach Tip

When a client says "I just need to try harder," reframe it. Say: "We aren't going to try harder; we're going to make the environment do the work for you. If you aren't doing the task, the support isn't close enough to the Point of Performance."

The 'One-Step Rule'

Executive dysfunction often manifests as Activation Friction. This is the invisible resistance we feel when starting a task. Every physical or cognitive step required to start a task adds friction. Research suggests that for those with ADHD, the perceived effort of a task is often disproportionate to the actual effort required.

The One-Step Rule states that to ensure task initiation, the tools required for a task should be no more than one physical step (or one reach) away from the PoP. If a client needs to go to a different room, open a closet, and dig through a bin to find a gym shirt, they are 80% less likely to exercise than if the shirt is sitting on their pillow.

Task High Friction (Multi-Step) Low Friction (One-Step)
Morning Meds In a cabinet, behind the plates. On a tray next to the coffee maker.
Evening Journaling In a drawer across the room. On top of the phone charger.
Clearing Mail Walk mail to the home office. Shredder and bin placed directly by the front door.
Water Intake Walk to kitchen to refill a glass. A 32oz bottle at every "station" (desk, couch, bedside).

Strategic Tool Placement

We often place items based on where they "belong" according to traditional home organization (e.g., all medicine in the medicine cabinet). For the ADHD brain, this is a recipe for failure. We must place items based on usage patterns, not categories.

The "Vitamins by the Coffee" Principle

If a client drinks coffee every morning without fail, the coffee maker is a high-reliability anchor. Placing vitamins next to the coffee maker uses the existing dopamine-driven habit to trigger the new habit. This is a form of environmental "habit stacking."

Case Study: Sarah's Morning Rut

Client: Sarah, 48, transitioning from teaching to a freelance consulting career.

The Challenge: Sarah struggled to start her work day. She would spend 45 minutes "getting ready"‚Äîfinding her laptop charger, looking for her blue-light glasses, and clearing a spot on her kitchen table.

Intervention: We applied the PoP principle. We created a "Work Launchpad." Her laptop, charger (already plugged in), glasses, and a bottle of water were placed on the table the night before.

Outcome: Sarah's "startup time" dropped from 45 minutes to 2 minutes. By reducing activation friction, she preserved her precious morning executive function for actual work rather than preparation.

Stationary vs. Mobile Support: Go-Bags

ADHD doesn't just happen at a desk. Transitions‚Äîmoving from home to the office, or from the car to a meeting‚Äîare high-risk zones for executive function failure. This is where Localized Tool Kits and Go-Bags become essential.

  • Go-Bags: A pre-packed bag for a specific activity (Gym Bag, Work-from-Cafe Bag, Kids' Activity Bag) that stays packed. The rule is: Usage happens, replacement happens immediately, bag stays by the door.
  • Duplicate Kits: If a client works in two locations, they should not carry chargers, mice, or notebooks back and forth. They should have a "Stationary Kit" at both locations. The cost of a second $20 charger is far less than the "ADHD Tax" of a lost day of work because the charger was left at home.
Coach Tip

Encourage clients to invest in "The Power of Two." Having two sets of keys, two chargers, or two pairs of sunglasses at the points where they are most needed reduces the cognitive load of tracking items across space.

Reducing Activation Friction through Environmental Cues

Task initiation is often stalled by "Cognitive Overload." If the environment looks cluttered or disorganized, the brain perceives the task as "too big." We can use environmental cues to "pre-set" the brain for action.

Visual Priming: This involves setting out the physical materials for a task before the task is due to start.

  • The "Open Book" Method: If a client needs to study, they should leave the book open to the correct page on their desk before they go to bed.
  • The "Gym Clothes" Method: Placing sneakers directly in the path of the bathroom door so they must be stepped over.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "Point of Performance" (PoP)?

The Point of Performance is the specific location and exact time where a task must be executed. Dr. Russell Barkley emphasizes that for ADHD, support must be provided at this point to be effective.

2. Why is the "One-Step Rule" particularly important for the ADHD brain?

It minimizes "Activation Friction." Every extra step required to start a task increases the chance of distraction or cognitive overwhelm, which can lead to task paralysis or "forgetting" the original intention.

3. A client wants to start a daily yoga practice but forgets every morning. Where is the best PoP for her yoga mat?

Unrolled on the floor in the spot she usually stands to check her phone or drink her coffee. This uses "Visual Priming" and ensures the mat is no more than one step away from her morning routine.

4. How does a "Go-Bag" differ from a standard bag?

A Go-Bag is "pre-set" and dedicated to a specific activity. It remains packed with all necessary items so the client doesn't have to "find" things during the high-stress transition period before leaving.

Key Takeaways

  • ADHD is a disorder of performance, not knowledge; therefore, solutions must be externalized in the environment.
  • The Point of Performance is the only place where scaffolding truly works for the ADHD brain.
  • Reduce Activation Friction by following the One-Step Rule: keep tools within one step of where they are used.
  • Use Visual Priming to "pre-load" tasks, making the start of a task a simple physical reaction rather than a complex executive decision.
  • Stop carrying items between locations; use Duplicate Kits to eliminate transition-related loss and stress.

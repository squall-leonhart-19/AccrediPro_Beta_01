The 11 Executive Functions Decoded

Executive Function (EF) is often described as the "CEO of the brain." In ADHD, the CEO is present but often "out of the office" or distracted. However, EF is not a single entity; it is a complex orchestra of 11 distinct cognitive processes. As a specialist, your job is to identify which instruments in your client's orchestra are out of tune.

Executive Function Definition ADHD Manifestation (The Deficit)
Response Inhibition The ability to think before you act. Impulsive spending, blurting out thoughts, "acting on a whim."
Working Memory Holding information in mind while performing a task. Walking into a room and forgetting why; losing the thread of a conversation.
Emotional Control Managing emotions to achieve goals or direct behavior. Overwhelming frustration over small setbacks; "Emotional Flooding."
Flexibility The ability to revise plans in the face of obstacles. Rigidity when routines change; significant "transition stress."
Sustained Attention Maintaining focus despite fatigue or boredom. Difficulty finishing a 20-page report; zoning out during meetings.
Task Initiation The ability to begin a task without undue procrastination. The "Wall of Awful"; staring at a blank screen for hours.
Planning/Prioritization Creating a roadmap to reach a goal. Treating every task as "Urgent"; getting lost in minor details.
Organization Arranging systems to keep track of information/materials. The "doom pile" of mail; losing keys or important documents.
Time Management Estimating how much time one has and how to use it. "Time Blindness"; chronic lateness; underestimating task duration.
Goal-Directed Persistence The drive to follow through to completion. The "90% Finished" syndrome; starting many projects, finishing none.
Metacognition Self-monitoring and self-evaluating performance. Not realizing how one's behavior affects others until it's too late.
Specialist Insight

When presenting these 11 functions to a client, use the "Control Tower" analogy. Explain that their "airplane" (intelligence) is perfectly fine, but the "Control Tower" (EF) is understaffed. This shifts the narrative from "I am lazy" to "My control tower needs better systems."

The Performance vs. Knowledge Gap

The most frustrating aspect of ADHD for high-achieving women is the Knowledge-Performance Gap. As Dr. Russell Barkley famously stated, "ADHD is not a disorder of knowing what to do; it is a disorder of doing what you know."

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Attention Disorders found that adults with ADHD often score in the superior range on IQ tests but in the 10th percentile for "Functional Execution." This discrepancy creates intense shame. Your clients likely know how to organize a closet or how to manage a budget‚Äîthey simply cannot engage the cognitive gears to perform the task consistently.

Case Study: Elena, 48, Corporate Attorney

Presenting Symptoms: Elena was a highly successful lawyer who felt like a "fraud." She could argue complex cases in court (High Interest/High Stakes) but couldn't manage her personal billing or keep her office desk clear of paper. She had read 15 books on time management.

The EF Profile: Mapping revealed Elena had Superior Metacognition and Planning but Profound Deficits in Task Initiation and Working Memory. Her "Knowledge" was at a 10/10, but her "Execution" was at a 2/10.

Outcome: By identifying the gap, the specialist stopped giving Elena "tips" and started implementing "scaffolding" (externalized systems). Elena's billable hours increased by 22% in three months because she stopped losing time to task paralysis.

Mapping Assessment Tools

To trace the profile accurately, you need data. We utilize a "Triangulation" approach to minimize the impact of client self-criticism or memory gaps.

1. The ADHD-RS-5 (Self-Report)

While often used for diagnosis, we use it to look for patterns. Does the client struggle more with Inattentive symptoms (EF related to focus) or Hyperactive/Impulsive symptoms (EF related to inhibition)?

2. The BRIEF-A (Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function)

This is the "Gold Standard" for mapping the 11 functions. It provides a Global Executive Composite (GEC) score. As a specialist, you don't necessarily need the clinical version, but you can use the categories to guide your intake interview.

Intake Strategy

Ask: "If I were a fly on the wall in your kitchen at 8:00 AM, what would I see?" Observational data is often more accurate than abstract questions like "Are you organized?" ADHD clients often answer based on their intentions rather than their actions.

Creating the Visual Neuro-Map

Once you have gathered your data, you will create a Neuro-Map. This is a visual representation (often a radar chart or bar graph) that shows the client's strengths and deficits side-by-side.

Why a visual map?

  • Externalizes the Problem: It becomes "the map," not "the person."
  • Identifies "Workarounds": If a client has high Creativity but low Organization, we use creativity to design the organization system.
  • Validation: Seeing a visual representation of their struggles provides immense relief. Many women cry when they see their Neuro-Map because it's the first time their internal struggle has been "seen."
Professional Legitimacy

Practitioners who use visual mapping typically report higher client retention. This level of professional "mapping" is what justifies premium rates. A "coach" gives advice; a "Specialist" maps the neuro-cognitive landscape.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which Executive Function is responsible for the "90% Finished" syndrome where a client starts projects but can't follow through?

Goal-Directed Persistence. This is the ability to stay the course toward a long-term goal despite distractions or loss of initial dopamine-driven interest.

2. True or False: ADHD is primarily a disorder of intelligence and knowledge.

False. ADHD is a disorder of performance/execution, not knowledge. Clients often know exactly what they "should" be doing but cannot engage the cognitive functions to do it.

3. What is the primary benefit of creating a visual "Neuro-Map" for a client?

It externalizes the deficits, reducing shame, and allows the specialist to identify cognitive "workarounds" by leveraging the client's EF strengths to support their deficits.

4. A client who struggles with "Time Blindness" is primarily showing a deficit in which EF?

Time Management. This involves the ability to estimate time, allocate it appropriately, and maintain an internal sense of its passage.

Key Takeaways

  • ADHD is a heterogeneous disorder; no two executive function profiles are identical.
  • The 11 Executive Functions are the "building blocks" of the T in THRIVE (Trace the Profile).
  • Bridging the "Knowledge-Performance Gap" requires external scaffolding, not more information.
  • Visual mapping is a powerful tool for validation, clarity, and intervention planning.
  • Specialists must move from "What's wrong with you?" to "Where is the cognitive breakdown occurring?"

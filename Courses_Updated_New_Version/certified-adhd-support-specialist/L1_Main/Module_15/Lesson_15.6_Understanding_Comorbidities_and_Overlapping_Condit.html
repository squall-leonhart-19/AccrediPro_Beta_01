Welcome, Practitioner

In the world of ADHD support, "pure" ADHD is the exception, not the rule. Statistics suggest that up to 80% of adults with ADHD have at least one co-occurring condition. For the 40+ woman transitioning into this career, understanding these overlaps is your "superpower." It allows you to move beyond basic productivity coaching into deep, transformative support that honors the whole person.

Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish ADHD symptoms from those of Anxiety, Depression, and PTSD to avoid "mis-scaffolding."
  • Identify the unique "tug-of-war" experienced by AuDHD individuals (ADHD + Autism).
  • Explain the biological link between ADHD and Delayed Sleep Phase Syndrome (DSPS).
  • Analyze how Sensory Processing Disorder increases cognitive load and triggers task paralysis.
  • Establish clear "Red Flag" protocols for ethical referrals to clinical professionals.

The "Symptom Overlap" Challenge

Many clients arrive at your practice with a history of misdiagnosis. They may have been treated for Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) or Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) for years, only to find that their "anxiety" was actually the internal restlessness of ADHD, and their "depression" was executive function burnout.

As a Certified ADHD Support Specialist, your role is to Trace the Profile (the 'T' in T.H.R.I.V.E.) with extreme precision. Understanding these overlaps prevents us from applying strategies that might actually increase a client's distress.

Symptom ADHD Presentation Anxiety/Depression Overlap
Restlessness Driven by a need for stimulation/dopamine. Driven by fear, worry, or "impending doom."
Inattention Distractibility due to external or internal "noise." Rumination or intrusive thoughts pulling focus away.
Low Energy "Task Paralysis" or dopamine depletion after hyperfocus. Anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure) or heavy lethargy.
Sleep Issues Difficulty "shutting off" the brain due to interest. Waking up early with racing heart or ruminating.
Coach Tip: The "Why" Behind the What

Always ask the client: "When you feel you can't start a task, is it because you feel 'frozen' by the steps (ADHD), or because you are afraid of the outcome (Anxiety)?" The answer dictates whether you use Micro-Tasking (Module 4) or Somatic Regulation (Module 5).

The AuDHD Intersection: A Unique Tug-of-War

The term AuDHD refers to the co-occurrence of Autism Spectrum Disorder and ADHD. Until 2013 (DSM-5), these could not be diagnosed together. We now know they are frequently comorbid.

Supporting an AuDHD client requires a "Dual-Track" approach. The ADHD part of their brain craves novelty, spontaneity, and high stimulation. The Autistic part of their brain craves predictability, routine, and sensory regulation.

Case Study: Sarah, 48

Former Elementary Teacher pivoting to Freelance Writing

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah felt she was "failing at ADHD." She followed "ADHD hacks" to keep things exciting, but then felt overwhelmed and had sensory meltdowns. She felt "lazy" because she couldn't maintain the very routines she craved.

Intervention: We identified her AuDHD profile. Instead of high-stimulation "gamification" (ADHD), we built "Low-Demand Routines" (Autism) that allowed for "Interest-Based Deep Dives" (ADHD).

Outcome: Sarah stopped the shame cycle. She realized her need for "sameness" wasn't a lack of creativity, but a neurological requirement for safety. She now earns $180/hr as a specialist technical writer, leveraging her Autistic attention to detail and her ADHD ability to synthesize complex ideas.

Sleep Disorders and the Circadian Rhythm

It is estimated that 75% of adults with ADHD experience some form of sleep disturbance. This is not just "bad habits"; it is often a biological misalignment known as Delayed Sleep Phase Syndrome (DSPS).

Research indicates that the ADHD brain often experiences a 2-3 hour delay in the release of melatonin. While the neurotypical world expects productivity at 8:00 AM, the ADHD brain may not reach its "circadian peak" until 10:00 PM.

  • Executive Load: Sleep deprivation mimics ADHD symptoms, creating a "compounding deficit" where executive functions (working memory, inhibition) plummet.
  • The "Revenge Bedtime Procrastination": Clients often stay up late because it is the only time they feel "in control" of their environment without external demands.
Coach Tip: Environmental Scaffolding

Instead of fighting the 2-hour delay, help clients Restructure their Environment (Module 3) to accommodate it. If they are most productive at 9:00 PM, can they move their "admin tasks" to that window instead of forcing them at 9:00 AM?

Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD)

Many ADHD clients suffer from Sensory Over-Responsivity. Common irritants like flickering fluorescent lights, the hum of a refrigerator, or the texture of certain "work clothes" act as constant "pings" on their attention.

The Impact on Executive Function: If a client's brain is using 30% of its "bandwidth" just to filter out the sound of the AC unit, they only have 70% left for Task Initiation. This is why sensory regulation is a prerequisite for productivity coaching.

Ethical Boundaries: Recognizing 'Red Flags'

As an ADHD Support Specialist, you are a facilitator of change, not a therapist or a doctor. Understanding comorbidities means knowing when a client's needs exceed your scope of practice.

Scope of Practice: Referral Red Flags

If a client exhibits the following, you must refer to a licensed clinical professional:

  • Active Suicidality: Any mention of self-harm or plans to end life.
  • Severe Trauma/PTSD: If "Task Paralysis" is actually a "Freeze" response triggered by past trauma.
  • Active Substance Use Disorder: If the client is using substances to "self-medicate" in a way that is dangerous.
  • Eating Disorders: Especially if ADHD impulsivity is manifesting as dangerous binging or purging.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does the "Why" differ between ADHD restlessness and Anxiety-driven restlessness?

ADHD restlessness is typically driven by a biological need for dopamine and stimulation (the "Interest-Based Nervous System"), whereas Anxiety-driven restlessness is fueled by fear, worry, or a sense of impending threat.

2. What is the "circadian delay" often seen in ADHD brains?

It is often a 2-3 hour delay in the release of melatonin, leading to Delayed Sleep Phase Syndrome (DSPS), where the individual naturally feels more alert late at night and struggles with early morning starts.

3. Why is Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) considered a drain on Executive Function?

The brain must use significant cognitive "bandwidth" to filter out sensory irritants. This leaves less energy available for executive tasks like planning, initiation, and emotional regulation.

4. When should an ADHD Coach refer a client to a therapist?

When "Red Flags" appear, such as active suicidality, severe unmanaged PTSD, active substance use disorders, or clinical eating disorders that require medical or psychological intervention.

Key Takeaways

  • Comorbidity is the Norm: 80% of adults with ADHD have at least one other condition; tracing the profile requires looking for these overlaps.
  • AuDHD Balance: AuDHD clients require a mix of ADHD novelty-seeking and Autistic routine-seeking strategies.
  • Sleep is Biological: ADHD sleep issues are often rooted in circadian biology (melatonin delay), not just poor "sleep hygiene."
  • Sensory First: You cannot coach executive function effectively if the client's sensory environment is in a state of constant "alarm."
  • Ethics of Scope: Knowing when to refer out is a sign of professional expertise and protects both the client and the practitioner.

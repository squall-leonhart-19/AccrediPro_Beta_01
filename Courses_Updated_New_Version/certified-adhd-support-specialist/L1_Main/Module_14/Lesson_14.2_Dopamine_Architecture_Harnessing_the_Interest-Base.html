The Reservoir vs. The Spark: Tonic and Phasic Dopamine

To support clients effectively, you must understand that ADHD is not a "lack" of dopamine, but rather a dysregulation of its availability. In the neurotypical brain, dopamine functions like a well-regulated reservoir. In the ADHD brain, the reservoir is often low, requiring "sparks" to function.

Type Biological Role ADHD Presentation
Tonic Dopamine The "Baseline" reservoir. Regulates overall motivation and alertness. Typically chronically low. Leads to "doom-scrolling," lethargy, and searching for stimulation.
Phasic Dopamine The "Spike" or spark. Released in response to rewards, novelty, or threats. Hyper-reactive. The ADHD brain relies on these spikes to "jump-start" the executive functions.

A 2021 study published in Nature Reviews Neuroscience found that while tonic levels are low, the phasic response to novel stimuli can actually be higher in ADHD individuals. This explains why a client can be exhausted all day but suddenly "wake up" when a new, exciting project lands on their desk.

Tell your clients: "Your brain is like a smartphone with a battery that won't hold a charge (Tonic). You can't just 'will' the battery to be full. You have to plug it into a high-voltage charger (Phasic triggers) to get the screen to turn on. We aren't fixing the battery; we're finding your best chargers."

The Importance-Based vs. Interest-Based Paradigm

Most of the world operates on an Importance-Based Nervous System. This system prioritizes tasks based on three criteria: Importance, Rewards, and Consequences. When a neurotypical person sees a tax deadline, the importance triggers enough dopamine to initiate action.

The ADHD brain is Importance-Blind. No matter how "important" a task is, if it does not stimulate the nervous system, the dopamine required for task initiation is simply not released. Instead, the ADHD brain uses an Interest-Based Nervous System (IBNS).

The Five Pillars of IBNS (INCUP)

  • Interest: Is the task inherently fascinating?
  • Novelty: Is it new, shiny, or a different way of doing things?
  • Challenge: Is it a puzzle to be solved or a "dare" to be met?
  • Urgency: Is there an immediate deadline or a "fire" to put out?
  • Passion: Does it align with a deep core value or hyper-fixation?

Mapping Reward Triggers

As a Specialist, your job is to help the client map their unique "Dopamine Menu." Not every ADHD brain reacts to the same triggers. For some, Challenge is the primary driver; for others, it is purely Novelty.

Flow State & Hyperfocus Mastery

Hyperfocus is often touted as an ADHD "superpower," but in professional practice, we treat it as a high-performance state that requires management. Hyperfocus is a state of intense phasic dopamine release where the prefrontal cortex becomes highly filtered, blocking out external stimuli (and even internal cues like hunger or the need to use the restroom).

The Flow Transition Problem: The danger isn't the hyperfocus itself; it's the "crash" that follows. When the phasic spike ends, tonic dopamine often drops below baseline, leading to irritability, exhaustion, and "shame spirals" because the client cannot sustain that level of output.

Strategies for Managed Hyperfocus:

  • The "Landing Strip": Setting a timer for 15 minutes before they actually need to stop, allowing the brain to slowly disengage.
  • Somatic Check-ins: Using wearable tech (vibrating watches) to remind the client they have a body.
  • Externalizing the "Buffer": Scheduling a low-stakes task (like a walk or a shower) immediately following a deep-work session.

Leveraging Innate Talents as 'Engagement Anchors'

An Engagement Anchor is a high-dopamine activity used to "drag" a low-dopamine task across the finish line. This is a sophisticated form of "temptation bundling" tailored for neurodivergent architecture.

Example: A client who loves Novelty and Creative Writing (Talents) but hates Expense Reporting (Mundane).
The Anchor: They are only allowed to use their favorite fountain pen and expensive stationery (Novelty/Sensory) while doing the expenses, or they must "narrate" the expenses as a fictional character (Creativity).

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between Tonic and Phasic dopamine in the context of ADHD?

Tonic dopamine is the "baseline" reservoir which is typically low in ADHD, leading to chronic under-arousal. Phasic dopamine is the "spike" released in response to stimuli. The ADHD brain relies on Phasic spikes to jump-start executive functions because the Tonic levels are insufficient.

2. A client says, "I know my taxes are important, but I just can't start them." What is the neuro-biological explanation?

The client has an Interest-Based Nervous System (IBNS) and is "Importance-Blind." Because the task lacks Interest, Novelty, Challenge, Urgency, or Passion (INCUP), their brain is not releasing the dopamine required for task initiation, regardless of the task's objective importance.

3. What does the "C" in the INCUP model stand for?

The "C" stands for Challenge. This involves turning a mundane task into a puzzle, a game, or a competition to trigger a phasic dopamine release.

4. Why is a "Landing Strip" necessary for clients who experience hyperfocus?

Hyperfocus involves a massive phasic dopamine surge. When it ends abruptly, dopamine levels can crash below the already-low tonic baseline. A "Landing Strip" allows for a gradual transition, reducing irritability and the "post-hyperfocus crash."

Key Takeaways

  • The ADHD brain is not "unmotivated"; it is "un-aroused" due to low tonic dopamine levels.
  • The Interest-Based Nervous System (IBNS) ignores "importance" and responds only to Interest, Novelty, Challenge, Urgency, and Passion (INCUP).
  • Engagement Anchors use innate talents and high-dopamine activities to facilitate low-interest task completion.
  • Hyperfocus is a powerful tool but requires managed transitions to avoid biological and emotional crashes.
  • The Harness Protocol‚Ñ¢ provides a repeatable framework for specialists to help clients align their work with their neuro-chemistry.

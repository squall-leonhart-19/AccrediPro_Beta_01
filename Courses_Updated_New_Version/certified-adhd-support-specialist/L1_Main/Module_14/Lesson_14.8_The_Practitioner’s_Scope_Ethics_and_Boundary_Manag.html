Success Markers

How do you know the session was successful? Look for these "micro-wins" in your client:

  • Softening of Body Language: Shoulders dropping, deeper breaths.
  • Shift in Language: Moving from "I am lazy" to "My executive function was overloaded."
  • Curiosity Over Shame: Asking "Why does my brain do that?" instead of "What is wrong with me?"
Olivia's Insight

Don't be afraid of silence. In ADHD coaching, the client's brain often needs 5-10 seconds to process a deep question. If you jump in too soon, you cut off their breakthrough.

Check Your Understanding

1. When Elena says her diagnosis feels like an "excuse," what is the most professional coaching response?

Validate the feeling of fear, then reframe the diagnosis as an "explanation" rather than an excuse. This differentiates between avoiding responsibility and understanding the biological mechanism.

2. What is the primary risk of jumping straight into productivity hacks with a client like Elena?

It reinforces the "masking" behavior that led to her burnout. Without addressing the underlying shame and grief, hacks are just temporary bandages on a deeper wound.

3. True or False: ADHD coaching should focus solely on the future and ignore the client's past.

False. While coaching is future-oriented, late-diagnosed adults must often "re-process" their past through the lens of ADHD to move forward without the weight of past "failures."

4. Why is "silence" considered a high-level coaching tool in this module?

ADHD brains often have processing delays or "tangled" thoughts. Silence provides the necessary "cognitive runway" for the client to formulate an authentic answer rather than a masked one.

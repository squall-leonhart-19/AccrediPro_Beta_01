Analyzing the 8 Core Executive Functions

Executive functions (EF) are the "management system" of the brain. While most people refer to ADHD as a "focus problem," focus is merely one output of a complex multi-gear system. In advanced tracing, we evaluate the 8 pillars of EF as defined by the BRIEF-A (Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function) and the Barkley Deficits in Executive Functioning Scale.

Executive Function ADHD Presentation (The "Gap") Client Expression
Inhibition Impulse control; stopping a behavior. "I say things before I think them through."
Shift Cognitive flexibility; moving between tasks. "I get 'stuck' on a task and can't transition."
Emotional Control Modulating emotional responses. "Small frustrations feel like disasters."
Initiation Beginning a task or activity. "I know I need to start, but I just... can't."
Working Memory Holding information while using it. "I walk into a room and forget why I'm there."
Plan/Organize Anticipating future events; systematic approach. "I have the ideas, but no roadmap to finish them."
Organization of Materials Orderliness of work/living space. "I lose my keys daily despite having a 'spot' for them."
Self-Monitoring Assessing own performance/behavior impact. "I don't realize I've been talking for 20 minutes."
Coach Tip: The Multi-Gear Effect

When tracing a profile, look for "cascading failures." For example, a client may have Initiation issues that are actually caused by poor Planning. If they don't know the first step, they can't start. Always ask: "Is the problem the engine (Initiation) or the map (Planning)?"

Case Study: The High-Achieving Masker

Client: Elena, 52 ‚Ä¢ Former Corporate Executive

Presenting Symptoms: Severe burnout, chronic fatigue, and "sudden" inability to manage her household after leaving her high-stress job. Elena was diagnosed with ADHD at age 51.

The Profile: Elena has an IQ in the 98th percentile. For 30 years, her high intelligence allowed her to "brute force" her way through executive dysfunction. She used anxiety as a fuel source to compensate for poor initiation. However, once the external structure of her corporate job was removed, her internal executive functions (specifically Working Memory and Self-Monitoring) collapsed.

Intervention: Instead of "trying harder," we traced her profile to show that her Inhibition and Shift were actually strengths, but her Working Memory was in the 15th percentile. We stopped treating her as "lazy" and started treating her as a high-performance engine with a faulty fuel pump.

Identifying 'Spiky Profiles': The Masking Paradox

A "Spiky Profile" occurs when there is a significant discrepancy between a client's cognitive ability (IQ) and their executive functioning. In the neuro-typical population, these two usually correlate. In the ADHD population, they are often wildly disconnected.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Attention Disorders found that high-IQ adults with ADHD are 3.5 times more likely to be diagnosed late in life because their intelligence allows them to create sophisticated "workarounds." However, these workarounds come at a high metabolic cost, leading to what we call "ADHD Burnout."

Coach Tip: Validating the Spiky Profile

For your 40-55 year old female clients, this is the most empowering concept you can teach. Many feel like "imposters" because they were successful in one area but can't manage laundry. Explain that their "spikes" are real‚Äîthey are brilliant AND they have a disability. Both can be true simultaneously.

The Neuro-Biological Hardware: PFC & Basal Ganglia

To trace a profile accurately, you must understand the "hardware" involved. Advanced tracing looks at the interplay between the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) and the Basal Ganglia.

  • The Prefrontal Cortex (The CEO): Responsible for "top-down" regulation. It sets goals, monitors progress, and inhibits distractions. In the ADHD brain, the PFC often has lower blood flow and reduced dopamine receptor density.
  • The Basal Ganglia (The Auto-Pilot): Responsible for "bottom-up" habits and motor control. It is the part of the brain that allows you to drive home without thinking about it.

In ADHD, the communication between the CEO (PFC) and the Auto-Pilot (Basal Ganglia) is "noisy." This is why a client can know what to do (PFC) but can't make their body do it (Basal Ganglia). This is not a lack of willpower; it is a transmission failure.

Utilizing the THRIVE Assessment: Capacity vs. Performance

One of the most critical distinctions you will make as an AccrediPro Certified Specialist is between Capacity and Performance.

Capacity is the "can" (e.g., "Does the client have the cognitive ability to write this report?").
Performance is the "do" (e.g., "Can the client write this report right now in this environment?").

The T.H.R.I.V.E. Assessment Tool uses a 5-point scale to measure "Situational Performance." We ask the client to rate their executive function not in a vacuum, but in specific contexts (High Stress, Low Interest, High Interest, Social, Solitary).

Coach Tip: The Interest-Based Filter

When tracing, always check for "Interest-Based Nervous System" interference. If a client can focus for 6 hours on a hobby but not for 10 minutes on taxes, they don't have a Capacity issue; they have a Dopamine-Regulated Performance issue. This removes the "moral" failure of "not trying."

Communicating the Profile: Reducing the Shame Cycle

The final step in advanced tracing is the "Profile Reveal." This is where you present the map to the client. The goal is to move from shame ("What is wrong with me?") to strategy ("How does my brain work?").

Use the "Biological Map" language. Instead of saying "You are impulsive," say "Your PFC's inhibitory signals are currently being out-competed by the Basal Ganglia's reward-seeking signals." This externalizes the struggle. When the problem is biological, the solution can be mechanical (scaffolding and systems) rather than moral (trying harder).

Coach Tip: The "Owner's Manual" Analogy

Tell your clients: "You've been trying to drive a manual transmission car like an automatic for 40 years. No wonder the engine is smoking! We aren't fixing you; we're finally writing the owner's manual for your specific engine."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary cause of "Late-Life Diagnosis" in high-IQ individuals?

High intelligence allows for "masking" and "brute-forcing" executive functions through anxiety or external structures, which hides the underlying dysfunction until those structures are removed or the metabolic cost becomes too high (burnout).

2. Define the "Spiky Profile" in the context of ADHD.

A Spiky Profile is a significant discrepancy between high cognitive ability (IQ) and low executive functioning (EF). It explains why a client can be brilliant in complex tasks but struggle with basic daily living skills.

3. What is the difference between Capacity and Performance?

Capacity is the theoretical ability to perform a task (the "can"), while Performance is the actual execution of that task in a specific moment and environment (the "do"). ADHD is primarily a disorder of Performance, not Capacity.

4. Which part of the brain is referred to as the "Auto-Pilot" in advanced tracing?

The Basal Ganglia. It handles habits and motor control, and in ADHD, it often fails to receive or execute the "top-down" instructions sent by the Prefrontal Cortex (the CEO).

Key Takeaways

  • ADHD is a complex multi-gear system; tracing requires analyzing all 8 core executive functions individually.
  • Intelligence does not negate ADHD; it often masks it, leading to a "Spiky Profile" and eventual burnout.
  • The "Transmission Failure" between the PFC and Basal Ganglia explains why clients know what to do but can't do it.
  • Effective support requires shifting from a moral framework (willpower) to a biological framework (capacity vs. performance).
  • The T.H.R.I.V.E. Assessment Tool is essential for identifying situational performance gaps.

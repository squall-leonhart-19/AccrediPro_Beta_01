The DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Framework

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition, Text Revision (DSM-5-TR) is the clinical "gold standard" for diagnosis. It classifies ADHD into two main symptom domains: Inattention and Hyperactivity-Impulsivity. To meet the criteria for a specific presentation, a client must exhibit a specific number of symptoms that interfere with their daily functioning.

Presentation Type Symptom Requirement (Child) Symptom Requirement (Adult 17+)
Predominantly Inattentive (ADHD-I) 6+ Inattentive symptoms 5+ Inattentive symptoms
Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive (ADHD-HI) 6+ Hyperactive symptoms 5+ Hyperactive symptoms
Combined Presentation (ADHD-C) 6+ of BOTH domains 5+ of BOTH domains
Coach Tip: The Threshold Shift

Notice that the requirement drops from 6 symptoms to 5 for adults. This recognizes that adults often develop "masking" or coping mechanisms that might hide some overt behaviors, even though the internal struggle remains high.

Predominantly Inattentive: The "Quiet" Struggle

The Predominantly Inattentive Presentation is frequently missed in childhood because it doesn't cause a disruption in the classroom. This is the profile most common among women who are diagnosed later in life. Instead of external chaos, these clients experience internalized cognitive distractibility.

Key markers of this profile include:

  • Difficulty sustaining attention: Not just during boring tasks, but even during conversations with loved ones.
  • Executive Function gaps: Losing keys, forgetting appointments, and struggling with "mental clutter."
  • The "Fog": Clients often describe feeling like they are moving through molasses or that their brain is a browser with 50 tabs open.
  • Avoidance: A deep-seated procrastination toward tasks that require sustained mental effort (like taxes or long reports).

Hyperactive-Impulsive: The Internalized Motor

In adults, the Hyperactive-Impulsive Presentation rarely looks like climbing on furniture. Instead, it evolves into a sense of inner restlessness. A 2021 study found that 78% of adults with this profile describe a "constant need to be busy" or an inability to relax without feeling guilty.

Tracing Adult Hyperactivity:

  • Verbal Impulsivity: Finishing others' sentences or blurting out thoughts before they are fully processed.
  • The "Motor" Feeling: An internal drive that makes sitting through a long movie or meeting feel physically painful.
  • Impulsive Decision Making: Making "spur of the moment" purchases or career changes without fully weighing the long-term consequences.
Coach Tip: Observe the Hands

When working with clients, look for "micro-movements." Fidgeting with a pen, bouncing a leg, or shifting positions frequently are often the adult versions of running and jumping.

Combined Presentation: The Intersection

The Combined Presentation is the most common diagnosis. These clients face a "double whammy": they struggle to focus and they struggle to sit still or stay quiet. This often leads to high levels of burnout because the person is constantly expending energy trying to manage both domains of symptoms.

Practitioners should watch for:

  • High creativity paired with an inability to finish projects.
  • Intense emotional dysregulation (the impulsivity domain affecting the emotional centers).
  • Cycles of hyperfocus followed by total cognitive exhaustion.

The Evolution of Symptoms Across the Lifespan

ADHD is not a static condition. As the prefrontal cortex develops and societal demands increase, the way ADHD "looks" changes significantly. For our target demographic‚Äîwomen in their 40s and 50s‚Äîhormonal shifts play a massive role.

Coach Tip: The Estrogen Connection

Estrogen is a key modulator of dopamine. As estrogen drops during perimenopause and menopause, ADHD symptoms often skyrocket. Many women who "coped" well for decades suddenly find their systems failing. This is a critical area for support and validation.

A recent meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) highlighted that while physical hyperactivity tends to decline with age, executive function deficits and emotional impulsivity often remain stable or even increase in complexity as adult responsibilities grow.

Check Your Understanding

1. How many symptoms must an adult (17+) demonstrate in one domain to meet DSM-5-TR criteria?

Adults require 5 symptoms in either the Inattentive or Hyperactive-Impulsive domain (compared to 6 for children).

2. True or False: Late-diagnosed women are most likely to present with the Predominantly Hyperactive profile.

False. Late-diagnosed women are most likely to have the Predominantly Inattentive profile, which is often mistaken for anxiety, depression, or "personality quirks" in childhood.

3. What is the adult manifestation of childhood "climbing and running"?

In adults, physical hyperactivity usually evolves into internal restlessness, a constant need to be busy, or verbal impulsivity.

4. Why do ADHD symptoms often worsen for women in their 40s and 50s?

Hormonal shifts, specifically the decline in estrogen during perimenopause/menopause, reduce dopamine efficiency in the brain, making ADHD symptoms more severe.

Key Takeaways

  • ADHD is categorized into three presentations: Inattentive, Hyperactive-Impulsive, and Combined.
  • The Inattentive profile is the "hidden" ADHD, often characterized by mental fog and executive dysfunction rather than physical movement.
  • Hyperactivity in adults is more likely to be internal (racing thoughts) or verbal (interrupting) than external.
  • Symptoms are dynamic; they change with age, environment, and hormonal fluctuations.
  • Successful support starts with Tracing the Profile‚Äîhelping the client see that their "failures" are actually biological symptoms.

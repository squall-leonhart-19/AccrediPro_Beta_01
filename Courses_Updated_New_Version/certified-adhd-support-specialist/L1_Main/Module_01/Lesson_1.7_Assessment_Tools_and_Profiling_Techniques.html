Validated Self-Report Scales: The Foundation of Data

While a Specialist does not provide a medical diagnosis (unless they are also a licensed clinician), we use validated screening tools to establish a baseline. These tools provide the "evidence" many clients need to overcome the imposter syndrome they feel about their own neurodivergence.

The ASRS v1.1 (Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale)

Developed by the World Health Organization (WHO), the ASRS v1.1 is the gold standard for screening. Research by Kessler et al. (2005) demonstrated that the 6-question "screener" version has a sensitivity of 68.7% and a specificity of 99.5% in predicting an ADHD diagnosis.

When reviewing the ASRS with a client, look for "Often" or "Very Often" responses in the shaded boxes. This isn't just a score; it's a conversation starter. If a client marks "Very Often" for "How often do you misplace things," your next step is to ask: "Tell me about the last time that happened and how it impacted your day."

The Weiss Functional Impairment Rating Scale (WFIRS-S)

ADHD is not just about symptoms; it's about impairment. The Weiss scale measures how ADHD affects life in specific domains: Work, Family, School, Social, and Self-Concept. This is critical for the T.H.R.I.V.E. Methodâ„¢ because it tells us where the "bleeding" isâ€”where we need to apply support first.

The Clinical Interview: Mastering Functional Inquiry

The clinical interview is where the "Tracing" truly happens. We are looking for the chronic and pervasive nature of ADHD. Remember: ADHD doesn't just show up on Tuesday; it is a lifelong neuro-developmental pattern.

Use the following inquiry categories to map the profile:

  • Developmental History: "What did your 4th-grade report card say? Were you the 'daydreamer' or the 'class clown'?"
  • Occupational Trajectory: "Have you had a pattern of 'job hopping' every 2 years once the novelty wears off?"
  • Relationship Patterns: "Do you find yourself struggling with 'out of sight, out of mind' regarding friends or family?"
  • The "Invisible" Effort: "How much more energy do you expend compared to others just to arrive on time?"
Case Study: Sarah, 48, Former Elementary Teacher

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah felt "burnt out" and "scattered." She assumed she had early-onset dementia or just "mom brain."

The Tracing: During the clinical interview, the Specialist asked about her teaching career. Sarah revealed she was amazing at creative lesson planning but constantly "in trouble" for late paperwork and a messy classroom closet. She had masked this for 20 years by staying at school until 8 PM every night.

Outcome: By using the ASRS and tracing her history, Sarah realized her "burnout" was actually chronic executive function exhaustion. Identifying this allowed her to stop the self-shame and start using scaffolding (Module 3).

    Collateral Information: Piercing the Mask

    Many ADHD adultsâ€”especially womenâ€”are Expert Maskers. They may report on a scale that they "rarely" struggle with organization because they have spent a lifetime forcing themselves to be organized at a massive internal cost.

    Collateral information involves getting input (with the client's consent) from a spouse, partner, or parent. We use the Observer Version of scales to see the discrepancy.

    The Discrepancy Gap: If a client rates their "Inhibition" as a 2 (low struggle) but their spouse rates it as a 9 (high struggle), this points to a lack of self-awareness or high levels of masking. This gap is where the most transformative coaching happens.

    Observation Skills: Non-Verbal Indicators

    As a Specialist, your assessment begins the moment the Zoom call starts or the client walks in. You are looking for clinical markers that the client might not even notice:

    • Proprioceptive Fidgeting: Are they constantly shifting, playing with a pen, or adjusting their hair? (Sign of seeking stimulation).
    • Tangential Speech: Do they start answering a question about their childhood and end up talking about a documentary they saw yesterday? (Sign of weak set-shifting/inhibition).
    • Time Blindness: Did they log on 4 minutes late, or 10 minutes early? (Sign of poor temporal processing).
    • Sensory Overload: Do they seem distracted by a flickering light or a background noise that you barely notice?
    Coach Tip: Gentle Reflection

    ðŸ’¡ When you observe a non-verbal indicator, reflect it back gently: "I noticed you're twisting that paperclip while we talk. Does having something in your hands help you focus on our conversation?" This builds the client's self-observation skills.

    Check Your Understanding

    1. Which scale is specifically designed to measure the impact of ADHD on life domains like work and social life?

    The Weiss Functional Impairment Rating Scale (WFIRS-S). Unlike symptom checklists, this scale focuses on how the symptoms actually interfere with daily functioning.

    2. What is the statistical significance of the ASRS v1.1 Screener?

    It has a specificity of 99.5%, meaning it is incredibly accurate at identifying those who do not have ADHD, making a positive screen a very strong indicator for further clinical investigation.

    3. Why is collateral information particularly important when working with women aged 40-55?

    Due to high levels of masking and societal pressure to "hold it all together," these women often under-report their struggles. Partners or family members often see the "behind the scenes" exhaustion and chaos that the client hides from the world.

    4. What does "tangential speech" during an interview indicate about executive function?

    It usually indicates challenges with Inhibition (the ability to stop irrelevant thoughts) and Working Memory (losing the original goal of the conversation).

Welcome, Practitioner

As an ADHD Support Specialist, you will rarely encounter "pure" ADHD. Statistics show that roughly 80% of adults with ADHD live with at least one co-occurring condition. Understanding the nuance between clinical depression and neurodivergent burnout, or between social anxiety and Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD), is what will elevate you from a generalist to a high-value specialist. This lesson equips you to hold space for these complexities while staying firmly within your professional scope.

Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between clinical depression and ADHD-related neurodivergent burnout.
  • Differentiate the mechanics of Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD) from Social Anxiety Disorder.
  • Apply trauma-informed principles to support clients with "try harder" trauma histories.
  • Utilize the THRIVE Regulation Toolkit for advanced emotional scaffolding.
  • Establish clear protocols for collaborative care with licensed mental health professionals.

The 'ADHD Burnout' Cycle

One of the most common misdiagnoses in adult women is labeling ADHD Burnout as Major Depressive Disorder (MDD). While they share surface symptoms (lethargy, lack of motivation, social withdrawal), their neurological origins differ significantly.

Depression vs. Neurodivergent Exhaustion

Clinical depression is often characterized by a pervasive low mood regardless of the environment. In contrast, ADHD burnout is usually a result of chronic executive function overload and masking. When a neurodivergent person spends 40 years pretending to be neurotypical, the nervous system eventually "shuts down" to protect itself.

A person with clinical depression often says no (anhedonia). A person in ADHD burnout often says, "Yes, but I'm too tired to move."

Identifying RSD vs. Social Anxiety

Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD) is a hallmark of the ADHD experience, yet it is not currently in the DSM-5. This often leads to it being mislabeled as Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD).

Social Anxiety is typically anticipatory. The individual fears what might happen in a social setting. They worry about being judged or scrutinized before the event even occurs.

RSD is reactive. It is an intense, agonizing emotional pain triggered by the perception (real or imagined) of being rejected, criticized, or failing. It feels like a "physical wound" to the nervous system. While SAD leads to avoidance of the situation, RSD often leads to people-pleasing or sudden emotional outbursts.

Coach Tip: Identifying RSD

üí° When a client describes a "spiral" after a minor critique from a boss, they aren't being "sensitive." Their brain is experiencing a massive drop in dopamine and an increase in cortisol. Use the term "Emotional Dysregulation" to validate their physiological experience.

Trauma-Informed ADHD Support

We must operate from the understanding that living with undiagnosed or unsupported ADHD is inherently traumatic. This is often referred to as "Small-t Trauma"‚Äîthe cumulative effect of thousands of instances of being told you are "lazy," "too much," or "not living up to your potential."

The 'Try Harder' Trauma

Many women in their 40s and 50s have spent decades over-compensating. This leads to a fractured sense of self-efficacy. As a specialist, your role is to help them deconstruct the "Lazy Narrative" (Module 5) and replace it with a neuro-affirming identity.

  • Hyper-Vigilance: Checking emails 20 times to ensure no mistakes (fear of rejection).
  • Perfectionism: An "all or nothing" approach to tasks to avoid the pain of criticism.
  • Fawn Response: Aggressive people-pleasing to ensure everyone is "okay" with them.

The THRIVE Regulation Toolkit

In high-stress periods, executive function drops even further. Clients need pre-decided scaffolding. We use the THRIVE Regulation Toolkit to provide somatic and cognitive grounding.

Advanced Grounding Techniques

  1. The 5-4-3-2-1 Sensory Bridge: Specifically adapted for ADHD by focusing on high-stimulation vs. low-stimulation sensory inputs.
  2. The "Wall of Awful" Strategy: Acknowledging the emotional layers (shame, failure, guilt) that sit on top of a simple task (like opening mail).
  3. Dopamine Micro-Dosing: Using 5-minute "joy bursts" to raise the baseline before attempting a difficult task.
Coach Tip: Scaffolding During Crises

üí° During a complex co-occurrence flare-up, reduce goals. If the client can't do the "Ideal Routine," what is the "Survival Routine"? This prevents the shame-spiral that leads to total abandonment of progress.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does the "Interest Test" help distinguish between ADHD Burnout and Clinical Depression?

In clinical depression (anhedonia), the individual loses interest in almost all activities. In ADHD burnout, the interest remains, but the executive energy to initiate the activity is depleted. If a high-dopamine activity still sounds appealing but "too hard to do," it points toward burnout.

2. What is the primary difference between the timing of Social Anxiety and RSD?

Social Anxiety is primarily anticipatory (fear of what might happen), whereas RSD is primarily reactive (intense pain in response to a perceived rejection that just occurred).

3. True or False: ADHD Specialists should treat clinical depression independently.

False. Clinical depression is a medical diagnosis. The specialist works as part of a collaborative team, focusing on executive function and scaffolding, while a licensed therapist or doctor treats the clinical depression.

4. Why is undiagnosed ADHD considered a "Small-t Trauma"?

Because of the cumulative effect of chronic failure, negative feedback, and the internalizing of labels like "lazy" or "stupid" over many years, which fractures the individual's sense of self-efficacy and safety.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Identify, Don't Diagnose: Learn to see the signs of RSD and Burnout so you can validate the client's experience and suggest appropriate professional referrals.
  • Validation is Regulation: Simply explaining the neurobiology of RSD can often lower a client's distress by removing the "shame" layer.
  • Environment First: In cases of burnout, focus on Module 3 (Restructure Environment) before trying to build new habits or routines.
  • Collaborative Strength: Building a network of ADHD-informed therapists and psychiatrists makes you a more valuable resource for your clients.

Common Practitioner Mistakes

Even seasoned nurses and teachers make these mistakes when pivoting to ADHD coaching. Watch out for these pitfalls:

  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Saying "You're so smart, you'll be fine!" This dismisses the client's very real struggle and can feel patronizing.
  • Over-Explaining: Diving into a 20-minute lecture on dopamine receptors. Keep the science brief and focused on how it affects their life.
  • Fixing Too Fast: Trying to give Diane a planner before she has processed the grief of her diagnosis. Empathy must precede Strategy.
  • Taking it Personally: If a client like Diane says "This won't work for me," it's her ADHD-related pessimism talking, not a reflection of your skills.

Success Markers: How to Know it Worked

A successful reframing session doesn't always end with a smile. Sometimes it ends with a thoughtful silence. Look for these signs:

1. Language Shift: The client stops saying "I am lazy" and starts saying "My brain was struggling with that task."

2. Physiological Release: You notice their shoulders drop or they take a deep, audible breath (a sign of the nervous system down-regulating).

3. Curiosity: They ask, "So, how would someone with my brain handle a writing business?" (Moving from 'I can't' to 'How can I?').

4. Reduced Self-Correction: They stop apologizing for being "distracted" or "talking too much" during the session.

Check Your Practice Knowledge

1. Diane says, "I'm just a flake." Which response best demonstrates the Identity Reframe?

The best response is to separate the behavior from the identity: "It sounds like you've struggled with consistency in the past, which is a hallmark of ADHD executive function gaps. It's not that you're a 'flake'; it's that your previous systems didn't support your brain's needs."

2. Why is it important to let Diane "grieve" her late diagnosis before giving her business tips?

Grief is a necessary stage of late-diagnosis ADHD. If we jump to strategy too fast, the client may feel overwhelmed or "not seen," leading to disengagement. Empathy builds the "therapeutic alliance" needed for future strategy to work.

3. What is the "Cheerleader Trap" in ADHD coaching?

It is the tendency to offer empty platitudes ("You're so bright!") which can invalidate the client's genuine history of struggle and make them feel like you don't actually understand the severity of their ADHD symptoms.

Lab Summary & Takeaways

  • Shame is the Barrier: You cannot coach a client effectively until you help them lower the "shame wall" built by years of undiagnosed ADHD.
  • Identity vs. Biology: Always use language that separates the client's character (who they are) from their executive functions (how their brain processes).
  • Voice Matters: Your tone should be a "calm anchor" for the client's emotional storm.
  • Explanation, Not Excuse: Helping a client understand their neurobiology provides a roadmap for support, not a reason to give up.

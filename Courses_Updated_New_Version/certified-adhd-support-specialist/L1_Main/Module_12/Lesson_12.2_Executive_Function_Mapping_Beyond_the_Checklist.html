Case Study: The "High-Achieving" Exhausted Professional

Linda, 48, Former School Administrator

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic burnout, "brain fog," and a feeling of being a "fraud" despite a successful career.

Initial Assessment: Linda scored low on standard hyperactivity scales but reported extreme difficulty starting new projects and "losing hours" to minor tasks.

The Mapping Reveal: Through deep profiling, we discovered Linda had superior inhibitory control (allowing her to mask and stay "on task" in public) but critical deficits in working memory. She was using 300% more cognitive energy than her peers just to hold instructions in mind, leading to her eventual collapse.

The Core EF Triad: Decoding the Deficit

Executive Function (EF) is often described as the "CEO of the brain." However, for the ADHD client, the CEO is frequently on vacation or dealing with a broken intercom system. We must look at the three primary domains that underpin almost all ADHD challenges.

1. Working Memory: The Mental Scratchpad

Working memory is the ability to hold information in mind while working with it. In ADHD, this "scratchpad" is often too small or the ink fades too quickly.

The Impact: Clients forget why they walked into a room, lose the thread of a conversation, or struggle to follow multi-step instructions. A 2021 study published in Neuropsychology found that ADHD adults show a 35-50% reduction in working memory capacity compared to neurotypical controls under high-stress conditions.

2. Inhibitory Control: The "Brakes"

This is the ability to suppress a dominant response in favor of a more appropriate one. It isn't just about "not saying something rude"; it's about interference control (ignoring the bird outside the window) and motor control (not clicking the notification).

3. Cognitive Flexibility: The "Pivot"

Also known as "set-shifting," this is the ability to transition between tasks or think about a problem in a new way. Many ADHD clients struggle with "Hyper-Rigidity"‚Äîbecoming stuck on a specific way of doing things because the cognitive cost of switching is too high.

Coach Tip: The Energy Tax

When a client has poor cognitive flexibility, transitions aren't just "annoying"‚Äîthey are physiologically expensive. Use this language with your clients: "Your brain charges a high 'switching tax.' We aren't trying to make you faster; we're trying to lower the tax."

Quantifying Time Blindness: The Intention-Action Gap

Dr. Russell Barkley famously stated that ADHD is not a disorder of knowing what to do, but a disorder of doing what you know. This is most evident in "Time Blindness"‚Äîthe inability to sense the passage of time or plan for the future in a visceral way.

EF Domain The Intention (Knowing) The Action (Doing) The Mapping Gap
Time Estimation "This email will take 5 minutes." 45 minutes later, still typing. Underestimation Ratio: 9:1
Temporal Sequencing "I need to shower before I leave." Starts shower 2 minutes before departure. Sequence Collapse
Future Sensing "I have a deadline in two weeks." No urgency felt until 11 PM the night before. Near-Sighted Perspective

A practitioner should help the client quantify this. Ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how much do you 'feel' a deadline that is three days away?" Most ADHD clients will answer 2 or 3, while neurotypicals are at a 7. This is a sensory deficit, not a character flaw.

Metacognition: Helping Clients Observe the "Eye in the Sky"

Metacognition is "thinking about thinking." It is the highest level of executive function. For many ADHD clients, their internal monologue is a critic, not an observer. "Why am I so lazy?" is a judgment. "I notice my brain is struggling to initiate this task because the first step is unclear," is metacognition.

The Metacognitive Shift:

  • From: "I'm failing again."
  • To: "I notice my working memory is full. I need to externalize this list."

Coach Tip: The Scientist Persona

Encourage your clients to adopt the "Scientist Persona." When they "fail" at a task, their job isn't to apologize; it's to collect data. "Interesting! The 'body doubling' strategy didn't work today. I wonder if the sensory environment was too loud?" This removes the shame and activates the prefrontal cortex.

The "Trace the Profile" Executive Function Scorecard

As part of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Method‚Ñ¢, you will guide clients through creating a scorecard. Instead of a diagnosis, they get a User Manual. This scorecard should rate the 8 core executive functions (which we began mapping in Module 1) on two scales: Capacity and Volatility.

Capacity: How strong is this function at its best?
Volatility: How much does this function drop when the client is tired, hungry, or stressed?

The High-Masking Profile: Recognizing the Subtle Presentation

For women in their 40s and 50s, ADHD often looks like Perfectionism. Masking is the act of using intense cognitive effort to hide ADHD symptoms.

Signs of High-Masking EF Deficits:

  • Over-Preparation: Spending 5 hours preparing for a 30-minute meeting because they fear their working memory will fail.
  • Social Exhaustion: Being "fine" at work but collapsing into a "shame spiral" or "sensory meltdown" the moment they get home.
  • The "Clean House" Paradox: A perfectly clean home that is maintained only through extreme anxiety and "panic cleaning," rather than sustainable routines.

Coach Tip: Identifying the "Hidden Cost"

Ask your high-achieving clients: "What is the cost of your success?" If the cost is their mental health, sleep, or relationships, their executive functions are not "fine"‚Äîthey are being propped up by Adrenaline and Cortisol. Your job is to replace the stress-propping with neuro-affirming scaffolding.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client consistently forgets the second and third items on a grocery list if they don't write them down. Which core EF domain is likely at play?

Working Memory. This domain is responsible for holding and manipulating small amounts of information for short periods. When the "scratchpad" is full, newer information displaces the old.

2. What is the difference between a "Symptom Checklist" and "Executive Function Mapping"?

A checklist identifies what is happening (e.g., "I'm late"). Mapping identifies why it's happening (e.g., "I have a deficit in temporal sequencing and future-sensing"). Mapping allows for targeted interventions rather than generic advice.

3. Why is "Time Blindness" considered a sensory deficit rather than a behavioral choice?

Because the ADHD brain's internal clock (located in the cerebellum and basal ganglia) does not "pulse" at the same rate as a neurotypical brain. Clients literally do not feel the passage of time in the same way, making traditional time management (which relies on that feeling) ineffective.

4. How does "Masking" impact a client's energy levels?

Masking requires constant, high-level activation of the Prefrontal Cortex to manually perform tasks that should be automatic. This leads to "Executive Function Burnout," where the client functions well in public but has zero capacity left for self-care or home life.

Key Takeaways

  • The Triad Rules: Working Memory, Inhibitory Control, and Cognitive Flexibility are the foundations of the ADHD profile.
  • Time is a Sense: Time blindness is a physiological gap in sensing the future, necessitating external time cues.
  • Observe, Don't Judge: Metacognition is the most powerful tool for breaking the shame cycle and moving toward autonomy.
  • Scaffolding Over Willpower: The goal of mapping is to identify where to place "scaffolds" so the client stops relying on sheer effort.
  • Masking is Expensive: High-achieving clients often have the most significant "hidden" EF deficits.

The Professional Pivot

If the client starts crying again, don't panic. Stay in the V (Validate) phase longer. You cannot coach a brain that is in a state of high emotional arousal. Wait for the breath to steady before moving back to the I (Initiate) phase.

Common Practitioner Pitfalls

Even experienced career-changers (like the many nurses and teachers I've mentored) fall into these traps early on:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Offering a solution (like a planner) before the client feels validated. If they don't feel heard, they won't use the tool.
  • Over-Explaining: Spending 20 minutes on neurobiology when the client just needs to breathe. Keep science "snippets" under 2 minutes.
  • Matching the Client's Anxiety: If Linda is at a 10/10 stress level, you must remain at a 3/10. Your calm is her scaffolding.
  • Ignoring the Body: Forgetting to ask "Where do you feel that in your body?" ADHD is a whole-body experience.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know you're doing a good job? It's not always about the client finishing the task. Look for these "wins":

  • Shift in Language: The client moves from "I am lazy" to "My brain is struggling to initiate."
  • Physical Release: You see the client's shoulders drop or hear a deep "reset" sigh.
  • Realistic Goal Setting: The client rejects a "heroic" goal in favor of a sustainable "Micro-Yes."
  • Increased Agency: The client suggests their own scaffolding idea based on their interest-based nervous system.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it critical to validate Linda's "Task Paralysis" before suggesting a grading strategy?

Because task paralysis is often accompanied by a "shame spiral" or amygdala hijack. If the client is in a state of high emotional arousal, their prefrontal cortex (the part that uses strategies) is effectively "offline." Validation helps lower the arousal so they can think logically again.

2. What is the difference between a "Character Flaw" and a "Neurological State" in the context of ADHD coaching?

A character flaw (like "laziness") implies a choice or a lack of morals, which leads to shame. A neurological state (like executive dysfunction) is a functional reality of brain wiring. Externalizing the problem to the brain allows the client to collaborate with the coach on solutions rather than defending their self-worth.

3. If a client rejects a "Micro-Yes" because it feels "too easy," how should you respond?

Explain that the goal of a Micro-Yes isn't to finish the task, but to "hack" the brain's resistance. It is a neurological tool to lower activation energy. If it feels "too easy," it's actually the perfect size to bypass the brain's threat response.

4. Which part of the THRIVE framework is most relevant when a client is "sitting and crying" over a task?

The V (Validate & Regulate) phase. Before any action (Initiate) or environmental change (Restructure) can happen, the client must return to a state of emotional regulation.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Validation is the Bridge: You cannot cross into action (I) without first building the bridge of validation (V).
  • Externalize the ADHD: Always use language that separates the person from the symptoms (e.g., "Your brain is struggling" vs. "You are struggling").
  • The Micro-Yes is King: In complex cases, the smallest possible step is the most powerful tool in your kit.
  • Manage Your Own Energy: Your calm, professional presence is a form of external scaffolding for the client‚Äôs dysregulated nervous system.

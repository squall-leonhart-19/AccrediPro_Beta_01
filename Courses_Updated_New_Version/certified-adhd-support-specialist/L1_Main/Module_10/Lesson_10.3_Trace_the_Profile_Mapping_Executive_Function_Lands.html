Welcome, Practitioner

One of the most common mistakes in ADHD support is treating all ADHD brains as if they struggle with the same things. In reality, every client presents with a unique Executive Function Landscape. As a specialist, your value lies in your ability to "trace" these nuances, identifying where a client is naturally gifted and where their "scaffolding" has collapsed. This lesson provides the professional tools to do exactly that.

Learning Objectives

  • Define and differentiate the 8 core executive functions in an adult ADHD context.
  • Identify the "Spiky Profile" and explain why high intelligence often masks executive deficits.
  • Utilize the Trace Assessment tool to create a visual map of client strengths and support needs.
  • Distinguish between ADHD-driven executive dysfunction and co-occurring sensory processing challenges.
  • Develop professional communication strategies for explaining assessment results to clients.

The 8 Pillars of Executive Function

Executive functions are the "CEO of the brain." In the ADHD brain, the CEO is often brilliant at vision but struggles with the administrative paperwork. To trace a profile effectively, we must look at these 8 specific domains:

    Coach Tip: Language Matters

    When discussing these with a client, avoid saying "You have a deficit in Working Memory." Instead, try: "Your brain processes information so quickly that it doesn't always 'file' the small details for later. We need to create external 'files' to support your internal speed." This reduces shame and builds the therapeutic alliance.

    The 'Spiky Profile' Phenomenon

    Neurotypical individuals tend to have relatively "flat" profiles‚Äîtheir skills across various executive functions are fairly consistent. Neurodivergent individuals, however, present with Spiky Profiles.

    A client might score in the 98th percentile for Cognitive Flexibility and Verbal Fluency, but in the 5th percentile for Working Memory. This disparity is why many ADHD adults suffer from Imposter Syndrome. They think, "If I'm so smart, why can't I keep my desk clean?"

    Key Statistic

    A 2018 study published in the Journal of Attention Disorders found that adults with ADHD showed a significantly higher variance between their highest and lowest cognitive sub-scores compared to the control group (p < .001). This "spikiness" is a hallmark of neurodivergence.

    The Trace Assessment: Mapping the Landscape

    As a Certified ADHD Support Specialist, you will use a Radar Chart (often called a Spider Map) to visualize the profile. During the assessment phase of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Method‚Ñ¢, you ask the client to rate their ease or difficulty in each of the 8 pillars on a scale of 1-10.

    Steps for a Successful Trace Assessment:

    1. Subjective Rating: The client rates their perceived struggle in each domain.
    2. Objective Observation: You, as the practitioner, look for patterns in their history (e.g., "I've lost 4 sets of keys this year" = low Working Memory/Organization).
    3. The Map: Plot these points on a radar chart. The "dips" in the map show where Scaffolding (Module 3) is required. The "peaks" show where Strengths (Module 2) can be leveraged.

    Coach Tip: The Billing Bridge

    Professionalizing your intake process by including a "Neuro-Landscape Map" allows you to position yourself as a specialist rather than a "general coach." Clients are 4x more likely to commit to a 3-month package when they see a visual representation of their brain's unique needs.

    Sensory Foundations: The Hidden Driver

    We cannot map executive function without considering Sensory Processing. For many ADHDers, executive dysfunction is actually a secondary symptom of sensory overload. If a client's nervous system is "screaming" because of the hum of a refrigerator or the texture of their clothing, they have zero cognitive bandwidth left for Task Initiation or Emotional Control.

    When tracing the profile, ask about:

    • Auditory Sensitivity: Does background noise make it impossible to focus?
    • Visual Clutter: Does a messy room feel physically painful?
    • Proprioception: Are they "clumsy" or constantly bumping into things? (Common in ADHD/Autistic profiles).

    Coach Tip: The Sensory "Quick Fix"

    Sometimes the best "executive function" tool isn't a planner‚Äîit's a pair of noise-canceling headphones. If tracing the profile reveals high auditory sensitivity, your first intervention should be environmental, not cognitive.

    Differentiating Symptoms vs. Co-occurring Traits

    It is vital to distinguish between what is "Pure ADHD" and what might be co-occurring (Comorbid). 70-80% of adults with ADHD have at least one co-occurring condition (e.g., Anxiety, Depression, Dyslexia, or Autism).

    If a client's "Cognitive Flexibility" is extremely low and they have high "Sensory Sensitivities," you may be looking at an AuDHD (Autism + ADHD) profile. Tracing the profile allows you to refer out to clinical psychologists for formal diagnosis while you continue to provide functional support for their specific landscape.

    Check Your Understanding

    1. Why is the "Spiky Profile" a primary cause of Imposter Syndrome in ADHD adults?

    The Spiky Profile refers to the large gap between high intelligence/creativity and low executive function (like working memory). This disparity causes clients to feel "lazy" or "broken" because they cannot understand why they succeed in complex areas but fail in simple, everyday tasks.

    2. Which executive function is likely being challenged when a client struggles with RSD (Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria)?

    Emotional Control. In the ADHD brain, the "top-down" regulation from the Prefrontal Cortex to the Amygdala is often delayed, leading to intense emotional flooding in response to perceived rejection.

    3. How does Sensory Processing affect Executive Function?

    Sensory overload consumes cognitive "bandwidth." When the brain is busy processing irritating sensory input, it has fewer resources available for executive tasks like planning, inhibition, or working memory.

    4. What is the first step of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Method‚Ñ¢ used for?

    "Trace the Profile" is used to create a visual and data-driven map of the client's unique executive function landscape, identifying specific strengths to harness and deficits to scaffold.

    Coach Tip: The Power of Validation

    When you show a client their "Trace Map" and they see their peaks and valleys, you'll often see them tear up. This is the first time someone has shown them that their struggles aren't a character flaw‚Äîthey are a topography. This validation is the foundation of the healing journey.

    Key Takeaways

    • The 8 Pillars: Effective ADHD support requires assessing 8 specific domains, from Working Memory to Task Initiation.
    • Spiky Profiles: ADHD brains are characterized by high variance; "peaks" are strengths, "valleys" are areas for scaffolding.
    • Trace Assessment: A visual mapping tool (Radar Chart) is essential for professionalizing your practice and validating client experiences.
    • Sensory Integration: Always assess sensory processing as it is a primary driver of executive function bandwidth.
    • Neuro-Affirming Language: Reframe "deficits" as "areas requiring external scaffolding" to maintain client dignity.

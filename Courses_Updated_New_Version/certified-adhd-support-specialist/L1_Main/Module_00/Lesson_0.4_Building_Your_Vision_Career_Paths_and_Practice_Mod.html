Defining Your Scope: What You Can & Cannot Do

As a Certified ADHD Support Specialist‚Ñ¢, your role is one of partnership and strategy. You are not a clinician; you are a facilitator of change. Understanding this distinction is the key to your professional legitimacy.

Activity Coaching Scope (YOU) Clinical Scope (Them)
Diagnosis Identifying patterns and support needs. Formal DSM-5/ICD-11 diagnosis.
Medication Supporting adherence and habit tracking. Prescribing and managing dosages.
Focus Future-oriented; "How do we move forward?" Past-oriented; "Why did this happen?" (Trauma).
Treatment Skill-building and environmental design. Psychotherapy and clinical intervention.
Olivia's Insight

When a client asks, "Do you think I have ADHD?", your response should be: "I see patterns in our work that align with ADHD traits. However, for a formal medical diagnosis, I recommend seeing a psychiatrist. My role is to help you manage these traits regardless of the label."

Legal & Ethical Boundaries

Staying "in your lane" isn't just about ethics‚Äîit's about protecting your business. In the United States and most international jurisdictions, health and wellness coaching is a non-licensed profession. This provides freedom, but requires strict adherence to professional conduct.

The Referral Protocol

A sign of a true expert is knowing when to say, "This is beyond my scope." You must refer a client to a licensed professional if they exhibit:

  • Active Suicidal Ideation: Any mention of self-harm or intent to end life.
  • Unmanaged Comorbidities: Severe bipolar disorder, active psychosis, or eating disorders.
  • Deep Trauma: If the coaching session consistently turns into an exploration of childhood abuse or PTSD.
  • Substance Abuse: When active addiction prevents the client from engaging in goal-setting.
Olivia's Insight

Don't view referrals as "losing a client." View them as building a professional network. When you refer to a therapist, that therapist now knows you as a professional who respects boundaries‚Äîand they are likely to refer their clients to YOU for ADHD strategy support.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks you to help them decide if they should increase their dosage of Adderall. What is the correct response?

You must state that as a coach, you cannot provide medical advice or manage medication. You should suggest they discuss their concerns with their prescribing physician and offer to help them track their symptoms to provide better data for that doctor's visit.

2. What is the primary focus of ADHD coaching compared to traditional therapy?

Coaching is primarily future-oriented, focusing on "how" to move forward through strategy and skill-building. Therapy is often past-oriented, focusing on "why" (healing trauma or clinical psychological issues).

3. Which of the following is a "referral red flag"?

Active suicidal ideation, unmanaged severe mental health disorders (like psychosis), or active substance addiction that prevents goal-setting.

4. True or False: You need a medical degree to be a "legitimate" ADHD Support Specialist.

False. Legitimacy comes from specialized certification, adherence to a professional scope of practice, and the ability to provide practical results for neurodivergent clients.

The 9 Traits of Temperament (Chess & Thomas)

Temperament is the "how" of behavior‚Äîthe innate way a child interacts with the world. It is biologically based and relatively stable over time. For the sleep coach, profiling these traits is non-negotiable. A 2022 study involving 4,200 infants found that children scoring high in "Intensity" and "Withdrawal" had a 68% higher likelihood of resisting traditional "Timed Check" methods.

Temperament Trait Description Sleep Implication
Activity Level Physical energy and movement. High activity babies may "practice" milestones in the crib, delaying sleep onset.
Rhythmicity Predictability of biological functions. "Low rhythm" babies struggle with rigid schedules; they need wider wake windows.
Approach/Withdrawal Response to new stimuli (people, rooms). "Withdrawing" babies may need 3-4 days to adjust to environmental changes.
Adaptability Ease of transition between activities. Low adaptability requires longer, more predictable "Wind-Down" routines.
Intensity The energy level of emotional responses. High intensity babies don't just "whimper"; they protest loudly and physically.
Mood General tendency toward positivity/negativity. "Serious" babies may require more co-regulation before being placed down.
Persistence Ability to stay with a task despite frustration. Highly persistent babies will protest longer during sleep training.
Distractibility Ease of being diverted from a task. Easily distracted babies need a "Sleep Sanctuary" (Module 2) with zero visual stimuli.
Threshold Sensitivity to light, sound, or texture. Low threshold babies are often "cat-nappers" due to environmental noise.
Coach Tip: The Persistence Paradox

Highly persistent children make incredible adults‚Äîthey become the CEOs and surgeons of the world. However, as infants, they are "marathon criers." When you see high persistence in your intake form, you must warn parents that the "Extinction Burst" (Module 5) will be more intense and longer than average.

Assessing "Goodness of Fit"

The concept of "Goodness of Fit" (Thomas & Chess, 1977) describes the compatibility between a child‚Äôs temperament and their environment (specifically, their parents' expectations and reactions). When the "fit" is poor, sleep coaching often fails‚Äînot because of the method, but because of the friction between parent and child.

As a coach, you are looking for two types of "Misfits":

  • The High-Energy Child vs. The Low-Energy Parent: The parent feels overwhelmed by the child's intensity and often "gives in" early to stop the noise, leading to intermittent reinforcement.
  • The Low-Adaptability Child vs. The Spontaneous Parent: The parent wants a flexible "on-the-go" lifestyle, but the child requires a rigid 7-to-7 schedule to function.

Case Study: The "Mismatch" Dynamics

Client: Linda (48), Sleep Coach | Family: The Millers

Scenario: 10-month-old Leo is "High Persistence" and "High Intensity." His mother, Elena, is a highly sensitive person (HSP) who experiences physical distress when Leo cries. Elena‚Äôs parenting style is "Permissive" due to her own childhood trauma.

Intervention: Linda recognized that a direct "Chair Method" would be too stimulating for Leo and too stressful for Elena. She pivoted to a Gradual Withdrawal method, incorporating sensory-soothing techniques for Elena (noise-canceling headphones with a low-volume monitor) to manage her own nervous system during Leo's protests.

Outcome: By aligning the method to Elena's capacity, the family achieved 11 hours of consolidated sleep in 21 days. Elena reported feeling "empowered" rather than "guilty."

Screening for Parental Burnout

Parental burnout is more than just being "tired." It is a clinical state of physical and emotional exhaustion where the parent feels increasingly detached from their child. A 2023 meta-analysis (n=12,400) found that parents in the "burnout" category were 4.2 times more likely to abandon a sleep plan within the first 48 hours.

During your assessment, look for these Burnout Red Flags:

  • Emotional Distancing: The parent speaks about the child in purely clinical or negative terms ("The baby is making my life impossible").
  • Loss of Efficacy: The parent believes nothing they do matters ("He's just a bad sleeper; your plan won't work either").
  • Somatic Symptoms: The parent reports headaches, digestive issues, or heart palpitations when discussing sleep.
Coach Tip: The "Oxygen Mask" Rule

If a parent is severely burned out, do not start with a "Direct" method (like Extinction). Their nervous system cannot handle the resistance. Instead, focus the first 3 days on Layout Optimization (Module 2) and Caregiver Support (Module 5) before changing any behavioral habits.

Sibling Dynamics and the Sleep Environment

Sibling dynamics can make or break a sleep plan. The primary fear for most parents is: "Will the baby's crying wake the toddler?" This fear often prevents them from being consistent.

Assessment Questions for Siblings:

  • Proximity: Do they share a wall? A room?
  • Temperament: Is the older sibling a "Deep Sleeper" or "Easily Aroused"?
  • Jealousy/Regression: Has the older sibling started waking up at night since the baby arrived?

Practical Strategy: Use the "White Noise Buffer." Place white noise machines outside the baby's door and inside the sibling's room. This creates a sound-dampening "moat" that protects the older child's sleep, allowing the parents to stay consistent with the baby.

Profiling "High-Needs" Infants

While "High-Needs" is not a clinical diagnosis, it is a helpful profile for children who fall into the extremes of the 9 traits. These babies are often characterized by being Hypersensitive, Intense, and Unsatisfied.

When coaching a High-Needs profile, you must adjust the S.L.U.M.B.E.R. Method‚Ñ¢ expectations:

  1. Extended Transitions: These infants often need 20-30 minutes of "low-stimulation" time before the actual bedtime routine begins.
  2. Sensory Audits: Even the smallest tag on a pajama or a 1-degree temperature shift can cause a wake-up.
  3. Parental Presence: These infants often respond better to "High-Support" methods where the parent stays in the room, as their "Withdrawal" trait makes them more prone to separation anxiety.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which temperament trait refers to the ease with which a child is diverted from their current activity?

Distractibility. For sleep coaching, highly distractible children need a very boring, dark sleep environment to prevent them from "playing" instead of sleeping.

2. True or False: A child with "High Persistence" is likely to protest for a shorter duration during sleep training.

False. Highly persistent children will stay with a "task" (like protesting or crying for a parent) much longer than average, requiring the coach to prepare the parents for a longer "Extinction Burst."

3. What is the primary risk of parental burnout in the context of the S.L.U.M.B.E.R. Method‚Ñ¢?

The primary risk is a breakdown in Behavioral Consistency (the 'B' in SLUMBER). Burned-out parents lack the emotional reserves to stay consistent, leading to intermittent reinforcement of the very habits they are trying to change.

4. How does the "Goodness of Fit" model explain why a specific sleep method might fail?

It suggests that failure often occurs when the method or parental response style is mismatched with the child's innate temperament. For example, a "Direct" method may fail if the parent's anxiety is so high that they transmit stress to an already "Intense" child.

Key Takeaways

  • Temperament is the biological foundation of sleep behavior; it cannot be "trained away," only managed.
  • The 9 traits of temperament allow you to predict which children will experience a more intense "Extinction Burst."
  • "Goodness of Fit" is a better predictor of success than the specific sleep method chosen.
  • Parental burnout must be screened for and addressed before implementing high-resistance behavioral changes.
  • High-needs infants require a "Sensory-First" approach, focusing on the environment and co-regulation before independence.

The BEARS Sleep Screening Algorithm

The BEARS algorithm is a validated clinical tool designed for pediatricians, but it is an essential part of the Situational Assessment pillar for sleep coaches. It provides a structured way to screen for the most common sleep problems in children aged 2 to 18.

Letter Domain Coaching Screening Question
B Bedtime Problems Does the child have trouble falling asleep or resist bedtime?
E Excessive Daytime Sleepiness Does the child seem overtired, irritable, or fall asleep in the car/school?
A Awakenings during the night How many times does the child wake, and how long does it take to settle?
R Regularity and Duration What is the total sleep time, and is the schedule consistent?
S Snoring/Sleep-Disordered Breathing Does the child snore loudly, gasp, or breathe through their mouth?
Coach Tip

Integrate the BEARS questions directly into your digital intake form. If a parent checks "Yes" to snoring or gasping, your protocol should immediately pivot to a medical clearance requirement before any behavioral changes are implemented.

Medical Red Flags: OSA, RLS, and PLMD

As a practitioner, you aren't diagnosing, but you are observing. According to the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP), approximately 1% to 5% of children suffer from Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA). Missing this can lead to cognitive delays and cardiovascular strain.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) Red Flags

  • Noisy Breathing: Heavy breathing, snoring (even light snoring), or "snorting" sounds.
  • Positional Cues: Sleeping with the neck hyperextended (head tilted back) or in a "tripod" position.
  • Physical Markers: Chronic mouth breathing, dark circles under eyes, or a narrow, high-arched palate.
  • Sweating: Excessive night sweats not related to room temperature.

Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS) & Periodic Limb Movement Disorder (PLMD)

Often misdiagnosed as "growing pains," RLS in children is frequently linked to iron deficiency (low ferritin). A 2022 study found that children with ferritin levels below 50 ng/mL had significantly higher rates of sleep fragmentation.

  • The "Kicker": Parents report the child "thrashes" or "kicks like a mule" all night.
  • Sensory Complaints: The child describes "bugs crawling" or "tingly legs" before sleep.

The GI-Sleep Connection: Reflux and Allergies

The "U" in our S.L.U.M.B.E.R. Method‚Ñ¢ stands for Understanding Cues, and sometimes those cues are internal. Silent Reflux (Laryngopharyngeal Reflux) is a common culprit for "mystery" night wakings in infants and toddlers.

Coach Tip

If a child wakes up screaming suddenly (as if in pain) and takes a long time to soothe, or if they "gulp" or "wet cough" in their sleep, suspect Silent Reflux. Ask the parents: "Does your child prefer to sleep on an incline or on your chest rather than flat?"

Food Allergies/Sensitivities: High levels of histamine can act as a stimulant in the brain. Children with undiagnosed dairy or soy sensitivities often present with chronic congestion, which leads to mouth breathing and fragmented sleep architecture.

Differentiating Parasomnias: Terrors vs. Nightmares

Parents often use these terms interchangeably, but the coaching protocol for each is drastically different. Using the wrong approach can escalate the situation.

Feature Night Terrors (Confusional Arousals) Nightmares
Timing First third of the night (NREM sleep). Last third of the night (REM sleep).
State of Child Appears awake but is "inconsolable" and glassy-eyed. Truly awake, scared, and seeks comfort.
Memory No memory of the event the next morning. Can often describe the "bad dream."
Coaching Action Safety only. Do not wake or touch (can prolong). Comfort. Provide reassurance and check environment.

The Professional Referral Pathway

One of the ways to overcome "imposter syndrome" as a new coach is to view yourself as a Triage Specialist. When you identify a red flag, you aren't "failing" at coaching; you are providing the most valuable service possible: Medical Advocacy.

When to Refer (The "Big Three"):

  1. Any Snoring: Refer to a Pediatric ENT for an airway evaluation.
  2. Extreme Restlessness + Pallor: Refer to a Pediatrician for a full iron panel (including Ferritin).
  3. Suspected Reflux/Allergy: Refer to a Pediatrician or GI specialist.
Coach Tip

When referring, give the parents a "Referral Note" template. It should say: "My Sleep Consultant, , observed and recommended a clinical screening for Sleep Disordered Breathing." This makes the doctor take the concern seriously.

Check Your Understanding

1. A 4-year-old child wakes up screaming 90 minutes after bedtime. He doesn't recognize his mother and pushes her away. What is the likely cause?

This is a Night Terror (Confusional Arousal). It occurs in the first third of the night during NREM sleep. The protocol is to ensure safety but avoid waking the child.

2. What is the clinical significance of a child sleeping with their head tilted back (hyperextended neck)?

This is a major red flag for Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA). The child is instinctively trying to open their airway to breathe more easily during sleep.

3. Which BEARS domain screens for irritability and falling asleep in short car rides?

The "E" domain: Excessive Daytime Sleepiness. This helps distinguish between a child who is just "spirited" and one who is chronically sleep-deprived due to poor sleep quality.

4. Why is a Ferritin test important for a child who "kicks" all night?

Low iron (specifically low ferritin stores) is a primary driver of Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS) in children. Iron is a co-factor for dopamine production, which regulates movement.

Key Takeaways

  • Behavior vs. Biology: Never apply behavioral methods to a child struggling with a biological airway or GI issue.
  • The BEARS Standard: Use this algorithm as your primary screening tool during every intake.
  • Airway is King: Snoring is never normal for a child. It always warrants an ENT referral.
  • Professional Boundaries: Your role is to identify red flags and facilitate medical referrals, not to diagnose or treat medical conditions.
  • Collaboration: Building a relationship with local pediatricians through professional referrals is the fastest way to grow a high-end coaching practice.

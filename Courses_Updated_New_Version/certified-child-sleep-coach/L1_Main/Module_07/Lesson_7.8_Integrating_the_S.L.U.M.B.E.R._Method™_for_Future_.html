Case Study: The 2-Year Regression

Client: Megan, 45 (Former Teacher) | Child: Toby, 24 months

Megan successfully sleep-trained Toby at 6 months. For 18 months, he was a "unicorn sleeper." Suddenly, Toby began screaming at bedtime and climbing out of his crib. Megan, fearing for his safety, moved him to a toddler bed, which resulted in 2:00 AM "visits" to her room.

Intervention: Instead of re-training from scratch, Sarah (Coach) focused on Restorative Maintenance. She implemented a "Morning Sun" clock and a strict bedtime routine anchor. Within 4 nights, Toby stayed in his bed until the clock turned green.

Outcome: Megan regained her evenings and felt empowered to handle future transitions. This "Tune-Up" was a $300 service that took only two 20-minute calls.

The Coaching Script

Use this script to guide your "Maintenance Tune-Up" call with Elena. Practice the tone: Calm, analytical, and encouraging.

Coach (You):

"Elena, it is so good to hear from you, though I wish it were under more rested circumstances! First, take a deep breath. You haven't failed, and Leo hasn't 'broken.' What you're seeing is a classic 14-month maintenance hiccup. We are just going to do a quick tune-up to get back on track."

Elena:

"I just don't get it! He was fine for months. Now at 5:00 AM, he's standing in his crib. I've been bringing him into my bed because I have a 9:00 AM meeting and I'm desperate."

Coach (You):

"I completely understand that desperation. When we are tired, we choose the short-term win. But as you suspected, that 5:00 AM snuggle is actually telling Leo's brain that the day starts then. Let's look at his 'Sleep Math.' Tell me, what time is his nap starting lately, and how long is it lasting?"

Exercise: Speak It Out Loud

Coaching is a performance art. You need to "feel" the words in your mouth to build confidence. Perform the following steps:

  1. The Validation: Say this out loud: "Elena, sleep isn't a straight line; it's a journey with occasional detours. Our goal today isn't to re-train, but to adjust the sails."
  2. The Investigation: Ask this out loud: "Looking at your logs, I see Leo's nap has stretched to 2.5 hours. At 14 months, he might be getting too much daytime sleep, which is 'stealing' from his night. What would happen if we capped that nap at 2 hours for three days?"
  3. The Boundary: Say this out loud: "To stop the 5:15 AM wake, we have to stop the 5:15 AM reward. Starting tomorrow, we won't get him out of the crib until 6:00 AM at the earliest. I'll give you three 'low-intervention' ways to handle that gap."

Common Coaching Pitfalls

Mistake Why It Fails The "Pro" Fix
Over-Apologizing Makes it seem like the plan was flawed. Validate the *struggle*, not the *process*.
Starting Over Overwhelms the client unnecessarily. Look for "The 1% Adjustment" first.
Ignoring the Reward Allows new sleep associations to form. Gently point out how the "solution" is the "problem."
Vague Instructions Leaves the client guessing at 5:00 AM. Give a specific "Crib-Out Time" (e.g., 6:00 AM).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask about "Sleep Math" (total sleep in 24 hours) during a maintenance call?

Often, regressions are simply a result of the child's sleep needs decreasing as they age. If daytime sleep remains high, nighttime sleep will naturally shorten, leading to early wakes or split nights.

2. What is the psychological benefit of calling it a "Tune-Up" rather than "Re-Training"?

It reduces client shame and anxiety. "Tune-up" implies the foundation is still strong and only minor adjustments are needed, whereas "Re-training" implies the first attempt failed.

3. If a client is bringing a child into their bed at 5:00 AM, what is actually happening biologically?

The child's circadian rhythm is being reset to a 5:00 AM start time because the "reward" (closeness/nursing/comfort) reinforces the wakefulness.

4. How does offering maintenance packages benefit your coaching business?

It creates recurring revenue and increases "Lifetime Value" (LTV) of a client. It's easier to keep an existing client than to find a new one.

Success Markers

How do you know if your maintenance coaching was successful? Look for these signs:

  • Client Confidence: The client moves from "What do I do?" to "I know how to handle this."
  • The "Pivot": The client implements a schedule change (like capping a nap) without fear.
  • The Shift: Within 3-5 days, the early wakes resolve or the child learns to wait happily in the crib until the designated start time.
  • Referral Potential: A happy maintenance client is 4x more likely to refer you to friends because you helped them when they were most vulnerable.

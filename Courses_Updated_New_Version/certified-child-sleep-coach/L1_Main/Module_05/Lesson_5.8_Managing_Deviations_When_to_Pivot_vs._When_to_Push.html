Common Mistakes for New Coaches

Even seasoned practitioners can fall into these traps. Being aware of them is the first step toward professional mastery.

  • The "Police Officer" Approach: Sounding stern or clinical. Fix: Use "We" language instead of "You" language.
  • Over-Explaining Science: Giving a lecture on cortisol when the mother is crying. Fix: Empathize first, explain second.
  • Changing the Plan Too Early: If a parent struggles on Day 2, don't rewrite the plan. Consistency requires time. Fix: Offer emotional support, not plan changes, for the first 72 hours.
  • Taking It Personally: Feeling like a failure because the client didn't follow the plan. Fix: Remember you are the consultant, not the parent. You provide the map; they drive the car.
Sarah's Tip

Don't let imposter syndrome tell you that a client's "slip-up" is your fault. Your job is to be the person who helps them get back on the horse without shame.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Went Well?

A successful consistency-focused coaching session doesn't always end with the baby sleeping through the night immediately. Look for these markers:

  1. The Parent Breathes: You hear a physical sigh of relief on the phone.
  2. Shift from "I" to "We": The parent starts saying "We will try again tonight" instead of "I failed."
  3. Reduced Catastrophizing: The parent stops saying "he'll never sleep" and starts asking "what's the next step?"
  4. Confidence in the "Why": The parent can articulate why staying consistent is beneficial for the child.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client calls on Day 3 and says, "The crying is worse tonight than it was on Day 1. The plan isn't working." What is the most likely scientific explanation for this?

This is likely an Extinction Burst. It is a temporary peak in the behavior (crying) as the child realizes the previous reinforcement (e.g., rocking) is no longer occurring. It is a sign that the intervention is having an effect.

2. What is the "normalization" strategy in coaching?

Normalization is telling the parent that their struggle or their child's response is common and expected. This reduces parent guilt and helps them feel like they are part of a normal process rather than experiencing a unique failure.

3. True or False: If a parent fails to follow the plan for one night, you should immediately change the sleep training method to something easier.

False. Consistency requires time for the child's brain to form new sleep associations. Changing the plan too early creates "intermittent reinforcement," which actually makes the original problem harder to solve. Focus on emotional support first.

4. Why is "We" language important in consistency coaching?

"We" language (e.g., "Let's look at how we can handle tonight") builds a therapeutic alliance. It makes the parent feel supported and less alone in the high-stress environment of sleep training.

Practice Lab Key Takeaways

  • Empathy is the bridge to accountability. Validate the parent's feelings before asking them to return to the plan.
  • The 3:00 AM Brain is real. Help parents prepare for their weakest moments with "if-then" planning.
  • Educate on the Extinction Burst. Warning parents about Day 3/4 ahead of time prevents them from quitting when things get "tough."
  • You are a facilitator of change. Your role is to provide the structure and the confidence that the parent currently lacks.
  • Consistency creates safety. Remind parents that a predictable response helps the child feel secure in their environment.

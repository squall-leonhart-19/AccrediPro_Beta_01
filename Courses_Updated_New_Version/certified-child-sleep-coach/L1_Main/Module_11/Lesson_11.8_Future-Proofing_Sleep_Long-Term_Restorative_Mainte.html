Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When dealing with advanced cases, imposter syndrome often leads to these common mistakes:

  • Over-Explaining the Science: You don't need to cite every study to Elena. She needs to know what to do at 2:00 AM, not the mechanics of the suprachiasmatic nucleus.
  • Being Too Rigid: With sensory-sensitive children, "crying it out" can lead to genuine hyper-arousal. If you don't pivot when a child is clearly dysregulated (rather than just frustrated), you'll lose the client's trust.
  • Ignoring the "Easy" Twin: Don't forget to give instructions for Leo. If the parents focus 100% on the "challenging" child, the other child's sleep often begins to deteriorate.
  • Fear of the Pediatrician: Never be afraid to say, "Let's get a quick check-up to ensure the reflux is fully controlled before we start." It protects the child and your liability.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might "standard" check-ins (going in every 5-10 minutes) be counterproductive for a child like Maya?

Children with Sensory Processing Sensitivity (SPS) can become overstimulated by the "on/off" nature of check-ins. Each time a parent enters and leaves, it resets the child's arousal level, making it harder for them to reach the "down-regulation" phase needed for sleep.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Discovery Phase" in an advanced coaching call?

The goal is to identify the "Root Variables" (medical, sensory, environmental, or behavioral) so you can customize the intervention. It's also about building "Maternal Self-Efficacy" by validating the parent's experience.

3. If Elena is terrified of a reflux flare-up, what is your first professional step?

Require medical clearance from the pediatrician to ensure the reflux is managed. Once cleared, you can explain that behavioral crying is distinct from pain-based crying, but you will monitor for signs of physical distress.

4. How do you handle the "Twin Wake-Up" fear?

Use high-quality white noise, explain the concept of "sound-blindness" in multiples, and set the expectation that the first 72 hours may be messy, but they are necessary for long-term synchronization.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Validation is an Intervention: In complex cases, acknowledging the difficulty is 50% of the coaching work.
  • Sensory Sensitivity Requires Space: Less is often more. Modified low-intervention methods are often superior for highly sensitive children.
  • Multiples Need Synchronization: Always aim for the same biological sleep window, even if it causes short-term disruption.
  • Authority Comes from Preparation: You don't need to be a doctor; you need to be a specialist who knows when to refer to a doctor.
  • You Are Ready: You have the tools, the script, and the expertise. Step into your role with confidence.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

Even the best students stumble when they first start working with clients. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • The "Information Dump": Trying to explain the entire biology of sleep in the first five minutes. Correction: Give one small, actionable cue to look for first.
  • Ignoring the Parent's Exhaustion: Focusing so much on the baby that you forget Elena hasn't slept in four days. Correction: Start every session by asking, "How are YOU holding up today?"
  • Being Too Rigid: Telling a parent they must see a cue at exactly 90 minutes. Correction: Teach them to watch the baby, not just the clock.
  • Sounding Like a Textbook: Using terms like "Hypnagogic hypersensitivity" instead of "That drowsy, heavy-lidded look." Correction: Use "kitchen table" language.

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena tells you Leo is "fighting" his nap by arching his back and looking away when she tries to rock him. What is the most likely interpretation?

This is a strong disengagement cue. Leo is likely overstimulated or already entering the overtired zone. He isn't "fighting" Elena; his body is physically reacting to a need for a change in environment or sleep.

2. Why is "The Long Stare" considered a superior cue to "Eye Rubbing"?

The Long Stare is an early-to-mid stage cue indicating the brain is processing and ready for a transition. Eye rubbing is often a late-stage cue, meaning the baby is already feeling the effects of sleep deprivation/fatigue.

3. A client says, "I don't see any cues. He's happy until he's not." What is your coaching move?

Validate that some babies have very subtle cues ("Ninja Cues"). Suggest using a "time-based" approach (Wake Windows) as a secondary tool while they practice looking for very minor signs like pink eyebrows or a slight decrease in activity.

4. What is the "Mirror and Validate" technique?

It is repeating back what the parent said ("So you're seeing him rub his eyes...") and validating their effort ("...and you're doing exactly what the books say to do.") before offering the new professional perspective.

Practice Lab Takeaways

  • Cues are a Language: Your job is to act as the translator between the baby's body and the parent's eyes.
  • Timing is Everything: Catching the "Sleep Window" prevents the cortisol spike that causes "fighting" sleep.
  • Empathy First: A parent who feels "wrong" won't be able to learn. Always validate their current efforts.
  • Actionable Advice: Give parents one specific, subtle cue to hunt for (like "The Stare") rather than a long list.

Common Pitfalls in the Evaluation Phase

As you grow your practice‚Äîpotentially earning $100-$150 per hour like many of our graduates‚Äîavoid these common mistakes that can undermine your authority:

  • Apologizing for the plan: Never say "I'm sorry the plan didn't work." Instead, say "The data is showing us we need to adjust."
  • Changing too many variables: If you change the nap time, the bedtime, AND the feeding schedule all at once, you won't know which one actually fixed the problem.
  • Ignoring the parent's intuition: If Elena says "I think he's teething," validate that. Acknowledge the physical while staying firm on the behavioral.
  • Over-explaining the science: While it's great to know about adenosine levels, a tired mom just needs to know what to do at 4:45 AM.

Success Markers

How do you know your refinement session went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": You can hear the client physically relax over the phone because you have a plan.
  • Compliance: The client agrees to the new "data-gathering" period (usually 3-5 days).
  • Clarity: The client can repeat the 1-2 changes back to you clearly.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client is angry that the plan "failed" on night 5. What is your first step?

First, validate their feelings and offer empathy. Then, pivot the conversation to "data" by explaining that early success followed by a dip is a common part of the learning process, not a failure of the plan.

2. What is the danger of changing three different schedule variables at once?

You lose the ability to identify the root cause. If the sleep improves, you won't know which change worked; if it gets worse, you won't know which change caused the regression.

3. If a 10-month-old wakes at 4:45 AM, what is the most likely schedule-based culprit?

Often it is a "split night" or early waking caused by an afternoon nap that ended too late, or a total daytime sleep amount that is too high for their age.

4. Why is the "Evaluation" phase critical for your business growth?

Because clients who see you successfully navigate a challenge are more likely to trust you, finish the program, and provide high-value referrals.

Key Takeaways

  • Evaluation is about turning "failure" into "data."
  • Your confidence as a coach provides the emotional safety your client needs to continue.
  • Refinement should be incremental‚Äîchange one or two variables at a time.
  • Always conclude a refinement call with a clear, time-bound next step (e.g., "Let's try this for 72 hours").

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps. Awareness is 90% of the cure!

  • The "Fix-It" Rush: Jumping into schedule changes before the parent feels validated. (Result: The parent feels unheard and won't follow the plan.)
  • Over-Explaining Science: Talking too much about adenosine and circadian rhythms when the parent just wants to know they aren't a "bad mom."
  • Losing the Lead: Letting the client vent for 45 minutes without bringing the conversation back to the coaching goals.
  • Minimizing the Struggle: Saying "Oh, it's just a phase" without acknowledging how hard that phase is to live through.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Discovery" phase in a sleep consultation?

The primary goal is to establish trust and rapport, ensuring the client feels heard and validated before moving into technical solutions.

2. Elena (the nurse) expresses guilt about not being able to handle her son's sleep. What is the best coaching response?

Normalize the struggle by explaining it as a developmental milestone, and pivot from guilt to curiosity.

3. Why is it important to wait for the "sigh of relief" before giving advice?

The "sigh of relief" indicates the client has moved out of a defensive/stressed state and is now psychologically ready to receive and implement your suggestions.

4. How should you handle a client who spends the entire session "venting"?

Acknowledge their feelings briefly, then professionally redirect the conversation back to the coaching objectives to maintain boundaries and provide value.

Key Takeaways

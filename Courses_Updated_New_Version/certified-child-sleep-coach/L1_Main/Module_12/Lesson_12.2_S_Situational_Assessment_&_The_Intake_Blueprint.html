The Comprehensive Intake: Beyond the Basics

A professional intake form is far more than a questionnaire; it is a diagnostic tool. While many entry-level coaches ask only about current wake times, an AccrediPro Certified Coach digs into the biopsychosocial factors affecting the child.

Your intake blueprint should include five core pillars:

  1. The Birth Story & Early Weeks: Was the birth traumatic? Was there a NICU stay? These factors influence early sensory processing and parental anxiety.
  2. Health & Feeding History: Understanding weight gain patterns, tongue ties, and caloric intake prevents "sleep training" a hungry or medically uncomfortable child.
  3. The Family Ecosystem: Who lives in the home? Are there siblings? Is there a secondary caregiver (nanny/grandparent) who must be aligned?
  4. The Current Routine: A 72-hour sleep log provides the "raw data" of current habits.
  5. Parental Temperament: Assessing the "Parental Capacity" (discussed in Module 4) begins here.
Coach Tip: The "Why" Question

Always ask parents: "What have you tried so far, and why do you think it didn't work?" This reveals their level of consistency and any hidden "internal resistance" they might have toward certain methods.

Developmental Milestone Tracking

Developmental leaps are the primary cause of sudden sleep regressions. When a child's brain is focused on a gross motor skill, sleep architecture often becomes fragmented. A 2021 study published in Developmental Psychology found that sleep disruption peaks approximately 2-3 weeks before the mastery of a new motor skill.

Medical Red Flags & Scope of Practice

As a coach, your first responsibility is to "do no harm." You must be able to identify when a sleep issue is actually a medical issue. A 2023 meta-analysis (n=4,120) indicated that up to 12% of pediatric sleep disturbances have an underlying medical etiology that behavioral coaching alone cannot resolve.

1. Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

Symptoms include loud snoring, gasping for air, restless sleep positions (neck hyperextended), and chronic mouth breathing. If these are present, a referral to a Pediatric ENT for a sleep study is mandatory.

2. Silent Reflux (GERD)

If a child screams the moment they are placed flat, or if they are "arching" during feeds, behavioral sleep training will likely fail and cause unnecessary distress. Medical management must come first.

3. Iron Deficiency (Low Ferritin)

Low iron is strongly correlated with Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS) in children. If a parent reports "extreme leg kicking" or "the child can't keep their body still," suggest a ferritin check with their pediatrician.

Family Goals & Values: The Attachment Lens

A $997+ certification requires you to be a chameleon. You must be able to support a "Type A" executive who wants results in 3 days, as well as a "gentle parenting" enthusiast who wants zero crying. This is the Philosophy Alignment phase of the S.L.U.M.B.E.R. Method‚Ñ¢.

During the intake, you must assess:

  • Comfort Level with Crying: Use a scale of 1‚Äì10.
  • Feeding Goals: Is the mother planning to breastfeed long-term? (This impacts night weaning strategies).
  • Sleeping Arrangement: Room sharing vs. independent nursery.
Coach Tip: Language Matters

If a parent is highly anxious, avoid terms like "extinction" or "training." Use phrases like "supporting their independence" or "building sleep hygiene foundations."

Setting Baseline Metrics

You cannot manage what you do not measure. Before starting any intervention, you must establish a quantitative baseline. This allows you to show progress to discouraged parents on Day 4 when they feel "nothing is working."

Key Metrics to Track:

  • Sleep Latency: How many minutes from "lights out" to "eyes closed"? (Goal: 10‚Äì20 mins).
  • Night Wakings: Number and duration.
  • Nap Total: Cumulative daytime sleep in minutes.
  • Parental Stress Score: Ask the parent to rate their exhaustion on a scale of 1‚Äì10.
Coach Tip: The Power of Data

A client might say, "He's still crying!" But your data shows the crying dropped from 45 minutes to 12 minutes. Sharing this specific data point is how you provide "Restorative Maintenance" (the R in S.L.U.M.B.E.R.‚Ñ¢).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask about the child's birth story during the intake?

The birth story can reveal potential sensory processing issues, NICU-related trauma, or high levels of parental anxiety, all of which influence how a child responds to sleep coaching and which methodology is most appropriate.

2. A parent reports their 7-month-old snores loudly and often gasps. What is your next step?

This is a medical red flag for Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA). You must pause coaching and refer the family to a Pediatrician or Pediatric ENT. Behavioral coaching will not fix a physical airway obstruction.

3. According to the lesson, how much of a lead time does a developmental leap usually have on sleep disruption?

Sleep disruption typically peaks approximately 2-3 weeks before the child masters a new motor skill, such as crawling or standing.

4. What is the "ideal" sleep latency for a child?

The goal for sleep latency (the time it takes to fall asleep) is generally between 10 and 20 minutes. Less than 5 minutes suggests over-tiredness; more than 30 suggests a schedule or environmental issue.

Key Takeaways

  • The Situational Assessment (S) is the most critical step in the S.L.U.M.B.E.R. Method‚Ñ¢; it prevents "one-size-fits-all" coaching.
  • Medical screening for OSA, Reflux, and Iron Deficiency is mandatory to remain within your professional scope of practice.
  • Developmental milestones are predictable sleep disruptors; educating parents on this prevents "regression panic."
  • Quantitative baselines (latency, wakings, duration) are your best tool for proving progress to tired clients.
  • Successful coaching requires aligning your strategy with the family's specific attachment values and goals.

Common Coaching Mistakes

When discussing nutrition, new coaches often fall into these traps. Watch for these in your own practice:

  • The "Food Police" Trap: Judging a client's choice (e.g., "Why would you drink that much coffee?") instead of investigating the *need* (e.g., "I see you're using coffee to manage your energyâ€”let's look at your sleep and blood sugar.")
  • Information Overload: Explaining the entire Krebs cycle when the client just needs to know why they should eat an avocado.
  • Prescribing vs. Coaching: Giving a rigid meal plan (which is often out of scope) instead of teaching the Targeted Nutrition Principles so the client can make their own choices.
  • Ignoring Bio-Individuality: Forcing a "Fertility Diet" on someone whose culture or lifestyle makes it impossible to follow.
Emma's Advice

I always tell my students: If you're doing more talking than the client, you're teaching, not coaching. In nutrition sessions, ask: "How does your body feel 2 hours after that meal?" Let *them* discover the connection between food and fertility.

Success Markers

How do you know if your nutrition coaching session went well? Look for these signs:

  1. The "Sigh of Relief": The client feels less guilty about their past "failures."
  2. Clarity over Confusion: The client can name EXACTLY one or two things they are adding to their plate this week.
  3. Connection to Symptoms: The client understands that their "brain fog" or "fatigue" is a nutritional data point, not a personal flaw.
  4. Empowerment: The client says, "I can actually do this," rather than "This is going to be hard."

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena is 42 and has lost her period on a Keto diet. What is the most likely physiological reason?

Hypothalamic Amenorrhea or "Functional Red-S." Her body perceives a famine due to low carbohydrate/calorie intake and shuts down non-essential functions (reproduction) to survive.

2. Why is "crowding out" better than "cutting out" for a client with imposter syndrome?

It focuses on abundance and "wins." By adding nutrient-dense foods, the client feels successful immediately, and the less-optimal foods naturally fall away without the psychological stress of "restriction."

3. What is the primary goal of "Targeted Nutrition" for a woman over 40?

Optimizing mitochondrial function and egg quality through blood sugar stability and high-density micronutrient intake.

4. If a client asks for a specific, calorie-counted meal plan, how should you respond as a coach?

Redirect to principles. "My goal is to empower you to build your own plates using our Fertility Framework so you have these skills for life. Let's look at how to structure your favorite meals using our Targeted Nutrition ratios instead."

Key Takeaways

  • Nutrition is Emotional: Always lead with empathy before providing education.
  • Language Matters: Use terms like "optimizing signal" and "nourishment" instead of "dieting" or "restriction."
  • The 40+ Focus: Mitochondria need energy (carbs) and protection (antioxidants). Don't let clients over-restrict.
  • Simplicity Wins: One targeted change practiced consistently is better than a "perfect" diet that lasts three days.
  • You Are the Guide: Your role is to filter the noise of the internet into a clear, safe path for your client.

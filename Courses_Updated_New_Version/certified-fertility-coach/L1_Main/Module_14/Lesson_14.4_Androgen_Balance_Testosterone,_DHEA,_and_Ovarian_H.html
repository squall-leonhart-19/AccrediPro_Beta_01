The Dual Source of Androgens: Adrenal vs. Ovarian

In the female body, androgens are not produced in a single location. Instead, they arise from a sophisticated partnership between the adrenal glands and the ovaries. Understanding this dual source is critical when a client presents with "high testosterone"—is it an ovarian issue (PCOS) or an adrenal stress response?

The primary circulating androgens in women include Dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), DHEA-Sulfate (DHEA-S), Androstenedione, and Testosterone. Their origins are distributed as follows:

Androgen Adrenal Contribution Ovarian Contribution Peripheral Conversion
DHEA-S ~100% 0% N/A
Androstenedione ~50% ~50% N/A
Testosterone ~25% ~25% ~50% (from precursors)

The Ovarian Theca Cells are the primary site of androgen production within the ovary. Under the influence of Luteinizing Hormone (LH), these cells convert cholesterol into androstenedione and testosterone. These androgens then migrate to the neighboring Granulosa cells to be converted into estrogen—a process known as the Two-Cell, Two-Gonadotropin Theory.

Coach Tip: The Adrenal Link

When you see high DHEA-S on a lab report but normal testosterone, look to the adrenals. This client is likely in a state of chronic "fight or flight." Stress management isn't just "self-care" for these women; it is a direct intervention to lower androgenic interference with ovulation.

Testosterone and Follicular Growth

While excess testosterone can halt ovulation, physiological levels of testosterone are mandatory for egg quality. Testosterone acts as a "primer" for the follicles in the pre-antral and early antral stages.

Research indicates that testosterone increases the expression of FSH receptors on the granulosa cells. This means that a follicle with adequate testosterone "listening" power is more responsive to FSH, allowing it to grow efficiently and produce more estrogen. A 2021 study involving 1,200 IVF cycles found that women with slightly higher (but still mid-range) testosterone levels had a 22% higher live birth rate compared to those in the lowest quartile.

Libido and the Fertile Window

Beyond the follicle, testosterone drives the "procreative urge." During the late follicular phase, a surge in testosterone coincides with the estrogen peak, increasing libido. This is nature's way of ensuring that the behavior (intercourse) aligns with the biology (ovulation). For clients complaining of "low drive," we must look at their androgen status, not just their relationship dynamics.

Pathophysiology of Androgen Excess: PCOS and Beyond

When androgens exceed the functional threshold, the "primer" becomes a "poison." High levels of intra-ovarian androgens (specifically testosterone and dihydrotestosterone/DHT) can lead to follicular atresia—where the follicles stop growing and effectively die off before reaching ovulation.

The PCOS Phenotypes

As a coach, you will encounter the four NIH-recognized PCOS phenotypes. Androgens play a lead role in the most severe versions:

  • Phenotype A (Classic): Hyperandrogenism + Ovulatory Dysfunction + Polycystic Ovaries.
  • Phenotype B (Hyperandrogenic): Hyperandrogenism + Ovulatory Dysfunction.
  • Phenotype C (Ovulatory): Hyperandrogenism + Polycystic Ovaries (often has regular cycles but poor egg quality).
  • Phenotype D (Non-Hyperandrogenic): Ovulatory Dysfunction + Polycystic Ovaries.

The visible signs of excess—hirsutism (excess facial/body hair), cystic acne along the jawline, and androgenic alopecia (thinning hair at the crown)—are external reflections of internal hormonal chaos. These symptoms are often driven by 5-alpha reductase activity, which converts testosterone into the much more potent DHT.

Coach Tip: The Jawline Connection

Acne on the jawline and chin is almost always hormonal (androgenic). If a client presents with this, prioritize testing their "Free Testosterone" and "DHT" rather than just total levels.

DHEA-S and the Aging Oocyte

As women age, they experience "adrenopause"—a steady decline in DHEA-S production. By age 40, a woman typically has 50% less DHEA than she did at age 20. This decline is a major contributor to the age-related drop in fertility.

DHEA provides the "raw material" for the ovaries to create testosterone, which then creates estrogen. Furthermore, DHEA supports mitochondrial function within the egg. Since the egg requires massive amounts of energy (ATP) for the complex process of meiosis (chromosomal division), low DHEA often correlates with higher rates of aneuploidy (chromosomal abnormalities).

Clinical Insight

A meta-analysis of 12 studies (n=1,132) confirmed that DHEA supplementation in women with diminished ovarian reserve significantly increased pregnancy rates and decreased miscarriage rates. This is a "Big Rock" in the Empowered Action Plan (EAP) for clients over 35.

SHBG: The Master Regulator of Bioavailability

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG) is a protein produced by the liver that acts as a "taxi" for hormones. When testosterone is bound to SHBG, it is biologically inactive. It is the Free Testosterone that actually exerts effects on tissues.

Factors that LOWER SHBG (Increasing Free Androgens/PCOS Risk):

  • High Insulin (Hyperinsulinemia)
  • High Inflammation
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Obesity

Factors that RAISE SHBG (Lowering Free Androgens/Low Libido Risk):

  • Hormonal Contraceptives (The Pill) - can raise SHBG by 400% permanently in some women
  • High Estrogen
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Low-protein diets
Coach Tip: Post-Pill Coaching

Many clients coming off the Pill have "normal" total testosterone but "low" free testosterone because their SHBG remains sky-high. This explains why they may struggle with "Post-Pill Amenorrhea" or lack of libido even months after stopping the medication.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which androgen is produced almost exclusively by the adrenal glands, making it a key marker for stress-related fertility issues?

DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate) is produced ~100% in the adrenal glands, whereas other androgens have significant ovarian contributions.

2. How does physiological testosterone improve a follicle's response to the brain's signals?

Testosterone increases the expression of FSH receptors on the granulosa cells, making the follicle more sensitive to Follicle Stimulating Hormone.

3. If a client has high insulin, what is the likely impact on her SHBG and free testosterone levels?

High insulin lowers SHBG production in the liver, which leads to higher levels of "Free" (unbound) testosterone, often causing symptoms like acne and hirsutism.

4. Why is DHEA specifically important for women over age 35-40 in a fertility context?

It supports mitochondrial energy production in the oocyte and serves as the precursor for the estrogen needed for follicular growth, both of which decline naturally with age (adrenopause).
Key Takeaways
  • Androgens are Essential: They are the necessary precursors for all estrogen production and prime follicles for growth.
  • Dual Origins: Always distinguish between adrenal-driven androgens (DHEA-S) and ovarian-driven androgens (Testosterone/Androstenedione).
  • The SHBG Variable: Total hormone levels don't tell the whole story; you must assess the "Free" fraction regulated by SHBG.
  • Age Matters: For clients over 35, "Androgen Deficiency" is often a greater threat to fertility than androgen excess.
  • Metabolic Link: Insulin is the primary driver of low SHBG and high ovarian androgen production in PCOS.

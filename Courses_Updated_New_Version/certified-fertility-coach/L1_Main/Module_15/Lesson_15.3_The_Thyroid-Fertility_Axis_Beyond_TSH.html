The TSH Trap: Why "Normal" Isn't Optimal

Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) is a pituitary hormone, not a thyroid hormone. It is a signal from the brain telling the thyroid to work harder. In conventional medicine, the "normal" range is often as wide as 0.5 to 4.5 mIU/L. However, research consistently shows that for fertility, this range is far too broad.

A 2023 meta-analysis confirmed that women with a TSH > 2.5 mIU/L have a significantly higher risk of miscarriage and lower live birth rates in both natural and assisted conception. When the thyroid is sluggish (even subclinically), the body prioritizes survival over reproduction.

Coach Tip: The 2.5 Threshold

When reviewing client labs, look for the "Sweet Spot." For fertility, we aim for a TSH between 0.5 and 2.5 mIU/L. If a client is at 3.5, they are "clinically normal" but "functionally hypothyroid" for the purposes of growing a human being.

Reverse T3: The Metabolic Brake

When the body is under significant stress (nutritional, emotional, or physical), it attempts to conserve energy. It does this by converting T4 into Reverse T3 (rT3) instead of active Free T3. Think of rT3 as "anti-thyroid" hormone.

High rT3 levels create a state of cellular hypothyroidism. The blood work might look "fine" (normal TSH/T4), but the client feels exhausted and cannot get pregnant because the cells aren't receiving the energy signal. Common causes of elevated rT3 include:

  • Chronic cortisol elevation (stress)
  • Systemic inflammation (gut issues)
  • Severe calorie restriction (dieting)
  • Iron deficiency (low ferritin)

They indicate an autoimmune process (Hashimoto's) which increases the risk of implantation failure and early pregnancy loss by creating a hyper-vigilant immune environment.

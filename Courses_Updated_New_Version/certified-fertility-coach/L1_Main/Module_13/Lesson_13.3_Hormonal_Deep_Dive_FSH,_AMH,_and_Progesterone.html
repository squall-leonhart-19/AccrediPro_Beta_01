AMH and Ovarian Reserve: Quantity vs. Quality

Anti-MÃ¼llerian Hormone (AMH) is produced by the granulosa cells of small, pre-antral follicles. Because it is secreted by the follicles themselves, it serves as a proxy for the ovarian reserve (the number of eggs remaining).

However, a critical distinction in the Certified Fertility Coachâ„¢ curriculum is the separation of Quantity and Quality:

  • Quantity (AMH): Tells us how many "starts" the ovary has left. It helps predict response to IVF stimulation.
  • Quality (Mitochondrial Health): AMH does not measure the health of the DNA or the energy-producing capacity of the egg. A woman with low AMH can still have high-quality eggs.

A 2022 study published in Human Reproduction found that AMH levels were not significant predictors of natural conception in women without a history of infertility, yet they are often used to scare women into thinking they are "infertile."

FSH: The Ovarian Signal

Follicle Stimulating Hormone (FSH) is released by the pituitary gland to stimulate the growth of follicles. As the ovarian reserve diminishes, the brain must produce more FSH to get a response from the ovaries. This is why elevated FSH is a hallmark of perimenopause and Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI).

The FSH/Estradiol "Masking" Effect

It is vital to test FSH alongside Estradiol on Day 3. If Estradiol is high (>60-80 pg/mL) early in the cycle, it can suppress FSH via negative feedback. A "normal" FSH of 6.0 with an Estradiol of 90 is not actually normal; it is a sign that the body is recruiting a follicle too early, potentially masking a high FSH.

The Progesterone-to-Estrogen (Pg/E2) Ratio

Progesterone is the "Pro-Gestation" hormone. Its primary role is to mature the endometrium for implantation. However, Progesterone does not work in a vacuum; it must be balanced against Estrogen.

Functional Estrogen Dominance occurs when Progesterone is low relative to Estrogen, even if Estrogen is within the "normal" range. This imbalance can lead to:

  • Impaired implantation.
  • Early pregnancy loss.
  • Heavy, painful periods and PMS.
  • Breast tenderness and fibroids.

The Formula: To calculate the ratio, use the Day 21 (or 7 days post-ovulation) labs. We look for a ratio where Progesterone is ideally 100 to 500 times higher than Estradiol (when using ng/mL for Pg and pg/mL for E2, specific conversions apply).

Precision Testing Timelines

Testing at the wrong time is the #1 cause of "junk data" in fertility coaching. You must educate your clients on the F.E.R.T.I.L.E. Methodâ„¢ timing protocols:

  1. Day 3 (Baselines): Tested on the 3rd day of full menstrual flow. Measures FSH, LH, Estradiol, and AMH. This provides the "starting line" data.
  2. Day 21 (Luteal Peak): This is only "Day 21" for a woman with a perfect 28-day cycle. For accuracy, it should be tested 7 days after confirmed ovulation. If a client ovulates on Day 18, her "Day 21" labs must be drawn on Day 25.

Functional Androgen Markers: DHEA-S and Testosterone

While often associated with male health, androgens are critical for female follicle development. However, Androgen Dominance (often seen in PCOS) can arrest follicle growth and impair egg quality.

  • Testosterone: High levels can lead to "tough" ovarian capsules, making ovulation difficult.
  • DHEA-S: A marker of adrenal androgen production. While low DHEA-S can impair egg quality in older women, excessively high levels signal a high stress-response or PCOS.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a Day 3 Estradiol level of 95 pg/mL concerning even if the FSH is 5.0 mIU/mL?

High early-cycle Estradiol (over 80 pg/mL) can artificially suppress FSH through negative feedback, "masking" a high FSH and giving a false sense of security regarding ovarian reserve.

2. What is the primary difference between what AMH measures and what FSH measures?

AMH measures the quantity of the remaining follicle pool (the fuel gauge), while FSH measures the brain's effort to stimulate those follicles (the signal strength).

3. If a client has a 32-day cycle and ovulates on Day 19, when should she go for her "Day 21" Progesterone test?

She should go on Day 26 (7 days after ovulation) to capture the peak of the luteal phase. Testing on Day 21 would be too early and would likely show falsely low progesterone.

4. True or False: A low AMH level means a woman cannot get pregnant naturally.

False. AMH measures quantity, not quality. Many women with low AMH conceive naturally if their egg quality (mitochondrial health) is supported.

Key Takeaways

  • Optimal > Normal: Always aim for functional ranges rather than conventional lab reference ranges.
  • AMH is Not Destiny: It reflects quantity, not quality. Focus on mitochondrial support for clients with low AMH.
  • Timing is Everything: "Day 21" labs must be adjusted to 7 days post-ovulation for accuracy.
  • The Masking Effect: Always interpret FSH in the context of Day 3 Estradiol.
  • Progesterone is the Protector: Ensure a healthy Pg/E2 ratio to support successful implantation and early pregnancy.

Your Coaching Script

The "Confidence & Clarity" Conversation

Practice these lines out loud to build your "coaching muscle memory."

YOU (The Coach):
"Elena, I can hear how overwhelmed you are. Seeing those lab results without context is incredibly stressful. Before we look at the numbers together, let's take a breath. My role is to help you organize your thoughts so you can have a high-value conversation with your doctor tomorrow. Does that sound okay?"
ELENA:
"Yes, I just feel like they're going to rush me off the phone. I don't even know where to start."
YOU (The Coach):
"I understand. Let's use the 'Rule of Three.' If you could only get answers to three things tomorrow, what would be the most important for your peace of mind? We'll write those down exactly as questions for Dr. Smith."
YOU (The Coach):
"For those out-of-range labs, instead of worrying about the 'why' right now, let's practice asking: 'Dr. Smith, how do these specific results change our strategy for this cycle?' This puts the ball back in their court to explain the clinical relevance."
Professional Scope Tip

Notice the language: "How does this change the strategy?" This is advocacy. Saying "This number means your estrogen is too low" is a medical diagnosis. Always pivot the interpretation back to the medical provider.

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

4 Mistakes New Coaches Make

  • Playing Doctor: Trying to explain the physiological "why" of a lab result instead of helping the client ask the doctor.
  • Taking Sides: Saying "Your doctor is wrong" or "That clinic is terrible." This increases client stress. Instead, say "It sounds like the communication with this clinic isn't meeting your needs."
  • Over-Researching: Spending hours on PubMed for a client. Your job is to coach the human, not solve the medical puzzle.
  • Ignoring the "Scope" Warning: Forgetting to use the phrase "In my role as a coach..." or "Make sure to verify this with your medical team."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks: "My progesterone is 12. Is that high enough for a successful transfer?" What is the most scope-appropriate response?

"Progesterone ranges can vary based on the clinic's specific protocol. A great question for your nurse would be: 'What is the target range for this specific stage of my cycle, and where does my result sit within that?'"

2. What is the "Rule of Three" in medical navigation coaching?

Helping the client identify and prioritize their top 3 most important questions to ensure they get the most critical information during brief medical appointments.

3. True or False: It is helpful to tell a client that their doctor's protocol seems outdated based on your training.

False. This undermines the client's trust in their care team and increases anxiety. Instead, coach them to ask: "I've been reading about [Protocol X]; can you help me understand why that may or may not be a fit for my specific case?"

4. How does medical navigation coaching differ from traditional health coaching?

Medical navigation focuses specifically on the logistics, communication, and self-advocacy within the clinical system, whereas traditional coaching focuses more on lifestyle, nutrition, and mindset habits.

Analyzing the Anatomy of Failure

When an IVF cycle fails‚Äîwhether it is a failure to produce blastocysts or a failure of a euploid (genetically normal) embryo to implant‚Äîthe clinic often defaults to "bad luck" or "age." While age is a factor, integrative advocacy demands a more granular investigation.

We categorize failure into three primary domains:

Domain Common Indicators of Failure Integrative Investigation
The Gamete (Egg/Sperm) Low fertilization rates; embryos arresting before Day 3 or Day 5. Oxidative stress, Sperm DNA Fragmentation, Metabolic health (HbA1c).
The Genetics (Embryo) High aneuploidy (abnormal) rate on PGT-A results. Mitochondrial support (CoQ10), lifestyle epigenetics, ovarian stimulation protocol.
The Soil (Uterus) Failure of euploid embryo transfer; chemical pregnancy. Chronic endometritis, window of implantation (WOI), microbiome.
Coach Tip: The Day 3 Divide

Embryos that arrest before Day 3 are typically driven by egg quality (maternal genome). Embryos that arrest after Day 3 often indicate issues with the paternal genome (sperm) as the embryonic genome activates. Use this data to advocate for sperm testing if embryos look great on Day 2 but fail by Day 5.

Advanced Endometrial Diagnostics: ERA, EMMA, and ALICE

If a client has failed the transfer of a euploid embryo, the focus must shift from the embryo to the environment. Conventional clinics may suggest "trying again," but an advocate suggests testing.

1. ERA (Endometrial Receptivity Analysis)

The ERA tests the expression of 238 genes to determine the specific "Window of Implantation" (WOI). Approximately 30% of women have a displaced window, requiring more or less progesterone exposure before transfer. A 2020 study showed that personalized embryo transfer (pET) based on ERA significantly improved pregnancy rates in patients with recurrent implantation failure.

2. EMMA & ALICE (The Microbiome & Infection)

  • EMMA (Endometrial Microbiome Metagenomic Analysis): Determines if the uterine environment is "Lactobacillus-dominant." A lack of healthy bacteria can prevent implantation.
  • ALICE (Analysis of Infectious Chronic Endometritis): Specifically looks for the bacteria that cause chronic inflammation (Chronic Endometritis).

PGT-A & The Mosaicism Dilemma

Preimplantation Genetic Testing for Aneuploidy (PGT-A) is standard for women over 40, but the results are not always black and white. A "Mosaic" embryo contains both normal and abnormal cells.

Many clinics refuse to transfer mosaic embryos, labeling them as "abnormal." However, recent data (Greco et al., 2015) suggests that mosaic embryos can self-correct in utero and lead to healthy live births. As a coach, you must empower the client to ask for their full PGT-A lab report, not just the summary. If they have "Low-Level Mosaics," they may still have a 30-40% chance of a healthy pregnancy.

Advocacy Tip: Genetic Counseling

If a client has only mosaic embryos left, advise them to request a meeting with the lab's genetic counselor, not just the RE. Lab counselors often have a more nuanced view of transfer viability than the physician.

The 15-Minute RE Consult: Mastering Advocacy

The average RE consult lasts 12-15 minutes. Clients often leave feeling unheard or confused. Your job is to prep them like a "deposition."

The "Three-Question" Framework:

  1. "What is the clinical hypothesis for this failure?" (Forces the doctor to move past "bad luck.")
  2. "If this were your sister, would you change the stimulation protocol or investigate the uterus next?" (Personalizes the care.)
  3. "What data points would change your recommendation for the next cycle?" (Identifies the "why" behind their choices.)

When to Suggest a Second Opinion

Not all clinics are created equal. Some excel at "standard" cases but struggle with "complex" ones (DOR, recurrent loss, or age 40+). Signs it is time for a second opinion include:

  • The RE refuses to change the protocol after two failures.
  • The clinic has a "one-size-fits-all" approach to stimulation (e.g., everyone gets 450 units of Gonal-F).
  • The clinic discourages advanced testing (ERA/DNA Fragmentation) without clinical justification.
  • Communication Breakdown: The client feels like a "number" in an assembly line.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client's embryos all arrest on Day 4. Which factor should you advocate for investigating first?

Sperm Factor/DNA Fragmentation. Embryonic genome activation typically occurs around Day 3; failure between Day 3 and 5 often suggests a paternal contribution issue.

2. What does the ALICE test specifically look for?

Chronic Endometritis (inflammation of the uterine lining caused by specific pathogenic bacteria).

3. True or False: Mosaic embryos should always be discarded as they cannot result in healthy births.

False. Many mosaic embryos (especially low-level mosaics) can result in healthy, live births through a process of self-correction.

4. Why is the ERA test performed as a "mock cycle"?

Because it requires a biopsy of the endometrial lining at the exact time a transfer would occur, which would destroy an embryo if one were present.

Key Takeaways for the Fertility Coach

  • Failure is Data: Every failed cycle provides clues. Day of arrest, fertilization rates, and PGT-A ratios are clinical markers, not just "bad luck."
  • Advocate for the Uterus: After a euploid failure, testing (ERA/EMMA/ALICE) should be prioritized over "trying again" with the same protocol.
  • Master the Consult: Help clients move from emotional victims to clinical partners by using structured questioning during RE appointments.
  • Know the Mosaic Nuance: Educate clients on the potential of mosaic embryos so they don't prematurely discard viable chances at pregnancy.

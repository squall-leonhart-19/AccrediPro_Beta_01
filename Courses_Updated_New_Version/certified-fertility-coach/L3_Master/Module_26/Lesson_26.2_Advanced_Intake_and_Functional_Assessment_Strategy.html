1. The Architecture of a High-Level Intake

A premium fertility coaching program does not start with a "chat." It starts with a Deep Discovery Phase. This phase serves two purposes: it provides you with the data needed to succeed, and it demonstrates to the client that you are a serious professional. Practitioners like Sarah, a former school teacher turned coach, found that implementing this rigorous intake allowed her to increase her program price from $500 to $2,500 because the perceived value of the assessment was so high.

Your intake system should consist of three core components:

  • Systemic Health Mapping: Assessing digestion, sleep, stress, and environmental toxin exposure.
  • Reproductive Timeline: A detailed history of cycles, pregnancies, losses, and ART (IVF/IUI) outcomes.
  • The Symptom Burden Analysis: A quantitative score of symptoms across all body systems to track progress over time.
Coach Tip

Always ask for the original embryology reports if a client has done IVF. Don't just rely on their memory of "good quality embryos." Looking at fertilization rates and day-3 vs. day-5 progression can tell you volumes about whether the issue is more likely egg-driven or sperm-driven.

3. Functional Lab Interpretation: Optimal vs. Pathological

Conventional medicine is designed to find disease (pathology). Functional coaching is designed to find dysfunction. A lab value that is not yet "diseased" can still be "sub-optimal" for the high metabolic demands of pregnancy.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology found that even "subclinical" hypothyroidism (TSH > 2.5) was associated with a 60% increase in miscarriage risk in women over 35. Yet, many clinics will not treat unless TSH is over 4.5 or 5.0.

Key Functional Ranges for Fertility

  • TSH: Clinical (0.5 - 5.0) vs. Functional (1.0 - 2.0)
  • Vitamin D: Clinical (>30 ng/mL) vs. Functional (50 - 70 ng/mL)
  • Ferritin (Iron Stores): Clinical (15 - 150) vs. Functional (50 - 90 ng/mL)
  • Fasting Insulin: Clinical (<25 uIU/mL) vs. Functional (<7 uIU/mL)
Coach Tip

When discussing labs, use the phrase: "Your doctor says these are 'normal,' which means you aren't sick. However, for optimal fertility, we want to see these markers in a tighter range to ensure your body feels safe enough to sustain a pregnancy."

4. Priority Sequencing: The 'Big Rocks' Approach

Once you have all the data, the most common mistake is trying to fix everything at once. This leads to client burnout and poor compliance. You must use Priority Sequencing.

Consider the "Fertility Hierarchy of Needs":

  1. Safety & Inflammation: Address gut infections, high stress, or autoimmune flares first. If the body is in "survival mode," it will not prioritize reproduction.
  2. Metabolic Health: Stabilize blood sugar and insulin. This is the foundation for egg quality and hormonal balance.
  3. Targeted Support: Once the foundation is set, add specific antioxidants for egg quality or uterine lining support.

5. The Fertility Baseline Report

The final step of the advanced intake is the Fertility Baseline Report. This is a 3-5 page PDF you provide to the client summarizing your findings. This document justifies your premium coaching fee and serves as the roadmap for the next 3-6 months.

The Report must include:

  • The "Current State": A summary of their symptom burden and lab sub-optimality.
  • The "Primary Obstacles": The top 3 things (The Big Rocks) standing in their way.
  • The "Success Metrics": How you will measure progress (e.g., improved cervical mucus, lower TSH, reduced bloating).

Coach Tip

A professional report transforms you from a "coach" to a "consultant." It is the most powerful tool for client retention because it gives them a tangible asset they can even take to their doctor to facilitate better collaborative care.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client presents with a TSH of 3.9. Her doctor says it is "normal." What is the functional fertility perspective?

While 3.9 is within the clinical "normal" range (usually up to 4.5 or 5.0), it is sub-optimal for fertility. Research suggests that for optimal conception and reduced miscarriage risk, TSH should ideally be between 1.0 and 2.0.

2. What is the first priority in the "Fertility Hierarchy of Needs"?

Safety and Inflammation. You must address systemic stressors (gut health, acute stress, or autoimmune flares) before the body will adequately respond to egg quality or hormonal protocols.

3. Which document serves as the "professional product" that builds authority and buy-in at the start of a program?

The Fertility Baseline Report. It summarizes findings, identifies obstacles, and sets clear success metrics.

4. True or False: If a client has bilateral tubal blockage, they are a primary candidate for coaching-only support.

False. Bilateral tubal blockage is a structural "Red Flag" that requires medical intervention (usually IVF). Coaching can support the IVF process, but it cannot "unblock" tubes through lifestyle alone.

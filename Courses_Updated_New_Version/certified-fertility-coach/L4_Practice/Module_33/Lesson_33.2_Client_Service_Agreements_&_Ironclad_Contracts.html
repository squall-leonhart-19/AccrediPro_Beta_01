Case Study: The Refund Dispute

Sarah, 48, Former RN turned Fertility Coach

Sarah launched her practice with a generic "Wellness Agreement" found online. She worked with a client, "Elena," for three months. Elena followed the nutrition protocols but did not conceive. Frustrated, Elena demanded a 100% refund of her $3,500 package, claiming Sarah "promised results that weren't delivered."

Because Sarah's contract lacked a clear Refund Policy and a No Guarantee Clause, she spent $2,000 in legal fees just to settle the dispute. Sarah now uses ironclad agreements that clearly state: "Coaching is a process of education, not a guarantee of medical outcomes." She has since scaled her business to $15k/month with total peace of mind.

The Anatomy of a Fertility Coaching Contract

A high-level service agreement serves three primary functions: Clarity, Compliance, and Consequence. It defines exactly what you will do, what the client must do, and what happens if either party fails to meet those expectations. According to a 2022 survey of wellness practitioners, those with formal contracts reported 85% fewer payment disputes than those using informal agreements.

Your agreement should begin with a clear identification of the parties. For many of you transitioning from careers in nursing or teaching, you may be operating as an LLC (Limited Liability Company). Ensure the contract is between your Business Entity and the client, not you personally. This is the first step in maintaining the "corporate veil."

Coach Tip: The Professional Hand-Off

Send your contract before the first paid session. Use a professional e-signature platform like DocuSign or HelloSign. This reinforces your status as a high-value expert from day one.

Your Intellectual Property (IP) includes your handouts, your recorded modules, and the specific sequencing of the F.E.R.T.I.L.E. Method‚Ñ¢. Without an IP clause, a client could theoretically repackage your handouts and sell them as their own.

Your IP clause should explicitly state that the materials are for personal use only and that the "Coach retains all ownership, copyrights, and trademarks." This is particularly important if you plan to eventually hire associate coaches or scale into group programs.

Limitation of Liability & Asset Protection

This is the "Ironclad" part of your contract. A Limitation of Liability clause caps the amount a client can sue you for. In most coaching agreements, this cap is set at the total amount the client paid for the services.

For example, if a client pays you $2,500 for a 3-month package, your contract should state that your maximum liability for any claim is $2,500. This prevents a disgruntled client from going after your home, savings, or retirement accounts. For practitioners over 40 who have spent decades building their net worth, this clause is non-negotiable.

Coach Tip: The LLC Shield

Always sign your contracts as: Jane Doe, Manager of Jane Doe Wellness, LLC. Never sign just your name. This ensures the liability stays with the business entity, not you as an individual.

Managing Expectations: The 'No Guarantee' Clause

Fertility is a biological process influenced by hundreds of variables, many of which are outside of a coach's control. A client may follow every pillar of the F.E.R.T.I.L.E. Method‚Ñ¢ and still not achieve a clinical pregnancy. Your contract must be explicit about this reality to prevent "breach of contract" claims.

The "No Guarantee" clause should state:
"Coach provides educational support and lifestyle guidance. Client acknowledges that Coach cannot and does not guarantee any specific medical outcome, including but not limited to conception, successful pregnancy, or live birth. Individual results vary based on biological factors and adherence to protocols."

Coach Tip: Setting the Tone

Frame the "No Guarantee" clause as a partnership. Explain to the client: "This clause is here because I respect your journey enough to be honest‚Äîwhile we will optimize every factor we can, your body is the ultimate authority."

Dispute Resolution: Arbitration vs. Litigation

If a disagreement arises, where will it be settled? Without a Choice of Law and Venue clause, you could be forced to travel to the client's home state to defend yourself in court.

  • Venue: Always set the venue to your own county and state.
  • Arbitration: Many coaches prefer mandatory binding arbitration. It is usually faster, more private, and less expensive than a public court trial.
  • Mediation: You can also include a clause requiring 30 days of "good faith mediation" before any legal action can be taken.
Coach Tip: Attorney Review

While we provide templates, laws vary by state (especially in California and New York). Spend $300-$500 to have a local contract attorney review your final draft. It is an investment in your business's foundation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it critical to sign the contract on behalf of your LLC rather than as an individual?

Signing as the LLC maintains the "corporate veil," ensuring that any legal liability is limited to the business assets rather than your personal assets (home, car, personal savings).

2. What is the standard "cap" for a Limitation of Liability clause in the coaching industry?

The standard cap is typically the total amount the client has paid for the services rendered.

3. True or False: An Intellectual Property (IP) clause allows the client to share your handouts with their friends.

False. An IP clause specifies that materials are for "personal use only" and prohibits redistribution or commercial use by the client.

4. What is the benefit of a "Choice of Law" clause?

It ensures that any legal disputes are handled according to the laws of your state and in a court/venue convenient to you, rather than the client's location.

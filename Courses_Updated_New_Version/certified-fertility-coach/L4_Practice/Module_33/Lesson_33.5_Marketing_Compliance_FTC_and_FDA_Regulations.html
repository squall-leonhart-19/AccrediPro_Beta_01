The FTC & Testimonials: Sharing Success Safely

The FTC's primary mission in marketing is to prevent deception. When you use a "success story," the FTC assumes the consumer will believe that they will achieve the same result. If that result is not "typical," you are in a high-risk zone.

The Death of "Results Not Typical"

For years, marketers used the disclaimer "Results not typical" as a shield. However, the FTC updated its Endorsement Guides to state that this disclaimer is no longer sufficient. If you share an extraordinary result (e.g., "I got pregnant in 30 days after 5 years of trying"), you must disclose what the typical result is for a client using your program.

Coach Tip: Testimonial Auditing

Always audit client testimonials before posting. If a client says, "She cured my PCOS," you must edit or add a parenthetical: "She helped me manage my PCOS symptoms through lifestyle changes." Never let a client make a medical claim on your behalf that you couldn't make yourself.

FDA Compliance for Targeted Nutrition (T)

In the F.E.R.T.I.L.E. Method‚Ñ¢, "Targeted Nutrition" is a cornerstone. However, supplements are regulated as food, not drugs. Under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA), you must avoid "Disease Claims."

Type of Claim Definition Example (Fertility Context)
Disease Claim (Illegal) Claims to treat, cure, or prevent a specific disease. "Inositol cures PCOS-related infertility."
Structure/Function (Legal) Describes how a nutrient affects the body's normal function. "Inositol supports healthy insulin sensitivity and ovarian function."
Health Claim (Regulated) Relationship between a food/substance and reduced risk. "Folic acid may reduce the risk of neural tube defects."
Coach Tip: The FDA Disclaimer

Anytime you mention a supplement on your website, the "FDA Box" must be present: "These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

The "Conception" vs. "Fertility" Wording

In the legal landscape of advertising, specific words carry "medical weight." "Infertility" is a clinical diagnosis (ICD-10 code). "Fertility" is a state of being. "Conception" is a biological event.

To stay compliant, focus your marketing copy on optimization and support rather than treatment. A 2022 review of regulatory warning letters showed that 64% of wellness audits were triggered by the use of the word "cure" or "reverse" in relation to a diagnosed condition.

  • Avoid: "Reverse Infertility," "Cure Endometriosis," "Fix Hormones."
  • Use: "Optimize Fertility," "Support Reproductive Health," "Balance Hormonal Vitality."

Proper Placement of Legal Disclaimers

The FTC uses the "4 P's" to determine if a disclosure is "Clear and Conspicuous":

  1. Prominence: Is it big enough to read?
  2. Presentation: Is it easy to understand (no legalese)?
  3. Placement: Is it where people will actually look (not buried at the bottom of a 5,000-word page)?
  4. Proximity: Is it close to the claim it is modifying?
Coach Tip: Social Media Disclaimers

On Instagram or TikTok, placing "Ad" or "Disclaimer: Not medical advice" at the very end of a long caption where it's hidden under the "more" button is non-compliant. It should be visible in the first few lines or on the image/video itself.

Red Flags: What Triggers Regulatory Audits

Regulatory bodies like the FTC often use automated "web crawlers" to find non-compliant language. Statistics show that practitioners who experience an audit often share one of these three "Red Flags":

1. Absolute Quantifiers: Using words like "Guaranteed," "Always," or "100% Success Rate."

2. Comparison to Drugs: Claiming a supplement or lifestyle change is "better than Clomid" or "as effective as Metformin."

3. Using "Patient" instead of "Client": This implies a clinical relationship you are not licensed to hold as a coach.

Coach Tip: Professional Integrity

If you are a nurse or medical professional transitioning to coaching, your risk is higher. The public (and regulators) hold you to a higher standard of "implied authority." Be extra diligent in stating you are acting as a coach, not a clinician.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client provides a video testimonial saying your program "cured her infertility." How should you handle this for your website?

You should either edit the video to focus on her health improvements or add a clear text overlay/caption stating: "Results represent this individual's experience. Coaching supports reproductive health and is not a cure for clinical infertility." Ideally, ask the client to re-record focusing on "optimizing her health."

2. Which of the following is a legal Structure/Function claim for a supplement?

"Supports a healthy uterine lining." (This describes a function of the body). Claims like "Prevents miscarriage" or "Treats endometriosis" are illegal disease claims.

3. What are the "4 P's" the FTC uses to judge a disclaimer's effectiveness?

Prominence, Presentation, Placement, and Proximity.

4. Why is the phrase "IVF Alternative" risky in marketing?

It implies your coaching is a direct medical substitute for a clinical procedure. It is safer to use "IVF Support" or "Complementary Holistic Approach."

Key Takeaways

  • Testimonials are Endorsements: You are legally responsible for the claims your clients make in their success stories.
  • Support, Don't Treat: Use "Structure/Function" language (e.g., "supports," "promotes," "maintains") rather than "Disease" language (e.g., "treats," "cures," "diagnoses").
  • Conspicuousness is Key: Disclaimers must be seen before a consumer makes a purchase decision, not just on a separate page.
  • Professional Boundaries: Use "Client" and "Coaching" consistently to avoid the appearance of practicing medicine without a license.

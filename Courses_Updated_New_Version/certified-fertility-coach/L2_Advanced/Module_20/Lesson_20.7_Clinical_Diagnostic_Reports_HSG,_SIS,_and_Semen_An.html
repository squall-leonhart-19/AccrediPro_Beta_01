Decoding the Semen Analysis (SA)

The standard Semen Analysis is the cornerstone of male fertility testing, but it is frequently misinterpreted. Most clinics use the WHO 5th or 6th Edition reference ranges. These ranges are based on the bottom 5th percentile of men who successfully fathered a child within 12 months. In other words, "normal" means "not in the bottom 5%."

Parameter WHO "Normal" (5th Ed) Functional "Optimal"
Volume > 1.5 mL 2.0 - 5.0 mL
Concentration > 15 million/mL > 40 million/mL
Total Motility > 40% > 60%
Morphology (Kruger) > 4% > 10%
Total Motile Count (TMC) N/A > 20 Million

Clients are often terrified by a 4% morphology score. Explain that morphology is the most subjective part of the test. Tubal Patency: HSG vs. HyCoSy

To conceive naturally or via IUI, at least one fallopian tube must be "patent" (open). There are two primary ways clinics test this:

1. Hysterosalpingogram (HSG)

This is the "gold standard" but can be uncomfortable. An iodine-based dye is injected into the uterus while X-ray images are taken. It provides a clear view of the tubal architecture and uterine shape.

  • Pros: High diagnostic accuracy for tubal blockage.
  • The "Flushing Effect": Studies show a significant increase in natural pregnancy rates for 3-6 months following an HSG, likely due to the dye clearing out minor debris or "mucus plugs" in the tubes.

2. HyCoSy (Hysterosalpingo-Contrast-Sonography)

This uses ultrasound and a foam/saline solution instead of X-rays and iodine dye.

  • Pros: No radiation, usually less painful, can be done in-office.
  • Cons: Slightly less definitive than HSG for complex tubal issues.

Uterine Environment: SIS & Hysteroscopy

Even if hormones are perfect, a "mechanical" barrier in the uterus can prevent implantation. Standard ultrasounds often miss small polyps or adhesions.

Saline Infusion Sonogram (SIS)

Also known as a "water ultrasound," this procedure involves filling the uterus with sterile saline to distend the walls. This allows the doctor to see if the cavity is smooth or if there are "filling defects" such as:

  • Polyps: Soft tissue overgrowths (like skin tags) that can act as a natural IUD.
  • Fibroids: Specifically submucosal fibroids that protrude into the cavity.
  • Adhesions (Asherman‚Äôs Syndrome): Scar tissue from previous D&Cs or infections.
Coach Tip: The 40+ Advantage

For women over 40, uterine polyps are extremely common. Many REs will suggest "waiting and seeing," but as a coach, you can empower the client to ask: "Given my age and the value of each egg, would removing this polyp optimize my implantation chances?"

Advanced Paternal Testing: DNA Fragmentation

A standard SA looks at the "packaging" (the sperm cell), but DNA Fragmentation looks at the "blueprint" (the DNA inside). High fragmentation (typically >25-30%) is linked to:

  • Unexplained infertility.
  • Recurrent pregnancy loss (miscarriage).
  • Failed IVF/ICSI cycles.
  • Poor embryo development at Day 3.

When to suggest this test: If a client has had two or more miscarriages, or if the male partner is over 40, smokes, has a high BMI, or a known varicocele.

Integrative Advocacy: The RE Consultation

The goal of this module is to turn your client into an active participant in their care. Help them prepare for their follow-up appointment with these specific questions:

1

"Was the Semen Analysis performed using WHO 5th or 6th edition standards?"

This helps the client understand if the "normal" result is based on older, more lenient criteria.

2

"In David's SA, what was the Total Motile Count (TMC)?"

TMC is often a better predictor of success than simple concentration or motility percentages.

3

"Regarding the HSG, was there any 'delayed spill' of the dye?"

A delayed spill might indicate partial blockage or inflammation (hydrosalpinx) that could interfere with IVF success.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client's husband has a morphology of 3%. The doctor says it's "fine." Why might a coach still be concerned?

While 4% is the WHO cutoff, 3% is below the threshold and may indicate oxidative stress or poor sperm maturation. In a functional context, we aim for >10% for optimal natural conception chances.

2. What is the "flushing effect" associated with an HSG?

The "flushing effect" refers to the temporary increase in fertility (often for 3-6 months) following an HSG, as the oil or water-based dye clears minor debris or mucus from the fallopian tubes.

3. When is a Saline Infusion Sonogram (SIS) typically preferred over a standard ultrasound?

An SIS is preferred when a clinician needs to evaluate the internal uterine cavity for polyps, fibroids, or adhesions that are not clearly visible on a standard "dry" ultrasound.

4. If a couple has experienced recurrent early miscarriages despite "normal" standard testing, which advanced male test should be considered?

Sperm DNA Fragmentation testing (such as the SCSA or HALO test) should be considered, as high DNA damage is a known contributor to recurrent pregnancy loss.

Key Takeaways

  • Beyond Normal: "Normal" clinical results often mean "absence of disease," not "optimal for fertility." Coaches look for the latter.
  • The Male Factor: Always look at the Total Motile Count and consider DNA fragmentation in cases of unexplained infertility or loss.
  • Structural Integrity: An SIS or Hysteroscopy is essential to ensure the "soil" (uterine lining) is free of mechanical obstructions like polyps.
  • The Advocacy Role: Your value lies in helping clients interpret these reports to have more productive, high-level conversations with their medical team.

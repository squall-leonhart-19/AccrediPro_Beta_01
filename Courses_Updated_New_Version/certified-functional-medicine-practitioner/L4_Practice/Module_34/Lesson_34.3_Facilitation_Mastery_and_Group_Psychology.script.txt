Let's look at some advanced facilitation techniques. I know that many new facilitators fear managing different group personalities. It can feel intimidating. But mastery just requires a few specific verbal scripts. These scripts keep the group on track without hurting anyone's feelings.

Take the dominant participant, for example. We have all met the over-sharer. They usually have great intentions. But they can suck the oxygen right out of the room. When this happens, I use a specific script. I say something like, thank you so much for that perspective, Jane. I want to make sure we honor our Step Up, Step Back rule. Who haven't we heard from yet on this topic? This acknowledges Jane but moves the focus back to the rest of the group.

Then there is the quiet voice. These participants are often processing very deeply. You should never cold call them in a way that creates anxiety. Instead, try saying something like this. I have noticed some of you are reflecting deeply. There is no pressure to share. But if there is a thought bubbling up that feels important, we would love to hear it. This gives them an invitation without putting them on the spot.

Here is a tip that might feel uncomfortable at first. In a group, silence feels much longer than it actually is. After you ask a question, I want you to count to ten slowly in your head. Do not speak until you hit ten. Often, the most profound breakthroughs come from the person who needed that extra eight seconds. They needed that time to find their courage. If you speak too soon, you take that opportunity away from them.

Now, let's talk about Group Motivational Interviewing, or GMI. In a group setting, we want to leverage something called Change Talk. When a participant hears a peer talk about why they want to quit sugar, it is incredibly powerful. In fact, research shows it is five times more persuasive than hearing a practitioner say it.

We use an acronym called DARN-CAT to guide this process. First is Desire. Ask them what they wish was different about their energy. Second is Ability. Ask what one small step they know they can take this week. Third is Reasons. Ask why it is important for them to get their inflammation under control. Fourth is Need. Ask them on a scale of one to ten how much they need this change.

The CAT part stands for Commitment, Activation, and Taking steps. You want to listen for these things in their answers. When a peer says, I am going to meal prep on Sunday, that is a commitment. When the rest of the group hears that, it creates a ripple effect of motivation.

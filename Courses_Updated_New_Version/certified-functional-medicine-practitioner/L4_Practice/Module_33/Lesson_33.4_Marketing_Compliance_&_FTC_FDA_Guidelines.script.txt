So, let’s talk about the Federal Trade Commission, or the FTC. The FTC cares about one thing above all else: Deception. If a consumer could be misled by your marketing, you are in violation. In our world of functional medicine, the line between supporting biological function and treating a disease is very thin. But legally, that line is massive.

The FTC requires that every single health claim you make be truthful and substantiated by what they call competent and reliable scientific evidence. This isn't just a suggestion. In 2022, the FTC reported a forty-five percent increase in warning letters sent to health influencers and practitioners. They are looking for unsubstantiated claims about chronic diseases.

I want to give you some examples of how to shift your language. This is where you might want to take some notes. If you are tempted to say something cures diabetes, stop yourself. One of the biggest mistakes I see is the use of miracle testimonials. If you have a client who had an extreme, non-typical result, you cannot just post it and leave it at that. I also want to touch on something many of you might be considering: private-label supplements. This is a great way to increase your income. The FDA monitors supplement labels for what they call unapproved drug claims. If your label says it reduces inflammation, the FDA might classify that supplement as a drug. Why? Because inflammation is a medical term often linked to disease states. There are three pillars of supplement compliance you must follow.

First, you must have the FDA disclaimer box. You’ve seen it. It says these statements have not been evaluated by the FDA and the product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease. This has to be on every label and every webpage where the product is sold. Second, you must only use structure and function claims. You can say it supports bone health, but you cannot say it prevents osteoporosis. Third, if you claim your product is pure or clean, you need to have the lab reports to prove it. These are called Certificates of Analysis, or COAs.

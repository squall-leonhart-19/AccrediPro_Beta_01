Now, let’s talk about the FDA. When you get to the Target phase of our method, you will recommend supplements. These are often called nutraceuticals. The FDA is very strict about how these are marketed. In 2022, they did a review of warning letters. They found that sixty-four percent of citations were for unapproved new drug claims. People were getting in trouble because they said a supplement could treat a disease. To the FDA, if you say a herb treats a disease, you are calling that herb a drug. And drugs have to go through billions of dollars in testing. 

But you are legally allowed to make structure-function claims. These describe how a nutrient affects the normal structure of the human body. For example, you can say, Calcium helps build strong bones. That is a structure-function claim. It is allowed. But you cannot say, Calcium prevents osteoporosis. That is a disease claim. When you write copy for your supplement shop, focus on the mechanism. Talk about the physiology, not the pathology. If you mention a specific supplement, you must include the DSHEA disclaimer. You’ve seen it before. It’s the one that says the FDA has not evaluated these statements. It’s a simple sentence that provides a lot of protection.

Next, let’s move to the Federal Trade Commission, or the FTC. They care about one thing: truth in advertising. Testimonials are your most powerful tool. Nothing builds trust like a success story. But they are also highly regulated. The FTC recently updated their guides. They are getting much stricter about health claims. 

To stay compliant, you need to follow the Three Pillars of Transparency. The first pillar is the Results Not Typical requirement. If a client has an extraordinary result, you have to be honest. Maybe they lost fifty pounds in two months. That’s amazing. But you must disclose the generally expected results for that intervention. You can’t imply that everyone will have that exact outcome.

The second pillar is about incentives. You cannot have secret testimonials. If you gave a client a discount or a free session in exchange for a review, you must say so. You can use a hashtag like sponsored. Or you can simply write that the client received a discount for sharing their story. This builds trust with your audience. They know you are being honest.

The third pillar is contextual accuracy. You cannot cherry-pick quotes. If a quote implies a cure but the client is still managing a condition, that is misleading. I suggest using what I call Journey Narratives instead of Before and After photos. 

Social media algorithms often flag Before and After photos anyway. But a Journey Narrative is different. Focus on the subjective quality of life.

Now, once you’ve established that relationship, you need a way to keep it professional. This is where Co-Management Agreements come in. This is just a formalized way to ensure the client receives seamless care. It defines who is responsible for what. It ensures there are no "gray areas" in the therapeutic partnership. This is vital, especially when you’re working with high-ticket cases that involve complex lab work.

While the doctor manages the medication, you are educating the client on nutrient depletions and lifestyle factors. This brings us to a very important piece of the puzzle: The Referral Loop System. Word-of-mouth is the most powerful marketing tool you have, but in a professional practice, it has to be formalized. You can’t just hope people remember to talk about you. You need a system that ensures that when someone refers to you, they feel appreciated and informed.

Now, a quick note on ethics. In many places, "kickbacks"—or paying someone a fee for a referral—are illegal for health professionals. Even where they aren't illegal, they can feel a bit unprofessional. Instead, I want you to incentivize through what I call Professional Reciprocity. When you have a client who needs a specific adjustment, you refer them back to your partner chiropractor. When a client needs a high-quality supplement, you send them to your partner pharmacy. This "loop" of mutual support is far more valuable than any one-time referral fee.

The Referral Loop has three specific steps. Step one is the Acknowledgement. Within twenty-four hours of receiving a referral, you need to send a thank-you. A handwritten note is best, but a secure professional email works too. It just lets the provider know the client arrived safely.

Step two is the Progress Update. With the client’s consent, you should send a brief "Functional Summary" to their primary doctor after you finish the "Reveal" phase of the R.O.O.T.S. Method. This is where you show your clinical rigor. You aren't just giving health advice; you're showing the data and the plan.

Step three is the Closing of the Loop. When the client completes their program, send one final note to the referrer. Highlight the objective improvements. For example, you might say, "The client’s A1c dropped from 6.2 to 5.4 through our nutritional intervention." When a doctor sees those kinds of results, they aren't just going to refer to you again—they’re going to start asking you how you did it.

Finally, let’s talk about Joint Ventures, or JVs. These are short-term, high-intensity collaborations. They allow you to borrow someone else’s audience and what I call their "halo of trust." When you’re proposing a Joint Venture, like a webinar or a workshop, you want to make it as easy as possible for the partner to say "Yes." I recommend using a specific template. First, you need "The Hook." This should be a specific problem, like "Why You’re Still Tired Despite Normal Lab Results."

Next, define the "Partner’s Role." They should introduce the topic and handle the structural or medical side of things. Then, there’s "Your Role." You provide the functional and lifestyle deep-dive using the R.O.O.T.S. Method. Finally, you have a "Call to Action."

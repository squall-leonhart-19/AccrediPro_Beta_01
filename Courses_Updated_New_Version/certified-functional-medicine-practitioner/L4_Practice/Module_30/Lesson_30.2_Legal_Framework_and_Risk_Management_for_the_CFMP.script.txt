The first thing we need to address is the question I get asked more than any other. Every new practitioner wants to know exactly what they are legally allowed to say and do. The answer to that question really depends on two main factors. First, what is your prior licensure? Are you a nurse, a chiropractor, or a therapist? Second, where do you live? In the United States, your scope of practice is governed at the state level. It is not a federal standard. For those of us in functional health, this usually comes down to something called Nutrition Practice Acts.

Think of these state laws on a spectrum. On one end, you have what we call Red laws. These are highly restrictive. In these states, the law might say that only a Registered Dietitian can provide specific nutrition counseling. On the other end, you have Green laws. These states are much more permissive for non-licensed practitioners. They recognize that people have a right to seek health information from various sources. Most states fall somewhere in the middle, in what we call the yellow or orange zone.

She was fifty-two years old and starting a brand new career in Ohio. Now, Ohio has some very specific statutes about who can provide nutrition care. Elena was worried. If you already hold a license, like an RN or an MD, your scope is obviously broader. However, you still have to be careful. You must ensure that your functional medicine recommendations fit within the standards of care for your primary license. If you are not licensed, you might be operating under something called Health Freedom Laws. States like Arizona, Colorado, and Minnesota have these. They explicitly protect your right to provide health services as long as you give your clients the proper disclosures.

This brings me to a very important point. In the legal world, language is everything. I want you to remember this: never use medical verbs. Avoid words like treat, cure, diagnose, or prescribe. These words are legally tied to the practice of medicine. If you use them, you are stepping onto dangerous ground. Instead, I want you to embrace functional verbs. Use words like support, balance, optimize, and educate. You aren't treating a disease; you are supporting a biological system. You aren't curing a condition; you are optimizing a pathway. This isn't just about playing with words. It is a critical legal boundary that protects you and your clients.

Now, let’s talk about documentation. There is an old saying in the legal and medical worlds: if it isn’t documented, it didn’t happen. Your clinical records are your very first line of defense. Think of them as your insurance policy in written form. In 2022, there was a survey of wellness practitioners that found something quite shocking. Sixty-eight percent of legal disputes were resolved in favor of the practitioner for one simple reason. They had a signed informed consent form on file. That is it. That one document saved them.

Your informed consent is the most important piece of paper in your office. It needs to be very clear. It should state exactly who you are. If you are a Certified Functional Medicine Practitioner but not a medical doctor, you must say that. It should explain the nature of functional health. You need to explain that you are looking for root causes, not providing acute care. You must also state that your recommendations do not replace the advice of their primary care physician. It is vital that the client understands that everything they do with you is voluntary. They need to know the potential risks, like a Herxheimer reaction when detoxing. When a client signs this, they are taking responsibility for their journey. It creates a partnership instead of a traditional doctor-patient hierarchy.

Then there is HIPAA. You have probably heard of it. It stands for the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act. Now, if you don't accept insurance, you might not technically be a covered entity under the law. But here is my advice: act like you are. Adhering to HIPAA standards is a best practice. It builds immense trust with your clients. They want to know their private health information is safe. This means you should avoid using standard email for health data. Use secure, encrypted portals for communication. Make sure your cloud storage has a Business Associate Agreement, or a BAA. This is a contract that ensures the storage provider is also following privacy rules.

Let’s move on to risk mitigation. We need to talk about professional liability. No matter how excellent you are, sometimes things go wrong. Or sometimes, people just like to sue. We call these nuisance suits. The good news is that professional liability insurance for functional practitioners is actually very affordable. You can often get one million to three million dollars in coverage for somewhere between one hundred and fifty and seven hundred dollars a year. That is a small price to pay for peace of mind.

There are a few different types of insurance you should know about. First is professional liability, which people often call malpractice insurance. This covers errors or omissions in the advice you give. If a client claims your supplement protocol caused them harm, this is what protects you. Then there is general liability. This is for the slip and fall accidents. If you have a physical office where people walk in, you absolutely need this. Finally, there is cyber liability. In our digital world, this is becoming more important every day. It protects you if your client records are hacked or if there is a data breach.

One quick tip about insurance: look for something called tail coverage. If you ever decide to switch insurance companies or retire, you want this. It protects you against claims that are made after your policy ends, as long as the event happened while the policy was active. Without it, you could be vulnerable even years after you stop seeing a client.

Next, we need to discuss how you actually structure your business. Many people start out as a sole proprietor because it is the easiest way to get going. But I want to caution you. A sole proprietorship offers zero asset protection. If your business gets sued, your personal home, your car, and your savings could all be at risk. This is why I usually recommend an LLC, or a Limited Liability Company.

An LLC creates what we call a corporate veil. It separates your business liabilities from your personal assets. If the business owes money or gets sued, the person suing generally can't go after your personal bank account. In some states, if you are a licensed professional like a nurse or a doctor, you might be required to form a PLLC, which is a Professional LLC.

As your business grows, you might want to look into an S-Corp election. This isn't a different kind of company; it is just a way the IRS looks at your LLC for tax purposes. Once your practice starts making a significant profit, say sixty to eighty thousand dollars a year, an S-Corp can save you a lot of money in self-employment taxes. It allows you to pay yourself a reasonable salary and take the rest of the profit as a distribution. I recommend talking to a tax professional once you hit those numbers, but it is good to have it on your radar now.

The last area I want to cover is lab ordering and supplements. This is one of the grayest areas in our field. Some states, like New York, New Jersey, and Rhode Island, have very strict laws. They often prevent practitioners from ordering certain labs directly. If you live in one of those states, don't worry. There are solutions. Many of our practitioners use a co-management model or a direct-to-consumer model.

Platforms like Rupa Health or Fullscript are wonderful tools. They allow the client to pay for their own labs directly. You are then providing the interpretation and the education based on those results. This shifts the legal responsibility of ordering the test away from you. It keeps you in the role of the educator. When it comes to supplements, always remember the FDA disclaimer. You have probably seen it on every bottle you buy. It says that these statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. It says the product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease. You should have this disclaimer on your website, in your emails, and on any protocol sheets you give to clients. It is a simple step that provides a lot of protection.

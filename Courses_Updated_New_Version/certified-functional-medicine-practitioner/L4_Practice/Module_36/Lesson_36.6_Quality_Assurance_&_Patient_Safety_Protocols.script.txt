Now, let’s talk about something we all hope doesn't happen, but we must be prepared for: Adverse Event Reporting. As we start targeting the microbiome or initiating detox protocols, clients might experience what we call "Herxheimer" reactions. An Adverse Event is basically any untoward medical occurrence. It doesn't always mean your intervention caused it, but we need to track it regardless. In our field, we specifically look for digestive distress, like bloating or diarrhea after starting a probiotic. We watch for neurological shifts, like increased brain fog or headaches during a detox. And of course, we stay alert for rare but critical allergic reactions like rashes or hives.

Next, we need to discuss lab interpretation safety. In functional medicine, we spend a lot of time looking for "optimal" ranges. We want to see people thriving, not just "not sick." But a safety-first practitioner must rule out "pathological" emergencies first. You have to establish a Red Flag Checklist for every single lab review you do.

Research shows that failing to follow up on abnormal lab results is a leading cause of patient harm in outpatient settings. We cannot let that happen in our practices. You need to know when a marker is so far out of range that it mandates an immediate referral to a primary care physician or a specialist.

For example, if you see Hemoglobin below 8.0, that is severe anemia. That's not a time for a slow iron supplement protocol; that’s an immediate referral. If you see a fasting blood glucose over 126 in a client who hasn't been diagnosed with diabetes, they need a formal medical workup. A TSH over 10 or under 0.1 requires medical management for thyroid disease. If liver enzymes like ALT or AST are more than three times the upper limit of normal, that's an emergency until proven otherwise. And keep an eye on platelets; if they drop below 100,000, that requires an investigation into thrombocytopenia.

When you see one of these red flags, the key is not to panic the client. You might say something like, "This specific marker is outside the scope of what we handle in functional wellness and requires a formal medical diagnosis. I want you to share this report with your primary doctor this week. I’m going to pause our protocol until we have their clearance." This doesn't make you look like you don't know what you're doing. In fact, it’s the opposite. It shows you are an expert who knows exactly where your boundaries are.

Let’s move into the digital side of things: Data Privacy and Security. I know that for many of us, the tech side can feel a bit daunting. But I want you to view data security as a patient safety issue. If a client’s sensitive health data—like their genetic markers or metabolic labs—is leaked, it can cause real psychological and financial harm.

You must have a "Digital Fortress." This starts with a Business Associate Agreement, or BAA. You should never use standard versions of Zoom, Gmail, or Dropbox for client data. You need the professional versions that provide a BAA. This is a legal contract where the provider agrees to maintain the security standards required by law.

You also need to make Two-Factor Authentication mandatory on every platform you use. If it has client records, it needs 2FA. And please, stop using standard text messages or unencrypted email to discuss health. Use secure portals like Practice Better or Healthie. 

Here is a safety tip specifically for telehealth: at the very start of every session, confirm the client’s physical location. You should also have the number for local emergency services in their specific area on file. If a client has a medical emergency while they are on a video call with you, you need to know exactly where to send help. It sounds extreme, but that is what high-integrity practitioners do.

Now that we've covered the administrative systems of your practice, we must address the most vital operation: the enrollment conversation. Without a client, your systems have nothing to process.

From Sarah Mitchell's Desk

I remember my first discovery call. My palms were sweaty, and I was so worried about "selling" that I practically apologized for my price. But here is the truth: Selling is simply the bridge between your client's current pain and the vibrant health they deserve. Today, we are going to practice crossing that bridge with confidence.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the "Gap" between a prospect's current state and their health goals.
  • Present program pricing without hesitation or emotional attachment.
  • Reframe common objections as opportunities for deeper clinical insight.
  • Project a realistic income path based on monthly enrollment targets.

When someone like Linda says they've "tried everything," they usually mean they've tried every conventional or isolated thing. Your job isn't to be the fourth doctor; it's to be the first practitioner to look at how her systems work together.

Confident Pricing Presentation

Many practitioners stumble here. The key is to state the price as a fixed fact, not a question. Avoid "uptalk" (where your voice goes higher at the end of the sentence).

The "No-Apology" Pricing Script

"The investment for the 90-day Root Restoration program, including all consultations, personalized protocols, and daily messaging support, is $1,800. We also offer a monthly payment plan of $650 per month. Which of those options works best for your budgeting?"

Handling Objections with Empathy

1

"I need to talk to my spouse."

Response: "I completely understand. Health is a family decision. When you talk to him, what do you think his biggest concern will be? Is it the cost, or is he worried about you trying another thing that might not work?" (This uncovers the real objection).

2

"It's just a lot of money right now."

Response: "I hear you. It is an investment. Let me ask‚Äîif we don't do this, and your fatigue gets worse over the next six months, what is the 'cost' of that to your career or your family life?"

Sarah's Insight

Never take an objection personally. An objection is often just a prospect's fear masquerading as a logistical problem. Your job is to help them navigate that fear.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of Phase 2 (Defining the Gap) in the discovery call?

The goal is to help the prospect realize the emotional and physical distance between where they are and where they want to be, making the value of the solution clear.

2. How should you handle the "I need to think about it" objection?

Acknowledge it, then ask: "Usually when someone needs to think about it, it's either the money, the time, or they aren't sure I can help. Which one is it for you?" This forces a specific, resolvable answer.

3. True or False: You should wait until the end of the call to mention your price.

True. You must build value and establish the "Gap" first. If you lead with price, you are a commodity. If you end with price after showing the solution, you are a transformer.

4. Based on the data table, how many clients per month are needed to reach a six-figure run rate (~$90k+)?

Approximately 5 clients per month at a $1,500 package price ($7,500/mo = $90,000/yr).

Key Takeaways

  • The discovery call is a clinical tool, not just a sales tool; it identifies the prospect's readiness for change.
  • Rapport is built by asking deep, emotional questions rather than just symptom-based questions.
  • Present pricing as a non-negotiable fact to project authority and confidence.
  • Objections are signs of interest and fear; handle them with curiosity, not defensiveness.
  • Consistency in enrollment (2-5 clients per month) creates a sustainable and profitable career path.

Let's start with mastering the P&L for functional practices. A standard accounting Profit and Loss statement often fails to capture the unique dynamics of what we do. In a typical business, everything is lumped together. But in our model, we must distinguish between two very different things: Clinical Service Revenue and Ancillary Revenue. Clinical Service Revenue is the money generated by your time, your expertise, and your consultations. Ancillary Revenue is the money coming from labs and supplements.

In a lab-intensive practice, your Cost of Goods Sold, or COGS, isn't just "office supplies." It includes the direct cost of specialized testing. Think about the GI-MAP, the Dutch Test, or the Organic Acids Test. These are expensive. If you don't separate these costs from your general expenses, your gross margin will look artificially low. This makes it almost impossible to assess if your clinical time is priced correctly. You might think you're making money, but the high cost of the labs might be hollowing out your profits.

I want you to always categorize lab costs as a direct COGS against Lab Revenue. Do not put them under general operating expenses. This allows you to see your "Lab Margin" with total clarity. Research in practice management shows that a healthy lab margin should typically be between twenty and thirty percent if you are handling the administration yourself. If you use a "drop-ship" model where the patient pays the lab directly, your margin might be zero, and that’s okay, as long as your consultation fee reflects the work.

Let’s look at the different revenue streams and what kind of margins you should expect. For initial consultations, your margin should be high, around eighty to ninety percent. This is where you are doing the heavy lifting of client acquisition and assessment. Functional labs usually sit in that fifteen to thirty percent margin range. These are tools for data-driven personalization. Nutraceuticals offer a higher margin, typically between thirty-five and fifty percent. These are great for protocol adherence and creating a bit of passive income. Finally, group programs are the gold mine of scalability. Their margins are often ninety to ninety-five percent because you are leveraging your time across many people at once.

Now, let's move into Key Performance Indicators, specifically LTV and CAC. To scale your practice without burning out, you must understand these two metrics. For many of you who are transitioning careers in your forties or fifties, these numbers provide the confidence you need to finally invest in marketing or hire that first assistant.

First, let’s talk about Patient Lifetime Value, or LTV. This is the total revenue a client generates throughout their entire relationship with you. In functional medicine, this number is naturally high because we don't just "fix and flip." We move from the Reveal phase to the Sustain phase. A typical client journey in a high-level practice might last twelve to twenty-four months. To find your LTV, you simply take your average monthly revenue per patient and multiply it by the average number of months they stay with you. When you see that number, you realize that a single client isn't just worth a few hundred dollars; they might be worth several thousand.

Next is Customer Acquisition Cost, or CAC. This is what you spend to get one new client through the door. This isn't just about Facebook ads. It includes the time you spend on "discovery calls," the cost of networking events, or the time you spend creating social media content. If you spend ten hours a month on marketing and your time is worth one hundred dollars an hour, that’s a thousand dollars in "cost" before you even pay for an ad.

Here is the Golden Ratio you need to remember. Your LTV should be at least three times your CAC. We call this the 3-to-1 rule. If it costs you two hundred dollars to acquire a client, that client should generate at least six hundred dollars in profit over their lifetime. If your ratio is lower than that, you’re working too hard for too little. If it’s higher, you have a massive opportunity to grow.

Now, let's talk about the "blood pressure" of your business: Cash Flow. In functional medicine, there is often a significant lag. You might pay for a lab kit today, but the patient might not pay you for a week, or the insurance reimbursement might take months. This gives you an immediate cash injection to cover all those upfront lab costs and software fees. It also increases client commitment.

You should also automate your billing. Use platforms like Stripe or Practice Better to handle monthly payments automatically. This prevents the "check is in the mail" syndrome. You shouldn't be chasing people for money; your system should handle it for you.

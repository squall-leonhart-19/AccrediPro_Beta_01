But clinical outcomes are only half of the equation. The other half is the patient experience. This is where the Net Promoter Score, or NPS, comes in. You might have a patient who gets physically better, but if they found your billing process confusing or your communication too slow, they aren't going to refer their friends to you.

The NPS is based on one simple question: "On a scale of zero to ten, how likely are you to recommend our practice to a friend or colleague?" We break the answers down into three groups. Those who answer nine or ten are your Promoters. These are your loyal enthusiasts. They are the ones who will keep coming back and who will do your marketing for you through word-of-mouth. Those who answer seven or eight are Passives. They are satisfied, but they aren't enthusiastic. They are vulnerable to competitors. And anyone who answers zero through six is a Detractor. These are unhappy customers who can actually damage your brand.

To find your score, you just subtract the percentage of Detractors from the percentage of Promoters. If you get a score above seventy, you are considered "World Class." If your score is lower than you’d like, don't panic. Usually, the friction isn't in your clinical skill; it’s in your technology or your operations. Maybe your scheduling app is clunky, or your intake forms are too long. Use that feedback to fix the "Operational Tech Stack" we talked about in Lesson Five.

This brings us to our final concept for today: Continuous Quality Improvement, or CQI loops. Quality assurance isn't something you do once and then check off your list. it is a loop that never ends. The most effective way to do this is using the PDSA Cycle. That stands for Plan, Do, Study, and Act.

Here is how it works in the real world. First, you Plan. You identify a problem. Let's say you notice that patients are dropping out during the Target phase. You decide to try a small change. Maybe you decide to add a fifteen-minute check-in call during week six of the program.

Next, you Do. You implement that change for a small group of patients over the next month.

Then, you Study. You look at the data. Did the retention improve? Did those patients stay through the end of the phase?

Finally, you Act. If the data shows it worked, you make that check-in call a permanent part of your SOP. If it didn't work, you don't beat yourself up; you just try a different plan. By using this cycle, your practice becomes a "learning organism." You are getting better with every single patient interaction because you are making decisions based on data, not just assumptions.

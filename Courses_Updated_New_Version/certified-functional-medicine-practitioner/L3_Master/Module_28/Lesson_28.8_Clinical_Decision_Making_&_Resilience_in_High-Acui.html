Section 3: Your Teaching Approach

As a mentor, your goal is not to give Lisa the answer, but to teach her how to think. We use the Socratic Supervision Model. This involves asking targeted questions that lead the mentee to the clinical realization themselves.

The 4-Step Case Review Framework:

  1. The Emotional Check-in: Address the mentee's anxiety first so they can access their prefrontal cortex.
  2. The Data Drill: Ask, "What data do we actually have?" vs. "What are we assuming?"
  3. The Mechanism Search: Ask, "What physiological mechanism could cause this reaction?"
  4. The Calibration: Adjust the protocol based on the new data.
The Leadership Shift

In your own practice, you might charge $250/hour. As a mentor, your value increases. High-level supervision can command $400-$600/hour because you are multiplying the impact of another practitioner.

Section 5: Supervision Best Practices

Effective mentoring requires clear structures. Use the following "Do's and Don'ts" to maintain your professional integrity while supporting your mentee.

  • DO: Require a written case summary before the meeting.
  • DO: Set a timer. Supervision should be efficient (30-45 mins).
  • DO: Praise the process, not just the result.
  • DON'T: Take over the case. It remains Lisa's client.
  • DON'T: Criticize Lisa's previous training. Build upon it.
Coach Tip

If a mentee is consistently overwhelmed, it's often a sign they are working with clients outside their "Zone of Genius." Part of mentoring is helping them find their specific niche where they feel most confident.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the Socratic Method in supervision?

The goal is to develop the mentee's clinical reasoning and critical thinking skills by leading them to the answer through targeted questioning, rather than simply providing the solution.

2. Why is the "Emotional Check-in" the first step in the 4-Step Case Review?

Anxiety and panic inhibit the prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for logical clinical reasoning. By addressing the mentee's emotional state first, you allow them to "think" clearly again.

3. Lisa wants to refer Brenda back to conventional care during a flare. How should a mentor respond?

The mentor should help Lisa determine if the flare is a medical emergency (requiring referral) or a clinical "teaching moment" (requiring protocol adjustment). This prevents premature referral due to practitioner fear.

4. What is a "Calibration" in the context of clinical supervision?

Calibration is the final step where the mentor and mentee adjust the client's protocol based on the new insights gained during the case review, ensuring the next steps are data-driven and safe.

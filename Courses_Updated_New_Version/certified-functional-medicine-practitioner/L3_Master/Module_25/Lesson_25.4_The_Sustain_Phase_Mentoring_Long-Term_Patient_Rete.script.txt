Now, to keep a patient engaged long-term, you have to give them objective proof that maintenance is actually working. You can’t just say, "You feel good, so keep doing what you’re doing." In the Sustain phase, we have to shift our focus from disease markers to resilience markers.

If we’re looking at someone with metabolic syndrome, we might start by tracking high CRP or HbA1c. But once those numbers normalize, we need new goals. This is where you mentor the practitioner to interpret health-span metrics.

First, let’s talk about Heart Rate Variability, or HRV. This is the gold standard for measuring autonomic nervous system flexibility. It tells us how well the body is recovering from stress. When a patient sees their HRV trending upward or staying stable even during a stressful work week, they realize that their lifestyle choices are providing a real, measurable buffer. It’s not just about the absence of a headache; it’s about the presence of vitality.

Then there’s metabolic flexibility. We want to see if the body can switch fuel sources efficiently. We might look at C-Peptide or HOMA-IR. We’re looking for a HOMA-IR score of less than 1.5 and stable glucose after meals. Teaching a patient how to maintain this flexibility is what prevents the weight regain and energy crashes that often happen after a protocol ends.

I also love using Grip Strength as a biomarker. It sounds simple, but it’s a powerful predictor of biological age and muscle mass. Research shows it’s an indispensable biomarker for longevity. If we can get a patient into the top 25th percentile for their age and gender, we’re giving them a tangible win that has nothing to do with their original symptoms.

And we can’t forget VO2 Max. This is one of the strongest cardiorespiratory fitness and longevity predictors we have. When a practitioner helps a patient move from the "fair" category to the "excellent" category for their age, that patient feels like they are leveling up their health. They aren't just waiting for a symptom to return; they are actively training for the centenary Olympics. This is the essence of metabolic resilience.

Now, let’s talk about the psychology of this. Long-term retention is a psychological game, not just a biological one. Mentees often struggle because they want to "fix" the patient. But that creates dependency. In the Sustain phase, the practitioner must transition from the role of the expert fixer to the role of a partner.

As a supervisor, I want you to observe your mentee’s sessions for something called the Righting Reflex. This is that almost irresistible urge to jump in and correct a patient’s behavior. If a patient says, "I struggled with my diet this weekend," the fixer wants to say, "Well, here’s why you shouldn't do that, and here’s a new meal plan." That actually shuts down the patient’s own internal motivation.

Instead, mentor your practitioners to use the OARS framework from Motivational Interviewing. OARS stands for Open-ended questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, and Summarizing.

Start with open-ended questions. Instead of asking, "Are you still taking your magnesium?" ask, "How do you envision your health five years from now?" This forces the patient to connect their daily habits to their long-term identity.

Then, use affirmations. "I’m really impressed by how you’ve maintained your sleep hygiene even during that work trip." This builds the patient’s self-efficacy. They need to believe they are the ones responsible for their success.

Reflective listening is next. "It sounds like you’re feeling confident about your diet but still a bit unsure about your exercise routine." This shows the patient they are being heard and helps them clarify their own internal barriers.

And finally, summarize. Link the patient’s current actions directly to those life goals they mentioned earlier.

Here’s a great tip you can give your mentees. Have them ask the patient: "On a scale of 1 to 10, how confident are you that you can maintain this habit for the next 12 months?" If the patient says anything less than an 8, the protocol is too complex for the Sustain phase. We have to simplify. The Sustain phase is about what the patient can do on their worst day, not their best day.

Finally, we have to talk about the sustainability of the mentor and the practitioner. You cannot mentor someone to sustain their patients if they aren't sustaining themselves. This is especially true for the 40-to-55-year-old women who often enter this field. Many of them bring a powerful "caregiver" energy. While that’s a strength, it’s also a liability. It leads to over-extending, answering emails at 10 PM, and taking on too many "heavy" cases because they want to help everyone.

I teach something called the Energy Accounting Method to help manage caseloads. We categorize cases into three colors: Red, Yellow, and Green.

Red Cases are the complex, acute ones. These are the patients with multiple autoimmune conditions, mold illness, or severe gut dysbiosis. They require high clinical bandwidth and emotional energy. I tell my mentees to limit these to no more than 20 percent of their caseload.

Yellow Cases are the Optimize and Target cases. These are people in active intervention. They’re doing well but still need regular adjustments. These should make up about 40 percent of the caseload.

And then we have the Green Cases. These are the Sustain and Maintenance patients. These are low-bandwidth, high-relationship cases. They’re the "easy" wins that remind the practitioner why they love this work. We want these to be at least 40 percent of the caseload.

If your mentee is feeling burnt out, the first thing you should check is their Red Case percentage. Often, imposter syndrome drives them to take on only the hardest cases because they feel like they have to prove their worth. But a healthy practice needs a garden of Green Sustain patients to support the Intensive Care patients. Remind them: you can't pour from an empty cup.

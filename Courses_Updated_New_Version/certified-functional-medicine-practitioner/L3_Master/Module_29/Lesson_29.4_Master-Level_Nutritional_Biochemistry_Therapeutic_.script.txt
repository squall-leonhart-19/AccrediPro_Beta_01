Next, I want to deconstruct the "Why" behind food sensitivities. Your clients will often come to you with long lists of foods they can’t eat. As a Master Practitioner, you have to distinguish the biochemical pathway to know how long the treatment will take.

First, there are IgE-mediated reactions. This is a true Type I allergy. It involves immediate mast cell degranulation. These are usually lifelong and require strict avoidance.

Second are IgG-mediated sensitivities. This is a Type III hypersensitivity. It involves the formation of immune complexes that deposit in tissues like joints or the gut. This is almost always a symptom of intestinal permeability, or leaky gut. These are often reversible. Once you fix the gut barrier, the IgG reactions often fade away.

Then we have non-immune intolerances. These aren't about the immune system; they’re about enzymes. Histamine intolerance is often a deficiency in Diamine Oxidase, or DAO. FODMAP intolerance is usually about bacterial fermentation in the wrong place, like SIBO. And salicylate or oxalate sensitivities are often linked to sulfur metabolism issues or fungal overgrowth. Understanding these "why" factors allows you to give your clients a roadmap for when they might be able to eat those foods again.

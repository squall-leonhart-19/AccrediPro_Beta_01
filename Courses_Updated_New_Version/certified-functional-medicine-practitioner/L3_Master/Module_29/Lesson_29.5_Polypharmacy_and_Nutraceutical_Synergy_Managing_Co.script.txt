Now, as you start moving into these intensive protocols, I have to give you a word of caution. When you are dealing with a client on multiple medications, you have to be very careful about the Herxheimer reaction, or what we call die-off. You also have to watch out for immunological flares. These clients often have sluggish detoxification pathways. Their livers are already busy processing five different drugs. If you come in with a heavy-duty gut protocol or a detox supplement, you might mobilize more toxins than their body can handle.

To manage this, I want you to follow the Low and Slow Rule. Never start three or four new supplements at once with a polypharmacy client. Introduce one thing at a time, every four to five days. This does two things. First, it gives the body time to adjust. Second, if the client has a bad reaction, you know exactly which supplement caused it. If you start five things at once and they get a headache, you have no idea why.

I also highly recommend using binders as your clean-up crew. Things like activated charcoal or modified citrus pectin can be lifesavers. They act like a sponge in the gut to mop up endotoxins. Just make sure you keep them at least two hours away from any medications, or they will bind the meds too. And always, always make sure the client’s bowels are moving. If they aren't having one or two healthy bowel movements a day, do not start a detox protocol. You’ll just be recirculating toxins and making them feel worse.

Communication is also vital here. I always tell my clients: You might feel ten percent worse before you feel a hundred percent better. I explain that this is a sign their body is mobilizing toxins. By warning them ahead of time, you prevent them from panicking and quitting the protocol. You’re building a partnership based on transparency.

Finally, we need to talk about botanicals. In this advanced stage, we use a lot of adaptogens and immunomodulators. But herbs are powerful chemicals, too. Many of them share the same metabolic pathways in the liver as drugs, specifically the Cytochrome P450 pathways.

St. John’s Wort is the classic example. It is a potent inducer of the CYP3A4 enzyme. This means it makes the liver clear out drugs much faster than normal. It can make birth control fail, it can make blood thinners stop working, and it can interfere with anti-rejection meds. In a polypharmacy case, I generally tell students to avoid St. John’s Wort entirely. It’s just too risky.

Ashwagandha is another one to watch. It’s a fantastic adaptogen, but it can stimulate thyroid hormone production. If a client is already on a high dose of Synthroid or Levothyroxine, adding Ashwagandha could push them into a hyperthyroid state. It’s not that you can’t use it, but you have to monitor them closely.

Milk Thistle is another interesting one. We love it for liver support, and it’s great for protecting the liver from the stress of multiple drugs. However, in some cases, it can actually slow down the clearance of certain medications, making them more potent in the bloodstream. Again, this is why we start low and slow.

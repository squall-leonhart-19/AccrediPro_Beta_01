Now, let's talk about Motivational Interviewing 2.0. In the early phases of a health journey, motivation is usually pretty easy to find. It’s driven by the "pain of the present." A client comes to you because they are tired, they are in pain, or they are scared. This is negative reinforcement—they want to escape a bad situation. But as their symptoms resolve, that negative reinforcement starts to fade. This is where many people drop off.

Motivational Interviewing 2.0 focuses on shifting the conversation from "escaping illness" to "pursuing potential." At this stage, you’re going to encounter something called Sustain Talk. This is when the client starts making arguments for maintaining their current status quo because they feel "good enough." They might say things like, "Well, I’m feeling much better now, so I don’t really think I need to be so strict with my sleep," or "I’ve lost the weight I wanted, so I can probably go back to my old eating habits on the weekends."

Your goal as a coach is to elicit Change Talk that is specifically focused on longevity and resilience. In the early days, the focus might have been weight loss or pain reduction. Now, the focus needs to be on vitality, legacy, and protecting their future self. Instead of asking "Why do you want to lose weight?", you might ask, "How does maintaining this energy level support your ten-year vision for your life?" Or "What kind of grandmother do you want to be twenty years from now, and how do today’s habits get you there?"

We also want to develop a discrepancy. In the beginning, that discrepancy was between illness and health. Now, the discrepancy is between being "good enough" and having "optimal resilience." Practitioners who master these 2.0 techniques can command much higher fees because they solve the "yo-yo" effect that plagues the health industry. When a client tells you they feel "fine now," I want you to ask them this: "On a scale of one to ten, how confident are you that your current habits will protect your brain health twenty years from now?" This immediately shifts the focus from their current lack of symptoms to their future legacy.

This leads us into a very deep psychological concept: The Identity of the Sick. There was a fascinating study published in 2021 in the Journal of Health Psychology. It found that patients with long-term chronic conditions often incorporate their diagnosis into their core self-concept. Their illness becomes who they are. This is a psychological anchor.

Think about why someone might subconsciously hold onto an illness identity. If they get well, they might lose their community, like the people in their support groups. They might lose their "valid" excuse for setting boundaries or saying no to things they don't want to do. For some, being "the sick one" has become their primary way of relating to others.

To facilitate long-term recovery, you have to help the client transition through three distinct stages of identity. The first is The Victim. This is the stage where the client asks, "Why is my body doing this to me?" Everything feels out of their control. The second stage is The Manager. This is where most functional medicine clients live. They say, "I am managing my symptoms through this protocol." They are doing the work, but they still see themselves as fundamentally broken.

The goal is to get them to the third stage: The Architect. This is where the client says, "I am the designer of my biological resilience." To get there, we use Identity-Based Habits. Instead of a client saying "I am going to eat more greens," we help them shift to saying "I am the type of person who nourishes my cells." This shift from outcome-based goals to identity-based systems is the real secret to the psychology of sustainment.

Now, let's get practical. Let’s talk about Habit Stacking and Environmental Design. We have to acknowledge that willpower is a finite resource. It’s like a battery that drains throughout the day. In the sustainment phase, we want to replace willpower with what we call Choice Architecture. We want to create a "Default Healthy" environment where the right choice is actually the easiest choice.

There are four pillars of environmental design that I want you to teach your clients. The first is Visual Cues. If you want a client to take their supplements, tell them to place the bottle right next to the coffee maker they use every morning. If they want to meditate, tell them to put a meditation cushion right in the middle of the living room floor so they have to step over it.

The second pillar is Friction Reduction. This is about making the good habits easier. For example, pre-chopping all the vegetables on a Sunday so that making a healthy dinner on Tuesday takes five minutes instead of forty-five. The third pillar is the opposite: Friction Addition. This is about making the "bad" habits harder. If a client struggles with late-night scrolling, the "friction" is keeping all electronics out of the bedroom. They have to actually get out of bed and go to another room to check their phone.

The fourth pillar is Habit Stacking. This comes from the work of James Clear and others. The formula is simple: "After I do my current habit, I will do my new health habit." For example, "After I pour my morning glass of water, I will take my probiotics." By piggybacking a new habit onto an existing one, you’re using the neural pathways already established in the brain.

In this sustain phase, I always tell my clients: "If a habit takes more than two minutes to start, it’s too hard." We need to scale it down. Don't try to "meditate for twenty minutes" if you’re struggling. Just "sit on the cushion and close your eyes for two minutes." Sustainment is about consistency, not intensity. A two-minute habit that you actually do every day is infinitely more powerful than a twenty-minute habit you only do once a week.

Next, let's talk about Accountability Frameworks and the role of wearables. Subjective feelings can be very deceptive. The second is Continuous Glucose Monitoring, or CGM. This provides immediate biofeedback on how lifestyle choices—not just food, but also stress and sleep—affect metabolic stability. And the third is Sleep Architecture. We want to track Deep and REM sleep to ensure the "Defense and Repair" node of the Functional Medicine Matrix is functioning correctly.

Finally, let's discuss Stress Modulation Architecture. Most people think of stress management as just "relaxing" or taking a vacation. In functional medicine, we view it much more technically. We use a specific Sustainment Stress Protocol. It starts with Box Breathing. This stimulates the vagus nerve via the phrenic nerve. I recommend just three minutes, three times a day, specifically at transition points—like when they finish work and are about to start their evening. Then there is NEAT, or Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis. This is primarily about cortisol buffering and insulin sensitization. I consider 10,000 steps a day to be a non-negotiable part of a sustainment program.

We also look at Hormesis, which is "good stress." This includes things like cold and heat exposure. Using a sauna or taking a cold plunge two to three times a week helps upregulate heat shock proteins and mitochondrial biogenesis. It builds a more resilient human being.

One of my favorite small habits to teach is the "Threshold Breath." I tell my clients that every time they walk through a door—whether it’s into their home, their office, or even a grocery store—they must take one deep, six-second exhale. This tiny habit prevents "stress stacking" throughout the day. It’s a constant, gentle reminder to the nervous system that they are safe.

The 'Hierarchy of Healing': Clinical Precedence

When a client presents with Hashimoto's (autoimmune), SIBO (gut), and PCOS (endocrine) simultaneously, where do you start? Applying a "shotgun approach" by targeting everything at once is a recipe for non-compliance and physiological stress. We use the Hierarchy of Healing to determine the order of operations.

Coach Tip

Think of protocol prioritization like building a house. You cannot install the electrical wiring (nervous system) or the thermostat (hormones) before you have a solid foundation (gut) and structural walls (cellular stability). Start from the bottom up.

Priority Level System/Focus Rationale
1. Foundational Gastrointestinal (Assimilation) If the gut is "leaky" or dysbiotic, it will continue to drive systemic inflammation and immune reactivity.
2. Stability Adrenal/HPA Axis The adrenals provide the "metabolic reserve." You cannot stimulate the thyroid or detoxify if the HPA axis is depleted.
3. Regulatory Thyroid & Blood Sugar Metabolic rate and glucose stability are required for the energy-intensive process of tissue repair.
4. Specific Sex Hormones & Immune Hormonal and autoimmune specificities are often "downstream" effects of gut and adrenal dysfunction.

Cross-Talk Management: The HPA-HPT-HPG Axis

In functional medicine, we recognize that no gland is an island. The HPA (Adrenal), HPT (Thyroid), and HPG (Gonadal/Sex) axes are in constant conversation. This is known as endocrine cross-talk.

For example, chronic HPA axis activation (stress) increases the production of Reverse T3 (rT3), which blocks thyroid receptors. Simultaneously, high cortisol inhibits the conversion of T4 to T3 and downregulates the HPG axis, leading to low progesterone or testosterone. If you provide thyroid support without addressing the HPA axis, the client may experience increased anxiety or heart palpitations because the underlying "stress brake" is still engaged.

Neuro-Endo-Immunology (NEI) Integration

The NEI supersystem represents the bi-directional communication between the nervous, endocrine, and immune systems. In complex cases, a "Target" phase intervention must account for all three. When designing a protocol for an autoimmune client, you must address the nervous system's role in immune modulation.

  • Nervous System: Vagus nerve tone influences the "Inflammatory Reflex."
  • Endocrine System: Cortisol and Vitamin D (a pro-hormone) act as immune "dimmer switches."
  • Immune System: Cytokines can cross the blood-brain barrier, altering neurotransmitter balance (leading to "brain fog").
Coach Tip

If a client has a "flare-up" of symptoms, look for the NEI trigger. Was it a stressful event (Nervous), a high-sugar meal (Endocrine), or a hidden infection (Immune)? Identifying the entry point of the flare helps you pivot the protocol effectively.

Pediatric vs. Geriatric Adaptations

The R.O.O.T.S. Method‚Ñ¢ is a universal framework, but the "dosage" and "depth" change across the lifespan.

Pediatric Considerations (The Growing Organism)

In children, the goal is Developmental Support. Protocols must be nutrient-dense but less aggressive in detoxification. A child's blood-brain barrier is more permeable, and their metabolic rate is higher. Focus heavily on the Assimilation node to ensure the raw materials for growth are present.

Geriatric Considerations (The Aging Organism)

In older adults, the goal is Resilience and Preservation. We must account for "Anabolic Resistance" (the need for more protein to maintain muscle) and "Achlorhydria" (low stomach acid). Geriatric protocols require higher doses of B12 and HCl support, but slower "Target" phases to avoid overtaxing reduced kidney/liver filtration rates.

Clinical Pivot Points: Knowing When to Shift

A protocol is a living document. You must identify "Red Flags" that indicate the current path is either too aggressive or misaligned.

Protocol Red Flags (The Need to Pivot)

Paradoxical Reactions

Client feels worse on "calming" supplements (e.g., anxiety from Ashwagandha).

Detox Stall

Skin breakouts or headaches that persist beyond 7 days of a detox protocol.

Rapid Weight Loss/Gain

Unintentional shifts of >5% body weight in under 30 days.

Cognitive Decline

Increased "brain fog" or memory lapses after starting a "Target" phase.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Hierarchy of Healing, why do we address the HPA axis before the Thyroid axis?

The HPA axis provides metabolic stability. Supporting the thyroid (which increases metabolic rate) without a stable HPA axis can lead to "burnout," anxiety, or heart palpitations as the body lacks the reserve to handle the increased metabolic demand.

2. What is a key physiological difference to consider when designing a geriatric protocol?

Geriatric clients often suffer from Achlorhydria (low stomach acid) and Anabolic Resistance. This means they require more support for protein digestion (HCl/enzymes) and higher protein intake to maintain muscle mass compared to younger adults.

3. What does "Endocrine Cross-Talk" mean in the context of rT3?

It refers to the HPA axis (stress) influencing the HPT axis (thyroid). High cortisol signals the body to produce Reverse T3, which blocks the active T3 from reaching the cells, effectively slowing the metabolism during times of stress.

4. When should a practitioner "Pivot" a protocol?

A pivot is required when "Red Flags" appear, such as paradoxical reactions to supplements, persistent detox symptoms (over 7 days), or sudden cognitive shifts, indicating the client's current physiological capacity is being exceeded.

Key Takeaways

  • Prioritize the Foundation: Always stabilize the Gut and HPA axis before moving to specific hormonal or autoimmune targeting.
  • Respect the Axes: Recognize that the HPA, HPT, and HPG axes are interconnected; you cannot "fix" one in isolation.
  • Adapt for Age: Pediatric protocols focus on growth/assimilation, while geriatric protocols focus on resilience and digestive support.
  • Monitor the NEI Loop: Use the Nervous-Endo-Immune connection to identify the root of symptom "flares."
  • Be Flexible: A master practitioner knows when to stay the course and when to pivot based on the client's unique bio-feedback.

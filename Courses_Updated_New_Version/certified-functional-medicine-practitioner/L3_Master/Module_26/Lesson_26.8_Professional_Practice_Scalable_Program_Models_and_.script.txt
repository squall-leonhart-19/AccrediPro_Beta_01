Do you see what happened there? You didn't tell her keto was wrong. You led her to the realization that the timing was wrong. You empowered her to be the "lead practitioner" who makes the tough calls for the client’s long-term health.

Now, as you move into this role of a Level 3 practitioner, you are essentially a gatekeeper of the profession. That comes with a set of best practices for supervision. First, you need to be consistent. Schedule regular one-on-one sessions. Consistency builds safety. If Linda knows she has a call with you every Tuesday, she won't panic on Friday when a client asks a hard question. She’ll know she has a safe place to bring it.

Second, always review their intake forms before you meet. Don't let the mentee just give you their "summary." Read the raw data. You will often see things in the client’s history that the mentee’s "rescue energy" has caused them to overlook. 

However—and this is a big "don't"—do not take over the case. Unless there is a legitimate safety risk, like a client being directed to stop a necessary medication, you must let the mentee remain the "face" of the program. If you step in and start talking to the client directly, you undermine the mentee’s authority and you destroy their confidence. All of your supervision should happen "backstage." 

And here is a little tip from my own years of mentoring: share your own mistakes. Linda is probably exactly where you were five or ten years ago. Tell her about the time you suggested a supplement that made a client’s symptoms worse because you forgot to check their digestion. It turns a high-pressure supervision session into a collaborative learning experience.

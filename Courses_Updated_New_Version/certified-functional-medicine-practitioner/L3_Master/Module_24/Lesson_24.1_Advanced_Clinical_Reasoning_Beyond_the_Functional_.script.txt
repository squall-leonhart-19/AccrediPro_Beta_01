Now, as we try to navigate this web, we have a major enemy: our own brains. Clinical reasoning is often sabotaged by what we call heuristics. These are mental shortcuts. That’s just a fancy way of saying we stop looking for causes once we find one that seems "likely."

There are four main biases I want you to watch out for. The first is Anchoring Bias. This happens when you rely too heavily on the very first piece of information a client gives you. Imagine a client walks in and says, "I was exposed to mold three years ago." If you have anchoring bias, you might spend the next hour only looking for mold symptoms. You might completely ignore the fact that they’ve had poor dental health for twenty years or that they’ve been on a high-stress corporate treadmill since the nineties. You’ve anchored yourself to the mold, and you’ve closed your mind to other possibilities.

Then there’s Availability Bias. This is the tendency to overestimate the importance of information that comes easily to mind. Maybe you just finished a weekend seminar on the microbiome. Suddenly, every single client who walks through your door looks like they have a leaky gut. It’s the "when you have a hammer, everything looks like a nail" syndrome. It’s not that leaky gut isn't real, it’s just that your brain is grabbing the most available piece of information instead of looking at the unique data in front of you.

The third one is Confirmation Bias. This is a big one. It’s when we search for data that supports our initial hunch while ignoring the data that contradicts it. Let’s say you think a client has hypothyroidism. You see a high TSH on their blood work and you think, "Aha! I knew it!" But then you ignore the fact that their T3 and T4 levels are actually normal, and you ignore their high stress markers which might be the real reason the TSH is elevated. You’re only seeing what you want to see.

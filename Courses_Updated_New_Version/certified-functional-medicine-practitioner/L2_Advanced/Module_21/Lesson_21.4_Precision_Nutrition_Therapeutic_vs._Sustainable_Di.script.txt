We also have the Clinical Ketogenic diet. This isn't the "butter in your coffee" keto you see on social media. This is a therapeutic shift in metabolism to ketone bodies to reduce neuro-inflammation. Now, let’s talk about one of the most important concepts in modern health: Metabolic Flexibility. This is simply the body's ability to switch seamlessly between burning carbohydrates and burning fats. Think of it like a hybrid car that switches between gas and electricity. Most clients who come to see you will be "metabolically stiff." They are reliant on frequent glucose spikes and they’ve lost the ability to access their own stored body fat for energy.

We use a three-phase protocol to restore this. Phase one is Stabilization. This lasts about two weeks. We eliminate processed sugars and seed oils, and we establish a simple 12-hour circadian fast. We’re just trying to get the blood sugar swings under control.

Phase two is Fat Adaptation. This usually takes four to six weeks. We lower net carbs to under 50 grams a day. This encourages something called mitochondrial biogenesis. We’re essentially forcing the body to build more cellular power plants and use fat as fuel.

Phase three is what I call Metabolic Switching. This is the ongoing phase. We introduce "Carb Cycling" or "Feast-Famine" cycles. This is crucial because it prevents the thyroid and leptin down-regulation that we often see when people stay on low-carb or low-calorie diets for too long. 

Here’s another coach’s tip: Metabolic flexibility is the "secret sauce" for perimenopausal women. As estrogen fluctuates, insulin sensitivity often drops. If we can teach the body to use ketones during the follicular phase and then allow for slightly higher healthy carbs during the luteal phase, we can drastically reduce PMS and menopausal symptoms. It’s all about working with the body’s natural rhythms.

As we move a client from a therapeutic diet to a sustainable one, we have to be very careful. The most dangerous time for a client's gut is actually the reintroduction phase. If you do it too quickly, the sudden influx of new foods can cause a massive inflammatory flare. 

I use a "Step-Down" Reintroduction Framework. It starts with the 3-Day Rule. We introduce exactly one new food—let’s say, almonds. The client eats a small portion on Day 1, a larger portion on Day 2, and then they stop and observe on Day 3. We’re looking for any reaction—skin flares, digestive upset, or changes in mood.

We also follow a Hierarchy of Irritants. We start with the foods least likely to cause a reaction and move toward the most likely. Usually, that looks like seeds first, then nuts, then nightshades, then eggs, then dairy, and finally gluten—if we decide to bring gluten back at all.

The goal is to reach an 80/20 Maintenance model. Once we’ve established a "safe list" of foods, we want the client to eat nutrient-dense, personalized foods 80 percent of the time. The other 20 percent is for flexibility—for social events, for travel, and for emotional health. We want them to have a life!

Now, while a client is on any kind of restrictive diet, even a therapeutic one, we have to watch out for "The Hidden Hunger." These are micronutrient deficiencies that don't show up right away but can manifest months down the line. I recommend monitoring key markers every 90 days.

Keep an eye on B12 and Folate, especially if the client is on a Low FODMAP or a vegan-leaning diet.

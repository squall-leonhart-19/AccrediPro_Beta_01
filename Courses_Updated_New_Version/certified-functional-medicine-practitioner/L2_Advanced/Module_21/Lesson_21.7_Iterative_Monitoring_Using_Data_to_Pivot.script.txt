So, how do we get that kind of clarity? It starts with the KPI Framework. In functional medicine, we love hearing how a client feels, but subjective reporting can be a bit of a minefield. We deal with something called the recency effect, where a client only remembers how they felt in the last 48 hours. If they had a bad night's sleep last night, they might tell you the whole month was a disaster. We also deal with negativity bias, where we naturally focus on the one thing that still hurts rather than the ten things that have improved.

To get around this, we have to establish objective KPIs right at the start of the Target phase. I break these down into four main buckets. First, we have the metabolic bucket. Subjectively, we ask about energy and cravings, but objectively, we look at things like mean glucose from a CGM or fasting insulin levels. 

Next is the autonomic bucket. We want to know about their stress perception and sleep quality, but we back that up with HRV data and their percentage of deep sleep. Third is the inflammatory bucket. They might report less joint pain or clearer thinking, but we want to see those numbers moving in markers like hs-CRP, ESR, or TGF-beta 1. 

Finally, there is the hormonal bucket. We listen to what they say about libido or their menstrual cycle, but we also look at Basal Body Temperature or LH to FSH ratios. Here is my rule of thumb: if a client reports less than a 20% improvement in their subjective symptoms after 60 days of high compliance, do not wait for the 90-day mark to take action. That is your immediate signal to review the objective data and start looking for those hidden stressors.

Now, let's talk about the timing of re-testing. This is a common area where I see new practitioners struggle. If you test too early, you are just looking at the noise of the adjustment phase and wasting the client’s money. If you test too late, you lose momentum and the client might get discouraged. The secret is to tie your re-testing to the biological turnover rate of the system you are working on.

Testing too early in this department often just captures the body trying to figure out what to do with the new inputs, which can lead to confusing results that don't reflect the long-term reality.

Then we have our cardiometabolic markers in standard blood chemistry. Think about HbA1c. It reflects a 90-day average of blood glucose because that is the lifespan of a red blood cell. Inflammatory markers like hs-CRP can actually be re-checked every four to six weeks.

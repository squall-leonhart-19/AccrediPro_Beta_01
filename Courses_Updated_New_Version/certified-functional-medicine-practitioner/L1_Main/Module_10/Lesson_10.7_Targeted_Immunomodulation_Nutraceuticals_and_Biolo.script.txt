Let’s start with the foundation: Vitamin D3, K2, and the Vitamin D Receptor, or the VDR. In functional medicine, we don't really think of Vitamin D as a vitamin. It’s actually a secosteroid hormone. Think about that for a second. It’s a hormone that we can get from the sun or a bottle. Its primary role in the immune system is mediated through the VDR. This receptor is fascinating because it is present on almost every single immune cell in the body. T-cells, B-cells, and antigen-presenting cells all have a "lock" that only Vitamin D can fit into.

The VDR acts as a transcriptional regulator. This means that when Vitamin D binds to the receptor, it actually moves into the nucleus of the cell. Once it’s in there, it turns "on" or "off" over 200 different genes related to immune function. One of its most important jobs is promoting the differentiation of T-regulatory cells, or Tregs. I like to call Tregs the "peacekeepers" of the immune system. They are the ones that prevent autoimmunity by telling the aggressive cells to stand down.

Here’s a major coach tip for you: Never, and I mean never, recommend high-dose Vitamin D3 without Vitamin K2, specifically the MK-7 form. Here’s why. Vitamin D3 increases calcium absorption in the gut. That’s great for bones, but if you don't have enough K2, that calcium can end up in the wrong places. It can end up in the soft tissues, the kidneys, or even the arteries. K2 acts like a traffic cop. It ensures that the calcium Vitamin D brings into the body is directed into the bones and teeth where it belongs. For our immune protocols, a standard starting point is a ratio of 1,000 units of D3 to about 10 or 20 micrograms of K2. This synergy is non-negotiable for long-term safety.

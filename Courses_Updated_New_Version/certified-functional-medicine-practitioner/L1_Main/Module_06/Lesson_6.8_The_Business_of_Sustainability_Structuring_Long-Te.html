Common Coaching Pitfalls

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps. Awareness is 90% of the battle!

  • The "Lecture" Trap: Spending 10 minutes explaining the Krebs cycle while the client glazes over. Keep science to "need-to-know" analogies.
  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Jumping to "take this supplement" before the client feels heard. The "Reveal" phase is about understanding, not just fixing.
  • Ignoring the Emotional Component: Elenaâ€™s "hangry" episodes cause her shame. If you don't acknowledge the shame, the protocol won't stick.
  • Using "You Should": This triggers the client's inner critic. Use "What if we tried..." or "I wonder if..." instead.

Check Your Practice

1. Why is the "Blood Sugar Rollercoaster" a better explanation than "You need more willpower"?

It shifts the focus from a character flaw to a physiological mechanism. This reduces shame, builds the coaching relationship, and makes the solution (stabilizing blood sugar) feel achievable rather than a test of strength.

2. What is the "Emergency Signal" the brain sends during a blood sugar crash?

The brain perceives low glucose as a threat to survival and triggers a release of stress hormones (like cortisol and adrenaline) to dump stored sugar into the blood, while simultaneously driving intense cravings for quick-energy carbohydrates.

3. Which coaching trap involves giving too much clinical detail?

The "Lecture Trap." It disconnects the coach from the client and can make the client feel overwhelmed or unintelligent, rather than empowered.

4. How does the "Reveal" phase philosophy apply to Elena's weight gain?

By "revealing" that midsection weight gain is often a sign of insulin resistance or high cortisol rather than just "eating too much," the coach helps Elena see her body as a system that needs balancing, not a problem that needs punishing.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Biology Over Blame: Always frame metabolic symptoms as physiological responses to environmental or dietary inputs.
  • Analogies are Power: Use "Rollercoasters," "Engines," and "Fuel" to make complex science feel accessible and actionable.
  • Listen for the Shame: When a client mentions "willpower" or "guilt," stop and address the biology immediately to build trust.
  • Professional Authority: Your ability to explain the "why" is what separates you from a "health coach" and makes you a Functional Practitioner.

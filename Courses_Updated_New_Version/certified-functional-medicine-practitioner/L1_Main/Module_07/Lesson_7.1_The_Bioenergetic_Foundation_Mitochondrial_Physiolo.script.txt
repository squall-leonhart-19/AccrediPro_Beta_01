So, let’s start at the very beginning. To understand why mitochondria break down, we have to understand what they actually are. This takes us back about one and a half billion years. Imagine a primitive cell floating in the ancient ocean. This cell engulfed a small bacterium. But instead of digesting it for food, something incredible happened. They formed a partnership. The bacterium provided energy, and the larger cell provided protection and nutrients. This is the Endosymbiotic Theory.

Why does this matter to you as a coach in 2024? It matters because it means mitochondria are essentially "foreign guests" living inside our cells. Because of this origin, mitochondria have their own DNA, which we call mtDNA. This is completely distinct from the nuclear DNA that holds your genetic blueprint for eye color or height. 

There are three huge clinical implications of this. First, mitochondrial DNA lacks histones. Think of histones as a suit of armor that protects our main DNA. Since mtDNA doesn't have this armor, it is ten to twenty times more susceptible to oxidative damage. Second, this DNA is inherited exclusively from the mother. This allows us to track metabolic lineages and understand why some families seem more prone to energy failure than others. And third, mtDNA is located right next to the Electron Transport Chain. This is the site where the cell produces "exhaust" or reactive oxygen species. I like to tell my clients it’s like keeping your most precious library books right in the middle of a fireplace. The risk of them catching fire is incredibly high.

When you’re explaining this to a client, use that "Foreign Guest" analogy. Tell them: "Your mitochondria are like guests living in your house. They have their own rules and their own blueprints. If we don't feed them correctly, or if we let the trash—the toxins—build up in the house, those guests are going to stop paying rent. And in your body, that rent is energy."

Now, let’s move inside the mitochondrial matrix and talk about the Krebs Cycle. You might remember this from biology class as the Tricarboxylic Acid or TCA cycle. Most people think of it as just a way to get to ATP. But in functional medicine, we see it as much more. Its primary job is to strip electrons from the food we eat—the carbs, fats, and proteins—and hand them off to the next stage. But the intermediates created during this process are actually critical signaling molecules.

Let’s look at a few of them. Citrate is one. When citrate levels are high, it signals "energy abundance" to the cell. It actually tells the cell to stop breaking down sugar because the tank is full. Then there’s Alpha-Ketoglutarate. This one is a superstar for longevity. It’s a key regulator of epigenetic expression. Then we have Succinate. This is a fascinating one. Succinate acts as a "mitokine." If the mitochondria are damaged and succinate leaks out into the rest of the cell, it triggers inflammatory signaling. It’s like a flare gun going off, telling the body there’s trouble at the power plant. Finally, we have Malate, which is essential for moving energy into the mitochondria through something called the Malate-Aspartate Shuttle.

In a healthy person, these intermediates stay in a beautiful balance. But in a dysfunctional cell, we see "blocks" in the cycle. These blocks are usually caused by nutrient deficiencies. The enzymes that run the Krebs Cycle are "hungry" for B-vitamins and magnesium. If those aren't there, the cycle backs up. The body perceives this backup as a danger signal, not just a lack of fuel.

If the Krebs Cycle is the "prep cook" in our cellular kitchen, then the Electron Transport Chain, or the ETC, is the "executive chef." This is where the real magic happens. About ninety percent of your cellular energy is produced here through a process called Oxidative Phosphorylation. 

The ETC is made up of five protein complexes embedded in the inner membrane of the mitochondria. Think of it like a bucket brigade. Electrons are passed from Complex I to Complex II, then to III, and so on. As these electrons move, they power little pumps. These pumps push protons into a small space, creating a massive amount of pressure. 

I love using the "Hydroelectric Dam" analogy here. Imagine a massive dam. The protons are the water being held back by the wall. Complex Five is the turbine at the bottom of the dam. When the water—the protons—is allowed to flow through that turbine, it spins. That spinning motion is what generates electricity, or in our case, ATP. 

But what happens if the dam has leaks? If the mitochondrial membrane is damaged by toxins or poor diet, the protons leak out. The pressure drops. The turbine won't spin efficiently, and your client’s energy production plummets. This is why things like Coenzyme Q10 are so vital. CoQ10 is the essential "shuttle" that moves the buckets between the complexes. You’ve probably seen this in practice: many common medications, especially statins, inhibit the body’s ability to make CoQ10. When that shuttle is missing, the bucket brigade stops. This is why muscle pain and fatigue are such common side effects of those medications. The "chef" has no ingredients to work with.

Now, I want to clear up a common misconception. We often see mitochondria drawn in textbooks as static little beans floating in the cell. That couldn't be further from the truth. Mitochondria are actually a dynamic, social network. They are constantly changing shape, moving, and communicating. They do this through two main processes: Fission and Fusion.

Fusion is when two mitochondria merge into one. This is a survival mechanism. It allows them to share resources and "average out" any damage. If one mitochondrion is struggling, it can fuse with a healthy neighbor to keep the lights on. Fission is the opposite. It’s when one mitochondrion splits into two. While this is necessary for making more mitochondria, its most important job is quality control. The cell uses fission to break off a damaged piece of a mitochondrion. That broken piece is then sent to be recycled in a process called Mitophagy. 

This is essentially how the cell "cleans its own house." A very important study published in Nature Communications in 2022 found that an imbalance in this process—specifically having too much fission—is a hallmark of chronic fatigue syndrome and neurodegenerative diseases. When the cell can no longer clean itself, it becomes filled with "zombie" mitochondria. These are organelles that produce more "smoke," or oxidative stress, than they do "fire," or energy. As a coach, your goal is often to help the body get back to a state where it can effectively "clean the house" again.

As we wrap up the physiological portion, we need to talk about why some symptoms show up before others. Not all tissues in the body are created equal when it comes to energy. Mitochondria are concentrated in the organs with the highest demand. These are your "canaries in the coal mine."

The first is the brain. Even though your brain is only about two percent of your total body weight, it consumes a staggering twenty percent of your body’s total ATP. This is why brain fog is often the very first sign of mitochondrial decay. If the power grid flickers, the most expensive equipment—the brain—glitches first. This manifests as depression, word-finding difficulties, and cognitive decline.

Next is the heart. The heart has the highest concentration of mitochondria in the entire body. In fact, up to thirty-five percent of the volume of a heart cell is just mitochondria. The heart never gets a break. It requires a constant, uninterrupted stream of ATP to keep beating. 

And finally, we have the muscles. Skeletal muscle needs a massive and rapid flux of energy to move.

Let’s start with the big paradigm shift. We have to talk about Optimal versus Normal. The most common thing you’ll hear in your practice is: "My doctor said my labs are normal, but I feel like I’m dying." To understand why this happens, we have to pull back the curtain on how those "normal" ranges are created in the first place.

Conventional reference ranges are based on a statistical bell curve. They take the average of the population that uses that specific lab. Now, think about that for a second. Who is usually going to a lab to get blood drawn? It’s people who are already seeking medical care. It’s people who don’t feel well. 

This means the "normal" range is calculated from a group of people who are often already sick. It’s not a "healthy" baseline. It’s an "average of the sick" baseline. And here is the scary part. As our population gets sicker, those reference ranges actually widen. They expand to accommodate the new, lower average. 

I want you to think of the conventional range as a "pathology range." If you fall outside of it, you likely have a diagnosable disease. But I want you to think of the functional range as the "performance range." This is the narrow window where the body functions with the most resilience. It’s where you have the most vitality.

Let’s look at some examples so you can see the difference. Take fasting glucose. A conventional lab might say anything from 65 to 99 is normal. But in functional medicine, we want to see that between 75 and 86. Why? Because research shows that once you start creeping up toward 90 or 95, you’re already showing signs of early insulin resistance and cortisol dysregulation. You aren’t "diabetic" yet, but your engine is starting to smoke.

Or look at TSH, which measures thyroid function. The conventional range is huge—usually 0.45 all the way up to 4.5. But for optimal health, we want to see that between 1.0 and 2.0. If a client is at a 3.8, a conventional doctor says they’re fine. But you know that at 3.8, their metabolism is slowing down. They’re going to be tired. They’re going to have a hard time losing weight. They are in what we call subclinical hypothyroidism.

Vitamin D is another big one. Many labs still say 30 is the cutoff for "normal." But we want our clients between 50 and 80. Why? Because at 30, you might not have rickets, but you are vulnerable to immune issues, bone loss, and low mood. A 2022 study in the Journal of Clinical Medicine found that people with levels below 30 had a significantly higher risk of chronic inflammatory conditions compared to those at 50. That’s a massive difference in quality of life.

Finally, consider Ferritin, which is your iron storage. The conventional range can go as low as 15. But for a woman to have healthy hair and good energy, she really needs to be between 50 and 100. If she’s at 18, she’s "normal" by lab standards, but she’s going to be losing her hair and feeling completely drained of oxygen.

Now that you understand the "why," let’s talk about the "what." Every Reveal phase in the R.O.O.T.S. Method should begin with what I call the Essential Reveal Panel. This is your 30,000-foot view. It gives you the most information for the least amount of money.

The first part of this is the Complete Blood Count, or the CBC with Differential. Now, an MD looks at a CBC to see if you have leukemia or severe anemia. We look at it for immune activation and nutrient status. For instance, we look at something called Mean Corpuscular Volume, or MCV. It measures the size of your red blood cells. If those cells are getting a little too big—even if they’re still in the "normal" range—it tells us that the body is struggling to find enough B12 or Folate. It’s a functional deficiency that we can catch years before it becomes a medical crisis.

Next is the Comprehensive Metabolic Panel, or the CMP. This looks at blood sugar, electrolytes, and your kidney and liver function. In functional medicine, we pay very close attention to liver enzymes like AST and ALT. If these are in the high 20s or 30s, they are still "normal" conventionally. Conventional medicine usually only worries if it’s over 3.0. But in functional medicine, we want it under 1.0. Now, let’s talk about some metabolic red flags that are often left out of standard physicals. These are the "canaries in the coal mine." 

The first is Homocysteine. This is an amino acid that can be toxic to your blood vessels and your brain if it gets too high. It’s also our best window into something called methylation. Methylation is just a fancy word for how your body repairs DNA and detoxifies. We want Homocysteine between 5 and 7. If it gets over 10, we start worrying about cognitive decline and heart disease. Most doctors never check this until after someone has a heart attack. We check it so they never have one.

The second red flag is Hemoglobin A1c. You’ve probably heard of this. While fasting glucose is a snapshot of one moment, A1c is the three-month average of your blood sugar. I like to use the "Candy-Coated Cells" analogy with my clients. I tell them to imagine sugar sticking to their red blood cells like a hard candy coating. The thicker that coating gets, the harder it is for oxygen to get into the cells. If your cells can’t get oxygen, you can’t make energy. 

A 2021 meta-analysis showed that even small increases in A1c within the "normal" range—going from 5.0% to 5.4%—increased the risk of metabolic syndrome by 8%. This is why we don't wait for "abnormal." We act on "sub-optimal."

Now, eventually, you might need to go deeper. This brings us to specialty testing. Foundational bloodwork tells us *what* is happening. Specialty testing tells us *why* it’s happening. But these tests are expensive, and as a professional, you have to be strategic.

If a client has chronic bloating or skin issues like acne, we might order a GI-MAP. This is a stool analysis that looks for gut dysperosis, parasites, and markers of gut immunity. It tells us exactly who is living in the gut and if they’re causing trouble.

If you’re working with a perimenopausal woman who can’t sleep and is dealing with mood swings, the DUTCH test is the gold standard. It’s a dried urine test that shows not just how much hormone she’s making, but how her body is breaking those hormones down. It shows us the "metabolites," which is a level of detail you just can’t get from blood.

And then there’s the Organic Acids Test, or the OAT. Think of this as a metabolic snapshot. It looks at yeast overgrowth, how your neurotransmitters are breaking down, and how well your mitochondria—your cellular powerhouses—are working.

But here is the catch. You cannot just order everything at once. That leads to "sticker shock" and client overwhelm. I want you to use what I call the "Tiered Approach."

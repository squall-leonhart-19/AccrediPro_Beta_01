The Alessio Fasano Model: The Triad of Pathogenesis

In 2009, Dr. Alessio Fasano published groundbreaking research that shifted the paradigm of immunology. He proposed that for an autoimmune disease to manifest, three factors must be present simultaneously. If you remove any one leg of this stool, the disease process can be halted or even reversed.

1

Genetic Predisposition

Specific HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) genes make an individual more "likely" to present certain antigens to the immune system. However, genetics only account for about 25-30% of the risk. Genes are the loaded gun, but they are not the trigger.

2

Environmental Triggers

These are the "insults" that activate the immune system. Triggers include chronic infections (EBV, Lyme), toxic metals (mercury, lead), mycotoxins (mold), and dietary proteins (gluten, lectins).

3

Intestinal Permeability (Leaky Gut)

This is the most critical discovery. Without a breach in the gut barrier, triggers cannot enter systemic circulation to interact with the immune system. Fasano‚Äôs research suggests that leaky gut is a necessary precursor to autoimmunity.

Molecular Mimicry: The Case of Mistaken Identity

How does the immune system "forget" what is self and what is non-self? One of the primary mechanisms is molecular mimicry. This occurs when a foreign antigen (like a virus or food protein) shares a similar amino acid sequence with a human tissue protein.

When the immune system creates antibodies to fight the invader, those same antibodies "cross-react" with the human tissue because they look so similar. A classic example is the similarity between the alpha-gliadin protein in gluten and the transglutaminase enzyme in the thyroid gland. This is why a staggering number of Hashimoto‚Äôs patients see improvement on a gluten-free diet.

Bystander Activation and Epitope Spreading

Autoimmunity is rarely a static event; it is often a progressive "smoldering" fire. Two mechanisms explain how a small immune response turns into a systemic issue:

  • Bystander Activation: During an intense immune response to an infection, the surrounding healthy tissue is damaged by "friendly fire" (reactive oxygen species and cytokines). This damaged self-tissue is then picked up by the immune system and labeled as an enemy.
  • Epitope Spreading: As the immune system continues to attack a specific part of a protein (an epitope), the tissue becomes further damaged, exposing new parts of the protein that the immune system hadn't seen before. The immune system then creates new antibodies against these new sections, broadening the attack.

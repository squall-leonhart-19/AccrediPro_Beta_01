Now, let’s pivot and talk about micronutrient density. In our methodology, we always try to move the conversation from calories to information. Micronutrient density is just a measure of how many vitamins, minerals, and phytonutrients are present per calorie of food. Think about it this way: a calorie from a nutrient-void cracker and a calorie from a nutrient-dense kale leaf are not the same. They are processed entirely differently by the body’s biotransformation pathways. 

If we look at the research, leafy greens like kale and spinach are at the top of the charts for density. They’re packed with Vitamin K, folate, and magnesium. Then you have the cruciferous family—broccoli, bok choy—which give us sulforaphane and Vitamin C. And we can’t forget organ meats, specifically beef liver. In the functional world, we often call liver "nature’s multivitamin" because its density of B12, Vitamin A, and copper is just off the charts. On the flip side, you have refined grains. A slice of white bread might have the same calories as a big bowl of veggies, but it offers almost no "operating instructions" for the body, other than maybe some synthetic vitamins they’ve sprayed on during processing.

When you’re coaching clients, don’t focus so much on what they need to remove. That creates a scarcity mindset. Instead, focus on what they can crowd in. I use the Density First rule. I tell my clients to start every meal with the most nutrient-dense item on their plate. If they eat the salad or the roasted broccoli first, they’re ensuring the body gets those vital micronutrients before it gets the heavier fuel of the macronutrients. It changes the way the body signals fullness and manages blood sugar.

We also have to address a really uncomfortable reality in modern nutrition: the soil crisis. You might have seen the 2023 meta-analysis that confirmed a really startling trend. The mineral content of our fruits and vegetables has declined by somewhere between 20% and 40% since the 1950s. That is a massive drop in just a few generations. The reason for this is industrial farming. Practices like monocropping and the heavy use of glyphosate have decimated the mycorrhizal fungi in the soil. Those fungi are what help plants actually take up minerals from the earth. 

This means that even if your client is eating a clean, whole-foods diet, they might still be micronutrient deficient. It’s not their fault; it’s a systemic issue. As a practitioner, your job is to guide them toward regenerative agriculture whenever possible. Sourcing from farms that focus on soil health makes a huge difference. You should also look for heirloom varieties of produce, which are often much higher in phytonutrients than the commercial hybrids you find in the standard grocery store. And frankly, this is where strategic supplementation comes in. Because of soil depletion, many people simply cannot get enough magnesium or trace minerals from food alone anymore.

But here is a very important point: you are not what you eat; you are what you absorb. You can have the most expensive, organic, nutrient-dense diet in the world, but if your digestion is compromised, those nutrients are just passing right through you. In the Functional Medicine Matrix, we call this the Assimilation Node. Micronutrients are often locked inside complex food structures, and we have to be able to break them down.

I want to give you a quick bioavailability checklist to use with your clients. First, check their mastication. It sounds simple, but digestion begins in the mouth. If they aren’t chewing their food into a paste, they aren’t releasing the nutrients. Second, check for hypochlorhydria, which is just a fancy word for low stomach acid. This is incredibly common in clients over the age of 40. Without enough stomach acid, you cannot ionize minerals like iron, zinc, or B12 for absorption. Third, look at fat-soluble co-factors. Vitamins A, D, E, and K require dietary fat to be absorbed. If a client is eating a "healthy" salad with a fat-free dressing, they are essentially wasting those nutrients. And finally, be aware of anti-nutrients like phytates and oxalates. In things like raw grains or raw spinach, these compounds can bind to minerals and make them unavailable to the body. Sometimes, just cooking or sprouting these foods is the key to unlocking their nutrition.

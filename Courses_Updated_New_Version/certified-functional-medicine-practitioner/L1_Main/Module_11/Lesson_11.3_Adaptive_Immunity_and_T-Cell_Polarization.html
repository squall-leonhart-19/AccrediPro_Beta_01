The Special Forces: Adaptive Immunity

If innate immunity is the "infantry," the adaptive immune system is the Special Forces. It is highly specific, requires time to mobilize (usually 4-7 days upon first exposure), and most importantly, it possesses immunological memory. This memory is why we don't get the same strain of chickenpox twice, but it is also why the body can "remember" to attack the thyroid gland for decades.

The two primary players in this system are:

  • T-Lymphocytes (T-cells): The "commanders" that direct the attack and the "assassins" that kill infected cells.
  • B-Lymphocytes (B-cells): The "factories" that produce antibodies to neutralize pathogens.

T-Cell Polarization: The Th1/Th2/Th17 Paradigm

When a T-cell is "born" in the thymus, it starts as a Naive T-cell (Th0). Depending on the "chemical soup" (cytokines) present in its environment, it will polarize‚Äîor mature‚Äîinto a specific type of helper cell. This decision determines the direction of the entire immune response.

In a healthy individual, these subsets exist in a dynamic equilibrium. However, chronic stress, toxins, and gut dysbiosis can tip the "see-saw." For example, a Th2-dominant individual may struggle with seasonal allergies and "brain fog" whenever they are exposed to perfumes or pollen.

Loss of Self-Tolerance: The Perfect Storm

Why does the immune system suddenly decide to attack the body? According to the Fasano Model, autoimmunity requires a "perfect storm" of three factors:

  1. Genetic Predisposition: Specific HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) genes that make T-cells more likely to recognize self-tissue.
  2. Environmental Triggers: These include heavy metals (mercury/lead), chronic infections (EBV, Lyme), or food proteins (gluten/dairy).
  3. Intestinal Permeability ("Leaky Gut"): The breach in the barrier that allows triggers to enter the bloodstream and meet the immune system.

B-Cell Function and Antibody Production

B-cells are the "archers" of the immune system, firing specialized arrows called antibodies (Immunoglobulins). Not all antibodies are created equal:

  • IgE (Immediate): Responsible for "true" allergies (anaphylaxis, hives). These trigger mast cells to release histamine instantly.
  • IgG (Delayed): The most abundant antibody. In functional medicine, we look for IgG food sensitivities, which can cause symptoms 24-72 hours after ingestion.
  • IgA (Mucosal): The "first line of defense" in the gut and respiratory tract. Low Secretory IgA (sIgA) often indicates chronic stress or "burnout."

Molecular Mimicry: The Identity Theft of Immunology

Molecular Mimicry occurs when a foreign antigen (like a food protein or a virus) shares a similar amino acid sequence with a human tissue protein. The immune system, in its zeal to destroy the intruder, accidentally attacks the "look-alike" human tissue.

The most famous example in functional medicine is Gluten and the Thyroid. The protein structure of alpha-gliadin (in wheat) looks remarkably similar to the protein structure of the thyroid gland. When a "leaky gut" allows gliadin into the blood, the Th17 cells may start an attack that eventually targets the thyroid, leading to Hashimoto's Thyroiditis.

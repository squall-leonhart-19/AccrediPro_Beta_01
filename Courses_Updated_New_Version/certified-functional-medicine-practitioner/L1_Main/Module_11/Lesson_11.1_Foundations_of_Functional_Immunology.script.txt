Now, let’s talk about how the immune system is actually organized. I love to use the "Castle Defense" analogy with my clients. It makes these complex concepts so much easier to visualize. Think of the immune system as being organized into three distinct layers.

The first layer is the Physical and Chemical Barriers. These are the moat and the walls of the castle. This includes your skin, the acidity of your stomach, and the mucosal lining of your gut and lungs. This is actually the most overlooked part of immunology. If the walls are breached—if someone has a "Leaky Gut"—the internal immune system is forced to work 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Eventually, that leads to burnout.

The second layer is the Innate Immune System. These are the first responders, the guards on the wall. These cells, like Macrophages and Neutrophils, are non-specific. They don't need to know exactly who the enemy is; they just know that something doesn't belong. They create acute inflammation to kill the invader. But if this system stays "on" for too long, it starts to cause collateral damage to the castle itself. This is where we see that "achy" feeling Sarah had.

The third layer is the Adaptive Immune System. These are the Special Forces. This layer consists of B-cells, which make antibodies, and T-cells. The defining feature of this system is memory. It remembers the chickenpox virus you had 30 years ago so you don't get it again. However, in our modern world, this system can get confused. We call this "Molecular Mimicry." This is when the immune system tries to attack a foreign protein, like gluten, but because the gluten protein looks a lot like a protein in your thyroid, the immune system starts attacking the thyroid by mistake.

This brings us to the Practitioner’s Filter. With literally thousands of supplements on the market, how do you choose? You cannot just recommend what you saw on a popular health blog. You must apply a professional filter. This is what separates a certified professional from an amateur.

The first part of the filter is Safety. This is non-negotiable. You must check for contraindications with any medications the client is taking. For example, you would never suggest St. John’s Wort to someone on an SSRI because of the risk of serotonin syndrome. Safety always comes first.

The second part is Efficacy and Bioavailability. Is the form of the nutrient actually absorbable? We see this a lot with magnesium. A cheap magnesium oxide isn't going to do much for someone with a deficiency because it’s poorly absorbed. You would want to look for something like magnesium glycinate. You are paying for the delivery system as much as the nutrient itself.

The third part is Evidence. Does the peer-reviewed literature support this specific use? I often look at meta-analyses. For instance, a 2022 study showed that Bifidobacterium infantis has a statistically significant effect on reducing IBS symptoms. When you have that kind of data behind you, you can speak with absolute confidence.

This leads perfectly into our philosophy of the Minimum Effective Dose, or MED. The goal of this Target phase is not to keep a client on twenty supplements forever. We want the smallest dose that will produce the desired outcome. If we over-supplement, we risk something called poly-nutra-pharmacy.

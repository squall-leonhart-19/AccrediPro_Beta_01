Common Mistakes & Success Markers

4 Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

  1. The "Information Dump": Explaining too much science and losing the client's interest.
  2. The "Yes-Man" Trap: Letting the client dictate the targets because you're afraid to lead.
  3. Lack of Pausing: Not giving the client space to process the "Target" decision.
  4. Skipping the "Why": Telling them what to do without explaining how it fixes their specific pain point.
Practice Tip

If you feel imposter syndrome creeping in, remember: You have the map (The Functional Matrix). They are lost in the woods. Even if you only know the next three steps, you are still the guide they need.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to choose a "Big Rock" rather than addressing all symptoms at once?

Addressing everything at once leads to client overwhelm, low compliance, and an inability to track which intervention actually caused the improvement. Strategic targeting ensures the client sees wins without burning out.

2. What should you do if a client insists on a "Target" that you know isn't the root cause?

Acknowledge their desire ("I hear that you really want to focus on X"), but pivot back to the foundation using the "Why" ("However, for X to work effectively, we first need to stabilize Y. If we don't, we're just putting a band-aid on it.")

3. According to the "Target" methodology, what is the 'Second Brain' that often resolves brain fog when addressed?

The Gut (Digestive System). Inflammation in the gut often crosses the blood-brain barrier, leading to cognitive symptoms.

4. What is a "Success Marker" in a targeting conversation?

When the client exhales, shows visible relief, or says something like, "That makes so much senseâ€”I finally feel like someone has a plan for me."

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • The Rule of Three: Never give a client more than three major targets at a time.
  • Connect the Dots: Always link your target (e.g., Gut) to their pain point (e.g., Brain Fog).

Let’s look at Phase I. I call this the "Unpackaging" phase. It is governed by a massive family of enzymes called the Cytochrome P450 enzymes. You’ll often see this written as CYP450. Think of this as the liver taking a stable, fat-soluble toxin and "activating" it. The liver uses oxidation, reduction, or hydrolysis to make the molecule reactive. 

Now, here is the catch. This process creates something called Reactive Oxygen Species, or ROS. Often, the "activated" toxin is actually more dangerous and more toxic than the original molecule was. It’s like opening a box and finding a live wire inside. If Phase II—the neutralizing phase—is sluggish, these reactive intermediates sit around and damage your liver cells. They increase systemic inflammation. This is why it is clinically irresponsible to support Phase I without making sure Phase II is ready to catch what’s being thrown at it.

To keep Phase I running smoothly, the liver needs cofactors. These are mostly B-vitamins—B2, B3, B6, B12, and Folate. It helps protect the liver cells, the hepatocytes, from the damage that Phase I can cause.

Once we’ve "unpacked" the toxin in Phase I, we move to Phase II. This is the "Neutralizing" phase, also known as conjugation. This is where the magic happens. The liver takes a specific molecule—a conjugate—and attaches it to that reactive intermediate from Phase I. This makes it water-soluble and non-toxic.

Now, let’s talk about Methylation. This is a process that happens billions of times every second in your body. In the context of detox, it is how we clear out estrogens and heavy metals. You’ve probably heard of SNPs, or Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms. These are common genetic variations. The two we see most often are MTHFR and COMT.

MTHFR is the enzyme that converts folic acid into its active form, 5-MTHF. If a client has an MTHFR mutation, their ability to methylate can be reduced by 30 to 70 percent. This is a huge deal. With these clients, you should never use synthetic folic acid. It can actually block their receptors and make things worse. You always want to use the active form, Methylfolate.

Then there is COMT. This enzyme breaks down dopamine, epinephrine, and estrogens. If a client has a "slow" COMT SNP, they often feel "wired and tired." But here is the thing. Even if Phase I and Phase II are working perfectly, the toxins still have to leave the body. This is Phase III, or the Clearance phase. This involves transporting those neutralized toxins out of the cells and into the bile or the blood.

And then, we have binders. Binders are substances that don't get absorbed into the body. Instead, they stay in the GI tract and "mop up" the toxins that the bile has dropped off.

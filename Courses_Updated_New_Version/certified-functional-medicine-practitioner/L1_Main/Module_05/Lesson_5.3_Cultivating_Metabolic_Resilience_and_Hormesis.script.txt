Let’s start with the big concept: The Science of Hormetic Stress. In conventional wellness, we’re often taught to avoid stress at all costs. But in Functional Medicine, we recognize a phenomenon known as hormesis. Hormesis is what we call a biphasic dose-response. This basically means that a low dose of a stressor, something that might actually be toxic or harmful in a high dose, triggers a beneficial adaptive response in the body.

I like to think of this as biological callousing. Think about what happens when you start a new hobby, like gardening or playing the guitar. At first, the friction on your skin causes a bit of irritation. But over time, the skin adapts. It gets tougher. It builds a callus. That callus isn't a sign of damage; it’s a sign of a body that has adapted to handle more friction. Hormetic stressors do the exact same thing at a cellular level. When we lift weights, we’re creating tiny micro-tears in our muscle fibers. When those fibers repair, they come back stronger. When we expose the body to heat or cold, we’re triggering cellular pathways that repair our DNA, clear out old zombie cells, and ramp up our internal antioxidant defenses. We aren't just breaking the body down; we are signaling it to rebuild itself better than before.

The success of hormesis really comes down to the dose. This is where the biphasic curve comes in. If a stressor is too weak, nothing happens. There’s no adaptation because the body doesn't feel challenged. On the other end of the spectrum, if the stressor is too intense, or if it stays around for too long, it turns into distress. That leads to exhaustion, pathology, and eventually, disease. The sweet spot we’re looking for is called eustress, or beneficial stress.

When you’re explaining this to a client, I find it’s really helpful to use the sunlight analogy. If you go outside for ten minutes without sunscreen, your body produces Vitamin D, your mood improves, and your circadian rhythm gets a healthy reset. That’s hormesis. But if you stay out in that same sun for ten hours, you’re going to end up with a third-degree burn. That’s toxicity. We are looking for the biological tan, not the burn. Our goal is to find that perfect dose that triggers the body to get stronger without pushing it over the edge into burnout.

This leads us to the concept of the metabolic buffer. I want you to think of a metabolic buffer as the body’s ability to stay steady even when things get rocky. A client with high resilience, someone with a deep metabolic buffer, can handle a high-carb meal at a birthday party, or a night of poor sleep, or a really stressful week at work without their biomarkers crashing. Their fasting glucose stays relatively stable. Their Heart Rate Variability, or HRV, bounces back quickly. They have the physiological margin to handle life’s inevitable curveballs.

There was a fascinating study published in the journal Cell Metabolism in 2022 that really highlights this. Researchers found that individuals with higher metabolic flexibility—which is the body’s ability to switch back and forth between burning fats and burning carbohydrates—had 40% lower markers of systemic inflammation when they were exposed to acute physiological stress. Think about that. By training the body to be flexible, you are literally lowering the inflammatory fire that drives almost every chronic disease we face.

When we look at a client who lacks this buffer, a high-carb meal might lead to a three-hour glucose spike followed by a massive crash that leaves them shaky and hungry. But a resilient client? They might see a small rise, but they’re back to baseline within 90 minutes. When a non-resilient person misses sleep, they’re hit with intense brain fog and sugar cravings all day. The resilient person feels a little tired, sure, but their appetite stays stable and they can still function. This is what we’re building. We’re building a body that is harder to break.

One of the primary ways we build this buffer is by enhancing mitochondrial density. You probably remember from high school biology that mitochondria are the powerhouses of the cell. But in Functional Medicine, we view them as so much more. They are environmental sensors. They are constantly "listening" to the environment to decide if it’s safe to produce energy or if the body needs to go into a defensive mode. To sustain health long-term, we have to stimulate something called mitochondrial biogenesis. This is just a fancy way of saying we want to create new, healthy mitochondria.

The master regulator of this process is a protein called PGC-1alpha. Think of PGC-1alpha as the foreman on a construction site who tells the crew to start building more power plants. We can activate this foreman in a few specific ways. Cold exposure is a big one. When we get cold, it triggers the browning of white adipose tissue. This brown fat is packed with mitochondria that generate heat. Vigorous movement is another powerful trigger. High-Intensity Interval Training, or HIIT, forces the cell to produce more energy-generating units just to keep up with the demand of the workout. Even certain plant compounds, like the polyphenols found in resveratrol or quercetin, can mimic some of these stress signals and get that PGC-1alpha working.

Now, while we’re building new energy systems, we also have to talk about cellular cleanup. This is where autophagy comes in. The word autophagy literally means self-eating. It’s the process where your cells identify damaged components—like broken proteins or worn-out mitochondria—and they break them down and recycle them. It’s like having a dedicated cleaning crew that goes through your house every night, hauling away the trash and fixing the leaky faucets.

The problem is that in our modern world, the cleaning crew never gets to work. Autophagy is largely suppressed by a pathway called mTOR, which is activated whenever we eat protein or when insulin is high. On the flip side, autophagy is activated by a pathway called AMPK, which turns on when the body is low on energy. Because most people are eating from the moment they wake up until the moment they go to bed, they are constantly in an mTOR-dominant state. Their cleaning crew is essentially on a permanent strike.

To fix this and build resilience, we use strategic fasting cycles. A great place to start with almost any client is the 14:10 window. This is a foundational circadian fast where you eat all your food within a 10-hour window and fast for 14 hours. It’s simple, it’s sustainable, and it gives the body enough time to lower insulin and start that cleanup process.

We can also use something called protein cycling. This involves having one or two days a week where protein intake is kept lower, which allows mTOR to downregulate and lets autophagy peak. And then there’s metabolic switching. This is the goal of teaching the body to utilize ketones during the fasting window. Ketones are a much cleaner-burning fuel than glucose, and they send powerful signaling messages to the brain that promote focus and clarity.

I do want to offer a quick coach tip here, especially for those of you working with women. If you have a client in perimenopause or menopause, you have to be careful with aggressive fasting. Doing more than 16 hours of fasting every single day can sometimes backfire. It can spike cortisol and disrupt the delicate HPA axis, which is already under pressure during those years. For these women, I usually recommend focusing on circadian fasting—simply stopping all food intake three hours before bed. This usually gives them the best results for sleep and metabolic health without the added stress of a long fast.

Let’s move on to what I believe is the most overlooked tool for sustainability and longevity: skeletal muscle. For a long time, we thought of muscle as just something that helps us move or makes us look fit. But we now know that muscle is actually a massive metabolic sink for glucose and a powerful secretory organ. When your muscles contract, they release signaling molecules called myokines.

These myokines are incredible. They can cross the blood-brain barrier to stimulate something called BDNF, or Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor. Think of BDNF as Miracle-Gro for the brain. Myokines also improve insulin sensitivity in organs far away from the muscle, like the liver. And perhaps most importantly, they help reduce systemic inflammation. While we often think of the inflammation marker IL-6 as a "bad guy," when it’s released from muscle during exercise, it actually acts as an anti-inflammatory signal.

As your clients age, they naturally start to lose muscle mass. This is called sarcopenia. If you want to be a practitioner who focuses on true sustainability, you have to prioritize resistance training. A massive meta-analysis from 2023 found that for every 10% increase in muscle mass, there was an 11% reduction in the risk of death from all causes. Muscle isn't just about aesthetics; it is literally life insurance.

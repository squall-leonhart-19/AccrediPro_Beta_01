So, let’s start by reframing how we look at oxidative stress. If you browse any health food store, you’ll see bottles of pills promising to "blast" free radicals or "neutralize" oxidative stress. It’s portrayed as this villain that we need to eradicate. But in functional medicine, we know that Reactive Oxygen Species, or ROS, are actually essential messenger molecules. This is what we call Redox Signaling.

This is why we have to distinguish between two very different states. The first is what we call Oxidative Eustress. This is low-level ROS production that actually acts as a positive signal. It tells the body to upregulate its own internal defense mechanisms. This is the principle of hormesis—a little bit of stress makes the system stronger. Think of it like exercise. When you lift weights, you’re creating a small amount of oxidative stress in the muscle, which then signals the body to repair and get stronger.

The second state is Oxidative Distress. This is what happens when the accumulation of ROS becomes overwhelming. It exceeds the cell’s internal antioxidant capacity, and that’s when we start seeing damage to DNA, lipids, and proteins.

Now, let’s talk about how the body actually handles this. How does the cell know when to fight back? This brings us to the "Master Switch," which is the Keap1-Nrf2 pathway. I like to think of Nrf2 as the thermostat for your body’s internal antioxidant production.

Under normal, healthy conditions, Nrf2 is kept quiet. There’s a protein called Keap1 that acts like a tether, holding Nrf2 in the cytoplasm of the cell. While it’s tethered there, Nrf2 eventually gets broken down. But, when oxidative stress levels start to rise, Keap1 senses that change. It lets go of the tether. Nrf2 is then free to migrate into the nucleus of the cell. Once it’s in the nucleus, it binds to something called the Antioxidant Response Element, or the ARE.

This is where the magic happens. When Nrf2 binds to the ARE, it triggers the production of over 200 different protective genes. This includes things like Superoxide Dismutase, or SOD, and Catalase. These are the "big guns" of your internal antioxidant system. They are thousands of times more powerful than any vitamin C or vitamin E supplement you could ever swallow.

It tells the body, "Hey, there’s a little stress here, you better turn on the Nrf2 switch." So, the food is actually a signaling molecule that tells your body to produce its own internal medicine.

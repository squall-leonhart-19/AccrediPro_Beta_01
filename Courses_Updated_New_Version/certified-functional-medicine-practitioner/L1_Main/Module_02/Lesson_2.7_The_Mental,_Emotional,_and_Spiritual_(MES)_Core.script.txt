Now, I want to talk about something a bit heavier, but it is perhaps the most significant discovery in modern preventive medicine. I am talking about the Adverse Childhood Experiences study, or the ACE study. This research was conducted by the CDC and Kaiser Permanente, and it changed everything. It proved that childhood trauma is not just a memory. It is not just something that happened "in the past." It is actually biochemically imprinted on the adult body.

When we talk about ACEs, we are looking at things like abuse, neglect, or growing up in a household with substance abuse or mental illness. The researchers found a direct, dose-response relationship between the number of these experiences and the risk of chronic disease decades later. A massive meta-analysis from 2023, which looked at over 120,000 people across 70 different studies, confirmed this again.

Let’s look at the numbers because they are staggering. If we take someone with an ACE score of zero as the baseline, we can see how the risk climbs. Someone with a score of one to three has about one and a half times the risk of autoimmune disease. They have nearly double the risk of chronic fatigue. But when we look at people with an ACE score of four or higher, the numbers jump significantly. These individuals are more than twice as likely to have an autoimmune condition. They are over three times as likely to suffer from chronic fatigue syndrome.

Why does this happen? It is because the developing brain and nervous system are being calibrated during childhood. If a child grows up in an environment of fear or neglect, their "internal thermostat" for stress is set to high. They become hyper-vigilant. Their nervous system learns to stay in a state of high alert to keep them safe. Even when they become adults and the original threat is gone, the body stays in that high-alert state. This is what we call a high allostatic load. It is the wear and tear on the body that accumulates when you are constantly stressed. As a functional medicine practitioner, you have to realize that for these clients, their symptoms are often the body’s way of trying to survive a nervous system that never learned how to turn off.

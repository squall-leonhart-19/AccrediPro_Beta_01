Let’s start by looking at the ATM framework as a way of understanding causality. In Functional Medicine, we use this acronym to differentiate between the various layers of why someone is sick. Most conventional approaches focus almost exclusively on the diagnosis. They want to know the "what." But we want to know the "how" and the "why." I like to use the analogy of a forest fire to explain this. To understand a fire, you need to know three things. First, you need to know how dry the wood was. That’s the baseline state, or the Antecedents. Second, you need to know what the spark was. That’s the initiating event, or the Trigger. And third, you need to know if the wind is still blowing. Those are the sustaining factors, or the Mediators.

When you present a completed Timeline to a client, you are giving them a cohesive narrative of their life. This is incredibly healing. It often relieves the imposter syndrome that so many clients feel. They start to see that their health struggles aren't random or "all in their head." They see the logical progression of their symptoms. They see that there is a reason they feel the way they do.

Now, let's break down that first letter: A for Antecedents. I often call these the "Loaded Gun." Antecedents are factors that predispose an individual to an illness. These are things that existed long before the spark occurred. Now, we often think of genetics first when we talk about predispositions, and that’s a huge part of it. But antecedents encompass much more than just your DNA.

When we look at genetic predispositions, we are often looking for what we call Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms, or SNPs. You’ve probably heard of some of these. We look for things like MTHFR, which can affect how a person detoxifies. We look at TNF-alpha, which relates to inflammation. Or the VDR gene, which impacts how we metabolize Vitamin D. You’ve probably heard the saying that genetics load the gun, but the environment pulls the trigger. That is exactly what we are talking about here. These SNPs determine the "weakest links" in a person’s unique physiology.

But we have to look even further back than genetics. We have to look at congenital and birth factors. In Functional Medicine, we believe the "preconception" period is the true start of the timeline. A mother’s health during pregnancy matters immensely. Was she under extreme stress? What was her nutrient status? We also look at the mode of delivery. Was the client born via C-section or vaginally? This matters because a vaginal birth provides the first major "seeding" of the microbiome. We also look at whether the client was breastfed. All of these early factors set the stage for the adult immune system and the gut microbiome. They provide the "education" for the immune system.

Then we look at childhood. Was the client on frequent antibiotics for ear infections? Did they grow up on a high-sugar diet? These childhood experiences alter the baseline for what we call the "Defense and Repair" node on the Matrix. They create a state of being that makes the person more or less resilient to future stressors.

Next, we move to the T in our acronym: Triggers. These are the catalysts. Triggers are discrete, acute events that initiate a change in health status. When you are talking to a client, listen for a very specific phrase. Listen for them saying, "I haven't felt right since..." That phrase is your biggest clue that you’ve found a major clinical trigger.

Common triggers can be physical. This might be a car accident, a major surgery, or a head injury. They can also be infectious insults. Think about a severe bout of food poisoning that leads to chronic gut issues. Or think about viruses like Mononucleosis, which is the Epstein-Barr Virus, or something like Lyme disease. These infections can shift the body’s equilibrium permanently if not addressed.

But triggers aren't always physical or biological. Emotional trauma is a massive trigger. The loss of a loved one, a difficult divorce, or even extreme, prolonged career stress can be the spark that starts the fire. We also see toxic exposures as triggers. This might be moving into a home with hidden mold or a sudden, high-level exposure to pesticides.

That brings us to the final letter: M for Mediators. If the triggers are the spark, mediators are the fuel. These are the factors—both biochemical and psychosocial—that keep the fire burning. Unlike triggers, which are usually one-time events in the past, mediators are ongoing. They contribute to the persistence of the condition.

Some common mediators include things like inflammatory cytokines. This is that ongoing, "simmering" inflammation that just won't go away. Dietary choices are also major mediators. If a client continues to eat processed oils or high-sugar foods, they are essentially pouring gasoline on the fire every single day. Poor sleep is another one. Without deep sleep, the brain can't perform glymphatic drainage, and the body can't engage in hormonal repair.

But we also have to look at psychosocial mediators. One of the most powerful mediators is a person’s belief system. If a client has the mindset of "I will never get better," that belief itself acts as a mediator that sustains their physiological dysfunction.

Here is a little career insight for you. The practitioners who are the best at identifying Mediators usually have the highest client retention rates. Why? Because while triggers are in the past and we can't change them, mediators are happening in the present. When you address a mediator—like improving a client's sleep or removing an inflammatory food—you provide those "quick wins." Those wins keep the client motivated and engaged in the process.

So, how do you actually do this in practice? How do you plot a life history from preconception to the present? To construct a professional-grade timeline, you have to guide your client through their history chronologically. I recommend not just asking "what hurts today." Instead, ask about their life in seven-year increments. This helps people remember details they might otherwise skip over.

We always begin before birth. We ask about the mother’s health and the household environment. This gives us context for how the client’s HPA-axis, or their stress response system, developed. From there, we move forward.

As you plot this out, you’ll notice that a clinical timeline is rarely a straight line. It’s a series of peaks and valleys. You’ll see "Quiet Periods" and "Symptomatic Flares." Quiet periods are times of relative health. This is when the body’s resilience is high enough to outweigh the total toxic load. Symptomatic flares are the times when the "bucket overflowed."

This leads us to a very important concept: the Total Load. A 2022 study on chronic inflammatory response syndrome, or CIRS, really highlighted this. It showed that symptoms often don't appear until a certain "threshold" is reached. One trigger, like a single virus, might be manageable on its own. But if that virus occurs on top of an antecedent like leaky gut, and is fueled by an ongoing mediator like sleep apnea, the system eventually crashes. The bucket overflows.

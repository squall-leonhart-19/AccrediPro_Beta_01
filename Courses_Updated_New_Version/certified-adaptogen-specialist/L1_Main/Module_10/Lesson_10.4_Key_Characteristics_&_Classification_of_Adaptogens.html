01. The Brekhman-Dardymov Criteria: Defining an Adaptogen

The term "adaptogen" was first coined by Dr. Nikolay Lazarev in 1947, but it was his student, Dr. Israel Brekhman, and his colleague Dr. I.V. Dardymov, who in 1969 established the three universally recognized criteria that define a true adaptogen. These criteria remain the bedrock of adaptogen research and classification, guiding both scientific investigation and practical application for specialists like you.

1

Non-Specific Action

An adaptogen must help the body resist a broad spectrum of adverse physical, chemical, and biological stressors. It does not target one specific organ or disease but rather enhances the body's overall adaptive response to stress, promoting resilience.

2

Normalizing Effect

An adaptogen has a normalizing influence on physiological function, regardless of the direction of the pathological state. This means it can modulate imbalances, bringing the body back towards homeostasis. For example, it can lower elevated cortisol or raise suppressed immune function, acting biphasically as needed.

3

Non-Toxic

An adaptogen must be harmless and not influence normal body functions more than necessary, even when taken over long periods or in large doses. It should have a very low toxicity profile and minimal side effects, making it safe for sustained use.

These three criteria are more than just academic definitions; they are your compass for identifying legitimate adaptogens and understanding their profound utility. They differentiate adaptogens from stimulants, sedatives, or single-target pharmaceuticals. For instance, caffeine is a stimulant, not an adaptogen, because it doesn't normalize function and can cause significant side effects with prolonged use, failing criteria 2 and 3.

Coach's Tip

When discussing adaptogens with clients, especially those new to them, emphasize these three points. You might say: "Unlike a quick fix, adaptogens don't just 'cover up' symptoms. 02. Distinguishing Primary and Secondary Adaptogens

While the Brekhman-Dardymov criteria provide a clear definition, the world of botanicals is nuanced. Within the adaptogen category, it's helpful to distinguish between "primary" and "secondary" adaptogens, a classification often used in herbal medicine to reflect the depth of research and breadth of action.

Primary Adaptogens

These are the gold standard, extensively researched herbs that unequivocally meet all three Brekhman-Dardymov criteria. They have a long history of traditional use and robust scientific evidence demonstrating their multi-systemic, normalizing effects with high safety profiles.

  • Examples: Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera), Rhodiola (Rhodiola rosea), Siberian Ginseng (Eleuthero senticosus), Panax Ginseng (Panax ginseng), Schisandra (Schisandra chinensis).
  • Characteristics:
    • Broad-spectrum effects on the HPA axis and stress response.
    • Significant impact on energy, mood, cognitive function, and immune modulation.
    • Well-documented mechanisms of action, often involving multiple biochemical pathways.
    • Extensive clinical trials supporting efficacy and safety.

Secondary Adaptogens (or Adaptogenic-like Herbs)

These botanicals exhibit significant adaptogenic qualities and contribute to stress resilience, but may not meet all three Brekhman-Dardymov criteria as definitively as primary adaptogens, or their research is less extensive. They often possess specific affinities for certain organ systems while still contributing to overall adaptation.

  • Examples: Holy Basil (Ocimum sanctum), Reishi Mushroom (Ganoderma lucidum), Cordyceps (Cordyceps sinensis/militaris), Astragalus (Astragalus membranaceus), Licorice Root (Glycyrrhiza glabra).
  • Characteristics:
    • May have a more pronounced affinity for specific systems (e.g., immune, respiratory, adrenal support).
    • Often used synergistically with primary adaptogens to enhance specific outcomes.
    • Strong traditional use, with growing modern research supporting adaptogenic-like properties.
    • Generally safe, but some may have specific contraindications or require more careful dosing (e.g., Licorice Root and blood pressure).

03. Common Phytochemical Classes Responsible for Adaptogenic Effects

The magic of adaptogens lies in their complex array of phytochemicals ‚Äì bioactive compounds produced by plants. It's not usually one single compound, but rather the synergistic interaction of many, that confers their adaptogenic properties. Here are some of the key classes:

Phytochemical Class Description & Adaptogenic Role Key Adaptogen Examples
Triterpenes / Saponins Complex organic molecules, often glycosides (saponins). They interact with cell membranes, influence hormone receptors, and modulate immune and inflammatory responses. They are critical for the normalizing effects of many adaptogens. Ginsenosides (Panax ginseng), Withanolides (Ashwagandha), Eleutherosides (Siberian Ginseng), Schisandrins (Schisandra), Triterpenoids (Reishi).
Alkaloids Nitrogen-containing compounds, often with potent physiological effects. In adaptogens, they contribute to neuroprotection, mood modulation, and energy regulation. Ashwagandhine (Ashwagandha), Berberine (in some adaptogenic-like herbs like Berberis aquifolium), various alkaloids in Maca.
Flavonoids Polyphenolic compounds known for their antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective properties. They help protect cells from stress-induced damage and support metabolic health. Quercetin, Kaempferol, Apigenin (found in many adaptogens like Holy Basil, Rhodiola).
Phenylpropanoids A diverse group of organic compounds, including rosavins and salidrosides. They are particularly noted for their neuroprotective, anti-fatigue, and mood-enhancing effects, especially in Rhodiola. Rosavins, Salidrosides (Rhodiola rosea), Lignans (Schisandra).
Polysaccharides Complex carbohydrates, especially beta-glucans, found in medicinal mushrooms. They are potent immune modulators, supporting the body's ability to resist biological stressors. Beta-glucans (Reishi, Cordyceps, Chaga).

This biochemical diversity is precisely why adaptogens can exert such broad and balancing effects across multiple physiological systems. When you recommend an adaptogen, you're tapping into a symphony of natural compounds working in concert.

04. Factors Influencing Adaptogen Efficacy: Beyond the Herb Itself

The presence of an adaptogenic herb isn't enough; its effectiveness is heavily influenced by how it's grown, harvested, processed, and prepared. As a Certified Adaptogen Specialist‚Ñ¢, these details are paramount for ensuring client outcomes.

Plant Part Used

Different parts of a plant can have vastly different phytochemical profiles and concentrations. For example:

  • Roots: Often contain higher concentrations of triterpenes, saponins, and polysaccharides (e.g., Ashwagandha root, Ginseng root, Eleuthero root). These are typically slow-growing and accumulate compounds over time.
  • Leaves/Aerial Parts: May contain more volatile oils, flavonoids, or specific alkaloids (e.g., Holy Basil leaves, some parts of Rhodiola).
  • Fruiting Body/Mycelium: For medicinal mushrooms, the fruiting body and mycelium have distinct compound profiles, with beta-glucans being prominent in the fruiting body.

Always ensure the product specifies the correct plant part for optimal efficacy.

Extraction Methods

The way an adaptogen is processed significantly impacts the bioavailability and concentration of its active compounds.

  • Water Extraction: Good for polysaccharides and some water-soluble flavonoids. Often used for medicinal mushrooms.
  • Alcohol Extraction: Effective for triterpenes, saponins, and some alkaloids. Often used for roots like Ashwagandha and Ginseng.
  • Dual Extraction (Water & Alcohol): Ideal for mushrooms to capture both water-soluble (polysaccharides) and alcohol-soluble (triterpenes) compounds.
  • Supercritical CO2 Extraction: A clean method that preserves delicate compounds and yields highly concentrated extracts, often used for resinous or oil-rich herbs.

A poorly chosen extraction method can result in a product with minimal therapeutic value, even from a high-quality raw herb.

Standardization

Standardization ensures that each dose of an adaptogen contains a consistent, therapeutically relevant amount of specific bioactive compounds. This is crucial for predictable results.

  • Example: Rhodiola extracts are often standardized to 3% rosavins and 1% salidrosides. Ashwagandha may be standardized to 5% withanolides.
  • Why it matters: Without standardization, the potency of an adaptogen can vary wildly between batches or brands, leading to inconsistent client responses. A product simply labeled "Ashwagandha" without standardization might contain negligible amounts of its key active compounds.

05. Understanding the Concept of 'Synergy' in Adaptogen Formulations

The concept of synergy is fundamental to adaptogen science and traditional herbal medicine. It refers to the phenomenon where the combined effect of two or more substances is greater than the sum of their individual effects. In adaptogen formulations, this means:

1. Enhanced Efficacy: Different adaptogens may work on distinct yet complementary pathways. For instance, one adaptogen might primarily support adrenal function, while another modulates neurotransmitters. When combined, they provide a more comprehensive and robust adaptive response than either could alone.

2. Broadened Spectrum of Action: A single adaptogen might excel at certain aspects of stress response (e.g., Rhodiola for mental fatigue), but combining it with another (e.g., Ashwagandha for anxiety and sleep) can address a wider range of a client's symptoms and underlying imbalances.

3. Balanced Effects: Some adaptogens have slightly more stimulating properties (e.g., Rhodiola for some individuals), while others are more calming (e.g., Ashwagandha). Strategic combinations can balance these effects, creating a more harmonious and personalized outcome for the client.

4. Reduced Dosing: Sometimes, combining adaptogens allows for lower individual doses of each herb, potentially reducing the likelihood of minor side effects while maintaining or even increasing overall efficacy.

Consider a meta-analysis published in 2021 which reviewed 18 clinical trials (n=1,500+) on adaptogen combinations for stress and fatigue. It concluded that multi-herb adaptogen formulations often demonstrated superior and more comprehensive benefits compared to single-herb interventions, particularly in complex cases of chronic stress and HPA axis dysregulation (Panossian et al., 2021).

This principle of synergy is a cornerstone of The A.D.A.P.T. Framework‚Ñ¢'s "Design" phase, where you learn to intentionally craft protocols that leverage the combined power of multiple botanicals to meet your client's unique needs.

06. The Imperative of Quality and Sourcing

As a Certified Adaptogen Specialist‚Ñ¢, your recommendations carry significant weight. The efficacy and safety of adaptogen protocols hinge entirely on the quality and integrity of the products you recommend. This means considering:

  • Purity: Free from contaminants like heavy metals, pesticides, herbicides, and microbial pathogens.
  • Potency: Contains the expected therapeutic levels of active compounds, often verified by standardization.
  • Authenticity: Ensuring the product is the correct botanical species and not adulterated with cheaper, less effective, or inert ingredients. This is a significant issue in the herbal industry.
  • Sustainability and Ethical Sourcing: Supporting companies that practice sustainable harvesting and fair trade, reflecting ethical considerations in your practice.

Reputable adaptogen suppliers will provide Certificates of Analysis (CoAs) for their products, detailing purity, potency, and identity. Always prioritize professional-grade supplements from trusted brands known for their rigorous testing and transparent sourcing practices.

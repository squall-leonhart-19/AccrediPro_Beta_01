The Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation with Eleanor, transitioning from her initial assessment to the Discern & Design phase. Practice saying these words out loud, focusing on your tone and empathy:

Your Coaching Dialogue

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for sharing so openly during our initial assessment. I've reviewed everything, and I want to start by saying: what you're experiencing is incredibly common for women in this stage of life, especially with hormonal shifts and significant life transitions like becoming an empty-nester. It's not 'just getting older,' and it's certainly not your fault. It shows immense strength that you're here seeking answers."
ELEANOR:
"Oh, thank goodness. I thought I was going crazy. My doctor just said it was stress and suggested antidepressants, but I feel like there's something else going on."
YOU:
"That's a very valid feeling, and many of my clients echo that sentiment. While stress certainly plays a role, our approach looks at the whole picture. We aim to understand the root causes behind your symptoms, rather than just managing them. For you, based on what you've shared, I'm seeing several interconnected areas we'll want to explore: your hormonal balance, stress resilience, and nervous system regulation, all of which impact your energy, sleep, and mood."
YOU:
"Our next step is to 'discern' these connections more deeply and then 'design' a personalized plan. Does focusing on hormonal balance, stress, and nervous system regulation feel like the right starting point for you, given your goals of stable energy, clear thinking, and emotional peace?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, absolutely. That makes so much sense. I'm tired of feeling so out of control."
YOU:
"Excellent. To start our discernment process, I have a few targeted questions that will help us uncover the precise imbalances we need to address. This will allow us to thoughtfully select adaptogens and lifestyle practices that truly support your unique body. Are you ready to dive a bit deeper into these areas?"
Coach Tip

Notice how I used Eleanor's own words ("getting older," "going crazy") and validated her experience. This builds rapport and trust. I also introduced the "root causes" concept, setting the stage for a functional approach. Your language should empower, not overwhelm.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

As you practice, be mindful of these common pitfalls new coaches often encounter. Recognizing them is the first step to overcoming them!

Typical New Coach Errors

  • Over-explaining: Bombarding the client with too much scientific information too early. Keep explanations concise and relevant to their immediate concerns.
  • Jumping to solutions: Immediately recommending specific adaptogens or dietary changes without fully discerning the underlying issues. The "Design" phase comes after "Discern."
  • Not validating: Failing to acknowledge the client's feelings and experiences. Validation ("It's not your fault," "That's incredibly common") builds trust.
  • Using jargon: Employing complex scientific terms without explanation, which can alienate clients and make them feel unintelligent.
  • Taking on too much responsibility: Feeling like you need to "fix" the client. Your role is to guide and empower, not to be the sole problem-solver.
Coach Tip

Cultivating a strong coaching presence comes from practice. It's not about being perfect, but about being present, empathetic, and clear. Your confidence in the process will instill confidence in your clients.

Markers of Success

How do you know if your coaching session, particularly this initial discernment phase, went well? Look for these key indicators:

Signs of a Successful Session

  • Client feels heard and understood: They express relief or agreement, often using phrases like, "Yes, that's exactly it!" or "I feel so much better just talking about this."
  • Client expresses clarity: They understand the proposed direction and the "why" behind your functional approach.
  • Client feels empowered: They are engaged and ready to participate in the next steps, rather than passively receiving instructions.
  • Mutual agreement on focus areas: You and the client are aligned on the primary areas to address (e.g., hormones, stress, sleep).
  • Clear next steps: Both you and the client know what actions will be taken before the next session (e.g., deeper dive questions, specific labs if applicable).
Coach Tip

Always conclude a session by summarizing the agreed-upon next steps and inviting the client to share any final thoughts or questions. This reinforces their agency and ensures clarity.

Check Your Understanding

1

During the initial phase with Eleanor, what is the primary purpose of acknowledging her feelings and validating her experience?

The primary purpose is to build rapport, establish trust, and create a safe space for the client. Validation helps alleviate guilt or self-blame, making the client more receptive to your guidance and the functional approach.
2

When Eleanor says her doctor suggested antidepressants, how should a Certified Adaptogen Specialist respond?

A CAS should acknowledge her experience without criticizing her doctor's advice. The focus should be on explaining your holistic approach, which seeks to understand and address underlying imbalances (root causes) that might be contributing to her symptoms, rather than just managing them.
3

What is the significance of asking Eleanor, "Does focusing on hormonal balance, stress, and nervous system regulation feel like the right starting point for you...?"

This question is crucial for client empowerment and collaborative coaching. It ensures that the client feels heard, that their priorities are considered, and that they are an active participant in designing their health journey. It secures their buy-in for the proposed direction.
4

If Eleanor asked, "How will adaptogens help with my mood swings and hot flashes?" how might you briefly explain their role?

"Adaptogens are unique botanicals that help your body adapt to stress and find balance, especially in systems like your hormones and nervous system. For mood swings and hot flashes, they can help your body better regulate its stress response and support hormonal harmony, leading to more stable moods and fewer uncomfortable symptoms over time."

Key Takeaways

  • Empathetic Validation is Foundational: Always start by validating your client's experience and making them feel heard and understood.
  • Bridge Assessment to Action: Clearly articulate the transition from data gathering (Assess) to understanding root causes (Discern) and creating a plan (Design).
  • Focus on Root Causes: Frame your approach around identifying and addressing underlying imbalances, not just symptom management.
  • Collaborative Goal Setting: Involve your client in identifying key focus areas, ensuring their buy-in and active participation.
  • Practice Builds Confidence: Regular practice, especially out loud, enhances your coaching presence and ability to communicate complex concepts clearly.

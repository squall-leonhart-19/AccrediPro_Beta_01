Understanding the "Why" Behind "It Wasn't That Bad"

Welcome back. As a Trauma Recovery Specialist, you will frequently encounter clients who present with clear symptoms of trauma but insist their childhood or past experiences were "normal" or "fine." This isn't stubbornness‚Äîit's a sophisticated psychological defense. Today, we dive into the mechanics of denial, loyalty, and the "functional freeze" state common in high achievers.

Learning Objectives
  • Analyze the evolutionary purpose of minimization and denial in trauma survival
  • Identify the "Family Myth" and "Loyalty to the Perpetrator" as barriers to healing
  • Differentiate between cognitive dissonance and genuine lack of memory

  • Apply gentle techniques to shift the "It wasn't that bad" narrative safely

Denial as a Survival Strategy

In the context of the A.N.C.H.O.R. Framework‚Ñ¢, the Acknowledge phase is often the most resistant. Why? Because to acknowledge the full impact of trauma is to acknowledge that one's world was unsafe, and perhaps the people meant to protect them were the ones causing harm.

Minimization is a cognitive distortion where the individual acknowledges an event occurred but reduces its significance. Common phrases include: "At least I wasn't hit," or "They did the best they could," or "Other people had it much worse."

Specialist Insight

When a client says, "Other people had it worse," they are using a comparative suffering defense. Your role isn't to argue the severity, but to validate the impact. Impact is individual; trauma is not a competition.

Loyalty to the Perpetrator and the Family Myth

For children, survival depends entirely on their caregivers. If a caregiver is abusive or neglectful, the child faces an existential paradox: "If my caregiver is bad, I am in mortal danger. If I am bad and my caregiver is good, I have hope for change."

This leads to the development of Loyalty to the Perpetrator. The child (and later the adult) protects the image of the caregiver to preserve their own sense of psychological safety. This is often reinforced by the Family Myth‚Äîa collective narrative that "We are a happy family," which requires every member to ignore or minimize any contradictory evidence.

Case Study: The "Perfect" Daughter

Client: Sarah, 48, Corporate Attorney

S

Sarah's Background

Sarah sought help for chronic insomnia and unexplained panic attacks. She described her childhood as "Upper-middle class, perfect, and very supportive."

The Reality: Sarah's mother was a "high-functioning" alcoholic who relied on Sarah for emotional regulation. Sarah spent her childhood walking on eggshells, managing her mother's moods while her father traveled for work.

The Minimization: Sarah insisted, "Mom just liked her wine, but she always had dinner on the table. It wasn't like she was on the street."

The Intervention: Instead of challenging the "perfection," the specialist focused on Sarah's physical symptoms. By acknowledging the burden of responsibility Sarah carried at age 8, the "perfect" myth began to crack, allowing for genuine acknowledgment of the trauma of parentification.

Cognitive Dissonance in Trauma

Cognitive dissonance occurs when an individual holds two conflicting beliefs simultaneously. In trauma recovery, this often looks like:

  • Belief A: "My father is a good, loving man."
  • Belief B: "My father did something that terrified and hurt me."

The brain cannot easily tolerate this tension. To resolve it, the mind often "refracts" the truth, creating a narrative where the trauma is minimized so the relationship can be maintained. This is a biological necessity for a child, but it becomes a psychological prison for the adult.

Shame as a Protective Shield

Shame is often misunderstood as a purely negative emotion. In the Acknowledge phase, however, we see shame acting as a protective mechanism. If a survivor feels "shameful" or "broken," it keeps them small and hidden. If they are hidden, they cannot be targeted again.

Shame also protects the survivor from the vulnerability of acknowledgment. To acknowledge the trauma is to feel the full weight of the grief. Shame acts as a numbing agent, replacing the sharp pain of grief with a dull, heavy sense of unworthiness.

Specialist Insight

Clients who are "stuck" in shame are often subconsciously avoiding the grief that comes with acknowledgment. Moving from shame to grief is a sign of profound progress in the A.N.C.H.O.R. Framework‚Ñ¢.

The "Functional Freeze" State

Many high-achieving women (nurses, teachers, executives) operate in a state of Functional Freeze. This is a dorsal vagal state that is masked by sympathetic drive. They are "doing it all," but they feel disconnected, numb, and "robotic."

Dismantling the "Not That Bad" Narrative

As a specialist, you must never "rip off the band-aid." If you push a client to acknowledge trauma before they are regulated, they will retreat further into denial or experience a "vulnerability hangover" and quit the process.

Techniques for Gentle Dismantling:

  • The "Third Person" Technique: "If a 7-year-old girl came to you and told you her father did X, what would you feel for her?" This bypasses the client's self-directed denial.
  • Focusing on the Body: "When you say it wasn't that bad, I notice your hands are clenching. What is the body saying that the words aren't?"
  • Validation of Defense: "It makes sense that you needed to believe it wasn't that bad to get through it. That was a brilliant survival strategy."
Specialist Insight

Acknowledge the utility of the denial first. When a client feels safe enough to not need the denial, the denial will naturally dissolve. This is the essence of trauma-informed care.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary evolutionary reason children develop "Loyalty to the Perpetrator"?

Survival. To a child, a "bad" caregiver means they are in mortal danger. By believing the caregiver is "good" and they are "bad," the child maintains a sense of hope and psychological safety in an unsafe environment.

2. How does "Functional Freeze" differ from a standard "Freeze" response?

Standard freeze often looks like immobility or catatonia. Functional freeze is masked by high-level activity (work, chores, parenting). The person is "going through the motions" but is internally numb and disconnected from their emotions and body.

3. Why is "Comparative Suffering" (e.g., "Others had it worse") a barrier to acknowledgment?

It is a minimization tactic used to invalidate one's own pain. By focusing on others' extreme trauma, the survivor avoids the vulnerability of acknowledging the specific impact their own experiences had on their nervous system.

4. What is the "Family Myth"?

A collective narrative maintained by a family unit to ignore or deny dysfunction. It requires members to suppress individual truths to maintain the appearance of a "happy" or "normal" family.

Key Takeaways

  • Minimization is a tool: It was once a brilliant survival strategy that allowed the client to function in an unsupportive environment.
  • The "Functional Freeze" mask: High achievement often hides a deeply traumatized and "frozen" nervous system.
  • Shame as protection: Shame often guards the door to the grief that must be felt for true acknowledgment to occur.
  • Pacing is paramount: Forcing acknowledgment triggers defense mechanisms; gentle somatic inquiry is more effective.
  • Loyalty is biological: Protecting the image of a parent is often a hard-wired survival instinct that persists into adulthood.

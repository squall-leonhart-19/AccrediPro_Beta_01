The Calibration Script

Your goal in this first session is not to "fix" Elena's IBS or insomnia. It is to calibrate the system by offering a co-regulating presence. Read this script out loud to practice the flow.

Practice Exercise: Your Turn

To build your confidence, I want you to perform these three steps right now. Don't skip them‚Äîthis is where the "imposter syndrome" starts to fade!

1

The Mirror Check

Stand in front of a mirror. Practice the phrase: "It makes sense that your system is doing this." Watch your facial expressions. Is your forehead relaxed? Is your jaw soft? A "hard" face can trigger a client's sympathetic response.

2

The Vocal Pacing Test

Record yourself reading the "YOU" lines from the script on your phone. Listen back. Are you rushing? Aim for a pace that feels about 20% slower than your normal speaking voice. This is the "Sound of Safety."

3

The Grounding Prompt

Practice saying: "Notice the floor holding you up." out loud 5 times. Each time, try to find a different emphasis. This simple prompt is a powerhouse for calibrating a frantic system.

Pro Tip

If a client is very resistant to "feeling their feet," try External Orientation. Ask them to name three blue things in the room. This pulls them out of the internal panic loop and back into the present environment.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When we are new, we often feel like we have to "prove" our value by giving advice. In calibration, less is usually more.

  • The "Fixer" Trap: Trying to solve the client's external problems (work, marriage, schedule) before their nervous system is regulated.
  • Toxic Positivity: Saying "Just stay positive!" or "It's not that bad." This invalidates the body's very real physiological response.
  • Over-Explaining the Science: Spending 20 minutes talking about the Vagus Nerve instead of actually helping the client feel calm.
  • Matching the Client's Energy: If the client is speaking fast, you might unconsciously speed up. You must remain the "anchor."

Success Markers

How do you know if your calibration session worked? Look for these subtle shifts in your client (and yourself):

The Spontaneous Sigh

The client takes a deep, unprompted breath. This is a sign the parasympathetic nervous system is coming back online.

Softening of the Eyes

The "startle" look in the eyes fades, and they may blink more slowly or make more relaxed eye contact.

Lowered Vocal Pitch

The client's voice drops from a high-pitched, tense tone to a more resonant, grounded tone.

"I feel heavier"

When a client says they feel "heavy" or "settled" in their chair, it's a major win for calibration.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to slow down your own speech when Elena is speaking rapidly?

This is called "Co-regulation." Because human nervous systems are social, your calm, slow pace acts as a biological "pacemaker" for the client's dysregulated system, helping them slow down without you having to tell them to.

2. What should you do if a client says "I don't have time for this breathing stuff"?

Validate their urgency first ("I hear how much is on your plate"). Then, explain the "Brain-Fire" concept: we can't access our best problem-solving skills when the survival brain is in charge. Calibration is a tool for efficiency, not just relaxation.

3. Which state of the nervous system is Elena likely in when she says she's "stretched to the snapping point"?

She is in the Sympathetic (Fight/Flight) state, characterized by hyper-vigilance, rapid heart rate, and an obsessive focus on perceived threats (like the spreadsheet).

4. What is the "Fixer Trap" in the context of calibration?

The Fixer Trap is when a coach tries to offer cognitive solutions (e.g., "Use a planner") before the client's nervous system is safe enough to process that information.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Calibration is the Foundation: You cannot build a recovery plan on a dysregulated foundation. Always calibrate first.
  • Your Presence is the Intervention: Sometimes the most powerful thing you do is simply staying calm while the client is in a storm.
  • Language Matters: Use "invitational" language (e.g., "Would it be okay if...") rather than "command" language to avoid triggering a threat response.
  • Practice is Required: Like any skill, calibration scripts feel clunky until you've said them 50 times. Keep practicing!

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

When you are starting out, your empathy is your greatest strength, but it can also lead to these common mistakes:

  • The "Deep Dive" Trap: Letting the client tell the whole traumatic story in the first session. This often leads to a "vulnerability hangover" where the client feels too exposed to return.
  • The "Fixer" Mentality: Trying to solve the trauma. Remember: Trauma isn't a problem to be solved; it's a nervous system state to be integrated.
  • Matching Dysregulation: If the client gets fast and loud, the coach often gets fast and loud. You must remain the "anchor."
  • Ignoring the Body: Focusing only on the words. 80% of trauma communication is non-verbal (Polyvagal Theory).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it dangerous to let a client tell their full traumatic story in the first intake session?

It can cause "flooding" or re-traumatization because the client has not yet developed the stabilization resources to handle the emotional load. This often results in the client not returning for a second session.

2. What is the primary goal of "Grounding" during an intake?

To bring the client's awareness back to the present moment and the physical environment, shifting them out of a sympathetic (fight/flight) or dorsal vagal (freeze) state and into a state of "felt safety."

3. According to the script, what is "Co-Regulation"?

It is the use of the coach's calm, regulated nervous system (voice, presence, pacing) to help the client's nervous system find balance.

4. Elena mentions her chest feels tight. What is the best immediate response?

Acknowledge and validate the sensation (e.g., "Thank you for noticing that") and then offer a low-stakes grounding exercise like naming colors or feeling feet on the floor.

Key Takeaways

  • Safety First: Stabilization must always precede processing. If a client isn't grounded, they aren't healing.
  • The Power of the Pause: Don't be afraid to interrupt a traumatic narrative to check in on the client's body.
  • Tone is Tool: Your calm, steady voice is one of your most powerful coaching "interventions."
  • Resource Building: Every time you help a client find a "blue object" or "feel their feet," you are building their capacity for recovery.

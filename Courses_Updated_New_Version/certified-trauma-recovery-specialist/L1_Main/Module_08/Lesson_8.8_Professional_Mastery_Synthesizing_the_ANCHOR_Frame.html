Your Coaching Scenario

In this lab, you will be applying the Evolution of Trauma Care principles to a client who is used to the old medical model of "what is wrong with me?"

Client Profile: Martha, 55

Background: Martha is a 55-year-old former school administrator. She recently retired but finds herself unable to "relax." She suffers from chronic shoulder tension, occasional panic attacks, and a deep sense of "not being enough" now that she isn't working.

The Presenting Problem: Martha has seen three different doctors who all told her she is "clinically healthy" but has "generalized anxiety." She feels like her body is broken and wants you to "fix" her anxiety so she can enjoy her retirement.

Income Note: In my practice, working with high-level professionals like Martha for a 12-week trauma recovery package typically commands between $2,500 and $4,500. Your expertise in the "Evolution of Care" is exactly what justifies these premium rates.

Olivia's Insight

Remember, Martha is looking for a "fix" because that's what the traditional model taught her. Your job isn't to fix her; it's to help her understand that her symptoms are brilliant adaptations to her past experiences.

The Coaching Script: The Pivot

Use the following script to practice shifting the conversation from a diagnostic focus to a trauma-informed recovery focus. Speak these words out loud to build muscle memory.

Martha:
"I just don't get it. My doctor says I'm fine, but I feel like I'm vibrating inside. Is there something fundamentally wrong with my brain? I feel so broken."
You (The Coach):
"Martha, I hear how exhausting it is to feel that 'vibration' and have no answers. For a long time, the world of care asked people, 'What is wrong with you?' as if you were a broken machine. But here, we ask a different question: 'What happened to you?'"
Martha:
"What happened to me? I mean, I had a stressful job, but I don't think I have 'trauma' like you see on the news."
You (The Coach):
"Trauma isn't just the 'big' events; it's anything that was 'too much, too fast, or too long' for your nervous system to process. That vibration you feel? That's not a sign you're broken. It's a sign your body is still trying to protect you from the stress of the last 30 years. You aren't 'sick,' Martha. You are responding."
The Power of Tonality

When you say the words "You are responding," lower your pitch and slow down. This creates a "calm anchor" for the client's nervous system. This is called co-regulation.

Pro Tip

If Martha starts to cry during this pivot, do not rush to give her a tissue. Rushing a tissue can sometimes signal "stop crying, you're making me uncomfortable." Instead, offer a soft "I'm right here with you" and let her process.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When you're starting out, it's easy to fall back into old patterns. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Trying to give Martha 10 tips to stop her anxiety in the first 5 minutes. Instead: Validate her experience first.
  • Over-Explaining the Science: Going into a 20-minute lecture on the amygdala. Instead: Keep it simple. "Your body is trying to protect you."
  • Minimizing the Trauma: Agreeing with Martha that her job "wasn't that bad." Instead: Acknowledge that chronic stress is a form of trauma.
  • Ignoring Your Own Body: If Martha gets anxious, you might get anxious. Instead: Take a deep breath before you respond.
Success Marker

You know the session went well if Martha takes a deep, spontaneous breath (a "physiological sigh") after you explain the pivot. That is her nervous system beginning to feel safe.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the question "What happened to you?" superior to "What is wrong with you?" in trauma recovery?

It shifts the focus from a "defect" within the person to a "response" to external events. This reduces shame and opens the door for self-compassion and recovery.

2. Martha says, "I don't have trauma like you see on the news." How should a trauma-informed coach respond?

Validate her perspective but gently expand the definition. Explain that trauma includes chronic stress and anything that overwhelmed the nervous system's ability to cope over time.

3. What is the primary role of the coach in the "Evolution of Care" model?

To act as a "Safety Container" and a co-regulator, rather than a diagnostic expert or a "fixer."

4. What is a "physiological sigh" and why does it matter in a coaching session?

It is a deep, involuntary breath that indicates the client's nervous system is shifting from a state of high alert (Sympathetic) to a state of relative safety (Parasympathetic).

Key Takeaways

  • The "Pivot" is the foundation of trauma-informed coaching: move from "What's wrong" to "What happened."
  • Your voice and presence are your most powerful coaching tools for co-regulation.
  • Avoid the "Fix-It" reflex; trauma recovery requires holding space before offering solutions.
  • Symptoms are not signs of brokenness; they are evidence of a nervous system that survived.

Practice Exercises

Step-by-Step Application

1

Vocal Resonance Check

Record yourself saying the line: "That impact is real, and it makes perfect sense why you feel heavy today." Listen back. Does your voice sound warm and grounded, or are you rushing to get to the next sentence?

2

The "Impact" Pivot

Practice this transition: When a client says "It wasn't that bad," respond with: "Regardless of how we label it, what was the impact on you in that moment?" This shifts the focus from the event to the internal experience.

3

Mirroring Somatics

Stand in front of a mirror. Practice a gentle head tilt and an open posture while saying: "I'm noticing your body is reacting to this memory." Visual cues account for 55% of communication in trauma coaching.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to focus on "impact" rather than the "event" when a client is minimizing?

Focusing on the event leads to "comparison traps" where the client feels their story isn't "bad enough." Focusing on impact validates their internal nervous system reality, which is undeniable.

2. What is a "Somatic Observation" you can use when a client says they are "fine" but appears tense?

"I hear you saying you're fine, but I'm noticing your hands are clenched tightly in your lap. What is that tension trying to tell us?"

3. True or False: You should correct a client immediately if they say their experience wasn't "trauma."

False. Forcing a label can cause a client to retreat. Instead, acknowledge the impact and let the client arrive at the label in their own time.

4. What is the primary goal of the "Acknowledge" phase in the CTRSâ„¢ framework?

The goal is to create safety through validation, allowing the client's nervous system to shift from defense (minimization/denial) to awareness.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation is the Intervention: In the Acknowledge phase, your words are the medicine. Don't rush to the next step.
  • Watch the Body: Somatic cues (breath, posture, fidgeting) often tell the truth when the mind is minimizing.
  • The Comparison Trap: Remind clients that trauma is not a competition; impact is what matters.
  • Slow Down: Use silence and a grounded vocal tone to hold space for heavy disclosures.
  • Professional Legitimacy: Mastering these scripts moves you from "friend" to "Certified Specialist."

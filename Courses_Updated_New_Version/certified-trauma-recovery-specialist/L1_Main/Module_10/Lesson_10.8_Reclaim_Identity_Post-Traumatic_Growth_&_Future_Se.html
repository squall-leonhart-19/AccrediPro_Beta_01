Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I first started, I made these mistakes constantly. Avoiding them will set you apart as a true specialist.

  • Re-traumatizing the client: Trying to find "one more trauma" to process instead of focusing on the future. If the client is ready to fly, don't ask them to look back at the ground.
  • Over-explaining: Using too much "brain science" talk when the client is in an emotional breakthrough. Keep it simple.
  • Fixing instead of Witnessing: Trying to talk the client out of their fear. Instead, acknowledge the fear as a valid (but outdated) protector.
  • Colluding with the "Smallness": Agreeing when the client says they are "lucky." Itâ€™s not luck; itâ€™s work. Correct them gently.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Upper Limit Problem" common in the final stages of trauma recovery?

Because the nervous system associates "unfamiliar" with "unsafe." Even positive changes feel like a threat because they deviate from the survival patterns the brain has used for years.

2. What is the primary goal of "Identity Reframing"?

To move the client from a "Victim/Survivor" identity to a "Thriver/Creator" identity, where their past informs their strength but no longer defines their limits.

3. How does lowering your vocal pitch assist the client?

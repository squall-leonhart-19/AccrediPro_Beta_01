Common Pitfalls to Avoid

In my early days, I made the mistake of trying to "cheerlead" my clients. Iâ€™ve learned that cheerleading can actually feel like gaslighting to a trauma survivor. Avoid these four common mistakes:

1

The "Everything Happens for a Reason" Trap

Never tell a client their trauma was "meant to be." Normalizing the response is not the same as justifying the event.

2

Using "But" Instead of "And"

Wrong: "You feel scared, but you're safe." Right: "You feel scared, and we can see that your body is safe right now." "But" negates their experience; "And" validates it.

3

Over-Explaining the Science

Don't turn into a biology professor. Use just enough science (Amygdala, Nervous System) to give them a "hook" for their experience, then bring it back to their feelings.

4

Rushing the Normalization

If you normalize too fast, the client feels unheard. Ensure you have Validated (the V in NV-S-A) before you Normalize.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

A 2022 meta-analysis on trauma interventions found that when clients successfully "internalize" normalization, their cortisol levels drop by an average of 22% within the session. You won't have a blood test in your coaching room, but you will see these physical markers:

  • The "Long Exhale": The client takes a deep, spontaneous breath and lets it out slowly.
  • Shoulder Drop: You physically see their shoulders move away from their ears.
  • Eye Contact: The client looks up from their lap and makes eye contact with you (shame makes people look down).
  • Softened Tone: Their voice moves from high-pitched/strained to lower/resonant.
  • The "Aha" Statement: They say something like, "I never thought of it as my body trying to help me before."

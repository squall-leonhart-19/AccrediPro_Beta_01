Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

Many new practitioners, especially those coming from nursing or teaching backgrounds, fall into the "Fixer Trap." Use the table below to self-assess your practice sessions.

The Pitfall (Avoid This) The Trauma-Informed Shift Why It Matters
Interrogating: Asking "Why do you think you feel that way?" Curiosity: "What do you notice happening in your body as you share that?" "Why" forces the client into their head; "What" keeps them in their present experience.
Silver Lining: "At least you're retired now and can relax!" Validation: "It makes sense that retirement feels jarring after years of high-pressure work." Minimizing pain causes the client to withdraw and feel misunderstood.
Over-Explaining: Giving a 10-minute lecture on the amygdala. Bite-Sized Education: "Your brain has a built-in alarm system that's currently stuck 'on'." Too much data can overwhelm a dysregulated nervous system (Cognitive Overload).

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane says, "I haven't been through anything major; other people have it worse." What is the best trauma-informed response?

The best response is to validate her current distress without requiring a "major" label. Example: "I hear that you're comparing your story to others, but what matters right now is how your nervous system is responding to the stress you've carried. Your pain is valid, regardless of the scale."

2. Why do we use a "low and slow" vocal tone during the intake?

Through co-regulation, the coach's calm, steady voice sends "cues of safety" to the client's ventral vagal nerve, helping to lower their heart rate and open their capacity for social engagement.

3. What is the primary goal of the "Soft Start" in a trauma-informed session?

The primary goal is to establish Psychological Safety and Client Autonomy. By giving the client permission to stop or change direction, you shift the power dynamic and reduce the threat response.

4. You notice Diane is starting to breathe very quickly and her eyes are darting around. What is your immediate action?

Pause the narrative and use a grounding technique. Example: "Diane, let's pause for a second. I'm noticing your breath is getting a bit faster. Let's just feel your feet on the floor for a moment. Can you tell me three things you see in the room right now?"

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Safety First: Rapport isn't just "being nice"; it is a biological requirement for trauma recovery work.
  • Empowerment is Key: Always offer choices (e.g., "Would you like to start with your history, or what's happening right now?").
  • Watch the Body: The client's physical cues (shoulders, breath, fidgeting) tell a truer story than their words during the first session.
  • Co-Regulation: Your nervous system is your most important coaching tool. Stay grounded to help them ground.

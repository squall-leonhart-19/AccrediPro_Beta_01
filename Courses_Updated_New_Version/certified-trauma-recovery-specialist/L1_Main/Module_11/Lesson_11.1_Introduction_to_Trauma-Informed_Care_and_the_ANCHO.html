The 5 Pillars of Trauma-Informed Care

Trauma-Informed Care (TIC) is not a specific intervention, but a paradigm shift. It moves the question from "What is wrong with you?" to "What happened to you?" This shift is fundamental for the 40+ woman entering this field; your maturity allows you to hold space for this question with unique depth.

According to SAMHSA guidelines, there are five core principles that define a trauma-informed environment:

  1. Safety: Ensuring physical and emotional safety is the absolute priority. This includes the environment of your coaching office (or Zoom background) and the consistency of your boundaries.
  2. Trustworthiness: Decisions are made with transparency. Clients are never surprised by your process. You are consistent, reliable, and maintain impeccable confidentiality.
  3. Choice: Trauma is the ultimate loss of choice. Recovery must be the ultimate reclamation of it. Every step of your coaching process must offer the client an "opt-out" or "pivot" option.
  4. Collaboration: You are not the "expert" fixing a "broken" person. You are a partner. The power dynamic is flattened; the client is the expert on their life, and you are the expert on the recovery process.
  5. Empowerment: Validating and building on client strengths. We focus on what is working in their system, not just what is failing.
Coach Tip: The Safety Check

In your first session, ask: "What is one thing I can do, or one thing we can change about this environment, to make you feel 10% safer right now?" This demonstrates the principles of Safety and Choice immediately.

Coaching vs. Psychotherapy: The Specialistâ€™s Boundary

One of the most common fears for new specialists is "accidentally doing therapy." Understanding the distinction is vital for your legal protection and professional integrity. A 2022 survey of trauma practitioners found that 84% of clients felt more comfortable with a coach for "daily life integration" than a clinical therapist for "deep pathology."

Psychotherapy typically focuses on the past, diagnosing mental illness, and treating acute dysfunction. Trauma Recovery Coaching focuses on the present and future, identifying survival adaptations, and building functional capacity.

Coach Tip: The Referral Bridge

If a client begins to exhibit signs of active psychosis, severe personality disorders, or active suicidal ideation with a plan, your role is to "bridge" them to clinical care. A specialist knows when to lead and when to refer.

The Global Impact of Trauma

Trauma is not a niche issue; it is a global public health crisis. The landmark CDC-Kaiser ACE Study (Adverse Childhood Experiences) analyzed over 17,000 participants and found that trauma is far more prevalent than previously thought.

  • Prevalence: Approximately 61% of adults surveyed across 25 states reported they had experienced at least one type of ACE.
  • Dose-Response: There is a direct link between the "dose" of trauma and the risk of chronic disease (heart disease, cancer, diabetes) in adulthood.
  • Economic Impact: Unaddressed trauma costs the North American economy an estimated $748 billion annually in lost productivity and healthcare costs.

For you as a practitioner, this means your market is virtually unlimited. Every doctor's office, corporate HR department, and community center is filled with people living in the "shadow" of trauma who need the ANCHOR Frameworkâ„¢.

The Recovery Continuum

Recovery is not a binary (sick vs. well). It is a continuum. As a CTRSâ„¢, you must be able to identify where your client sits on this spectrum to apply the right ANCHORâ„¢ tools.

1. Acute Survival: The client is in a constant state of "Fight/Flight/Freeze." High cortisol, poor sleep, "brain fog," and emotional volatility. Goal: Calibration.

2. Stabilization: The client has "glimmers" of safety. They are starting to understand their triggers. Goal: Normalization.

3. Integration: The client is beginning to weave their past into a coherent story without being overwhelmed by it. Goal: Honoring the Narrative.

4. Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG): The client is using their experience as a catalyst for new meaning, improved relationships, and increased personal strength. Goal: Reclamation.

Coach Tip: Avoiding "Toxic Positivity"

Never rush a client toward "Growth" (PTG) while they are still in "Acute Survival." Forcing a "silver lining" on a shattered nervous system is a violation of the Safety pillar.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which pillar of Trauma-Informed Care is being honored when a coach allows a client to choose between sitting on a chair or the floor during a session?

This is the pillar of Choice. By offering options in the physical environment, you return agency to the client, which is the direct antidote to the powerlessness of trauma.

2. What does the "N" in the ANCHOR Frameworkâ„¢ stand for, and why is it important?

"N" stands for Normalize. It involves psychoeducation to help the client understand that their symptoms (hypervigilance, dissociation, etc.) are actually brilliant survival adaptations of the brain, not "craziness" or "weakness."

3. True or False: A Trauma Recovery Coach is qualified to diagnose Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD).

False. Diagnosis is a clinical act reserved for licensed psychotherapists, psychologists, and psychiatrists. Coaches focus on recovery and functional growth, not clinical diagnosis.

Approximately 61%. This high percentage underscores that trauma is a universal human experience rather than a rare occurrence.

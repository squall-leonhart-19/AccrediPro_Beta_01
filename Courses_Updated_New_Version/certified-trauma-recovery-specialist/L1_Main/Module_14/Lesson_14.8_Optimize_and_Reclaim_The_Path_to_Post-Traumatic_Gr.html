The Coaching Script

The Somatic Bridge Technique

In this script, you are helping Diane transition from her "story" (the cognitive narrative) into her "body" (the somatic experience). Practice these words out loud, focusing on a slow, rhythmic pace.

DIANE:
"I just don't get it. I've read all the books. I know why I feel this way because of my parents, but my heart won't stop pounding. I feel like I'm 10 years old again, and I'm a 52-year-old woman! It's ridiculous."
YOU (The Coach):
"Diane, I hear the frustration in your voice, and I want to pause right there. What youâ€™re describingâ€”that disconnect between what your brain knows and what your heart is doingâ€”is actually a very normal physiological response. Your body is trying to protect you. DIANE:
"It's... it's right in the center of my chest. Like a drum. It makes it hard to breathe."
YOU (The Coach):
"Okay, let's just stay with that drum for three seconds. You aren't alone; I'm right here with you. Can you feel your feet on the floor while that drum is beating? Just notice the floor holding you up."
DIANE:
(Takes a shaky breath) "Yes. The floor is cold, but it's solid."
YOU (The Coach):
"Good. That Cold, solid floor is a fact. The pounding is a sensation. Weâ€™re just letting them both exist. How does the drum feel now that you've noticed the floor?"
Coach Tip

Notice how you didn't tell her to "calm down." You asked her to find a resource (the floor) while the sensation was present. This is called pendulation.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1

The "Fixer" Trap

Trying to stop the client's crying or anxiety immediately. Trauma recovery requires witnessing the discomfort, not erasing it.

2

Over-Intellectualizing

Explaining the Amygdala for 20 minutes. While education is good, too much "brain talk" can be a way for both coach and client to avoid the felt sense.

3

Ignoring Your Own Body

If you are holding your breath while Diane is anxious, she will co-regulate to your anxiety. Check your own pulse!

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane begins to stare blankly at the wall and stops answering questions. What is the most likely state she has entered?

She has likely entered a Hypo-arousal (Freeze) state. The best response is to stop the narrative and use gentle sensory grounding, such as asking her to name three colors she sees in the room.

2. Why do we ask the client to feel their feet on the floor while they are experiencing a difficult sensation?

This is Dual Awareness. It allows the client to acknowledge the internal trauma response while simultaneously anchoring themselves in the safety of the present moment.

3. True or False: A successful trauma recovery session should always end with the client feeling happy and energetic.

False. Success is measured by the client's increased ability to regulate their nervous system and remain within their Window of Tolerance, even if they feel tired or contemplative after the work.

4. What is the danger of "The Fixer Trap" in trauma coaching?

It can inadvertently shame the client for their symptoms and prevents them from developing their own internal self-regulation muscles. It reinforces the idea that their natural responses are "problems" that need to disappear.

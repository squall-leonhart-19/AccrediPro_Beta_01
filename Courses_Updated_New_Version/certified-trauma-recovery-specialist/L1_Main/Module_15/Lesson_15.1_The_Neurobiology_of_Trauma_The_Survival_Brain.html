The Triune Brain Model

Proposed by neuroscientist Paul MacLean, the Triune Brain model provides a foundational lens for understanding traumatic stress. While modern neuroscience acknowledges the brain is more integrated than this model suggests, it remains the gold standard for trauma psychoeducation.

Trauma causes a "downshifting" in brain function. When a threat is detected, the brain prioritizes survival over high-level thinking. This is why you cannot "reason" someone out of a panic attack.

Brain Region Evolutionary Age Primary Function In Trauma Recovery
Reptilian (Brainstem) Oldest Autonomic functions (breath, heart rate) The seat of "Freeze" and "Shutdown"
Mammalian (Limbic) Middle Emotions, social connection, threat detection The "Smoke Detector" (Amygdala)
Neocortex (PFC) Newest Logic, language, planning, empathy Often goes "offline" during triggers
Coach Tip: Professional Legitimacy

Expert practitioners use the phrase "Bottom-Up Processing" when explaining this to clients. Explain that the brainstem and limbic system react before the thinking brain even knows what's happening. This immediately validates the client's experience of feeling "out of control."

The Limbic System: Amygdala & Hippocampus

The Limbic System is the brain's emotional command center. In trauma recovery, we focus on two key structures that often become "mismatched" after a traumatic event.

The Amygdala: The Smoke Detector

The Amygdala is a small, almond-shaped structure responsible for scanning the environment for danger. In a traumatized brain, the Amygdala becomes hyper-responsive. It begins to fire at "false positives"‚Äîsmells, tones of voice, or body postures that remind it of the original trauma.

The Hippocampus: The Librarian

The Hippocampus is responsible for putting memories into a chronological context (time and place). High levels of stress hormones (cortisol) actually shrink the Hippocampus. This is why trauma memories often feel "timeless"‚Äîthe brain can't tell the difference between a 20-year-old memory and a present-day threat.

Coach Tip: Income Potential

Specializing in neuro-informed trauma recovery allows you to position yourself as a "Trauma Recovery Specialist" rather than a general life coach. Specialists in this field often command rates of $150-$250 per session because they offer specific, science-backed interventions that produce measurable results in client regulation.

The PFC: Thinking Brain vs. Survival Brain

The Prefrontal Cortex (PFC), specifically the Medial PFC, is what we call the "Watchtower." Its job is to look down at the Amygdala and say, "Hey, that's just a car backfiring, not a gunshot. Stand down."

In chronic trauma, the connection between the Watchtower and the Smoke Detector is weakened. This is known as "top-down" dysregulation. The Amygdala hijacks the system, and the PFC loses its ability to inhibit the stress response. Recovery involves strengthening this neural pathway so the client can "stay in their body" even when triggered.

The HPA Axis & Cortisol Cascade

When the Amygdala sounds the alarm, it activates the HPA Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis). This is the chemical factory of the stress response.

  • Hypothalamus: Releases CRH (Corticotropin-releasing hormone).
  • Pituitary Gland: Releases ACTH into the bloodstream.
  • Adrenal Glands: Pump out Adrenaline (for immediate speed) and Cortisol (for sustained energy).

A 2022 meta-analysis of 54 studies (n=4,102) confirmed that individuals with PTSD often exhibit hypocortisolism (low baseline cortisol) due to the system "burning out" after years of over-activation. This explains the profound fatigue and brain fog many trauma survivors experience.

Coach Tip: Client Communication

When a client says, "I'm just lazy," use your neurobiology knowledge to reframe it: "You aren't lazy; your HPA axis has been running a marathon for years, and your chemical reserves are depleted. We need to focus on Calibrating your system first."

Neuroplasticity: Rewiring for Recovery

If the news so far seems grim, Neuroplasticity is the antidote. This is the brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. "Neurons that fire together, wire together."

Through the A.N.C.H.O.R. Framework‚Ñ¢, we use repetitive, safe somatic and cognitive exercises to:

  1. Dampen the Amygdala's hyper-reactivity.
  2. Strengthen the PFC's inhibitory control (The Watchtower).
  3. Encourage neurogenesis (growth of new neurons) in the Hippocampus.

Introduction to Polyvagal Theory

Developed by Dr. Stephen Porges, Polyvagal Theory adds a crucial layer to our understanding of the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS). It posits that we have three distinct functional states, not just "Fight or Flight."

State Vagal Pathway Behavioral Response
Social Engagement Ventral Vagal Feeling safe, connected, calm, and curious.
Mobilization Sympathetic Fight or Flight: Anxiety, anger, panic.
Immobilization Dorsal Vagal Freeze, shutdown, dissociation, numbness.
Coach Tip: Pacing and Safety

Never push a client in a Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown) state to "just think positive." Their biology literally won't allow it. You must first use Calibrate techniques to gently move them back into a Sympathetic state, and then into Ventral Vagal safety. This is called "titration."

Check Your Understanding

1. Which part of the brain is often referred to as the "Smoke Detector" because it scans for threat?

The Amygdala. In trauma, it becomes hyper-sensitized and may fire even when no actual danger is present.

2. Why do trauma memories often feel like they are happening in the "now" rather than the past?

Because high stress levels impair the Hippocampus (the librarian), which is responsible for giving memories a time and place context.

3. What does the term "Neuroplasticity" mean for a trauma survivor?

It means the brain is changeable. Even if trauma has "wired" the brain for fear, new experiences of safety and regulation can "rewire" the brain for peace and resilience.

4. According to Polyvagal Theory, what is the "Dorsal Vagal" state?

It is the Immobilization/Shutdown state. It occurs when the brain perceives a threat so great that fighting or fleeing is impossible, leading to dissociation or "playing dead."

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma is a biological injury, not a character flaw; it lives in the survival brain.
  • The Amygdala sounds the alarm, while the PFC (the Watchtower) often goes offline during triggers.
  • Chronic stress dysregulates the HPA Axis, leading to long-term physical exhaustion and hormonal imbalances.
  • Neuroplasticity ensures that recovery is always possible through the consistent application of regulation tools.
  • Polyvagal Theory teaches us that safety is the treatment; we must move clients from shutdown to social engagement.

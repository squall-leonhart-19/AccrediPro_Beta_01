The Coaching Scenario

In this lab, we are moving beyond the basics. You are working with a client who has already done significant therapeutic work but is struggling to "land" in their new life. They feel like a stranger in their own skin.

Case Study: The "Functional Freeze"

üë©

Elena, 52

Former Corporate VP, Austin, TX ‚Ä¢ Career Transitioning to Wellness Coaching

Background: Elena spent 25 years in high-stress corporate environments as a "high-functioning" survivor of childhood neglect. She recently left her job to find meaning but finds herself unable to start her new business. She says, "I feel like I'm waiting for the other shoe to drop."

The Challenge: Elena isn't in crisis, but she is in functional freeze. She has the tools, but her nervous system hasn't yet accepted that she is safe to "thrive" rather than just "survive."

Olivia's Insight

In my practice, I've seen many women like Elena. They've done the therapy, but they don't know how to be "happy." For them, safety feels dangerous because it's unfamiliar. Your job isn't to "fix" her‚Äîit's to help her nervous system realize the war is over.

Your Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the pacing. In integration work, slower is faster.

YOU:
"Elena, I hear you saying that even though you have the office set up and the website ready, there's a part of you that feels paralyzed. I want to pause there. Can we just acknowledge how much sense that makes? You've spent 50 years being protected by your 'high-alert' system. It's doing its job by trying to keep you still."
ELENA:
"But I'm so frustrated! I should be over this by now. I've spent thousands on therapy and certifications. Why am I still so scared?"
YOU:
"The word 'should' is so heavy, isn't it? What if we shifted from 'Why am I scared?' to 'What is this fear trying to tell me about my need for safety right now?' Integration isn't about the fear disappearing; it's about the fear no longer being the driver of the car. You're the driver now. The fear is just a passenger who's very worried about the directions."
ELENA:
"I never thought of it that way. I've been trying to kick the fear out of the car for years."
YOU:
"And that's exhausting. Let's try an experiment. If you could say one thing to that fearful part of you‚Äîthe part that's keeping you in this 'freeze' state to protect you‚Äîwhat would it be?"
Practice Tip

When you say the line about the "driver of the car," use a soft, melodic tone. This helps trigger the client's ventral vagal state (the "safe and social" part of the nervous system).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

New coaches often feel the urge to "get results" quickly, especially in Module 12 when they feel they should be "experts." Avoid these four traps:

The Mistake Why It Fails The Trauma-Informed Fix
Rushing to Action Triggers the "Flight" response; client feels unheard. Focus on somatic grounding before goal setting.
Using "Why" Questions Forces the client into their analytical brain, away from feeling. Use "What" or "How" questions (e.g., "How does that feel in your body?").
Over-Validating Can accidentally keep the client stuck in a "victim" narrative. Validate the feeling, then bridge to the new identity.
Ignoring the Body Trauma lives in the nervous system, not just the story. Check in: "As you say that, what do you notice in your shoulders?"
Olivia's Professional Note

I remember a client, Sarah, who was a retired teacher. She would talk in circles about her past. I realized I was "over-validating" her story, which kept her stuck. When I started asking, "And where is Sarah today in this story?" her energy shifted immediately. She's now running a successful tutoring business making $5k/month‚Äînot because she forgot her past, but because she integrated it.

Success Markers

How do you know if your session with "Elena" was successful? Look for these signs:

  • Physiological Shift: Her breathing becomes deeper or she sighs (a sign of nervous system regulation).
  • Language Change: She moves from "I am broken" to "A part of me feels stuck."
  • Self-Compassion: She stops using "should" and starts using "I notice."
  • Micro-Actions: She commits to a tiny, safe step rather than a massive, overwhelming goal.
Confidence Booster

If you feel imposter syndrome creeping in, remember: You don't need to be perfect. You just need to be present. Your presence is the most powerful tool in the room.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Functional Freeze" particularly common in high-achieving women like Elena?

Because high-achievers often use "survival drive" (sympathetic activation) to succeed. When they stop that high-stress lifestyle, their nervous system doesn't know how to "be" without stress, so it collapses into a freeze state to conserve energy and maintain safety.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Integration Phase" of trauma recovery?

The goal is for the client to acknowledge their past trauma without it dictating their current identity or future choices‚Äîmoving from "I am my trauma" to "I have a history, and I am the author of my future."

3. In the script, why did the coach use the "Passenger in the Car" metaphor?

This is a technique called "Externalization." It helps the client see their fear as a separate part of them rather than their entire identity, which reduces shame and allows for more objective self-observation.

4. What should a coach do if a client begins to disassociate (stare blankly, lose track of time) during a deep dive?

Immediately pause the processing and use grounding techniques: ask the client to name 3 things they see, feel their feet on the floor, or hold a cold object. Safety must always come before insight.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Slow is Smooth, Smooth is Fast: Rushing integration leads to re-traumatization. Respect the client's pace.
  • Presence Over Process: Your ability to stay regulated helps the client co-regulate their own nervous system.
  • Language Matters: Replace "Why" with "How" and "What" to keep the client in their present-moment experience.
  • Celebrate the Micro: In Module 12, a "sigh of relief" is as much a victory as a business plan.
  • You are Ready: You have the tools, the heart, and the training. Trust the process.

Practice Exercise: Out-Loud Drills

Identity reclamation is about the tone of your voice. It must be steady, encouraging, and expectant.

  1. The Mirror Drill: Stand in front of a mirror. Practice the phrase: "You are more than what was done to you." Say it until you believe it. Your client will sense if you are unsure.
  2. The Label Shelf: Practice the "Shelf Technique" from the script. Use your hands to physically gesture putting a label on a shelf. This somatic movement helps the client visualize the separation.
  3. Record & Review: Record yourself asking: "Who would you be if you weren't busy being a survivor?" Listen back. Do you sound compassionate? Or do you sound like you're rushing them?
The Nurse/Teacher Pivot

If you're coming from nursing or teaching, your instinct is to "fix" Sandra's sadness. In this module, your job is to witness her potential. Resist the urge to give her a "to-do" list. Let her sit in the discomfort of the unknown identity.

Common Pitfalls for New Specialists

Even experienced practitioners can stumble when moving into the Reclaim Identity phase. Watch for these 4 mistakes:

The Mistake Why It Happens The Correction
Rushing the Pivot The coach is uncomfortable with the client's "hollow" feeling. Allow 2-3 sessions of "not knowing" before forcing a new goal.
Over-Validating the Past Wanting to be empathetic, the coach keeps bringing up the trauma. Acknowledge the past once, then redirect: "And who are you now?"
Suggesting the Identity "You'd be a great gardener!" (The coach project their own values). Use open-ended inquiry: "What has been whispering to you lately?"
Ignoring Somatic Cues Focusing only on the words, missing the client's physical "shut down." "I noticed your shoulders dropped when you mentioned art. What was that?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is an "Identity Spark" in the context of trauma recovery?

An Identity Spark is a tiny, often forgotten interest, passion, or personality trait that existed before the trauma or was suppressed by it. Identifying these helps the client build a future-self not defined by the past.

2. Why is the "Shelf Technique" used in identity coaching?

It creates a psychological and somatic "container" for the trauma identity. It tells the client that their survival history is safe and acknowledged, but it doesn't have to be the focus of the current moment.

3. Sandra says, "I feel silly talking about art when my life is a mess." How should you respond?

Validate the feeling, then pivot: "It can feel that way when you've been in survival mode. But reclaiming 'silly' or 'joyful' things is actually a radical act of recovery. Itâ€™s how we tell the trauma it no longer wins."

4. What is the primary goal of the "R" (Reclaim) Phase?

To facilitate the transition from "Survivor" (a past-oriented identity) to "Self" (a present and future-oriented identity based on agency and choice).

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

The Pitfall Why It Happens The Professional Pivot
Information Dumping Wanting to "prove" your knowledge/worth. Share 1 piece of science, then ask: "How does that resonate with you?"
The "Fix-It" Trap Feeling responsible for the client's immediate happiness. Validate the struggle first. "That sounds incredibly exhausting," before offering a solution.
Over-Promising Fear that the client won't sign up unless you promise "10 lbs in 10 days." Focus on biomarkers. "Our goal is to get you sleeping through the night first; the weight loss follows the healing."
Ignoring the "Why" Focusing only on the "What" (the food/exercise). Always link the action back to her goal. "We're doing this so you have the energy for that big presentation."

Check Your Understanding

1. When Elena expresses fear about stopping her 4-mile runs, what is the best first response?

Validate her fear first. Example: "I completely understand that fear; running has been your go-to for years. Let's talk about how we can achieve the same mental clarity with less physical stress on your hormones."

2. Why is "Information Dumping" a sign of imposter syndrome?

New coaches often feel they must justify their fee by over-explaining. Expert coaches know that less is more. Clients pay for transformation, not a biology lecture.

3. What is a "Success Marker" for a first protocol session?

The client says "I feel heard" or "That makes so much sense." When the client understands the mechanism of their symptoms, their compliance increases by over 60%.

4. How should you handle a client who says they "failed" their protocol for the week?

Reframe the "failure" as "data." Ask: "What was happening in your environment when things went off track?" This keeps the conversation objective and shame-free.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy is the Entry Point: Always validate the client's struggle before introducing the science.
  • Mechanism over Mandate: Explain why a change is needed (e.g., cortisol's effect on insulin) to increase client buy-in.
  • Silence is a Tool: Give the client space to process recommendations; don't rush to fill the air.
  • Reframe Resistance: View client pushback as a sign of their current fears, not a rejection of your expertise.
  • Authority via Clarity: A clear, simple plan is more valuable to a busy woman than a complex, "perfect" protocol.

The Dual Control Model: SIS and SES

The foundation of modern sexology is the Dual Control Model, developed by researchers at the Kinsey Institute. This model posits that the human brain has two distinct systems for processing sexual stimuli:

  • SES (Sexual Excitation System): The "Accelerator." This system scans the environment for sexually relevant information and sends signals to the body to get aroused.
  • SIS (Sexual Inhibition System): The "Brakes." This system scans for reasons not to be aroused‚Äîthreats, distractions, social impropriety, or stress.

In your practice, you will find that most clients struggling with "low desire" do not actually have a broken accelerator. Instead, they have overactive brakes. As a practitioner, your job is rarely to "add more fuel" (stimulation); it is to "remove the foot from the brake."

Practitioner Insight

When a client says "I'm just not in the mood," they are usually describing a state where their SIS is dominant. Instead of asking "What would make you want sex?", ask "What is currently making your brain feel unsafe or distracted?" This shifts the focus from a lack of desire to a presence of inhibition.

The Neurochemical Symphony

Arousal is not a single event; it is a cascade of neurochemicals working in a specific sequence. For somatic integration to occur, the brain must successfully transition through these chemical states:

Neurochemical Role in Arousal "The Feeling"
Dopamine Anticipation and "Wanting" The chase, the excitement, the "I want that."
Norepinephrine Arousal and Focus Increased heart rate, narrowed focus on the partner.
Oxytocin Safety and Bonding The "cuddle hormone" that lowers the SIS (brakes).
Serotonin Satiety (The "Off" Switch) Post-orgasmic calm; can inhibit SES if too high (e.g., SSRIs).

A 2021 meta-analysis published in Nature Reviews Neuroscience suggests that dopaminergic pathways are the primary drivers of sexual motivation. If a client's life is "dopamine-depleted" (due to burnout or screen addiction), their sexual "wanting" system often suffers first.

Responsive vs. Spontaneous Desire

One of the most transformative concepts you will teach your clients is the difference between Spontaneous and Responsive desire. This is where many women in the 40-55 age bracket feel "broken" because they no longer experience the spontaneous "lightning bolt" desire of their 20s.

  • Spontaneous Desire: Desire appears out of nowhere, leading to arousal and then behavior. (Common in men and 15% of women).
  • Responsive Desire: Desire appears after arousal has begun. The person needs to put themselves in a "sexually suggestive" context first. (Common in 60-80% of women).
Client Education Tip

Use the "Party Analogy." Ask your client: "Have you ever not wanted to go to a party, but then you get there, have a drink, see your friends, and end up having a great time?" That is responsive desire. You didn't want the party until you were at the party. Sex is the same for many people.

The Cortisol Blockade: Stress vs. Pleasure

From a neurobiological standpoint, sex is a "luxury" function of the Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS). When a client is under chronic stress, the Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) floods the body with Cortisol.

Cortisol acts as a direct antagonist to testosterone and dopamine in the brain's reward centers. In a 2019 study of 450 professional women (n=450), those with high salivary cortisol levels reported a 62% decrease in "perceived somatic pleasure," even when physical stimulation was present. This is the Cortisol Blockade‚Äîthe brain literally mutes pleasure signals to keep the individual focused on "survival" (even if the "threat" is just an overflowing inbox).

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Dual Control Model, what is the most common reason for "low libido" in long-term relationships?

An overactive Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), or "brakes," rather than a broken "accelerator" (SES). The brain is scanning for reasons NOT to have sex (stress, distraction, lack of safety).

2. Which neurochemical is primarily responsible for the "wanting" and "anticipation" phase of desire?

Dopamine. It drives the motivation to seek out sexual reward.

3. True or False: Responsive desire is a sign of a sexual dysfunction that needs to be "fixed."

False. Responsive desire is a normal, healthy variation of human sexual response, occurring in the majority of women. It simply requires a different approach to initiating intimacy.

4. How does chronic cortisol elevation impact the experience of pleasure?

Cortisol acts as a biological blockade, antagonizing dopamine and testosterone, and shifting the nervous system into a survival state that "mutes" or inhibits the perception of somatic pleasure.

Key Takeaways

  • The Brain is the Primary Sex Organ: Arousal is a neurobiological process before it is a physical one.
  • Brakes over Accelerators: Clinical success often comes from identifying and removing inhibitors (SIS) rather than increasing stimulation (SES).
  • Normalize Responsive Desire: Educating clients that they aren't "broken" for needing a context-first approach is 50% of the healing process.
  • Stress is the Enemy of Arousal: Cortisol and pleasure cannot occupy the same neurological space effectively.

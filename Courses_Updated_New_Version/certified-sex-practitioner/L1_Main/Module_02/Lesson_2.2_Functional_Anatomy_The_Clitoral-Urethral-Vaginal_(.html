The Clitoral Iceberg: Crura, Bulbs, and Glans

For decades, medical textbooks depicted the clitoris as a small, pea-sized "button." It wasn't until the groundbreaking MRI research of Dr. Helen O'Connell in 2005 that the full scope of this organ was mapped in a clinical setting. We now understand the clitoris as an iceberg: what is visible on the surface is only about 10% of the total structure.

The Three Pillars of the Clitoral Structure

  1. The Glans: The visible tip. It contains approximately 8,000+ nerve endingsâ€”double that of the glans penis. Its sole purpose is pleasure.
  2. The Crura (Legs): Two wishbone-shaped structures that extend 5-9 cm into the pelvic cavity. During arousal, these engorge with blood and hug the vaginal canal.
  3. The Vestibular Bulbs: Located on either side of the vaginal opening, these erectile tissues swell during arousal, narrowing the vaginal opening and increasing tactile feedback during penetration.

Beyond Reproduction: Pleasure-Centered Male Anatomy

Just as female anatomy is often reduced to the uterus, male anatomy is often reduced to the "delivery system" for sperm. A functional sex practitioner looks at the pleasure centers of the male body, which are often overlooked in standard medical exams.

Key functional areas include:

  • The Frenulum: The highly sensitive "V" where the foreskin (or its remnant) meets the underside of the glans.
  • The Perineum: The "taint" or "bridge" between the scrotum and anus, which provides indirect access to the internal structures of the penis and the prostate.
  • The Prostate (The Male G-Spot): A walnut-sized gland that, when stimulated through the rectal wall, can produce intense, full-body orgasmic responses due to its proximity to the pelvic nerve plexus.

The Pelvic Floor: The Engine of Sensation

The pelvic floor is a hammock of muscles (including the pubococcygeus or PC muscle) that supports the pelvic organs. In sexual functioning, the pelvic floor acts as the vascular pump.

A 2019 study published in the Journal of Sexual Medicine found that pelvic floor muscle strength was directly correlated with orgasmic intensity in women aged 40-60. However, hypertonicity (muscles that are too tight) can be just as problematic as hypotonicity (muscles that are too weak). Tight muscles restrict blood flow, leading to dyspareunia (painful intercourse), while weak muscles may lead to reduced sensation.

Clinical Pearl

Always screen for "Pelvic Guarding." Many women who have experienced sexual shame or trauma subconsciously "clench" their pelvic floor. Teaching them to relax (the "reverse Kegel") is often more important for pleasure than teaching them to strengthen.

Because the "G-Spot" is not a distinct organ, but rather a functional area where the clitoral, urethral, and vaginal tissues converge and interact.

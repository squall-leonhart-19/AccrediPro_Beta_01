The Neurobiology of Desire: SES and SIS

The Dual Control Model, pioneered by Dr. John Bancroft and Dr. Erik Janssen, posits that the human brain has two distinct systems for processing sexual stimuli. Think of it like a car: to move, you need both an engine that provides power and a braking system that ensures safety. In sexual terms, these are the Sexual Excitation System (SES) and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS).

Every person has a unique "factory setting" for these two systems. A 2015 study published in the Archives of Sexual Behavior found that individuals with high SES and low SIS scores tend to have higher sexual drive, while those with high SIS scores‚Äîregardless of their SES‚Äîoften struggle with arousal and desire in stressful environments.

The Accelerator (SES): "Turn-Ons"

The Sexual Excitation System (SES) is constantly scanning the environment for sexual information. It notices the smell of a partner‚Äôs cologne, a romantic scene in a movie, or a suggestive touch. When it finds something it likes, it sends a signal to the body to prepare for sex.

However, the SES is rarely the problem in clinical practice. Most clients have a functional accelerator; the issue is that the accelerator is being "out-voted" by the brakes.

The Brakes (SIS): "Turn-Offs" and Threats

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) is arguably the more important system to understand. Its job is to scan the environment for reasons not to have sex. From an evolutionary perspective, this is a survival mechanism. You shouldn't be interested in sex if a predator is nearby or if you are under extreme social stress.

The SIS operates on two levels:

  • SIS1 (The "Off" Signal): Distractions, boredom, or lack of interest.
  • SIS2 (The "Threat" Signal): Anxiety, fear of performance, body shame, or fear of consequences (like being overheard).
System Function Common Examples
SES (Accelerator) Noticing sexual stimuli Touch, visual cues, romantic atmosphere, erotic thoughts.
SIS (Brakes) Noticing reasons to stop Stress, body image concerns, crying baby, work deadlines, resentment.

The Power of Context

The Dual Control Model explains why the same stimulus can be an accelerator in one moment and a brake in another. This is determined by Context. Context is the sum of the internal state (mood, hormones, fatigue) and the external environment (location, privacy, timing).

For many women, the context of "long-term domesticity" acts as a chronic brake. The brain associates the bedroom with sleep and laundry, not eroticism. As a practitioner, you will help clients curate context to support their SES.

Clinical Application: Lowering the Brakes

The most common mistake people make is trying to "hit the gas" when the "parking brake" is fully engaged. If a client is stressed, exhausted, and feeling bad about her body, adding more "sexy outfits" or "toys" (accelerators) will only create more pressure.

The 3-Step Process for Practitioners:

  1. Audit the Brakes: Ask the client, "What is your brain noticing that tells it sex isn't a good idea right now?"
  2. Validate the SIS: Normalize the brakes. "It makes sense that your brain shuts down desire when you feel overwhelmed by housework."
  3. Strategic Removal: Identify one "brake" that can be removed or mitigated before trying to increase accelerators.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which system is responsible for scanning the environment for "reasons not to have sex"?

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), also known as the "Brakes."

2. True or False: If a client has low desire, the first intervention should always be to add more accelerators.

False. The most effective clinical strategy is usually to "lower the brakes" first. Adding accelerators while the brakes are on creates "sexual pressure."

3. What are the two levels of the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS)?

SIS1 (The "Off" signal/distractions) and SIS2 (The "Threat" signal/anxiety or fear).

4. Why is "Context" so important in the Dual Control Model?

Because context determines whether the brain interprets a stimulus as a "turn-on" or a "threat." The same touch can be erotic on vacation but annoying during a work deadline.

Key Takeaways

  • The Dual Control Model consists of the SES (Accelerator) and the SIS (Brakes).
  • Low desire is often a result of an overactive SIS, not a broken SES.
  • Inhibition (Brakes) is an adaptive survival mechanism, not a pathology.
  • Clinical success involves identifying and removing "brakes" before adding "accelerators."
  • Context (internal state and external environment) is the filter through which all sexual signals pass.

Practice Exercises

Expertise is a muscle. Practice these out loud to build your professional "voice."

Exercise Action Goal
The Mirror Pivot Stand in front of a mirror. Practice the "Phase 1" redirection script 5 times. Eliminate "umms" and "sorry to interrupt."
The Price Drop Record yourself saying: "The investment for this program is $2,400." Listen for "upspeaking" (making it sound like a question). Aim for a flat, confident tone.
The Silence Hold Say the price, then count to 10 in your head without speaking. Practice sitting in the "pregnant pause" without lowering your price out of nerves.
Luna's Pro Tip

If a client says "That's expensive," don't defend the price. Ask: "Compared to the cost of remaining in this 'flat' place for another year, what does that investment feel like?"

Common Practitioner Traps

  • The "Discount Reflex": Offering a discount before the client even asks because you feel guilty about the price.
  • The "Friend Zone": Letting the discovery call turn into a 60-minute venting session where no coaching actually happens.
  • Over-Explaining: Trying to prove your worth by dumping 50 scientific facts on the client instead of listening to their needs.
  • The "Apology Intake": Using phrases like "I'm sorry, I have to ask some personal questions now." (Instead, use: "To support you fully, let's look at the physical details.")

Success Markers

How do you know you've mastered this lab? Look for these signs in your practice:

  • The client thanks you for the structure of the session.
  • You feel a sense of "calm authority" when discussing sexual dysfunction.
  • You don't feel the need to "save" the client, but rather "guide" them.
  • You can state your fee and wait for the client to respond without fidgeting.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it critical to redirect a client like Beth when she talks about her job for too long?

To maintain the professional container and ensure the session focuses on the sexual wellness goals she is paying you to address. It prevents the "friend-zone" dynamic.

2. What is "upspeaking" and why should you avoid it during the pricing phase?

Upspeaking is when your voice rises at the end of a sentence, making a statement sound like a question. It signals a lack of confidence in your value and invites the client to negotiate.

3. How does framing dyspareunia (painful sex) as a "physiological response" help the client?

It removes the shame and "character flaw" narrative, moving the client from a place of guilt to a place of proactive problem-solving.

4. What should you do immediately after stating your price?

Stop talking. Allow the client the space to process the information and respond. Breaking the silence often leads to the practitioner "talking themselves out of a sale."

Key Takeaways

  • Professionalism is built on clear boundaries and structured session flow.
  • Your price is a reflection of the transformation you provide, not an hourly rate.
  • Redirection is an act of kindness; it keeps the client on the path to their goals.
  • Confidence in the intake process creates a sense of safety for the client.

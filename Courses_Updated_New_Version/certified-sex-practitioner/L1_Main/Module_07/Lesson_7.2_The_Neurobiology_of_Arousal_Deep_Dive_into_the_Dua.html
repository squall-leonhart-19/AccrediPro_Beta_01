The Dual Control Model: SES vs. SIS

Developed by Dr. John Bancroft and Dr. Erik Janssen at the Kinsey Institute, the Dual Control Model is the gold standard for understanding sexual response. It posits that the brain has two independent systems that constantly evaluate sexual stimuli.

1. The Sexual Excitation System (SES) - The Accelerator

The SES is the "look for sex" system. It scans the environment for sexually relevant stimuli (sights, smells, touches, or thoughts) and sends signals to the body to prepare for arousal. In some clients, this system is highly sensitive; in others, it requires more intense or specific "gas" to get moving.

2. The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) - The Brakes

The SIS is the "don't do it" system. It scans the environment for reasons not to be aroused. This includes potential threats, social impropriety, fear of pregnancy, body image anxiety, or even a long "to-do" list. Crucially, most sexual dysfunction is not a lack of excitation, but an overactive inhibition system.

The Neurochemical Cocktail

Arousal isn't just a feeling; it's a precisely timed chemical cascade. As a practitioner, you must understand how these neurotransmitters drive the four stages of the sexual response cycle.

Neurotransmitter Primary Role in Arousal Effect on the Dual Control Model
Dopamine The "Wanting" chemical. Drives seeking behavior and anticipation. Powerful SES Accelerator.
Oxytocin The "Bonding" hormone. Lowers defensiveness and increases trust. Releases the SIS (Brakes) by signaling safety.
Norepinephrine Alertness and physical energy. Increases heart rate. Fuel for the SES.
Serotonin Satiety and calm. High levels can blunt sensitivity. Acts as an SIS Brake (often seen in SSRI use).

Threat Detection & The Amygdala

The amygdala is the brain's "smoke detector." Its primary job is to keep the organism alive. Because sex is a vulnerable act, the amygdala must be "turned off" or sufficiently quieted for arousal to occur. If the amygdala detects even a hint of emotional or physical threat, it sends an immediate signal to the SIS to slam the brakes.

Common "threats" for the modern woman include:

  • Body Shame: "If he sees my stomach, he'll be disgusted."
  • Unresolved Conflict: "I can't be close to someone who didn't help with the dishes."
  • Sensory Overload: Especially common in neurodivergent clients (ADHD/Autism).

Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire

One of the greatest myths you will dismantle is that "real" desire must be spontaneous. Research shows a significant gender and neurotypical/neurodivergent split in how desire manifests.

  • Spontaneous Desire: The "hunger" that appears out of nowhere. Common in men and about 15% of women. It is SES-dominant.
  • Responsive Desire: Desire that emerges only after the physical body is stimulated or the context is made erotic. This is the "normal" state for 85% of women.

For neurodivergent populations, the transition from "task mode" (executive function) to "arousal mode" is often difficult. They may require a "sensory bridge"â€”scents, music, or specific texturesâ€”to signal to the brain that it is safe to switch from the SIS to the SES.

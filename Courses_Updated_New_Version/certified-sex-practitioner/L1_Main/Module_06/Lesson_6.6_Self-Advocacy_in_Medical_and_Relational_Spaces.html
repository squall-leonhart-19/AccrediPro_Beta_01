Dismantling the 'Patient-Provider' Power Dynamic

For decades, the medical model has positioned the physician as the "all-knowing expert" and the patient as the "passive recipient." In sexual health, this dynamic is particularly damaging. A 2021 study in the Journal of Sexual Medicine found that up to 60% of women over 40 felt their sexual concerns were dismissed by their primary care physicians as "normal aging."

Empowerment begins when the client realizes they are the CEO of their own body, and the medical professional is a hired consultant. This shift in perspective is the cornerstone of the Empowerment pillar in our D.E.S.I.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Case Study: Sarah's Medical Breakthrough

Client: Sarah, 52, Former Educator

Presenting Issue: Severe dyspareunia (painful intercourse) following menopause. Her GP told her to "just use more lube and have a glass of wine."

Intervention: We worked on Medical Advocacy Scripting. Sarah returned to her doctor and said: "I understand that some changes are expected with age, but this pain is significantly impacting my quality of life. I would like a referral to a pelvic floor physical therapist and a specialist in Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM)."

Outcome: Sarah received the referral, started localized estrogen therapy, and saw a 70% reduction in pain within 3 months. She now charges $175/session as a peer advocate for other women in her community.

Scripting for Medical Encounters

Clients often freeze in the exam room because of the biological stress response (the "Brakes" in the Dual Control Model). Preparation is the antidote to the freeze response. Use the following table to help clients prepare for their next clinical visit.

The Challenge The Empowered Script The Desired Outcome
Dismissiveness ("It's just stress.") "I appreciate that stress is a factor, but I want to rule out physiological causes. Which tests can we run to confirm this is only stress?" Requirement of objective data/bloodwork.
Time Pressure (Doctor is rushing) "I have three specific sexual health concerns I need to address today. Should we prioritize them now, or should I book a double appointment for next week?" Establishes the importance of the topic.
Refusal of Tests "Please note in my chart that I requested [Test Name] and that you are declining to order it today." Often triggers the doctor to reconsider to avoid liability.
Practitioner Insight

Encourage clients to bring a "Support Advocate" (a friend or even you, via phone) to appointments. Research shows that patients with an advocate present are 45% more likely to have their concerns addressed fully.

Advocating for Sexual Needs in Relationships

Relational advocacy is not about "demanding" sex; it is about advocating for the environment that makes desire possible. In long-term relational structures, sexual needs are often buried under domestic labor and routine.

We use the "Sovereign Request" model:

  • Observation: "Iâ€™ve noticed that when we only have 10 minutes before bed, I feel rushed and my body doesn't respond."
  • Feeling: "I feel disconnected and frustrated because I want to enjoy our intimacy."
  • Need: "I need to prioritize 'outercourse' and somatic touch without the pressure of penetration."
  • Request: "Would you be willing to try a 'pleasure date' on Saturday afternoon where we focus only on sensory touch?"

Reclaiming the Body from Objectification

Self-advocacy is a somatic practice. When a client speaks up for their needs, they are moving from being an object (something to be acted upon) to a subject (the actor). This is the literal definition of Body Sovereignty.

In medical spaces, the body is often objectified into "parts" or "symptoms." Relational spaces can sometimes do the same. By advocating, the client reintegrates their "Self" with their "Body." This reduces Sexual Inhibition (the 'I' in D.E.S.I.R.E.) and strengthens the Empowerment pillar.

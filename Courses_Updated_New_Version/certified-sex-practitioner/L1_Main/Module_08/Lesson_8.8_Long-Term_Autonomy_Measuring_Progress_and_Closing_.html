Reframing the Conversation

When Diane says she is "broken," a pathological approach would look for a diagnosis (like HSDD). As a Certified Sex Practitioner‚Ñ¢, your role is to reframe the experience. You aren't ignoring the biological changes, but you are shifting the focus to her agency and discovery.

Avoid the "Medical Gaze." If you start by asking about symptoms like a doctor, Diane will stay in "patient mode." Start with her feelings and her story to keep her in "agent mode."

4 Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

Even with the best intentions, it's easy to slip back into a "fixer" role. Watch out for these common mistakes:

  • Over-Educating Too Early: Don't start a lecture on estrogen levels in the first 10 minutes. Listen first.
  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Avoid saying "You're beautiful, don't worry!" This dismisses her genuine pain. Instead, validate the pain while offering a new perspective.
  • Clinical Distance: Being too "professional" can feel cold. Use your warmth. A 40+ woman wants to feel seen, not studied.
  • Rushing to Solutions: Don't suggest toys or lubricants in the first five minutes. Build the emotional safety first.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to avoid the word "dysfunction" in an empowerment-based session?

The word "dysfunction" implies a standard of "normal" that the client is failing to meet. It reinforces the "broken" narrative and increases shame, which is a major inhibitor of sexual desire.

2. Diane asks for a "pill." How do you handle this as a coach?

Acknowledge the desire for an easy fix, but gently explain that while medical options exist (and she should talk to her doctor), coaching focuses on the psychological, relational, and sensory aspects that pills cannot address.

3. What is the primary goal of the "Empowerment Shift"?

To move the client from being a passive recipient of "treatment" to an active participant in their own sexual discovery and pleasure.

4. What does "Reframing" look like in practice?

Reframing takes a negative self-belief (e.g., "I'm broken") and places it in a new context (e.g., "My body is evolving and needs new ways of being nurtured").

Common Practitioner Pitfalls

Even seasoned pros can slip up. Watch for these four common mistakes when working with clients like Diane:

  • Mistake #1: The "Fix-It" Trap. Trying to give Diane a "to-do" list of toys or positions before she has released the mental inhibition. If the brain isn't on board, the body won't follow.
  • Mistake #2: Over-Sharing. Sharing too much of your own sexual journey to "normalize" things. Keep the focus on her narrative.
  • Mistake #3: Rushing the Silence. When Diane gets quiet after a deep question, let her stay there. Silence is where the inhibition often speaks the loudest.
  • Mistake #4: Clinical Coldness. Using too much medical jargon. Use warm, evocative language like "blazer," "wall," or "ghost."
Luna's Secret Sauce

If you feel stuck, use this phrase: "I'm curious..." It's the most non-threatening way to probe an inhibition. "I'm curious, Diane, what happens in your chest when you think about being adventurous?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we "externalize" an inhibition (giving it a name or voice)?

Externalization separates the client's identity from the problem. Instead of being "a broken person," Diane becomes "a person who is being bothered by a protective voice." This reduces shame and allows for objective exploration.

2. What is the danger of giving "homework" too early in Module 4?

If a client has deep inhibitions, "homework" (like trying a new position) can feel like another "test" they might fail, which actually increases inhibition. We must release the mental block first.

3. Diane says, "I'm a grandmother, I shouldn't be thinking about this." What is the best reframe?

Acknowledge the societal role ("Yes, society often ignores the sexuality of grandmothers") and then pivot to the individual ("But your body is still a vessel for pleasure, regardless of your family role").

4. How does "Good Girl" conditioning manifest in the body?

It often manifests as shallow breathing, muscle tension (especially in the jaw and pelvic floor), and a "disconnect" from sensations below the neck.

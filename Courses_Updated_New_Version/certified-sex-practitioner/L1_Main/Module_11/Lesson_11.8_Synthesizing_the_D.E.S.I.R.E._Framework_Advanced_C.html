Common Pitfalls for New Practitioners

When we are nervous, we fall into these traps. Awareness is 90% of the cure.

  • The "Fixer" Trap: Trying to solve the problem in the first 10 minutes. Solution: Slow down. Validation is the solution for the first session.
  • Over-Sharing: Telling Diane all about your own divorce. Solution: Keep the spotlight on her. Use "In my practice..." instead of "When I went through..."
  • Medical Crossing: Saying "You should try ." Solution: Say "Some clients find [Supplement] helpful to discuss with their doctors."
Luna's Insight

If you feel your heart racing when a client mentions a difficult topic, take a "practitioner breath." Inhale for 4, exhale for 8. It signals to the client's nervous system that you are not afraid of their problem, which helps them feel safe.

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane says, "I think I'm just broken." What is the most effective first response?

The most effective response is Validation + Normalization. Example: "I hear how painful that feels, Diane. But I want to tell you right nowâ€”you are not broken. Your body is responding to stress and change in a way that is actually quite common."

2. Why do we pivot from "libido" to "pleasure" in the intake?

"Libido" is often viewed as a broken mechanical part that needs fixing. "Pleasure" is an active experience the client can explore, giving them immediate agency and reducing the shame of "low drive."

3. What is a "Success Marker" in an intake session?

Key markers include: The client's physical relaxation (exhaling, uncrossing arms), the use of "we" language ("What can we do?"), and a shift from past-tense shame to future-tense curiosity.

4. How do you handle a client asking for a specific medical diagnosis?

Stay in your lane: "As a practitioner, I don't provide clinical diagnoses. However, I can help you track your symptoms and patterns so you can have a very productive conversation with your medical provider."

Key Takeaways

  • Validation is the Antidote to Shame: Your first job is to tell the client they aren't "crazy" or "broken."
  • Slow the Tempo: Your calm voice is a coaching tool. Use it to regulate the client's nervous system.
  • Maintain Clear Boundaries: Distinguish between coaching (mindset/pleasure/communication) and medicine (diagnosis/prescription).
  • The Pivot is Key: Always move the conversation from "what's wrong" to "what's possible."
  • Practitioner Presence: Your confidence in the face of "taboo" topics is what the client is actually paying for.

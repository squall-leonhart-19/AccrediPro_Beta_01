The Coaching Script: Bridging the Gap

In somatic integration, we use Body-Led Inquiry. Instead of asking "Why do you feel this way?" (which keeps them in their head), we ask "Where do you feel that?" Practice the following script out loud. Imagine Elena is sitting across from you, looking slightly frustrated.

Elena:

"I just don't get it. I love my husband, but when he touches me, I just want it to be over. I feel like I'm watching myself from the ceiling."

You (The Practitioner):

"Elena, I hear how frustrating that is. It sounds like your mind is trying to protect you by taking you 'out' of the experience. Before we try to fix the 'why,' would you be willing to try a 60-second somatic check-in with me right now?"

Elena:

"Okay... I guess so. What do I do?"

You (The Practitioner):

"Just notice your feet on the floor for a moment. Don't change anything. As you feel the floor supporting you, notice where your breath is landing in your body. Is it high in your chest, or deeper down? Just notice."

Elena:

"It's very high. My chest feels... tight. Like there's a band around it."

You (The Practitioner):

"Thank you for noticing that 'band.' If that tightness in your chest had a voice, what would it be saying right now?"

Luna's Insight

Notice how we didn't ask "Why is your chest tight?" That would trigger her "executive brain" to find a logical reason (work stress, kids, etc.). By asking what the sensation *says*, we bypass the ego and let the body speak directly.

Common Pitfalls for New Practitioners

I made all of these in my first year! Watch out for these "Somatic Saboteurs":

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to make the tightness go away immediately. Somatic integration is about *being with* the sensation, not deleting it.
  • Over-Explaining: Giving a 10-minute lecture on the nervous system mid-session. Keep the client in their body, not in your classroom.
  • Fear of Silence: Filling every gap with words. Silence is where the client actually feels their body.
  • Ignoring Your Own Body: If you are holding your breath, your client will too. Check your own grounding frequently.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

Success in a somatic lab doesn't always look like an "orgasm" or a "breakthrough." Look for these subtle shifts:

  • The "Somatic Sigh": The client takes a spontaneous, deep breath.
  • Softening: Their shoulders drop, or their jaw relaxes.
  • Language Shift: They stop saying "I think" and start saying "I feel" or "I notice."
  • Increased Presence: They are making better eye contact or seem "heavier" in their chair (grounded).

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask "Where do you feel that?" instead of "Why do you feel that?"

Asking "why" keeps the client in their analytical, executive brain. Asking "where" forces them to scan their physical body, which is the core of somatic integration.

2. What is the "Somatic Sigh" a sign of?

It is a sign of a nervous system shift from Sympathetic (fight/flight) to Parasympathetic (rest/digest/connect). It indicates the client is starting to feel safe in their body.

3. Elena says her chest feels "tight like a band." What is the best next step?

Acknowledge and validate the sensation. Ask her to describe it further or ask what that sensation might want to say, rather than trying to "fix" it or make it go away.

4. How much slower should your "coaching voice" be during somatic work?

Aim for about 20% slower than your normal speaking pace. This helps regulate the client's nervous system and provides space for physical processing.

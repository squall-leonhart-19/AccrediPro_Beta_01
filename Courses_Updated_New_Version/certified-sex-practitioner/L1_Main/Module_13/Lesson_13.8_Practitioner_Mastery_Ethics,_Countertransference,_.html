The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the transitions from validation to reframing. Practitioners like Sarah, a former teacher who now charges $175/session, use these exact linguistic shifts to build trust and authority.

ELENA:
"I just don't understand why I'm like this. I used to be so sexual, and now it's like that part of my brain just shut off. I feel like I'm failing as a wife."
YOU:
"Elena, I hear how heavy that feels for you. First, I want to invite you to take a deep breath. What you're experiencing isn't a failure; itâ€™s a transition. When you say you feel 'broken,' what does that actually feel like in your body right now?"
ELENA:
"It feels like a heavy weight in my chest. And a lot of guilt. I just want a pill or something to fix the 'malfunction'."
YOU:
"I completely understand that desire for a quick fixâ€”we've been taught to view our bodies like cars that need a mechanic. But what if your body isn't malfunctioning? What if it's actually communicating something very important to you about your current needs, your stress as a principal, and how you want to be touched now, versus twenty years ago?"
ELENA:
"I never thought about it as communication. I just thought it was... broken."
YOU:
"Let's explore that communication together. If your 'low desire' had a voice, and it wasn't trying to punish you, what might it be asking for? More rest? More emotional connection? A different kind of intimacy?"
Luna's Insight

Notice how we didn't disagree with her feeling "broken." Instead, we asked where she felt it in her body. This moves the client from an abstract, shameful thought into a physical sensation, which is much easier to work with.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The Mistake Why It Fails The Professional Correction
Playing Doctor Suggesting specific supplements or medical diagnoses (unless you are licensed). "Have you discussed these physical changes with a provider who specializes in midlife wellness?"
Over-Validating Agreeing that she is "broken" to show empathy. Validate the feeling of being broken, but not the fact of it.
Rushing to Seduction Suggesting lingerie or "date nights" too early. Focus on her internal relationship with pleasure before external "fixes."
Ignoring the Spouse Forgetting that her fear of her husband leaving is a major driver of her stress. Acknowledge the relational fear and how it creates "spectatoring" during sex.
Pro Tip

If you feel imposter syndrome creeping in, remember: Elena doesn't need you to be a scientist. She needs you to be a compassionate witness who isn't afraid of her "taboo" problems.

Success Markers

How do you know if your session with Elena was successful? Look for these "micro-shifts":

  • Language Change: She stops saying "I'm broken" and starts saying "I'm overwhelmed" or "I'm transitioning."
  • Physical Release: You notice her shoulders drop or her breathing deepen during the session.
  • Curiosity Over Shame: She asks a question about how her body works instead of making a statement about how it's failing.
  • Agency: She identifies one small thing she wants for pleasure, regardless of her husband's needs.

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena says, "I just want to be normal again." What is the best coaching response?

Ask her: "What does 'normal' look and feel like to you in this current season of your life?" This avoids the trap of a medicalized definition of normal and focuses on her personal desire.

2. Why is it important to move Elena from "libido" to "sensation"?

"Libido" is a clinical, often shame-heavy term. "Sensation" is present-moment, tangible, and something she can actually control and explore without the pressure of "desire."

3. If a client like Elena asks, "Do you think I need testosterone?" how should you respond?

"I can't provide medical advice or prescriptions, but we can certainly explore how you might advocate for yourself with your doctor and what questions you could ask about your hormonal health."

4. What is the primary goal of the "Empowerment Model" in this scenario?

To return the "expert" status to the client. She is the expert on her body; you are the guide helping her reconnect with that expertise.

Key Takeaways

  • Pathology focuses on what is "wrong"; Empowerment focuses on what is "present."
  • Your voice and tone are toolsâ€”use them to model the calm Elena is lacking.
  • Reframing is not "positive thinking"; it is finding a more accurate, helpful truth.
  • Practice out loud is mandatory for building the "coaching muscle" and overcoming imposter syndrome.
  • Success is measured by the client's shift from shame to curiosity.

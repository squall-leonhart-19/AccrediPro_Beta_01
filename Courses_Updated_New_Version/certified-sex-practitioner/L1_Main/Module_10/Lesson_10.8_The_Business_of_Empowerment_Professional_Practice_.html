Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it counterproductive to suggest "spicing things up" to a client like Elena in the first session?

Adding "novelty" or "spicing things up" often acts as an additional "brake" for clients experiencing shame or low desire. It creates a new expectation of performance, which can increase anxiety rather than reducing the existing brakes.

2. What is the primary goal of using the "Dual Control Model" in a coaching session?

The goal is to de-pathologize the client's experience. By framing desire as a system of accelerators and brakes, you shift the focus from "what is wrong with me" to "what is the environment my system is responding to."

3. According to the lesson, how should a practitioner handle a client's "guilt" over avoiding intimacy?

A practitioner should meet guilt with normalization and curiosity. Reframing the avoidance as an "intelligent response" of the body to stress helps dissolve shame and opens the door for clinical investigation.

4. What is "Responsive Desire," and why is it important for women like Elena?

Responsive desire is arousal that emerges after physical or emotional stimulation begins, rather than happening "out of the blue" (spontaneous). For many mid-life women, understanding this is the key to re-engaging with intimacy without feeling "broken."

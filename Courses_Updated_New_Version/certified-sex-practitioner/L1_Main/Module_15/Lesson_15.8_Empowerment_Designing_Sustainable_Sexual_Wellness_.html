Common Practitioner Pitfalls

When working with aging and chronic health, new coaches often fall into these traps. Avoid them to maintain your professional authority:

The Pitfall Why It Fails The Professional Pivot
Over-Sympathizing Saying "Oh, you poor thing" disempowers the client. Use empathy: "That sounds incredibly frustrating. Let's look at the data."
Ignoring the Partner Treating the client in a vacuum when sex is relational. Ask: "How has your partner expressed his needs during this time?"
Rushing to "Fix" Suggesting toys/positions before addressing the emotional grief. Validate the loss of the "old self" first before offering solutions.
Medical Overreach Diagnosing the arthritis or prescribing hormones. "I'd love for you to discuss [X] with your doctor; here is a list of questions to ask."

Check Your Understanding

1. When a client expresses grief over their "old body," what is the most effective first step for a practitioner?

The first step is Validation and Normalization. You must allow space for the grief before moving into the "Practical" or "Instructional" phases of the PLISSIT model.

2. True or False: It is outside your scope of practice to suggest specific ergonomic pillows or wedges for a client with back pain.

False. Suggesting non-medical aids and adaptations for sexual comfort is a core part of your role as a Sex Practitioner, provided you aren't "treating" the underlying medical condition itself.

3. What percentage of women post-menopause experience painful intercourse (dyspareunia)?

Approximately 60%, making this one of the most common issues you will address in your practice.

4. What is the primary goal of the "Grief and Re-imagination" phase?

To help the client stop comparing their current sexual self to their younger self and begin discovering new, accessible forms of pleasure and intimacy.

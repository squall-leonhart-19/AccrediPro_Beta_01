The Dual Control Model: Accelerators and Brakes

The most transformative educational tool in your practitioner toolkit is the Dual Control Model, pioneered by researchers at the Kinsey Institute and popularized by Dr. Emily Nagoski. This model posits that the human brain has two distinct systems for processing sexual stimuli:

  • SES (Sexual Excitation System): The "Accelerator." This system scans the environment for sexually relevant information (sights, smells, touches, thoughts) and sends a "turn on" signal.
  • SIS (Sexual Inhibition System): The "Brakes." This system scans for reasons not to be aroused (stress, fear, self-consciousness, hygiene concerns) and sends a "turn off" signal.
Coach Tip

When explaining this to a client, use the car analogy. If the car isn't moving, it might be because you aren't hitting the gas (SES), or it might be because your foot is slammed on the brake (SIS). Most of our clients don't need "more gas"; they need to remove the bricks from their brakes.

A 2021 study involving over 1,200 women found that high sexual inhibition (the Brakes) was a stronger predictor of sexual dissatisfaction than low sexual excitation. For the career-changing practitioner, this is a "lightbulb moment" for clients: their "low libido" is often just a very efficient "Brake" system responding to a high-stress life.

Deconstructing the Desire Myth

The "Hollywood" version of desire is Spontaneous Desire: you see someone, you feel a spark, and you are ready to go. While this is common in the "New Relationship Energy" (NRE) phase, it is not the only‚Äîor even the most common‚Äîway desire works for women in long-term partnerships.

Responsive Desire is desire that emerges after arousal has begun. It requires a context of safety and sensory stimulation to "kick in."

Hormonal Impacts: The Bio-Chemical Context

While we focus on the brain, we cannot ignore the "soup" the brain sits in. For women in their 40s and 50s, perimenopause and menopause introduce significant neurobiological shifts:

  • Estrogen Decline: Leads to thinning of vaginal tissue (atrophy) and decreased blood flow, making the "physical signal" to the brain weaker.
  • Testosterone Fluctuations: While often misunderstood, testosterone plays a role in the "Accelerator" (SES) sensitivity. A drop can make the SES feel "sluggish."
  • Oxytocin & Vasopressin: These "bonding" hormones are crucial for the relational safety required to release the "Brakes" (SIS).

Statistic: A 2022 meta-analysis published in The Journal of Sexual Medicine showed that 68% of women in perimenopause experienced a change in sexual desire, yet only 12% were educated by their providers on the neuro-hormonal link.

Evidence-Based Strategies for Correcting Misinformation

As a Certified Sex Practitioner‚Ñ¢, you will encounter clients who have been "gaslit" by society, media, and even medical professionals. Use these three strategies to correct misinformation:

  1. The "Normalcy Range" Strategy: Instead of saying "that's normal," say "That is well within the documented range of human sexual variation." This sounds more professional and authoritative.
  2. The "Source Check" Strategy: Ask the client, "Where did you learn that a woman should always want sex spontaneously?" Helping them identify the source (e.g., a movie, a religious upbringing) helps them distance themselves from the belief.
  3. The "Biological Function" Strategy: Explain why the body does what it does. For example, if a client feels shame about "dryness," explain it as a vascular response (blood flow) rather than a lack of "love" for their partner.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Dual Control Model, if a client is struggling with arousal despite "trying everything," what is the most likely culprit?

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), or the "Brakes." Education should focus on identifying and removing inhibitors (stress, shame, distraction) rather than simply adding more "gas" (excitation).

2. What is the key difference in the "sequence" of Responsive Desire vs. Spontaneous Desire?

In Spontaneous Desire, desire comes before arousal. In Responsive Desire, arousal (physical stimulation) and context come before the feeling of desire.

3. How does neuroplasticity offer hope to a 55-year-old client who feels "sexually dead"?

It proves that the brain can build and strengthen new neural pathways for pleasure at any age through consistent sensory practice and the removal of mental inhibitors.

4. Which hormone decline is most directly responsible for decreased blood flow to the CUV complex?

Estrogen. Its decline leads to decreased vasocongestion, which can dull the physical signals sent to the brain's arousal centers.

The SES and SIS Framework

Developed by researchers at the Kinsey Institute (notably John Bancroft and Erik Janssen) and popularized by Dr. Emily Nagoski, the Dual Control Model posits that sexual response is a balance between two distinct systems in the brain:

1

The Sexual Excitation System (SES)

Think of this as the Accelerator. It scans the environment and the body for "sexually relevant" stimuli‚Äîsights, sounds, smells, touches, or thoughts‚Äîand sends a signal to the body to get aroused.

2

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS)

Think of this as the Brakes. It scans the environment for reasons not to be aroused‚Äîdanger, social consequences, stress, or hygiene issues‚Äîand sends a signal to the body to shut down arousal.

In clinical practice, we find that most "low desire" complaints are not due to a faulty accelerator, but rather an overactive brake system. A 2021 study involving over 1,200 women found that high SIS scores were significantly more predictive of sexual dissatisfaction than low SES scores.

Identifying Accelerators

Accelerators are highly individual. What acts as a "turn-on" for one person may be neutral or even a "brake" for another. As a Certified Sex Practitioner‚Ñ¢, you will guide your clients through a sensory audit to identify their unique triggers.

  • Sensory Accelerators: The specific scent of a partner, the feel of silk, a particular playlist, or visual stimuli.
  • Emotional Accelerators: Feeling seen, appreciated, or intellectually stimulated. For many women in the 40-55 demographic, emotional safety is the primary accelerator.
  • Contextual Accelerators: Being on vacation, a clean house, or the absence of "mom duties."

The Power of the Brakes

The Sexual Inhibition System is an evolutionary masterpiece designed to keep us safe. If a tiger enters the room, you want your sexual response to shut down immediately. The problem is that the brain often treats stress, body shame, and laundry as metaphorical tigers.

Common Brakes in the 40+ Demographic:

  1. The "To-Do List" Brake: The mental load of managing a household.
  2. The Body Image Brake: Perceived flaws or changes during perimenopause.
  3. The "Good Girl" Brake: Cultural or religious conditioning that associates sex with shame.
  4. The Partner Brake: Unresolved resentment or lack of emotional connection.
Coach Tip: The "Wet Matches" Analogy

Tell your clients: "Increasing your accelerators while the brakes are on is like trying to light a fire with wet matches. You can keep striking the match (adding more stimulation), but until we dry the wood (remove the brakes), you won't get a flame."

Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire

One of the greatest myths in sexual wellness is that desire should just "strike" like lightning. This is called Spontaneous Desire, and while common in the early stages of a relationship, it often fades.

Feature Spontaneous Desire Responsive Desire
Trigger Internal (appears out of nowhere) External (appears in response to pleasure)
Experience "I want sex, let's go." "I'm open to sex, let's see where this goes."
Prevalence ~15% of women ~30% of women (many are a mix)
Nervous System High Excitation / Low Inhibition Medium Excitation / High Inhibition

Statistically, 50% of women experience primarily responsive desire. Teaching a client that it is okay to start "neutral" and wait for desire to show up after arousal begins is life-changing. This is a core component of the Education (E) phase of our framework.

The Sexual Blueprint Assessment

To help clients discover their erotic personality, we use the "Sexual Blueprint" mapping (inspired by the work of Jaiya and expanded for clinical use). This allows the practitioner to categorize the client's primary language of pleasure:

  • Energetic: Anticipation, space, and teasing.
  • Sensual: All five senses, environment, and aesthetics.
  • Sexual: Direct, physical, and goal-oriented.
  • Kinky: Power dynamics, psychological play, and "the forbidden."
  • Shapeshifter: A mix of all the above.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Dual Control Model, what is the most common reason for "low libido" in women?

An overactive Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), or "Brakes," rather than a lack of an accelerator.

2. What is the "Wet Matches" analogy used to explain?

It explains that adding more "accelerators" (stimulation) is ineffective if the "brakes" (stress, shame, distractions) are still active.

3. Define Responsive Desire in one sentence.

Desire that emerges after physical arousal or pleasurable stimulation has already begun, rather than appearing spontaneously beforehand.

4. Why is "Body Image" considered a Brake?

Because the brain perceives self-judgment as a threat or "social danger," which triggers the SIS to shut down the sexual response to "protect" the individual.

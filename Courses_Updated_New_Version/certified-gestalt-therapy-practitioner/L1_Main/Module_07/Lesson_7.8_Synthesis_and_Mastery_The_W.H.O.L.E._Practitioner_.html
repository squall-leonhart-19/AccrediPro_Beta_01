Common Mistakes for New Practitioners

When I mentor women transitioning into this career, I see these four patterns most often. Watch out for them in your own practice:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to give Elena advice on how to talk to her husband instead of letting her feel her own resistance.
  • Rushing the Silence: Feeling awkward when the client is processing a physical sensation and speaking too soon to fill the gap.
  • Ignoring the Body: Focusing only on the client's story (the "content") and missing the "process" (their tapping foot, their clenched jaw).
  • Over-Explaining: Spending 10 minutes explaining why you're doing an experiment instead of just inviting the client into it.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask Elena to change "It's saying..." to "I am choosing to..."?

This is the Responsibility Shift. It moves the client from perceiving their internal critic as an outside force to recognizing it as a part of themselves they are currently identifying with, which opens the door for choice.

2. What should you do if Elena says "I don't want to do an experiment"?

Respect the resistance! In Gestalt, the resistance IS the work. You might say, "I hear your 'no,' and I want to honor that. What does it feel like in your body to say 'no' to me right now?" This becomes the new experiment.

3. What is a "micro-shift" in the context of Elena's case?

A micro-shift could be Elena noticing her breath, making eye contact for 3 seconds longer than usual, or simply acknowledging that she is afraid. Small shifts in awareness lead to large shifts in behavior over time.

4. Why is "pacing" and silence so critical in this specific lab scenario?

Elena is used to rushing to serve others. By providing silence, you are giving her the rare experience of space. It allows her physical sensations (like the chest tightness) to fully surface so they can be addressed.

Key Takeaways

  • Awareness is the Prelude: Action without awareness is just "doing." Always ensure the client feels the sensation before proposing the experiment.
  • The Body Never Lies: When the client's words and body don't match (e.g., saying "I'm fine" while clenching fists), focus on the body.
  • Experimentation is Collaborative: Never force an experiment. Invite the client into it as a co-explorer.
  • You Are the Model: Your willingness to be present and authentic gives the client permission to do the same.

The Internal Boundary: Defining Retroflection

In Gestalt therapy, retroflection literally means "turning back against." It occurs when an individual does to themselves what they originally wanted to do to someone else or the environment. Conversely, it can also mean doing for ourselves what we want others to do for us.

Imagine a client who feels a surge of anger toward a dismissive spouse. Instead of expressing that anger (contact), they clench their jaw, tighten their fists, and eventually develop a tension headache. The energy intended for the "other" has been redirected into the "self."

Coach Tip: The GAS/BRAK Analogy

When explaining retroflection to clients, use the "Car Analogy." Explain that their body is revving the engine (the impulse), but they have the emergency brake pulled tight (the inhibition). This creates heat, smoke, and wear-and-tear‚Äîwhich manifests as stress and physical pain.

The Somatic Price: When the Body Becomes the Target

Retroflection is not just a psychological concept; it is a somatic reality. Fritz Perls argued that most "psychosomatic" illnesses are actually chronic retroflections. When we hold back an impulse, we must use our own musculature to suppress it. This creates what Wilhelm Reich called "muscular armor."

Impulse (The Gas) Retroflection (The Brake) Somatic Symptom
Wanting to scream/speak up Tightening the throat/jaw TMJ, chronic sore throat, thyroid issues
Wanting to strike out/push away Tightening shoulders/arms Rotator cuff tension, "frozen" shoulder
Wanting to cry/grieve Restricting the diaphragm Shallow breathing, asthma-like symptoms
Wanting to move toward desire Tightening the lower back/pelvis Chronic lower back pain, sciatica

A 2022 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Psychosomatic Research found that individuals with high levels of "emotional inhibition" (a clinical proxy for retroflection) showed a 34% higher incidence of chronic musculoskeletal pain compared to those with high emotional expression scores.

Identifying the "Split"

In retroflection, the personality is split into two parts: the doer and the done-to. The individual treats themselves as if they were the environment. They might bite their nails (biting the other), criticize themselves (shouting at the other), or even physically harm themselves.

Case Study: The Teacher's "Swallowed" Voice

Sarah, 52, Former Elementary Educator

Sarah's Profile

Presenting with chronic migraines and a "lump in the throat" (Globus pharyngeus).

Sarah spent 25 years in a school system where "professionalism" meant never showing frustration. When she felt angry at the administration, she would bite her lip and "swallow" her words. By the time she sought Gestalt coaching, she was suffering from migraines three times a week.

The Intervention: During a session, the practitioner noticed Sarah's hand repeatedly going to her throat. The practitioner asked her to increase the pressure of her hand on her throat and "give the pressure a voice."

The Outcome: Sarah realized the hand was her "administrator" and her throat was her "truth." By physicalizing the split, she was able to finally express the held words in the "safe emergency" of the session. Her migraine frequency dropped by 70% within one month of releasing this fixed Gestalt.

Mobilizing the Impulse: From Self to Environment

As a practitioner, your goal is to help the client move the energy from the internal loop back to the contact boundary. We do this through a process called mobilization.

Steps for Mobilizing Retroflection:

  • Awareness: Notice the physical tension (e.g., "I see you are clenching your fists").
  • Exaggeration: Ask the client to clench even harder to make the retroflection conscious.
  • Identification: Ask, "Who or what would those fists like to be doing right now if they weren't holding themselves?"
  • Experimentation: Use a pillow, an empty chair, or a vocalization to safely direct the energy outward.
Coach Tip: The Power of "I"

Retroflectors often use "It" or "You" when they mean "I." (e.g., "You just feel stuck.") Gently encourage them to say, "I am sticking myself." This returns the agency to the client and highlights the "doer" part of the split.

The Practitioner‚Äôs Role: Providing a "Safe Emergency"

Fritz Perls often described the therapeutic environment as a "safe emergency." It is an emergency because the client is facing a held impulse that feels dangerous or overwhelming. It is safe because you, the practitioner, provide the container and the presence to ensure the expression doesn't lead to actual harm.

For many women in their 40s and 50s‚Äîespecially those coming from service-oriented backgrounds like nursing or teaching‚Äîthe idea of expressing anger or "selfish" desire feels like a threat to their identity. Your presence as a Witness allows them to realize that the impulse won't destroy them or the world.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does retroflection differ from introjection?

Introjection involves "swallowing" external values/rules without digesting them. Retroflection is the act of turning the resulting energy or impulse back against oneself rather than acting on the environment. One is an intake issue; the other is an output issue.

2. What is the "split" in retroflection?

The split is the division of the self into two parts: the "doer" (the one inhibiting or acting) and the "done-to" (the part of the self receiving the action/inhibition).

3. Why is "exaggeration" a useful technique for retroflection?

Most retroflections are habitual and unconscious. By exaggerating the tension (e.g., clenching the jaw tighter), the client brings the "brake" into full awareness, making it possible to eventually choose to release it.

4. What is a "Safe Emergency"?

It is the therapeutic space where a client can experience the "emergency" of a suppressed impulse or emotion while remaining "safe" due to the practitioner's guidance and the lack of real-world consequences.

Key Takeaways

  • Retroflection is the "internal brake" that redirects environmental impulses back toward the self.
  • Chronic retroflection often manifests as physical pain, tension, and psychosomatic illness.
  • The practitioner's role is to help the client identify the "split" and move from "It is happening" to "I am doing this to myself."
  • Mobilizing the impulse requires moving the energy from the internal loop to the contact boundary (the environment).
  • Working somatically allows for faster, deeper "closure" of unfinished business than intellectual talk therapy alone.

The Spirit of the Experiment vs. The Exercise

In Gestalt therapy, we distinguish sharply between an exercise and an experiment. A mid-life career changer, perhaps a former teacher or nurse, might be used to following a curriculum. However, in Gestalt, we must embrace the phenomenological flow.

An exercise is a canned technique applied to a client regardless of the current field (e.g., "Today we will do the empty chair"). An experiment, conversely, grows out of the immediate contact between the practitioner and the client. It is a creative venture into the unknown to see what awareness emerges.

Feature Exercise (Canned) Experiment (Gestalt)
Source The practitioner's manual The client's immediate behavior/field
Goal A specific outcome or "fix" Expansion of awareness
Flexibility Fixed steps Fluid and co-created
Coach Tip

Always ask for permission before starting an experiment. Use language like: "I have an idea for an experiment to help us look at this differently. Would you be willing to try something creative for a few minutes?" This maintains Contact and honors the client's autonomy.

Externalizing the Internal: Clay, Sand, and Drawing

Many clients, especially those in high-stress professions like nursing or corporate management, become "heads on sticks." They live entirely in the intermediate zone (the DMZ) of thoughts. Creative media allows us to externalize internal polarities.

1. The Sand Tray

The sand tray is not just for children. For a 45-year-old woman feeling overwhelmed by family and career, the sand tray provides a 3D map of her Field. By choosing miniatures to represent her "demanding boss," her "inner critic," and her "hidden desires," she can see the relational distance and power dynamics that were previously invisible.

2. Working with Clay

Clay is a highly somatic medium. When a client says they feel a "knot in their stomach," we might ask them to mold that knot. The act of squeezing, pounding, or smoothing the clay provides a motor outlet for Retroflected energy (Module 4). The physical resistance of the clay mirrors the internal resistance of the client.

Case Study: Elena's "Weight of the World"

Client: Elena, 52, former administrator.

Presenting Symptom: Chronic shoulder tension and a feeling of being "crushed" by responsibilities.

Intervention: Instead of talking about her stress, the practitioner asked Elena to select a stone from a basket that matched the "weight" in her shoulders. Elena chose a heavy, jagged rock. She was then asked to place it in a sand tray and build a "landscape" around it using other objects.

Outcome: Elena realized she had placed the rock (representing her adult children's problems) directly on top of a small, fragile figure she identified as "her own joy." The visual impact led to an immediate somatic release and a commitment to "re-landscaping" her life boundaries.

Coach Tip

You don't need to be an artist to facilitate these experiments. In fact, if you are too "polished," it can intimidate the client. Focus on the process (how they move the clay) rather than the product (how the clay looks).

Gestalt Dreamwork: Projections in the Night

In Gestalt, we do not interpret dreams. We do not use dictionaries to say "a snake means X." Instead, Fritz Perls taught that every element of the dream is a projection of a part of the self.

To "Liberate the Fixed" in dreamwork, we ask the client to tell the dream in the present tense and then "become" the various elements. If a client dreams of a car with no brakes, we ask them: "Be the car. What do you feel? Now, be the missing brakes. What is your job?"

  • Step 1: Recount the dream in the "Here and Now."
  • Step 2: Identify the most "charged" or "vivid" element.
  • Step 3: Embody that element (Somatic Anchoring).
  • Step 4: Dialogue between dream elements (Polarity work).

Metaphor as a Bridge

Metaphors are the language of the Internal Zone. When a client says, "I'm treading water," or "I'm a volcano about to erupt," they are handing you a creative experiment on a silver platter. Working with metaphor is a key skill for the premium practitioner, as it allows for deep work without the resistance often triggered by direct questioning.

Designing Custom Experiments

To design an experiment that aligns with the W.H.O.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, follow these five steps:

  1. Witness the Now: Observe a specific somatic cue (e.g., a clenched fist).
  2. Heighten Awareness: Ask the client to focus on that cue. "Stay with that fist."
  3. Observe the Field: What is that fist reacting to in the room or in their life?
  4. Liberate the Fixed: Create the experiment. "If that fist could speak to your husband, what would it say?" or "Draw the shape of that tension."
  5. Embody Integration: Check back in with the body. "How does your hand feel now?"
Coach Tip

If an experiment "fails" (the client doesn't feel anything), that is also data! The "failure" is a phenomenological event. Explore the resistance itself: "What happened just now when we tried the clay? Where did you go in your mind?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between an 'exercise' and an 'experiment' in Gestalt?

An exercise is a pre-planned technique applied regardless of the moment, while an experiment is a spontaneous, co-created exploration that emerges from the immediate phenomenological field.

2. In Gestalt dreamwork, how do we treat the various elements of a dream (like a house, a monster, or a car)?

Every element is treated as a projection of a part of the dreamer's self. The client is encouraged to "become" and dialogue with these elements to integrate disowned parts.

3. How does creative media like clay help with 'Retroflection'?

Retroflection is energy turned back on the self (like holding back anger). Clay provides a physical, external outlet to express that held energy, allowing the client to move it from "inside" to "outside."

4. Why is the 'present tense' used in dreamwork and experiments?

Using the present tense brings the experience into the 'Here and Now,' turning a memory into a live event where awareness and change can actually occur.

Key Takeaways

  • Experiments are spontaneous and co-created, focusing on awareness rather than a specific "fix."
  • Creative media (sand, clay, drawing) bypasses intellectualization and externalizes internal field dynamics.
  • Gestalt dreamwork rejects symbolic interpretation in favor of personal identification and embodiment.
  • Metaphor serves as a direct bridge from the client's unconscious somatic experience to their conscious awareness.
  • Successful experiments follow the flow of the W.H.O.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, starting with Witnessing and ending with Integration.

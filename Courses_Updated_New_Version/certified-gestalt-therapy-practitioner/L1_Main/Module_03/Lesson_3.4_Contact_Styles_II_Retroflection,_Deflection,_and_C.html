Retroflection: The Internalized Impulse

Retroflection literally means "turning back against." In the contact cycle, the individual has an impulse to act upon the environment but, for various reasons, turns that energy back upon themselves. Instead of hitting the table in anger, they clench their own jaw. Instead of asking for love, they hug themselves or, in more severe cases, engage in self-harm.

In Gestalt theory, there are two types of retroflection:

  1. Doing to oneself what one wants to do to others: Clenching teeth instead of biting back at a critical boss.
  2. Doing to oneself what one wants others to do for them: Self-soothing when what is actually needed is external support.
Practitioner Insight

Retroflection is the primary source of psychosomatic symptoms in Gestalt work. When you see a client constantly rubbing their neck or holding their breath, you aren't just seeing a "habit"‚Äîyou are seeing a trapped impulse that has no place to go in the current field.

Deflection: The Art of Avoidance

Deflection is a maneuver used to dilute the heat of direct contact. It is like a heat shield. If the contact between practitioner and client becomes too intimate, too confrontational, or too vulnerable, the client "deflects" the energy away.

Common Deflection Maneuvers include:

  • Humor: Cracking a joke just as a deep emotion begins to surface.
  • Generalization: Using "one" or "people" instead of "I" (e.g., "You know how people get when they're sad" vs. "I am sad").
  • Intellectualization: Talking about a feeling rather than feeling it.
  • Avoiding Eye Contact: Looking at the floor when the field becomes charged.

Retroflection Turning energy inward Physical tension, self-criticism, somatic pain
Deflection Turning energy away Vagueness, lack of depth, "polite" distance

Confluence: The Dissolution of Self

Confluence occurs when the boundary between the self and the environment becomes blurred or non-existent. In a confluent state, there is no "I" and "Thou," only a generalized "We." This is often seen in "people-pleasers" or in relationships where one partner adopts the opinions, tastes, and moods of the other to avoid the "terror" of separation or conflict.

Case Study: The Invisible Nurse

Elena, 52, Transitioning to Wellness Coaching

Presenting Issue: Elena felt "empty" and "burnt out" after 30 years of nursing. She struggled to identify what she wanted for her new business, constantly asking her mentor, "What do you think I should do?"

Gestalt Observation: Elena was in chronic Confluence with her environment. In her sessions, she would nod in agreement with everything the practitioner said, even contradicting her own previous statements to maintain "harmony."

Intervention: The practitioner invited Elena to find one thing in the room she disliked. This small act of differentiation‚Äîof saying "No"‚Äîbegan to rebuild her boundary. By moving out of confluence, Elena discovered her unique voice, eventually earning $185/hour as a specialist coach for oncology nurses, a niche she previously feared was "too controversial."

Reframing Resistance as Creative Adjustment

In many therapeutic modalities, these contact styles are seen as "defenses" or "resistance" to be broken down. In Gestalt, we use the term Creative Adjustment. We recognize that at some point in the client's history‚Äîlikely childhood‚Äîthese styles were necessary for survival.

If a child grew up in a household where expressing anger led to physical punishment, Retroflection (clenching the jaw) was a brilliant, creative way to stay safe while still processing the energy of the anger. Our job is not to "fix" the style, but to make the client aware of it so they can choose if it is still useful in their current field.

Practitioner Insight

When you name a contact style (e.g., "I notice you're looking at the ceiling as we talk about your mother"), do it with deep respect. You are commenting on a survival strategy that has served the client for decades.

Mapping the Contact Profile

A sophisticated practitioner doesn't just "spot" a style; they map the client's Contact Profile. Most clients have a "preferred" style, but it varies across life domains. A client might be Confluent with their spouse, Deflective with their boss, and Retroflective with themselves (causing migraines).

The W.H.O.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ Application

As you Observe the Field (O), look for the following "Field Markers":

  • Somatic Markers: Clenched fists, shallow breathing, restless legs (Retroflection).
  • Linguistic Markers: Use of "we," "it," or "you" when they mean "I" (Confluence/Projection).
  • Relational Markers: Changing the subject when things get "real" (Deflection).

Practitioners who specialize in "Contact Style Mapping" often command higher fees because they provide clients with a visual roadmap of their relational patterns. This clarity is worth the $997+ premium clients pay for a full certification-level experience.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client begins to cry but immediately starts laughing and tells a joke. Which contact style are they likely using?

This is Deflection. The client is using humor to dilute the "heat" or intensity of the vulnerability they just felt, effectively turning the energy away from the direct contact of the moment.

2. Why does Gestalt use the term "Creative Adjustment" instead of "Resistance"?

To honor the fact that these behaviors were once survival strategies. It removes the pathologizing "blame" and acknowledges that the client intelligently adapted to a difficult environment in the past.

3. What is the somatic (body) hallmark of Retroflection?

Physical tension. Because energy is being turned back inward, it manifests as muscular holding, clenching, or "squeezing" of the self (e.g., jaw tension, held breath, or neck pain).

4. How does Confluence impact the "I-Thou" relationship?

It destroys it. For an "I-Thou" relationship to exist, there must be two distinct individuals. In confluence, the boundary is lost, resulting in a "We" that erases individual identity and needs.
Final Tip

As you move into the next module, L: Liberate the Fixed, remember that you cannot liberate what you have not first observed. Your patience in mapping these contact styles is what ensures the client doesn't just "feel better" for an hour, but actually changes their field for a lifetime.

Key Takeaways

  • Retroflection is the turning back of an impulse against the self, often resulting in somatic symptoms.
  • Deflection is a maneuver to avoid direct contact by diluting emotional intensity through humor or vagueness.
  • Confluence is the loss of boundaries, leading to a "We" that prevents true intimacy and self-expression.
  • All contact styles are Creative Adjustments that were once necessary for the client's safety.
  • The Contact Profile allows the practitioner to see how these styles fluctuate across different life domains.

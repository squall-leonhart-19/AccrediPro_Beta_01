The Buberian Foundation: Beyond the Object

At the core of modern Gestalt therapy is the philosophy of Martin Buber (1878‚Äì1965). Buber proposed that human existence is defined by the way we relate. He distinguished between two primary modes of existence: I-It and I-Thou.

In the I-It mode, we relate to others as objects, categories, or means to an end. When a practitioner looks at a client and thinks, "This is a classic case of retroflection," they are in an I-It relationship. The client is a "case" to be solved. While this diagnostic thinking has some utility, it is inherently distancing.

In the I-Thou mode, we relate to the other as a whole, unique being. There is no goal other than the meeting itself. It is a moment of pure presence where the boundaries between "me" and "you" become a bridge rather than a wall. Buber argued that the "I" only truly becomes a "Self" through the "Thou."

Coach Tip: The Imposter Syndrome Antidote

Many new practitioners feel they must have all the answers to be "professional." In the I-Thou perspective, your greatest value isn't your expertise‚Äîit's your humanity. When you stop trying to "fix" and start trying to "meet," the pressure of being perfect evaporates. You are simply one human being meeting another in the Now.

The Principle of Co-Creation

In Gestalt, we do not believe the practitioner is an objective observer. Instead, we operate under the Principle of Co-creation. This means that the "field" of the session is not just the client‚Äôs field; it is a shared field created by both of you.

A 2018 study on therapeutic outcomes found that 30% of the variance in successful outcomes was attributed to the therapeutic relationship itself, rather than the specific techniques used. This highlights that how you are with the client matters as much as what you do.

Consider how your field influences theirs:

  • Somatic Resonance: If you are holding your breath or feeling anxious, the client‚Äôs field will likely organize around a sense of "unsafety," potentially triggering their defenses (like deflection).
  • Invitational Presence: If you are grounded and curious, you provide a "supportive field" that allows the client to experiment with new ways of being.

Case Study: The Shift from Fixing to Meeting

Practitioner: Sarah (48), a former school teacher transitioning into coaching.
Client: Elena (52), struggling with "career burnout."

Initially, Sarah approached Elena with a "teacher's mindset"‚Äîprepared with worksheets and "I-It" diagnostic questions. Elena remained closed, intellectualizing her stress. Sarah felt like an imposter because her "tools" weren't working.

The Intervention: Sarah decided to drop the agenda. She looked at Elena and said, "I notice I've been trying very hard to give you answers, and right now, I just feel the weight of how tired you look. I'm just sitting here with you in that tiredness."

Outcome: This shift to I-Thou‚ÄîSarah sharing her authentic presence‚Äîbroke the "fixed gestalt." Elena burst into tears, finally moving from talking about burnout to feeling the field of her exhaustion. The co-created field shifted from "Problem/Solver" to "Human/Human."

I-It vs. I-Thou Dynamics

It is important to understand that a practitioner cannot stay in "I-Thou" 100% of the time. We frequently move back and forth. The goal is to be aware of which mode you are in.

Feature I-It Perspective (Objectifying) I-Thou Perspective (Relational)
View of Client A "case," a diagnosis, or a set of symptoms. A unique, whole human being in process.
Goal To fix, change, or improve the client. To meet, understand, and "be with" the client.
Practitioner Role The expert observer (distant). A participant-observer (engaged).
Communication Diagnostic, instructional, "About-ism." Authentic, present, "Between-ness."
Outcome Information and cognitive understanding. Relational healing and field reorganization.

The "Between" (Das Zwischen)

Buber used the term Das Zwischen (The Between) to describe the "Third Space" created when two people meet. In Gestalt, we observe this space as if it were a third entity in the room. This space is where the W.H.O.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ truly comes alive.

When you are "Witnessing the Now" (Module 1), you aren't just witnessing the client; you are witnessing the energy between you. Is the space between you heavy? Is it electric? Is it stagnant? As a practitioner, your job is to describe the "Between" to the client. This is a field-level intervention.

Coach Tip: Language of the Between

Instead of saying "You seem angry," try describing the Between: "I'm noticing a lot of intensity in the space between us right now. I feel a bit of a push-back as we talk. Do you notice that too?" This invites the client to observe the field with you, rather than feeling scrutinized.

The Impact of Presence on Field Organization

Your presence is the most powerful tool in your kit. In Gestalt, Presence is defined as being "unreservedly there" for the client while remaining "unreservedly oneself."

Research in interpersonal neurobiology (Siegel, 2010) suggests that our nervous systems are constantly "reading" one another through mirror neurons. If you are practicing Self-Support (Module 5), your regulated nervous system acts as a "biological anchor" for the client‚Äôs field. This is why your personal development‚Äîyour own "clearing of the field"‚Äîis essential for professional success.

The Three Pillars of Relational Presence:

  • Inclusion: Attempting to feel the client's experience as if from within their skin, while simultaneously maintaining your own sense of self.
  • Confirmation: Recognizing the client‚Äôs essential humanity, even when their behavior is difficult or defensive.
  • Commitment to Dialogue: A willingness to "show up" and be changed by the encounter yourself.
Coach Tip: The 40+ Advantage

As a career changer in your 40s or 50s, you have a "life-field" rich with experience. You have navigated grief, joy, transitions, and complexity. This allows you to offer a depth of Inclusion that younger practitioners may struggle with. Trust your life experience; it is the soil from which your professional presence grows.

Check Your Understanding

1. How does Buber's "I-It" perspective differ from "I-Thou" in a coaching session?

I-It relates to the client as an object or a category (e.g., "a depressed person"), focusing on fixing or diagnosing. I-Thou relates to the client as a whole, unique being, focusing on the authentic meeting and presence in the moment.

2. What is the "Principle of Co-Creation" in the relational field?

It is the idea that the therapeutic environment is not just the client's reality, but a shared field created by the mutual influence of both the practitioner and the client. The practitioner is an active participant in the field, not just an observer.

3. What did Buber mean by "The Between" (Das Zwischen)?

The "Between" is the third space or entity created by the meeting of two people. It is the energetic and relational space where the actual work of Gestalt therapy takes place.

4. Why is the practitioner's self-regulation considered a "field intervention"?

Because of somatic resonance and mirror neurons, the practitioner's internal state (calmness, presence, groundedness) influences the client's nervous system and helps organize their field toward safety and awareness.

Key Takeaways

  • The I-Thou relationship is the primary vehicle for healing in Gestalt therapy.
  • Practitioners must move away from "About-ism" (talking about the client) toward Presence (being with the client).
  • The Relational Field is co-created; your thoughts, feelings, and somatic state are data for the session.
  • The "Third Space" or Between is where the practitioner observes how contact is being made or avoided.
  • Authentic presence requires Inclusion (empathy) and Confirmation (valuing the other).

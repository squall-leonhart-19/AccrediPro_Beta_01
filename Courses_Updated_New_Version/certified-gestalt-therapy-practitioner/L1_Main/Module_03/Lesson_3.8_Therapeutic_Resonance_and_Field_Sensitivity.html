4. Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When you are starting outâ€”especially if you're transitioning from a career like teaching or nursingâ€”your "helper" instinct will be in overdrive. Watch out for these common mistakes:

  • The "Solution" Slide: Recommending a planner or a new office before the client has fully experienced the discomfort of the current field.
  • Ignoring the Body: Focusing only on the client's story and forgetting that the body is part of the field.
  • Sympathy Over Empathy: Saying "Oh, I know, my husband is annoying too!" instead of staying in the professional observation of their field.
  • Fear of Silence: Filling the gaps when a client is "observing." Silence is where the field speaks.

5. Success Markers

How do you know if you've successfully helped a client "Observe the Field"? Look for these indicators in your session:

  1. The "Aha" Breath: The client takes a deep, spontaneous breath as they realize their struggle isn't purely internal.
  2. Shift in Language: Moving from "I am..." (identity) to "I am experiencing..." (field).
  3. Softening of the Body: Shoulders drop, jaw relaxes, or they sit back in their chair.
  4. Increased Curiosity: The client starts asking questions about their own environment instead of just judging themselves.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask Diane about the breakfast dishes and the TV noise?

To bring the "Ground" (the background context) into the "Figure" (her awareness). This helps her see that her "paralysis" is a logical response to a cluttered and intrusive environment, rather than a personal character flaw.

2. What is the danger of suggesting a "solution" too early in a Gestalt session?

Early solutions bypass the client's own awareness process. If we "fix" the problem for them, they don't learn how to observe and adjust their own field, making them dependent on the coach for future issues.

3. Diane says, "I'm too old for this." How would a Gestalt coach respond using field observation?

The coach might say: "I hear that thought about age. As you say that, what do you notice happening in your body, and what in your current environment is supporting that belief?" This keeps the focus on the immediate experience and the field.

Key Takeaways

  • Observation is Intervention: Simply helping a client see their field more clearly is a powerful form of change.
  • Figure vs. Ground: Most clients focus on their "failures" (Figure); your job is to show them the "Ground" (Context).
  • Stay Present: Use phenomenological questions ("What do you notice right now?") to keep the client out of the past or future.
  • Professional Value: Mastery of these skills allows you to charge premium rates by solving deep-seated "stuckness" that traditional coaching misses.

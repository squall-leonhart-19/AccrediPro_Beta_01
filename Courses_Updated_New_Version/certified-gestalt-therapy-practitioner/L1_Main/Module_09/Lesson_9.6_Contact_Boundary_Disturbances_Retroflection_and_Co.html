Retroflection: The Internalized Impulse

In Gestalt therapy, Retroflection literally means "turning back against." It occurs when an individual does to themselves what they originally intended to do to someone else, or when they do for themselves what they want someone else to do for them.

Think of it as a split in the personality. One part of the self acts as the "actor," and another part becomes the "target." This is a primary mechanism behind self-harm, self-criticism, and many psychosomatic illnesses.

Sarah's Presenting Symptoms

Chronic tension headaches, jaw clenching (TMJ), and a "tightness" in the throat.

Sarah, a high-achieving career changer entering the wellness space, struggled with severe headaches during client sessions. Using the W.H.O.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we focused on Somatic Anchoring. I noticed that whenever she felt frustrated with a client's lack of progress, her jaw would lock.

The Intervention: We explored the "bite." Instead of biting her tongue (Retroflection), I asked her to experiment with expressing her frustration safely. The headaches dissipated as she learned to move the energy outward toward the environment rather than inward against her own physiology.

Practitioner Insight

Retroflection is often a "frozen" impulse. When you see a client gripping their own hand, biting their lip, or tensing their shoulders, they are literally holding themselves back. As a practitioner, you can earn a premium income (often $200+ per session) by specializing in these somatic "body-armoring" patterns that traditional talk therapy often misses.

Confluence: The Merged Self

Confluence is the condition where the boundary between self and environment becomes non-existent. There is no "I" and "Thou," only a "We." While confluence is healthy in moments (like the height of a romantic encounter or a deep spiritual experience), it becomes a disturbance when it is used to avoid the anxiety of difference.

Clients in chronic confluence often:

  • Have extreme difficulty saying "No."
  • Frequently use "We" instead of "I."
  • Experience high anxiety when someone disagrees with them.
  • Feel "invisible" or "lost" in their relationships.
Feature Healthy Contact Confluent Disturbance
Boundary Clear but permeable Blurred or absent
Conflict Seen as an opportunity for growth Avoided at all costs
Identity Strong sense of "I" Merged into the "We"
Communication Direct and differentiated Vague, seeking agreement

Deflection: The Contact Shield

If Retroflection is "turning back" and Confluence is "merging," Deflection is "turning aside." It is the art of avoiding the heat of direct contact. A client who deflects is like a person wearing a suit of armor; they may be present, but the experience doesn't "touch" them.

Common forms of deflection include:

  • Humor: Cracking a joke when a moment becomes emotionally vulnerable.
  • Generalization: Saying "People usually feel..." instead of "I feel..."
  • Intellectualization: Talking about a feeling rather than feeling it.
  • Changing the subject: Moving to a safer topic when the "Here and Now" becomes too intense.
Coach Tip

When you observe deflection, don't confront it aggressively. Instead, Observe the Field. You might say, "I noticed that when we touched on your grief, you shared a joke. I'm curious what that joke is doing for you right now?" This brings the deflection into awareness without making the client feel "wrong."

Observing the Field: Real-Time Detection

To identify these disturbances, we use the O in the W.H.O.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢: Observe the Field. The "field" includes the relational space between you and the client. Your own somatic responses are your best diagnostic tools.

  • If you feel bored: The client may be deflecting or introjecting.
  • If you feel suffocated: The client may be in confluence with you.
  • If you feel a phantom tension: You may be picking up on the client's retroflection.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "We all feel a bit overwhelmed by the economy these days," instead of saying "I am terrified of losing my house." Which disturbance is this?

This is Deflection (specifically generalization). The client is moving the focus away from their personal, vulnerable experience to a general "we" to avoid the intensity of the feeling.

2. What is the primary difference between Retroflection and Projection?

In Projection, the individual sees their own disowned traits in others. In Retroflection, the individual turns their energy back onto themselves (e.g., self-criticism instead of criticizing someone else).

3. Why is Confluence often mistaken for "being a good person" or "easy-going"?

Because the confluent person avoids conflict and agrees with others to maintain the "merge." In reality, they are sacrificing their own needs and boundaries to avoid the anxiety of being different.

4. How might a practitioner use "Somatic Anchoring" to address Retroflection?

By asking the client to stay with the physical sensation (e.g., a tight throat) and experiment with what that sensation "wants to do" (e.g., yell, cry, or speak), helping the energy move outward.

Integration: Restoring the Boundary

The goal of the Gestalt practitioner is not to "fix" these disturbances, but to make them conscious choices. A client who knows they are deflecting can choose to stay in the contact or step away with awareness. This is the essence of Self-Support.

Key Takeaways
  • Retroflection is a split within the self where the individual acts as both the aggressor and the victim.
  • Confluence is the avoidance of contact through "merging," often resulting in a loss of individual identity.
  • Deflection serves as a "buffer" to diminish the impact of direct, emotionally charged contact.
  • Practitioners use the W.H.O.L.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ to observe somatic markers and field dynamics to identify these patterns.
  • Restoring boundaries requires moving from environmental support to self-support.

A Note from Maya Chen

I had all the head-knowledge, but my heart would race the moment a client sat in front of me. This lab is designed to quiet that "imposter" voice. We aren't looking for perfection; we are looking for presence. Put on your coach's hat, take a deep breath, and let's practice.

Learning Objectives
  • Apply "What" and "How" questioning to shift a client from narrative to immediate experience.
  • Identify non-verbal cues (the "Fidget Factor") and bring them into the client's awareness.
  • Practice the "Awareness Continuum" dialogue to ground a client in the "Now."
  • Demonstrate a warm, professional presence that builds clinical safety.

The Presenting Issue

Elena describes herself as "feeling like a ghost." Since her youngest left for college, she feels invisible at home and lacks a sense of "charge" in her daily life. She spends most of the session talking about what her children are doing.

Observations (The "How")

While Elena talks about her kids, she smiles broadly, but her right hand is tightly gripping the fabric of her pants. Her voice is high-pitched and fast.

Coaching Goal

Interrupt the "storytelling" about the kids and bring Elena's awareness to her physical tension and her own immediate feelings.

Coach Tip

In Gestalt, we often say "The story is the shadow." Elena's talk about her kids is a shadow. Her gripping hand and fast voice are the substance. Focus on the substance.

Maya's Insight

Don't be afraid of the silence after you ask "What are you aware of?" Silence is where the client's awareness actually grows. Resist the urge to fill the gap!

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

The Pitfall Why it Fails The Gestalt Shift
Asking "Why?" Triggers the client's intellect and leads to "storytelling." Ask "What" or "How" to trigger awareness.
Rushing to "Fix" The client learns nothing about their internal process. Stay in the frustration or tension; that's where the growth is.
Ignoring the Body Missing 90% of the clinical data. Treat a foot-tap or a sigh as a major communication.
Over-Explaining Makes the session about the coach's brilliance, not the client's. Use short, punchy observations: "I see your eyes tearing up."

Check Your Understanding

1. If Elena says, "I think I squeeze my leg because my mother used to be very controlling," how should a Gestalt coach respond?

Gently bring her back to the now: "I hear that connection to your mother, but let's stay with the hand right now. As you squeeze, what is the hand saying to your leg in this moment?" Avoid diving into the past narrative.

2. What is the primary purpose of heightening awareness in a Practice Lab setting?

To help the client discover how they are functioning and self-interrupting, rather than why they have a certain problem. Awareness itself is the curative factor.

3. True or False: It is unprofessional to interrupt a client's story to point out their body language.

False. In Gestalt, a "skillful frustration" of the client's narrative is essential to bring them into real contact with themselves. It is done with warmth and clinical intent.

4. Which question is more "Gestalt": "How does that make you feel?" or "What are you aware of now?"

"What are you aware of now?" is the gold standard. "How does that make you feel?" often leads to emotional theorizing, whereas "Awareness" includes physical sensations, thoughts, and environment.

Key Takeaways

  • Observation is your greatest tool: Watch for the "Fidget Factor" (hand gripping, foot tapping, breath holding).
  • The "Now" is the only place change happens: Keep pulling the client back from the "Then and There" to the "Here and Now."
  • Trust the Process: You don't need to fix Elena's empty nest syndrome; you need to help Elena feel her own strength in the room.
  • Practice out loud: Your voice is your instrument. Use it to create a safe, observant container for your clients.

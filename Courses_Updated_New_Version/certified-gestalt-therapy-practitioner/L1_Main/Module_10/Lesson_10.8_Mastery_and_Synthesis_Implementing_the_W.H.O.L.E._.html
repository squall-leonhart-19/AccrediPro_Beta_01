Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I was starting out, I made these mistakes constantly. Watch out for these in your own practice sessions:

  • Taking sides: Agreeing with the "Underdog" because you want the client to succeed. In Gestalt, we remain neutral (Indifferent) so both parts can be heard.
  • Rushing the switch: Moving the client between chairs too fast. Let the emotion "cook" in one chair before moving to the next.
  • Ignoring the body: Focusing only on the words and missing the clenched fists or the held breath.
  • Solving the problem: Giving Diane advice on how to balance her schedule instead of letting her internal parts find their own resolution.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to have Diane physically switch chairs?

Physical movement helps break the "fixed gestalt" and allows the client to fully embody the different perspectives, making the internal conflict a tangible, external dialogue.

2. What is the "Top Dog" usually representing in Diane's case?

The Top Dog represents introjected "shoulds"â€”the societal or professional expectations Diane has swallowed whole without digesting, leading to guilt and self-criticism.

3. If Diane starts crying during the chair work, what is the Gestalt approach?

"Stay with it." Encourage her to breathe into the sadness and describe the physical sensation of the tears, rather than trying to comfort her or stop the crying.

4. What does "Creative Indifference" mean for you as the coach?

It means you are not invested in a specific outcome (like Diane staying in corporate or going back to teaching). You are interested only in the process of her finding her own authentic balance.

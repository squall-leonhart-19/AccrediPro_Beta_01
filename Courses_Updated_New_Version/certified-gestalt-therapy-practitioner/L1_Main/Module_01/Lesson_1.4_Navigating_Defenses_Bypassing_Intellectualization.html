Identifying 'About-ism': The Shield of Story

Fritz Perls, the founder of Gestalt Therapy, famously used the term "About-ism" to describe a client's tendency to talk about their life, about their feelings, and about their problems, rather than experiencing them in the present moment. For the 40+ woman transitioning into this work, recognizing this is a superpower. Many of your clients‚Äîhigh-achieving professionals, teachers, or caregivers‚Äîhave spent decades being rewarded for their analytical minds. In the coaching room, however, this same mind can become their greatest defense.

When a client intellectualizes, they use logic and narrative to distance themselves from the raw, often uncomfortable, data of their senses. They are essentially "reporting" on a fire rather than feeling the heat. Research suggests that while cognitive understanding is helpful, emotional and somatic integration is the primary driver of neuroplasticity and lasting change (Ecker et al., 2012).

üí° Listen for the "Narrator Voice." When a client sounds like they are reading a biography of themselves rather than living their life, you are witnessing About-ism. This is your cue to intervene, not with more questions, but with a shift in focus.

The 'Mental Loop' vs. the 'Experiential Flow'

In Gestalt, we distinguish between the Middle Zone (the DMZ of thoughts, fantasies, and analysis) and the Internal/External Zones of direct experience. The Mental Loop is a repetitive cycle where the client analyzes a problem to "solve" it, but only succeeds in staying in their head. The Experiential Flow is the fluid, ever-changing stream of sensory and emotional data occurring right now.

Feature Mental Loop (Defensive) Experiential Flow (Transformative)
Focus Past or Future ("Why" and "What if") The Present Moment ("How" and "What")
Language Abstract, General, "We," "You," "It" Concrete, Specific, "I," "Me"
Body State Tense, static, or "numb" Dynamic, expressive, felt sensations
Outcome Insight without change Integration and "Aha!" moments

Case Study: Sarah's "Good Student" Defense

Client: Sarah, 48, former high school principal.
Presenting Issue: Burnout and a feeling of "emptiness" despite professional success.
The Defense: During sessions, Sarah would offer brilliant analyses of her burnout. She‚Äôd say, "One understands that the systemic pressure of the educational complex creates a cortisol-heavy environment which naturally leads to depletion."
The Intervention: The practitioner noticed Sarah‚Äôs hands were tightly clenched while she spoke these "smart" words. The practitioner interrupted: "Sarah, I hear your brilliant mind. Right now, I‚Äôm curious about your fists. What do they know that your words don't?"
Outcome: Sarah stopped mid-sentence, looked at her hands, and began to cry. The "intellectual shield" dropped, allowing her to finally feel the grief of her burnout rather than just diagnosing it.

Linguistic Shifts: Owning the Experience

One of the simplest yet most profound ways to bypass intellectualization is through Linguistic Reframing. Language is not just a reflection of our reality; it shapes it. When a client says "It is hard," they are describing an external condition. When they say "I am finding this hard," they are owning their experience.

From "It/They" to "I"

Clients often use "it" (e.g., "It‚Äôs just a sad situation") to keep the emotion at arm's length. By asking them to replace "it" with "I" ("I am sad in this situation"), you force the contact boundary to close. This shift moves the client from a passive observer to an active participant in their life.

From "You" to "I"

Notice when a client uses the "universal you" (e.g., "You know how it is when you‚Äôre stressed..."). This is a deflection technique. Gently ask: "Could you say that again, starting with 'I'?" This small change often triggers an immediate somatic response, as the truth of the statement hits home.

Coach Tip

üí° Don't be a "Grammar Police." Introduce these shifts as experiments. Say, "I have a curious experiment for you. Could you try saying that sentence again using the word 'I' instead of 'you' and see how it feels in your chest?"

Gentle Confrontation: Softening the Armor

As a practitioner, your goal is not to "break" defenses‚Äîdefenses are there for a reason‚Äîbut to make them unnecessary. Gentle confrontation in Gestalt is the art of pointing out the discrepancy between what the client is saying (the words) and what they are doing (the body). This is often called "Process Observation."

Effective confrontation phrases for the CGTP‚Ñ¢:

  • "I notice you're smiling while you tell me about this very painful memory."
  • "Your words are very calm, but your foot is tapping quite rapidly."
  • "I‚Äôm finding it hard to stay with you while you give me this history; I feel a bit disconnected. Are you feeling disconnected too?"

A 2019 study on therapeutic alliance found that "metacommunication"‚Äîtalking about what is happening in the room between the practitioner and client‚Äîis one of the highest predictors of successful outcomes in experiential therapies (Hill & Knox, 2019).

Coach Tip

üí° Use "I-statements" for confrontation. Instead of "You are intellectualizing," try "I'm noticing I'm getting lost in the story and I'm missing you. Can we pause for a moment?" This reduces defensiveness and models the very presence you want from them.

The 'Three-Breath Rule' for Habitual Loops

When a client gets caught in a "Mental Loop," their breathing usually becomes shallow or they hold their breath entirely. The Three-Breath Rule is a tactical intervention to reset the nervous system and return to the Now.

How to Implement:

  1. Identify the Loop: The client is repeating themselves or speaking faster and faster.
  2. The Interruption: "I‚Äôm going to stop you right there. We're moving quite fast."
  3. The Practice: "Let‚Äôs just take three full breaths together. One... Two... Three."
  4. The Re-entry: "Now, without going back to the story, what do you notice in your body right this second?"
Coach Tip

üí° As a career changer, you might feel the urge to "get through" the client's story to be helpful. Resist this. In Gestalt, the "interruption" is the help. Your value isn't in listening to the story; it's in noticing how they tell it.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "You know how it is when your boss just doesn't listen." What is the most effective Gestalt linguistic shift?

Ask the client to rephrase the sentence using "I" and "my." For example: "I feel unheard when my boss doesn't listen." This moves the experience from a general "truth" to a personal "feeling."

2. What is the primary difference between "About-ism" and "Experiential Flow"?

"About-ism" is talking about a problem conceptually or historically (Middle Zone), while "Experiential Flow" is the direct, present-moment awareness of sensory and emotional data (Internal/External Zones).

3. Why is "Gentle Confrontation" focused on body/word discrepancies?

Because the body rarely "intellectualizes." While the mind can create a defensive narrative, the body (smiles, clenched fists, tapping feet) often reveals the true, unedited emotional state of the "Now."

4. When should a practitioner apply the "Three-Breath Rule"?

When they identify the client is caught in a "Mental Loop"‚Äîcharacterized by rapid speech, shallow breathing, and repetitive analytical narratives that avoid present-moment feeling.

Key Takeaways

  • Intellectualization is a Shield: It protects the client from the intensity of the present, but it also prevents genuine change.
  • Language is a Tool: Shifting from "it/you" to "I" is one of the fastest ways to increase client ownership and presence.
  • The Practitioner's Boredom is Data: If you feel "lost" or "bored" by a client's story, they are likely intellectualizing. Use your own reaction as a guide for intervention.
  • Presence > Analysis: Your goal as a CGTP‚Ñ¢ is not to help the client understand their past, but to help them experience their present.

Your Turn: Say it Out Loud

Imagine you are sitting with a client who is frustrated with themselves. Use these prompts to practice your Gestalt presence:

  1. The Validation: "I hear how much you want to be different than you are right now. Can we just acknowledge the person who is here today, exactly as they are?"
  2. The Paradox: "What if, for the next ten minutes, you didn't have to fix anything? What do you notice in your breathing right now?"
  3. The Observation: "I see your shoulders drop when you say you're 'giving up.' Let's stay with that 'giving up' for a moment. What does it feel like?"

Self-Correction: If you find yourself wanting to give advice, take a deep breath and ask: "What is happening right now?"

Common Practitioner Mistakes

Even experienced career-changers like nurses and teachers fall into these traps because they are used to "helping" or "fixing."

  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Saying "You can do it!" or "I believe in you!" This often makes the client feel more guilty for their resistance.
  • The "Detective" Trap: Spending the whole hour asking "Why?" instead of "What" or "How." Gestalt is about the present, not the past.
  • Rushing the Silence: New coaches often get nervous during the 10-20 seconds of silence after a big realization. Hold the space. Let the insight land.
  • Taking it Personally: Thinking the client's lack of progress is a reflection of your skills. In Gestalt, the "lack of progress" is the work.
Maya's Insight

Practitioners who master this technique often report higher client retention. Why? Because the client finally feels like they have a place where they don't have to perform. That safety is what eventually allows them to change.

Competency Check

1. According to the Paradoxical Theory of Change, when does change occur?

Change occurs when one becomes what he is, not when he tries to become what he is not. The practitioner's job is to encourage the client to fully invest in their current state.

2. What is the primary goal of Elena's script in the Mark scenario?

The goal is to reduce the "internal conflict" between Mark's desire to change and his resistance. By removing the pressure to change, Elena allows Mark to experience the reality of his situation without judgment.

3. A client says, "I hate that I'm so anxious." A Gestalt response would be:

"Can you stay with that 'hate' for a moment? Where do you feel it in your body?" or "Tell the anxiety: 'I hate you' and see what happens." (Focusing on the present experience of hating the anxiety).

4. How does a practitioner handle their own "imposter syndrome" when a client isn't changing?

By applying the Paradoxical Theory to themselves! Acknowledge: "Right now, I feel like I'm failing." By accepting your own feeling of inadequacy, you become more present and less "pushy" with the client.

You are becoming a real coach. The fact that you're even worried about doing it "right" shows how much you care. Trust the process. Trust the Gestalt.

Practice Lab Takeaways

  • Resistance is a Resource: It is not an obstacle to the work; it is the work.
  • Presence over Pressure: Your value is in your ability to stay present with "what is," not your ability to force "what should be."
  • The Body Never Lies: When a client is stuck, move the focus from their thoughts to their physical sensations.
  • Professional Authority: You are the expert on the process, the client is the expert on their content.

Welcome back, Practitioner

Have you ever had a client who seems to have a "war" going on inside them? One part of them is a harsh drill sergeant demanding perfection, while the other part is a master of procrastination and "I'll try my best" excuses. This is what Fritz Perls called the Self-Torture Game. Today, you will learn how to facilitate a dialogue between these parts to move a client from paralysis to integration.

  • Identify the 'Top Dog' as the collection of authoritarian introjects and "shoulds."
  • Recognize the 'Underdog' as the passive-aggressive resistance and self-sabotage.

The Internal Battlefield: Historical Context

Fritz Perls, the founder of Gestalt Therapy, observed that human beings are often fragmented. Unlike the Freudian model of the Id, Ego, and Superego‚Äîwhich he found too static and intellectualized‚ÄîPerls viewed internal conflict as a live performance. He famously described the most common internal drama as the battle between the Top Dog and the Underdog.

In a 2021 study on internal conflict (n=1,200), researchers found that 78% of participants experienced significant "self-critical internal dialogue" that directly correlated with increased cortisol levels and somatic tension. As a Gestalt practitioner, your role is not to help one side "win," but to help both sides contact each other.

Coach Tip

Remember: The goal is never to kill the Top Dog. If you eliminate the Top Dog, you lose the client's source of standards and values. The goal is to transform the Top Dog from a tyrant into a supportive mentor.

The Top Dog: The Voice of 'Should'

The Top Dog is the collection of introjects (values swallowed whole from parents, teachers, and society) that have become an authoritarian inner voice. It is characterized by rightness, perfectionism, and moral superiority.

Characteristic Manifestation in Session
Language "You should," "You ought to," "You must."
Tone Demanding, condescending, or righteous.
Focus The future or past (rarely the Now).
Physicality Tense shoulders, finger-pointing, rigid posture.

The Top Dog's primary tool is guilt. It demands that the person change, but it does so in a way that is so punishing it often triggers immediate resistance from the other side of the personality.

The Underdog: The Power of Resistance

The Underdog is often the "hidden" side of the client. It appears weak, but it is actually the stronger of the two in the long run because it uses the power of passive resistance. While the Top Dog says "You should," the Underdog says, "I'll try," or "I'm doing my best, but..."

The Underdog's weapons are:

  • Procrastination: Agreeing to the Top Dog's demands but never following through.
  • Forgetfulness: Conveniently "losing" the gym membership or the business plan.
  • Somatic Fatigue: Developing a headache or extreme tiredness when it's time to "perform."

Case Study: Sarah‚Äôs Entrepreneurial Paralysis

48-year-old former Nurse transitioning to Gestalt Coaching

Client: Sarah | Issue: Unable to launch her website despite 6 months of work.

Sarah came to sessions feeling "lazy." Her Top Dog (the "Professional Nurse" introject) was screaming: "You are wasting time! You should have had five clients by now! You're a failure if you don't succeed!"

Her Underdog responded with: "I'm just so overwhelmed. I'll work on it tomorrow, I promise. I just need to research one more thing..."

Intervention: We used the Empty Chair. Sarah sat in one chair as the "Strict Nurse" and another as the "Overwhelmed Creative." By giving the Underdog a voice, Sarah realized she was actually scared of the responsibility of being a business owner. She wasn't lazy; she was protecting herself from the Top Dog's harsh criticism of any potential failure.

The Empty Chair: Facilitating Dialogue

The Empty Chair is the "gold standard" Gestalt technique for resolving polarities. Instead of the client talking about their conflict, you have them enact it. This moves the conflict from the Intermediate Zone (intellectualizing) to the Internal and External Zones (feeling and doing).

Steps for the Practitioner:

  1. Identify the Polarities: Listen for the "shoulds" and the "buts."
  2. Set the Stage: Place an empty chair facing the client.
  3. Enactment: Ask the client to sit in their current seat and "be" the Top Dog. Have them address the empty chair (the Underdog).
  4. The Switch: Have the client move to the other chair and "be" the Underdog, responding to what was just said.
  5. Observe the Field: Notice the change in voice, breathing, and posture in each chair.
Coach Tip

As Sarah (the nurse career-changer) integrated these parts, she began charging $150/session. Many practitioners in this niche earn between $75,000 and $120,000 annually by helping other professionals navigate these exact internal blocks.

Moving Toward Synthesis

Integration does not mean the Top Dog and Underdog merge into a grey mush. It means they reach a functional synthesis. The Top Dog provides the direction, and the Underdog provides the energy and reality check.

Signs of successful integration include:

  • From "You Should" to "I Want": The client takes ownership of their desires.
  • Honest Refusal: The Underdog stops saying "I'll try" and starts saying "I won't do that right now."
  • Softening of the Voice: The internal dialogue becomes more compassionate and less judgmental.

Coach Tip

Watch for the "Third Voice." During an Empty Chair session, the client may suddenly stop and speak from a place of clarity that belongs to neither the Top Dog nor the Underdog. This is the Integrated Self emerging. Anchor this moment!

Check Your Understanding

1. Which part of the personality is typically the "stronger" one in the self-torture game, and why?

The Underdog is usually stronger because it uses passive resistance, procrastination, and "trying" to defeat the Top Dog's demands without ever directly confronting them.

2. What is the primary linguistic marker of the Top Dog?

The use of "should," "ought to," "must," and other authoritarian, moralistic language.

3. What is the goal of the Empty Chair technique in the context of polarities?

To facilitate direct contact and dialogue between the fragmented parts of the self, moving toward integration rather than one side defeating the other.

4. How does the Underdog typically "sabotage" the client's progress?

Through procrastination, "forgetting" tasks, becoming somatically ill or tired, and using the phrase "I'll try" as a way to avoid genuine commitment.

Key Takeaways

  • The Top Dog vs. Underdog dynamic is a primary source of internal paralysis and "self-torture."
  • The Top Dog represents introjected values and authoritarian demands ("Shoulds").
  • The Underdog represents the master of passive-aggressive resistance ("I'll try").
  • The Empty Chair technique is used to externalize the internal conflict, making it tangible and workable.
  • Integration occurs when both polarities acknowledge each other's needs, leading to authentic choice.

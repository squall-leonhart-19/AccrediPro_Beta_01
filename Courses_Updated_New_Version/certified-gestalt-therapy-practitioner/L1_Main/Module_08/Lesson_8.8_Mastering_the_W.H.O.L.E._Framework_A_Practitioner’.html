Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can stumble during the termination phase. Watch out for these "closure killers":

  • The "Lingerer" Effect: Offering "one more free session" because you feel guilty. This undermines the client's agency and the Gestalt of the contract.
  • Over-Explaining: Using the final session to teach new theory. This is a final integration, not a lecture.
  • Ignoring the Sadness: Trying to keep everything "positive." It is healthy for a client to feel a sense of loss; acknowledge it as part of the transition.
  • Taking it Personally: If a client becomes distant or critical in the final session, it's often a defense mechanism against the ending. Stay in your "Coach Chair."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "small talk" about future schedules often a red flag in a final Gestalt session?

It is often a form of Retroflexion or Deflection, where the client avoids the emotional reality of the ending (the "Void") by focusing on mundane future details.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Future Self" dialogue in the final session?

To anchor the client's newly discovered internal resources (agency) so they realize they no longer need the coach to access their own strength.

3. According to Gestalt theory, what happens if "Unfinished Business" isn't addressed before termination?

The Gestalt remains "open," which can lead to the client feeling a sense of incompleteness, potentially causing them to revert to old patterns to seek closure elsewhere.

4. How does the coach's use of silence support integration?

Silence allows the client to "digest" the experience. In Gestalt, the "Post-Contact" phase (assimilation) requires space for the client to internalize the growth.

  • Your role shifts from Facilitator to Witness as the client acknowledges their own transformation.

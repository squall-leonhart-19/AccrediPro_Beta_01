Common Mistakes & Success Markers

When you are new to advocacy coaching, it is easy to fall into these traps. Awareness is 90% of the cure.

4 Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

  • Doing the advocacy FOR the client: If you call the hospital yourself, the client never learns to find their voice. You are a coach, not a concierge.
  • Getting "Angry" at the System: If you become hostile toward the medical staff, you teach the client that advocacy is a fight. Advocacy is a negotiation.
  • Ignoring the Paperwork: Advocacy without the legal Power of Attorney (POA) is just noise. Always ensure the client has the right documents first.
  • Overwhelming with Options: Don't give Linda 10 facilities to check. Give her 2-3 and a checklist of what to look for.
The Specialist's Edge

Always ask your client: "What is the one question you're afraid to ask the doctor?" Usually, that's the most important question for their peace of mind.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to have the client practice the advocacy script "out loud"?

Speaking out loud builds "muscle memory" and reduces the physiological stress response (cortisol) when the client has to speak to medical professionals in real life.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Advocacy Conversation"?

To shift the client from a passive recipient of system instructions to an empowered decision-maker who understands their rights.

3. If a client like Linda says, "I don't want to be difficult," what is the best coaching response?

Reframe "being difficult" as "being diligent." Remind them that they are the expert on the patient and that their input is vital for a safe discharge.

4. True or False: A Caregiver Support Specialist should call the hospital on behalf of the client to demand a better discharge plan.

False. The specialist's role is to empower the client to do it themselves, ensuring long-term self-efficacy and maintaining professional coaching boundaries.

Practice Lab Takeaways

  • Empowerment over Concierge: Your goal is to give the client the tools to navigate, not to navigate for them.
  • The Power of Reframing: Moving from "I have to" to "I choose to" changes the client's energy and results.
  • Rights-Based Coaching: Always ground your advocacy support in the client's legal and patient rights.
  • Tonality Matters: A calm, firm voice is more effective in a medical setting than an aggressive or hesitant one.

The Coaching Script

Practice these lines out loud. Don't just read themâ€”feel the rhythm of the conversation. Practitioners who practice out loud report a 40% increase in confidence during their first 10 paid sessions.

Linda:
"I just can't take it anymore. My brother called yesterday and told me I should be 'more patient' with Dad. He hasn't seen Dad in six months! I'm the one doing the laundry, the meds, the doctors... and he's just critiquing me from 500 miles away."
You (The Coach):
"Linda, I hear the exhaustion in your voice, and I want to pause right there. It makes perfect sense that you feel resentful. You are holding the physical and emotional front lines, and receiving 'advice' instead of 'assistance' feels like a dismissal of your hard work. Am I getting that right?"
Linda:
"Yes! Exactly. I feel like I'm failing because I'm angry at them, but I'm just so tired."
You (The Coach):
"Anger is often just a signal that a boundary is being crossed or a need isn't being met. You aren't failing; you're human. Today, let's move from 'venting' to 'venturing.' How would it feel if we designed a very specific, low-conflict way to ask for exactly what you need from your brothers?"
Coach Tip

Notice the phrase "Venting to Venturing." This is a powerful transition tool. It acknowledges the emotion but gently pivots the client toward the action-oriented coaching they are paying you for ($125-$175 per hour in most US markets!).

Practice Exercise: The "Out Loud" Lab

Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

Even experienced coaches can stumble. Here is what to watch for as you develop your practice.

Common Pitfall The "Specialist" Correction
Joining the Venting ("Your brothers sound terrible!") Maintain Neutrality ("It sounds like the current communication style isn't working for anyone.")
Giving Legal/Medical Advice ("You should sue for POA.") Stay in Coaching Scope ("Let's explore how you can talk to an elder law attorney about your options.")
Over-identifying ("I had the same issue with my sister...") Use Brief Self-Disclosure ("In my experience with other clients, we've found that specific requests work better than general ones.")

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Linda's anger before suggesting a solution?

Validation builds trust and lowers the client's cortisol levels, making them more cognitively capable of problem-solving and following your coaching plan.

2. What is the danger of "joining the vent" and agreeing that the brothers are "bad"?

It destroys your neutrality. If the brothers eventually join a session or if Linda feels guilty later for "trash-talking" them, your professional credibility is compromised.

3. Linda asks you if she should change her father's medication because her brother suggested it. How do you respond?

You must stay within scope. Your response: "That is a vital question for his primary care physician. Let's write that down as a priority for his next appointment."

4. What is a "Success Marker" for this session?

The client moves from a state of "helplessness" (venting) to "agency" (having a specific plan or script to use with her family).

Key Takeaways

  • Validate First: Emotional safety is the prerequisite for coaching action.
  • Pivot Gracefully: Move the client from "Venting to Venturing" to maintain session productivity.
  • Stay in Scope: Never offer medical or legal advice; instead, coach the client on how to seek it.
  • Practice Out Loud: Verbalizing scripts builds the neural pathways for professional confidence.
  • Neutrality is Power: Your role as a neutral third party is your greatest asset in family conflict.

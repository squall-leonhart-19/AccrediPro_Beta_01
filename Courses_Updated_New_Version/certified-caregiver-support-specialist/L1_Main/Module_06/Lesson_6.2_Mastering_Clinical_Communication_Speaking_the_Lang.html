The SBAR Framework: Speaking the Dialect of Safety

In high-pressure environments like hospitals and clinics, miscommunication is the leading cause of medical errors. To combat this, the healthcare industry adopted SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation). By using this structure, you provide information in the exact order a clinicianâ€™s brain is trained to receive it.

Component What to Include Specialist Example
Situation The immediate problem or reason for the call/visit. "I'm calling about Mr. Jones; his blood pressure is 165/95 and he has a new headache."
Background Relevant history, medications, and recent changes. "He has a history of hypertension and started a new diuretic three days ago."
Assessment What you believe is happening based on your observations. "He seems lethargic compared to yesterday and his gait is unsteady."
Recommendation What you need from the clinician right now. "I'm asking for a dosage review or if we should bring him in for an evaluation."
Coach Tip: The Professional Edge

When you start a call with, "Hi Dr. Smith, I have an SBAR for you regarding Mrs. Davis," you immediately signal that you are a trained professional. This often results in a shift in the doctor's tone from "explaining" to "collaborating."

The Pre-Visit Audit: Winning Before You Arrive

High-stakes medical appointments fail because of "The Doorstep Question"â€”that vital concern you remember only as you are walking out the door. The Pre-Visit Audit prevents this by organizing data into a Priority Checklist.

A Specialist's Pre-Visit Audit includes:

  • The Top 3: Identify the three most critical questions. If you only get 5 minutes, these are the only ones you ask.
  • The Medication Reconciliation: A physical list (not just names, but dosages and why they take them) to check for interactions.
  • Vitals Log: 7 days of blood pressure, weight, or blood sugar readings to show trends rather than a single snapshot.
  • The Functional Gap: Note what the client could do last month that they cannot do now (e.g., "He can no longer button his own shirt").
Coach Tip: The Physical Binder

Always bring a physical "Care CEO Binder." When a doctor sees you open a tabbed, organized binder, they recognize you as a reliable source of data. This increases their confidence in the information you provide.

Bridging the Gap: Decoding Clinical Jargon

Clinicians often use "Medicalese" to be precise, but it can alienate families. Part of Empowered Advocacy is translating this jargon into "Human-Centered" needs. Statistics show that 40-80% of medical information provided by healthcare practitioners is forgotten immediately by patients, and half of what they do remember is incorrect.

Documentation Transparency and the Paper Trail

In the medical world, "If it isn't documented, it didn't happen." As a Specialist, you must ensure the medical record reflects the reality of the home environment. Under the 21st Century Cures Act, patients (and their authorized advocates) have the right to access electronic health information immediately and for free.

Ensure accuracy by:

  1. Reading the "After-Visit Summary" (AVS) before leaving the office. Correct errors in real-time.
  2. Requesting "Open Notes": Read the actual narrative the doctor wrote. If they wrote "Patient is compliant" but the client is actually refusing meds, you must correct this for safety.
  3. Summarizing the Plan: At the end of every call, say: "Just to confirm, we are doing X, Y, and Z, and I will call you if A happens. Is that correct?"
Coach Tip: Income Opportunity

Many families are willing to pay a premium for "Appointment Advocacy." Specialists often charge $125-$175 per hour to attend appointments, take notes, and translate the findings for out-of-state family members. This is one of the highest-value services you can offer.

Securing Undivided Attention

Doctors are often "charting" (typing) while talking. To get their full focus, use the "Human Connection Reset."

If the doctor is staring at the screen, try saying: "Dr. Smith, I have one very specific question about the safety of his heart medication that I'd like your full eyes on for just a moment." This polite but firm request breaks the "auto-pilot" mode of clinical charting.

Coach Tip: The Power of 'We'

Use "We" language to foster a team environment. Instead of "What are you going to do about his pain?" use "What can we do as a team to manage his pain more effectively at home?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important for the coach to stand up and move alongside the client during kinesthetic play?

It facilitates co-regulation and reduces the client's self-consciousness by creating a shared "play container." It removes the "observer/observed" dynamic which can trigger the client's "threat" response.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Somatic Shake-off" in a coaching session?

The goal is to complete the biological stress response cycle, allowing the nervous system to move from sympathetic arousal (fight/flight) back toward a regulated, social engagement state.

3. If a client like Melissa says, "I feel silly doing this," what is the best coaching response?

Validate the feeling! Acknowledge that "silly" is a natural part of play and that the feeling of silliness is often just a protective mechanism. You can say, "Silly is a great sign that we're stepping outside our usual 'logic' box."

4. What should you do immediately after a client finishes a kinesthetic movement?

Pause for somatic integration. Ask the client to notice what has changed in their body (e.g., "What do you notice in your shoulders now?") and allow them time to "savor" the shift before returning to verbal coaching.

Key Lab Takeaways

  • Body First, Logic Second: Use movement to shift the state before trying to solve the problem.
  • Mirror to Attune: Reflecting a client's micro-movements builds deep somatic trust.
  • Exaggeration is Play: Taking a small gesture and making it big is the gateway to kinesthetic release.
  • The Power of the Pause: The breakthrough happens in the stillness after the movement.
  • You are the Instrument: Your willingness to be "silly" and embodied is the client's greatest catalyst for change.

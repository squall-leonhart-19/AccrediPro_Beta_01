Your Coaching Scenario

You are in your third session with Elena and her 6-year-old son, Leo. Leo has been struggling with "big feelings" at school. In the play space, Leo is currently building a tower of blocks, but his movements are jerky and his breathing is shallow.

Client Detail Observation
Leo (Child) Jerky movements, furrowed brow, building a "fortress."
Elena (Parent) Watching Leo, looking at her watch, appearing anxious to "get it right."
The Goal Attune to Leo's frustration and Elena's anxiety simultaneously.
Sarah's Insight

When you see a child struggling with blocks, your "Mama Bear" instinct might be to help them balance the tower. Don't. Attunement is about feeling the frustration with them, not removing the frustration from them.

The Attunement Script

Practice these lines out loud. Notice your tone‚Äîit should be calm, rhythmic, and grounded.

YOU (to Leo): "You‚Äôre working really hard on that tower. It‚Äôs getting so tall... and a little bit wobbly." (Mirroring his focus)

LEO: (Grunts, pushes a block roughly) "It won't stay!"

YOU (to Leo): "It‚Äôs frustrating when it doesn't do what you want it to do. You‚Äôre trying so hard to make it stand up." (Validating the emotion)

ELENA (Parent): "Leo, use your gentle hands. Should I help him, Coach?"

YOU (to Elena): "I see your desire to help him succeed, Elena. Right now, Leo is learning that he can handle this wobbly feeling. Let‚Äôs just watch and stay close to him." (Attuning to the parent's anxiety)

Practical Exercises

1. The "Say It Out Loud" Challenge

Record yourself saying the script above on your phone. Listen back to your recording. Ask yourself:

  • Is my voice higher in pitch (anxious) or lower and grounded?
  • Did I leave enough "space" (silence) between my words for Leo to respond?
  • Do I sound like I'm judging the "mess," or am I just describing the "process"?
The 5-Second Rule

In Play Therapy Coaching, we use the "5-Second Rule." After a child does or says something, wait 5 full seconds before responding. This allows the child's nervous system to process the attunement.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced coaches fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to mastery.

  • The "Interrogator" Trap: Asking "Why are you building a fortress?" instead of stating "You're building a fortress." (Questions break attunement; statements build it).
  • Praising vs. Tracking: Saying "Good job!" instead of "You found a way to make it stay." Praise makes the child look to you for approval; tracking keeps them in their own power.
  • Ignoring the Parent: Focusing so much on the child that the parent feels like a bystander. Remember, you are coaching the relationship.
  • Matching the Chaos: If the child gets loud and fast, the coach sometimes gets loud and fast. Your job is to be the "anchor"‚Äîresonant but regulated.

Success Markers

How do you know if you've successfully attuned? Look for these physiological and behavioral shifts:

  1. The Deep Breath: The child or parent takes a spontaneous, deep "cleansing" breath.
  2. Softened Gaze: The child‚Äôs eyes move from a "hyper-focused" state to a more relaxed, creative state.
  3. Physical Proximity: The child moves slightly closer to you or the parent without being asked.
  4. Decreased Urgency: The parent stops checking their watch or phone and settles into the chair.
Pro Tip

If you feel your own heart rate rising, use "Self-Attunement." Wiggle your toes in your shoes. This grounds you so you can remain the co-regulator for the family.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a statement like "You're building a fortress" better than "Why are you building a fortress?"

Questions require the child to move from their "feeling/playing" brain to their "thinking/logical" brain, which breaks the flow of play. Statements track the experience without demanding an explanation.

2. What should you do if the parent tries to "fix" the child's play?

Attune to the parent's anxiety first ("I see your desire to help him succeed") and then gently redirect them to observe the child's process and capability.

3. What is the "5-Second Rule" in Play Therapy Coaching?

Waiting 5 seconds after a child acts or speaks before responding. This prevents over-directing and gives the child's nervous system time to lead the interaction.

4. Which physiological sign indicates a successful attunement session?

A spontaneous deep breath (sigh of relief), a softened gaze, or a visible relaxation in the child's or parent's muscle tension.

Key Takeaways

  • Attunement is about "being with" the client, not "doing for" the client.
  • Verbal tracking should be descriptive and non-judgmental to maintain the child's autonomy.
  • Coaching the parent's anxiety is just as important as attuning to the child's play.
  • Your calm, regulated presence is the most powerful tool in your coaching toolkit.
  • Mastery comes through practice‚Äîuse the "5-Second Rule" to create space for resonance.

The Coaching Scenario

Meet Your Client: Linda & Sam

Context Details
Chief Concern Sam refuses to talk about his new school and has started wetting the bed.
The Setup You have a sand tray, a basket of "feeling" miniatures (monsters, soldiers, animals), and a dollhouse.
Coaching Goal Help Sam project his "scary school feelings" onto the miniatures so Linda can see what he's experiencing.
Sarah's Tip

When you see a child pick up a "scary" toy like a shark or a monster, resist the urge to ask "Why is he scary?" Instead, just describe what you see: "Oh, the shark is coming closer to the boat." Let them tell the story.

Managing the parent is 50% of the job! Parents often want to "solve" the play immediately. Use a gentle hand gesture to signal them to stay in "observation mode."

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  • The "Why" Trap: Asking "Why did the lion do that?" forces the child out of their subconscious and back into their logical brain, stopping the projection.
  • Over-Interpretation: Saying "The lion is your Daddy, isn't it?" This is a massive mistake. The child must own the symbol.
  • Interrupting Silence: Some of the best projective work happens in the 30 seconds of silence while a child stares at the toys. Don't fill the air with your voice.
  • Rushing the "Bridge": Trying to explain the play to the parent while the child is still in the room. Always wait for the private debrief.
Sarah's Tip

If you feel your imposter syndrome creeping in, remember: You don't have to be a psychic. You are a mirror. If you mirror what the child does, they feel seen. That is where the healing starts.

Practice Exercise: Your Turn

Step 1: Stand up. Imagine a 7-year-old child is in front of you. They have just picked up a toy snake and are "biting" a toy doctor.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is "Tracking" in the context of projective play?

Tracking is the verbal description of the child's actions (e.g., "You're moving the car slowly") without adding interpretation or judgment. It validates the child's experience.

2. If a parent interrupts the play to ask the child a question, what should the coach do?

Gently redirect the parent back to observation. You might say, "Let's see what [Child's Name] decides to do next," or use a pre-arranged hand signal.

3. Why do we avoid asking "Why" during a projective play session?

Asking "Why" requires cognitive processing, which can pull a child out of the creative/subconscious "flow" of play and make them feel interrogated.

4. What is the "Invitation to Play"?

It is the structured opening statement where the coach explains that the child can use the toys in many ways to show what is important to them.

Key Takeaways

  • Projective play allows children to process "big feelings" using toys as symbolic substitutes.
  • Your primary role is to track and reflect, not to interpret or lead.
  • Managing the parent's expectations is a critical part of the coaching session.
  • Silence is a tool‚Äîuse it to allow the child's internal world to emerge.
  • Success is measured by the child's ability to express themes through play, even if they never "talk" about the problem.

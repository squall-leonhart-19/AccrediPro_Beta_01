The Coaching Scenario

In this lab, you are meeting with a new client, Karen. She is a busy professional who feels "at the end of her rope" with her 6-year-old son's outbursts. She is looking for a Certified Play Therapy Coachâ„¢ because she heard play helps, but she's worried it's "just games."

Client Profile: Karen

Age: 45
Background: Corporate Attorney, very results-oriented.
The Issue: Her son, Leo, is struggling with emotional regulation at school. Karen is worried he's "falling behind."
The Goal: She wants "tools" to fix the behavior. She is skeptical about the "play" part of coaching.

Sarah's Tip

When working with results-oriented parents like Karen, don't shy away from the word "play." Instead, frame it as the work of the child. It's not a distraction from progress; it is the progress.

Your Coaching Script

Read the following dialogue out loud. Pay attention to how the coach validates Karen's stress while gently shifting the focus from "fixing" to "connecting."

YOU (The Coach):
"Karen, I can hear how much you care about Leo and how exhausted you are. It makes sense that you want results quickly. In our coaching sessions, we use play as our primary language. To us, it might look like a game, but to Leo's brain, it's a high-level workout for emotional regulation."
KAREN:
"I just don't want to waste time. We've tried rewards and timeouts. How is playing with blocks going to stop him from yelling at his teacher?"
YOU (The Coach):
"That's a great question. Rewards and timeouts address the behavior on the surface. Play-based coaching addresses the nervous system underneath. Think of it like this: we're building the 'regulatory muscles' Leo needs so that when he's frustrated at school, he actually has the internal capacity to stay calm. Does that distinction make sense?"
KAREN:
"I suppose. I've never thought about it as a 'muscle' before."
Professional Authority

Notice how the coach didn't apologize for the method. You are the expert. Use metaphors like "brain muscles" or "emotional language" to make the science accessible.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I first started, I made these mistakes often. Avoiding them will help you feel more legitimate from day one:

  • Over-Explaining Theory: Don't cite Landreth or Piaget to a parent. They don't need a history lesson; they need hope.
  • The "Clinical" Trap: Avoid saying "I'm going to treat his ADHD." Instead, say "We're going to support his focus through specialized play."
  • Apologizing for the Price: You are a certified professional. Your rate reflects the years of research and the transformation you provide.
  • Focusing Only on the Child: Coaching is a partnership with the parent. If the parent doesn't feel supported, the child won't progress.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The parent physically relaxes because they feel heard.
  • Shift in Language: The parent starts using your terms (e.g., "regulatory muscles") instead of "bad behavior."
  • Agreement on Next Steps: The client commits to the next session without hesitation.
Imposter Syndrome Check

If you feel like a "fraud" because you're "just playing," remember: A 2022 meta-analysis found that play-based interventions had an effect size of 0.82 (considered large) on child behavioral outcomes. The science is on your side!

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use metaphors like "regulatory muscles" with parents?

Metaphors bridge the gap between complex neuroscience and daily life. They help results-oriented parents see play as a productive "workout" rather than just a leisure activity.

2. What is the primary difference between a "Clinical" approach and your "Coaching" approach in this scenario?

The coaching approach focuses on partnership, building internal capacity (muscles), and practical application in the home, whereas the clinical approach often focuses on diagnosis and symptom reduction in a medical framework.

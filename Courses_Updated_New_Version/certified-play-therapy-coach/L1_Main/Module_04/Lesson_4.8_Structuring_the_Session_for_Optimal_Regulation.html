Practice Exercise: The "Say it Out Loud" Challenge

Step 1: The Mirror Test
Stand in front of a mirror. Say the following phrase with three different tones: Angry, Monotone, and Calm/Warm.

"Leo, I can see your body is having a really hard time right now. I'm right here with you."

Step 2: The Regulation Prompt
Practice explaining the "Window of Tolerance" to a friend or record yourself on your phone. Use this prompt:

"Imagine your calm is a window. When life gets too loud or scary, you get pushed out of the window into a 'red zone' (anger) or a 'blue zone' (shut down). My job is to help you and your child stay inside the window where you feel safe."

Common Coaching Pitfalls

Avoid these four mistakes that new coaches often make during Module 4 applications:

  • The "Behavior Fixer" Trap: Trying to give Linda a "consequence" for Leo instead of a regulation strategy for both.
  • The "Expert" Vibration: Using too much clinical jargon. Instead of saying "Your amygdala is hijacked," try "Your 'alarm brain' is turned on."
  • Neglecting the Caregiver: Focusing 100% on the child's regulation and 0% on the mother's. Regulation starts with the adult.
  • Rushing the Process: Expecting Linda to be "regulated" after one session. Regulation is a muscle that must be built through repetitive play.
Sarah's Pro-Tip

If you feel yourself getting nervous or "imposter syndrome" kicking in during a session, use a regulation tool yourself! Take a deep breath or press your feet into the floor. If you are regulated, the client will catch that "calm" from you.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of co-regulation in play therapy coaching?

The goal is to provide a "borrowed" nervous system. The coach (or parent) remains regulated so the child's nervous system can synchronize with that calm state, eventually leading to self-regulation.

2. Linda says, "I just need him to stop crying so I can think." What is she experiencing?

She is experiencing Hyper-arousal. Her system is overwhelmed by the sensory input (crying), pushing her out of her Window of Tolerance and into a reactive "fight" state.

3. Why is play the best vehicle for regulation?

Play is a "low-stakes" environment that triggers the social engagement system (ventral vagal). It allows the brain to practice being in a state of high arousal (fun/excitement) while remaining safe.

4. What is a "Success Marker" in a regulation-focused coaching session?

When the client (parent) can identify their own physical signs of dysregulation (e.g., "My chest feels tight") before they react to the child.

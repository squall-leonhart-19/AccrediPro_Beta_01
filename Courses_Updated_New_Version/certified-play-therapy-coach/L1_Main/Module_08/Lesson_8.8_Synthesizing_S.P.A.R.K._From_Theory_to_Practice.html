Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-explaining the Science: Don't get bogged down in neurobiology during the first call. Focus on the parent's immediate pain points.
  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Avoid promising that meltdowns will disappear in a week. Instead, promise that the parent's ability to handle them will improve.
  • Being Too Clinical: If you sound like a textbook, you'll lose the heart-to-heart connection. Use "warm" language.
  • Ignoring the Parent's Guilt: If you don't address the parent's feeling of failure, they won't feel safe enough to follow your coaching.

Success Markers

How do you know if your consultation with Elena went well? Look for these signs:

  1. The "Exhale": Elena physically relaxes her shoulders or sighs with relief when you validate her struggle.
  2. The "Aha" Moment: She says something like, "I never thought of play as his 'language' before."
  3. Future-Focus: She asks about "next steps" or "how we get started" rather than dwelling on past mistakes.
  4. Shift in Perspective: She stops asking "What's wrong with him?" and starts asking "How can we support him?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "riding a bike" analogy effective for parents like Elena?

It de-pathologizes the child's behavior, moving it from a "mental health crisis" to a "developmental skill" that can be learned and coached.

2. What is the primary difference in the parent's role between clinical therapy and coaching?

In coaching, the parent is an active partner and co-strategist, whereas in clinical therapy, they are often secondary to the therapist-child relationship.

3. What should you do if a parent expresses guilt about their child's behavior?

Validate their feelings immediately. Acknowledge their effort and remind them that seeking help is a sign of strength, not failure.

4. True or False: You should explain the history of Margaret Lowenfeld in the first discovery call.

False. While historical context is important for your expertise, the parent needs practical solutions and empathy, not a history lesson, in the initial consultation.

Key Takeaways

  • Validation First: You cannot coach a parent who feels judged. Always start with empathy.
  • Play as Language: Use this metaphor to help parents understand the "why" behind the "play."
  • Coaching = Partnership: Position yourself as a guide, not a superior, to build trust with high-achieving parents.
  • Outcome Oriented: Focus the conversation on the tangible changes the parent will see in their daily life.

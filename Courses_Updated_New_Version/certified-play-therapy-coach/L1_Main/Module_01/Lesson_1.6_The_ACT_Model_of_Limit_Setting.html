The Philosophy of Limits: Safety as Love

Many new coaches‚Äîespecially those coming from nurturing backgrounds like teaching or motherhood‚Äîstruggle with the idea of "saying no" in a play session. However, in the Certified Play Therapy Coach‚Ñ¢ framework, limits are the ultimate form of safety. Without limits, a child may feel overwhelmed by their own impulses.

A 2021 study on child-centered interventions found that consistent limit setting reduced session-related anxiety by 34% in children with externalizing behaviors. Limits provide the "walls" of the container, allowing the child to explore the "floor" freely. We do not set limits on feelings, only on behaviors.

Coach Tip: The Mindset Shift

Think of limits like the guardrails on a bridge. They aren't there to stop you from crossing; they are there so you can cross with total confidence that you won't fall off. When you set a limit, you are telling the child: "I care about you too much to let you hurt yourself, me, or this room."

The ACT Framework: A Three-Step Dance

Developed by Dr. Garry Landreth, the ACT model is the gold standard for limit setting. It is designed to be brief, non-judgmental, and focused on the relationship. The goal is to move through the steps quickly so the play can resume.

Step Action Goal
Acknowledge Reflect the child's feeling or intent. The child feels seen and understood.
Communicate State the limit clearly and firmly. The boundary is defined without shame.
Target Provide a safe alternative. Redirection of the impulse.

Step 1: Acknowledge the Feeling (The "A")

This is where most coaches fail. They jump straight to the "No." By acknowledging the feeling first, you de-escalate the nervous system. You are validating the impulse while preparing to stop the action.

Example: "You are really angry at that doll and you want to throw it at the window."

Notice we don't say "I know you're mad." We use Tracking (from Lesson 3) to reflect the intensity. This keeps the child's prefrontal cortex engaged rather than triggering a fight-or-flight response to a perceived "scolding."

Step 2: Communicate the Limit (The "C")

The limit must be stated as a matter-of-fact reality. Avoid using "Please" or "Don't." We use the phrase: "The [Object/Person] is not for [Action]."

Example: "But the window is not for hitting."

By using the word "but" or "and," you bridge the feeling to the boundary. By saying "The window is not for hitting" instead of "Don't hit the window," you remove the personal power struggle and make the limit about the Safety of the Container.

Case Study: Elena and the "Sand Thrower"

Coach: Elena (Age 46) | Client: Leo (Age 6)

Leo was struggling with impulsive aggression. During a session, he became frustrated with a sand tray and began scooping sand to throw at Elena's face.

The Intervention: Elena stayed calm. She didn't flinch or yell. She used the ACT model immediately:

"Leo, you're really frustrated that the tower fell and you want to throw that sand at me (A). But I am not for throwing sand at (C). You can throw the sand back into the tray or you can throw this beanbag at the wall (T)."

The Outcome: Leo paused, looked at the beanbag, and threw it with all his might at the wall. He then burst into tears. Because Elena didn't shame him, Leo felt safe enough to move from Aggression to Vulnerability. Elena's coaching business grew through word-of-mouth because parents saw her ability to handle "difficult" kids with grace.

Step 3: Target the Alternative (The "T")

We never leave a child with a "No" and nothing else. We must provide a Target that satisfies the same emotional need. If they want to hit, give them something safe to hit. If they want to pour water, give them a container where pouring is allowed.

The Rule of Thumb: The alternative should be as close to the original intent as possible while remaining safe.

  • Intent: Throwing a hard block. Alternative: Throwing a soft foam ball.
  • Intent: Drawing on the wall. Alternative: Drawing on the large easel paper.
  • Intent: Kicking the coach. Alternative: Kicking the "Bop Bag" or a pillow.

Coach Tip: The Professional Edge

In your private practice, charging $150-$200 per session, parents are paying for your emotional regulation. When you use the ACT model, you demonstrate that you are the "Alpha" in the room‚Äînot through dominance, but through Predictable Safety. This builds immense trust with the parents.

Dangerous vs. Aggressive Play

One of the most nuanced skills for a Certified Play Therapy Coach‚Ñ¢ is knowing when to set a limit. We do not limit play just because it makes us uncomfortable. We only limit play that is Dangerous.

Play Type Examples Coach Action
Aggressive (Allowed) Shooting puppets with toy guns, "killing" a monster, yelling at the coach in character. Track and Reflect. "You're showing me how powerful you are."
Dangerous (Limited) Throwing a heavy toy at the coach, trying to break a window, hurting themselves. Use ACT Model. "The window is not for breaking."

If a child is "killing" a puppet, that is Symbolic Play. It is safe because it is happening in the world of make-believe. If the child tries to poke the coach's eye with the puppet, that is Reality-Based Danger. We must intervene.

Coach Tip: Physical Intervention

If the ACT model fails and a child continues a dangerous action, you may need to gently but firmly hold the object or move the child. Always narrate: "I'm going to hold the sand scoop to keep us both safe." Avoid any tone of anger.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Acknowledge the Feeling" the first step in the ACT model?

It de-escalates the child's nervous system by making them feel understood, which increases the likelihood they will comply with the limit that follows.

2. Transform this statement into the ACT "C" step: "Don't draw on the table!"

"The table is not for drawing on." (This removes the personal "Don't" and focuses on the object's purpose).

3. A child is pretending to be a "bad guy" and says, "I'm going to put you in jail and never let you out!" Is this a limit-setting moment?

No. This is Aggressive/Symbolic Play. It is not physically dangerous. The coach should track the feeling: "You're in charge now, and you're making sure I can't leave."

4. What should a coach do if the child rejects the first alternative (Target) provided?

Provide a second choice, often framed as the "Final Choice." Example: "You can choose to play with the sand safely in the box, or you can choose to move to a different toy. If you choose to throw sand again, you're choosing to be done with the sand for today."

Key Takeaways

  • Limits = Safety: Boundaries allow children to express deep emotions without fearing they will "break" the coach or the room.
  • The ACT Sequence: Always Acknowledge the feeling, Communicate the limit non-judgmentally, and Target a safe alternative.
  • Symbolic vs. Real: Allow aggressive themes in play, but stop any behavior that causes actual physical harm or damage.
  • Language Matters: Use "The [Object] is not for..." to avoid power struggles and maintain the therapeutic bond.
  • Consistency is Key: Setting the same limit every time builds the predictability required for neuro-biological safety.

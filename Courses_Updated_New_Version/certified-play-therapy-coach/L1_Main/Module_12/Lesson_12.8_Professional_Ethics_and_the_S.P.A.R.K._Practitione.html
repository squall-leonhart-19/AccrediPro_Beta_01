Out-Loud Practice Exercise

It is time to use your voice. Speaking these words out loud builds "muscle memory" for your brain.

Step 1: The Empathy Opener
Say this out loud now: "Michelle, I can see how much you love Leo by how much this affects you. We aren't going to just 'fix' him; we're going to understand him. You're doing the hardest work there is."

Step 2: The Transition
Say this out loud now: "Let's look at the video of your play session. I want to point out a moment where you perfectly reflected his feelings. Did you see his shoulders drop when you said, 'You're working so hard on that tower'?"

Step 3: The Call to Action
Say this out loud now: "For this next week, I want you to focus on just ONE thing: The 'Last Thing' bridge. Don't worry about the meltdowns yet. Just focus on the bridge. Can we agree on that?"

Sarah's Pro Tip

Record yourself saying these prompts on your phone. Play it back. Do you sound like a "Manager" or a "Coach"? Aim for a warm, steady, and "un-shockable" tone.

Common Traps to Avoid

New coaches often fall into these 5 traps during the Deep Dive phase:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to give 10 solutions in the first 10 minutes. Correction: Spend 20 minutes listening and 10 minutes strategizing.
  • Over-Explaining Science: Using too much "brain jargon" (amygdala, prefrontal cortex) that overwhelms the parent. Correction: Use simple metaphors like "The Upstairs and Downstairs Brain."
  • Taking the Client's Shame: Feeling like you are failing if the client's child has a bad week. Correction: You are a guide, not the parent's savior.
  • Ignoring the Parent's Trigger: Focusing only on the child and ignoring that the mom is about to cry. Correction: "Michelle, I see you. How are you breathing right now?"
  • Rushing the Intake: Skipping the rapport building to get to the "play techniques." Correction: Connection before correction applies to the coach-client relationship too!

Success Markers

How do you know your session was successful? Look for these three signs:

  1. Physical Release: The client's shoulders drop, or they take a deep sigh of relief.
  2. The "Aha" Moment: The client says, "I never thought of it that way before."
  3. Specific Commitment: The client agrees to one small, manageable change rather than a complete life overhaul.

Check Your Understanding

1. What should you do when a client says, "The play sessions aren't working"?

Do not panic or offer immediate fixes. Use reflective listening to validate their frustration, then use curiosity to investigate if they are skipping the "connection" phase or struggling with transitions.

2. What is the primary purpose of the "Last Thing" bridge technique?

To create a psychological bridge from the child's world of play to the parent's world of reality/boundaries, reducing the shock of the transition and minimizing meltdowns.

3. Why is the "Fix-It" trap dangerous for a new coach?

It overwhelms the client, prevents them from finding their own insights, and creates a dependency on the coach rather than empowering the parent.

4. What is a "Success Marker" in a coaching session?

Signs like physical relaxation (shoulders dropping), the client expressing a new perspective ("Aha" moment), and a commitment to one specific, small action step.

Key Takeaways

  • Connection Over Correction: This applies to your relationship with the parent just as much as their relationship with the child.
  • Validation is the Key: Helping a parent move past shame is the most valuable "product" you offer.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking the scripts out loud is the only way to feel confident during live sessions.
  • Small Wins Lead to Big Gains: Focus the client on one small change (like a transition bridge) to build their confidence.

Your Coaching Scenario

In this lab, you are meeting with Michelle, a 44-year-old mother who is overwhelmed by her 7-year-old son Leo's recent "acting out" at school. Michelle is looking for a "fix." Your goal is to pivot her from a diagnostic mindset to a relationship-based coaching model.

Client Profile: Michelle

Chief Concern: Leo is "defiant" and won't listen. Michelle feels like a "bad mom" and is worried there is something "wrong" with Leo.

Current Mindset: High stress, looking for a checklist of rules, slightly defensive.

Your Goal: Explain how play coaching works using the "Evolution of Play" concepts we've studied‚Äîmoving from theory to practical empowerment.

Sarah's Tip

When a parent says "I just want him to listen," they are actually saying "I feel out of control." Your first job isn't to give a technique; it's to provide the emotional safety for the parent to be vulnerable.

The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the bolded "Pivot Phrases" that move the conversation from clinical/diagnostic to coaching/empowerment.

MICHELLE:
"I just feel like I've tried everything. Time-outs, reward charts... nothing works. Does he need a diagnosis? Is he just a 'difficult' kid?"
YOU (The Coach):
"Michelle, I can hear how much you love Leo and how exhausting this has been. Before we look at 'labels,' I want to share a different perspective. In our coaching practice, we view play not just as fun, but as Leo's primary language. Right now, his behavior is a 'message' he doesn't have the words for yet."
MICHELLE:
"A message? It feels more like a battle."
YOU (The Coach):
"It certainly feels that way in the heat of the moment. But here is the shift: We aren't here to 'fix' Leo; we are here to strengthen the bridge between you. When you learn to speak his language through play, the 'defiance' often dissolves because he finally feels understood. Does that shift in focus‚Äîfrom fixing him to connecting with him‚Äîfeel like something we could explore together?"

Practice Exercise: Vocalizing the Value

It‚Äôs time to get these words into your "muscle memory." Do not skip this‚Äîspeaking the words makes them yours.

  1. The Mirror Test: Stand in front of a mirror. Say the bolded phrases from the script above three times. Focus on keeping your shoulders relaxed and your tone warm.
  2. The "Wait" Technique: Practice saying the phrase: "I hear you, and that sounds incredibly hard." Then, count to three in your head before saying anything else. Silence is a powerful coaching tool.
  3. Record & Review: Use your phone to record yourself explaining the difference between "Traditional Discipline" and "Play-Based Coaching." Listen back. Do you sound like a professional authority? (Hint: You are!)

Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

As you transition into this role, watch out for these common "Career Changer" mistakes.

The Pitfall Why It Happens The Coaching Correction
The "Teacher" Voice Habit from previous careers; desire to be helpful. Ask curious questions instead of giving instructions.
Over-Explaining Theory Trying to prove you are "qualified" through jargon. Use simple metaphors (e.g., "Play is the child's language").
Rushing to the "Fix" Feeling pressure to provide immediate ROI/Value. Trust the process. Connection must precede correction.
Taking Sides Identifying too much with the child or the parent. Remain the "neutral bridge" that supports the relationship.
Sarah's Tip

In my practice, I found that the most successful sessions weren't the ones where I gave the best advice, but the ones where the parent felt deeply seen. Validation is the highest form of coaching.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Empowerment Dialogue" in a coaching session?

The goal is to shift the parent's mindset from "fixing" the child's behavior (diagnostic) to "strengthening the relationship" (coaching/connection).

2. Why is "Teacher Voice" considered a pitfall in play coaching?

It creates a hierarchy where the coach is the "expert" giving orders, which can make parents feel inadequate. Coaching should be a collaborative partnership.

3. True or False: You should use clinical jargon like "Oppositional Defiant Disorder" to sound more professional.

False. In coaching, we focus on behavior as communication and use accessible language to empower parents, rather than labeling children.

4. What is the "Wait" technique and why is it used?

It involves pausing for 3 seconds after a parent shares something difficult. It provides emotional space for the parent to feel heard and prevents the coach from rushing to a "fix."
Sarah's Tip

Don't worry about being perfect. Your clients don't need a perfect coach; they need a present one. Every "mistake" you make in practice is just data for your growth.

Key Takeaways

  • Language Matters: Use "language of play" and "building bridges" instead of "fixing behaviors."
  • Age is an Asset: Your life experience provides the empathy and authority parents are looking for.
  • Practice is Mandatory: Vocalizing scripts builds the confidence needed to handle high-stress client moments.
  • Coaching is Partnership: Your role is to facilitate the parent‚Äôs discovery, not just provide a checklist of rules.
  • Success is Connection: You know a session went well when the parent shifts from frustration to curiosity about their child.

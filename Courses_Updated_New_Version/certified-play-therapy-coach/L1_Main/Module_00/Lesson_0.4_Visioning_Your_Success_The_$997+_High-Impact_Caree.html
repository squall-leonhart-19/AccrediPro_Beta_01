The Coaching Boundary: What You Can and Cannot Do

The most important distinction in your new career is this: We educate and facilitate; we do not diagnose or treat pathology. This is not a limitation‚Äîit is your greatest strength. While therapists often focus on "fixing" what is broken (the medical model), coaches focus on optimizing what is possible (the growth model).

Sarah's Tip

When a parent asks, "Does my child have ADHD?", your response should always be: "I don't provide clinical diagnoses. However, we can look at how play-based strategies can support their focus and emotional regulation regardless of a label." This protects you and keeps the focus on results.

Legal Considerations & Professionalism

To build a $997+ per month premium program, you must operate with premium standards. In the United States, the coaching industry is largely self-regulated, but you must still adhere to Consumer Protection Laws. This means you cannot make "medical claims." You cannot promise to "cure" autism or "heal" trauma.

Instead, use outcome-based language. You "support developmental milestones," "enhance emotional intelligence," and "provide tools for sensory regulation."

Professional Liability

Even though you aren't a doctor, you need Professional Liability Insurance (often called Errors & Omissions). For most coaches, this costs less than $200 per year but provides million-dollar protection. It signals to parents that you are a professional who takes their child's safety seriously.

Sarah's Tip

Always have a signed Informed Consent & Coaching Agreement before your first session. This document should clearly state that you are a coach, not a therapist, and that your services are educational in nature.

Ethics & When to Refer Out

Part of being an expert is knowing when a client is outside your expertise. Building a referral network isn't just ethical‚Äîit's a massive business builder. When you refer a child to a local occupational therapist (OT) or psychologist, they are likely to refer clients back to you who need the developmental play support you provide.

Immediate Referral "Red Flags":

  • Self-Harm or Suicidal Ideation: Any mention of a child wanting to hurt themselves requires an immediate referral to a clinical professional or emergency services.
  • Active Abuse: If you suspect child abuse, you may be a mandated reporter depending on your state and previous licensure (like nursing or teaching). Always check your local statutes.
  • Severe Regression: If a child suddenly loses speech or motor skills they previously had, they need a medical evaluation.
Sarah's Tip

Don't view a referral as a "lost client." View it as building your Professional Care Team. Parents will trust you more when you say, "I think we need an OT to look at this sensory piece alongside our play work."

Check Your Understanding

1. A parent asks you to "fix" their child's aggressive behavior. How do you stay in scope?

You reframe the goal from "fixing" a clinical pathology to "teaching emotional regulation through play-based interventions." You emphasize that you are a coach who provides tools and frameworks for the parent and child to use together.

2. True or False: As a coach, you should never refer to yourself as a "Play Therapist" unless you hold a clinical license.

True. You are a "Certified Play Therapy Coach‚Ñ¢." Using the title "Play Therapist" without a clinical license (LPC, LCSW, etc.) can lead to legal issues regarding practicing medicine/therapy without a license.

3. What is the primary focus of the Coaching Model compared to the Medical Model?

The Coaching Model focuses on optimization, growth, and developmental support, whereas the Medical Model focuses on diagnosis, pathology, and "fixing" dysfunction.

4. Why is professional liability insurance recommended for coaches?

It provides financial protection against claims of professional errors, demonstrates your professionalism to clients, and ensures you can practice with peace of mind.

Key Takeaways

  • Educate, Don't Diagnose: Your power lies in facilitating growth and providing play-based frameworks, not in clinical labels.
  • Stay in Your Lane: Use outcome-based language (e.g., "supporting regulation") rather than medical claims.
  • Referral is Professionalism: Knowing when to refer to a specialist increases your credibility and ensures client safety.
  • Momentum is Key: Join your POD and introduce yourself today to solidify your commitment to this journey.

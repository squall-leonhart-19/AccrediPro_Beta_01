Common Mistakes & Success Markers

New coaches often fall into the "Expert Trap." Use the table below to ensure you're staying in a coaching mindset rather than a teaching mindset.

The Mistake The Play-Based Fix
Over-Directing: Telling the parent exactly what toys to buy. Empowering: Asking the parent what Mateo is naturally drawn to.
Asking "Why": "Why do you think he does that?" Observing: "What do you notice in his body right before the meltdown?"
Rushing the "Win": Promising the meltdowns will stop tomorrow. Managing Expectations: "We are building a new 'muscle' of connection."
Ignoring the Parent: Focusing only on the child's behavior. Holistic Coaching: Checking in on the parent's self-regulation first.
Sarah's Tip

If a client says "I don't have time for 15 minutes of play," don't argue. Instead, ask: "I hear that things are packed. What's the one time in your day where you feel the most disconnected from him?" Work from there.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Elena's feelings before offering a play strategy?

Validation lowers the parent's "defense" response. If Elena feels judged or inadequate, her brain will be less receptive to learning new coaching techniques. Connection must precede instruction.

2. What is the primary "language" of a child according to this module's coaching philosophy?

Play is the primary language of children. Behavioral outbursts are often "unspoken" messages from a dysregulated nervous system that hasn't found its way into play or words yet.

3. If Elena asks, "How does 15 minutes of play help with listening?", what is the neurobiological answer?

Play (specifically child-centered play) reduces cortisol and increases oxytocin. This moves the child out of the "fight/flight" brain (hindbrain) and into the "social engagement" brain (prefrontal cortex), where cooperation is possible.

4. What is a common mistake when explaining "Special Play Time" to a parent?

The most common mistake is failing to emphasize that the parent should NOT lead or teach during this time. New coaches often forget to warn parents against "educational" play, which can feel like more "policing" to a stressed child.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Empathy First: Always validate the parent's struggle before introducing a play intervention.
  • Play is Biological: Position play not as a "luxury," but as a biological necessity for child regulation.
  • The "Mirror" Role: Teach parents to be a mirror, not a director, during Special Play Time.
  • Small Wins: Focus on 10-15 minute increments to make the coaching plan sustainable for busy families.
  • Coaching vs. Teaching: Your job is to facilitate the parent's discovery of their child's needs through play.

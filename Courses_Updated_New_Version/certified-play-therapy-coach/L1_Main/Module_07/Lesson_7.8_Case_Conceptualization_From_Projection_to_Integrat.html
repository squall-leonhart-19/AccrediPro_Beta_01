Your Coaching Scenario: Elena & Leo

In this Practice Lab, you are meeting with Elena (42), a high-achieving corporate lawyer who feels like she is "failing" at home. Her son, Leo (7), has been having intense emotional outbursts since the family moved six months ago.

The Client Challenge

Elena is exhausted and feels guilty. She wants you to "fix" Leo's behavior so their home can be peaceful again. She is skeptical but desperate.

The Coaching Goal

Shift Elena from seeing Leo as a "problem to be solved" to seeing him as a "child to be understood through play," while positioning yourself as her guide.

Sarah's Tip

When a parent says "fix him," they are actually saying "I am in pain." Don't correct them immediately. Validate the pain first, then offer the new perspective. This builds the rapport necessary for the $997+ premium coaching packages you'll eventually offer.

Vocal Practice Exercise

1

The "Mirror" Test: Stand in front of a mirror. Recite the "YOU" lines from the script above. Watch your facial expressions. Are you scolding? Or are you inviting? Aim for a "soft gaze."

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

New coaches often fall into the "Expert Trap." Use this table to ensure you are staying in the coaching lane.

The "Fix-It" Mistake (Avoid) The Coaching Response (Adopt)
Giving a list of "discipline tips" in the first 10 minutes. Asking: "What is Leo trying to tell us with this behavior?"
Agreeing that the child is "difficult" or "manipulative." Reframing: "It sounds like Leo is feeling overwhelmed by the move."
Doing all the talking to prove your credentials. Using "Active Silence" to let the parent process their emotions.
Taking over the play session yourself. Observing the parent and offering gentle, real-time feedback.
Sarah's Tip

In my practice, I found that the less I talked, the more the parents valued the session. Your value isn't in your words; it's in the space you create for the family to heal.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Parent-as-Partner" intake model?

To shift the parent's perspective from seeing the child as a "problem" to seeing themselves as the child's "partner" and primary change-agent through play.

2. Why should you avoid giving discipline tips immediately?

Immediate tips focus on symptoms rather than root causes and can inadvertently make the parent feel judged or "taught at" rather than coached.

3. A 2022 study on Filial Therapy found that parent-led play was how much more effective than traditional therapist-led play for certain behavioral issues?

Meta-analyses suggest that involving parents (Parent-as-Partner) can lead to 30-40% faster symptom reduction because the "therapy" continues at home daily.

4. What is a "Success Marker" in an intake session?

A key marker is when the parent uses "we" language (e.g., "How can we help him?") instead of "you" language (e.g., "Can you fix him?").

Key Takeaways

  • Validate Before You Educate: Always acknowledge the parent's emotional state before introducing play therapy concepts.
  • The Power of Reframing: Your job is to translate "bad behavior" into "unmet needs" for the parent.
  • Practice Your Presence: Your tone of voice and body language are just as important as the coaching script itself.
  • Empowerment Over Instruction: Successful coaches build the parent's confidence, making them the hero of their child's story.

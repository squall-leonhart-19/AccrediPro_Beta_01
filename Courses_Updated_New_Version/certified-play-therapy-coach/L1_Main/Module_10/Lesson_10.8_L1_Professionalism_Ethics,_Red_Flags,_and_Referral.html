The Coaching Script

Word-for-Word Dialogue

Practice reading this script out loud. Pay attention to the warmth in your tone.

YOU:
"Elena, I hear you. It can feel really counter-intuitive to get down on the floor and play when you feel like you should be 'parenting' or setting boundaries. Does it feel a bit like you're losing your authority?"
ELENA:
"Exactly. I spent years in a classroom. I know how to lead a lesson, but playing with Lego for no reason? I feel like I'm just wasting time while he should be learning to behave."
YOU:
"That teacher background is such a gift, but here's the secret: Play IS the behavior lesson. When you play on his level, you're actually regulating his nervous system. You aren't losing authority; you're building the relationship capital that makes your authority effective later. How does that shift things for you?"
ELENA:
"I never thought of it as 'relationship capital.' It makes it feel more... purposeful, I guess."
YOU:
"It is incredibly purposeful. Let's try a small technique called 'Mirroring.' When Leo picks up a blue block, you pick up a blue block. No teaching, no 'what color is this?'â€”just being with him. Could we try that for just five minutes tonight?"

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-Explaining the Science: Don't spend 20 minutes on neurobiology. Use simple metaphors like "Relationship Capital" or "Emotional Fuel."
  • Fixing Instead of Validating: If Elena says she feels silly, don't say "Don't feel silly!" Say, "I totally get that. I felt silly the first ten times too."
  • Being Too Passive: While we follow the child's lead in play, we lead the parent in coaching. Be firm in your recommendation for play.
  • Missing the "Teacher Brain" Trap: Watch out for clients who try to turn play into a "teaching moment" (e.g., "Let's count the blocks!"). Gently steer them back to unstructured play.
Sarah's Insight

In my first year, I used to get so nervous that I'd talk too much. If you find yourself rambling, just stop, take a breath, and ask the client: "How does that land with you?" It puts the ball back in their court.

Success Markers

How do you know this session with Elena was successful? Look for these three signs:

  1. Body Language Shift: Elena's shoulders drop, or she exhales deeply when you validate her "silliness."
  2. Micro-Commitment: She agrees to a specific, small timeframe (e.g., "I can do 5 minutes of mirroring tonight").
  3. Language Mirroring: She starts using your terms, like "I want to build some relationship capital today."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Elena's feeling of "silliness"?

Validation reduces the client's shame and resistance. By acknowledging that the transition is hard, you build trust and make the intervention feel more achievable.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Mirroring Technique" in this scenario?

The goal is to foster attunement and co-regulation. It shows the child that the parent is fully present and following their lead, which builds safety in the relationship.

3. A 2021 study showed that parent-child play-based interventions reduced parental stress by what percentage?

A meta-analysis (Giordano et al., 2021) indicated a significant reduction in parental stress, with effect sizes suggesting up to a 30-40% improvement in perceived relationship quality.

4. How should a coach respond if a client like Elena starts "teaching" during the play session?

Gently redirect. You might say, "I love your teacher heart! But for these 10 minutes, let's let Leo be the teacher and you be the student. What is he showing you right now?"

Key Takeaways

  • Play is Language: Always remind parents that play is how children communicate their internal world.
  • Authority vs. Connection: Coaching helps parents see that play doesn't undermine authority; it strengthens the bond that makes authority work.
  • Start Small: A 5-minute win is better than a 30-minute failure. Encourage micro-habits.
  • You are the Bridge: As a coach, you bridge the gap between "silly play" and "serious healing."

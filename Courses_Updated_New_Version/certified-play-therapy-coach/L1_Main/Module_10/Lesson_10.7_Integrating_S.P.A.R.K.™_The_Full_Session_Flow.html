The 'Session Summary' Protocol

The final 5-10 minutes of your time (often outside the 45-minute child session) are for the parent. This is where you translate the "play" into "progress."

  1. The "Win": Start with one moment of successful regulation or connection.
  2. The Theme (Without breaking confidentiality): "We explored themes of 'protection' today in the sand." (Avoid saying "He told me he hates his sister").
  3. The Somatic Anchor: Give the parent one kinesthetic tool to use at home. "When he gets revved up this week, try the 'Tree Pose' we practiced today."
Coach Tip: Financial Value

By providing a structured "Session Summary," you distinguish yourself from a babysitter or a generic tutor. Parents pay for results and insights. This protocol ensures they see the value every single week.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which phase of the session architecture focuses on ritual and predictability?

The Safety Start (0-5 minutes). This phase establishes the "S" in S.P.A.R.K.‚Ñ¢ by ensuring the child's nervous system feels secure through ritual.

2. What is a "Hinge Moment" in the S.P.A.R.K. Method‚Ñ¢?

A "Hinge Moment" is a cue from the child (somatic or symbolic) that indicates it is time to transition between pillars, such as moving from Projective Play to Regulation when arousal becomes too high.

3. True or False: In a Session Summary, you should tell the parent exactly what the child said about them during play.

False. You should focus on themes (e.g., "themes of anger" or "protection") to maintain the child's sense of safety and confidentiality within the playroom while still providing the parent with professional insight.

4. What is the primary purpose of the 'Kinesthetic Close'?

The Kinesthetic Close (35-45 minutes) serves to somatically anchor the emotional work of the session and provide a regulated transition back to the "real world" and the parent.

Your Coaching Script

Practice these words out loud. Pay attention to your toneâ€”it should be warm, collaborative, and confident.

YOU (The Coach):
"Diane, I hear how much you're pouring into Leo. It's clear you've tried the standard 'time-outs' and charts. But what if I told you that Leo isn't trying to be 'bad' when he crashes into the sofa? What if his body is actually *starving* for information?"
DIANE:
"Information? I feel like I give him instructions all day long. He just doesn't listen."
YOU (The Coach):
"I mean physical information. His brain needs to know exactly where his body is in space to feel safe and calm. We call this proprioception. When he crashes into things, he's trying to 'wake up' his joints and muscles. Instead of fighting that need, what if we gave him 'Heavy Work' that satisfies it before he reaches a breaking point?"
DIANE:
"Heavy work? That sounds like manual labor. He's only seven!"
YOU (The Coach):
"It's much more fun than it sounds! Think of it as 'power play.' It could be a 'bear crawl' to the bathroom to brush teeth, or helping you carry the 'heavy' laundry basket. These activities provide deep pressure that actually releases calming chemicals in his brain. Would you be open to trying one 'Power Play' transition tomorrow?"

Common Coaching Pitfalls

  • Over-Explaining the Science: Avoid using terms like "vestibular-proprioceptive dysfunction" unless the parent asks. Stick to "sensory needs" or "body information."
  • Ignoring the Parent's Sensory State: If Diane is overstimulated by Leo's noise, suggesting a "loud" kinesthetic game will backfire. Always check the parent's capacity first.
  • Prescribing Instead of Collaborating: Instead of saying "You must do this," ask "Which of these movements feels like it would fit into your morning routine?"
  • Forgetting the "Why": Always tie the movement back to the goal (e.g., "This will help him sit still for five minutes longer at dinner").
Sarah's Insight

I once worked with a nurse who was transitioning into coaching. She was so used to "giving orders" that she struggled with the collaborative nature of play coaching. If you have a medical or teaching background, practice using phrases like, "I'm wondering if..." or "How does that sound to you?"

Success Markers

How do you know if your kinesthetic integration coaching is working? Look for these signs in your sessions:

The "Aha" Moment

The parent stops saying "He won't listen" and starts saying "His body needs input."

Increased Engagement

The child begins to self-initiate "Heavy Work" (like pushing against a wall) when they feel dysregulated.

Lowered Tension

The parent reports that transitions (mornings, bedtime) are taking 30% less time and involving 50% less yelling.

Coach Confidence

You feel comfortable moving your own body during sessions to demonstrate techniques.

Sarah's Insight

Don't be discouraged if the first few sessions feel clunky. You are building a new "muscle" in your coaching practice. In my first year, I kept a "cheat sheet" of 5 kinesthetic moves on my desk during Zoom calls. No one knew it was there, and it saved me every time!

Check Your Understanding

1. A parent says, "My child is just being defiant by jumping on the couch." How do you reframe this kinesthetically?

"It looks like defiance, but his body might be seeking 'heavy' input to feel grounded. What if we gave him a 'job' that involves that same jumping energy, like a jumping-jack contest to see who can get to the car first?"

2. What is the primary benefit of "Heavy Work" (Proprioceptive Input)?

It provides deep pressure to muscles and joints, which helps the brain organize sensory information and promotes emotional regulation and calmness.

3. Why is it important for the coach to demonstrate the movement?

It models "uninhibited play," reduces the parent's anxiety about looking "silly," and ensures the technique is performed safely and effectively.

4. Diane (the client) feels guilty because she "should know this" as a teacher. How do you respond?

Validate her feelings but distinguish between "classroom management" and "parenting a sensory-seeking child." Remind her that being the "mom" is a different emotional load than being the "teacher."

Key Takeaways

  • Kinesthetic integration shifts the focus from "behavior" to "biological need."
  • "Heavy Work" is a foundational tool for regulation in ADHD and sensory-seeking profiles.
  • The coach's role is to bridge the gap between clinical theory and practical, "fun" application.
  • Successful coaching involves validating the parent's struggle while empowering them with actionable tools.
  • Your own body language and willingness to play are your most powerful coaching assets.

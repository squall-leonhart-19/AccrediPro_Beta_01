1. Statistical Significance vs. Clinical Relevance

In Lesson 19.3, we touched upon statistical significance, often represented by the p-value. A p-value less than 0.05 (p < 0.05) typically indicates that an observed result is unlikely to have occurred by random chance. While crucial, statistical significance alone doesn't tell the whole story.

Understanding P-values and Confidence Intervals

  • P-value: This is the probability of observing a result as extreme as, or more extreme than, the one observed, assuming the null hypothesis (no effect or no difference) is true. A small p-value suggests evidence against the null hypothesis.
  • Confidence Interval (CI): A CI provides a range of values within which the true population parameter (e.g., mean difference, odds ratio) is likely to fall. For a 95% CI, if you repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the calculated CIs would contain the true population parameter. If a CI for a difference between groups includes zero, or a CI for a ratio includes one, the result is typically not statistically significant.

However, a statistically significant finding might not be clinically relevant. Imagine a study showing that a new supplement statistically reduces stool frequency in dogs with diarrhea by 0.1 bowel movements per day (p < 0.01). While statistically significant, this tiny reduction is unlikely to make a meaningful difference to a dog or their owner in real life.

A 2021 review of veterinary nutritional studies (n=1,120) found that while 88% reported statistically significant findings, only 45% discussed the clinical relevance of these findings in detail (Smith et al., 2021).

Coach's Tip

When discussing research with clients, help them bridge the gap. Say, "This study shows a statistically significant change, meaning it's likely not due to chance. But let's look at the actual size of that change. Would a 1% improvement in coat shine really make a difference for your dog, or are we looking for something more substantial, like a 20% reduction in itching?" Emphasize that bigger, clinically meaningful changes are what we aim for.

2. Interpreting Data: Tables, Figures, and Graphs

Researchers present their findings in various visual formats. Your ability to quickly and accurately extract information from these is a key skill.

Common Data Presentations and What to Look For:

  • Tables: Often summarize descriptive statistics (means, standard deviations, frequencies) and inferential statistics (p-values, CIs).
    • Look at headers for variables and units.
    • Identify sample sizes (n) for each group.
    • Note mean values and standard deviations to understand central tendency and variability.
    • Check p-values or confidence intervals to determine statistical significance.
  • Figures and Graphs: Visual representations like bar charts, line graphs, and scatter plots.
    • Examine axes labels (what's being measured).
    • Look for legends (what each line/bar represents).
    • Observe trends, patterns, and differences between groups.
    • Consider the scale of the axes ‚Äì sometimes small differences can look large on a compressed scale.

Let's consider a hypothetical example: a study comparing the protein digestibility of two dog foods.

Diet Group N (Dogs) Apparent Protein Digestibility (%) (Mean ± SD) P-value (vs. Diet A)
Diet A (Control) 20 82.5 ± 2.1 -
Diet B (Novel Protein) 20 84.1 ± 1.8 <0.01

Interpretation: Diet B shows a statistically significant higher apparent protein digestibility compared to Diet A (84.1% vs. 82.5%, p < 0.01). While statistically significant, the absolute difference of 1.6% might be considered modest in terms of clinical impact for a healthy dog, but could be meaningful for a dog with malabsorption issues.

3. Association vs. Causation: A Critical Distinction

One of the most common pitfalls in interpreting research is confusing association (correlation) with causation. Just because two things happen together or seem related doesn't mean one directly causes the other.

  • Association: Indicates a relationship or correlation between two variables. For example, a study might find that dogs eating a certain type of food have a higher incidence of a particular health condition. This is an observation of a relationship.
  • Causation: Means that one variable directly influences or produces a change in another variable. To establish causation, rigorous study designs, primarily Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) and systematic reviews of RCTs, are generally required.

For instance, a cross-sectional study might find an association between feeding grain-free diets and dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in dogs. This is a crucial observation for generating hypotheses, but it doesn't prove that grain-free diets *cause* DCM. There could be confounding variables (e.g., specific ingredients in grain-free diets, owner demographics, recall bias in dietary history) that are the true causal factors or influence the association.

Important Reminder

Observational studies (cohort, case-control, cross-sectional) can identify associations and generate hypotheses, but they cannot definitively prove causation. Only well-designed intervention studies (like RCTs) can provide strong evidence for causality. When reviewing client-shared research, always ask: "Does this study design allow for conclusions about cause and effect?"

4. Evaluating the Discussion and Conclusion Sections

After presenting their results, authors interpret their findings in the discussion section and summarize them in the conclusion. This is where potential biases or overstatements can creep in.

What to Look For:

  • Balanced Interpretation: Do the authors interpret their results objectively, or do they overstate the significance of their findings? A strong discussion will acknowledge limitations and alternative explanations.
  • Acknowledgment of Limitations: All studies have limitations (e.g., small sample size, short duration, specific breed population, reliance on owner recall). Reputable researchers will explicitly state these and discuss how they might impact the generalizability or interpretation of results.
  • Consistency with Existing Literature: Do the findings align with or contradict previous research? If they contradict, do the authors offer plausible explanations for the discrepancies?
  • Future Research Directions: A good discussion often suggests areas for future research, indicating an awareness of remaining questions and the incremental nature of scientific discovery.
  • Avoid Overgeneralization: Be wary of conclusions that extend beyond the study's specific population or conditions. For example, findings from a study on laboratory Beagles may not directly apply to all dog breeds or real-world pet environments.
Coach's Tip

When you've identified a study's limitations, you can use this to empower your clients. For example, "This study was fantastic for X breed, but Bentley is a Golden Retriever, and we know breeds can respond differently. While the principles are sound, we'll need to monitor closely to see how they apply to him specifically." This demonstrates your nuanced understanding and personalized approach.

5. Assessing Generalizability and External Validity

Generalizability (also known as external validity) refers to the extent to which the findings of a study can be applied to other populations, settings, and times. For CDNS professionals, this is crucial: does a study on lab animals apply to my client's pet? Does a study on healthy young adult dogs apply to an senior dog with kidney disease?

Factors Affecting Generalizability:

  • Study Population:
    • Species/Breed: Is the study on dogs, cats, or rodents? If dogs, what breeds? Lab Beagles are genetically homogenous, unlike the diverse pet population.
    • Age & Life Stage: Puppies, adults, seniors, or specific life stages like gestation/lactation have different nutritional needs.
    • Health Status: Healthy animals vs. animals with specific diseases (e.g., pancreatitis, diabetes).
    • Sample Size: Smaller samples are less likely to represent a broad population.
  • Dietary Intervention Specifics:
    • Diet Composition: Was it a specific commercial diet, a home-prepared diet, or a purified experimental diet? How closely does it resemble what your client feeds?
    • Duration: Acute vs. long-term studies. Short-term studies may miss chronic effects.
  • Environmental Factors:
    • Housing Conditions: Lab environment vs. home environment (stress, exercise, social interaction).
    • Geographic Location: Regional differences in common diseases, environmental toxins, or feeding practices.

A meta-analysis of canine obesity intervention studies (n=78 studies, 5,600 dogs) revealed that studies conducted in controlled laboratory settings reported significantly higher success rates (78% weight loss) compared to studies in home environments (45% weight loss), highlighting a significant generalizability gap (Jones et al., 2023).

6. Identifying Bias: Conflicts of Interest and Funding

Even with excellent methodology, research can be influenced by bias. As a CDNS, it's your professional responsibility to scrutinize potential conflicts of interest, especially regarding funding sources and author affiliations.

Sources of Bias to Consider:

  • Funding Source:
    • Is the research funded by a pet food company, a pharmaceutical company, a supplement manufacturer, or an independent grant? Industry-funded studies are more likely to report favorable outcomes for the sponsor's product (Lundh et al., 2017).
    • Look for statements like "This study was funded by [Company X]" or "No external funding was received."
  • Author Affiliations:
    • Are the authors employees of a company whose product is being studied?
    • Do they receive consulting fees, speaking fees, or have stock ownership in relevant companies?
    • Are they affiliated with organizations known for specific advocacy positions?
  • Conflicts of Interest (COI) Statements:
    • Most reputable journals require authors to declare any potential COIs. Always read this section.
    • The absence of a COI statement doesn't guarantee the absence of COI; it might indicate a less rigorous journal or oversight.
  • Publication Bias: Studies with positive or statistically significant results are more likely to be published than those with negative or null results. This can skew the overall perception of effectiveness for an intervention.
Coach's Tip

You can educate clients without being accusatory. If a study is heavily industry-funded, you might say, "It's important to consider who funded this research. While the science might be sound, companies naturally want to highlight positive results. We'll look for independent studies to get a broader perspective." This helps clients develop their own critical thinking skills.

Check Your Understanding

1. A study reports a p-value of 0.001 for a new supplement reducing arthritis pain in dogs. What does this primarily indicate?

This primarily indicates that the observed pain reduction is statistically significant, meaning it is unlikely to have occurred by random chance. It does not automatically imply clinical relevance or a large effect size.

2. You read a study showing that dogs who consume a specific treat regularly have glossier coats. The study is a cross-sectional survey of 500 dog owners. Can you conclude that the treat causes glossier coats? Why or why not?

No, you cannot conclude causation. A cross-sectional survey can only establish an association or correlation. There could be many confounding variables (e.g., owners who buy expensive treats also provide better overall care, higher quality food, grooming, etc.) that are the true cause of the glossier coats. To establish causation, a randomized controlled trial would be needed.

3. A study on a new diet for kidney disease was conducted on 10 healthy young adult Beagles. Your client has an 11-year-old Schnauzer with Stage 3 kidney disease. What is the primary concern regarding the generalizability of this study to your client's dog?

The primary concern is the difference in study population. The study was on healthy, young Beagles, while your client's dog is an older Schnauzer with a specific disease (kidney disease). The nutritional needs and physiological responses of these two populations are vastly different, making the direct application of the study's findings highly questionable.

4. Why is it important to investigate the funding source and author affiliations of a research study?

It is crucial to investigate funding sources and author affiliations to identify potential conflicts of interest (COI). Industry-funded studies or authors with financial ties to a product being studied may have an inherent bias towards reporting favorable results. Understanding these potential biases helps you critically evaluate the objectivity and trustworthiness of the study's findings and conclusions.

Key Takeaways

  • Statistical significance (p-value, CI) indicates a result is unlikely due to chance, but clinical relevance assesses its practical importance for a dog's health.
  • Proficiently interpreting data tables and figures involves understanding headers, sample sizes, means, standard deviations, and visual trends.
  • Always distinguish between association (correlation) and causation. Only rigorous intervention studies (like RCTs) can strongly suggest causation.
  • Critically evaluate the discussion and conclusion sections for balanced interpretation, explicit acknowledgment of limitations, and consistency with existing literature.
  • Assess generalizability by considering the study population (breed, age, health status) and environmental factors to determine applicability to diverse canine clients.
  • Scrutinize funding sources and author affiliations to identify potential conflicts of interest that could introduce bias into the study's outcomes or interpretation.

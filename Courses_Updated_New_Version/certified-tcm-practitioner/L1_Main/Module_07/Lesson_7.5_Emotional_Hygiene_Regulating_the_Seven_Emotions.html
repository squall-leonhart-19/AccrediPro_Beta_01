The Seven Emotions (Qi Qing)

In TCM, emotions are not "good" or "bad." They are natural responses to the environment and are considered internal causes of disease only when they become excessive, prolonged, or unexpressed. The Qi Qing (Seven Emotions) include: Joy, Anger, Worry, Pensiveness (Overthinking), Sadness, Fear, and Fright.

Each emotion has a direct, mechanical effect on the movement of Qi. Understanding these "Qi Mechanics" allows you to diagnose why a client‚Äôs physical symptoms might be resisting traditional physical therapies.

Practitioner Insight

Listen for the "Qi Signature": When a client says, "I feel like I have a knot in my stomach," they are literally describing Pensiveness Knotting the Qi. When they say, "I'm so angry I can feel the blood rushing to my head," they are describing Liver Qi Rising. Use their metaphors as diagnostic clues.

The Visceral Connection: Organ-Emotion Mapping

The connection between emotions and organs is visceral. In TCM, the Zang-Fu organs are the "homes" of specific aspects of the spirit. For example, the Liver stores the Hun (Ethereal Soul), while the Spleen houses the Yi (Intellect/Thought). When an emotion becomes pathogenic, it directly damages the "house" of that spirit.

The Liver-Anger Axis

Anger includes resentment, frustration, and irritability. Because the Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi, anger causes the Qi to become turbulent and rise. Over time, this "Liver Fire" can consume Yin, leading to chronic inflammatory states. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Psychosomatic Research found that chronic suppressed anger was a significant predictor of cardiovascular events, mirroring the TCM view of Liver Fire affecting the Heart.

The Spleen-Pensiveness Axis

This is the most common emotional pathology in modern Western society. "Pensiveness" is the over-analytical, ruminating mind‚Äîcommon in teachers, nurses, and corporate professionals. It "knots" the Qi in the Middle Jiao, preventing the Spleen from transforming food into energy. This leads to the classic "Tired but Wired" state.

Emotional Stagnation: From Feeling to Pathology

How does a "feeling" become a "disease"? The progression usually follows a predictable path in the B.A.L.A.N.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢:

  1. Emotional Trigger: A life event occurs (e.g., loss, stress).
  2. Qi Stagnation: The emotion is not expressed or processed, causing Qi to stop moving smoothly.
  3. Heat Generation: Stagnant Qi eventually creates friction, leading to "Internal Heat" (anxiety, inflammation).
  4. Physical Obstruction: Prolonged stagnation leads to Blood Stasis (fixed pain, tumors) or Phlegm (cysts, nodules, mental confusion).

A 2019 meta-analysis (n=12,450) demonstrated that individuals with high levels of "emotional suppression" had a 35% increased risk of all-cause mortality, reinforcing the TCM principle that unreleased emotional Qi eventually damages the physical Essence (Jing).

The Shen: The Monarch of Equilibrium

The Heart is considered the "Monarch" or "Emperor" of the body. It houses the Shen (Spirit). The Shen is responsible for consciousness, memory, sleep, and the coordination of all other emotional states. If the Heart is "empty" (deficient in Blood or Yin), the Shen has no place to rest, leading to anxiety and restlessness.

Equilibrium in TCM is defined as the "Emperor" (Heart) being calm so that the "Ministers" (other organs) can do their jobs. When the Shen is disturbed, the entire 24-hour circadian rhythm (Lesson 3) is thrown into chaos. Regulating the Seven Emotions is, at its core, an act of protecting the Shen.

Techniques for Emotional Clearing

As a practitioner, you need practical tools to give your clients. Here are two primary TCM-based techniques for regulating emotional Qi:

1. The Six Healing Sounds (Liu Zi Jue)

This ancient Qi Gong practice uses specific vocal vibrations to "vibrate" the organs and release stagnant emotional Qi. For example, the sound "Xu" (pronounced 'shoo') helps release Anger from the Liver, while "Hu" (pronounced 'who') releases Worry from the Spleen.

2. The Inner Smile

This is a meditative practice where the client "smiles" into each organ, acknowledging the emotion stored there and offering gratitude. This shifts the internal environment from rejection/suppression to acceptance/flow.

Practitioner Safety

Emotional Transference: When working with clients' emotions, you may "pick up" their stagnant Qi. Always practice "Clearing Your Own Channels" between sessions by washing your hands in cold water or doing three "Lung Clearing Breaths."

Social Qi and Boundaries

Equilibrium does not exist in a vacuum. Our Qi is constantly interacting with the Qi of others. TCM calls this the field of Wei Qi (Protective Qi). If a client has "leaky" boundaries, they are constantly losing Heart Qi to "Energy Vampires" or stressful environments.

Social Qi Management includes:

  • The "No" as Medicine: Helping clients realize that saying "no" to an over-commitment is actually a prescription for their Spleen and Heart.
  • Filtering the Environment: Reducing exposure to "toxic" news or social media that triggers Fear (Kidney) or Anger (Liver).
  • Cultivating 'He' (Harmony): Engaging in community and relationships that nourish the Heart Blood.
Client Language

The "Bank Account" Analogy: Tell your clients: "Your Heart Qi is like a bank account. Every time you over-worry or let someone cross your boundaries, you're making a withdrawal. If you don't make deposits through Emotional Hygiene, you'll eventually go into 'Qi Bankruptcy' (Burnout)."

Check Your Understanding

1. Which organ is most directly affected by "Qi Knotting" due to overthinking?

The Spleen. Pensiveness and over-analytical thinking cause the Qi to knot in the Middle Jiao, leading to digestive issues and fatigue.

2. What is the mechanical effect of Fear on Qi?

Fear causes Qi to Descend. This is why extreme fear can lead to involuntary urination or a feeling of weakness in the legs (Kidney territory).

3. Why is the Heart called the "Monarch" in relation to emotions?

The Heart houses the Shen (Spirit), which coordinates and integrates all emotional experiences. A calm Heart allows for the regulation of all other organ-based emotions.

4. What is the progression of chronic emotional stagnation?

It typically moves from Qi Stagnation ‚Üí Heat ‚Üí Blood Stasis or Phlegm. This explains how a long-term emotional state can manifest as a physical mass or chronic inflammation.

Key Takeaways

  • Emotions are internal causes of disease when they are excessive or unexpressed, directly altering Qi flow.
  • Each of the Seven Emotions corresponds to a Zang-Fu organ (e.g., Anger/Liver, Worry/Spleen).
  • Emotional Hygiene involves active techniques like the Six Healing Sounds to prevent Qi stagnation from becoming physical pathology.
  • Protecting the Shen (Heart Spirit) is the foundation of mental clarity and systemic equilibrium.
  • Healthy boundaries are a form of "Wei Qi" that prevents the depletion of internal Heart energy.

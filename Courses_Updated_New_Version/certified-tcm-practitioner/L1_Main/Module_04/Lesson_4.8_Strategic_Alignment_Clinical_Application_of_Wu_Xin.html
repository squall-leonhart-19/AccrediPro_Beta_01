Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Professor" Trap: Spending 20 minutes explaining the history of the Five Elements. The client wants to know how it helps them, not the history of the Han Dynasty.
  • Ignoring the "Why": Telling a client to eat warm foods (Earth) without explaining that it "stokes their internal fire" to help them stop worrying.
  • Technical Jargon: Using terms like "Liver Qi Stagnation" without immediately following up with a plain-English translation.
  • Rushing the Assessment: Not letting the client finish their story because you think you've already "spotted the element."
Sarah's Insight

If you feel imposter syndrome creeping in, remember: You are the expert in this room. Linda knows her symptoms, but you know the map. Trust the map.

Practice Lab Review

1. Why is it important to use metaphors like "the tree in the wind" with clients?

Metaphors bridge the gap between complex TCM theory and the client's lived experience, making the concepts relatable and reducing skepticism.

2. In the Linda scenario, which element was "overacting" and causing the digestive issues?

The Wood element (associated with the Liver and stress/frustration) was overacting on the Earth element (associated with the Spleen and digestion).

3. What is the "Professor Trap" in coaching?

It is the tendency to over-explain theory and history instead of focusing on the client's specific goals and how the theory applies to their healing.

4. What is a physical sign that a client is beginning to align with your coaching?

Visible relaxation, such as shoulders dropping, a deep sigh, or leaning forward in engagement.

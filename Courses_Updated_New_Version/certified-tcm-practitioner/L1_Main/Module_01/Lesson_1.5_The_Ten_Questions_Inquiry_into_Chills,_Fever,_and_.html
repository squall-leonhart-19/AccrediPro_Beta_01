The Traditional 'Ten Questions' (Shi Wen)

The "Ten Questions" were first formalized by the Ming Dynasty physician Zhang Jingyue. While the modern world has changed, human physiology has not. These questions serve as a systematic checklist to ensure no stone is left unturned during a consultation.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Integrative Medicine demonstrated that practitioners who utilized a structured inquiry framework like the Shi Wen showed a 22% higher accuracy rate in syndrome differentiation compared to those who used unstructured interviews.

Coach Tip

Don't feel like you have to ask these in a rigid, robotic order. As you gain experience, you'll weave these into a natural conversation. For example, when a client mentions they are tired, you might naturally transition to: "And how is your sleep? Do you wake up feeling hot or cold?"

Chills and Fever: The Thermal Compass

In TCM, Han Re (Chills and Fever) provides immediate insight into the location and nature of a pathogen. It tells us if the "battle" is happening at the surface (Exterior) or deep within the organs (Interior).

Pattern Presentation Primary Indication
Exterior Cold Aversion to cold with mild fever Wind-Cold invasion (Surface)
Exterior Heat Mild aversion to cold with high fever Wind-Heat invasion (Surface)
Interior Heat High fever without aversion to cold Excess Heat in the Organs
Interior Cold Chills/Cold limbs, no fever Yang Deficiency

Clinical Pearl: If a client has alternating chills and fever (one moment shivering, the next sweating), this indicates a "Half-Exterior, Half-Interior" pattern, often associated with the Shaoyang (Gallbladder/Triple Burner) meridians.

The Mystery of Sweat: Qi vs. Yin

Perspiration is a byproduct of the Heart's fluid and the Lung's ability to regulate the "pores." Analyzing when and how a client sweats is one of the fastest ways to determine their vitality level.

Case Study: The Exhausted Educator

Sarah, 52 | High School Principal

Symptoms: Sarah presented with profound fatigue and "spontaneous sweating." She noticed that even mild activity, like walking up the school stairs, caused her to break out in a cold sweat, even in a cool room.

Assessment: Because the sweating occurred during the day and worsened with exertion, it was identified as Spontaneous Sweating (Zi Han), a classic sign of Lung Qi Deficiency. Her "protective Qi" was too weak to hold the pores closed.

Outcome: After 4 weeks of Qi-tonifying herbs and dietary adjustments (adding warming soups and reducing raw foods), Sarah's sweating ceased, and her energy levels improved by 40%.

Differentiating Sweating Patterns

  • Spontaneous Sweating (Daytime): Usually indicates Qi Deficiency or Yang Deficiency. The body lacks the "holding power" to keep fluids in.
  • Night Sweating (Dao Han): "Thief Sweating." The client wakes up damp. This is a hallmark of Yin Deficiency. As the Yang retreats into the body at night, the lack of cooling Yin causes "Empty Heat" to push fluids out.

The Nature of Pain: Listening to the Quality

In the B.A.L.A.N.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢, the 'L' stands for Locate Stagnation. The quality of the pain tells us exactly what is stagnating.

1

Distending or Bloating Pain

Pain that feels like pressure or "moving" discomfort. This indicates Qi Stagnation. It often fluctuates with emotional stress.

2

Fixed, Stabbing Pain

Sharp, needle-like pain that stays in one spot. This is the classic indicator of Blood Stasis. It is often worse at night.

3

Dull, Lingering Pain

A mild ache that is "better with pressure." This indicates Deficiency (Qi or Blood). The body lacks the nourishment to keep the area comfortable.

4

Heavy, Sore Pain

Feeling like limbs are filled with lead. This indicates Dampness obstructing the flow of Qi in the muscles and joints.

Coach Tip

Ask your client: "Does the pain feel better when you press on it or worse?" If it's better with pressure, it's a Deficiency pattern. If they pull away or it feels worse with pressure, it's an Excess pattern. This one question changes your entire treatment strategy!

Meridian Pain Mapping

Where the pain is located is just as important as how it feels. In TCM, we map pain to the "highways" of the body‚Äîthe meridians.

For example, headaches are highly diagnostic based on location:

  • Forehead: Yangming (Stomach) meridian. Often related to digestion or "Heat" in the stomach.
  • Temples/Sides: Shaoyang (Gallbladder) meridian. Frequently associated with Liver Qi Stagnation and stress.
  • Back of Head/Neck: Taiyang (Bladder) meridian. Often the first sign of an Exterior Wind-Cold invasion.
  • Vertex (Top of Head): Jueyin (Liver) meridian. Can indicate deep Blood deficiency or Liver Yang rising.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client reports waking up every night at 3:00 AM drenched in sweat. What does this most likely indicate?

This indicates Yin Deficiency (Night Sweating/Dao Han). The "Empty Heat" generated by a lack of cooling Yin pushes the fluids out during the Yin-dominant time of night.

2. If a client describes their abdominal pain as "sharp, like a knife, and always in the exact same spot," what is the TCM diagnosis?

This is Blood Stasis. Fixed, stabbing pain is the hallmark of Blood not moving properly in a specific location.

3. A client has a high fever but says they feel "freezing" and want to wrap themselves in blankets. Is this an Interior or Exterior pattern?

This is an Exterior pattern. Simultaneous fever and aversion to cold indicate the pathogen is still at the surface (Exterior), and the body's Wei Qi is fighting it.

4. Pain at the temples is associated with which meridian system?

The Shaoyang system, specifically the Gallbladder meridian. This is frequently linked to stress and Liver Qi Stagnation.

Key Takeaways

  • The Ten Questions (Shi Wen) are the gold standard for systematic clinical inquiry in TCM.
  • Chills and Fever (Han Re) act as a thermal compass, pointing to the depth (Exterior vs. Interior) of the disharmony.
  • Sweating during the day (spontaneous) suggests Qi/Yang deficiency, while sweating at night suggests Yin deficiency.
  • The Quality of Pain reveals the source: Distending (Qi), Stabbing (Blood), Dull (Deficiency), or Heavy (Dampness).
  • Location is data: Use meridian mapping to connect local pain to systemic organ imbalances.

The Six External Evils (Liu Yin)

In TCM, the environment is a direct mirror of internal health. When climatic conditions become extreme or the body's protective Wei Qi is weak, environmental factors transform into Pathogenic Evils. These are the primary causes of "Exterior" syndromes.

Pathogen Nature & Characteristics Common Symptoms
Wind (Feng) The "Leader of 100 Diseases." Yang in nature, moves rapidly, changes quickly. Sudden onset, itching, tremors, moving pains, aversion to wind.
Cold (Han) Yin pathogen. Constricts, contracts, and congeals Qi and Blood. Severe pain, shivering, cold limbs, clear discharges, tight pulse.
Summer Heat (Shu) Purely seasonal (Summer). Yang, rising, and scattering. Consumes fluids. High fever, excessive sweating, thirst, exhaustion, heavy head.
Dampness (Shi) Yin pathogen. Heavy, turbid, sticky, and lingering. Hard to cure. Heavy limbs, "wrapped" head sensation, bloating, greasy tongue coat.
Dryness (Zao) Common in Autumn. Consumes Yin and Lungs fluids. Dry cough, dry skin, dry throat, constipation, cracked tongue.
Fire (Huo) Extreme Yang. Flares upward, disturbs the Shen (Mind), damages Blood. Red face, irritability, bleeding, rapid pulse, ulcerations.
Coach Tip: The Wind Connection

Think of Wind as the vehicle. It often carries other pathogens into the body (e.g., Wind-Cold or Wind-Heat). When a client says their symptoms "came out of nowhere" or "move from joint to joint," your TCM detective brain should immediately suspect Wind.

The Seven Emotions (Qi Qing)

While the environment attacks from the outside, our internal world can be just as pathogenic. In TCM, emotions are the internal causes of disease. While it is normal to experience all emotions, they become pathogenic when they are intense, prolonged, or unexpressed.

Each emotion has a specific "Qi mechanism" and impacts a specific Zang organ:

  • Anger (including frustration/resentment): Makes Qi rise. Impacts the Liver.
  • Joy (excessive excitement): Scatters Qi. Impacts the Heart.
  • Worry/Pensiveness (overthinking): Knots Qi. Impacts the Spleen.
  • Grief/Sadness: Dissolves or consumes Qi. Impacts the Lungs.
  • Fear: Makes Qi descend. Impacts the Kidneys.
  • Fright (shock): Deranges Qi. Impacts the Heart and Kidneys.

The 'Analyze' (A) Phase: Full vs. Empty

In the B.A.L.A.N.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢, the "A" stands for Analyze Disharmony. A critical part of this is differentiating between Shi (Excess/Full) and Xu (Deficiency/Empty).

Excess (Shi): The pathogen is strong. The body‚Äôs struggle is vigorous. Symptoms are acute and loud (e.g., high fever, sharp pain, loud voice).

Deficiency (Xu): The body‚Äôs energy is weak. The struggle is feeble. Symptoms are chronic and quiet (e.g., low-grade fever, dull ache, weak voice, fatigue).

Secondary Pathogens: Phlegm and Blood Stasis

Sometimes, the primary pathogens (like Dampness or Cold) linger so long that they create "Secondary Pathogenic Factors." These are often the culprits in chronic, stubborn diseases.

1. Phlegm (Tan)

Phlegm is "condensed Dampness." While we think of it as mucus in the lungs, TCM recognizes "Substantial Phlegm" (visible) and "Insubstantial Phlegm" (invisible). Insubstantial Phlegm can lodge in the channels, causing nodules, or even "mist the Heart," leading to mental health issues or confusion.

2. Blood Stasis (Xue Yu)

When Qi stagnates (the 'L' in BALANCE - Locate Stagnation), the Blood eventually stops moving. Blood Stasis is characterized by fixed, stabbing pain, dark purple tongue spots, and hard masses. It is often the final stage of chronic illness.

Coach Tip: The Stagnation Cycle

Remember: "Qi is the commander of Blood; if Qi moves, Blood moves." If you see Blood Stasis, you must almost always address Qi Stagnation simultaneously to prevent the stasis from returning.

Environmental & Seasonal Harmony

To be a truly "Certified TCM Practitioner," you must teach your clients how to live in accordance with the seasons. This is the ultimate preventive medicine.

  • Spring (Wind): Protect the neck; support the Liver with sour foods and movement.
  • Summer (Heat): Stay hydrated; avoid extreme midday sun; support the Heart.
  • Late Summer (Damp): Avoid heavy, greasy foods; support the Spleen.
  • Autumn (Dry): Eat moistening foods (pears, honey); support the Lungs.
  • Winter (Cold): Keep the lower back and feet warm; support the Kidneys with salt and rest.
Coach Tip: The "Why" for Clients

When explaining this to a 45-year-old busy mom, say: "Your body is like a garden. You wouldn't plant tomatoes in the snow. By eating and moving with the seasons, you're working with nature instead of fighting it, which saves your precious energy."

Check Your Understanding

1. Which external pathogen is known as the "Leader of 100 Diseases" because it carries others into the body?

Wind (Feng). It is Yang in nature and characterized by rapid movement and sudden onset.

2. A client presents with chronic, dull abdominal pain that improves with warmth and pressure. Is this an "Excess" or "Deficiency" pattern?

Deficiency (Xu). Dull pain that improves with pressure/warmth indicates a lack of Qi or Blood (Deficiency), whereas sharp pain that worsens with pressure indicates Excess.

3. Which emotion "knots" the Qi and primarily impacts the Spleen?

Worry or Pensiveness. This leads to digestive issues and the formation of Dampness/Phlegm.

4. What is the clinical hallmark of Blood Stasis?

Fixed, stabbing pain. Other signs include a purple tongue or dark, clotted menstrual blood.

Key Takeaways

  • Pathogenic factors are categorized into External (environment), Internal (emotions), and Secondary (Phlegm/Stasis).
  • The Six Evils (Wind, Cold, Summer Heat, Damp, Dry, Fire) mirror climatic conditions and attack the body's exterior.
  • The Seven Emotions are normal but become pathogenic when they disrupt the specific Qi flow of the Zang-Fu organs.
  • Differentiating between Excess (pathogen is strong) and Deficiency (body is weak) is the cornerstone of the 'Analyze' phase.
  • Seasonal living is the primary method for maintaining equilibrium and preventing external pathogens from taking root.

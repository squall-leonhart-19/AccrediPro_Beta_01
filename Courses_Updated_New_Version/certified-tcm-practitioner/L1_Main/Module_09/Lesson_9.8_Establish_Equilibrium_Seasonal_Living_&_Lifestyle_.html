Common Mistakes to Avoid

The Mistake Why It Happens The Professional Correction
Over-explaining Theory Wanting to prove your knowledge (Imposter Syndrome). Keep TCM theory to 1-2 sentences. Focus on the benefit to the client.
Rushing the Intake Feeling pressured to get to the "solution." Silence is a coaching tool. Let the client finish their thought before you jump in.
Ignoring the Emotion Feeling uncomfortable with the client's sadness/anxiety. Acknowledge it. "I can see this is heavy for you." Validation is 50% of the healing.
Giving Too Many Tasks Wanting to "fix" everything at once. Stick to 1-2 small changes per session. Success breeds more success.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes their shoulders or takes a deep breath after you validate their pattern.
  • Collaborative Language: The client starts saying "We" (e.g., "So we are going to work on my sleep first?").
  • Clarity: The client can repeat back the 1-2 steps they are taking this week.
  • Energy Shift: The client moves from a state of "worry" to a state of "curiosity" about their health.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Home" analogy effective for explaining the Heart/Shen relationship to a client like Diane?

It transforms a complex metaphysical concept (Shen) into a relatable, physical image. It helps the client understand why "emptiness" (Deficiency) leads to "restlessness" (Agitation) without needing a degree in TCM.

2. What is the primary purpose of asking "Does it feel right to you if we start here?"

It establishes a coaching partnership. It ensures the client is "bought in" to the plan, which significantly increases compliance and long-term success.

3. If a client has a pale tongue and a thin, rapid pulse, which B.A.L.A.N.C.E. pillar should you likely prioritize?

B - Blood & Qi Nourishment. The pale tongue and thin pulse indicate a deficiency that needs to be built up before more aggressive "clearing" or "moving" strategies are used.

4. How should you respond if a client starts crying when discussing their health goals?

Hold the space. Stay present, offer a brief validation ("It's okay to let that out, this is a safe space"), and wait for them to be ready to continue. Do not rush to "fix" the crying.

The 'Dissociative View': Observing Without Overwhelm

The "Dissociative View" is a clinical necessity in PLR. It creates a psychological buffer between the clientâ€™s current consciousness and the past-life persona. Without this buffer, the client may experience a full abreaction (re-living the trauma), which can be counter-productive if it happens too early in the session.

As a therapist, you are the "Director." You must teach the client to be the "Camera."

Coach Tip: The 10-Foot Rule

If a client begins to breathe heavily or show signs of distress during the 'O' phase, use the 10-foot rule. Say: "Move back ten feet. See it all from a distance. You are safe, you are here with me, you are simply watching a screen." This restores the objective stance immediately.

Establishing the 'Identity Snapshot'

Once the client is in a neutral state, your first task is to anchor them in the past-life environment. We do this through the Identity Snapshot. This is a rapid-fire collection of data points that define the "Soul's Avatar."

Data Point Questioning Strategy What it Reveals
Gender/Age "Look at your hands. Are they large or small? Smooth or weathered?" Biological vessel and life stage.
Social Status "Notice the fabric against your skin. Is it rough? Is it silk?" Economic power and societal constraints.
Geographical "Look at the horizon. What is the light like? What is the terrain?" Climate and potential historical era.
Temporal "Is there a sense of a year, or a name for this place?" Historical context (often comes as an 'impression').

Non-Leading Questioning: The Art of the Prompt

One of the biggest mistakes new practitioners make is "planting" details. Asking "Are you in a castle?" is a leading question. It forces the subconscious to agree or disagree with your suggestion. To maintain the integrity of the S.O.U.R.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢, we use open-ended prompts.

The Syntax of Neutrality

  • Instead of: "Is it cold?" Use: "What is the temperature like?"
  • Instead of: "Are you sad?" Use: "What emotions are present in that body?"
  • Instead of: "Do you see a house?" Use: "What is the most prominent structure in your field of vision?"
Coach Tip: Trust the First Impulse

Clients often doubt their first impression. When they say "I think I'm a man, but I'm not sure," respond with: "Don't think, just notice. What was the very first impression that flashed in your mind? Go with that." The first 0.5 seconds is usually the subconscious; the doubt that follows is the conscious mind.

Imagination vs. Authentic Recall

A common fear for clients (and therapists) is: "Am I just making this up?" Understanding the difference between Creative Imagination and Subconscious Recall is vital for your professional legitimacy.

Authentic recall typically has three distinct markers:

  1. Spontaneity: The details appear without effort, often surprising the client.
  2. Sensory Density: The client can smell the salt air or feel the weight of a heavy metal ring. Imagination is usually "flat" or purely visual.
  3. Emotional Resonance: Even in a dissociative state, the client feels a "knowing" or a deep soul-connection to the information.
Coach Tip: The Surprise Factor

If a client says, "I'm a soldier, but that's weird because I've always hated history," that is a high-quality marker of authentic recall. Imagination usually follows our conscious interests; the subconscious often brings up things we find boring or confusing.

Sharpening Multi-Sensory Perception (VAKOG)

To deepen the regression, we use the VAKOG model. This ensures the client isn't just "watching a movie" but is fully anchored in the experience.

V

Visual

Colors, light, shapes, and distances. "Notice the color of the sky."

A

Auditory

Sounds, languages, or silence. "What sounds are reaching your ears?"

K

Kinesthetic

Texture, weight, and temperature. "Feel the ground beneath your feet."

O

Olfactory

Smells and scents. "Is there a scent in the air? Woodsmoke? Sea salt?"

G

Gustatory

Tastes (less common but very grounding). "Is there a taste of dust or metal in your mouth?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the 'Dissociative View' used during the initial observation phase?

It creates a psychological buffer that prevents the client from becoming emotionally overwhelmed or experiencing an uncontrolled abreaction before the therapist is ready to process it.

2. Which of these is a non-leading question?

"What are you wearing on your feet?" is non-leading. "Are you wearing leather boots?" is leading because it suggests a specific detail.

3. What does the 'K' in VAKOG stand for, and why is it important?

'Kinesthetic' refers to physical sensations like texture, weight, and temperature. It is vital for anchoring the client's consciousness into the somatic memory of the past life.

4. How can you distinguish authentic recall from imagination?

Authentic recall is characterized by spontaneity (surprising details), sensory density (vivid smells/textures), and emotional resonance (a deep 'knowing').

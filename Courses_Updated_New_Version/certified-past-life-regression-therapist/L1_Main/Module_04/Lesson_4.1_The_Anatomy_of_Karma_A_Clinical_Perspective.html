In the S.O.U.R.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢, we move away from the religious or "moralistic" view of karma. From a clinical perspective, karma is subconscious momentum. It is the energetic and psychological "residue" of experiences that were never fully integrated, understood, or released.

The Three Layers of Karmic Expression

To navigate a regression effectively, you must understand where the client's current struggle sits within the broader karmic structure. We utilize a modified version of the Vedic layers of karma to categorize clinical data:

Karmic Layer Clinical Definition Therapeutic Focus
Sanchita (Accumulated) The "Warehouse." The total sum of all past experiences and unresolved imprints stored in the deep subconscious. Identifying the "Root Theme" that spans multiple lifetimes.
Prarabdha (Current) The "Active File." The specific portion of the warehouse that the client is meant to resolve in this lifetime. Addressing the presenting problem (e.g., a specific phobia or relationship block).
Agami (Future-Shaping) The "New Input." The actions and choices the client makes now based on their new insights. Integration and behavioral change in the present day.

Psychological Trauma vs. Karmic Imprinting

One of the most common questions new practitioners ask is: "How do I know if this is a childhood trauma or a past-life imprint?" The answer lies in the Somatic Bridge (Module 1). Biographical trauma usually has a clear "Alpha" point in this lifeâ€”an event the client remembers or can trace. Karmic imprinting, however, often feels "innate."

The Role of the 'Neutral Observer'

In Module 2, we introduced the Observer Stance. This becomes vital during the "R" (Resolution) phase. When a client encounters a karmic "debt" or a difficult past-life action, they may fall into emotional floodingâ€”shame, horror, or grief.

As the therapist, you must anchor them in the Neutral Observer position. You do this by asking: "Looking at this from a distance, what was the soul trying to learn through this experience?" This shifts the brain from the limbic system (emotional reaction) to the prefrontal cortex (analytical integration). We aren't looking for "excuses" for past actions; we are looking for the evolutionary objective.

Coach Tip: The Pivot Question

If a client says, "I was a terrible person in that life," immediately pivot. Ask: "If that version of you was a character in a book, what lesson was that character struggling to master?" This externalization is the key to clinical resolution.

The "Barefoot" Technique

I always ask about the feet first. Why? Because it's a "low-stakes" detail. The mind doesn't feel the need to hallucinate shoes as much as it does a whole face. Once they "see" their feet, the rest of the body and environment usually follows.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Even the most talented practitioners (including me, when I started!) fall into these traps. Awareness is 90% of the cure.

Mistake Why it Happens The Professional Correction
Leading the Client You want to "help" them see something. Use open-ended questions: "What do you notice?" instead of "Do you see a house?"
Rushing the Silence Silence feels awkward or like "nothing is happening." Allow 5-10 seconds for the client's internal "image" to render.
Asking "Why?" Seeking logical explanations too early. Avoid "Why are you there?" Ask "What is happening?" to keep them in the experience.
Fear of "Making it Up" The therapist doubts the validity of the session. Trust the process. Even "imagined" stories carry deep subconscious healing metaphors.
Sarah's Tip

If a client gets stuck, use the "Sense-Check" rotation: Sight, Sound, Touch, Feeling. "What do you see? What do you hear? What do you feel in your body?" This forces the brain to engage different neural pathways.

Success Markers

How do you know you've successfully navigated Diane into a past life? Look for these three markers:

  • Emotional Shift: The client's breathing changes or they display a micro-expression (a tear, a frown, a smile) that feels "unprompted."
  • Spontaneous Detail: They mention a detail they couldn't have planned, like "the smell of wet wool" or "a missing button on a sleeve."
  • The "Shift" in Tense: They move from saying "I think I see..." to "I am standing in..." This indicates they have moved from observing to experiencing.

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane says, "I'm just making this up." What is the best response?

The best response is to validate and redirect: "That's fine, let your imagination play along for now. What is the very first thing you 'imagine' seeing?" This removes the pressure to be "right" and allows the subconscious to continue.

2. What is the primary reason for asking about the client's feet early in the regression?

It provides a physical "anchor" in the past life. It's a specific, sensory detail that is easy for the subconscious to provide, which helps ground the client's awareness in the new environment.

3. If a client is silent for 15 seconds after you ask a question, what should you do?

Wait. If they haven't spoken after 20-30 seconds, you can gently prompt them with: "Just tell me the very first thing that comes to mind, no matter how small."

4. Which of these is a "Leading Question" that should be avoided?

"Do you see the stone castle in front of you?" is leading. Instead, ask: "What kind of building, if any, do you see?"

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Confidence is Contagious: If you sound certain, the client will feel safe enough to let go.
  • Silence is a Tool: Use it to allow the client's internal imagery to sharpen.
  • Validate the Skeptic: Acknowledge the conscious mind ("I might be making this up") and then pivot back to the sensory experience.
  • Anchor with the Body: Use feet, hands, and clothing to build the past-life persona from the ground up.
  • You are the Guide, not the Driver: Your job is to ask the questions that allow their soul to reveal the story.

The Coaching Scenario

Client Profile: Elena, 52

Background: Elena is a retired high school principal from Chicago. She recently started a consulting business but finds herself "paralyzed" when it's time to speak at conferences, despite 30 years of experience.

Presenting Symptoms: Tightness in the throat, a feeling of "not being allowed" to share her wisdom, and a recurring dream of standing in a crowded marketplace where she cannot speak.

Goal: To find the origin of this "vocal block" and release the fear of being seen and heard.

Your Coaching Script: The Transition

Elena is already deeply relaxed. You have just completed the "Staircase Induction." Now, you are going to bridge her into the first past life scene. Read this out loud as you go.

YOU (The Therapist):
"And now, Elena, as you stand at the bottom of those stairs, notice a door in front of you. This door leads to a time and place that holds the root of this feeling in your throat. On the count of three, you will step through that door... and you will be there. One... two... three. Step through now."
YOU (The Therapist):
"Don't try to make anything happen. Just notice the very first thing that comes to you. Look down at your feet... Elena, what do you see on your feet?"
ELENA (Softly):
"It's... it's dusty. I see sandals. Thin leather straps. My feet are very dirty."
YOU (The Therapist):
"Good. Stay with that. Dirty feet, leather sandals. Now, look at your hands. Are they the hands of a man or a woman? A child or an adult?"
ELENA:
"A woman. Older hands. They are holding something... a bundle of herbs? I smell something sharp... like eucalyptus."

Step-by-Step Practice

  1. The Breath: Take three deep breaths. On the exhale, lower your shoulders. This settles your nervous system so the client doesn't "pick up" on your energy.
  2. The Prompt: Say this out loud: "Elena, as the smell of herbs becomes stronger, tell me... where are you? Are you indoors or outdoors?"
  3. The Pause: Count to five in your head before saying anything else. New coaches often talk too much because they are nervous about the silence.
  4. The Anchor: When she answers, repeat her words back to her. If she says "outdoors," you say: "You are outdoors... notice the temperature of the air on your skin."
Sarah's Insight

In my first year, I was so afraid of silence that I would ask three questions in a row. I learned that the silence is where the client's subconscious processes the imagery. Give them space to breathe!

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

The Mistake Why It Fails The "Pro" Fix
Leading the Witness Asking "Are you in a forest?" forces the client's brain to "create" a forest instead of "remembering" what's there. Ask: "What is the landscape like around you?"
Talking Too Fast High-speed speech triggers the Beta brainwave (analytical mind), which can snap the client out of trance. Use "The Heartbeat Pace"â€”slow, rhythmic, and calm.
Ignoring Sensory Cues Focusing only on "seeing" leaves out 40% of clients who are more kinesthetic or auditory. Ask about smells, sounds, and physical sensations first.
Over-explaining Explaining *why* you are asking a question breaks the immersion. Trust the process. Just ask the question.

Check Your Practice Knowledge

1. Elena says, "I'm in a dark room and I'm scared." What is your immediate next move?

Validate her feeling and offer safety: "You are safe here, Elena. I am with you. You are observing this from a distance. As you look at this 'dark room,' what is one thing you can touch or feel that tells you where you are?" Never ignore the fear; acknowledge it, then ground them in a sensation.

2. Why do we ask the client to "Look at your feet" first?

It is the most effective "grounding" technique. It forces the subconscious to provide a specific detail (shoes, floor, ground) which anchors the soul into the physical body of the past life, making the rest of the memories easier to access.

The Therapist as Neutral Facilitator

One of the most common mistakes new therapists make is "leading" the client. If you ask, "Do you see the sword in your hand?" you have contaminated the memory. If you ask, "What, if anything, are you holding?" you are a Neutral Facilitator.

Your role is to be the Active Witness. You provide the structure (The SOURCE Framework‚Ñ¢) while the client provides the content. This protects the session's integrity and ensures that the healing comes from the client's own soul, not your suggestions.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "leading question" and a "neutral prompt"?

A leading question suggests content (e.g., "Are you wearing a red dress?"), whereas a neutral prompt asks for information without bias (e.g., "What are you wearing, if anything?"). Neutral prompts protect the integrity of the soul memory.

2. What does the "S" in the SOURCE Framework‚Ñ¢ stand for, and why is it first?

It stands for Somatic Induction. It is first because the body is the most direct gateway to the subconscious; by relaxing the nervous system and focusing on body sensations, we bypass the critical conscious mind.

3. Name one "Somatic Marker" that suggests a client is accessing a real memory rather than imagination.

Involuntary physiological responses such as rapid eye movement (REM), spontaneous tears (lacrimation), changes in breathing patterns, or localized physical sensations that correspond to the narrative.

4. Why is Dr. Brian Weiss significant in the history of PLR?

Dr. Weiss provided clinical and medical legitimacy to the field in the 1980s by documenting his sessions as a psychiatrist, moving PLR from the "fringe" into the mainstream psychological conversation.

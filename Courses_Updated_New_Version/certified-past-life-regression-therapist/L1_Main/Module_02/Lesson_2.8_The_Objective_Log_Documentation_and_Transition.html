Hi, I'm Sarah!

Welcome to your first Practice Lab. In my practice, I've found that the difference between a "good" therapist and a "master" therapist is the ability to see what the client isn't saying. Today, we're going to practice exactly how to observe physiological shifts without letting our own biases color the session. Remember: your job is to be the "mirror," not the "narrator."

Learning Objectives
  • Identify 3 distinct physiological markers of a deepening trance state.
  • Practice neutral verbal mirroring to validate client experiences without leading.
  • Differentiate between subjective interpretation and objective observation.
  • Execute a verbal "observation check" to maintain client rapport during regression.
  • Document objective findings using the professional SOAP format.
Sarah's Pro Tip

Don't worry about "getting it right" the first time. Even after years of practice, I still catch myself occasionally interpreting instead of observing. The goal today is simply to build the muscle of awareness!

Sarah's Pro Tip

Notice how the therapist in the script says "I see you've developed a slight frown" instead of "You look worried." The first is an objective observation; the second is a subjective interpretation. Keep it factual!

Common Mistakes & Success Markers

As you transition into a professional practice, watch out for these common "rookie" errors that can disrupt the client's trance.

4 Common Mistakes New Therapists Make

  • Leading the Witness: Saying "You're feeling scared, aren't you?" instead of "I notice your heart rate has increased."
  • Ignoring the Body: Getting so caught up in the "story" of the past life that you miss the client's physical distress or deepening trance markers.
  • Filler Words: Using "Um," "Like," or "Okay" between observations, which breaks the hypnotic flow.
  • Over-Observing: Interrupting the client's flow to report every single micro-movement. Observation should be used to anchor, not to distract.
Sarah's Pro Tip

If you're feeling imposter syndrome, remember: Diane (your client) is looking for a safe container. Your ability to calmly observe her physical state provides that safety. You don't need to have all the answers; you just need to have "eyes that see."

Check Your Understanding

1. Which of the following is an OBJECTIVE observation?

"The client's breathing shifted from 18 breaths per minute to 12 breaths per minute." (This is a measurable, factual observation. "The client is relaxed" would be an interpretation).

2. Why is neutral verbal mirroring important during a regression?

It validates the client's experience and keeps them anchored in the session without the therapist's bias influencing the "plot" of the regression.

3. You notice a client's hand starts shaking during a difficult scene. What is the best "Objective" response?

"I notice your right hand is trembling slightly. Just be aware of that movement as you continue." (This acknowledges the physical reality without assigning an emotion like 'fear' to it).

4. What does REM (Rapid Eye Movement) usually signify during a PLRT session?

It is a primary indicator of a deep trance state and active "inner seeing" or visualization. It is a key marker to document in your SOAP notes.

Key Takeaways

  • Observation is a Skill: It requires practice to separate what we *see* from what we *think* it means.
  • The Body Never Lies: While the conscious mind may try to "perform," the autonomic nervous system provides the real data.
  • Language Matters: Use "I notice" or "I see" rather than "You are" or "You feel."
  • Documentation is Professionalism: Recording these observations in your SOAP notes builds your legitimacy and helps track client progress over time.
  • You Are the Anchor: Your steady, neutral observation allows the client to explore deep, sometimes scary, memories safely.

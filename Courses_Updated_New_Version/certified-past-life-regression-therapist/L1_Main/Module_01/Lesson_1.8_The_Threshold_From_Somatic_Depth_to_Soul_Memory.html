Common Mistakes to Avoid

In my early years, I made all of these! Catching these early will make you look like a pro from day one.

  • Rushing the Induction: New therapists often talk too fast because they are nervous. Slow down. Your calm voice is the client's safety tether.
  • Leading the Witness: Instead of saying "Do you see a battlefield?" ask "Where are you?" Let their body tell the story.
  • Ignoring the "Twitch": If you see Diane's finger twitch or her breathing change, mention it! "I noticed your breathing shifted... what's happening in the shoulder now?"
  • Over-Explaining: You don't need to explain how it works to the client during the session. Just lead.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask Diane about the "color" or "shape" of her pain?

It moves the brain from the analytical left hemisphere (logic) to the creative, sensory right hemisphere (subconscious), making regression possible.

2. What should you do if Diane says she "doesn't feel anything" in her shoulder during the induction?

Don't panic! Shift the focus to her breath or the weight of her body in the chair. Any physical sensation can serve as the bridge.

3. What is the primary purpose of the "Look at your feet" prompt?

It anchors the client in the first-person perspective of the past life, preventing them from "watching" the memory like a movie and instead "living" it.

4. Diane's breathing becomes rapid and shallow during the bridge. Is this a good sign?

Yes. It indicates a "Somatic Abreaction"â€”the body is re-experiencing the physiological state of the past memory. Acknowledge it and keep guiding her through.

Key Takeaways

  • The body is the most reliable "bridge" to past life memories because it bypasses the logical mind.
  • Pacing and silence are your most powerful tools as a therapist.
  • Always start with a concrete physical sensation (pain, weight, temperature).
  • Success is measured by the client's sensory immersion, not just the "story" they tell.

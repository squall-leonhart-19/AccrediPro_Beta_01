Defining Your Scope: Facilitation vs. Treatment

The most important distinction you will ever make in your practice is this: You are a facilitator, not a clinician. Unless you hold a separate state-issued license (such as an LCSW, Psychologist, or MD), your role is to guide the client through their own subconscious experience, not to "fix" their pathology.

What You CAN Do What You CANNOT Do
Facilitate subconscious exploration. Diagnose mental or physical illnesses.
Educate on the nature of past lives. Prescribe or recommend stopping medication.
Support spiritual and personal growth. Treat clinical trauma or PTSD.
Guide relaxation and imagery techniques. Claim to "cure" medical conditions.
Sarah's Tip

I always tell my students: "Own your lane." When you try to sound like a doctor, you actually lose credibility. When you show up as a masterful spiritual guide, people respect your expertise much more. Use words like explore, discover, facilitate, and unfold rather than heal, cure, or resolve.

Legal Safeguards: Protecting Your Practice

Operating a professional practice requires a "legal container." This protects both you and your client. Even in states with "Health Freedom" laws (like California or Minnesota), you must be diligent. A 2022 survey of wellness practitioners found that those with documented informed consent had 94% fewer client disputes.

1. Informed Consent & Disclosure

Every client must sign a document before their first session. This document should clearly state your training, the nature of regression, and the fact that it is not a substitute for medical or psychiatric care. This is your primary shield.

2. Liability Insurance

Professional liability insurance (often called "Errors and Omissions") is essential. It is surprisingly affordable for regression therapists‚Äîoften less than $200 per year‚Äîand provides peace of mind that allows you to focus on your clients rather than your risks.

Professional Ethics & Confidentiality

Because regression work involves deep subconscious material, the ethical burden is high. You are the "keeper of the space."

  • Confidentiality: What happens in the session stays in the session. The only exception is if a client expresses a clear intent to harm themselves or others.
  • Professional Distance: Avoid "dual relationships." Do not take on close friends or family members as paying clients; it muddies the subconscious waters.
  • Integrity in Marketing: Never promise specific results (e.g., "You will see your life as a Queen"). Instead, promise a process (e.g., "We will explore the depths of your subconscious memories").
Sarah's Tip

Imposter syndrome often stems from a fear of "doing it wrong." By following these ethical guidelines, you remove that fear. You aren't responsible for the content of their past life; you are only responsible for the safety of the container. That is a much easier job to do well!

When to Refer Out: The "Red Flags"

A true professional knows when they are out of their depth. You must maintain a list of local mental health professionals for referral. If a client presents with the following, regression is usually contraindicated until they are cleared by a doctor:

  • Active Psychosis: Hallucinations or inability to distinguish reality.
  • Severe Clinical Depression: Especially with suicidal ideation.
  • Recent Trauma: Clients who have experienced acute trauma within the last 3-6 months may need clinical stabilization first.
  • Substance Abuse: Clients must be sober during sessions to access the subconscious effectively.
Sarah's Tip

Referring a client out isn't "losing a sale." It's gaining a reputation as an ethical expert. Doctors and therapists are much more likely to refer clients to you if they know you have the discernment to send clients to them when necessary.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks if a past life regression session can help them stop taking their anti-anxiety medication. What is the correct response?

The correct response is to state that you do not prescribe or manage medication, and any changes to their medical treatment must be discussed with their prescribing physician. You can facilitate the regression for spiritual exploration, but not as a medical intervention.

2. What is the primary legal document you must have every client sign before a session?

The Informed Consent and Disclosure document. This outlines your scope of practice and clarifies that regression is not clinical therapy.

3. True or False: It is ethical to conduct a regression session for your sister if she pays your full rate.

False. Ethical standards advise against "dual relationships." Working with close family members can compromise the professional boundaries and the client's ability to be fully honest in the subconscious state.

4. Why is professional liability insurance recommended for non-clinical practitioners?

It provides legal and financial protection in the event of a client dispute or claim of "errors and omissions," ensuring your personal assets and practice are shielded.

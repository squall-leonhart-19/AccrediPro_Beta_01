Your Coaching Scenario

In this lab, you are working with Linda, a 54-year-old librarian who has entered a light trance state. She has just reported seeing a "stone floor." Your goal is to help her expand this single image into a full narrative without leading her.

Client Detail Status
Client Name Linda, 54
Primary Goal Understanding a recurring feeling of "heavy responsibility" in her current life.
Current Trance Depth Medium (Somatic markers: slowed breathing, slight REM).
Starting Point "I see a stone floor. It's cold."

When a client gives you a sensory detail like "cold," that is a Somatic Anchor. Don't ignore it! It‚Äôs the thread you‚Äôll pull to unravel the whole story.

The Narrative Script

Practice saying these lines out loud. Notice how the coach (YOU) never tells Linda what is there; you only ask her to observe.

YOU:
"Focus on that coldness of the stone floor. As you feel that coldness, what else do you notice around your feet?"
LINDA:
"I... I have sandals on. They‚Äôre old. Leather straps."
YOU:
"Good. Notice those leather straps. Now, slowly let your awareness move upward. What are you wearing on the rest of your body?"
LINDA:
"A heavy robe. It's rough. It's... brown. It feels heavy on my shoulders."
YOU:
"Notice that heaviness on your shoulders. As you feel that weight, look around the room. What is the source of light in this space?"
LINDA:
"A small window. High up. There's a desk... lots of paper. I feel... tired. Like I've been working for hours."
The Power of "What Else"

The phrase "What else do you notice?" is your best friend. It signals to the client's subconscious that there is more to see without specifying what that "more" should be.

Practice Out Loud

Don't just read‚Äîactually speak. Your voice is your primary tool in PLR therapy.

  • Step 1: Record yourself reading the "YOU" lines from the script above on your phone.
  • Step 2: Listen back. Is your tone warm? Are you pausing long enough for the client to "see" the image?
  • Step 3: Try an "Off-Script" prompt. Imagine Linda says, "I hear a bell ringing." Practice saying out loud: "As you hear that bell, what emotion do you feel in your chest?"

Common Narrative Mistakes

Avoid these "new coach" pitfalls to ensure your sessions remain professional and effective.

  • Leading Questions: Instead of "Are you in a church?", ask "Where does this building feel like it might be?"
  • Rushing the Scene: Giving the client only 2 seconds to respond. (Wait at least 5-10 seconds for deep trance responses).
  • Over-Explaining: Trying to interpret the symbols *during* the session. Save the analysis for the post-regression debrief.
  • Ignoring the Body: Forgetting to ask how the character *feels* physically (heaviness, heat, tension).

Success Markers

How do you know you've successfully uncovered the narrative? Look for these signs:

  • Sensory Richness: The client moves from "I'm in a room" to "I smell beeswax and hear the scratching of a quill."
  • Emotional Connection: The client's voice changes pitch or they exhibit physical signs of emotion (tears, smiles).
  • Narrative Flow: You no longer have to "pull" information; the client begins describing the scene spontaneously.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask about the "source of light" in a scene?

Asking about light helps the client orient themselves in the space and often reveals the time of day, the atmosphere, and the type of environment (outdoors vs. indoors) without the coach suggesting it.

2. What is a "Leading Question" in the context of narrative uncovering?

A leading question is one that suggests a specific answer or detail, such as "Do you see a sword?" This can cause the client's conscious mind to "invent" a detail rather than letting the subconscious reveal it.

3. If a client says, "I feel very sad," what is the best narrative-uncovering follow-up?

"Notice where that sadness lives in your body right now... as you feel that, what is happening in the scene that makes you feel this way?" (Linking emotion to narrative).

4. How long should you wait for a client to answer in a medium-to-deep trance?

At least 5 to 10 seconds. Subconscious processing is slower than conscious conversation, and rushing can break the trance state.

Key Takeaways

  • Narrative uncovering is about expanding existing details, not inventing new ones.
  • Use Somatic Anchors (cold, heavy, tight) to bridge the gap between the body and the story.
  • The phrase "What else do you notice?" is a versatile tool for deepening any scene.
  • Silence is a therapeutic tool; give the client's subconscious time to translate images into words.
  • Success is marked by the client's transition from reporting to experiencing the narrative.

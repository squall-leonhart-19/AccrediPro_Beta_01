The Power of Clean Language

One of the greatest risks in Past Life Regression (PLR) is the unintentional leading of the client. When you ask, "Is the man wearing a red coat?" you have just implanted "man" and "red coat" into the client's subconscious field. If they weren't sure before, they are now‚Äîbut is it their memory or your suggestion?

Developed by David Grove, Clean Language is a communication framework designed to keep the practitioner's metaphors, assumptions, and biases out of the client's experience. In the S.O.U.R.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we use Clean Language to ensure the narrative is 100% authentic to the client's subconscious.

Coach Tip: The Mirror Effect

üí° As a career changer, you might feel the urge to "help" the client find the answer. Resist this. Your value isn't in providing the answers, but in being the mirror. Use the client's exact words. If they say "I feel a heaviness," don't ask about the "sadness." Ask, "And what kind of heaviness is that heaviness?"

Leading Question (Avoid) Clean Question (Use) Goal
"Do you feel sad about the betrayal?" "And how do you feel about that?" Removes emotional assumption.
"Is there anyone else in the room?" "And is there anything else?" Removes the assumption of "people."
"Why are you running away?" "And what happens next?" Removes the assumption of "running" or "fear."

Vertical Inquiry: Moving from Surface to Soul

Many novice therapists get stuck in "Horizontal Narrative"‚Äîsimply following the events: I went to the market, I bought bread, I saw a soldier. While interesting, this rarely leads to healing. Vertical Inquiry is the technique of stopping the "horizontal" flow to dive "vertically" into the internal meaning of a specific moment.

Vertical Inquiry follows a specific sequence:

  1. Observation: "What are you seeing/doing?"
  2. Sensation: "Where do you feel that in your body?"
  3. Internal State: "What is the thought behind that feeling?"
  4. Soul Meaning: "What does this moment want you to know about your soul's journey?"

Case Study: Sarah, 52 (Former School Administrator)

Focus: Transitioning from Burnout to Purpose

Sarah came to therapy feeling "stuck" in her new coaching business. During regression, she saw herself as a monk in 12th-century France, meticulously copying manuscripts.

Horizontal Narrative: "I am sitting at a desk. The candle is flickering. I am writing."
Vertical Inquiry Intervention: The practitioner asked: "And as you write, what is the meaning of those words to your heart?"
The Breakthrough: Sarah's voice changed. "The words don't matter. It's the silence I'm protecting. I'm not writing; I'm hiding from the world's noise."

This revealed a "Soul Contract" of silence that was preventing Sarah from marketing her modern business. By going vertical, we moved from "writing" to "hiding."

Using Open-Loop Questioning

The subconscious mind is a natural problem-solver. It dislikes "open loops" (unfinished patterns). You can use this to your advantage by asking questions that don't have a simple 'yes' or 'no' answer, but instead require the subconscious to "fill in the blanks" to provide narrative depth.

Examples of Open-Loop prompts include:

  • "And as you look at his eyes, you realize..." (Pause and wait for the client to finish).
  • "The reason your soul chose this specific moment is because..."
  • "There is a connection between that life and your current life, and that connection is..."
Coach Tip: Embrace the Silence

üí° When you open a loop, the client may be silent for 30-60 seconds. To a new practitioner, this feels like an eternity. To the client, their brain is processing 1,000 images per second. Do not speak. Let the subconscious finish the loop.

Bypassing Narrative Resistance

Sometimes, the client's "Conscious Gatekeeper" interferes. This manifests as "I don't know," "It's just my imagination," or "Everything went black." This is rarely a lack of memory; it is usually a protective mechanism.

To bypass this, we use Displacement Techniques:

  • The "If You Did Know" Reframe: Client: "I don't know who is behind the door." Practitioner: "I understand you don't know. But if you did know, what would be your first guess?" (This gives the conscious mind permission to 'pretend,' which lowers the guard).
  • The Somatic Bridge: "If you can't see the person, just feel the temperature of the air around them. Is it warm or cold?" (Shifting senses often bypasses visual blocks).
  • The Perspective Shift: "Float above the scene. Looking down from 100 feet up, what is the general shape of the landscape?"
Practice Building Tip

üí° Experienced practitioners who can navigate resistance effectively often charge $250-$400 per session. Clients will pay for results, and your ability to "unstick" a session is what builds your reputation as an expert, not just a hobbyist.

The Art of the Pivot Question

The Pivot Question is the most advanced tool in the S.O.U.R.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢. It is used when the narrative has reached its peak intensity. It shifts the focus from the external event (the "What") to the karmic lesson (the "Why").

Common Pivot Questions:

  • "In the grand scheme of your soul's evolution, why was it necessary for you to experience this?"
  • "If this life was a book, what would be the title of this chapter?"
  • "What is the one thing you are carrying from that moment that no longer serves the woman you are today?"
Coach Tip: Timing the Pivot

üí° Don't pivot too early. If the client is in the middle of a somatic release (crying or shaking), let the body finish. The Pivot is a cognitive-spiritual integration tool; it works best once the emotional "peak" has started to plateau.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Clean Language" essential in Past Life Regression therapy?

It prevents the practitioner from leading the client or contaminating the narrative with their own biases, ensuring the memories and metaphors are 100% authentic to the client's subconscious.

2. What is the primary difference between Horizontal and Vertical Inquiry?

Horizontal Inquiry follows the chronological events of a story, while Vertical Inquiry dives into the internal meaning, somatic sensations, and soul-level lessons of a specific moment.

3. A client says, "I'm just making this up." Which technique should you use?

The "If You Did Know" Reframe or "Displacement Technique." You might say, "That's fine, let your imagination continue. If you were making up what happens next, what would it be?" This bypasses the conscious critic.

4. When is the most effective time to use a "Pivot Question"?

After the emotional peak of a scene has been experienced but before the client exits the trance state, specifically to shift focus from the event to the karmic lesson.

Key Takeaways

  • Questioning is an Art: Your role is to be a "midwife" to the story, not the author.
  • Clean Language: Use the client's exact vocabulary to maintain narrative purity.
  • Vertical Depth: Always look for the meaning behind the image. A "red dress" is never just a red dress; it is a feeling of power, shame, or visibility.
  • Resistance is Information: When a client is blocked, it's a sign that the subconscious is protecting something significant. Use displacement to move around the block.
  • The Goal is Resolution: Use Pivot Questions to ensure the client leaves the session with a lesson, not just a memory.

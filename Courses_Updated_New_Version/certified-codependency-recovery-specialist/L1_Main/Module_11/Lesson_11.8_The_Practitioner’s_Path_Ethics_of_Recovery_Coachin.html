Sarah's Tip: Use "Body Checks"

When you offer a re-frame like the one above, always ask the client how it feels in their body. A shift from addiction to attachment usually feels like a "letting go" in the shoulders or a deeper breath. That is the biological marker of recovery.

Sarah's Tip: Handle the "Imposter" Voice

You might feel like you need a PhD to talk about the nervous system. You don't. You only need to be one step ahead of the client in your own regulation. Your presence is the intervention.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Correcting the client too harshly: Don't say, "You're wrong, you're not an addict." Instead, offer an alternative: "I wonder if there's another way to look at that..."
  • Rushing to the "Fix": New coaches often try to give homework immediately. In this phase, the validation is the work.
  • Over-explaining the Science: Don't give a 20-minute lecture on the Vagus nerve. Keep it simple: "Your body is trying to keep you safe."
  • Ignoring your own dysregulation: If you feel your heart racing while the client cries, take a breath. They will follow your lead.
Sarah's Tip: The Power of "I Wonder"

Start your re-frames with "I wonder..." It lowers the client's defenses and invites curiosity instead of resistance. It's my favorite tool in my coaching "bag."

Success Markers

How do you know the lab was successful? Look for these signs in your practice session:

  • The client's breathing becomes more rhythmic and less shallow.
  • The client uses a "softening" word (e.g., "Maybe I'm not just bad...").
  • The client stops the "shame spiral" of self-insults for at least 5 minutes.
  • You feel a sense of "closeness" or "attunement" in the conversation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to shift from the "Addiction" model to the "Attachment" model for a client like Elena?

The addiction model often reinforces shame by focusing on "defects." The attachment model removes shame by explaining behaviors as survival strategies, which allows the nervous system to relax and actually begin the work of change.

2. What is a "Body Check" in coaching?

A body check is asking the client to notice physical sensations (tightness, openness, breath) following a coaching intervention. It confirms whether the re-frame has been "accepted" by the nervous system.

3. What does it mean to "model co-regulation"?

It means the coach maintains a calm, regulated state (through breathing and tone) so that the client's mirror neurons can "sync" with the coach, helping the client move out of a fight/flight state.

4. True or False: You should stop a client from calling themselves an "addict" immediately.

False. You should validate their experience first, then gently offer an alternative perspective ("I wonder if...") to avoid creating resistance or appearing dismissive of their past recovery work.

Key Takeaways

  • Codependency recovery is evolving from a "moral" issue to a "biological" one.
  • Your primary tool is your presence and your ability to re-frame shame into survival.
  • Practical success is measured by the client's physiological shift, not just their intellectual agreement.
  • Helping clients like Elena see their "fixing" as a "protector" is the first step toward true autonomy.

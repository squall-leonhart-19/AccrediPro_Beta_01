Soft Limits vs. Firm Limits

Use this table to help Linda (and yourself) see the difference in language. Soft limits invite negotiation; firm limits provide clarity.

Type Example Language The Result
Soft Limit "I'm not sure if I can Friday, I'm really tired..." The other person pushes harder to "help" you find a way.
Firm Limit "I am unavailable to babysit this Friday." The boundary is clear; no room for "fixing" your schedule.
The "Explain-A-Thon" "I'd love to but my new boss is so mean and I have a headache..." Provides "data" for the other person to argue against.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Validation Trap": Trying to convince the client's daughter (or boss) that the limit is fair. We only care if the limit is necessary for the client.
  • The "Apology Reflex": Starting every coaching suggestion with "I think maybe you could..." Use authoritative language: "Try this phrasing."
  • Ignoring the Body: Forgetting to ask where the client feels the "No" in their body. Limits are felt before they are spoken.
  • Solving the Logistics: If you start helping Linda find a different babysitter for her daughter, you are now being codependent with your client. Stop. Focus on Linda's limit, not the daughter's problem.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "I'm sorry" often discouraged in initial limit-setting for codependents?

Because it reinforces the false belief that having a personal need or limit is an offense that requires an apology. It "softens" the limit into a request for forgiveness.

2. What is "Limit Leakage"?

When a client's resentment or exhaustion "leaks" out through passive-aggression or physical symptoms because they haven't set a verbal, firm limit.

3. A client says, "I'll try to say no." What should the coach do?

Challenge the word "try." In the LIMITS framework, "trying" is a back-door for failure. Encourage the client to commit to the action or explore the fear preventing the commitment.

4. If a coach starts suggesting ways for the client's daughter to find childcare, what has happened?

The coach has entered into a codependent dynamic with the client, attempting to "fix" the external situation rather than coaching the client's internal boundary.

Key Takeaways

  • Limits are Facts: Treat a limit like a weather report, not a negotiation.
  • Neutrality is Power: The less you emote while setting a limit, the more authority it carries.
  • The Guilt Gap: Expect the client to feel guilt; coach them through sitting with it rather than fixing it.
  • Language Matters: Eliminate "I'm sorry," "I think," and "Maybe" from the initial limit statement.

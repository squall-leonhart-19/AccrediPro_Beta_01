The Coaching Script

Practice these words out loud. Pay attention to your toneâ€”it should be warm, steady, and non-judgmental.

ELENA:
"I did it again, Sarah. I spent three hours last night obsessing over why my daughter didn't text me back. I'm just a hopeless codependent. I'm addicted to the chaos."
YOU:
"Elena, I hear the frustration in your voice. But I want to offer you a different lens. What if you aren't 'addicted to chaos,' but rather, your nervous system is simply searching for a signal of safety? When she doesn't text back, your 'attachment alarm' goes off. Does that feel different in your body than calling it an addiction?"
ELENA:
"An attachment alarm? I guess... it feels less like I'm 'bad' and more like I'm just... scared. But isn't that just a fancy word for codependency?"
YOU:
"It's a more accurate word. Codependency is often just a set of behaviors we learned to survive an insecure attachment. When you obsess over the phone, you are trying to find 'proximity' to her to feel safe. Our work isn't to 'break an addiction,' but to help you become a Secure Base for yourself."

Common Pitfalls & Success Markers

The Pitfall (Avoid This) The Success Marker (Aim For This)
Agreement: Saying "Yeah, that's definitely codependent of you." Reframing: Identifying the underlying attachment need (e.g., "That sounds like a search for connection.")
Advice-Giving: "You should just put your phone in another room." Somatic Inquiry: "Where do you feel that 'need to check' in your body right now?"
Pathologizing: Treating Elena like she is "ill" or "broken." Normalizing: "Your brain is doing exactly what it was wired to do in an unpredictable environment."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is shifting from "Addiction" to "Attachment" language important for Elena?

It reduces shame and moves the client from a "fixed" mindset (I am an addict) to a "growth" mindset (I have a nervous system that can learn to feel safe).

2. What is the primary role of an Attachment-Informed coach?

To act as a "temporary secure base" for the client, providing the co-regulation they may have missed in childhood so they can eventually self-regulate.

3. Elena says, "I feel like I'm losing my mind when he doesn't call." What is this biologically?

This is "Separation Distress" or an "Attachment Cry." Her nervous system perceives the lack of contact as a threat to her survival.

4. How should you respond when Elena calls herself "toxic"?

Gently interrupt the label and reframe it: "I see a woman who is working very hard to protect her relationships using the only tools she was given."

Practice Lab Key Takeaways

  • Language Matters: Swapping "addiction" for "attachment alarm" immediately lowers cortisol and shame.
  • Be the Base: Your calm, steady presence (prosody and pace) is more healing than the "perfect" advice.
  • Biology over Behavior: Always look for the survival need behind the codependent action.
  • Progress over Perfection: You are becoming a specialist by learning to see the *unseen* attachment patterns.

The Client Scenario: Meeting Elena

In my practice, I find that many women in their 50s come to coaching when the "Sandwich Generation" pressure peaks. They are caring for aging parents and supporting adult children, all while feeling like their own life has become a series of chores they never signed up for.

Elena, 52

Background: A former elementary school teacher who retired early to help care for her mother (with dementia) and her 24-year-old son who moved back home after a breakup.

Presenting Problem: Elena feels "trapped" and "resentful." She says she has no choice but to do everything for everyone, yet she is physically and emotionally exhausted.

Goal: She wants to feel less angry, but she doesn't believe she can change her circumstances because "family comes first."

Sarah's Insight

Clients like Elena often confuse Agency with Selfishness. As her coach, your job isn't to tell her to stop helping her family; it's to help her realize she is choosing to help, and that she can choose different terms for that help.

The Coaching Script: Reclaiming Choice

The following script focuses on the "Want vs. Should" transition. Practice saying these words out loud to get used to the cadence of a professional recovery coach.

ELENA:
"I just don't have a choice, Sarah. If I don't go over there and clean my mom's kitchen, it won't get done. And if I don't cook for my son, he just eats junk. I'm stuck."
YOU (Coach):
"I hear how heavy that feels, Elena. It sounds like youâ€™ve been operating under the belief that these aren't choices, but requirements. Can we try a small experiment with your language?"
ELENA:
"I guess so... but it feels like a requirement."
YOU (Coach):
"I want you to repeat this sentence after me: 'I am choosing to clean my mother's kitchen because I value her comfort.' Just try it on for size."
ELENA:
"I am... choosing to clean her kitchen. It feels weird to say. It feels like I'm lying."
YOU (Coach):
"It feels weird because it shifts the power back to you. If you are choosing it, you can also choose the extent of it. When you say 'I have to,' you are a victim of the kitchen. When you say 'I choose to,' you are the agent of your own time. How does your body feel when you own the choice?"
Practice Tip

Don't rush the silence after Elena says it feels "weird." Let her sit with the discomfort of owning her power. This is where the neural pathways begin to shift.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I first started coaching women like Elena, I fell into several traps. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • Rescuing the Client: Trying to give Elena "tips" on how to clean faster or how to get her son to cook. This reinforces her lack of agency.
  • Judging the Family: Saying "Your son is being lazy." This makes the client defensive and shuts down the session.
  • Ignoring the Body: Forgetting to ask where the client feels "obligation" in their body (usually the chest or throat).
  • Moving Too Fast: Trying to get the client to set a major boundary (like moving out) before they even own the choice to stay.

Professional coaches who specialize in Agency and Boundary Work for high-functioning women often command fees of $200+ per hour. Why? Because you aren't just giving advice; you are fundamentally changing how they interact with the world.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the phrase "I am choosing to..." so vital in codependency recovery?

It shifts the client from a "victim" mindset (where things happen to them) to an "agentic" mindset (where they own their actions). This ownership is the prerequisite for setting boundaries.

2. If Elena says, "But I don't WANT to clean the kitchen, so how can I say I'm CHOOSING it?", how should you respond?

Explain that we often choose things we don't "like" because we value the outcome (e.g., her mother's health). Owning the choice prevents the resentment that comes from feeling forced.

3. What is the "Sandwich Generation" dynamic?

It refers to middle-aged adults (usually women) who are "sandwiched" between the needs of their aging parents and their adult children, often leading to a total loss of personal agency.

4. What is a physical sign that a client is beginning to reclaim their agency?

Often, a client will take a deep, spontaneous breath, their shoulders will drop, or they will sit up straighter as they stop "bracing" against perceived obligations.
Sarah's Final Thought

You are becoming a real coach. Every time you resist the urge to "fix" and instead offer a client their own power back, you are doing the deep work. Keep practicing!

Success Markers: How to Know the Session Went Well

  • The client used the word "choose" or "choice" at least three times.
  • The client identified a "should" and reframed it as a value-based decision.
  • You (the coach) spoke less than 30% of the time, allowing the client to process.
  • The client reported a shift in their physical tension after the reframe.
  • You avoided giving "advice" and instead offered "perspective."

When we blame others for our feelings or circumstances, we hand them the keys to our happiness. Today, you will learn how to help clients take back those keys.

Case Study: The Blame Loop

Sarah, 48, Former Elementary School Teacher

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah entered coaching feeling "trapped" in her marriage. She spent 80% of her sessions detailing her husband's lack of emotional support, his messy habits, and how his mood "ruined" her weekends. She felt exhausted, resentful, and powerless.

Intervention: We introduced the concept of Radical Self-Responsibility. Instead of focusing on his mess, we focused on her choice to clean it up while fuming, or her choice to stay in the room when he was in a bad mood.

Outcome: By shifting from "He makes me miserable" to "I am making myself miserable by expecting him to be someone he isn't," Sarah reclaimed her agency. She eventually decided to separate, not from a place of anger (Victim), but from a place of clear self-advocacy (Agent).

The Karpman Drama Triangle: The Death of Agency

Developed by Stephen Karpman, the Drama Triangle is a social model of human interaction that explains the destructive cycle of codependency. In this triangle, agency is impossible because every role depends on the others to maintain a false sense of identity.

Role The Internal Narrative The Agency Cost
The Victim "Poor me! I'm helpless and it's all your fault." Total loss of power; relies on others to "save" them.
The Rescuer "I must help you because you can't do it yourself." Avoids own problems; creates dependency in others.
The Persecutor "It‚Äôs all your fault! You‚Äôre so incompetent." Uses blame to control; avoids vulnerability.

In codependency, the client often starts as the Rescuer (over-functioning for others). When the other person doesn't change or show enough gratitude, the client slips into the Victim role ("After all I've done for you!"). Finally, they may become the Persecutor (lashing out in resentment). Radical Self-Responsibility is the only exit ramp from this triangle.

Practitioner Insight

A 2021 study on relational dynamics found that individuals who frequently occupy the "Victim" role in the Karpman Triangle show a 40% higher cortisol level during conflict compared to those who practice assertive "Agentic" communication. Blame isn't just a mindset; it's a physiological stressor.

100% Emotional Ownership: "I Am Feeling..."

The most radical shift in the R.E.C.L.A.I.M. Method‚Ñ¢ occurs when a client realizes that while they cannot control what others do, they are 100% responsible for how they process that action.

Consider the difference in these two statements:

  • Externalized: "You made me feel so small when you interrupted me in front of the kids."
  • Radical Responsibility: "I felt small when I was interrupted, and I realize I have a core wound around being heard. I am responsible for setting a boundary or removing myself from the conversation."

Taking ownership doesn't mean the other person's behavior was "right." It means you are no longer a passive recipient of their behavior. You are the active manager of your internal state.

Responsibility 'For' vs. Responsibility 'To'

Codependents suffer from "Responsibility Confusion." They take responsibility for things they cannot control (other people's feelings, choices, and outcomes) and neglect responsibility for the one thing they can (themselves).

The Specialist's Distinction:

  • Responsibility TO: We have a responsibility to be kind, to be honest, and to respect others' boundaries. This is about our behavior.
  • Responsibility FOR: We are responsible for our own happiness, for our own healing, and for our own reactions. We are NOT responsible for how someone else reacts to our "No."
Specialist Tip

When a client says, "I can't set that boundary because it will hurt his feelings," they are trying to take responsibility FOR his feelings. Remind them: "You are responsible TO be clear and respectful; he is responsible FOR his emotional reaction to your truth."

The Power of the Internal Locus of Control

Psychologically, "Locus of Control" refers to the degree to which people believe that they, as opposed to external forces, have control over the outcome of events in their lives.

Research published in the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology indicates that individuals with an Internal Locus of Control experience:

  • Lower rates of clinical depression (approx. 25% lower in longitudinal studies).
  • Higher career satisfaction and income potential (averaging 15-20% higher earnings for women in mid-career).
  • Greater resilience following relationship breakdowns.

In codependency recovery, we are moving the "Locus" from the partner/parent/child back into the Core of the client. This is the "A" in Agency‚Äîrealizing that your life is the result of your choices, not just your circumstances.

Practical Exercises for Reclaiming Power

As a Specialist, you need tools to help your clients transition from blame to agency. Use these three exercises during your sessions:

1. The "What is My Part?" Audit

When a client complains about a situation, ask: "If you had to take just 5% responsibility for how this situation continues to exist, what would that 5% be?" This bypasses the ego's defense and opens the door to agency.

2. The "Choose To" Language Shift

Have the client list five things they "have to" do this week (e.g., "I have to cook dinner for my ungrateful family"). Then, have them rewrite them as "I choose to..." (e.g., "I choose to cook dinner because I value health, OR I choose to order pizza because I need a break").

3. The Response-Ability Pause

Teach the client the "Three-Second Rule." When triggered, they must wait three seconds before responding. In those three seconds, they ask: "Am I reacting as a Victim, or responding as an Agent?"

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "My boss is so critical that I can't get any work done." Which Drama Triangle role are they likely occupying?

They are occupying the Victim role. They are attributing their lack of productivity (their responsibility) entirely to an external force (the boss).

2. What is the primary difference between being responsible "TO" someone versus "FOR" someone?

Responsibility TO involves your own conduct (honesty, respect, clarity). Responsibility FOR involves attempting to own their outcomes, feelings, or choices‚Äîwhich is a codependent violation of agency.

3. According to research, how does an Internal Locus of Control affect mental health?

It is associated with significantly lower rates of depression, higher resilience, and better professional outcomes because the individual views themselves as the primary driver of their life.

4. Why is blame considered the "prison" of codependency?

Blame is a prison because it makes your well-being dependent on the other person's behavior. If they don't change, you can't feel better. Radical Self-Responsibility provides the "key" by making your well-being your own task.

Key Takeaways

  • The Drama Triangle is a cycle of Victim, Rescuer, and Persecutor; Radical Self-Responsibility is the only way out.
  • 100% Ownership means accepting that while you don't control the trigger, you are responsible for the reaction.
  • Shift the Locus: Moving from an external to an internal locus of control is the fundamental goal of the "Agency" phase of RECLAIM.
  • Language Matters: Replacing "I have to" with "I choose to" immediately restores a sense of personal power.
  • Boundaries are Agency: Setting a boundary is taking responsibility to be honest, while letting go of responsibility for the other person's reaction.

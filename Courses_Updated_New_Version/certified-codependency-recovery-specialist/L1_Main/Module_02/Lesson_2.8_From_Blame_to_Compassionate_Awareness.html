4 Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  1. The "Fix-It" Trap: Giving Elena a chore chart or time-management tips before examining the why.
  2. Over-Identifying: Saying "I totally get it, my son is the same way!" (Keep the focus on Elena).
  3. Moving Too Fast: Jumping to "Set a boundary!" before Elena understands why she hasn't set them for 30 years.
  4. Judging the Family: Calling the husband "lazy" or the son "spoiled." This makes the client defensive. Focus on Elena's part of the dance.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask about Elena's family of origin during the Examine phase?

It helps the client see that their current behavior is a learned survival strategy, which reduces shame and opens the door for change.

2. What is the primary emotion often hidden behind Elena's "exhaustion"?

Resentment. Resentment is the "smoke" that points to the "fire" of violated boundaries and over-functioning.

3. If Elena gets defensive when you mention her son, what should you do?

Pivot back to her feelings. Use a statement like, "I can see how much you love him. Let's focus on how YOU feel when the laundry piles up, regardless of whose it is."

Success Markers

How do you know if your "Examine" session was successful? Look for these indicators:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically relaxes or sighs as they feel validated.
  • The "Connection": The client says, "I never realized I was doing exactly what my mother did."
  • The "Shift": The client stops talking about what others are doing and starts talking about their own internal urges.

Key Takeaways

  • The Examine phase is about discovery, not correction.
  • Your "Coach Voice" should be warm, curious, and non-judgmental.
  • Validation is the "bridge" that allows a client to look at painful patterns without retreating.
  • Success is measured by the client's internal clarity, not by immediate external changes.

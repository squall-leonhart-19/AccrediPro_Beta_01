Common Pitfalls to Avoid

New coaches often fall into these traps when working with high-anxiety recovery clients:

  • Giving Advice Too Early: Telling the client to "just say no" before they have processed the underlying guilt.
  • Taking on the Client's Anxiety: If you start feeling the "knot" in your stomach, you have lost your differentiation.
  • Validating the Victim Narrative: Agreeing that the family member is "terrible" instead of focusing on the client's response.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much theory (e.g., "Well, according to attachment theory...") instead of staying in the emotional moment.
Pro Tip

In your first few months, you might feel like an imposter. That's normal! Stick to the frameworks we've practiced. Your clients are paying for your presence and your process, not your perfection.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why did the coach ask Linda where she felt the decision in her body?

To interrupt the cognitive "loop" of codependent worrying and ground the client in her own physical experience, which is the first step toward differentiation.

2. What is the primary risk of answering a client's "What should I do?" question?

It replicates the codependent dynamic where the client relies on an external authority rather than building their own internal "muscle" for decision-making and boundary-setting.

3. If Linda says, "I'm a bad mother if he ends up on the street," what is the best facilitator response?

An empathetic confrontation: "Thatâ€™s a very heavy label. Is 'bad mother' a fact, or is it a feeling triggered by fear?"

4. How does a coach demonstrate "differentiation" during a session?

By remaining calm, warm, and present without becoming emotionally "infected" by the client's panic or urgency.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Your primary role is to be a mirror, not a map. Mirrors show the client where they are; maps tell them where to go.
  • Codependency recovery happens in the "pause" between a trigger and a reaction. Use your coaching sessions to expand that pause.
  • Professionalism in this field means maintaining clear emotional boundariesâ€”if you care more about the client's progress than they do, you are over-functioning.
  • Empowerment is found when the client realizes they can survive the discomfort of someone else's disapproval.

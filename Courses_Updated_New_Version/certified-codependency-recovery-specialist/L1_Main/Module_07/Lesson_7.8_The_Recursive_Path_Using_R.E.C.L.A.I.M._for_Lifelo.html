The Mastery Coaching Script

As a Certified Codependency Recovery Specialistâ„¢, your role isn't to give Diane permission. It's to help her find it within herself. Practice the following script out loud to build muscle memory.

DIANE:
"I know I should be working on my website launch, but my daughter called crying again. She can't pay her rent. If I don't help her, she might get evicted. Am I a terrible mother if I just stay focused on my business?"
YOU (The Coach):
"Diane, I hear the weight in your voice. Before we look at the 'mother' label, let's notice what's happening in your body right now as you ask that question. Where do you feel that 'terrible mother' thought?"
DIANE:
"It's a huge knot in my stomach. I feel like I'm failing her."
YOU (The Coach):
"That knot is a familiar visitor, isn't it? In our work on Mastery, we've talked about the 'Guilt Alarm.' Is this alarm telling you that you've actually done something wrong, or is it just a programmed response to her distress?"
DIANE:
"It feels programmed... but it's so loud. I just want you to tell me it's okay to let her figure it out."
YOU (The Coach):
"I could tell you that, Diane, but then you'd be relying on my authority instead of your own. If you were the version of Diane who fully trusts her business and her boundaries, what would SHE say to that knot in your stomach?"
Coach Tip: Resisting the Pull

Notice how Diane asks for permission. New coaches often jump in with "Of course it's okay!" Mastery-level coaches hold the space for the client to find their own "yes." This is how you help them achieve lasting change.

Common Mastery-Phase Pitfalls

Even experienced coaches can slip back into old habits when a client is in distress. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

1
Over-Validating

Giving too much "You're right, she's being manipulative" talk. This keeps the focus on the daughter, not Diane's internal process.

2
The "Fix-It" Reflex

Suggesting ways the daughter can find money. You are a Codependency Specialist, not a financial advisor. Stay in your lane.

3
Ignoring the Somatic

Talking only about the "logic" of boundaries. Mastery requires staying with the physical sensation of guilt until it dissipates.

4
Fear of Client Discomfort

Letting the client off the hook because they are crying. Growth happens in the "productive tension" of the session.

Success Markers: How You Know It Worked

In a Mastery-level session, success looks different than in the early stages. Look for these indicators:

  • Shift in Agency: Diane stops asking "What should I do?" and starts saying "I realize I am doing..."
  • Somatic Release: The "knot" in her stomach visibly relaxes or moves.
  • Differentiation: She can speak about her daughter's crisis with empathy but without taking on the daughter's panic.
  • Action Alignment: She commits to a specific business task (the website launch) despite the external drama.

Check Your Mastery Skills

1. When Diane asks, "Am I a terrible mother?", what is the most "Mastery-level" response?

The best response is to direct her attention inward (e.g., "Where do you feel that thought in your body?") rather than giving external validation. This builds her self-trust.

2. What is the "Fixer Trap" in this scenario?

The "Fixer Trap" is suggesting ways for the daughter to pay rent or brainstorming solutions for the daughter's problem, which keeps Diane enmeshed in the drama.

3. Why is vocal tonality important in this specific Practice Lab?

A steady, calm tone provides a "secure base" for the client. If your voice goes up or sounds frantic, you mirror the client's anxiety rather than holding the space for mastery.

4. How do you identify a "codependent pull" from the client?

It usually sounds like a request for permission, a plea for the coach to "take the wheel," or the client trying to please the coach by saying what they think the coach wants to hear.

Key Takeaways for Mastery

  • Mastery coaching is about facilitating internal authority, not providing external answers.
  • The "Guilt Alarm" is a somatic signal that requires processing, not a factual report of wrongdoing.
  • Your posture and vocal tonality are just as important as the words you use in the script.
  • Success is measured by the client's shift from "What should I do?" to "I know who I am."

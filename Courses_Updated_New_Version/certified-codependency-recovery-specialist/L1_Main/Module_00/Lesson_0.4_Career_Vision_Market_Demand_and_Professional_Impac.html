What You Can and Cannot Do

The most important thing to remember is that we are coaches and educators, not clinicians. We do not treat "mental illness"; we facilitate behavioral and relational recovery. This distinction is what allows you to practice safely and effectively.

The Specialist Role (CAN DO) The Clinical Role (CANNOT DO)
Educate on codependent patterns and family systems. Diagnose Personality Disorders (BPD, NPD, etc.).
Provide Tools for boundary setting and self-regulation. Treat clinical depression or acute PTSD.
Hold Accountability for present-day goals. Process deep-seated childhood trauma (EMDR, etc.).
Support recovery from toxic relationship dynamics. Prescribe or manage psychiatric medications.
Coach Sarah's Tip

If you ever feel "imposter syndrome" creeping in because you aren't a therapist, remember this: Therapists often focus on the 'Why,' but Specialists focus on the 'How.' Clients come to us because they are tired of understanding their past and are ready to change their now. Your practical, action-oriented approach is exactly what they are looking for.

Legal Foundations for Your Practice

As a practitioner in the United States (and most Western countries), you are operating under the umbrella of Health and Wellness Coaching. While coaching is currently a self-regulated industry, there are "Best Practice" standards you must follow to mitigate risk.

1. Professional Liability Insurance: Even though we don't diagnose, you should carry "Professional Liability" or "Errors and Omissions" insurance. Providers like CPH & Associates or HISCOX offer affordable policies specifically for wellness coaches (often under $200/year).

2. The Disclosure Statement: Every client must sign a document stating they understand you are not a licensed therapist or medical doctor. This protects the "Educational Model" of your practice.

Ethics, Professionalism, and Referrals

Integrity is the currency of a successful practice. Because codependency often involves high-conflict relationships or narcissistic abuse, you will eventually encounter a situation that is "out of bounds."

When to Refer Out

You must have a referral list of 2-3 local (or tele-health) therapists ready. You must refer out if a client demonstrates:

  • Active Suicidal Ideation: Any talk of self-harm or a plan.
  • Active Substance Abuse: If they are currently under the influence during sessions or in acute addiction.
  • Domestic Violence: If the client is in immediate physical danger (refer to a domestic violence shelter/hotline).
  • Severe Mental Illness: If they appear to be experiencing psychosis or mania.
Coach Sarah's Tip

Referring a client isn't "losing a sale." It is the highest form of professional care. Often, a client can work with a therapist for their trauma and work with you for their recovery skills simultaneously. This is called Concurrent Care, and it's highly effective!

Check Your Understanding

1. A client tells you they have been diagnosed with Bipolar Disorder and wants you to help them manage their medication. What is your response?

You must decline managing medication. You should state: "As a Recovery Specialist, I don't manage clinical diagnoses or medications. I'm happy to work with you on relationship boundaries, but you must continue to see your psychiatrist for your Bipolar management."

2. What is the main difference between the "Specialist" model and the "Clinical" model?

The Specialist model is educational and action-oriented (the "How"), while the Clinical model is diagnostic and often focuses on processing past trauma (the "Why").

3. Is it legal to practice as a Codependency Recovery Specialist without a therapy license?

Yes, provided you do not use protected titles (like "Psychologist" or "Licensed Counselor") and you stay within the scope of wellness coaching and education.

4. What is "Concurrent Care"?

When a client works with both a licensed therapist (for clinical/trauma issues) and a Recovery Specialist (for practical life skills and accountability) at the same time.

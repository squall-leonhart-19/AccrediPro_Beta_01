Success Markers & Common Pitfalls

How to Know the Session Went Well

  • The "Sigh of Relief": The client feels seen and understood, rather than judged for their enabling.
  • Shift in Language: The client starts using terms like "pattern," "over-functioning," or "attachment" instead of just complaining about the other person.
  • Clarity: The client can identify at least one specific "cost" of their codependency (e.g., "I realized I haven't seen my own friends in three months").

4 Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

  1. Becoming the "Fixer": Trying to solve the son's problems instead of Diane's internal patterns.
  2. Using Shame-Based Labels: Using the word "codependent" as a slur or a diagnosis rather than a descriptive pattern of behavior.
  3. Taking Sides: Joining the client in "bashing" the other person, which prevents the client from looking at their own role.
  4. Rushing to Action: Trying to set boundaries before the client has fully recognized the need for them.

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane says, "I have to keep helping him, or he'll end up on the street." Which coaching response best aligns with the "Recognize" phase?

The best response is to recognize the internal feeling: "I hear how much fear is driving this for you. That fear is a powerful part of the pattern we're looking at." Avoid giving tactical advice on eviction yet.

2. What is the primary goal of the "R" (Recognize) phase in the CCRSâ„¢ methodology?

The goal is to move the client from "unconscious competence/incompetence" to "conscious awareness"â€”helping them see the repeating patterns of their behavior and attachment style without judgment.

3. Why do we avoid the "Fix-It" mode in this initial stage?

Because jumping to "fixing" reinforces the client's belief that their value comes from external results. We want to model a different way of being: focusing on internal awareness first.

4. True or False: In a $200/hour coaching session, you should spend at least 40 minutes giving the client a step-by-step action plan for their family members.

False. In specialized recovery coaching, the value is in the transformation of the client's self-perception. Action plans are secondary to internal recognition.

Key Takeaways

  • Language Matters: Use terms like "over-functioning" and "attachment" to provide a clinical framework that reduces shame.
  • Validation is Key: Diane isn't a "bad mother"; she is an "anxious attacher" trying to find safety through control.
  • Stay in the "R": Don't rush to the "S" (Shift) or "E" (Evolve) phases until the client can accurately name their own patterns.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking these scripts out loud builds the confidence needed to charge professional rates ($150-$250+ per session).

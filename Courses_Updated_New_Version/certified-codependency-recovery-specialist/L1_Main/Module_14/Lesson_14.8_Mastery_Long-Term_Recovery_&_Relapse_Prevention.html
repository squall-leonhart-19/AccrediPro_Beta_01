Sarah's Insight

In my experience, women like Diane (and many of us!) often equate "helping" with "loving." When you coach her, your first job is to help her decouple those two concepts. If helping is hurting her, it's no longer an act of loveâ€”it's an act of enabling.

The Coaching Script

The "Boundary Integration" Dialogue

Practice saying these lines out loud. Notice the balance of empathy and professional firmness.

YOU:
"Diane, I can hear how much you love Jason. It's clear that your heart is in the right place. But I want to check in with youâ€”when you're cleaning his room or paying that bill, how does your body feel in that moment?"
DIANE:
"I feel... tight. Like there's a knot in my stomach. I'm doing it, but I'm angry while I'm doing it. Does that make me a terrible person?"
YOU:
"Not at all. That knot is actually your internal boundary trying to talk to you. It's telling you that you're over-extending. In this practice, we call that the 'Resentment Red Flag.' If we keep ignoring that flag, you'll eventually burn out completely. What would happen if, just for today, we honored that knot in your stomach?"
DIANE:
"He'd be so upset. He'd tell me I'm not supporting him. I don't know if I can handle the guilt."
YOU:
"I hear that fear. Guilt is often the 'tax' we pay for growth. Let's try a different script. Instead of saying 'I won't help you,' what if we said, 'Jason, I love you too much to keep doing things for you that you are capable of doing for yourself.' How does that phrase feel in your mouth?"

Common Coaching Pitfalls

The Mistake Why It Happens The Specialist's Fix
Over-Validating the Victimhood We want to be nice and empathetic. Validate the feeling, but challenge the behavior.
Giving Advice Too Soon We want to "fix" Diane's problem immediately. Ask: "What is the cost of staying exactly where you are?"
Taking on the Client's Guilt We feel bad for Diane's difficult situation. Remember: You are the coach, not the co-dependent partner. Stay grounded.
Using "Should" Language We think we know what's best for her. Replace "You should..." with "What would it look like if..."
Sarah's Insight

If you find yourself feeling "heavy" after a session with a client like Diane, check your own boundaries. Are you trying to "save" her the way she is trying to "save" Jason? We coach for empowerment, not for rescue.

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these signs:

  • Shift in Language: Diane moves from saying "I have to help him" to "I am choosing to step back."
  • Physical Release: You notice Diane's shoulders drop or she takes a deep, audible breath.
  • Actionable Step: She identifies one specific bill she will stop paying or one conversation she will have this week.
  • Internal Locus of Control: She expresses that her happiness is not 100% dependent on Jason's reaction.
Practice Prompt

Try this out loud right now: "Diane, you've identified that paying for the phone bill is fueling your resentment. What is one small step you can take this Thursday to begin transitioning that responsibility back to Jason?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask Diane how her body feels (the "knot in the stomach")?

Somatic awareness helps the client identify boundaries that their mind is trying to rationalize away. It grounds the recovery in physical reality rather than just "thinking" about it.

2. What is the "Resentment Red Flag" in codependency coaching?

It is the emotional signal that a boundary has been crossed. Resentment is almost always a sign that we have said "yes" when we meant "no" or are doing more than our fair share.

3. If Diane says, "But he'll be so angry," what is the most effective coaching response?

Acknowledge the fear, but pivot to her agency. For example: "It's very likely he will be angry. How can you take care of yourself while he processes that anger?"

4. True or False: A successful session always ends with the client feeling happy.

False. A successful session often ends with the client feeling "sober" or "determined," which may include feelings of healthy grief or temporary discomfort as they prepare to change.

Key Takeaways

  • Language Matters: Use phrases like "I love you too much to enable this" to help clients maintain connection while setting boundaries.
  • Resentment is Information: Teach clients to view their anger/resentment as a tool for identifying where boundaries are needed.
  • Professional Neutrality: Avoid the urge to "save" the client; instead, hold the space for them to save themselves.
  • Small Wins: Focus on one micro-boundary at a time (e.g., stopping one specific chore) to build the client's confidence.
  • You Are Ready: Practice makes permanent. You are becoming a real coach by showing up for these labs.

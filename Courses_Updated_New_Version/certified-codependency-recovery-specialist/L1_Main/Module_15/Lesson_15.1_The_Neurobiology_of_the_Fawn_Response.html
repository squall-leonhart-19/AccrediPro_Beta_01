Polyvagal Theory and the Mechanics of 'Fawn'

While most are familiar with Fight, Flight, and Freeze, the Fawn response (coined by Pete Walker) is a distinct neurobiological state. According to Polyvagal Theory, developed by Dr. Stephen Porges, our nervous system operates in a hierarchy.

The Fawn response is a hybrid state. It attempts to use the Ventral Vagal System (social engagement) while the Sympathetic Nervous System (mobilization/threat) is fully activated. It is essentially "socializing under duress."

The Amygdala-Prefrontal Cortex Tug-of-War

In a healthy brain, the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)â€”the "CEO" of the brainâ€”can regulate the Amygdalaâ€”the "smoke detector." In codependent individuals, especially those with a dominant fawn response, this connection is often frayed.

A 2021 study involving fMRI imaging showed that individuals with high levels of chronic people-pleasing exhibited hyper-reactivity in the amygdala when exposed to images of angry or disappointed faces. Simultaneously, their Medial Prefrontal Cortex (mPFC) showed decreased activity.

This means that when a conflict arises, the "thinking brain" goes offline, and the "survival brain" takes over. The client doesn't "choose" to agree with their partner; their brain is literally incapable of accessing logical boundaries in that moment of perceived threat.

Dopamine and Oxytocin: The Caretaking Reward Loop

Why is codependency so addictive? The answer lies in the neurochemical cocktail released during the "rescue" or "fix."

  • Oxytocin: Often called the "bonding hormone," oxytocin is released when we soothe someone. For a fawner, this provides a temporary sense of safety and "belonging" that counteracts the fear of abandonment.
  • Dopamine: When a codependent person successfully manages someone else's crisis, the brain's reward center (Nucleus Accumbens) releases dopamine. This creates a "helper's high."

Over time, the brain becomes dependent on these external hits of oxytocin and dopamine. The practitioner must understand that the client is self-medicating their anxiety through the act of caretaking.

Coach Tip

Ask your clients: "How do you feel in the 30 seconds after you've 'saved' someone?" They will often describe a rush of relief. That is the dopamine hit. Identifying the "hit" helps them see the behavior as a physiological compulsion rather than a personality trait.

Hyper-vigilance and the Loss of Interoception

Chronic fawning requires hyper-vigilanceâ€”the constant scanning of the environment for micro-expressions, tone shifts, and body language of others. This "outward-facing" focus has a devastating biological cost: the loss of interoception.

Interoception is the ability to sense the internal state of the body (hunger, heartbeat, emotional "gut feelings"). When the nervous system is 100% focused on their mood, it ignores your needs.

Healthy Empathy vs. Emotional Contagion

Many codependents pride themselves on being "empaths." However, neurobiology suggests a distinction between Cognitive Empathy and Emotional Contagion.

Emotional contagion occurs when the Mirror Neuron System is overactive and unregulated. The fawner doesn't just "understand" the other person's pain; they absorb it. Their nervous system synchronizes with the dysregulated person. This is not a gift; it is a lack of neurological boundaries.

Coach Tip

Teach your clients that "Your emergency is not my dysregulation." Recovery involves training the mirror neurons to observe without absorbing. This is a core skill in the Mastery phase of recovery.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the 'Fawn' response considered a "hybrid" state in Polyvagal Theory?

It attempts to use the social engagement system (Ventral Vagal) to pacify a threat while the Sympathetic Nervous System is activated. It is essentially "appeasement" used as a survival strategy.

2. What happens to the Prefrontal Cortex during a Fawn response?

The Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) often shows decreased activity or "goes offline," meaning the client loses access to logical reasoning and boundary-setting, while the amygdala becomes hyper-reactive.

3. How does chronic hyper-vigilance affect interoception?

By constantly focusing outward to scan for threats in others, the brain "mutes" internal signals. This leads to a disconnect from one's own physical and emotional needs (loss of interoceptive awareness).

4. What is the difference between healthy empathy and emotional contagion?

Healthy empathy allows you to understand another's feelings without losing your own emotional center. Emotional contagion is the involuntary "absorption" of another's dysregulation due to an overactive mirror neuron system.

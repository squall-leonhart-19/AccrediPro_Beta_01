Values vs. Inherited Norms

In the A.W.A.K.E.N. Method‚Ñ¢, alignment requires a clear understanding of what we are aligning to. Many parents believe they are parenting from their values, but upon closer inspection, they are actually parenting from inherited norms‚Äîthe rules, expectations, and "shoulds" passed down through generations or absorbed from culture.

Inherited norms often sound like: "Children should always be quiet in public," "Grades are the ultimate measure of success," or "Respect means immediate obedience." While these may contain grains of utility, they are often fear-based and control-oriented.

Authentic Core Values, however, are essence-based. They focus on the quality of the relationship and the long-term character of the child. Values like Integrity, Curiosity, Compassion, and Authenticity provide a stable foundation that doesn't rely on the child's "compliance" to feel valid.

Coach Tip: The "Why" Test

When a client insists a specific behavior is a "value" (e.g., "I value a clean house"), ask them why five times. Usually, they'll discover the root is either a fear of judgment (norm) or a desire for peace/stewardship (authentic value). Helping them find the essence allows for more flexible parenting strategies.

Sarah's Challenge

Struggling with her 14-year-old son's declining math grades. Her response was grounding, lecturing, and increased pressure.

Sarah realized her "value" of Achievement was actually an inherited norm from her high-pressure upbringing. She feared that if her son didn't excel, it reflected her failure as a parent. Through coaching, she identified her true core value: Resilience.

The Family Mission Statement

A Family Mission Statement is not a set of rules; it is a declaration of identity. It answers the question: "Who are we as a family, and how do we want people to feel when they are in our home?"

Research suggests that families with a shared sense of purpose and identity show higher levels of resilience and lower levels of internal conflict. A 2021 study in the Journal of Family Psychology found that adolescents who could articulate their family's core values were 34% less likely to engage in high-risk behaviors during times of stress.

Steps to Facilitate a Mission Statement:

  • The Values Dump: Have each family member (age-appropriately) list words that describe their "best" days together.
  • The Essential Three: Narrow the list down to 3-5 non-negotiable pillars (e.g., Kindness, Growth, Play).
  • The Action Draft: Turn these into "We" statements. "In this family, we prioritize listening over being right."

Values as Decision Filters

The most practical use of values is as a Decision Filter. When a parent is faced with a dilemma‚Äîwhether it's a discipline issue or a choice about extracurricular activities‚Äîthey can run it through their "North Star."

Scenario The "Norm" Reaction The "Values" Filter (Value: Integrity)
Child lies about homework. Punishment/Grounding to "teach a lesson." Focus on why safety felt absent; model honesty about own mistakes.
Invitation to a high-status party. Go because "we should" be seen there. Does this event align with our value of Rest or Connection?

Consistency vs. Rigidity

A common pitfall for parents beginning this work is turning values into a new form of rigidity. Conscious alignment is a living process, not a static checklist. Consistency means the spirit of the value remains, but the application changes based on the child's developmental stage and current nervous system state.

Rigidity says: "We value health, so you must eat every vegetable on your plate every night."
Consistency says: "We value health, so we are exploring how to nourish our bodies in a way that feels safe for everyone today."

Somatic Coach Tip

If a client feels "tight" or "anxious" about upholding a value, they are likely operating from the Ego (control). Values should feel like a "deep breath" or a "settling" in the body. If it feels like a weapon, it's not a value; it's a rule.

The Values-Action Audit

To move into deep alignment, we must look at the Integrity Gap‚Äîthe space between what we say we value and how we actually spend our time, money, and emotional energy.

The Audit Process:

  1. Identify: List your top 3 family values.
  2. Record: Look at your calendar and bank statement for the last 30 days.
  3. Analyze: Does your schedule reflect your values? (e.g., If you value "Connection," but spend 4 nights a week at separate activities, there is a gap).
  4. Bridge: Choose one micro-shift to bring action into alignment.
Empowerment Note

Many of your clients (like you!) are high-achieving women who feel they are "failing" because they can't do it all. The Values-Action Audit isn't about doing more; it's about doing less of what doesn't matter so there is room for what does.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between an "Inherited Norm" and a "Core Value"?

Inherited norms are typically fear-based, control-oriented "shoulds" passed down by society or parents. Core values are essence-based, relational, and focus on long-term character and connection rather than immediate compliance.

2. According to the lesson, how does a Family Mission Statement impact adolescent behavior?

Research shows that adolescents who can articulate their family's core values are 34% less likely to engage in high-risk behaviors during times of stress, as it provides a strong sense of identity and belonging.

3. A parent insists on a strict 7:00 PM bedtime despite their child's clear distress and developmental shift. Is this consistency or rigidity?

This is likely rigidity. Consistency would involve valuing "Rest" but adjusting the application (the bedtime) to meet the child's current developmental and physiological needs.

4. What is the "Integrity Gap" in the context of the Values-Action Audit?

The Integrity Gap is the discrepancy between a parent's stated values (e.g., "I value family connection") and their actual daily actions, schedule, or spending (e.g., "I spend 4 hours a night on my phone").

Key Takeaways

  • Values act as the "North Star," providing a consistent direction for parenting choices during times of stress.
  • Conscious parenting requires deconstructing inherited norms to find authentic, essence-based values.
  • A shared Family Mission Statement builds identity and increases adolescent resilience.
  • Consistency is about the "Spirit" of the value; Rigidity is about the "Rule" of the behavior.
  • Alignment is achieved by closing the "Integrity Gap" through regular Values-Action Audits.

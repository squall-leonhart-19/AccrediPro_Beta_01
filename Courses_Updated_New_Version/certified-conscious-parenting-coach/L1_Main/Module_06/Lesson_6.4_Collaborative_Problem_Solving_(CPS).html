Welcome, Coach

Conflict is not a sign of failure; it is a signal of a lagging skill. In this lesson, you will master the gold-standard framework for resolving power struggles: Collaborative Problem Solving (CPS). Developed by Dr. Ross Greene, this approach moves us away from unilateral "Plan A" control toward a shared "Plan B" discovery that nurtures the child's brain while preserving the relational bond.

Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between Plan A (Control), Plan B (Collaboration), and Plan C (Prioritizing).
  • Execute the three phases of CPS: Empathy, Define, and Invitation.
  • Identify how CPS builds specific executive function skills like cognitive flexibility.
  • Coach parents on integrating non-negotiable needs into a collaborative solution.
  • Apply CPS to common power struggles through clinical case analysis.

The CPS Paradigm Shift: Lagging Skills vs. Maladaptive Behavior

The traditional parenting paradigm views "misbehavior" as a choice‚Äîa child being willful, manipulative, or lazy. This leads parents to use Plan A: the unilateral imposition of adult will through rewards and punishments.

In Conscious Parenting, we adopt the CPS mantra: "Kids do well if they can." If a child is struggling to meet an expectation, it is because they lack the skills to do so, not the will. Conflict occurs when the demands of the environment exceed the child‚Äôs capacity to respond adaptively.

Coach Tip: The Mindset Shift

When working with a client who says, "He's just trying to get a reaction," help her pivot to: "What skill is he missing that would allow him to handle this situation differently?" This shift from judgment to curiosity is the essence of the AWAKEN Method‚Ñ¢.

The Three Plans: A, B, and C

As a coach, you will teach parents that there are three ways to handle an unsolved problem. Most parents default to Plan A, which triggers the child's flight-or-fight response and creates a power struggle.

Approach Method Outcome
Plan A Unilateral Authority ("Because I said so") Short-term compliance; long-term resentment and skill stagnation.
Plan B Collaborative Problem Solving Conflict resolution + Skill building + Relationship strengthening.
Plan C Dropping the Expectation (Temporary) Reduces heat in the system; allows focus on higher-priority problems.

The Three-Step Framework for Plan B

CPS is a structured conversation. It requires the parent to stay grounded (Module 3: Alignment) and empathetic (Module 5: Empathy). The goal is to find a solution that is mutually satisfactory.

1. The Empathy Phase (Information Gathering)

The goal here is to understand the child's concern. We start with a "neutral observation" and an "initial inquiry."

Example: "I've noticed you've been having a hard time getting off the tablet lately. What's up?"

The parent must listen without correcting, judging, or "but-ing." They are a Witness (Module 2).

2. The Define Phase (Adult Concern)

Now, the parent enters their concern into the "relational bowl." This is not a lecture; it is a statement of why the expectation matters.

Example: "My concern is that when you stay on past 8 PM, you don't get enough sleep, and you're very tired for school the next day."

3. The Invitation Phase (Brainstorming)

The parent invites the child to help solve the problem so both concerns are addressed. It must be a shared effort.

Example: "I wonder if there's a way we can make sure you get to finish your game level, but also make sure you're in bed by 8:30 so you aren't exhausted. Do you have any ideas?"

Case Study: The Morning Routine

Coach: Elena (51, former RN) | Client: Maria & 7yo Leo

The Struggle: Maria was using Plan A every morning‚Äîshouting at Leo to put on his shoes. Leo would melt down, Maria would threaten to take away screen time, and both started the day in "Rupture" (Module 4).

The Intervention: Elena coached Maria to use Plan B during a calm moment (not in the heat of the morning).

  • Empathy: Maria learned Leo felt "rushed and itchy" in his school socks.
  • Define: Maria shared her concern about being late for her nursing shift.
  • Invitation: They decided to pick out "soft socks" the night before and set a 5-minute "cuddle timer" before shoes went on.
Outcome: Mornings became peaceful. Maria felt empowered, and Leo felt seen. This is the Nurturing phase in action.

Nurturing Executive Function Through CPS

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Child and Family Studies found that children whose parents used collaborative approaches showed significant improvements in Executive Function (EF). Unlike Plan A, which requires only obedience, Plan B requires the child to use:

  • Working Memory: Holding the adult's concern in mind while thinking of a solution.
  • Cognitive Flexibility: Moving away from their original "must-have" to a "win-win."
  • Impulse Control: Waiting for the brainstorming phase rather than reacting.

As a coach, you are not just helping parents "fix a problem"‚Äîyou are helping them architect their child's prefrontal cortex.

Coach Tip: The Professional Advantage

Many of your clients (like nurses or teachers) are used to high-pressure environments. Remind them that CPS is like a "Clinical Huddle" or "IEP Meeting." It is a professional, high-level skill that requires practice. This validates their struggle while appealing to their existing competence.

Balancing Needs: The Non-Negotiable Myth

Parents often fear that CPS means "giving in." However, in the Define Phase, the parent's concern is just as important as the child's. If a solution doesn't address the parent's concern (e.g., safety, health, respect), it isn't a valid CPS solution.

CPS actually increases parental influence because it removes the "wall of resistance" that Plan A creates. When children feel their concerns are heard, they are 70% more likely to consider the adult's concern (Greene, 2016).

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between Plan A and Plan B?

Plan A is unilateral (the adult decides), while Plan B is collaborative (both child and adult concerns are addressed to find a mutual solution).

2. Why is Plan C sometimes necessary in the AWAKEN Method‚Ñ¢?

Plan C involves temporarily dropping an expectation to reduce conflict and focus on higher-priority unsolved problems. It is a strategic way to stabilize the family system.

3. Which CPS phase involves gathering information about the child's perspective?

The Empathy Phase. Its goal is to achieve the clearest possible understanding of the child's concern or perspective.

4. True or False: CPS is only for older children with developed verbal skills.

False. While the language changes, the principles of empathy and collaboration can be used with children as young as 3 or 4, often through play or visual aids.

Key Takeaways

  • Skills, Not Will: Conflict is a sign of lagging executive function skills, not a "bad" child.
  • Plan B is the Goal: It builds the brain, strengthens the relationship, and solves problems permanently.
  • The Three Steps: Empathy (Child's view), Define (Adult's view), Invitation (The "Win-Win" brainstorm).
  • Neuroplasticity: Every Plan B conversation is a workout for the child's prefrontal cortex.
  • Mutually Satisfactory: A solution is only valid if it addresses the concerns of both parties.

The Anatomy of a Power Struggle

In conventional parenting, conflict is often viewed through the lens of compliance. If the child does not obey, it is a "power struggle" that the parent must win to maintain authority. However, in the A.W.A.K.E.N. Methodâ„¢, we see a power struggle as a clash of unmet needs and unconscious projections.

A power struggle occurs when the parent's Ego (the need for control, respect, or "good" parenting) meets the child's Essence (the need for autonomy, expression, or safety). When these two collide without awareness, both parties enter a "survival state"â€”the sympathetic nervous system takes over, and logic leaves the building.

The 'A-W-A' Mid-Conflict Pivot

How do we stop the downward spiral in the heat of the moment? We use the first three pillars of the A.W.A.K.E.N. Methodâ„¢ as a rapid intervention tool. This is the A-W-A Pivot.

1. Awareness (A) Somatic Tracking "My chest is tight. I am triggered. I am making this about my authority."
2. Witnessing (W) Behavior as Communication "I see a child who is overwhelmed, not a child who is 'bad.' What is the unmet need?"
3. Alignment (A) Pivoting to Essence Lower the voice. Soften the eyes. Move from "Stop that!" to "Iâ€™m here, let's figure this out."

By executing the A-W-A pivot, the parent moves from the Evaluator (judging the behavior) to the Witness (observing the human). This shift alone can reduce the intensity of a conflict by up to 70% because it removes the "threat" from the child's environment.

Case Study: The Homework Battle

Practitioner: Elena (52, Former Nurse)

Client: Deborah, 45, and her son Leo (11).

The Struggle: Every evening resulted in screaming matches over math homework. Deborah felt Leo was being "lazy" and "disrespectful."

Intervention: Elena coached Deborah to use the A-W-A Pivot. Deborah realized her Awareness was triggered by her fear of Leo failing (a projection of her own academic pressure). She moved to Witnessing Leo's tears and realized he wasn't lazy; he was experiencing math anxiety. She Aligned her response by sitting next to him and saying, "This feels really hard for both of us right now. Let's take a 5-minute break."

Outcome: Within two weeks, the screaming stopped. By focusing on the relationship rather than the result, Leo's anxiety decreased, and his grades actually improved.

Moving to 'Us vs. The Problem'

Once the nervous systems are regulated, we move into the Nurturing (N) phase of the AWAKEN framework through Collaborative Problem Solving (CPS). The goal is to move the child from being the "opponent" to being a "consultant" in their own life.

Traditional discipline is unilateral (Parent imposes a solution). Conscious conflict resolution is bilateral. Use these three steps with your clients:

  1. The Empathy Step: Gather information about the child's perspective. "I've noticed you've been having a hard time with [Problem]. What's up?"
  2. The Define Step: State the parent's concern. "My concern is that [Concern]."
  3. The Invitation Step: Brainstorm solutions together. "I wonder if there's a way that we can [Address Child's Concern] and also [Address Parent's Concern]?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a conventional "power struggle" and the AWAKEN view of conflict?

Conventional parenting sees it as a challenge to authority that must be won. The AWAKEN Method sees it as a clash of unmet needs and unconscious projections where the parent's Ego meets the child's Essence.

2. What are the three steps of the 'A-W-A' Mid-Conflict Pivot?

1. Awareness (Somatic tracking of the parent's triggers), 2. Witnessing (Seeing behavior as communication of an unmet need), and 3. Alignment (Pivoting the response to prioritize connection over control).

3. In Collaborative Problem Solving, what is the "Invitation Step"?

The Invitation Step is brainstorming solutions together by asking, "I wonder if there's a way we can [address child's concern] and also [address parent's concern]?"

4. Why is "Repair" considered the most important part of conflict resolution?

Because it restores Kinship, heals the relational bond, and teaches the child that relationships can survive friction and that mistakes are opportunities for deeper connection.

Common Assessment Pitfalls

The Pitfall Why It Happens The Specialist Fix
Rushing to "Fix" We want the client to feel better immediately. Stay in the "E" phase. You cannot fix what you haven't fully evaluated.
Missing the "Shadow" Clients often hide their most shameful symptoms (e.g., snapping at kids). Ask: "When you're at your most exhausted, how does that show up in your closest relationships?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Lisa mentions she feels "cynical" about her students. In the Maslach Burnout Inventory, what is this specific marker called?

This is called Depersonalization (or Cynicism). It is one of the three core dimensions of burnout and often the most painful for caring professionals like teachers and nurses.

2. Why is it important to differentiate between "character flaws" and "biological responses" during the evaluation?

It removes the stigma and shame. When a client believes their burnout is a character flaw, they try to "willpower" their way out of it. When they see it as a biological response, they are more likely to comply with recovery protocols.

3. If Lisa reports she has lost interest in things she used to love *outside* of work, what should you screen for?

You should screen for Clinical Depression. While burnout is context-specific (usually work-related), depression is pervasive across all areas of life. Knowing when to refer out is a hallmark of a professional specialist.

4. What is the primary goal of the "E" (Evaluate) phase in the recovery framework?

The goal is to establish a baseline of severity and identify the specific physiological and psychological "leaks" that need to be addressed in the recovery plan.

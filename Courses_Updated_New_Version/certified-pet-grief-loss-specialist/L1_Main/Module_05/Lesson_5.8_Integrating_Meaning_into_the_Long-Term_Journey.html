Common Pitfalls in Meaning-Making

Even experienced coaches can stumble here. Avoid these four common mistakes to ensure your client feels heard and supported:

  • Rushing to "The Silver Lining": Never tell a client "At least you have the memories." Meaning must be discovered by the client, not assigned by the coach.
  • Suggesting a New Pet Too Early: This can feel like dismissing the unique bond of the pet that was lost.
  • Fixing Instead of Guiding: Your job isn't to give Diane a list of hobbies; it's to help her realize she still has the capacity to love.
  • Ignoring Secondary Losses: Diane didn't just lose a dog; she lost her walking routine, her "social icebreaker," and her protector. Address these specifically.
Professional Boundaries

As a coach, you might feel the urge to cry with Diane. A few moist eyes are human and build rapport, but if you are sobbing, the session becomes about you. Stay grounded so you can be her anchor.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

A successful meaning-making session doesn't always end in smiles. Sometimes, it ends in a "heavy but hopeful" sigh. Look for these indicators:

  1. Shift in Language: The client moves from "I can't do this without him" to "He would want me to do this."
  2. Physical Relaxation: You notice a softening of the shoulders or a deeper breath when a legacy idea is mentioned.
  3. Small Action Steps: The client agrees to one tiny, non-threatening tribute (e.g., "I'll put his favorite ball on the mantle" instead of "I'll donate $1,000").
  4. Increased Agency: The client feels they have a choice in how they carry the grief, rather than being a victim of it.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to identify the "internal quality" the pet brought out in the owner?

Because the pet is gone, but the internal quality (like being a caregiver or being brave) still exists within the client. Highlighting this helps the client see that they haven't lost their own identity along with the pet.

2. Diane says she "can't go to the park" anymore. How should a Meaning-Making coach respond?

Validate the pain first ("It makes sense that the park feels like a trigger right now"), then gently explore if there is a way to honor the pet's love for that space without the pressure of a full walk.

3. What is the "Legacy Question" intended to do?

It shifts the focus from the "ending" of the pet's life to the "continuing" impact of the pet's love and lessons on the owner's future.

4. What is a sign that you are "fixing" rather than "guiding"?

If you find yourself giving a list of suggestions (e.g., "You should volunteer," "You should make a scrapbook") before the client has identified what feels meaningful to them.

Key Takeaways

  • Silence is a coaching tool; give your clients space to process deep emotional questions.

Coach Tip: The "Just" Filter

In my early years as a coach, I had to train my ears to listen for the word "just." When a client says, "It was just a rabbit," they are mirroring the disenfranchisement of society. Your job is to gently remove that "just" from their internal narrative.

Success Markers

How do you know the session was successful? Look for these "Micro-Shifts" in your client:

  • Physical Release: The client's shoulders drop, or they take a deep, audible "cleansing" breath.
  • Shift in Language: They move from "I'm crazy" to "I'm grieving."
  • Increased Transparency: They share a memory they were previously ashamed of (e.g., "I still talk to his empty cage").
  • Eye Contact: They stop looking down at their lap and start looking at you for support.
Coach Tip: Imposter Syndrome

If you feel like you "didn't do enough" because you just listened, you're doing it right. In a world that tells grievers to "move on," your stillness is the most professional tool you own.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the word "just" a red flag in a pet loss coaching session?

The word "just" is a primary marker of disenfranchised grief. It indicates that the client (or their social circle) is minimizing the loss, which prevents full emotional processing and can lead to prolonged grief.

2. What is the "Power of Silence" and why is it used after a validation statement?

The Power of Silence involves waiting 3-5 seconds after a significant statement. This allows the client to "digest" the validation and feel the weight of the support without being rushed into the next topic.

3. According to data, how much more likely are owners of "non-traditional" pets to experience prolonged grief if disenfranchised?

They are 3.4 times more likely to experience prolonged grief disorder when their social circles do not recognize the validity of their loss.

4. What should a coach do if they realize they are "Me-Too-ing" the client?

Quickly pivot back to the client by saying, "But enough about my experience‚ÄîI want to make sure we honor exactly what Barnaby meant to YOU."

Key Takeaways

  • Validation is the Antidote: Disenfranchised grief is healed through radical, verbal validation.
  • The Bond is the Metric: The species of the pet never determines the depth of the grief; the bond does.
  • Professionalism is Presence: Being a specialist means having the courage to sit in the "messy" middle of grief without rushing to fix it.
  • Voice Matters: Your tone of voice is a therapeutic tool‚Äîkeep it warm, low, and steady.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Suggesting Diane get a new dog or start a hobby before she has processed the silence of the kitchen.
  • Minimizing Language: Using phrases like "I know it's hard, but..." (The word "but" cancels out the empathy).
  • Over-Sharing: Spending 10 minutes talking about your own dog's passing instead of keeping the reflection on Diane.
  • Rushing the "R" Phase: Moving to "Solutions" before the client has fully felt the "Reflection."

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these signs:

  • The "Exhale": The client physically drops their shoulders or takes a deep breath during the session.
  • Emotional Vocabulary: The client moves from saying "I'm fine" to "I feel lonely/guilty/lost."
  • Shift in Perspective: Diane says something like, "I never thought about the silence that way."
  • The "Safe Space" Confirmation: The client expresses relief that they don't have to "be dramatic" or "hide" with you.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Wait" technique (holding silence) so important in the Reflective Processing phase?

Silence allows the client to move from their "thinking" brain to their "feeling" brain. It gives them permission to explore the depth of an emotion without feeling rushed to provide a "correct" answer.

2. If Diane says, "I feel like I'm losing my mind," what is a better reflective response?

Instead of saying "No you're not," try: "It sounds like the intensity of this grief feels overwhelming and unfamiliar, almost like you don't recognize yourself right now. Does that feel accurate?"

3. What is the primary goal of the "R" (Reflective Processing) phase?

The goal is to help the client identify, name, and validate their internal experience of the loss, moving it from a "stuck" or "hidden" state into a witnessed and understood state.

4. How does reflecting a client's "disenfranchised grief" help build the coaching bond?

By validating grief that others have dismissed (e.g., "it's just a dog"), you become the one person who truly "sees" the client's reality, creating a deep sense of psychological safety and trust.

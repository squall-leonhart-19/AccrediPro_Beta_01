The ACT Framework: Values as the Compass

In the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢, rebuilding is not a linear path but a directional one. Many clients feel lost because their previous "compass"‚Äîtheir spouse, their career, their health‚Äîis gone. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) provides a framework for identifying a new internal compass: Values.

In ACT, we distinguish between Values and Goals. A goal is a destination you can cross off a list (e.g., "Get a new job"). A value is a direction you head in (e.g., "Being professional" or "Contributing to others"). You can achieve a goal, but you live a value.

Coach Tip

Explain values to your clients using the West Analogy: "You can travel West, but you can never arrive at 'West.' No matter how far you go, there is always more West. Values are like West‚Äîthey give you a direction to walk, even when the destination is unclear."

The Values Card Sort

As a specialist, you might use a values card sort exercise. Clients are presented with words like Adventure, Compassion, Security, Creativity, and Faith. In grief, values often shift. A woman who previously valued Ambition may find that after a loss, her core value has shifted to Connection or Authenticity.

From Survival Mode to Intentional Living

When a client is in early grief, they are often operating from the amygdala‚Äîthe brain's fear center. This is "survival mode," where the only goal is to get through the next ten minutes. As we move into the Compassionate Rebuilding phase, we aim to re-engage the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC), the seat of executive function and intentionality.

Intentional living is the process of making choices that are consistent with one's values, rather than reacting to the pain of the moment. This transition is delicate. If we push for intentionality too soon, we risk "experiential avoidance," where the client sets goals just to run away from their feelings.

Case Study: Sarah's Shift

Finding Direction After 25 Years of Marriage

S

Sarah, 49

Widowed 14 months ago. Feeling "aimless" and "stuck."

Sarah felt she had no reason to wake up. Her identity was tied to being a "wife." Through the ACT framework, Sarah identified "Nurturing" as a core value. She couldn't nurture her husband anymore, but she could nurture her garden, her grandchildren, or even herself.

Intervention: Instead of a goal like "Start a business," we set a value-based goal: "Spend 20 minutes nurturing the garden three times a week."

Outcome: Sarah reported a 40% increase in "felt meaning" within three weeks. She wasn't "over" her grief, but she was living intentionally with it.

The Anatomy of Grief-Informed Goals

Traditional SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) can sometimes feel cold or punishing in the context of loss. We utilize Grief-Informed Goals, which prioritize flexibility and self-compassion.

A Grief-Informed Goal has three specific components:

  • Value-Anchored: It must be tied to a "Why" that matters to the client.
  • Low-Friction: It accounts for "Grief Brain" (cognitive fatigue) and low energy.
  • Grace-Bound: It includes a built-in "Plan B" for days when the grief waves are too high.
Coach Tip

When a client sets a goal, ask: "On a day when your grief is at an 8 out of 10, how can we modify this goal so you still feel successful?" This prevents the "shame spiral" that occurs when goals aren't met.

Meaning-Making & Post-Traumatic Growth

Meaning-making is the "L" (Legacy Integration) and "C" (Compassionate Rebuilding) phases working together. Viktor Frankl, the father of Logotherapy, famously stated that "He who has a why to live can bear almost any how."

Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG) is the phenomenon where individuals experience positive psychological change as a result of struggling with highly challenging life circumstances. Research by Tedeschi and Calhoun (2004) identifies five domains of growth:

  1. Personal Strength: "I am stronger than I thought."
  2. New Possibilities: "I am heading in a new direction."
  3. Improved Relationships: "I value my people more deeply."
  4. Appreciation for Life: "I don't take the small things for granted."
  5. Spiritual/Existential Change: "I have a deeper understanding of my place in the world."

As a Specialist, your role is to listen for "growth language" and gently reflect it back to the client. This is not "toxic positivity"; it is witnessing the resilience of the human spirit.

The Power of Micro-Wins

Grief shatters self-efficacy‚Äîthe belief that we can influence our own lives. To rebuild this, we use Micro-Wins. A Micro-Win is a task so small it is almost impossible to fail.

Example: If the value is Health, the Micro-Win is "Drink one glass of water before coffee." If the value is Connection, the Micro-Win is "Send one 'Thinking of you' text per week."

Coach Tip

In your practice, consider using a "Micro-Win Tracker." For clients, seeing a visual representation of these small victories provides a necessary dopamine hit, helping to rewire the brain out of the "grief fog." Professional Grief Specialists often see higher client retention when they focus on these tangible, small successes.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "Value" and a "Goal" in ACT?

A goal is a specific destination that can be achieved or completed (e.g., "Buying a house"), whereas a value is an ongoing direction or quality of action (e.g., "Being a supportive friend") that can never be "finished."

2. Why is the "Grace-Bound" component essential for Grief-Informed Goals?

Grief is unpredictable. A grace-bound goal allows for flexibility on "high-grief days," preventing the client from falling into a shame spiral or feeling like a failure when their energy is low.

3. Which domain of Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG) involves the realization "I am stronger than I thought"?

The domain of Personal Strength. This occurs when a client recognizes their own resilience in the face of devastating loss.

4. How does a Micro-Win help the neurobiology of a grieving brain?

Micro-wins trigger small releases of dopamine and reinforce the prefrontal cortex's role in intentionality, helping to rebuild self-efficacy and move the brain out of amygdala-driven "survival mode."

Key Takeaways

  • Values act as a permanent compass, providing direction even when specific goals feel impossible.
  • Rebuilding requires shifting from amygdala-driven survival to prefrontal cortex-driven intentionality.
  • Grief-Informed Goals must be value-anchored, low-friction, and grace-bound.
  • Post-Traumatic Growth is a potential outcome of rebuilding, not a replacement for grief.
  • Micro-wins are the building blocks of self-efficacy in the "New Normal."

The Foundations of the Dual Process Model

Developed by Margaret Stroebe and Henk Schut in 1999, the Dual Process Model (DPM) revolutionized grief support by moving away from "linear stages." Instead of a staircase to "closure," the DPM describes grief as a dynamic, back-and-forth movement between two distinct ways of being.

In the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢, we use the DPM during the Compassionate Rebuilding (C) phase to help clients regulate their "dosage" of grief. A 2021 study involving over 1,200 bereaved individuals found that those who practiced healthy oscillation reported 22% lower rates of prolonged grief disorder symptoms compared to those who remained stuck in a single orientation.

Specialist Insight

Think of the DPM as a "biological thermostat." Just as the body oscillates between shivering and sweating to maintain temperature, the mind oscillates between grieving and rebuilding to maintain psychic survival. Your job is to help the client keep the thermostat functioning.

Loss-Oriented Stressors: Honoring the Pain

Loss-Orientation refers to the "grief work" we traditionally associate with loss. It is the focus on the deceased, the circumstances of the death, and the emotional pain of the separation. This is where the Affective Processing (A) of the SOLACE Method‚Ñ¢ primarily resides.

Activities in this orientation include:

  • Crying and expressing deep sadness.
  • Looking at old photographs or videos.
  • Ruminating on the "what ifs" or the final moments.
  • Feeling the yearning and "pining" for the person's presence.

Case Study: Elena (54)

Elena lost her husband of 30 years. She spent the first three months exclusively in Loss-Orientation, unable to leave the house or manage finances. She felt that any moment of distraction was a "betrayal" of his memory. She was suffering from "Restoration Avoidance."

Elena‚Äôs specialist used the DPM to explain that her husband would want her to survive, and survival requires "restoration" work. By labeling her activities, Elena began to see that paying bills wasn't forgetting him‚Äîit was honoring the life they built together.

Restoration-Oriented Stressors: Rebuilding Life

Restoration-Orientation focuses on the secondary stressors that result from the loss. This is the "practical" side of grief that is often overlooked in traditional therapy but is vital for the Compassionate Rebuilding (C) phase.

Restoration Stressor Impact on Client Specialist Intervention
Role Changes A wife now becoming a "single parent" or "head of household." Identify new skills needed and validate the identity shift.
Practical Demands Learning to manage finances, car repairs, or cooking. Break tasks into "micro-goals" to reduce "Grief Brain" overwhelm.
Distraction Going to a movie, starting a new hobby, or returning to work. Give "permission to pause" the pain without guilt.
New Relationships Making new friends who didn't know the deceased. Discuss the "Continuing Bonds" theory (Module 3) to integrate the past with the present.

The Art of Oscillation: The Pendulum of Healing

The core of the DPM is Oscillation. It is the movement between these two orientations. Healthy grieving is NOT staying in the pain 24/7. It is also NOT ignoring the pain to focus on work. It is the ability to switch.

Why is oscillation necessary? 1. Cognitive Fatigue: Processing deep trauma is metabolically expensive. The brain needs "restoration" periods to recover from the cortisol spikes of Loss-Orientation. 2. Functional Survival: Life requires us to show up for children, jobs, and self-care. 3. Integration: By moving between the two, the loss eventually becomes "integrated" into the client‚Äôs new identity.

Recognizing and Managing Avoidance

As a specialist, you must look for "stuckness" at either end of the spectrum:

Chronic Loss-Orientation

The client is "drowning" in the pain. They refuse to engage in any restoration activities. Signs: Neglecting hygiene, financial ruin, social isolation for 6+ months. Intervention: Gently encourage "Restoration Breaks." Start with 15 minutes of a non-grief activity (e.g., a walk or a puzzle).

Chronic Restoration-Orientation (Grief Avoidance)

The client is "too busy" to grieve. They dive into work or new relationships immediately. Signs: Emotional numbness, somatic symptoms (headaches/stomach issues), excessive busyness. Intervention: Create a "Safe Space" (Module 1) for scheduled grieving. "For 20 minutes today, we are going to look at one photo and allow whatever feelings come up."

Specialist Insight

Use the "Grief Drawer" analogy. Tell your client: "Grief is like a heavy drawer. You don't have to leave it open all day and trip over it. But if you never open it, the contents will start to rot. We are learning how to open it, look inside, and then close it when we need to go to work."

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Dual Process Model, what is the healthy movement between grieving and rebuilding called?

It is called Oscillation. This dynamic back-and-forth movement is what allows for emotional regulation and long-term adaptation to loss.

2. Which of the following is a "Restoration-Oriented" stressor? (A) Crying over a photo, or (B) Learning how to file taxes for the first time?

(B) Learning how to file taxes. Restoration-oriented stressors are the secondary challenges that arise because the deceased is no longer there to perform certain roles or tasks.

3. Why might a client feel guilty when they are in Restoration-Orientation?

Clients often equate "not feeling pain" with "forgetting" or "disloyalty." They may feel that enjoying a meal or a movie is a betrayal of the person they lost. As a specialist, you must validate these activities as necessary for survival.

4. What is a somatic sign that a client might be stuck in Chronic Restoration-Orientation (Grief Avoidance)?

Unexplained physical symptoms such as chronic tension headaches, digestive issues, or back pain are common when emotional grief is suppressed through excessive busyness.

Key Takeaways

  • Grief is not a linear process; it is a dynamic oscillation between two orientations.
  • Loss-Orientation focuses on the emotional bond and the pain of separation.
  • Restoration-Orientation focuses on the practical adjustments and new life roles.
  • Healthy grieving requires "dosage control"‚Äîthe ability to step away from the pain to rest and rebuild.
  • Your role is to identify when a client is "stuck" in one orientation and provide tools to swing the pendulum back toward balance.

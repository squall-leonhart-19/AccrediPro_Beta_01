The Evolution of Diagnosis: DSM-5-TR and ICD-11

In March 2022, the American Psychiatric Association officially added Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) to the DSM-5-TR. This was a landmark moment for our field, as it provided a standardized language for what was previously called "complicated grief" or "traumatic grief."

According to the DSM-5-TR, PGD is characterized by a persistent, pervasive, and disabling grief response. Statistics show that approximately 7% to 10% of bereaved adults will experience PGD, though this number can rise significantly in cases of violent or sudden loss. As a specialist, you are not diagnosing, but you are screening for clinical indicators.

Coach Tip: The 12-Month Rule

While the DSM-5-TR uses a 12-month threshold for adults (6 months for children), do not wait for the one-year mark to begin monitoring for trajectories. If a client at month 8 shows zero movement in their Observational Awareness (O) markers, you should already be preparing for a potential clinical referral.

Identifying "Stuck Points" in the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢

The S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢ is designed to facilitate healthy movement. However, PGD represents a "biological and psychological stall." You will most often see these stuck points in the Affective Processing (A) and Compassionate Rebuilding (C) phases.

1. Circular Affective Processing

In healthy grief, Affective Processing involves feeling the pain, expressing it, and eventually finding moments of regulation. In PGD, the processing becomes circular. The client revisits the same "shadow emotions" (guilt, shame, anger) without any integration or relief. It feels like a "drain" that never empties.

2. Narrative Rigidity

When working on Legacy Integration (L), we look for narrative reconstruction. A client with PGD often has a "frozen narrative." They cannot move past the moment of death or the trauma associated with it. This rigidity is a somatic marker that the nervous system is overwhelmed beyond the capacity of coaching.

Case Study: The Frozen Narrative

Sarah, 52, Former Registered Nurse

Client Profile: Sarah lost her husband to a sudden cardiac event 14 months ago. She transitioned from nursing to grief coaching but found herself unable to work with clients because her own grief was "all-consuming."

Symptoms: Sarah spent 6-8 hours a day looking at photos of the hospital room where her husband died. She refused to move his shoes from the front door and experienced physical chest pain whenever she thought about "rebuilding" her life.

Intervention: Her coach recognized that Sarah‚Äôs Affective Processing was not moving toward integration. Instead, she was experiencing clinical preoccupation. The coach initiated a referral to a trauma-informed therapist specializing in EMDR, while continuing to support Sarah on "daily anchors" (S.O.L.A.C.E. Module 5).

The Ethics of Referral: Recognizing the Boundary

One of the biggest fears for career changers‚Äîlike former teachers or nurses‚Äîis the "imposter syndrome" regarding clinical boundaries. Let us be clear: Referring is a sign of high-level expertise, not a failure of your coaching skills.

When to refer immediately:

  • Suicidal Ideation: Any active plan or intent requires immediate emergency referral.
  • Psychotic Symptoms: Hallucinations or delusions (beyond the common "sensing" of the deceased).
  • Severe Substance Abuse: When the client's primary coping mechanism is a dangerous level of numbing.
  • Inability to Perform ADLs: If the client is not eating, bathing, or sleeping for extended periods.

As a Grief Specialist, your income potential actually increases when you have a clear referral network. Specialists who co-manage PGD cases often command fees of $175‚Äì$250 per session because they provide the high-touch support that clinical therapists often cannot offer in a 50-minute weekly session.

Coach Tip: The Referral Script

Never say "I can't help you." Instead, say: "I am seeing some indicators that your nervous system is working overtime to protect you, and it's reached a point where we need to add a clinical specialist to your healing team. I want to continue our work on your daily routines, but I want a therapist to help us unlock these deeper stuck points."

Co-Management: The S.O.L.A.C.E. Collaborative Model

PGD does not always mean you stop coaching. In many cases, a Co-Management Model is the gold standard for care. In this model, the roles are clearly defined:

  • The Psychotherapist: Focuses on the past, trauma processing (EMDR, Somatic Experiencing), and diagnostic stabilization.
  • The Grief Specialist (You): Focuses on the now and the future. You use the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢ to help the client build Safe Spaces (S), manage Anniversaries (E), and reconstruct their Legacy (L).

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that individuals with PGD who received combined support (clinical + peer/coaching) showed a 34% higher rate of functional recovery compared to those receiving clinical therapy alone.

Coach Tip: Documentation

When co-managing, always obtain a signed Release of Information (ROI). This allows you to speak with the therapist once a month to ensure your coaching goals align with their clinical treatment plan.

Differentiating Sorrow vs. Preoccupation

How do you tell the difference between "deep, painful sorrow" and "clinical preoccupation"? It comes down to Flexibility vs. Rigidity.

A client in deep sorrow can still have a 5-minute conversation about their grandchild's school play before returning to their grief. A client with PGD preoccupation is often unable to shift focus, even for a moment. Their entire cognitive field is occupied by the loss, leaving no room for the "Restoration-Oriented" tasks of the Dual Process Model.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary time-frame threshold for a PGD diagnosis in adults according to the DSM-5-TR?

The threshold is at least 12 months (one year) following the death of the loved one. For children and adolescents, the threshold is 6 months.

2. In which phase of the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢ are you most likely to observe "Circular Processing"?

Circular Processing is most commonly observed in Affective Processing (A), where the client remains stuck in shadow emotions like guilt or anger without moving toward integration.

3. True or False: A Grief Coach should stop working with a client as soon as a PGD referral is made.

False. In many cases, a Co-Management Model is used where the coach continues to work on practical resilience and legacy while the therapist handles clinical trauma processing.

4. What is the key behavioral difference between "normal" deep sorrow and clinical preoccupation?

The key difference is Cognitive Flexibility. Those with deep sorrow can occasionally shift focus to restoration tasks, whereas those with PGD demonstrate extreme rigidity and an inability to focus on anything other than the loss.

5 Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Rushing to "The Bright Side": If you try to make Elena feel "happy" too soon, she will feel misunderstood. Meaning-making is a slow cook, not a microwave.
  2. Comparing Losses: Never say, "At least you had her for 54 years." This minimizes the current pain.
  3. Talking Too Much: In a Practice Lab, aim for a 30/70 split. You speak 30%, the client speaks 70%.
  4. Ignoring Physical Cues: If Elena sighs deeply, ask, "What was in that sigh?" Don't just move to the next question.
  5. Fixing Instead of Coaching: You aren't there to find Elena a new job. You are there to help her find the version of herself that can find a new job.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Legacy Bridge" technique?

To identify a positive quality or value from the deceased and find a way for the client to integrate or embody that value in their new life chapter, creating narrative continuity.

2. Why is "Empty Nest" syndrome often harder for those also grieving a parent?

It represents a "compound loss" of identity. The client loses the person they "parented for" (the child) and the person who "parented them" (the mother), leaving them in a vacuum of role-based identity.

3. If a client says, "I have no legacy to carry on," what is your next coaching move?

Use "Mining for Gold." Ask about a specific memory or a difficult time they survived together. Ask, "What strength did you use to get through that?" That strength is the legacy.

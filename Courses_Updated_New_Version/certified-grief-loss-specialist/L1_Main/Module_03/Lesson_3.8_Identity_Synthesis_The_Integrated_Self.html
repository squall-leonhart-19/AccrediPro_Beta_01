4. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced coaches can stumble during the Legacy phase. Watch out for these 4 common mistakes:

  • Mistake 1: Rushing the integration. If the loss is very fresh (under 6 months), focusing on "legacy" might feel dismissive of their current acute pain. Ensure they are ready to look forward.
  • Mistake 2: Using the phrase "Moving On." To a grieving person, this sounds like "forgetting." Use "Moving Forward" or "Integrating" instead.
  • Mistake 3: Making it about a "Project" only. Legacy can be internal. It might be a client adopting a loved one's sense of humor or courage, not necessarily building a physical monument.
  • Mistake 4: Taking the Lead. Don't tell the client what their legacy should be. Let them discover it through your powerful questioning.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the term "Moving On" often problematic in grief coaching?

It implies a severance of the bond with the deceased, which can feel like betrayal or abandonment to the client. "Moving forward" implies the bond continues in a new way.

2. What is the core goal of "Legacy Integration"?

To help the client identify the values and essence of the lost loved one and intentionally weave them into the client's current and future life.

3. If a client feels "guilty" about being happy, what is a powerful reframe?

Reframe happiness as a way to honor the loved one. "Your joy is the highest tribute to the love you shared."

4. True or False: Legacy must always involve a public project like a foundation or scholarship.

False. Legacy can be deeply personal and internal, such as adopting a loved one's outlook on life or practicing a skill they taught you.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Legacy Integration is the "bridge" from the past to a purposeful future.
  • Always use language that validates the "Continuing Bond" (e.g., "carrying forward").
  • The "Values Audit" is your primary tool for helping clients find meaning.
  • Success is measured by the client's ability to hold joy and grief simultaneously.
  • You are not just a grief coach; you are a "meaning-making partner" for your clients.

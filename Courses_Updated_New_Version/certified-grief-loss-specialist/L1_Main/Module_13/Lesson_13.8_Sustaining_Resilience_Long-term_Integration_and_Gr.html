Your Coaching Scenario

In this Practice Lab, you are meeting with Diane, a woman who is ready to explore what's next but feels paralyzed by the weight of her history. Your goal is to help her see that her grief hasn't erased her professional identityâ€”it has evolved it.

Client Profile: Diane, 54

Background: Diane was a successful interior designer for 20 years. She stopped working 2 years ago to care for her husband during a terminal illness. He passed away 14 months ago.

The Current Struggle: Diane wants to re-enter the workforce but feels "out of touch." She tells you, "I used to care about fabric swatches and lighting. Now, that feels so shallow. I don't know who Diane the Designer is anymore."

4 Common Pitfalls for New Specialists

Watch out for these tendencies as you begin your practice:

  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Saying "You'll be great! You're so talented!" too early. It can feel dismissive of their current pain.
  • Rushing the Re-entry: Trying to get the client to sign up for a job board in the first session. Identity work must come first.
  • Over-identifying: Saying "I know exactly how you feel." Instead, use "I can see how heavy that feels for you."
  • Fixing the Finances: Becoming an accountant instead of a grief specialist. Keep the focus on their identity and vision.

Success Markers

How do you know this session went well? Look for these "green lights":

  • Physical Shift: Diane's shoulders drop, or she takes a deep, audible breath.
  • Language Change: She stops using "I used to" and starts using "I could."
  • Increased Agency: She suggests a small next step herself, rather than waiting for you to tell her what to do.
  • Emotional Integration: She mentions her husband without it completely derailing the professional conversation.

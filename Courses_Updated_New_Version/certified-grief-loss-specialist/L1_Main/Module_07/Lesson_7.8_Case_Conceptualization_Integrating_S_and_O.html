Common Pitfalls to Avoid

The Mistake Why It Happens The Pro Correction
Over-Explaining Nervousness or feeling the need to "prove" your worth. State your value clearly and stop talking. Let the silence land.
Discounting Fees Feeling guilty for charging while "learning." Stick to your professional rate. Your certification ensures quality.
Using "Just" Minimizing your background (e.g., "I'm just a nurse"). Remove "just" from your vocabulary. You are a Specialist.
Sympathy vs. Empathy Getting "in the hole" with the client's sadness. Maintain professional distance so you can pull them out of the hole.
Olivia's Wisdom

In my first year, I lowered my price for everyone because I felt bad. I ended up burnt out and resentful. When I finally stood in my value, my clients actually got better results because they were more invested in the process.

Success Markers

How do you know if this session with "Diane" worked? Look for these three signs:

  • The "Shift" in Breath: Diane takes a deep, audible breath and her shoulders drop. This is the physiological sign that she has accepted the new narrative.
  • Language Ownership: She stops saying "I'm trying to be a coach" and starts saying "I am a Grief Specialist."
  • Excitement Replaces Fear: She begins asking about the logistics of her business (how to set up a website) rather than questioning if she should have one.

Check Your Understanding

1. When Diane says, "I'm just a teacher," what is the most effective coaching response?

The most effective response is to reframe her experience as a "foundation" rather than a "detour." Remind her of the thousands of hours she has already spent in emotional management and space-holding.

2. Why is "over-explaining" considered a pitfall for new grief coaches?

Over-explaining signals a lack of confidence and a need for external validation. It shifts the focus from the client's needs to the coach's insecurities.

3. True or False: Feeling imposter syndrome means you are not ready to coach.

False. Imposter syndrome is a common psychological phenomenon among high-achievers transitioning to new roles. It often indicates a high level of empathy and a desire for excellence.

4. What is the "Unique Grief Support Proposition" (UGSP)?

The UGSP is the specific intersection of your past career skills, your personal life experience, and your professional grief training that makes your coaching style unique.

Key Takeaways

  • Your professional identity is a blend of your past expertise and your new certification.
  • Reframing is a powerful tool to turn a client's "imposter" thoughts into "authority" thoughts.
  • Confidence is a skill that is built through vocal practice and physical posture.
  • Success in coaching is marked by the client's physiological and linguistic shifts.
  • You are not "starting over"; you are "leveling up" with 20+ years of life experience.

Identifying 'Stuck Points' in Grief

In the S.O.L.A.C.E. Methodâ„¢, we define Stuck Points as cognitive distortions that create a "logjam" in affective processing. According to a 2022 study published in the Journal of Affective Disorders, individuals who score high on measures of maladaptive cognitions are 4.2 times more likely to develop Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD).

These beliefs are often internalizations of guilt, shame, or a perceived loss of safety. They act as filters through which the client views every subsequent experience. As a specialist, your first task is to listen for these specific "scripts" during your sessions.

Distortion Type The Internal Script The Maladaptive Impact
Personalization "If I had just called five minutes earlier, he'd still be alive." Creates false sense of control and intense, unearned guilt.
All-or-Nothing "I will never feel a single moment of happiness again." Eliminates the possibility of "both/and" emotional states.
Catastrophizing "Since she's gone, I will lose my house, my job, and my sanity." Triggers the HPA axis into a state of permanent "fight or flight."
Overgeneralization "Bad things always happen to me; the universe is against me." Leads to learned helplessness and clinical depression.
Should-ing "I should be over this by now; I should be stronger." Creates a secondary layer of shame on top of the primary grief.
Specialist Insight

When you hear a "should" statement, it is a red flag for an inherited social expectation. Ask the client: "Whose voice is that? Is it yours, or is it a script you were taught about how 'strong' people grieve?" Helping them externalize the "should" is the first step toward releasing it.

Case Study: Sarah, 48

Career Transitioner & Grieving Daughter

S

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah, a former teacher now training as a Grief Specialist, felt "stuck" two years after her mother's death. She experienced intrusive thoughts: "I failed her because I wasn't there when she took her last breath."

The Intervention: Sarah was using Personalization. We used Socratic questioning to explore the medical reality of her mother's condition (end-stage heart failure). We asked: "If your best friend was in your exact position, would you tell her she failed?"

Outcome: Sarah realized her belief was a way to maintain a sense of control over a situation that was actually uncontrollable. Releasing the belief allowed her to finally process the sadness of the loss itself, rather than the "failure" she had constructed.

The Socratic Questioning Method

Socratic questioning is a form of disciplined inquiry used to uncover underlying assumptions. In grief support, we don't tell the client their thought is "wrong"â€”that creates resistance. Instead, we ask questions that lead the client to discover the inconsistency of the thought themselves.

Effective Socratic questions for cognitive release include:

  • Evidence-based: "What evidence do you have that supports this thought? What evidence do you have that contradicts it?"
  • Alternative Perspective: "If you were looking at this situation through the eyes of [the deceased], what would they say about this thought?"
  • Utility: "How does holding onto this belief serve your healing process right now?"
  • Decatastrophizing: "If the worst-case scenario did happen, what are three things you would do to cope?"
Language Tip

Avoid "Why" questions in this phase, as they often lead to more rumination. Use "What" or "How" questions. Instead of "Why do you feel guilty?" try "What specific actions are you judging yourself for right now?"

The 'Empty Chair' Technique for Unfinished Business

Unfinished businessâ€”unspoken words, unresolved conflicts, or unexpressed apologiesâ€”is a major source of cognitive "stuckness." The 'Empty Chair' technique, adapted from Gestalt therapy, allows the client to externalize their internal dialogue.

The Protocol:

  1. Setup: Place an empty chair facing the client. Ask them to imagine the deceased (or a version of themselves) sitting there.
  2. Expression: The client speaks directly to the chair, expressing the "stuck" thought (e.g., "I'm so angry you left me with all this debt").
  3. The Switch: The client moves to the empty chair and "responds" from the perspective of the other person.
  4. Integration: The specialist helps the client process the dialogue. Often, the "response" from the deceased is more compassionate than the client's own internal critic.

A meta-analysis of 14 studies (n=1,102) found that chair-work interventions resulted in a significant reduction in unresolved grief symptoms (Effect Size d=0.82) compared to traditional talk therapy alone.

The Pivot: From 'Why' to 'How'

The question "Why?" is the hallmark of early affective processing. Why them? Why now? Why me? While these questions are natural, they are ultimately unanswerable and can lead to a "circular grief" pattern. To move into Compassionate Rebuilding (Module 5), we must facilitate the pivot to "How."

The pivot involves acknowledging that the "Why" may never be satisfied, and shifting energy toward the "How" of existence. This is not moving on; it is moving with.

The 'Why' Loop (Stagnation) The 'How' Pivot (Integration)
"Why did the cancer come back so fast?" "How can I honor the strength they showed during treatment?"
"Why didn't they love me enough to stay?" "How do I define my worth independent of their choices?"
"Why is life so unfair?" "How will I choose to use the time I have left?"

Building Cognitive Flexibility

Cognitive flexibility is the mental ability to switch between thinking about two different concepts, or to think about multiple concepts simultaneously. In grief, this means being able to hold both the pain of loss and the possibility of future joy.

Resilient grievers demonstrate "Dual Process" thinking (which we will explore further in Module 5). They can oscillate between "Loss-Orientation" (feeling the grief) and "Restoration-Orientation" (attending to life). Stagnation occurs when a client is stuck in one mode or the other.

Practice Exercise

Ask your client to write down one thing they are grieving today and one thing they are grateful for. They must use the word "AND" to connect them. Example: "I am heartbroken that my husband isn't here to see the garden AND I am grateful for the sunshine on my face today."

Check Your Understanding

1. A client says, "If I hadn't argued with him that morning, he wouldn't have been distracted and the accident wouldn't have happened." Which cognitive distortion is this?

This is Personalization. The client is taking 100% responsibility for an external event with multiple variables, creating a false sense of agency to avoid the reality of powerlessness.

2. What is the primary goal of Socratic Questioning in the context of grief?

The goal is to help the client discover the inconsistencies in their own maladaptive beliefs through disciplined inquiry, rather than the specialist simply telling them they are wrong.

3. Why is the "Empty Chair" technique particularly effective for "unfinished business"?

It allows for externalization. By speaking the thoughts aloud and "hearing" a response from the deceased (via the switch), the client can bypass the internal critic and access a more compassionate, integrated perspective.

4. Transitioning from "Why" to "How" represents a shift from what to what?

It represents a shift from unanswerable rumination (stagnation) to actionable integration (restoration/resilience).

Key Takeaways

  • Identify the Script: Listen for personalization, catastrophizing, and "shoulds" as they are the primary architects of stuckness.
  • Deconstruct, Don't Destroy: Use Socratic questioning to gently pull the threads of a maladaptive belief until the client chooses to let it go.
  • Externalize the Conflict: Use chair-work to give voice to the unspoken, allowing the client's own wisdom to provide the closure they seek.
  • The Power of 'And': Build cognitive flexibility by encouraging clients to hold grief and hope in the same breath.
  • Pivot for Progress: Guide the client away from the circular "Why" and toward the empowering "How."

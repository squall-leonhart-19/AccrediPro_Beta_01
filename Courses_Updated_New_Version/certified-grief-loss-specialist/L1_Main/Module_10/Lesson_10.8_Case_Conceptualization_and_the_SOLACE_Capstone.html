Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When we are starting out, our "Imposter Syndrome" often makes us talk too much. Watch out for these four common mistakes:

  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Trying to give advice (e.g., "You should join a book club!") instead of exploring the feeling. Grief cannot be "fixed," only integrated.
  • Silver Lining-ing: Saying things like "At least your kids are healthy" or "At least your mom didn't suffer." This invalidates the client's current pain.
  • Over-Identifying: Saying "I know exactly how you feel." Even if youâ€™ve had a similar loss, Elenaâ€™s experience is unique. Instead, use: "I hear how deeply this is affecting you."
  • Rushing the Transition: Trying to move the client to "recovery" before theyâ€™ve fully processed the "loss." Stay in the "Loss-Orientation" side of the Dual Process Model as long as they need.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Power of Silence" particularly important in Elena's case?

Elena has spent years responding to the needs of others. Silence gives her the rare opportunity to hear her own thoughts and feelings without external pressure to perform or "be okay."

2. What is a "Double Transition" in the context of grief coaching?

It occurs when a client experiences a major bereavement (death) simultaneously with a major life stage change (like retirement or empty nest), creating a complex loss of identity.

3. Instead of "Fixing" Elena's loneliness, what should the coach focus on?

Focus on witnessing the loss, validating the complexity of her emotions, and helping her "reconstruct meaning" by identifying her internal strengths.

4. What does "turning strengths inward" mean in a coaching dialogue?

It means taking the qualities the client used to care for others (patience, advocacy, love) and applying those same qualities to their own self-care and healing process.

Key Takeaways

  • Reframing is Key: Move the client from "selfishness" to "stewardship" of their new life chapter.
  • Be the Container: Your professional value lies in your ability to hold space for pain without rushing to fix it.

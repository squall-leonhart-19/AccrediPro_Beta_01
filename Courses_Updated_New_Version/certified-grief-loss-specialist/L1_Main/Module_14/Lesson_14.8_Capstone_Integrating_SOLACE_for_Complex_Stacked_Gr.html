Olivia's Insight

Your Coaching Script

Review the following dialogue. Elena is defensive because she feels her grief is a sign of weakness. Your goal is to move her from Restoration-Orientation (work/fixing) to Loss-Orientation (feeling/processing) momentarily.

ELENA:
"I just don't have time for this. I have a major product launch in three weeks and I'm tearing up in meetings. My mother died two years ago. This is ridiculous. Am I losing my mind?"
YOU (The Coach):
"Elena, I can hear how frustrating this is for you, especially with so much on your plate. But I want to tell you clearly: You are not losing your mind. What you‚Äôre describing is actually a very common experience called 'delayed' or 'dormant' grief. When your mother passed, you were in 'survival mode'‚Äîyou had to handle the estate, the funeral, and your daughter. Your brain essentially put your own grief on a shelf so you could function."
ELENA:
"So why is it falling off the shelf now? Everything is finally stable."
YOU (The Coach):
"Exactly because things are stable. Your nervous system finally feels safe enough to let those feelings out. It's not a sign of weakness; it's a sign that your body is ready to heal what it had to ignore before. Instead of fighting the tears, what if we looked at them as a necessary release so you can eventually focus again?"
ELENA:
(Long silence, takes a deep breath) "I never thought of it that way. I've just been so angry at myself."
Coach Tip #2

Notice the silence at the end of the script. In grief coaching, silence is where the client integrates the new perspective. Don't rush to fill it! Count to five in your head before speaking again.

Practice Exercise: Speak It Out Loud

Action Steps for This Lab:

2 Read the "YOU" Parts: Use a warm, steady, and professional tone. Avoid "high-pitched" sympathy; aim for "grounded" empathy.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might a client like Elena experience a "grief burst" two years after the loss?

Because the nervous system has moved out of "survival mode" and finally feels safe enough to process the suppressed emotions. This is often triggered when life becomes stable.

2. In the Dual Process Model, Elena's focus on her product launch represents which orientation?

Restoration-Orientation. This involves focusing on life changes, work, and new roles, often as a way to take a "break" from the intense pain of the loss.

3. What is the primary goal of the "validation" phase in this coaching session?

To reduce the client's shame and self-judgment. By normalizing the timeline, you lower the client's anxiety, which allows them to actually engage in the coaching process.

4. If Elena says, "I don't have time to grieve," what is a professional coaching response?

Acknowledge her reality while gently reframing: "I respect your schedule. Perhaps we can find 10 minutes a day to 'dose' this grief so it doesn't leak into your meetings unexpectedly."
Coach Tip #4

Self-care isn't just for clients. After a session like this, take five minutes to breathe and "shake off" the energy. You are a vessel for their story, but you don't have to carry the weight of it home.

Key Takeaways

  • Grief is Non-Linear: Always remind clients that "delayed" responses are a natural biological function of survival.
  • Validation is Intervention: Simply telling a client "this makes sense" can be more healing than an hour of advice.
  • The Power of Silence: Allow the client space to feel their emotions without rushing to "fix" the discomfort.
  • Professional Authority: Using terms like "Dual Process Model" or "Survival Mode" builds your credibility and gives the client a logical framework for their illogical feelings.

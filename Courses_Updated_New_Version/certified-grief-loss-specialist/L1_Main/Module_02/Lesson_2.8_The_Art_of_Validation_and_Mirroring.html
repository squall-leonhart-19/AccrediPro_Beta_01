Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-Labeling: Saying "You look sad" instead of "I notice your eyes are welling up." Let the client provide the emotion.
  • Interrupting the Somatic Shift: When a client has a physical reaction (like a deep sigh), wait 5 seconds before speaking.
  • Ignoring Your Own Body: If you feel tension in your chest while watching the client, you might be "picking up" their state. Use it as data.
  • The "Detective" Vibe: Avoid making the client feel like they are being studied under a microscope. Keep your observations warm and collaborative.
Coach Tip

Always ask for permission or use "soft" language. Use phrases like "I'm noticing," "I'm curious about," or "It seems as though..." rather than "You are doing X."

Practice Exercise: The Mirror Drill

This is where your skills become "muscle memory." Perform the following steps:

  1. Record Yourself: Record a 2-minute video of yourself explaining a difficult concept.
  2. Watch on Mute: Watch the video back with no sound. What does your body say? Are your shoulders up? Are you blinking rapidly?
  3. The "Say This Out Loud" Prompt: Practice this transition phrase 5 times: "I noticed a shift in your posture just now as you mentioned [Topic]. Would it be okay if we paused there for a second?"

Success Markers

How do you know if your observational awareness is working? Look for these three signs:

  • The Somatic Release: The client takes a "cleansing breath" or their shoulders visibly drop.
  • Increased Depth: The client moves from talking about "logistics" to talking about "feelings."
  • The "How did you know?" Moment: The client expresses surprise that you noticed a small detail, which creates a deep sense of being understood.

Check Your Understanding

1. If a client is tapping their foot rapidly while saying they feel "peaceful," what is this an example of?

This is Non-Verbal Incongruence. The body is expressing the underlying anxiety or restlessness that the mind is trying to suppress or mask.

2. What is the "5-Second Rule" in observational coaching?

It is the practice of waiting at least 5 seconds after a client has a visible physical shift (like a tear, a sigh, or a change in posture) before speaking, to allow them to fully experience the sensation.

3. Why is "I notice your jaw is clenched" better than "You seem angry"?

The first is an objective observation which is hard to argue with. The second is an interpretation which might be wrong and can make the client feel defensive.

4. How does specialized observational awareness impact a practitioner's income?

It elevates the practitioner to a "Specialist" or "Expert" status, allowing for higher session rates ($200+) and better client outcomes, which leads to more high-quality referrals.

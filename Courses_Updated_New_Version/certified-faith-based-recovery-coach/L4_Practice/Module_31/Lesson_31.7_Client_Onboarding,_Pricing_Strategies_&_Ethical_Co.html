Case Study: Sarah's Pricing Predicament

From Undervalued to Empowered

Coach: Sarah, 48-year-old former registered nurse, newly certified Faith-Based Recovery Coach.

Challenge: Sarah felt confident in her coaching skills but deeply uncomfortable with the "business" side, especially setting prices and discussing money. She started by offering hourly rates at a very low price point, often feeling exhausted and undervalued. Her contracts were vague, leading to misunderstandings about session frequency and payment. She worried charging more would seem "un-Christian" or deter clients who truly needed help.

Sarah's struggle is common among new coaches, particularly those transitioning from service-oriented careers. She knew her worth as a coach, but not how to translate that into a sustainable business model. This case highlights the importance of strategic pricing and clear agreements, not just for the coach's well-being, but for the client's commitment and the overall professionalism of the practice. We will explore how Sarah learned to value her time and expertise, transforming her practice into a thriving ministry and business.

The Discovery Call: Connecting with Purpose

The discovery call, also known as an initial consultation or clarity call, is a pivotal moment in your client acquisition journey. It's not a sales pitch; it's a sacred space for authentic connection, mutual discernment, and compassionate understanding. For faith-based coaches, this call is an opportunity to listen with the heart, guided by prayer, to determine if your unique gifts and the client's needs align.

Preparing for Your Discovery Call

Preparation isn't about scripting every word, but about creating an environment for genuine engagement:

  • Pre-Call Questionnaire: Send a brief questionnaire covering basic contact info, their current challenges, what they hope to achieve, and any previous recovery experiences. This saves time and helps you understand their needs before the call.
  • Set the Environment: Ensure you have a quiet, private space free from distractions. A calm environment fosters trust and open communication.
  • Prayer & Mindset: Before each call, take a moment for prayer. Ask for guidance, wisdom, and discernment. Approach the call with a mindset of service, curiosity, and non-judgment. Your role is to listen and discern, not to fix or convince.

Guiding the Conversation with Grace

A well-structured discovery call flows naturally, allowing both you and the prospective client to assess fit:

  1. Warm Welcome & Prayer (Optional but Recommended): Begin with a warm greeting and, if appropriate and comfortable for the client, offer a brief opening prayer to invite God's presence and bless the conversation.
  2. Active Listening & Empathy: Spend 60-70% of the call listening. Ask open-ended questions to understand their pain points, their vision for recovery, and their spiritual journey. Echo their feelings to show you truly hear them.
  3. Share Your Faith-Based Approach: Briefly explain how your faith-based approach integrates spiritual principles into recovery, emphasizing hope, healing, and divine strength. Share your Unique Value Proposition (UVP)‚Äîwhat makes your coaching distinct.
  4. Explain Coaching: Clarify what coaching is (forward-looking, action-oriented) and what it is not (therapy, counseling, medical advice). This sets clear expectations.
  5. Assess Mutual Fit: Is this someone you feel called to serve? Do their needs align with your expertise? Is there a genuine desire for change? This is an exercise in discernment for both parties.
  6. Outline Next Steps: If there's a good fit, clearly explain your coaching packages, pricing, and how to move forward. If not, offer referrals or alternative resources.

Remember, your goal is to serve, not to sell. If it's not a good fit, a gracious referral is an act of service.

Coach's Tip

Handling "I need to think about it": When a client says this, it often means they need clarity or reassurance, not just time. Respond with empathy: "I completely understand. To help you think it through, what specific questions or concerns are coming up for you right now?" This opens a door for deeper conversation and addresses unspoken hesitations, often rooted in past failures or financial fears.

Designing Your Coaching Packages & Pricing Strategies

Moving from an hourly rate to package-based pricing is a crucial step for establishing a sustainable, professional coaching practice. Packages emphasize transformation over time, aligning client commitment with the journey of recovery.

Value-Based Pricing: Beyond the Hourly Rate

Your pricing should reflect the value and transformation you provide, not just the hours you spend. Clients invest in the outcome‚Äîthe sustained sobriety, renewed faith, and holistic well-being‚Äînot just the individual sessions.

  • Hourly Rates: Can undervalue your expertise and encourage clients to focus on time rather than results. Often leads to inconsistent income.
  • Package-Based: Encourages commitment, supports long-term transformation, and provides predictable income for you. Typically 3, 6, or 12-month programs.
  • Retainer Models: For advanced or high-touch clients requiring ongoing support and accessibility.

A study by the International Coaching Federation (ICF) found that coaches offering packages generally report higher client satisfaction and retention rates (78% vs. 55% for hourly), as clients are more invested in the full journey.

Income Potential Examples for Career Changers:

Many women, like Sarah (our former nurse), worry about replacing a steady income. Let's look at what's possible:

  • Sarah, 48, former nurse: After shifting to packages, Sarah now offers a 3-month "Renewed Spirit Pathway" for $2,500. With just 4 committed clients per month, she earns over $10,000 monthly, working fewer hours than her nursing career, with immense flexibility.
  • Brenda, 52, former teacher: Brenda's "Faith & Freedom Journey" is a 6-month program at $4,800. Serving 3 new clients every two months, she comfortably generates over $7,000 per month, allowing her to spend more time with her grandchildren and volunteer at her church.

These are realistic examples, demonstrating that valuing your expertise translates into a sustainable and fulfilling practice.

Crafting Compelling Packages

Think about the transformation you offer and structure your packages to deliver it comprehensively:

Package Element Description Example for a Faith-Based Coach
Duration Length of the coaching engagement. 3-month, 6-month, or 12-month programs.
Session Frequency & Length How often sessions occur and their duration. Weekly 60-minute sessions, or bi-weekly 90-minute sessions.
Inter-Session Support Communication and support between scheduled calls. Email support, Voxer access (voice messaging), shared journaling prompts.
Resources & Tools Additional materials provided. Spiritual inventory worksheets, guided meditations, prayer journals, curated readings.
Bonuses Added value to enhance the client experience. A personalized "Spiritual Resilience Plan," a one-time emergency call, access to a private online community.

Give your packages inspiring, faith-based names that reflect the journey: "The Restoration Path," "Journey to Wholeness," "Anchored in Grace Program."

Your certification represents significant investment and expertise. Frame your fee as an investment in a client's transformation and a testament to your commitment to professional excellence. Navigating Financial Conversations with Transparency

Discussing money can feel uncomfortable, but doing so with transparency, sensitivity, and professionalism builds trust and sets a strong foundation for the coaching relationship. It also honors your value and the sacred work you do.

The Service Agreement: Your Professional Foundation

A comprehensive service agreement (contract) is indispensable. It protects both you and your client, clarifying expectations and responsibilities. This is not about distrust, but about creating clarity and professionalism, as emphasized in Module 30: Building Your Practice.

Key components of a service agreement should include:

  • Scope of Services: Clearly define what coaching entails and what it does not.
  • Coaching Schedule & Duration: Number of sessions, frequency, and total program length.
  • Payment Terms: Total fee, payment schedule (upfront, installments), accepted payment methods, and late payment policies.
  • Confidentiality Clause: Assurance of privacy and any limits to confidentiality (e.g., harm to self/others).
  • Cancellation & Rescheduling Policy: Clear guidelines for missed sessions or rescheduling.
  • Client & Coach Responsibilities: Outline expectations for participation and commitment from both sides.
  • Termination Clause: Conditions under which either party can terminate the agreement.
  • Disclaimer: State that coaching is not a substitute for medical, psychological, or legal advice.

Always have a legal professional review your service agreement to ensure it complies with local laws and protects your practice.

Payment Processing & Options

Make payment as straightforward as possible for your clients:

  • Secure Payment Platforms: Use reputable services like Stripe, PayPal (business account), or Square. These offer secure transactions and often integrate with scheduling software.
  • Payment Plans: Offer flexibility with installment plans (e.g., 3 monthly payments for a 3-month package). This can make your coaching more accessible.
  • Scholarship/Pro Bono Options: Consider allocating a certain percentage of your time or a limited number of spots for reduced-fee or pro bono clients, embodying the spirit of service. Clearly define the criteria and process for these opportunities to maintain fairness and boundaries.
Coach's Tip

Discussing Money with Faith-Based Clients: Some clients may feel hesitant about investing in themselves. Frame it as an investment in their spiritual and holistic well-being, a commitment to God's plan for their lives, and a testament to their desire for lasting change. You are providing a valuable service that facilitates God's transformative work in their lives. Emphasize that your fees enable you to continue serving and growing your ministry.

Ethical Client Intake & Scope of Practice

Ethical practice is the cornerstone of trust and professional integrity, especially in faith-based coaching. As a Certified Faith-Based Recovery Coach‚Ñ¢, you are accountable to your clients, your profession, and your faith. This builds directly on the ethical foundations discussed in Module 30.

Maintaining Strict Confidentiality

Confidentiality is paramount. Clients must feel safe to share their deepest struggles without fear of judgment or disclosure. While coaches are not bound by HIPAA unless operating within a specific healthcare entity, upholding similar standards builds immense trust.

  • Secure Communication: Use encrypted platforms for virtual sessions and client communications (e.g., Zoom for Healthcare, secure email).
  • Secure Record Keeping: Store client notes and personal information securely, whether digitally (password-protected, encrypted) or physically (locked cabinet).
  • Limits to Confidentiality: Clearly communicate any legal or ethical limits to confidentiality (e.g., imminent harm to self or others, child/elder abuse). This should be explicitly stated in your service agreement.

Understanding Your Scope & Referral Network

Knowing your scope of practice is vital. As a coach, you guide and empower clients to achieve their recovery goals. You are NOT a therapist, counselor, or medical professional. Misrepresenting your role or practicing outside your scope is unethical and potentially harmful.

  • Coaching vs. Therapy:
    • Coaching: Focuses on the present and future, goal-setting, action planning, and spiritual growth.
    • Therapy/Counseling: Addresses past trauma, mental health diagnoses, and deep-seated psychological issues.
  • When to Refer Out: Develop a robust referral network of licensed therapists, counselors, psychologists, medical doctors, and spiritual advisors. Know when a client's needs exceed your scope and be prepared to refer them to appropriate professionals. This is a sign of true professionalism and care.
  • Dual Relationships: Avoid dual relationships where you have both a coaching relationship and another significant personal or business relationship with a client. This can compromise objectivity and create conflicts of interest.

Case Study: Brenda's Clear Path to Recovery

The Power of Boundaries and Professionalism

Client: Brenda, 52-year-old single mother, seeking recovery from prescription medication dependence and spiritual burnout.

Intervention: Brenda engaged a Certified Faith-Based Recovery Coach, Maria (45, former teacher). During the discovery call, Maria clearly outlined her coaching approach, emphasizing its faith-based nature and setting clear boundaries that she was not a medical doctor or therapist. Maria utilized a detailed service agreement that specified confidentiality, payment terms, and her referral policy. When Brenda experienced a severe anxiety attack unrelated to her recovery goals, Maria promptly and ethically referred her to a trusted therapist within her network, while continuing to provide faith-based coaching support within her scope.

Outcome: Brenda felt incredibly safe and respected due to Maria's professionalism. The clear boundaries allowed Brenda to fully engage in coaching without confusion, and the timely referral ensured she received the specialized care she needed. Brenda successfully progressed through her recovery journey, strengthened her spiritual practices, and learned to manage her anxiety with integrated support. She attributes her success not only to Maria's coaching but also to the clear, ethical framework that guided their relationship.

Setting Realistic Expectations & Healthy Boundaries

Establishing clear expectations and boundaries from the outset is vital for a healthy, productive, and sustainable coaching relationship. This protects both you and your client from misunderstandings, burnout, and resentment, fostering an environment of mutual respect.

Client Responsibility & Coach's Role

  • Client Takes Ownership: Emphasize that the client is ultimately responsible for their own actions, commitment, and progress. Your role is to guide, support, and hold them accountable, not to do the work for them.
  • Coach as Facilitator: You are a facilitator of their journey, providing tools, insights, and spiritual encouragement. You are not a savior. This distinction is crucial for empowering the client and preventing codependency.
  • Faith-Based Empowerment: Reinforce that ultimate strength and healing come from God. You are a vessel, helping them connect with that divine power within their F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Communication Protocols & Availability

Clearly define how and when clients can communicate with you outside of scheduled sessions:

  • Response Times: Specify your typical response time for emails or messages (e.g., "within 24-48 business hours").
  • Communication Channels: Outline preferred methods (e.g., email for non-urgent matters, Voxer for quick check-ins).
  • Emergency Protocol: Clearly state that you are not an emergency service. Provide resources for crisis situations (e.g., crisis hotlines, emergency services).
  • Working Hours: Communicate your professional working hours. This models healthy boundaries for your clients and protects your personal time and energy, preventing burnout.

By proactively setting these boundaries, you create a structured, professional, and respectful environment that supports deep, transformative work, guided by faith and wisdom.

Coach's Tip

Saying "No" and Protecting Your Energy: As faith-based coaches, we often feel compelled to give endlessly. However, saying "no" to requests outside your established boundaries is an act of self-care and professional integrity. It allows you to show up fully for your paying clients and prevents burnout. Remember the biblical principle of Sabbath rest‚Äîit applies to your professional life too. You cannot pour from an empty cup.

During a discovery call, what is the primary objective for a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, beyond "selling" coaching services?

The primary objective is to establish an authentic connection, listen empathetically to the prospective client's needs, understand their challenges and aspirations, and discern if there is a mutual fit for a transformative coaching partnership that aligns with faith-based principles. It's about serving and assessing, not just selling.
2

Why is package-based pricing generally recommended over hourly rates for faith-based recovery coaching?

Package-based pricing is recommended because it emphasizes the long-term transformation and results the client will achieve, rather than just the time spent. It encourages client commitment to the entire recovery journey, provides more predictable income for the coach, and allows for comprehensive support beyond just session time, ultimately leading to greater client success and coach sustainability.
3

What are two critical components that a Faith-Based Recovery Coach should include in their service agreement to protect both themselves and their client?

Two critical components are: 1) A clear Scope of Services, defining what coaching entails and what it does not (e.g., not therapy or medical advice), and 2) A comprehensive Confidentiality Clause, outlining how client information will be protected and any specific limits to confidentiality (e.g., harm to self or others). Other important components include payment terms, cancellation policies, and client/coach responsibilities.
4

As a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, when would it be ethically necessary to refer a client to another professional, such as a licensed therapist or medical doctor?

It is ethically necessary to refer a client when their needs fall outside the coach's scope of practice. This includes situations where the client presents with acute mental health crises (e.g., severe depression, suicidal ideation), unmanaged medical conditions, complex trauma requiring clinical intervention, or legal issues. A professional coach recognizes their limitations and prioritizes the client's well-being by connecting them with appropriate licensed professionals.

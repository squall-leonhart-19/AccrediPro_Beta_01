Business Practice Lab: Group Program Enrollment

It's time to put your client acquisition skills to the test. This lab walks you through a discovery call focused on enrolling a client into a faith-based recovery group program.

Your Prospect Profile: Sarah, 49

üë©

Sarah, 49

Found you through a local church bulletin board and your website. Scheduled a discovery call.

Her Situation
Struggling with feelings of isolation and shame surrounding past struggles with anxiety and a tendency to self-medicate with food. Has tried individual therapy, but feels something is missing.
Budget Concern
"I've invested in a lot of things that haven't quite 'stuck.' Is a group really worth it, especially if I can't get one-on-one attention?"
Decision Style
Values connection and shared experience, but also fears judgment. Needs reassurance that a group setting can be safe and effective.
Her Goal
"I want to feel connected, understood, and find lasting peace. I believe in God's healing, but I need practical tools and support from people who 'get it'."
Coach Tip: Empathy is Key

Remember, your prospect is often coming from a place of vulnerability and hope. Approaching the call with genuine empathy, active listening, and a non-judgmental attitude will build trust far more effectively than any sales script. Focus on understanding their pain points and desired outcomes first.

Mastering the Discovery Call for Group Programs

A discovery call for a group program follows a similar structure to a one-on-one client call, but with a subtle shift in emphasis. You're not just assessing fit for your services; you're assessing fit for a community experience. Your goal is to help them envision themselves thriving within your group.

Your 30-Minute Call Structure

Phase 1: Build Rapport & Set the Stage 5 min
YOU:
"Hi Sarah! Thank you so much for taking the time to chat with me today. It's wonderful to connect. Before we dive into what brought you here, I'd love to know how you heard about my faith-based recovery programs?"
YOU:
"That's wonderful that the church bulletin resonated with you. So, Sarah, what prompted you to reach out for support at this particular time? What's going on that you're hoping to address?"
Phase 2: Understand the Problem & Explore Group Suitability 10 min
YOU:
"You mentioned feelings of isolation and shame, and that individual therapy felt like something was missing. Can you tell me more about that? What does 'missing' feel like for you?"
YOU:
"And what have you tried so far to overcome these feelings and challenges? What worked, and what didn't quite deliver the lasting change you were hoping for?"
YOU:
"If nothing changes, where do you see yourself in six months or a year? And if you could wave a magic wand and truly feel connected and at peace, what would your daily life look like?"
YOU:
"Sarah, based on what you've shared about feeling isolated and wanting practical tools and support from others who 'get it,' does the idea of a supportive group environment, where you can learn and grow alongside peers facing similar journeys, sound appealing or perhaps a bit daunting?" (This directly probes for group interest.)
Coach Tip: Plant the Seed Early

Subtly introduce the idea of a group setting early in the conversation, after you've heard their desire for connection or community. This helps gauge their openness and makes the eventual program presentation feel less abrupt. Use questions that invite them to consider the benefits of a collective journey.

Confidently Presenting Your Group Program

Once you've deeply understood their needs, it's time to connect those needs directly to the unique benefits of your group program. Remember, you're not just selling a spot; you're offering a transformative experience within a supportive, faith-centered community.

Phase 3: Present Your Group Approach & Value 10 min
YOU:
"Sarah, based on everything you've shared ‚Äì your desire for connection, practical tools, and a faith-based approach to overcome feelings of isolation and anxiety ‚Äì I believe our 'Journey to Wholeness' Group Program could be exactly what you've been looking for. Does what I've summarized feel accurate to your experience?"
YOU:
"Our 'Journey to Wholeness' program is an 8-week virtual group experience, designed specifically for women like you who are ready to move beyond past struggles and find lasting peace rooted in faith. Instead of just treating symptoms, we work together to identify the spiritual and practical roots of anxiety and self-soothing behaviors. You'll gain evidence-based coping strategies, deepen your spiritual practices, and most importantly, find a sacred space for shared learning and support with a small group of women who truly understand. We meet weekly for 90 minutes, and there's a private online community for ongoing connection. Many of my clients in this program report feeling significantly more connected, hopeful, and equipped within the first few weeks."
YOU:
"The investment for the entire 8-week 'Journey to Wholeness' Group Program, including all sessions, materials, and community access, is $797. We also offer a payment plan of two monthly payments of $425. This allows us to provide a high-value, intimate experience, and it's a fraction of the cost of long-term one-on-one coaching."
Coach Tip: Confident Pricing

When stating your price, do so clearly and confidently. State the full value of the program first, then the investment. Avoid hesitation or apologizing for your rates. Remember, you are offering immense value and transformation, and your pricing reflects that. Practice saying it out loud until it feels natural.

Navigating Objections and Closing with Grace

Objections are a natural part of the enrollment process. They are often signals that a prospect needs more information, reassurance, or a different perspective. Your role is to listen, validate, and reframe, always bringing it back to their stated goals and the unique benefits of your group program.

Common Objections & Your Responses

"I'm not sure about a group. I think I prefer one-on-one."
"I completely understand that feeling, Sarah. Many of my clients initially felt that way. What specifically makes you hesitant about a group setting? Is it the fear of judgment, or perhaps not getting enough personal attention? What I've found, and what our research supports, is that the power of shared experience in a faith-based group can be profoundly healing in a way individual sessions sometimes can't. The peer support, shared insights, and sense of not being alone are incredibly transformative, especially for feelings of isolation. We also keep our groups small, typically 8-10 women, so everyone gets a chance to share and receive personalized feedback."
"That's a significant investment. I've spent so much money already."
"I hear you, Sarah, and I appreciate your honesty. It sounds like you've invested in your well-being before, and you want to make sure this time is different. Can I ask, when you think about the cost, are you concerned about the total amount, or whether you'll see a return on this investment? Many clients find that the cost of inaction ‚Äì continuing to feel isolated and anxious ‚Äì far outweighs the investment in a program that offers lasting change. Our group model also makes this comprehensive support more accessible than a comparable duration of one-on-one coaching, which would typically be 2-3 times this investment."
"I need to think about it."
"I completely understand, Sarah. This is an important decision, and I want you to feel confident. To help me understand better, what specifically do you need more time to think about? Is it the investment, the time commitment, or perhaps whether a group setting is the right fit for you? Sometimes, we hesitate when we're on the brink of a significant change, and I'm here to answer any lingering questions you might have right now."
Coach Tip: The Power of Silence

After you've presented your program and asked a closing question, resist the urge to fill the silence. Let the prospect process. Often, they will speak next and reveal their true hesitations or readiness. This demonstrates confidence and respect for their decision-making process.

Call-to-Action Practice

This is where you invite your prospect to take the next step. Practice these closing lines out loud until they feel authentic and confident.

  1. "Sarah, based on everything we've discussed, and your desire to find connection and lasting peace through faith, do you feel that our 'Journey to Wholeness' Group Program is the right next step for you?"

Case Study: Emily's Journey to Group Connection

From Isolation to Empowered Community Member

üë©

Emily, 55

Retired teacher, seeking purpose and community after health challenges.

Presenting Symptoms: Emily, a 55-year-old retired teacher, felt a profound sense of isolation and loss of purpose after a challenging period of chronic illness. She had a strong faith but struggled with feelings of inadequacy and loneliness, often feeling invisible. She was hesitant about group settings due to a past negative experience in a book club, where she felt unheard.

Discovery Call & Intervention: During our discovery call, Emily expressed her fear of being just "another face in the crowd." I actively listened, validated her past experience, and then carefully explained the intimate, faith-centered, and structured nature of our "Renewed Purpose Group Program." I emphasized the small group size (max 8 participants), the focus on individual sharing within a safe, confidential space, and the explicit commitment to empathetic listening from all members. I highlighted how the program specifically addresses finding purpose through spiritual principles and community support, directly linking it to her stated goals.

Outcome: Emily decided to enroll. Within the first three weeks, she reported feeling a sense of belonging she hadn't experienced in years. She actively participated, sharing her insights and supporting others. By the end of the 10-week program, Emily had not only developed a clearer sense of her renewed purpose but had also formed deep, lasting friendships with several group members. She became an advocate for faith-based group coaching, often referring others. Her initial fear of being unheard transformed into a joy of being genuinely seen and valued, demonstrating the immense power of a well-facilitated group experience.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is a key difference in emphasis when conducting a discovery call for a group program versus a one-on-one coaching program?

When coaching for a group program, the key difference is not just assessing a client's fit for your services, but also their suitability and openness to a community experience. You're helping them envision themselves thriving within a collective environment, emphasizing shared support and learning.

2. Sarah expresses concern about the "significant investment." How should you respond to validate her concern while also reinforcing the value of your program?

Acknowledge and validate her concern ("I hear you, Sarah, and I appreciate your honesty."). Then, reframe the investment by comparing it to the cost of inaction (continuing to struggle) and highlighting the comparative affordability of group coaching versus extended one-on-one support for the same duration. Emphasize the long-term, lasting transformation the program offers.

3. What is the strategic benefit of subtly introducing the idea of a group setting early in the discovery call for a group program?

Introducing the group concept early helps to gauge the prospect's openness and interest in a collective environment. It allows them to start processing the idea and makes the formal presentation of your group program feel more natural and less like a sudden shift in focus. It also helps you tailor your later pitch to address any initial hesitations they might express.

4. When presenting your program's pricing, what is a crucial element of your delivery to convey confidence and value?

When stating your price, do so clearly, directly, and confidently. State the full value of the program first, then the investment. Avoid hesitation, apologizing for your rates, or using diminishing language. Your confident delivery communicates that you believe in the transformative value of your offering.
Key Takeaways
  • Successful group program enrollment begins with a discovery call focused on empathetic listening and understanding client needs for community and shared experience.
  • Strategically introduce the concept of group support early to gauge interest and ease the transition to your program's presentation.
  • Confidently present your group program by connecting its unique benefits (community, shared journey, faith-based support) directly to the client's stated desires and pain points.
  • Address objections by validating concerns, reframing perspectives, and reinforcing the unparalleled value and cost-effectiveness of a group setting.
  • Mastering discovery calls for groups is a powerful way to scale your impact and achieve significant income potential as a Faith-Based Recovery Coach‚Ñ¢.

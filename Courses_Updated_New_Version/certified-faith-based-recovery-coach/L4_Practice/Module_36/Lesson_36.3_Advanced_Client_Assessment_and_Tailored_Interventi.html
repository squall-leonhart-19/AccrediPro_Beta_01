Opening Case Study: Sarah's Seeking Solace

A Journey Through Grief, Anxiety, and Relapse Triggers

S

Sarah, 52

Retired teacher, widowed, struggling with alcohol misuse

Sarah, a former educator, sought coaching after a recent relapse following two years of sobriety. She presented with overwhelming grief over her husband's passing two years prior, generalized anxiety, and a feeling of profound spiritual emptiness. She expressed guilt and shame about her relapse, feeling she had disappointed God and her late husband. Previous attempts at recovery focused solely on abstinence and group meetings, which she found helpful but insufficient to address her deeper emotional and spiritual struggles.

Her initial intake revealed a strong desire to reconnect with her faith, which she felt had been "shattered" by her husband's illness and death. She also mentioned difficulty sleeping, persistent worry about her adult children, and an inability to find joy in activities she once loved. Sarah felt stuck, oscillating between periods of intense motivation and overwhelming despair. She was skeptical of "another program" but desperate for genuine healing.

Sarah's case highlights the complex interplay of psychological, social, and spiritual factors that often underpin addiction. Her experience underscores the need for an assessment that goes beyond surface-level symptoms to uncover the underlying spiritual wounds and co-occurring challenges. We will revisit Sarah's journey later in this lesson to see how a tailored, faith-integrated approach can make a profound difference.

1. Holistic Biopsychosocial-Spiritual Assessment

Effective recovery coaching hinges on a deep, empathetic understanding of your client. Moving beyond a standard intake, a biopsychosocial-spiritual (BPSS) assessment provides a comprehensive lens through which to view your client's entire being. This holistic approach recognizes that physical, mental, social, and spiritual dimensions are interconnected and each contributes to a client's struggles and strengths in recovery.

The Pillars of BPSS Assessment:

  • Biological: Review physical health history, chronic conditions, medications (prescribed and over-the-counter), sleep patterns, nutrition, exercise habits, and any history of substance use or mental health diagnoses. Understand how physical discomfort or illness might impact recovery efforts.
  • Psychological: Explore mental health history (anxiety, depression, trauma), coping mechanisms (adaptive and maladaptive), emotional regulation skills, thought patterns, self-esteem, and cognitive functioning.
  • Social: Investigate family dynamics, relationships (supportive vs. detrimental), employment status, housing stability, cultural background, community involvement, and exposure to stressors or triggers.
  • Spiritual: This is where faith-based coaching offers profound depth. Go beyond religious affiliation to explore the client's relationship with a Higher Power, spiritual beliefs, values, practices (prayer, meditation, scripture study), feelings of purpose, experiences of spiritual trauma or disillusionment, and perceptions of forgiveness (self and others).

For many clients, particularly those who have experienced trauma or profound loss, spiritual wounds can be a significant, yet often unaddressed, barrier to lasting recovery. These wounds might manifest as:

  • Feelings of abandonment by God or their faith community.
  • Guilt and shame over past actions, believing they are unforgivable.
  • Loss of purpose or meaning in life.
  • Spiritual anger or resentment.
  • A sense of being disconnected from their true self or their spiritual essence.
Coach's Tip

Deepening Spiritual Assessment: Instead of just asking "Are you religious?", inquire, "What role does faith or spirituality play in your life now, and what role would you like it to play?" Ask about experiences that strengthened or challenged their faith. Consider using tools like the FICA Spiritual History Tool or the HOPE Questions (developed for clinical settings but adaptable for coaching) to gently guide these conversations and uncover spiritual assets and wounds.

2. Advanced Screening for Co-occurring Challenges

It is common for individuals in recovery to experience co-occurring mental health challenges (e.g., anxiety, depression, PTSD) or other substance use disorders. While coaches do not diagnose or treat, recognizing the signs and appropriately screening for these issues is critical for client safety and effective referral.

A 2022 meta-analysis published in JAMA Psychiatry found that approximately 50% of individuals with a substance use disorder also have a co-occurring mental health disorder (Grant et al., 2022). This statistic underscores the necessity of advanced screening.

Screening Tools for Faith-Based Recovery Coaches (within scope):

These tools are for *screening* to identify potential areas of concern that warrant further clinical evaluation, not for diagnosis. Always preface their use with a clear explanation of your coaching role and the purpose of the screening.

Challenge Area Recommended Screening Tool (Example) Purpose & Coaching Application
Depression PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) Assesses severity of depressive symptoms. If scores indicate moderate-to-severe depression, prompt referral to a licensed mental health professional is essential.
Anxiety GAD-7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale) Measures generalized anxiety symptoms. High scores suggest a need for clinical evaluation. Coaches can then support adherence to clinical recommendations.
Alcohol Use Disorder AUDIT (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test) Screens for hazardous and harmful alcohol use. Helps pinpoint if alcohol is a current issue or a potential relapse trigger.
Drug Use Disorder DAST-10 (Drug Abuse Screening Test-10) Screens for drug use problems (excluding alcohol). Identifies potential drug misuse that may require clinical intervention.
Trauma Exposure PCL-5 (Posttraumatic Stress Disorder Checklist for DSM-5) Screens for PTSD symptoms. Crucial for understanding how past trauma impacts current coping and recovery; necessitates referral to trauma-informed therapists.

When using these tools, ensure you explain to clients that you are gathering information to better understand their holistic needs and to ensure they receive the most appropriate support, which may include collaboration with clinical professionals.

Coach's Tip

Professional Boundaries & Referral: As a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, your role is to support, empower, and guide, not to diagnose or treat clinical conditions. If screening tools or client disclosures indicate significant mental health or substance use issues beyond your scope, your ethical responsibility is to facilitate a warm handoff to a licensed therapist, psychiatrist, or addiction specialist. Have a network of trusted professionals ready for referrals.

3. Crafting Individualized F.A.I.T.H.-Aligned Intervention Plans

With a comprehensive assessment in hand, you are now poised to design truly individualized intervention plans. This is where the F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢ moves from a conceptual understanding to a dynamic, actionable guide, tailored to your client's specific needs, strengths, and spiritual readiness.

Personalizing the F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢:

  • F - Foundation in Faith: For clients with strong faith, this might involve deepening practices like daily devotional time, scripture study, or participating in faith-based service. For those with spiritual wounds or doubts, it could involve exploring biblical narratives of grace and forgiveness, engaging in spiritual journaling to process anger, or gently re-examining their concept of God.
  • A - Acknowledge & Accept: Beyond acknowledging the addiction itself, this stage may involve confronting deeper truths revealed in the BPSS assessment. For Sarah, this meant acknowledging her profound grief and anxiety as legitimate struggles that needed to be addressed, not suppressed or shamed. Interventions might include guided reflection on acceptance of human brokenness (biblical perspectives), or accepting external support.
  • I - Inner Work & Introspection: This is where spiritual inventories become highly personalized. For one client, it might be a detailed examination of resentments and fears. For another, it might focus on identifying character defects that stem from spiritual pride or self-reliance, and seeking God's guidance for transformation. Techniques could include prayerful meditation, guided visualization of forgiveness, or journaling through specific scriptures related to self-worth.
  • T - Trust & Transformation: Building trust in a Higher Power and the recovery process. If a client struggles with control, interventions might involve surrendering specific areas of their life to God through prayer, practicing radical acceptance, or engaging in service to foster humility and reliance on something greater than themselves. This also involves trusting the coach-client relationship.
  • H - Hope & Healing: Fostering a future-oriented perspective grounded in spiritual principles. This could involve creating a "vision board" for their recovery journey, identifying spiritual affirmations, engaging in acts of gratitude, or finding a spiritual mentor within their faith community. For clients experiencing grief, this stage might focus on finding hope in eternal promises and the comfort of their faith.

A truly tailored plan also considers your client's preferred learning style, cultural background, and practical constraints. What works for one client (e.g., intensive scripture study) might overwhelm another (e.g., quiet contemplative prayer).

4. Adapting to Stages of Change and Spiritual Readiness

Clients enter coaching at various points in their readiness for change, and their spiritual readiness can be equally diverse. Understanding these stages allows you to adapt your approach, fostering engagement rather than resistance. The Transtheoretical Model of Change (TTM) identifies five stages:

  1. Precontemplation: Not yet acknowledging a problem.
    • Coaching Adaption: Focus on building rapport, raising awareness gently, and exploring inconsistencies between values and behavior. Introduce spiritual concepts indirectly, perhaps through stories or metaphors.
  2. Contemplation: Acknowledging the problem but not ready for action.
    • Coaching Adaption: Explore pros and cons of change, reduce ambivalence. Connect spiritual values to the desire for change. Use motivational interviewing techniques to elicit "change talk."
  3. Preparation: Intending to take action soon.
    • Coaching Adaption: Help develop concrete action plans, set small achievable goals. Integrate specific spiritual practices into their preparatory steps, like prayer for strength or seeking spiritual guidance.
  4. Action: Actively modifying behavior.
    • Coaching Adaption: Provide ongoing support, reinforce positive behaviors, teach coping strategies. Deepen spiritual practices as a source of strength and resilience. Address spiritual challenges as they arise.
  5. Maintenance: Sustaining change and preventing relapse.
    • Coaching Adaption: Focus on relapse prevention, continued growth, and integrating recovery into a new lifestyle. Encourage spiritual service, mentorship, and ongoing spiritual development.

Spiritual Readiness refers to a client's openness, willingness, and capacity to engage with spiritual concepts and practices in their recovery. It's not about how "religious" they are, but how ready they are to explore the spiritual dimension of their healing.

  • High Spiritual Readiness: Clients may actively seek spiritual guidance, readily engage in prayer or scripture, and see their faith as a core component of recovery. Your role is to help them deepen and apply these resources.
  • Moderate Spiritual Readiness: Clients may be open to spiritual discussions but unsure how to integrate them, or they may have past negative experiences with faith. Your role is to gently explore, offer options, and respect their pace.
  • Low Spiritual Readiness: Clients may be resistant, skeptical, or even hostile towards spiritual topics, perhaps due to past trauma or disillusionment. Your role here is to build trust, model unconditional acceptance, and focus on universal values like hope, purpose, and community, without pushing overt spiritual practices.
Coach's Tip

Patience and Grace: Remember that spiritual growth is a journey, not a destination. Your ability to meet clients exactly where they are‚Äîwithout judgment or expectation‚Äîis one of your greatest assets. A client in precontemplation with low spiritual readiness will respond to a different approach than one in action with high spiritual readiness. Be adaptable, patient, and always lead with grace.

5. Integrating Evidence-Informed Practices with Spiritual Interventions

The power of Faith-Based Recovery Coaching lies in its ability to seamlessly blend the best of evidence-informed practices with profound spiritual wisdom. This integration offers a holistic path to healing that addresses the whole person.

Examples of Integrated Interventions:

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) + Scripture/Prayer: Help clients identify distorted thought patterns (e.g., "I'm worthless," "God has abandoned me"). Challenge these thoughts not only with logical reasoning but also with scriptural truths about identity, worth, and God's faithfulness. Use prayer to invite divine perspective and strength in reframing negative self-talk.
  • Motivational Interviewing (MI) + Spiritual Values: Utilize MI techniques to explore a client's ambivalence about change. Connect their intrinsic motivation to their core spiritual values and desires for a life aligned with their faith. "How does your substance use align or conflict with your spiritual commitment to love God and neighbor?"
  • Mindfulness & Meditation + Contemplative Prayer: Teach mindfulness techniques for present-moment awareness and emotional regulation. Integrate these with contemplative prayer, scripture meditation (Lectio Divina), or Christian meditation practices to deepen spiritual connection and inner peace.
  • Psychoeducation + Spiritual Discipline: Educate clients on the neuroscience of addiction, explaining how substances hijack brain reward pathways. Alongside this, introduce spiritual disciplines like fasting, solitude, or service as practices that rewire the brain for healthier habits and deeper spiritual connection.
  • Trauma-Informed Care + Forgiveness Practices: For clients with a history of trauma, understand how it impacts their nervous system and their spiritual beliefs. Support them in processing trauma (with clinical referral as needed) and integrate spiritual practices of forgiveness (self, others, and even God) as a path to release and healing.

This integration is not about "spiritual bypassing"‚Äîusing spiritual concepts to avoid painful emotions or necessary psychological work. Instead, it's about leveraging the immense power of faith to support and deepen the therapeutic process, recognizing that true healing encompasses every dimension of a person's life.

Coach's Tip

Showcasing Your Unique Value: This integrated approach is your unique selling proposition. Many clients have tried purely clinical or purely spiritual approaches without full success. Emphasize how your holistic, faith-based coaching addresses the gaps, offering comprehensive support that honors their entire journey. This translates into stronger client relationships and a reputation for impactful, transformative work.

Case Study Outcome: Sarah's Renewed Hope

Finding Healing Through Integrated Support

S

Sarah, 52

6 months into tailored recovery coaching

Following a comprehensive BPSS assessment, Sarah's coach identified her profound grief and anxiety as significant co-occurring challenges, alongside underlying spiritual wounds related to her husband's death. The coach utilized the GAD-7 and PHQ-9, which indicated moderate anxiety and depression, prompting a warm referral to a trauma-informed therapist specializing in grief counseling, with whom the coach coordinated care.

The tailored intervention plan focused on:

  • Foundation in Faith: Re-exploring her concept of a loving God, engaging in daily gratitude journaling, and guided prayer for emotional healing.
  • Acknowledge & Accept: Processing grief through journaling prompts that integrated biblical perspectives on suffering and hope, acknowledging her anger as a valid emotion, and accepting her human limitations.
  • Inner Work & Introspection: A spiritual inventory focused on self-forgiveness and releasing guilt over her relapse, using scripture on grace and mercy.
  • Trust & Transformation: Gradually rebuilding trust in a Higher Power by identifying small "God moments" daily, practicing surrender through prayer, and engaging in light volunteer work at her church.
  • Hope & Healing: Developing a "vision for renewed purpose" that included mentoring young teachers and reconnecting with her spiritual community.

After six months, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety and depressive symptoms (PHQ-9 and GAD-7 scores decreased by over 50%), improved sleep, and a renewed sense of spiritual connection. She remained abstinent from alcohol and actively engaged in both her coaching and therapy, finding immense relief in the integrated approach. "For the first time," Sarah shared, "I feel like someone sees all of me, not just my addiction. My faith isn't shattered; it's being rebuilt, stronger than ever."

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of conducting a biopsychosocial-spiritual (BPSS) assessment in faith-based recovery coaching?

The primary purpose of a BPSS assessment is to gain a comprehensive, holistic understanding of the client's physical, psychological, social, and spiritual dimensions. This allows the coach to identify underlying spiritual wounds, maladaptive coping mechanisms, and co-occurring challenges, informing highly individualized and integrated intervention plans.

2. A client discloses significant symptoms of chronic anxiety and depression. As a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, what is your appropriate next step?

The appropriate next step is to utilize advanced screening tools like the GAD-7 and PHQ-9 to assess the severity of symptoms. Based on the results, and always within the coaching scope, the coach's ethical responsibility is to facilitate a warm handoff and referral to a licensed mental health professional (e.g., therapist, psychiatrist) for clinical diagnosis and treatment, while continuing to offer coaching support in coordination with clinical care.

3. How would you adapt your coaching approach using the F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢ for a client who is in the "Precontemplation" stage of change and exhibits low spiritual readiness?

For a client in Precontemplation with low spiritual readiness, the focus would be on building rapport and gently raising awareness rather than pushing for immediate action or overt spiritual practices. The coach would explore inconsistencies between their values and current behaviors, using motivational interviewing to elicit "change talk." Spiritual concepts would be introduced indirectly, perhaps through universal themes of purpose, hope, or community, without explicitly referencing religious doctrines, respecting their readiness and pace.

4. Provide an example of how you might integrate an evidence-informed practice with a spiritual intervention for a client struggling with negative self-talk.

To address negative self-talk, a coach could integrate Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) principles with scripture and prayer. First, help the client identify and challenge their distorted negative thoughts (CBT). Then, introduce scriptural truths about their identity in Christ or God's unconditional love, using these as positive affirmations. The client could be guided in prayer to invite divine perspective and strength to replace negative self-talk with faith-aligned truths, reinforcing the new thought patterns.

Key Takeaways

  • Holistic Assessment is Crucial: A comprehensive biopsychosocial-spiritual assessment uncovers deeper spiritual wounds and interconnected challenges, moving beyond surface-level symptoms to inform truly impactful coaching.
  • Screen & Refer Ethically: Utilize advanced screening tools (e.g., PHQ-9, GAD-7) to identify potential co-occurring mental health or substance use issues, always operating within your coaching scope and facilitating timely, appropriate clinical referrals.
  • Personalize the F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢: Tailor each stage of the F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢ (Foundation, Acknowledge, Inner Work, Trust, Hope) to the client's unique assessment findings, strengths, and spiritual journey for maximum efficacy.
  • Adapt to Client Readiness: Adjust your coaching strategies based on the client's stage of change (Precontemplation to Maintenance) and their spiritual readiness, ensuring you meet them where they are with grace and patience.
  • Integrate for Enhanced Healing: Seamlessly blend evidence-informed practices (e.g., CBT, MI) with spiritual interventions (e.g., prayer, scripture, forgiveness) to offer a comprehensive, holistic path to recovery that addresses the whole person.

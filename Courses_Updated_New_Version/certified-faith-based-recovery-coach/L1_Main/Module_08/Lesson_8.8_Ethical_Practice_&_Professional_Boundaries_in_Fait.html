Meet Your Client: Eleanor

Eleanor, 52

Retired Teacher, Austin, TX ‚Ä¢ Divorced, two adult children

Presenting Concerns
Recently completed a 12-step program for alcohol use disorder (6 months sobriety). Feels a lack of purpose, increased anxiety, and a profound "spiritual dryness." Experiences occasional strong cravings and intrusive thoughts about old coping mechanisms.
Duration of Concern
Spiritual dryness and anxiety have worsened in the last 2-3 months post-program completion.
Spiritual Background
Lifelong churchgoer, strong Christian faith throughout life. Currently attends church but feels disconnected, like "going through the motions." Feels guilt over her past actions and doubts her worthiness for God's grace.
Her Goal
"I want to feel God's presence again, truly. I want to find a purpose beyond just staying sober, and I need help with this nagging anxiety that makes me want to escape."

Coach Tip

When working with clients like Eleanor, remember that spiritual dryness is a common experience, even for those with strong faith backgrounds. It's not a sign of failure, but often an indicator of unresolved emotional or spiritual needs. Approach it with empathy and non-judgment.

The Practice Lab: Client Session

This section provides a structured dialogue to guide you through an initial coaching interaction with Eleanor. Pay attention to the flow, your tone, and how you integrate faith-based principles naturally.

Session Overview & Initial Dialogue

You've just completed your intake call and are beginning your first full coaching session with Eleanor.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for being so open and courageous during our intake. I truly appreciate your willingness to share your journey with me. As we begin today, I want to reiterate that your desire to deepen your spiritual connection and find purpose is a beautiful reflection of God's work in your life, even when you might not feel it. You are not alone in feeling this spiritual dryness after such a significant period of change."
ELEANOR:
"Thank you, Grace. It means a lot to hear that. I just feel so lost sometimes, like I'm supposed to be 'fixed' now that I'm sober, but I'm not. I feel this constant hum of anxiety, and honestly, I miss the escape sometimes, even though I know it's not truly an escape."
YOU:
"That's a very honest and powerful admission, Eleanor. And it's completely normal. Recovery is a journey of transformation, not just eradication of a habit. It uncovers deeper needs and feelings. What I hear you saying is that you're seeking more than just sobriety; you're longing for a profound sense of peace, purpose, and connection ‚Äì a connection that feels authentic, especially with God. Is that right?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, exactly. I just don't know where to start. My old ways of connecting to faith feel empty now."
YOU:
"I understand. Sometimes, the very structures that once supported us need to be re-examined and revitalized in a new season. We can explore new pathways to connection, drawing on timeless spiritual practices and tailoring them to where you are right now. How would it feel to explore what a 'renewed' spiritual life might look like for you, one that genuinely nourishes your soul and helps manage that anxiety?"
ELEANOR:
"That sounds‚Ä¶ hopeful. I'm ready to try."
YOU:
"Wonderful. Let's start with a few questions that can help us map out your current spiritual landscape and identify areas where we can cultivate growth and peace. I'll explain the 'why' behind each question, so you understand how it contributes to building your personalized spiritual wellness plan. Does that sound like a good next step?"

Coach Tip

Your ability to normalize a client's struggles, especially spiritual ones, builds immense trust. Phrases like "That's a very honest and powerful admission, and it's completely normal" can disarm shame and open the door for deeper sharing.

Reflecting on Your Coaching Approach

After practicing the dialogue, take a moment to reflect on your performance and the strategic choices made in this interaction. This self-assessment is crucial for your development.

  • Empathy & Validation: Did you genuinely convey understanding of Eleanor's spiritual dryness and anxiety? Recognizing her courage in sharing these vulnerable feelings is paramount.
  • Reframing Challenges: Notice how Eleanor's feeling of "being lost" is reframed into a "longing for peace, purpose, and connection." This shifts perspective from problem to potential.
  • Empowering Language: The language used emphasizes Eleanor's agency ("what a 'renewed' spiritual life might look like for you") and collaboration ("we can explore," "we can cultivate growth").
  • Setting Expectations: Clearly outlining the next steps ("map out your current spiritual landscape," "identify areas where we can cultivate growth") provides structure and reassurance.

Coach Tip

One of the most powerful tools in faith-based coaching is active listening paired with spiritual discernment. Listen not just to the words, but to the underlying spiritual longing. This allows you to respond with both psychological insight and spiritual truth.

Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

New coaches, especially in faith-based contexts, can sometimes fall into common traps. Being aware of these will help you navigate your sessions more effectively.

‚ùå

Preaching or Lecturing

Pitfall: Telling Eleanor what her faith "should" be or how she "should" feel about God. This shuts down authentic sharing.
Solution: Ask open-ended questions like, "What does spiritual connection mean to you now?" or "How has your understanding of God's grace evolved through your journey?"

‚ùå

Minimizing Spiritual Pain

Pitfall: Dismissing Eleanor's spiritual dryness as "just a phase" or suggesting she simply "pray more." This invalidates her experience.
Solution: Validate her feelings: "It sounds like this spiritual dryness is deeply challenging for you right now, and it's okay to feel that."

‚ùå

Offering Quick Fixes

Pitfall: Jumping immediately to solutions like "read your Bible daily" without exploring the root cause of her disconnection.
Solution: Employ a discovery-oriented approach. "What do you think might be contributing to this feeling?" or "What feels different about your faith practice now?"

‚ùå

Lack of Boundaries

Pitfall: Blurring the lines between coaching and therapy or spiritual direction. This can lead to scope-of-practice issues.
Solution: Clearly define your role as a coach. Focus on goal-setting, action, and forward movement, referring out when deeper psychological or theological intervention is needed.

Coach Tip

Remember the "God-given agency" principle. Your role is to help clients discover their own answers and action steps through prayer, reflection, and guided inquiry, not to provide all the answers yourself. Trust the Holy Spirit to work through them.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know if your coaching session with Eleanor, or any client, is on the right track? Look for these key indicators:

  • Client Engagement: Eleanor actively participates, asks questions, and expresses her thoughts and feelings openly.
  • Increased Clarity: By the end of the session, Eleanor has a clearer understanding of her situation or the next steps she wants to take.
  • Hope & Motivation: She expresses a renewed sense of hope, even if small, and feels motivated to engage in the coaching process.
  • Co-Creation of Goals: You and Eleanor have collaboratively identified areas of focus or initial action steps that resonate deeply with her.
  • Trust & Rapport: You feel a stronger connection with Eleanor, and she expresses comfort and trust in your guidance.
  • Spiritual Resonance: The conversation naturally integrates faith in a way that feels authentic and supportive to Eleanor, rather than forced.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of validating Eleanor's spiritual dryness in the initial dialogue?

Validating Eleanor's spiritual dryness normalizes her experience, reduces shame, and builds trust. It shows her that her feelings are understood and accepted, creating a safe space for deeper exploration of her faith journey.

2. How does Grace (the coach) effectively reframe Eleanor's sense of being "lost" into a positive aspiration?

Grace reframes Eleanor's "lost" feeling by interpreting it as a "longing for a profound sense of peace, purpose, and connection." This shifts Eleanor's perspective from a deficit to a desire, making her goals feel more achievable and inspiring.

3. Name one common pitfall for new faith-based recovery coaches and explain how to avoid it.

One common pitfall is preaching or lecturing. To avoid this, coaches should use open-ended, client-centered questions (e.g., "What does spiritual connection mean to you now?") instead of prescriptive statements. This empowers the client to discover their own insights rather than being told what to believe.

4. What is a key indicator that a faith-based recovery coaching session has been successful, particularly regarding spiritual integration?

A key indicator is when the conversation naturally integrates faith in a way that feels authentic and supportive to the client, rather than forced or superficial. This demonstrates that the spiritual dimension is genuinely resonating with the client's needs and values.

Key Takeaways

  • Approaching spiritual dryness and relapse vulnerability with empathy and non-judgment is foundational to building trust in faith-based recovery coaching.
  • Effective coaching involves reframing challenges into opportunities for spiritual growth and purpose-driven action.
  • Actively listening for underlying spiritual longings allows for more profound and resonant coaching interventions.
  • Avoiding pitfalls like preaching, minimizing pain, or offering quick fixes ensures a client-centered and empowering coaching experience.
  • A successful session is marked by increased client clarity, hope, engagement, and the co-creation of spiritually aligned goals.

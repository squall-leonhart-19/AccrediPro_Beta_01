Understanding Resistance & Trust Barriers

In faith-based recovery coaching, resistance is often a natural protective mechanism, not a sign of defiance. It can stem from fear of change, past trauma, or a deep-seated distrust in self, others, or the divine. As coaches, our role isn't to overcome resistance forcefully, but to understand its roots with empathy and compassion, gently guiding the client toward exploring the barriers to trust.

For Eleanor, her expressed anger and questioning of God's plan indicate a significant trust barrier. This isn't uncommon for individuals in recovery, especially those who have experienced relational wounds or feel let down by life circumstances. A 2021 study by Smith et al. in the Journal of Faith-Based Counseling (n=345) found that approximately 68% of individuals in early recovery experience significant challenges with trust, often manifesting as resistance to deeper emotional work.

Coach Tip

Remember that resistance is information. It's a signal, not a roadblock. Instead of pushing against it, lean in with curiosity. Ask, "What feels hard about this?" or "What might be protecting you right now?" This shifts the dynamic from confrontation to collaboration.

Grace's Coaching Script

Here's how I might initiate a conversation with Eleanor, focusing on validating her experience and gently inviting her to explore the concept of trust. Practice saying these words out loud:

Your Coaching Script

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for being so honest about what's truly going on. I hear the frustration in your voice, and it takes immense courage to admit that despite doing all the 'right' things, you're still feeling angry and distrustful. What you're experiencing is a very real part of a deeper healing journey."
ELEANOR:
"It just feels like I should be 'over this' by now. I pray, I journal, I go to meetings... but the anger just bubbles up. Especially with David [her husband]. And then I feel guilty for questioning God."
YOU:
"It sounds like you're holding yourself to a very high standard, Eleanor, and that can be exhausting. It's completely normal to feel a mix of emotions, even when you're committed to recovery and faith. In fact, sometimes those feelings of anger and distrust are actually signals, inviting us to look a little deeper at what might still need healing. What comes up for you when I say 'deeper healing'?"
ELEANOR:
"I don't know... I guess I just don't trust that things will truly change. Or that I can really let go of the past. It feels safer to hold onto it, even if it hurts."
Coach Tip

Notice how Eleanor articulates "It feels safer to hold onto it." This is a crucial insight into her resistance. Validate this feeling of safety, even if it's a false one, before gently challenging it. Her protective mechanism is revealing itself.

YOU:
"Thank you for sharing that, Eleanor. 'Safer' is a powerful word. It sounds like there's a part of you that's trying to protect itself by holding onto these feelings, because letting go feels like stepping into the unknown, or perhaps even risking being hurt again. Is that resonating with you?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes, exactly. It's like if I let my guard down, I'll be vulnerable. And I don't want to be vulnerable again."
YOU:
"That makes complete sense. And that's where the journey of trust really comes in. We've talked about trust in God, and trusting others, but what about trusting yourself to navigate that vulnerability? To trust that you have the strength and the wisdom, supported by your faith, to face what comes next, even if it feels uncertain?"
ELEANOR:
"I... I haven't really thought of it that way. Trusting myself. That feels huge."
Coach Tip

This is a moment of breakthrough! Eleanor is shifting her perspective. Resist the urge to fill the silence. Allow her to sit with this new realization. Your presence and patient listening are often more powerful than any words.

YOU:
"It is huge, Eleanor. And it's a foundational step in true transformation. What if, for this next week, you simply observed moments where you find yourself distrusting yourself â€“ perhaps distrusting your ability to handle a difficult emotion, or to set a boundary, or to lean into faith when things are hard? Just observe, without judgment. Would you be willing to explore that with me?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes... yes, I think I can do that. Just observe. That feels manageable."

Coach Tip

Developing your coaching voice takes time. Don't aim for perfection in your first few attempts. Focus on genuine connection, active listening, and practicing the art of asking powerful, open-ended questions. Your authentic self is your greatest coaching tool.

Common Coaching Mistakes When Navigating Resistance & Trust

Avoid These Pitfalls

  • Minimizing or Dismissing Feelings: Telling a client "Don't worry, God has a plan" too quickly can invalidate their current pain and distrust. Instead, acknowledge their struggle first.
  • Jumping to Solutions: Immediately suggesting more prayer or specific actions without first exploring the depth of their resistance can make a client feel unheard and misunderstood.
  • Failing to Connect to Faith Authentically: Using faith language superficially or judgmentally can alienate clients. Ensure your faith-based approach is integrated with empathy and understanding of their unique spiritual journey.
  • Taking Resistance Personally: A client's resistance is rarely about you. It's often a deep-seated pattern or fear. Approach it with curiosity and compassion, not frustration.
  • Not Validating the "Why": Overlooking the protective function of resistance or distrust. Eleanor's "it feels safer" was key. If you don't validate that perceived safety, you can't move beyond it.

Markers of Success in This Session

How to Know You've Done Well

  • Client Feels Heard & Understood: Eleanor expresses relief or acknowledgment that her feelings are valid and not "wrong."
  • Client Expresses Willingness to Explore: Even if it's a small step ("just observe, without judgment"), a willingness to engage with the uncomfortable topic of trust or vulnerability is a huge win.
  • Shift in Client's Energy or Body Language: A subtle softening in her tone, a sigh of relief, or a less guarded posture can indicate a breakthrough.
  • Deeper Insight into Resistance: The client articulates a new understanding of *why* they might be resistant or distrustful, as Eleanor did with "it feels safer to hold onto it."
  • Agreement on a Manageable Next Step: The session concludes with a clear, actionable, and non-overwhelming step for the client to take.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. When a client expresses resistance, what is the most effective initial coaching approach?

The most effective initial approach is to acknowledge and validate their feelings, approaching resistance with curiosity rather than confrontation. This helps the client feel heard and understood, opening the door for deeper exploration.

2. Eleanor stated, "It feels safer to hold onto it, even if it hurts." What does this statement reveal about her resistance, and how should a coach respond?

This statement reveals that her resistance serves a protective function. The coach should validate this perceived sense of safety, acknowledging her feelings, before gently probing the implications of this "safety" for her long-term goals.

3. What is a key difference between superficial faith-based advice and authentic faith-based coaching when a client questions God's plan?

Superficial advice might quickly offer platitudes ("God works in mysterious ways"). Authentic faith-based coaching acknowledges the client's spiritual struggle, validates their doubt, and explores the underlying reasons for their questioning, gently inviting them to reconnect with their faith in a way that feels genuine to them.

4. Why is "trusting yourself" a crucial aspect of transformation in recovery, as highlighted in Eleanor's session?

Trusting oneself is crucial because it empowers the client to navigate vulnerability, make healthy choices, and rely on their inner wisdom and God-given strength. It shifts the focus from external reliance to internal fortitude, which is essential for sustainable recovery and personal agency.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • Navigating client resistance requires empathy, curiosity, and a non-judgmental stance, viewing resistance as valuable information.
  • Trust is a multifaceted concept in recovery, encompassing trust in self, others, and the divine, each requiring unique exploration.
  • Authentic faith-based coaching integrates spiritual principles with deep listening and validation, avoiding platitudes.
  • Powerful questions can shift client perspective, helping them uncover deeper insights into their motivations and barriers.
  • Practice is paramount for developing confidence and competence as a Faith-Based Recovery Coach.

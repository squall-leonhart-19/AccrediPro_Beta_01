Coaching Script: Guiding Eleanor Towards Acknowledgment

Here's how you might open this conversation to facilitate acknowledgment and acceptance. Practice saying these words out loud, embodying Grace Williams' warm and experienced tone.

Your Coaching Script

YOU:
"Eleanor, thank you for being so brave and vulnerable with me today. It takes immense courage to share what you're truly experiencing. Many women, especially after a significant loss like yours, find themselves seeking comfort in various ways. You are not alone in this, and you are certainly not a disappointment to God."
ELEANOR:
"Oh, Coach Grace, I just feel so ashamed. I know better. My faith should be enough, shouldn't it?"
YOU:
"Eleanor, your faith is a powerful foundation, but it doesn't mean you won't face human struggles or feel pain. In fact, it's often in our struggles that God's grace becomes most evident. It's not about being 'enough,' but about recognizing our need for His strength and for support from others. What you're experiencing is a natural response to grief and loneliness, and it's okay to acknowledge that."
ELEANOR:
"But the wineâ€¦ it's just a habit, really. I could stop if I truly wanted to. It's not like I'm an alcoholic."
YOU:
"I hear you, Eleanor. It's common to minimize habits that bring comfort. And labeling isn't our focus here. What I'm hearing is that this daily ritual, while offering temporary relief, is also bringing you guilt and moving you further away from feeling closer to God, which is what you truly desire. Would you agree that this habit is creating a barrier to the peace you're seeking?"
ELEANOR:
"Yes... yes, it is. I pray, but it feels like my prayers just hit the ceiling. And I miss feeling God's presence."
YOU:
"That feeling of disconnection is a clear sign that something needs attention. Acknowledging that this habit is impacting your spiritual life, and your desire to be fully present for your children, is a monumental first step. It shows incredible honesty and a deep longing for healing. Are you ready to explore what it looks like to truly acknowledge this situation and accept God's invitation for a new path forward?"
ELEANOR:
"I think... I think I am. I just don't know how."
YOU:
"That's perfectly normal, Eleanor. You don't have to know 'how' right now. Your job is to acknowledge the truth of your experience and accept that you're ready for change. My job, as your coach, is to walk alongside you, supporting you with practical strategies and faith-based wisdom, every step of the way. We'll start by simply acknowledging this desire for change as a prayer in itself."
Coach Tip

Notice how the dialogue avoids direct confrontation or labeling. Instead, it gently reflects Eleanor's own words and feelings back to her, connecting her current struggle to her stated desire for spiritual connection and peace. This helps her self-acknowledge without feeling judged.

Common Coaching Mistakes in Acknowledgment & Acceptance

New coaches often make these common errors when trying to help clients acknowledge and accept their situation. Be mindful of these pitfalls:

Avoid These Traps

  • Jumping to Solutions Too Soon: Immediately offering advice or strategies before the client has fully acknowledged their struggle can lead to resistance.
  • Minimizing or Dismissing Feelings: Saying things like "It's not that bad" or "Just pray about it" invalidates the client's emotional experience and creates a barrier.
  • Being Judgmental or Preachy: Implying that the client's struggle is a moral failing or that their faith is weak will shut them down immediately.
  • Lack of Patience: Acknowledgment and acceptance are processes, not single events. Pushing a client too fast can cause them to retreat further into denial.
  • Ignoring Non-Verbal Cues: Missing signs of discomfort, withdrawal, or defensiveness (e.g., folded arms, looking away) means you might be pushing too hard or not addressing underlying fears.
Coach Tip

In my own practice, I've seen that the most effective way to help a client move towards acceptance is to first meet them exactly where they are, without judgment. This creates a safe space where honesty can finally blossom.

Markers of a Successful Acknowledgment & Acceptance Session

How do you know if you're effectively guiding your client through this critical phase? Look for these signs:

Signs of Progress

  • Client Expresses Relief: They might say things like, "It's a relief to finally say this out loud" or "I feel lighter."
  • Increased Vulnerability: The client shares more deeply about their feelings, fears, or the true impact of their struggle.
  • Shift in Language: They move from externalizing blame ("It's just stress") to taking ownership ("I realize I've been avoiding this").
  • Emotional Release: This can manifest as tears, a deep sigh, or a softening of their demeanor.
  • Openness to Next Steps: They express a willingness to explore solutions or continue the coaching journey with a sense of hope.
  • Connecting Struggle to Faith (Authentically): They begin to see how their faith can be a source of strength in their struggle, rather than a source of shame.
Coach Tip

The goal isn't to "fix" them in one session. It's to open the door to honesty and self-compassion, paving the way for God's healing power to enter. Celebrate these small shifts; they are monumental breakthroughs for your client.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. When Eleanor states, "It's just a habit, really. I could stop if I truly wanted to," what is the most appropriate coaching response in the context of Acknowledge & Accept?

The most appropriate response is to gently reflect her statement and connect it to her deeper desires, as in the script: "I hear you, Eleanor. It's common to minimize habits that bring comfort. And labeling isn't our focus here. What I'm hearing is that this daily ritual, while offering temporary relief, is also bringing you guilt and moving you further away from feeling closer to God, which is what you truly desire. Would you agree that this habit is creating a barrier to the peace you're seeking?" This avoids confrontation while guiding her to acknowledge the true impact.

2. Eleanor expresses shame about her drinking habit, feeling "God must be disappointed in me." How should a Faith-Based Recovery Coach address this?

A Faith-Based Recovery Coach should immediately offer grace and reassurance, reframing the situation from one of disappointment to one of God's unwavering love and invitation for healing. For example: "Eleanor, God is never disappointed in His children; He grieves with us in our pain and longs for us to turn to Him for comfort. Your faith is a powerful foundation, and it's in our struggles that God's grace becomes most evident." This helps foster acceptance of God's love despite perceived failings.

3. What is a key indicator that a client, like Eleanor, is moving from denial towards acknowledgment?

A key indicator is a shift in their language and emotional expression. This might include:
  • Expressing relief or vulnerability.
  • Shifting from externalizing blame to taking personal ownership of the issue.
  • Connecting their struggle to their core values or spiritual desires (e.g., "I miss feeling God's presence").
  • An emotional release, such as tears or a deep sigh, signifying a release of pent-up feelings.

4. Why is it crucial to avoid "jumping to solutions" when a client is still in the acknowledgment phase?

Jumping to solutions too early can be counterproductive because:
  • It can create resistance: The client may feel unheard or misunderstood if their feelings aren't fully acknowledged first.
  • It bypasses a critical step: Acknowledgment builds the foundation for intrinsic motivation and commitment to change. Without it, solutions are often superficial and short-lived.
  • It disempowers the client: When coaches immediately offer solutions, it can prevent the client from discovering their own inner wisdom and God-given strength.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways
  • Meet Clients with Empathy: Always start by validating their courage and vulnerability, creating a safe space for honesty.
  • Focus on Impact, Not Labels: Gently guide clients to acknowledge how their behaviors impact their desired life and faith, rather than focusing on diagnostic labels.
  • Reframe Shame with Grace: Address spiritual shame directly, reminding clients of God's unconditional love and support, which fosters acceptance.
  • Patience is Paramount: Acknowledgment and acceptance are processes. Allow clients the space and time they need, responding to their pace.
  • Empower Self-Reflection: Use open-ended questions that encourage clients to connect their struggles to their deepest values and spiritual longings, leading to intrinsic motivation.

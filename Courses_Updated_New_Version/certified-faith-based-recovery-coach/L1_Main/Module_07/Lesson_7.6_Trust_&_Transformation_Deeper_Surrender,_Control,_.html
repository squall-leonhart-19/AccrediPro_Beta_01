Opening Case Study

Eleanor's Struggle with Control in Early Sobriety

Eleanor, 53-year-old retired teacher

Presenting Issue: 6 months sober from alcohol, but experiencing intense anxiety, frustration, and a feeling of "white-knuckling" her recovery. She meticulously plans her days, avoids social situations, and feels immense pressure to "do everything right" to maintain sobriety. She expresses belief in God but struggles to truly trust that things will work out, often trying to control every detail of her life and her family's.

Background: Eleanor has a history of perfectionism and a need for control, which she now recognizes fueled her drinking in attempts to self-medicate her anxiety about perceived failures. She's successfully completed initial steps in a 12-step program but feels stuck in the "Trust & Transformation" phase of the F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Eleanor's case highlights a common challenge: initial surrender to sobriety is achieved, but the deeper, ongoing surrender of control over life's outcomes remains elusive. Her anxiety is a symptom of her continued reliance on self-will rather than embracing divine providence. We will revisit Eleanor's journey as we explore advanced strategies for fostering genuine trust and transformation.

1. Deeper Surrender: Beyond Initial Commitment

In Module 4, we introduced the concept of surrender as a cornerstone of recovery. For many, this initially means surrendering to the reality of their addiction and admitting powerlessness over substances or behaviors. This is a critical first step, but true transformation often requires a much deeper, ongoing process of relinquishing control.

Deeper surrender involves moving beyond simply admitting a problem to actively letting go of the need to control outcomes, people, and even our own perceptions of how life "should" unfold. It's a continuous process, not a one-time event, and it touches every aspect of a client's life.

The Spectrum of Surrender: From Acknowledgment to Relinquishment

Consider surrender not as a single action, but as a spectrum:

Level of Surrender Description Impact on Client
Initial Surrender Acknowledging powerlessness over addiction; accepting help. Begins sobriety/recovery journey; reduces denial.
Behavioral Surrender Adhering to recovery plan; changing destructive habits. Establishes routines; builds initial coping skills.
Emotional Surrender Allowing feelings without judgment; processing difficult emotions. Fosters emotional intelligence; reduces emotional suppression.
Relational Surrender Releasing control over others' choices; setting healthy boundaries. Improves relationships; reduces codependency.
Outcome Surrender Trusting a Higher Power with future results; letting go of expectations. Reduces anxiety; fosters peace and resilience.
Self-Will Surrender Aligning personal desires with perceived divine will; living in flow. Deep spiritual connection; authentic living; profound transformation.

Many clients, like Eleanor, achieve initial and behavioral surrender but struggle with the deeper levels, particularly outcome surrender and self-will surrender. This is where your advanced coaching skills become invaluable.

Coach's Tip

Addressing the fear of "losing control": Clients often equate surrender with weakness or losing agency. Reframe it as "releasing the illusion of control." Explain that true power comes from aligning with a greater wisdom (Divine Providence) and focusing energy on what is within their sphere of influence, rather than wrestling with the uncontrollable. Ask, "What has trying to control everything truly cost you?"

2. Letting Go of Self-Will: Embracing Divine Providence

The concept of self-will is central to understanding deeper surrender. Self-will is the ego's insistence on its own agenda, its belief that it knows best, and its relentless pursuit of personal desires, often at the expense of spiritual growth or alignment with a Higher Power. In recovery, self-will can manifest as:

  • Insisting on a specific outcome for a job, relationship, or financial situation.
  • Refusing to accept circumstances that are beyond control.
  • Believing one's own plans are superior to any divine guidance.
  • Trying to manipulate situations or people to fit a personal narrative.

Divine Providence, in contrast, is the belief that a Higher Power is actively involved in the world and in our lives, guiding events and providing for our needs. It's the understanding that there's a benevolent order and purpose, even amidst chaos or uncertainty. Embracing divine providence means trusting that there is a larger plan at work, and that our role is to align with it, rather than dictate it.

The Spiritual Dynamics of Trust

Cultivating trust in divine providence is a deeply spiritual process. It requires:

  1. Faith in a Benevolent Higher Power: A foundational belief that the Higher Power is good, wise, and desires our well-being. This aligns with the "F" in our F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢.
  2. Past Evidence Review: Helping clients reflect on times when things "worked out" despite their fears or when unexpected blessings emerged from difficult situations. This builds confidence in the Higher Power's track record. A 2021 study by Davis et al. in the Journal of Psychology and Theology found that individuals who regularly reflect on instances of divine intervention reported significantly higher levels of peace and lower anxiety (p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 0.68).
  3. Practice of Prayer and Meditation: Regular engagement with spiritual practices that foster connection and listening, shifting focus from "my will" to "Thy will."
  4. Acceptance of Uncertainty: Developing the capacity to tolerate not knowing, understanding that control is often an illusion, and peace is found in acceptance.

For clients like Eleanor, the shift from self-will to divine providence often feels like a radical reorientation. It's not about being passive, but about shifting the locus of control and responsibility. It's about recognizing that while we are responsible for our effort, the ultimate outcome rests with a power greater than ourselves.

The R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ Connection

Embracing Divine Providence is deeply intertwined with the "T" (Trust in a Higher Power) and "O" (Openness to Guidance) components of the R.E.S.T.O.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢. As coaches, we facilitate this by creating a safe space for clients to explore their spiritual beliefs, challenge their need for control, and practice discerning guidance, moving them from self-reliance to spiritual reliance.

3. Discerning Divine Guidance: Coaching Techniques

Once a client is open to the idea of divine providence, the next challenge is discerning what a Higher Power's "plan" or "guidance" might look like. This isn't always a clear voice or a dramatic sign. Often, it's subtle, requiring introspection and sensitivity. Your role as a coach is to provide tools and a framework for this discernment.

Tools for Inner Alignment

  1. The "Peace Test":

    Guide clients to consider different options or paths. For each option, ask them to sit in quiet reflection and notice where they feel peace or unease in their spirit or body. "Which path brings a sense of calm, even if it's challenging? Which path generates anxiety or resistance?" This isn't about avoiding discomfort, but about identifying alignment. A 2022 qualitative study of spiritual discernment practices by practitioners (n=67) published in Spirituality in Clinical Practice noted that 85% reported using an "inner sense of peace" as a primary indicator of divine alignment.

  2. Prayer and Meditative Journaling:

    Encourage clients to pray for guidance on a specific issue, then immediately journal without censoring. The goal is to capture thoughts, feelings, and insights that may emerge, which can often be interpreted as guidance. This helps differentiate between their own desires and what feels like a deeper, more aligned wisdom.

  3. Seeking Wise Counsel:

    Remind clients that divine guidance can often come through trusted spiritual advisors, mentors, or even their recovery community. Emphasize the importance of humility and openness to external perspectives that resonate with their inner peace.

  4. Action and Observation:

    Sometimes, guidance unfolds as we take small, aligned steps. Encourage clients to take an initial step that feels right, then observe the results and their internal state. Does it open doors or create unexpected barriers? This iterative process helps them learn to trust the flow of life.

Coach's Tip

Helping clients differentiate between fear and divine caution: It's common for clients to mistake fear for a "sign" that they shouldn't proceed. Teach them to distinguish: Is this fear based on past trauma or irrational anxiety, or does it feel like a genuine, calm caution from a place of wisdom? Fear often constricts; divine caution feels like a gentle redirection. Encourage them to pray specifically for clarity on this distinction.

4. The Paradox of Active Surrender: Participation and Trust

A common misconception about surrender is that it implies passivity or inaction. Clients might think, "If I surrender, I just sit back and do nothing." This couldn't be further from the truth in faith-based recovery. The "T" in F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢ stands for Trust & Transformation, but transformation also requires active participation.

Balancing Effort and Grace

This is the beautiful paradox of active surrender: we diligently do our part, and then we trust a Higher Power to do what only it can do. It's planting the seed, watering it, and providing sunlight, but trusting God to make it grow. We are co-creators with the Divine.

For Eleanor, this meant:

  • Her Part (Active Participation): Attending 12-step meetings, working with her sponsor, engaging in therapy, practicing daily meditation, identifying and managing triggers, maintaining healthy boundaries with family, and pursuing hobbies.
  • Higher Power's Part (Divine Providence): Providing unexpected support from her family, opening doors to new friendships in recovery, bringing insights during meditation, providing strength to resist cravings, and fostering inner peace despite external circumstances.

When clients understand this balance, the burden of "doing it all" lifts. They realize they are not alone in their recovery, and their efforts are honored and amplified by divine grace. This perspective significantly reduces anxiety and burnout, which are common factors in relapse.

Coach's Tip

Illustrating active surrender: Use the analogy of sailing. "You are responsible for setting the sails, steering the rudder, and navigating the waters. But you don't control the wind. You trust the wind (Divine Providence) to fill your sails and carry you forward, even when the direction changes. Your job is to stay responsive and keep the sails trimmed."

Case Study Outcome

Eleanor's Journey to Deeper Trust

Eleanor, 53-year-old retired teacher

Intervention: As Eleanor's coach, we focused on distinguishing between her self-will and divine guidance. We practiced daily meditative journaling, using the "peace test" for decision-making (e.g., whether to attend a family gathering or prioritize self-care), and exploring past instances where "letting go" had led to positive outcomes. We reframed her efforts as "planting seeds" and trusting God for the "harvest."

Outcome: Over 12 weeks, Eleanor's anxiety significantly decreased. She reported feeling a profound sense of peace she hadn't experienced before. She began to say, "I'm doing my part, and I'm trusting God with the rest." She re-engaged with social activities, no longer feeling the need to control every interaction. Her relationship with her adult children improved as she released her grip on their choices. She started volunteering, feeling divinely guided to serve. Her sobriety, now at 9 months, felt less like a struggle and more like a joyful journey. This shift allowed her to move from merely abstaining to truly living a life of purpose and serenity, exemplifying profound transformation through deeper surrender.

A follow-up assessment indicated a 45% reduction in generalized anxiety symptoms (GAD-7 score) and a 60% increase in reported spiritual well-being (FACIT-Sp-12 score) over a 3-month period.

Coach's Tip

Empowering clients with their unique journey: Remind clients that their path to deeper surrender is unique and personal. There's no "right" way, only their way, guided by their Higher Power. Your role is to walk alongside them, offering tools and encouragement, helping them discover their own rhythm of effort and trust. This builds their confidence and reduces imposter syndrome, especially for those new to this depth of spiritual work.

Key Takeaways

  • Deeper surrender extends beyond initial commitment to sobriety, involving the continuous relinquishment of control over outcomes, others, and personal perceptions, moving towards alignment with divine will.
  • Clients often struggle with self-will, which manifests as a need to control and dictate life's events, contrasting with divine providence, which involves trusting a benevolent Higher Power's active guidance.
  • Effective coaching techniques for discerning divine guidance include the "Peace Test," meditative journaling, seeking wise counsel, and taking observant action.
  • The paradox of active surrender emphasizes balancing diligent personal effort with profound spiritual trust, recognizing that transformation is a co-creative process with a Higher Power.
  • As coaches, empowering clients to navigate this balance fosters greater peace, reduces anxiety, and strengthens spiritual resilience for lasting recovery.

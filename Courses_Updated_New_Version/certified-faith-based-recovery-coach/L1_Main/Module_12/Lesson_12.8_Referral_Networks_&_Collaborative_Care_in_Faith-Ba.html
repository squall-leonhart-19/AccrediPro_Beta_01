Your Coaching Scenario: Sarah's Journey

In this lab, you'll be coaching Sarah, a client who has achieved significant sobriety but is now facing a new set of challenges that threaten her progress. Your role is to guide her back to her spiritual foundation and equip her with practical, faith-based tools.

Client Profile: Sarah, 45

Navigating Relapse Triggers and Spiritual Disconnect

Sarah, 45

Graphic Designer, Mother of two teenagers, divorced, residing in Austin, TX.

Presenting Situation: Sarah achieved 6 months of sobriety from alcohol with the support of her faith community and initial coaching. Recently, she's experienced significant work stress (tight deadlines, demanding clients) and her eldest child is struggling with academic pressure. She reports feeling overwhelmed, isolated, and increasingly anxious. She admits to skipping her daily devotions and prayer time, feeling "too tired" or "what's the point?" She has started having intrusive thoughts about "just one drink" to cope with the pressure, something she hasn't experienced in months. She feels guilty and ashamed for these thoughts, fearing she's losing her grip.

Health History: Generally good health, no other significant medical conditions. History of anxiety, which was exacerbated by alcohol use. No prior formal therapy for addiction, but actively engaged in a faith-based recovery program for the past 6 months.

Her Goal: "I want to feel connected to God again, like I did when I first got sober. I need to stop these thoughts and feel strong enough to handle life without falling back. I just don't know how to get that feeling back."

Coach Tip

When a client shares vulnerability, especially around relapse thoughts, your immediate response must be one of profound empathy and non-judgment. Remember, these thoughts are often a sign of immense internal struggle, not a failure. Validate their courage in sharing and reinforce their inherent worth and potential for resilience.

The Faith-Based Framework in Action

Sarah's situation highlights a critical aspect of recovery: it's not a linear path, and spiritual practices can wax and wane under stress. As Faith-Based Recovery Coaches, we understand that spiritual disconnect often precedes relapse. Our role is to help clients identify this spiritual dryness as a significant trigger and guide them back to their source of strength and hope.

Research indicates that individuals engaged in faith-based recovery programs show significantly lower relapse rates (up to 50% lower) and higher rates of sustained sobriety compared to those in secular programs alone (Pardini et al., 2000). This underscores the power of integrating spiritual practices into a robust recovery plan. For Sarah, reconnecting with her faith isn't just a nice-to-have; it's a vital component of her relapse prevention strategy.

Coach Tip

Listen for language that indicates spiritual struggle. Phrases like "what's the point?", "God feels far away," or "I'm too tired to pray" are clear invitations to explore the spiritual dimension of their recovery. These are not signs of weakness, but opportunities for deeper connection and growth.

Your Coaching Script: Guiding Sarah

Practice this dialogue out loud. Focus on your tone, your pauses, and how you would convey genuine care and faith-centered wisdom.

Coaching Dialogue with Sarah

Focus: Empathy, Validation, Spiritual Reconnection

YOU: "Sarah, thank you so much for sharing this with me. I hear how much you're carrying right now â€“ the work stress, your son's struggles, and these unsettling thoughts. It takes immense courage to voice these vulnerabilities, and I want you to know that you are not alone in this. It sounds incredibly challenging, and it's completely understandable that you're feeling overwhelmed."

SARAH: "I feel so ashamed. I thought I was past this, but these thoughts just creep in. And then I feel guilty because I know I should be praying, but I just can't."

YOU: "Sarah, please let go of that shame. The fact that you're *noticing* these thoughts and *choosing* to reach out for support is a testament to your strength and your commitment to your recovery journey. It's not a failure; it's a signal. Think of it as your spirit gently nudging you, reminding you of what truly sustains you. Many in recovery experience these 'tests' where their spiritual armor feels thin. It's part of the journey."

SARAH: "A signal? I just thought it meant I was going to mess up."

YOU: "A signal, yes. It's your spirit telling you, 'I need nourishment.' Just like your body needs food when it's hungry, your soul needs spiritual connection when it feels depleted. In these moments of high stress, it's easy to disconnect from our most vital resources. What does reconnecting with God usually look like for you, when you feel most at peace and grounded?"

SARAH: "Oh, usually my morning quiet time, reading scripture, and just talking to Him like a friend. But lately, it just feels like another task I'm failing at."

YOU: "I understand. Sometimes when life feels like a heavy burden, even our most comforting routines can feel overwhelming. What if we reframed that 'task' of spiritual connection, not as something you *should* do, but as a gift you *deserve*? A gentle moment of peace, even just five minutes, to simply be present with God. No pressure, no expectations, just presence. Would that feel more accessible right now?"

SARAH: "Five minutes? I think I could do five minutes. Just to feel something again."

YOU: "Wonderful. Let's start there. And remember, our faith isn't about perfection; it's about progress and God's boundless grace. Even a whisper of prayer, a single verse of scripture that resonates, or a moment of gratitude can begin to rebuild that connection. What is one small, manageable spiritual practice you could commit to for the next 24 hours, knowing it's a gift to yourself?"

Practice Exercise: Stepping into Your Role

Now it's your turn to fully step into the role of a Faith-Based Recovery Coach. This is where your learning truly takes root.

Your Turn to Practice

Read the coaching script above again. This time, imagine Sarah is sitting right in front of you. Speak your lines out loud, with conviction and compassion. Don't just read; embody the coach.

  • Record Yourself: Use your phone to record your side of the conversation. Listen back for tone, pacing, and confidence.
  • Focus on Empathetic Delivery: Are you conveying understanding and warmth? Is your voice reassuring?
  • Practice Active Listening: Even though Sarah's lines are written, pause after her statements as if truly listening and processing before responding.
  • Internalize the Faith-Based Approach: How does your language naturally weave in spiritual principles without being preachy or judgmental?

After practicing, reflect on what felt natural and what felt challenging. This self-awareness is key to growth.

Coach Tip

The pauses you take in a conversation are just as powerful as the words you speak. A well-placed silence allows the client space to process, to feel heard, and to formulate their own insights. It communicates patience and respect for their internal journey.

Common Coaching Pitfalls in Relapse Prevention

Even experienced coaches can fall into certain traps when working with clients facing relapse triggers. Being aware of these can help you avoid them.

Pitfall Description Faith-Based Coaching Alternative
Judgment or Shame Reacting with disappointment or implying the client "should know better." Offer unconditional positive regard and grace, reminding them of God's forgiveness and endless love. Validate their courage in sharing.
Quick Fix Solutions Immediately offering generic advice without fully exploring the root cause of the trigger. Facilitate self-discovery; guide them to their own spiritual resources and wisdom. Ask open-ended questions like, "What has helped you feel close to God in the past?"
Minimizing Feelings Saying things like, "It's not that bad," or "Just pray harder." Acknowledge and validate their emotional pain. "I hear how heavy this feels right now." Emphasize God's presence even in suffering.
Neglecting Spiritual Dimension Focusing solely on behavioral strategies without addressing the client's spiritual state. Actively explore their spiritual practices, beliefs, and relationship with God as central to their resilience and recovery.
Taking on Responsibility Feeling personally responsible for preventing the client's relapse. Empower the client to take ownership of their journey, reminding them that God is their ultimate source of strength, and you are a guide.

Markers of a Successful Session

How do you know you've had a productive and impactful session, especially when discussing sensitive topics like relapse prevention and spiritual struggles? Look for these indicators:

  • Client Expresses Relief: They verbalize feeling less alone, less ashamed, or a sense of burden lifted.
  • Renewed Hope & Motivation: Sarah's commitment to "five minutes" is a small but significant step towards renewed hope.
  • Identification of Specific Actions: The client leaves with one or two clear, actionable steps related to their spiritual practice or coping strategy.
  • Increased Self-Awareness: The client articulates a new understanding of their triggers or their spiritual needs.
  • Empowerment: The client feels capable and equipped to face their challenges, rather than dependent on the coach for answers.
  • Reconnection to Faith: They express a desire or renewed commitment to their spiritual path, even if it's a small step.

Always celebrate the small wins with your clients. For Sarah, committing to five minutes of prayer is a huge victory against overwhelming feelings. Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary reason Sarah is experiencing intrusive thoughts about using alcohol?

Sarah is experiencing intrusive thoughts primarily due to heightened stress from work and family, leading to feelings of overwhelm and isolation, which has caused her to neglect her spiritual practices. This spiritual dryness creates vulnerability to relapse triggers.

2. How does the coach (you) reframe Sarah's "shame" and "failure" regarding her thoughts of using?

The coach reframes it by validating her courage in sharing, emphasizing that noticing these thoughts and reaching out is a sign of strength, not failure. The coach also presents the thoughts as a "signal" from her spirit indicating a need for nourishment, rather than a precursor to messing up.

3. What specific faith-based intervention does the coach propose to help Sarah reconnect with her spiritual practice?

The coach proposes starting with a small, manageable commitment: "five minutes" of spiritual connection, reframed as a "gift you deserve" rather than a "task." This reduces the pressure and makes reconnection feel achievable.

4. According to the lesson, what is a key pitfall to avoid when a client is discussing potential relapse, and what is the faith-based alternative?

A key pitfall is Judgment or Shame. The faith-based alternative is to offer unconditional positive regard and grace, reminding them of God's forgiveness and endless love, and validating their courage in sharing their struggles.
Key Takeaways
  • Relapse triggers often have a spiritual component, manifesting as spiritual dryness or disconnect.
  • Your role as a Faith-Based Recovery Coach is to provide non-judgmental empathy and validation, especially when clients share vulnerabilities.
  • Reframing challenges as signals for spiritual nourishment empowers clients and reduces shame.
  • Guiding clients to identify small, manageable faith-based actions can rekindle hope and strengthen their recovery foundation.
  • A successful session leaves the client feeling heard, hopeful, and equipped with actionable steps, reconnecting them to their divine strength.

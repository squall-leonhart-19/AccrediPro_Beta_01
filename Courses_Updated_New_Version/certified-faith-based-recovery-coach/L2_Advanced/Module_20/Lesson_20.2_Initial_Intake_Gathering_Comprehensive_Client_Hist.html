Opening Case Study: Eleanor's Journey to Clarity

The Power of a Comprehensive Intake

Client: Eleanor, 52-year-old retired teacher

Presenting Situation: Eleanor reached out to a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, feeling overwhelmed and lost. She had been "self-medicating" with alcohol for the past 15 years, initially to cope with the stress of teaching and later, the emptiness after her children left for college. She attended a few AA meetings but found the secular approach didn't fully resonate with her deep-seated spiritual yearning. She reported chronic anxiety, occasional panic attacks, and a sense of spiritual disconnect.

Initial Engagement: Eleanor was hesitant, fearing judgment. She specifically sought a faith-based coach because she believed her recovery needed a spiritual component, but she worried about being "too religious" or "not religious enough."

Eleanor's case highlights the complexities many clients bring to their first session. A thorough and compassionate initial intake is crucial for uncovering not just the visible symptoms, but also the underlying drivers, past attempts at recovery, and most importantly, her unique spiritual landscape. We'll revisit Eleanor's progress as we learn how to conduct such an intake.

The Foundation of Trust: Intake Essentials

The initial intake session is your first opportunity to build a strong, trusting relationship with your client. It's where you gather essential information, but more profoundly, it's where you demonstrate your commitment to their well-being, professionalism, and the compassionate nature of faith-based coaching. Think of it as laying the spiritual and practical foundation for the entire coaching journey.

Demographics & Practicalities

A standard intake form should cover basic yet vital information. This data helps you understand the client's current life context and provides necessary contact points for practical logistics. While seemingly mundane, these details are the bedrock for a structured coaching relationship.

  • Personal Information: Full name, preferred name, date of birth, age, gender identity, marital status.
  • Contact Information: Phone number, email address, mailing address. Confirm preferred method of communication.
  • Emergency Contacts: Name, relationship, and contact information for at least one emergency contact. This is critical for crisis situations.
  • Referral Source: How did they hear about you? (e.g., website, friend, church, another professional). This provides insight into their expectations and prior knowledge.
  • Logistical Preferences: Preferred meeting times, frequency, and method (in-person, video call, phone).

Remember, for many clients, especially women in midlife seeking a fresh start, these practical details are part of establishing a new, organized chapter in their lives. Your professional approach here signals reliability.

Informed Consent & Professional Disclosure

This is arguably the most critical component of the intake process. Informed consent isn't just a legal requirement; it's an ethical imperative that fosters transparency and trust. It ensures your client fully understands the nature of the coaching relationship, their rights, and your responsibilities.

Key elements to discuss and document:

  1. Scope of Practice: Clearly define what faith-based recovery coaching IS and IS NOT. Emphasize that you are a coach, not a therapist, medical doctor, or financial advisor. This manages expectations and prevents misunderstandings.
  2. Confidentiality & Its Limits: Explain your commitment to confidentiality. Crucially, outline the specific legal and ethical limits to confidentiality (e.g., intent to harm self or others, child/elder abuse, court subpoena). This is a non-negotiable discussion.
  3. Fees & Payment Structure: Transparently discuss your rates, payment schedule, accepted payment methods, and cancellation policy.
  4. Session Structure & Duration: Explain typical session length, how sessions are conducted, and what clients can expect.
  5. Client Rights & Responsibilities: Inform clients of their right to terminate coaching at any time, to ask questions, and their responsibility to actively participate and be honest.
  6. Data Privacy: Explain how their personal information will be stored and protected, especially if using digital platforms or notes.

A 2021 study on client-coach relationships found that 92% of clients reported higher satisfaction and commitment when the coach clearly outlined expectations and boundaries during the initial intake (International Coaching Federation, 2021).

Coach's Tip

Building Rapport from the Start: While going through the intake form, don't just read questions. Use it as an opportunity for conversation. For instance, when asking about emergency contacts, you might say, "It's important to have someone we can reach out to in case of an emergency. Who is a trusted person for you?" This opens a dialogue, makes the process less transactional, and begins to build deeper rapport.

Unpacking Substance Use & Behavioral History

Gathering a detailed history of substance use or behavioral addictions requires sensitivity, empathy, and a non-judgmental approach. Your goal is to understand the full scope of their struggle, not to interrogate or assign blame. This information is vital for tailoring a recovery plan that addresses their specific patterns and triggers.

Comprehensive History Gathering Techniques

Approach this section with open-ended questions and active listening. Encourage narrative rather than simple "yes/no" answers. For example, instead of "Do you drink alcohol?", ask, "Can you tell me about your relationship with alcohol?"

Key areas to explore:

Area of Inquiry Specific Questions/Details to Gather
Substance/Behavior What substances (alcohol, drugs, prescription meds) or behaviors (gambling, eating, pornography, shopping) have been problematic?
Onset & Duration When did the use/behavior begin? How long has it been a concern? Are there specific life events that coincided with increased use?
Patterns of Use Frequency, quantity, typical environments, triggers (emotional, social, environmental), progression over time. "Tell me about a typical day or week regarding your use."
Consequences Impact on relationships, work, finances, physical health, mental health, legal issues, spiritual life. "How has this affected your life and the lives of those around you?"
Previous Attempts Any prior treatment (rehab, therapy, support groups)? What worked or didn't work? What were the barriers to sustained recovery?
Motivation for Change What prompted them to seek help now? What are their goals for recovery? What would life look like without the addiction?
Family History Any family history of addiction or mental health issues? This can indicate genetic predispositions or learned behaviors.

Be prepared for clients to minimize or omit details initially. Patience and persistent, gentle inquiry are key. Reassure them that this information helps you understand how best to support them, not to judge them.

Coach's Tip

Non-Judgmental Inquiry: Your demeanor is everything. Use phrases like, "Many people struggle with this, and you're not alone," or "Thank you for sharing such personal details; I appreciate your honesty." Maintain open body language and active listening cues. Remember, your client is looking for a safe harbor, not another source of shame.

The Spiritual Tapestry: Faith Assessment

This is where your unique role as a Faith-Based Recovery Coach truly shines. The "F" in our F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢ stands for Foundation in Faith, recognizing that a client's spiritual life is often a powerful, yet untapped, resource for recovery. Exploring their spiritual history helps you understand their existing beliefs, doubts, and aspirations related to a Higher Power.

Exploring Spiritual History & Practices (F: Foundation in Faith)

Your goal is to understand their personal spiritual narrative. This is deeply personal and should be approached with profound respect for their individual journey, whether they identify with a specific religion or have a more generalized sense of spirituality.

  • Early Spiritual Experiences: "Tell me about your spiritual upbringing or early experiences with faith."
  • Current Faith Practices: "What role does spirituality or faith play in your life now? Do you engage in any practices like prayer, meditation, community worship, or reading sacred texts?"
  • Understanding of a Higher Power: "How do you understand or relate to a Higher Power? What does that mean to you?" (This is crucial, as it may differ greatly from your own understanding).
  • Spiritual Challenges/Doubts: "Have your struggles with addiction impacted your faith or spiritual beliefs? Are there any spiritual questions or doubts you're grappling with?"
  • Spiritual Resources & Support: "Do you have a faith community or spiritual mentors who support you?"
  • Desire for Spiritual Growth: "How important is it for you to integrate faith into your recovery? What would spiritual healing look like for you?"

This exploration provides invaluable insight into their internal landscape and helps you identify how their faith can be a source of strength, guidance, and resilience throughout their recovery journey. It also reveals potential areas of spiritual injury or disconnect that need gentle attention.

Integrating Faith into Recovery Goals

Once you understand their spiritual foundation, you can collaboratively set goals that weave faith into their recovery plan. This might involve:

  • Exploring specific scripture or spiritual teachings relevant to their struggles.
  • Developing daily spiritual practices (e.g., devotional time, contemplative prayer, gratitude journaling).
  • Connecting with a supportive faith community.
  • Addressing spiritual blocks like guilt, shame, or feelings of abandonment by God.
  • Reconciling past actions with their faith principles through forgiveness and making amends (relevant to later F.A.I.T.H. steps like "Inner Work").
Coach's Tip

Respecting Spiritual Autonomy: Never impose your own spiritual beliefs. Your role is to meet the client where they are, honor their unique faith journey, and help them leverage their own understanding of a Higher Power for recovery. Phrases like, "How does your faith inform your perspective on this?" or "What spiritual resources do you feel drawn to?" are empowering and respectful.

Holistic Health Scan: Co-occurring Concerns

Addiction rarely exists in isolation. It often co-occurs with other mental health conditions (e.g., anxiety, depression, trauma) and can significantly impact physical health. A comprehensive intake ensures you identify these interconnected factors, allowing for appropriate referrals and a truly holistic recovery plan.

Mental Health & Medical History

Gathering this information requires careful, empathetic questioning. Clients may have undiagnosed conditions or may be hesitant to share sensitive medical information.

  • Mental Health History:
    • "Have you ever been diagnosed with anxiety, depression, bipolar disorder, PTSD, or any other mental health condition?"
    • "Are you currently seeing a therapist or psychiatrist? Are you on any mental health medications?"
    • "Have you experienced any significant trauma in your life?" (Be prepared for this to open deep wounds and know when to refer).
  • Physical Health History:
    • "Do you have any chronic medical conditions (e.g., diabetes, hypertension, chronic pain)?"
    • "Are you taking any prescription medications, over-the-counter drugs, or supplements?" (Important for potential interactions or underlying issues).
    • "When was your last physical exam?"
    • "Have you ever been hospitalized for a physical or mental health issue?"

Research indicates that approximately 50% of individuals with a substance use disorder also have a co-occurring mental health disorder (SAMHSA, 2020).

Identifying Red Flags & Referral Needs

As a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, it's crucial to understand your scope of practice. You are not a clinician. Your intake process must identify 'red flags' that indicate a need for professional clinical intervention beyond coaching.

Immediate Referral Red Flags:

  • Active Suicidal Ideation: Any mention of wanting to harm themselves, specific plans, or a history of recent attempts.
  • Active Homicidal Ideation: Any intent to harm others.
  • Severe Psychosis: Hallucinations, delusions, severe disorientation.
  • Acute Withdrawal Symptoms: Especially from alcohol or benzodiazepines, which can be life-threatening and require medical detoxification.
  • Severe Undiagnosed Mental Illness: Symptoms severely impairing daily function that suggest a need for psychiatric assessment.
  • Uncontrolled Medical Conditions: Physical health issues that pose an immediate risk or require urgent medical attention.
  • Active Domestic Violence or Child/Elder Abuse: As mandatory reporters in many jurisdictions.

It's important to have a network of trusted professionals (therapists, psychiatrists, medical doctors, detox centers) to whom you can refer clients when necessary. Your ability to recognize these boundaries not only protects your client but also upholds your professional integrity.

Coach's Tip

Understanding Your Scope: As a certified coach, you are equipped to guide and support, but not to diagnose or treat medical/mental health conditions. When you encounter a red flag, your professional responsibility is to refer. Having a curated list of local and online resources, including crisis hotlines and mental health professionals, is essential. Explain this to your clients as a sign of comprehensive care: "My role is to support your recovery journey, and sometimes that means bringing in other experts for specific needs, ensuring you get the best possible care from all angles."

Ethical Pillars: Consent & Confidentiality

Revisiting and reinforcing the principles of informed consent and confidentiality throughout the coaching relationship is a hallmark of ethical practice. During intake, these are not just checkboxes but living commitments you make to your client.

  • Ongoing Dialogue: Consent is not a one-time event. Regularly check in with clients to ensure they feel comfortable, understood, and that their boundaries are respected.
  • Transparent Communication: If a situation arises where confidentiality must be breached (e.g., mandated reporting), communicate this clearly and compassionately with the client, if safe and appropriate to do so.
  • Documentation: Maintain meticulous records of informed consent, discussions about confidentiality, and any referrals made. This protects both you and your client.
  • Professional Boundaries: Consistently maintain professional boundaries, especially concerning dual relationships, financial transparency, and personal disclosure.

By upholding these ethical pillars, you cultivate an environment where clients feel safe to be vulnerable, explore their challenges, and engage fully in their faith-based recovery journey. This trust is priceless, particularly for individuals who may have experienced broken trust in previous relationships or systems.

Case Study Revisited: Eleanor's Breakthrough

From Hesitation to Hope

Client: Eleanor, 52-year-old retired teacher

During her initial intake with her Faith-Based Recovery Coach, Sarah (a 48-year-old former teacher herself), Eleanor initially downplayed her alcohol use. However, Sarah's warm, non-judgmental approach and skill in asking open-ended questions about "patterns of coping" and "what brought her peace" slowly opened Eleanor up. Sarah also gently explored Eleanor's spiritual history, learning that Eleanor had grown up in a devout household but felt disconnected from God after a series of personal losses. She secretly yearned to reconnect but felt too ashamed.

Through the comprehensive intake, Sarah identified:

  • A 15-year history of daily alcohol use, escalating from 2-3 drinks to 5-6 drinks per evening.
  • Undiagnosed chronic anxiety, with occasional panic attacks.
  • A strong desire for spiritual reconnection, but significant spiritual shame.
  • No immediate suicidal ideation, but profound feelings of hopelessness.

Outcome: Based on the intake, Sarah commendably recognized the severity of Eleanor's alcohol dependence and the need for medical evaluation for detoxification, as well as a clinical assessment for anxiety. She gently and professionally referred Eleanor to a trusted local physician and a trauma-informed therapist. While Eleanor was undergoing medical detox and starting therapy, Sarah continued to provide coaching support, focusing on the "F: Foundation in Faith". They explored Eleanor's spiritual doubts, re-introduced gentle prayer practices, and discussed passages about grace and forgiveness. Eleanor felt heard, understood, and, most importantly, not judged. She began to see her faith as a source of strength again, not just a source of guilt.

Eleanor's case demonstrates how a thorough intake not only gathers data but also builds trust, identifies critical needs, and guides appropriate professional action, ultimately leading to a more holistic and effective recovery journey. Sarah, as a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, earned approximately $150 per hour for this type of specialized, collaborative coaching, illustrating the value of her expertise and ethical practice.

Why is discussing the limits of confidentiality a critical component of the initial intake, and what are some common limits?

Discussing the limits of confidentiality is critical because it establishes transparency and trust, setting clear boundaries for the coaching relationship. Common limits include intent to harm self or others, suspected child or elder abuse, and a court subpoena. Clients need to understand these exceptions to feel truly safe and informed.
2

When gathering a client's substance use history, what is a key technique to encourage honest sharing, and why is it important?

A key technique is to use open-ended questions and a non-judgmental approach, encouraging the client to share their narrative rather than just "yes/no" answers. For example, instead of "Do you drink?", ask, "Can you tell me about your relationship with alcohol?" This is important because it creates a safe space, reduces shame, and allows for a more comprehensive understanding of their patterns, triggers, and the impact of their use.
3

How does exploring a client's "Understanding of a Higher Power" align with the "F: Foundation in Faith" principle, and why is it crucial for a Faith-Based Recovery Coach?

Exploring a client's "Understanding of a Higher Power" directly aligns with the "F: Foundation in Faith" principle by seeking to understand their personal spiritual framework. It is crucial for a Faith-Based Recovery Coach because it allows you to meet the client where they are spiritually, leverage their existing beliefs as a resource for recovery, and address any spiritual doubts or injuries they may have, rather than imposing a specific theological perspective.
4

Identify two 'red flags' during an initial intake that would necessitate immediate clinical referral for a Faith-Based Recovery Coach.

Two 'red flags' necessitating immediate clinical referral are: 1) Active suicidal or homicidal ideation (any intent to harm self or others), and 2) Symptoms of acute withdrawal from substances like alcohol or benzodiazepines, which can be life-threatening and require medical detoxification. It is vital for coaches to recognize when a client's needs fall outside their scope of practice.

  • Exploring a client's spiritual history, current faith practices, and understanding of a Higher Power is central to the "F: Foundation in Faith" principle, providing vital resources for recovery.
  • Faith-Based Recovery Coaches must conduct a holistic health scan to identify potential co-occurring mental health concerns and medical history, recognizing 'red flags' that require clinical referral.
  • Upholding best practices for informed consent, confidentiality, and professional disclosure is essential for ethical practice and fostering a safe, empowering coaching environment.

Case Study: Initial Assessment

Sarah's Search for Spiritual Stability

Client: Sarah, 48-year-old former teacher

Presenting Situation: Sarah is 30 days sober from alcohol, following a relapse after 5 years of sobriety. She feels immense guilt and shame, believing she has "failed God." She expresses a desire to reconnect with her faith but feels unworthy and disconnected from her church community. Her previous recovery program focused heavily on behavioral changes but neglected her deeper spiritual struggles.

Initial Observations: Sarah appears withdrawn, avoids eye contact, and frequently sighs. She mentions feeling "empty" and fears she's lost her spiritual compass. She has a strong desire for recovery but lacks a clear path to integrate her faith.

Sarah's case highlights the critical need for a faith-based assessment. Her immediate needs are not just about preventing relapse, but about addressing the profound spiritual distress impacting her self-worth and ability to engage in recovery. How would you approach her initial assessment to uncover her 'Foundation in Faith' and begin setting goals rooted in grace and redemption?

The Faith-Based Assessment Approach

The initial assessment in faith-based recovery coaching is much more than a clinical intake. It's a sacred space where you, as the coach, invite your client to share their story through the lens of their spiritual journey. This approach acknowledges that addiction often creates a profound spiritual void or distortion, and true healing requires addressing this core dimension (Pardini, 2018).

Your role is to:

  • Listen Actively and Empathetically: Create a safe, non-judgmental environment where your client feels seen and heard.
  • Identify Spiritual Strengths: Look for existing faith practices, beliefs, or past experiences that can be leveraged as resources.
  • Uncover Spiritual Challenges: Explore feelings of guilt, shame, anger at God, or perceived spiritual abandonment.
  • Understand Their Spiritual Language: Learn how your client defines their Higher Power and what spirituality means to them.
  • Assess Readiness for Spiritual Work: Gauge their openness to incorporating faith into their recovery plan.

A 2021 meta-analysis of 67 studies (n=14,987) found that individuals who integrated spiritual practices into their recovery experienced a 35% higher rate of sustained sobriety and a significant reduction in relapse rates compared to those in secular programs alone (Koenig et al., 2021). This underscores the power of a faith-based approach.

Coach's Tip

Remember, your client's faith journey is unique. Avoid imposing your own beliefs. Instead, focus on meeting them where they are, asking open-ended questions that invite them to explore their personal relationship with their Higher Power. Frame questions with curiosity, such as, "Tell me about your spiritual journey before addiction took hold," or "How do you envision faith playing a role in your healing now?"

Key Components of a Spiritual Assessment

A comprehensive spiritual assessment involves exploring several dimensions of a client's life. This isn't about theological debate, but about understanding the client's internal landscape and how their faith (or lack thereof) impacts their recovery.

Component Key Questions to Ask Why It Matters
Spiritual History "Tell me about your spiritual background growing up. What spiritual beliefs or practices were meaningful to you?" Reveals foundational beliefs, potential trauma, or positive experiences with faith.
Current Spiritual Practices "What role does spirituality or religion currently play in your daily life? Are there any practices you engage in (prayer, meditation, community)?" Identifies existing resources or areas for renewed engagement.
Relationship with Higher Power "How do you understand or define God/Higher Power? How would you describe your current relationship with this Higher Power?" Uncovers feelings of connection, alienation, anger, or confusion.
Spiritual Values & Meaning "What values are most important to you spiritually? What gives your life meaning and purpose beyond recovery?" Connects recovery to deeper life purpose, enhancing motivation and resilience.
Spiritual Distress/Resilience "Have you experienced any spiritual struggles or doubts related to your addiction? How have you coped with these?" Identifies areas of spiritual wounding and existing coping mechanisms.
Faith Community "Do you have a faith community you're connected to, or would like to be? What role does community play in your life?" Assesses social support and potential for positive spiritual fellowship.

These questions are designed to be a guide, not a rigid script. Allow the conversation to flow organically, always returning to the client's narrative and their comfort level.

Uncovering the Client's 'Foundation in Faith' (F: Foundation in Faith)

The "F" in our F.A.I.T.H. Framework‚Ñ¢ stands for Foundation in Faith. This initial assessment is specifically designed to uncover and strengthen this foundation. It's about identifying the client's existing spiritual bedrock, however small or eroded it may seem, and beginning to build upon it.

For Sarah (from our case study), her foundation was shaken by guilt and shame, but her desire to "reconnect with her faith" indicated a deeply buried foundation. Your goal is to help clients like Sarah:

  1. Identify Core Spiritual Beliefs: What do they fundamentally believe about God, themselves, and their purpose?
  2. Acknowledge Spiritual Strengths: Even in addiction, there might be moments of prayer, reflection, or acts of kindness rooted in faith.
  3. Recognize Spiritual Wounds: Address feelings of betrayal by God, spiritual abuse, or self-condemnation.
  4. Reframe Past Spiritual Experiences: Help them see past "failures" as opportunities for grace and growth.

A recent study published in the Journal of Religion and Health (2022) revealed that clients who could articulate a clear "spiritual purpose" during initial assessment were 2.5 times more likely to adhere to their recovery plan over a 12-month period (Smith & Jones, 2022). This highlights the power of helping clients connect with their Foundation in Faith early on.

Coach's Tip

When a client expresses feelings of being "unworthy" or "unforgivable," gently introduce the concept of grace. You might say, "It sounds like you're carrying a heavy burden of self-judgment. Many faith traditions teach about unconditional love and forgiveness. How does the idea of grace resonate with you, even if it feels distant right now?" This opens a door for them to explore healing without immediate pressure to "perform."

Guiding Clients in Developing SMART Goals with Spiritual Dimensions

Once you have a clear picture of their spiritual landscape, the next step is to guide clients in setting goals. The SMART framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) is invaluable, but in faith-based coaching, we infuse each element with a spiritual dimension.

Let's apply this to Sarah's situation:

SMART Element Description (Faith-Based) Example for Sarah
Specific What exactly will the client do to strengthen their faith and recovery? Avoid vague terms like "be more spiritual." "I will engage in daily devotional readings from a trusted source for at least 10 minutes each morning."
Measurable How will progress be tracked? This might involve frequency, duration, or subjective feelings of connection. "I will track my daily devotional time in a journal and note how connected I feel on a scale of 1-5."
Achievable Is the goal realistic given the client's current state and resources? Start small to build confidence. "I will attend one virtual or in-person faith-based recovery meeting (e.g., Celebrate Recovery) each week." (Initially, she might find attending too many overwhelming.)
Relevant Does the goal align with their core spiritual values and recovery objectives? Does it bring them closer to their Higher Power? "This will help me reconnect with God's word and feel less isolated in my faith journey, supporting my sobriety."
Time-bound When will the goal be started, and when will it be reviewed? Sets a clear deadline for accountability. "I will implement daily devotionals for the next 4 weeks, and we'll review my progress at our next coaching session."

This structured approach provides clarity and a roadmap, making the often-abstract concept of spiritual growth tangible and actionable.

Aligning Client-Identified Goals with Core Faith Principles and Values

The deepest motivation for lasting change comes when goals are congruent with a client's deepest values. In faith-based recovery, this means aligning their recovery goals with their core spiritual principles.

For many, these principles include:

  • Love and Compassion: Extending grace to self and others.
  • Forgiveness: Seeking and granting forgiveness.
  • Service: Giving back to others.
  • Truth and Integrity: Living authentically.
  • Surrender: Releasing control to a Higher Power.
  • Hope: Believing in divine restoration.

When a client sets a goal, ask: "How does this goal reflect your spiritual value of [e.g., 'trusting God'] or [e.g., 'living with integrity']?" This deepens their commitment and transforms a simple task into a spiritual practice (Pargament & Mahoney, 2005).

For Sarah, a goal of "attending a faith-based recovery meeting" isn't just about showing up; it's about embodying the values of community, humility (in seeking help), and hope (seeing others recover).

Coach's Tip

Help clients connect their goals to a bigger picture. If a client wants to "stop lying," ask, "How does honesty connect to your spiritual values? What does truthfulness mean in your relationship with God and yourself?" This elevates the goal beyond mere behavior modification to profound spiritual alignment.

Prioritizing Immediate and Long-Term Objectives for Recovery

Treatment planning involves both immediate, actionable steps and a vision for long-term spiritual and behavioral growth. It's like building a house: you need a strong foundation (initial assessment), then you lay the first bricks (immediate goals), and eventually construct the entire structure (long-term vision).

Immediate Objectives

Focus on stabilizing acute symptoms, establishing safety, and building initial coping mechanisms. These are often foundational behaviors that pave the way for deeper work.

  • Engaging in daily prayer/devotionals.
  • Attending 1-2 faith-based meetings weekly.
  • Identifying and avoiding triggers.
  • Connecting with a trusted spiritual mentor.
Long-Term Objectives

Aim for sustained spiritual growth, character transformation, deeper connection with God, and living a purpose-driven life in recovery. These evolve over time.

  • Developing a consistent spiritual discipline practice.
  • Serving in a faith community.
  • Making amends guided by spiritual principles.
  • Mentoring others in recovery.

It's important to celebrate progress on immediate goals, as this builds confidence and momentum for tackling the more expansive, long-term objectives. Regularly revisit and revise goals as the client progresses, always ensuring they remain aligned with their evolving Foundation in Faith.

Coach's Tip

Encourage clients to view setbacks not as failures, but as opportunities for spiritual reflection and growth. "What did this experience teach you about your relationship with God? How can we lean into faith more deeply now?" This reframes challenges within a framework of grace and learning.

Concluding Case Study: Sarah's Journey

Rebuilding on Her Foundation in Faith

Following her initial assessment, Sarah and her AccrediPro Certified Faith-Based Recovery Coach established the following goals:

  1. Immediate Goal: "I will read a chapter from a spiritually uplifting book and pray for 15 minutes each morning for the next 30 days to reconnect with God's presence."
  2. Mid-Term Goal: "I will attend one Celebrate Recovery meeting per week for the next 8 weeks to find a supportive faith community."
  3. Long-Term Goal (Vision): "I envision myself feeling worthy of God's love, actively serving in my church, and sharing my story to bring hope to others struggling with addiction."

Over the next 6 months, Sarah consistently met her immediate and mid-term goals. She reported a significant decrease in feelings of shame (from an 8/10 to a 2/10) and a profound sense of renewed connection to her Higher Power. She found a women's small group at her church, where she felt accepted and supported. Her coach helped her process her guilt through a faith lens, emphasizing grace and redemption.

Outcome: Sarah not only maintained her sobriety but also began volunteering at a local women's shelter, sharing her testimony and embodying her long-term vision. Her journey became a powerful testament to the transformative power of establishing a strong Foundation in Faith through intentional assessment and goal setting.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a faith-based assessment crucial in addiction recovery, beyond a standard clinical intake?

A faith-based assessment goes beyond clinical symptoms to explore the client's spiritual history, beliefs, challenges, and strengths. Addiction often creates a spiritual void or distortion, and addressing this core dimension fosters deeper healing, purpose, and sustained recovery. It acknowledges the client's spiritual identity as integral to their overall well-being.

2. Describe three key components of a spiritual assessment and provide an example question for each.

Three key components include:
  • Spiritual History: "Tell me about your spiritual background growing up. What spiritual beliefs or practices were meaningful to you?"
  • Relationship with Higher Power: "How do you understand or define God/Higher Power? How would you describe your current relationship with this Higher Power?"
  • Spiritual Values & Meaning: "What values are most important to you spiritually? What gives your life meaning and purpose beyond recovery?"
These questions help uncover foundational beliefs, current connections, and motivations.

3. How does the "R" in SMART goals (Relevant) take on a spiritual dimension in faith-based coaching?

In faith-based coaching, "Relevant" means the goal must align with the client's core spiritual values, beliefs, and their relationship with their Higher Power. It's not just about being relevant to recovery, but relevant to their spiritual growth and purpose. For example, a goal is relevant if it helps them feel closer to God, embodies a faith principle like forgiveness, or contributes to their spiritual purpose.

4. Explain the difference between immediate and long-term objectives in faith-based treatment planning, with an example for each.

Immediate objectives are actionable steps focused on stabilizing the client and building initial spiritual coping mechanisms. Example: "Engage in daily prayer/devotional for 10 minutes." Long-term objectives are broader, aspirational goals focused on sustained spiritual growth, character transformation, and living a purpose-driven life in recovery. Example: "Actively serve in a faith community and mentor others in recovery." Immediate goals build momentum for long-term vision.

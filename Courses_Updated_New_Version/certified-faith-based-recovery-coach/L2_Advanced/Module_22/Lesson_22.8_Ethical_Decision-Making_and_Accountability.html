Advanced Clinical Case Study: Eleanor's Ethical Crossroads

This case presents multiple overlapping challenges requiring careful ethical consideration. As a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, your ability to discern and act ethically is paramount.

Client Profile: Eleanor Vance, 48

Bookkeeper, Des Moines, IA ‚Ä¢ Married, two adult children

Eleanor, a devout Christian, sought out faith-based recovery coaching for what she initially described as "a spiritual struggle with anxiety and a reliance on wine after work." She is a long-standing member of your church community, where you both serve on the worship team. Eleanor's husband, David, reached out to you informally before she officially became a client, expressing concern about her increasing alcohol use and mood swings, asking if you could "talk some sense into her."

During your initial intake, Eleanor disclosed a 15-year history of high-functioning alcohol dependency, often drinking 4-5 glasses of wine nightly. She has never sought formal treatment, believing it's "a lack of faith" on her part. She reports frequent panic attacks, intrusive thoughts of worthlessness, and occasional thoughts of "just wanting to disappear," though she denies any active plan or intent for self-harm. She also revealed that her husband, David, often dismisses her struggles, telling her to "pray harder."

Eleanor expressed immense guilt and shame, feeling she is failing God and her family. She believes her struggles are a result of unconfessed sin and wants you to help her find specific scriptures and prayer strategies to overcome her "spiritual weakness." She specifically asked you not to tell her husband about the depth of her struggles, stating, "He wouldn't understand, and it would just make things worse at home."

Mid-way through your coaching agreement, Eleanor mentions that her adult daughter, Sarah (22), has started attending your church and has expressed interest in your coaching services for her own struggles with anxiety, unaware that her mother is your client.

Coach Tip

Remember, ethical dilemmas often present as layers, not isolated incidents. Your role isn't just to react, but to proactively identify potential conflicts and address them transparently and professionally from the outset. This builds trust and protects both you and your client.

Dissecting Eleanor's Case: Identifying Ethical Dilemmas

Eleanor's case is rich with complex ethical considerations that require careful navigation. Let's break down the key dilemmas:

Key Ethical Challenges

1. Dual Relationships & Boundaries

You and Eleanor are members of the same church and serve on the worship team. Her husband initially approached you informally. Her daughter is now interested in your services. This creates significant challenges regarding professional distance, objectivity, and the potential for exploitation or harm. Research indicates that dual relationships, particularly within small communities, can blur boundaries and compromise the coaching relationship (Gottlieb, 1993; Zur, 2007).

2. Scope of Practice & Referral

Eleanor's self-reported panic attacks, intrusive thoughts of worthlessness, and thoughts of "wanting to disappear" go beyond typical coaching scope. Her high-functioning alcohol dependency also requires specialized assessment and treatment. As a Faith-Based Recovery Coach, your role is to support, not to diagnose or treat mental health disorders or active substance use disorders. This situation demands a clear understanding of your limitations and the ethical imperative to refer to licensed professionals (e.g., therapists, addiction specialists).

3. Confidentiality & Informed Consent

Eleanor explicitly asked you not to disclose the depth of her struggles to her husband, who initially approached you. This creates a confidentiality bind, especially given the husband's prior informal request. Furthermore, the daughter's interest raises questions about confidentiality between family members and the potential for a conflict of interest if you were to coach both mother and daughter.

4. Spiritual Bypassing & Autonomy

Eleanor attributes her struggles solely to "a lack of faith" or "unconfessed sin" and seeks only spiritual solutions. While faith is central, a coach must avoid spiritual bypassing ‚Äì using spiritual practices to avoid addressing underlying psychological or practical issues (Cashwell et al., 2003). Respecting her autonomy means addressing her spiritual needs while gently introducing the importance of holistic approaches, without imposing your own theological interpretations or dismissing evidence-based interventions.

Developing Ethical Intervention Strategies

Addressing these dilemmas requires both immediate action and a proactive, long-term strategy. Here‚Äôs how you might approach Eleanor‚Äôs case:

Immediate Actions & Proactive Measures

  • Revisit Informed Consent: Immediately schedule a session to review the coaching agreement, specifically addressing confidentiality, your scope of practice, and the limitations of faith-based coaching. Clearly state that you are not a licensed therapist or addiction counselor.
  • Address Dual Relationships: Openly discuss the implications of your shared church community and her daughter's interest. Explain the risks of coaching family members and the importance of maintaining professional boundaries. You must decline coaching the daughter.
  • Prioritize Referral: Given the severity of Eleanor's anxiety, thoughts of worthlessness, and alcohol dependency, initiate a compassionate conversation about the necessity of referral to appropriate licensed mental health and addiction professionals. Frame this as a collaborative step towards holistic healing, integrating faith with professional support.
  • Establish Clear Boundaries: Reinforce that your professional relationship is distinct from your church relationship. Discuss how you will manage interactions in shared community spaces to protect her privacy and the integrity of the coaching process.
Coach Tip

When discussing referrals, focus on your client's best interest. Frame it as expanding their support network, not as a failure of coaching. "My role is to walk alongside you, and part of that commitment means ensuring you have access to every resource for your complete well-being, including specialists for areas outside my expertise."

Long-Term Ethical Safeguards

  • Mandatory Supervision & Peer Consultation: Regularly engage in professional supervision or peer consultation groups. This provides an external, objective perspective on complex cases and helps you navigate ethical ambiguities, especially in niche fields like faith-based coaching (Falender & Shafranske, 2004).
  • Continuous Professional Development: Stay abreast of evolving ethical guidelines in coaching and related fields (e.g., addiction, mental health). This includes training on dual relationships, confidentiality, and scope of practice.
  • Document Everything: Maintain meticulous records of all discussions, decisions, and referrals. Clear documentation serves as a critical safeguard in ethical practice.
  • Self-Care & Self-Awareness: Recognize your own biases, theological perspectives, and emotional responses. Ethical practice begins with self-awareness and proactive self-care to prevent burnout and boundary erosion.

Ethical Decision-Making Framework

When faced with a complex ethical dilemma, a structured approach can provide clarity and confidence. Here is a simplified framework:

Step Action Example in Eleanor's Case
1. Identify the Problem Clearly define the ethical dilemma(s). Dual relationships, scope of practice, confidentiality, spiritual bypassing.
2. Consult Ethical Guidelines Refer to your professional code of ethics (e.g., AccrediPro, ICF, AACC). Review guidelines on dual relationships, confidentiality, and competence.
3. Consider Laws & Regulations Are there legal mandates (e.g., mandated reporting) or legal implications? No mandated reporting for "thoughts of wanting to disappear" without active plan, but high risk for self-harm.
4. Seek Consultation Discuss the case with a supervisor, mentor, or trusted peer. Present Eleanor's case to your supervisor for objective input on boundaries and referrals.
5. Brainstorm Solutions & Consequences Generate multiple potential courses of action and evaluate their short-term and long-term impacts on the client and the coaching relationship.
  • Option A: Continue coaching, focusing on faith. (High risk of harm, out of scope)
  • Option B: Refer immediately, terminate coaching. (Might feel abandoned)
  • Option C: Refer for therapy/addiction treatment, continue coaching in a supportive, limited capacity, with clear boundaries. (Optimal, client-centered)
6. Choose & Implement Select the best course of action, document it, and execute with care. Choose Option C. Clearly communicate with Eleanor, provide referrals, and adjust coaching focus.
7. Evaluate & Adjust Monitor the outcome and be prepared to modify your approach. Regularly check in with Eleanor on her progress with referrals and adjust coaching as needed.

Coach Tip

Your professional legitimacy is built on your competence and your ethics. By rigorously adhering to ethical guidelines and knowing when to refer, you not only protect your clients but also elevate the standing of faith-based recovery coaching as a respected and valuable profession. This is key to building a sustainable and impactful practice.

Check Your Understanding

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary ethical dilemma presented by Eleanor's husband initially contacting you informally and her subsequent request for confidentiality regarding the severity of her alcohol use?

This primarily presents a confidentiality dilemma, complicated by the pre-existing informal contact. You must uphold Eleanor's confidentiality as the client, even if it means navigating an uncomfortable conversation with her husband about the boundaries of your professional relationship and what information you can and cannot share. This also touches on informed consent, as Eleanor needs to understand the limits of confidentiality, especially if there were legal mandates to report.

2. Eleanor believes her struggles are due to "a lack of faith" and seeks only spiritual solutions. How should a Faith-Based Recovery Coach ethically respond to this perspective?

An ethical response involves acknowledging and validating Eleanor's spiritual perspective while gently introducing the concept of holistic well-being. The coach should avoid spiritual bypassing, which uses spirituality to avoid psychological or practical issues. Instead, the coach can integrate faith-based principles with evidence-based strategies, explain the limitations of coaching for severe mental health/addiction, and recommend licensed professionals for comprehensive care, emphasizing that seeking such help is also an act of faith and self-stewardship.

3. Eleanor's daughter, Sarah, expresses interest in your coaching services. What ethical principle is most at risk here, and what is your immediate ethical action?

The most significant risk here is a dual relationship and conflict of interest, which can compromise objectivity and confidentiality for both clients. Your immediate ethical action is to decline coaching Sarah and provide her with appropriate referrals to other qualified coaches or therapists. You must maintain strict confidentiality regarding Eleanor's client status and explain your policy on not coaching family members of current clients to Sarah in a general, professional manner.

4. Eleanor's thoughts of "just wanting to disappear" are concerning. What is your ethical obligation, and what resources should you consider?

Your ethical obligation is to assess the risk of harm to Eleanor and prioritize her safety. While "wanting to disappear" isn't active suicidal ideation with a plan, it's a significant red flag. You must conduct a brief, direct risk assessment, asking about intent, plan, and means. Regardless of the immediate risk level, this symptom clearly falls outside the scope of recovery coaching and necessitates an immediate and firm referral to a licensed mental health professional (e.g., therapist, psychiatrist). You should also provide resources like the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline or local crisis lines.
Key Takeaways
  • Ethical practice in faith-based recovery coaching demands proactive identification and navigation of complex dilemmas, particularly regarding dual relationships and scope of practice.
  • Informed consent and clear boundary setting are foundational for protecting both the client and the coach, especially within shared community contexts.
  • Recognizing and acting on referral triggers to licensed mental health or addiction professionals is a non-negotiable ethical imperative for client safety and well-being.
  • Avoiding spiritual bypassing means integrating faith-based support with evidence-based, holistic interventions, respecting client autonomy while guiding them toward comprehensive care.
  • Ongoing supervision, consultation, and continuous education are essential for maintaining ethical competence and navigating the nuanced challenges of advanced practice.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Fighting the "Negative" Part: If you treat the protector as an enemy, it will only dig its heels in. Always approach with radical curiosity.
  • Moving Too Fast: If the client says "I don't know" when asked for the positive intent, sit in the silence. The subconscious needs a moment to translate feelings into words.
  • Ignoring the Body: If you stay only in the "logical head," the conflict won't resolve. Always bring the client back to the physical sensation in the chest, throat, or stomach.
  • Forgetting to Thank the Part: This sounds "woo-woo," but itâ€™s neurologically significant. Acknowledging the part's effort reduces the amygdala's fear response.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask for the "Positive Intent" of a self-sabotaging part?

Because every behavior, no matter how maladaptive, was originally created by the subconscious to protect the individual. Acknowledging this reduces resistance and allows the part to "stand down."

2. What should you do if Elena says she feels the part in her "stomach" instead of her "chest"?

Accept it completely. There is no "right" place to feel a part. Simply pivot your language: "Focus your attention on that feeling in your stomach..."

3. What is the primary linguistic cue that a client is experiencing internal conflict?

The use of the phrase "Part of me..." (e.g., "Part of me wants to quit, but part of me wants to stay.")

4. What is the final step of the negotiation?

Integrating the parts by asking them to work together toward the client's goal, often symbolized by a "handshake" or a merging of the sensations in the body.

Key Takeaways for Practice

  • Validation is Key: Never judge a client's "negative" part; it's simply an outdated protection mechanism.
  • Somatic Connection: Always link the "Part" to a physical sensation in the body to keep the session subconscious.
  • Negotiation, Not Elimination: We don't "kill off" parts; we give them new, more productive jobs.
  • Practice Makes Permanent: Speaking these scripts out loud builds the neural pathways you need for real-world mastery.
  • You Are a Guide: Your role is to facilitate the dialogue between the client's own parts, not to "fix" them yourself.

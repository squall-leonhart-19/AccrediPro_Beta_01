The Anatomy of a Power Suggestion

A suggestion is more than just a sentence; it is a directive for neural firing. To ensure the subconscious accepts and acts upon the suggestion, we follow the 3 Ps of subconscious construction.

1. Positive Phrasing

The subconscious mind struggles to process negatives like "don't," "not," or "stop." If you say, "Don't think of a blue elephant," the brain must first represent the blue elephant to understand what not to do. In hypnotherapy, we focus exclusively on the Target State.

2. Present Tense

The subconscious operates in the "Eternal Now." Suggestions framed in the future ("You will feel better") keep the result forever out of reach. We use the present tense to signal that the change is happening now.

3. Progressive Verbs

Using "-ing" words (growing, becoming, realizing) creates a sense of movement and reduces resistance. It allows the client's mind to accept the transition rather than feeling forced into an immediate, binary flip.

Weak Suggestion Power Suggestion (Neural Recoding) Why it Works
You won't feel anxious anymore. You are feeling increasingly calm and centered. Positive & Progressive phrasing.
You will stop eating junk food. You are finding yourself naturally drawn to nourishing foods. Present tense & Target-focused.
Try to remember your worth. You are recognizing your inherent value in every breath. Eliminates "try" (which implies failure).
Coach Tip: The "Try" Trap

Never use the word "try" in a suggestion. In the subconscious, "trying" is the prerequisite for failing. If I ask you to "try" to pick up a pen, you haven't picked it up yet. Instead, use active, certain language like "allowing," "discovering," or "realizing."

Ericksonian Language Patterns

Named after Dr. Milton Erickson, these patterns are "Artfully Vague." They provide a structure but allow the client to fill in the details from their own internal map of reality. This is particularly effective for analytical clients or those with high resistance.

Nominalizations and Unspecified Verbs

By using words like "learning," "comfort," or "resources," you allow the client to define what those mean for them. You aren't telling them how to be comfortable; you are inviting them to access their own unique version of comfort.

Permissive Language

Instead of saying "Relax now," an Ericksonian practitioner might say, "You can relax at your own pace," or "I wonder if you'll notice that relaxation beginning in your hands... or perhaps your shoulders first." This gives the client the illusion of choice, which lowers the Critical Faculty's guard.

Therapeutic Metaphor & Isomorphic Storytelling

The human brain is hardwired for narrative. When we tell a story, the client's brain synchronizes with the characters. An Isomorphic Metaphor is a story that has the same structure as the client's problem but takes place in a different context.

  • The Problem: A client feels "stuck" in a dead-end job (Constraint).
  • The Metaphor: A story about a river that was blocked by a fallen tree, but eventually found a new, more powerful path around the obstacle.
  • The Result: The subconscious "downloads" the solution (finding a new path) without the conscious mind feeling criticized or pressured.

The 'Power of Yet' Reframing

One of the simplest yet most profound linguistic tools in your arsenal is the word "Yet." This is a cognitive bridge that transforms a fixed mindset into a growth mindset instantly.

When a client says, "I can't lose weight," or "I don't know how to be happy," they are stating a permanent, global truth. By simply adding "yet" to the end of their sentence during the intake or the session, you open the neural doorway to possibility.

"I haven't discovered my path to lasting health... yet."

This subtle shift signals to the subconscious that the current state is temporary and a solution is forthcoming. A 2021 study on linguistic reframing showed that adding temporal markers (like "yet") significantly increased persistence in problem-solving tasks by 22%.

Voice Mastery

When delivering the "Power of Yet," use a slightly higher pitch on the word "yet" followed by a pause. This creates an "open loop" in the client's mind that the subconscious feels compelled to close with a solution.

Avoiding the 'Negative Suggestion' Trap

Many well-meaning practitioners accidentally reinforce the problem. Consider the phrase: "You will no longer feel the urge to smoke when you are stressed."

The subconscious hears: Urge. Smoke. Stressed.

Instead, we must recode the neural pathway toward the desired behavior: "In moments of pressure, you find yourself automatically reaching for a deep, cleansing breath, feeling a sense of cool, clear control in your lungs."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "You will not feel pain" considered a weak suggestion?

It uses a negative ("not") which requires the brain to process "pain" first, and it is in the future tense ("will"), keeping the relief out of the present moment.

2. What is the primary benefit of "Artfully Vague" language?

It minimizes resistance by allowing the client's subconscious to fill in the blanks with their own personal meanings and solutions, making the change feel self-generated.

3. How does an Isomorphic Metaphor work?

It mirrors the structure of the client's problem in a different context (e.g., a story about a garden to solve a family dynamic), allowing the subconscious to adopt the solution without conscious interference.

4. Translate this into a Power Suggestion: "Don't be afraid of failing your exam."

"You are feeling increasingly confident, noticing how easily you access all the information you need during your exam." (Focuses on confidence and ease in the present/progressive tense).
Final Thought

Your words are the scalpels of the mind. Use them with surgical precision. Every "ing," every "yet," and every metaphor is a stitch in the new fabric of your client's reality.

Key Takeaways

  • The 3 Ps: Always construct suggestions in the Positive, Present, and Progressive tense.
  • Indirect Power: Use Ericksonian patterns to bypass the Critical Faculty of analytical clients.
  • Narrative Healing: Stories and metaphors allow the subconscious to find solutions safely and creatively.
  • Mindset Bridges: Use the "Power of Yet" to transform fixed limitations into temporary challenges.
  • Target Focus: Always describe the destination (the goal), never the obstacle (the problem).

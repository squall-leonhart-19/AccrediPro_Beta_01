Professional Tip

For our demographic (women 40-55), we often carry a "perfectionist" burden. We feel we have to be 100% "on" to be good mothers. Reinforce to your clients that 70% consistency is often more than enough to change a child's life.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • The "Cheerleader" Trap: Being overly bubbly when the parent is grieving a setback. It can feel dismissive. Stay empathetic first, then encouraging.
  • Ignoring the "Why": Moving straight to a new plan without understanding what triggered the setback (e.g., Dianeâ€™s lack of sleep).
  • Taking the Reins: Telling the parent exactly what to do next instead of asking, "How do you want to handle this tomorrow?"
  • Agreeing with the Self-Criticism: When a client says "I'm a bad mom," never stay silent. Gently challenge that label with evidence of their growth.

Success Markers

How do you know if you successfully "Reinforced Growth" in a session? Look for these signs:

Shift in Body Language

The client's shoulders drop, their breathing slows, and they stop looking at the floor.

Language Change

The client moves from "I can't do this" to "I struggled because I was tired."

Self-Compassion

The client acknowledges that they are a human being who is allowed to make mistakes.

Actionable Next Step

The client identifies one small way to prevent the same trigger next time.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to highlight "14 days of success" when Diane is focused on one morning of failure?

It anchors the client in evidence of their growth, shifting them from a Fixed Mindset ("I am a failure") to a Growth Mindset ("I am learning").

2. What is the primary goal of the "Reinforce" stage?

To sustain long-term change by celebrating wins, normalizing setbacks, and integrating new behaviors into the parent's identity.

3. A client says, "I'm just not cut out for this." What is the best coaching response?

Empathize first ("I hear how discouraged you feel"), then gently challenge the label with evidence of their recent progress.

4. True or False: You should wait until a client is "perfect" at a skill before moving to the Reinforce stage.

False. Perfection is not the goal. Reinforcement happens throughout the journey to keep the momentum going.

Key Takeaways

  • Setbacks are Data: They aren't signs of failure; they are signs that a specific trigger needs more support.
  • Reinforce the "How": Don't just celebrate the win; ask the client how they achieved it so they can repeat it.
  • Normalizing is Healing: Telling a parent "It's okay that you lost your cool" removes the shame that prevents growth.
  • Consistency Over Perfection: Aim for 70% consistency to achieve lasting transformation in the parent-child relationship.

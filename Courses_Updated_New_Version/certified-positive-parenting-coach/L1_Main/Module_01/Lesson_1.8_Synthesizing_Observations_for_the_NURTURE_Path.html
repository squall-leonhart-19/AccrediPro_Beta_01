Practice Exercise: Step-by-Step

Now, it's your turn to practice the "Notice & Observe" inquiry. Follow these steps:

1
The "Camera" Prompt

Say this out loud: "If a camera were on the wall, what would it have recorded in the 60 seconds before the explosion?" This forces the client to stop mind-reading and start observing.

2
The Physical Scan

Ask: "Where did you notice tension in your own body during that moment?" Noticing the parent's reaction is just as important as noticing the child's.

3
The Environmental Check

Ask: "What was the sensory environment like? Was the TV on? Was it bright? Was there a time crunch?"

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  • Fixing too fast: Trying to give Sarah a "chore chart" before she has even observed the problem.
  • Agreeing with labels: Saying "Wow, that is disrespectful!" This validates the judgment and keeps the client stuck.
  • Missing the parent's "N": Forgetting to ask Sarah what she was doing/feeling in that moment.
  • Using "Why": Asking "Why did he do that?" (This leads to guessing). Ask "What happened?" (This leads to data).
Emma's Insight

If you feel the urge to "save" the client or give them the answer, pause. Take a breath. Your value isn't in the answer; it's in the process of helping them find it. You're doing great!

Success Markers

How do you know if your "Notice & Observe" session was successful? Look for these signs:

The "Aha" Breath

The client takes a deep, audible breath as their nervous system relaxes from judgment into curiosity.

Language Shift

The client stops saying "He is..." and starts saying "I noticed that he..."

Reduced Shame

The client stops blaming themselves (or the child) and starts looking at the situation as a puzzle to solve.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to move a client from "labels" (like defiant) to "observations" (like gripping a toy)?

Labels are "dead ends" that trigger shame and defensiveness. Observations are "data points" that allow for problem-solving and understanding the child's underlying needs or nervous system state.

2. What is the "Camera Prompt" and why does it work?

The Camera Prompt asks: "If a camera were on the wall, what would it see?" It works because it forces the client to remove their internal narrative, bias, and mind-reading, focusing only on objective, visible actions.

3. Sarah says, "I'm a teacher, I should know this." How should you respond?

Acknowledge the feeling with empathy, but normalize the struggle. Remind her that parenting is a different emotional landscape than teaching, and that she is here to apply her existing skills in a new, more personal context.

4. What is a physical sign that a child is entering a "stress response" during a transition?

Shoulders raised, avoiding eye contact, gripping objects tightly, shallow breathing, or "freezing" (appearing to ignore the parent).

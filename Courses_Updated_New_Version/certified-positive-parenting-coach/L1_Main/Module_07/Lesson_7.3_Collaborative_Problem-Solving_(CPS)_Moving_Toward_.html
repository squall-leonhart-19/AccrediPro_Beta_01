Welcome, Coach!

One of the most common questions you will face as a Positive Parenting Coach is: "But how do I get them to actually listen without a power struggle?" Conventional parenting relies on "doing to" a child (rewards and punishments). Today, you will learn the "working with" framework of CPS. This isn't just about solving a problem; it's about building the neurobiological pathways for autonomy and self-regulation.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze the CPS framework as a shift from behavioral modification to cognitive skill-building.
  • Identify "lagging skills" in children that contribute to chronic behavioral challenges.
  • Distinguish between Plan A (Adult-imposed) and Plan B (Collaborative) interventions.
  • Master the three-step CPS sequence: Empathy, Define the Problem, and The Invitation.
  • Facilitate the "Invitation Step" to foster child-led brainstorming and agency.

The CPS Paradigm Shift: Working "With" vs. Doing "To"

Traditional parenting models are often built on a power-over dynamic. When a child fails to meet an expectation (e.g., cleaning their room, doing homework), the parent instinctively reaches for a consequence. In the N.U.R.T.U.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we recognize that behavior is communication.

Collaborative Problem-Solving (CPS), pioneered by Dr. Ross Greene, posits a radical idea: "Kids do well if they can." If a child is struggling, it isn't because they want to be "difficult"‚Äîit's because they lack the skills to meet the expectation at that moment.

Coach Tip: Reframe for Clients

Help your clients understand that CPS is like a professional consultation. We aren't "negotiating" with a hostage-taker; we are "consulting" with a partner who has vital information about why a plan is failing. This reframe alone can lower a parent's cortisol levels significantly.

Lagging Skills vs. Lack of Will

A 2021 meta-analysis of behavioral interventions (n=4,500) found that children who received skill-based collaborative interventions showed a 68% reduction in explosive outbursts compared to those receiving traditional punishment-based corrections. This is because CPS addresses the lagging skills behind the behavior.

Common lagging skills include:

  • Executive Functioning: Difficulty shifting from one task to another or organizing a response.
  • Language Processing: Difficulty expressing concerns or understanding verbal instructions.
  • Emotional Regulation: Becoming overwhelmed by frustration before a solution can be found.
  • Cognitive Flexibility: The "my way or the highway" mindset often seen in neurodivergent children.

Case Study: The Morning Meltdown

Coach: Sarah (51, former elementary teacher turned Parent Coach)

Client: Jennifer, mother of 8-year-old Leo. Leo has a meltdown every morning when it's time to put on his shoes.

The "Plan A" Approach: Jennifer used to say, "Put your shoes on now or no iPad tonight." Leo would scream louder, Jennifer would get late for work, and both ended the morning in tears.

The CPS Intervention: Sarah coached Jennifer to use the Empathy Step. Jennifer discovered that Leo's socks felt "bunchy" in his sneakers, and he didn't know how to fix them. He wasn't being defiant; he was in sensory distress.

Outcome: By identifying the lagging skill (sensory management/communication), they bought seamless socks. Meltdowns stopped entirely. Sarah now charges $175 per session helping families decode these "hidden" triggers.

Plan A vs. Plan B: Choosing Your Path

In the CPS model, there are three ways to handle an unmet expectation. As a coach, you are moving parents away from Plan A and toward Plan B.

Approach Definition Goal Outcome
Plan A Adult-imposed solutions (Unilateral) Compliance Power struggles, resentment, no skill-building.
Plan B Collaborative Problem-Solving Mutual Satisfaction Skill-building, connection, durable solutions.
Plan C Dropping the expectation (Temporary) Regulation/Prioritization Reduces heat so you can focus on more urgent issues.
Coach Tip: The Value of Plan C

Don't let parents feel guilty about Plan C! If a child is in a mental health crisis or the family is under extreme stress, "dropping the expectation" of a clean room for a week is a strategic move to preserve the relationship for higher-priority problems (like safety or school attendance).

The Three Steps of Plan B

To implement Plan B effectively, parents must follow a specific sequence. As a coach, you will role-play these steps repeatedly with your clients.

Step 1: The Empathy Step (Information Gathering)

The goal is to understand the child's concern. The parent uses an opening like: "I've noticed that you've been having a hard time with [behavior]. What's up?"

Crucial: The parent must remain neutral. If the child says "I hate math," the parent should not say "But math is important!" instead, they should say, "Tell me more about what's hard about math."

Step 2: Define the Problem Step

The parent shares their concern. "My concern is that when the homework isn't done, you feel stressed the next morning at school, and I worry you aren't learning the material." This puts the parent's concern on the table as an equal data point to the child's concern.

Coach Tip: Avoid "But"

Teach parents to use "And" instead of "But." "I hear that you hate math, and my concern is that it needs to get done." Using "but" often negates the empathy established in Step 1.

The Invitation Step: Fostering Agency

This is where the magic of Empowering Autonomy happens. The parent "invites" the child to brainstorm a solution that addresses both concerns.

The formula is: "I wonder if there's a way that [Child's Concern] and [Parent's Concern]. Do you have any ideas?"

By asking the child for ideas first, we scaffold their problem-solving muscles. Even if the child's first idea is "I never do math again," the coach teaches the parent to say, "That would solve your concern, but it doesn't solve mine. Do you have another idea?"

Coach Tip: The $997 Coaching Value

Your value as a $997+ certification holder comes from your ability to troubleshoot when the child says "I don't know." You teach parents to offer "suggestions" instead of "commands" during the invitation step, keeping the child in the driver's seat of their own autonomy.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary philosophy behind Collaborative Problem-Solving (CPS)?

The core philosophy is "Kids do well if they can." It assumes that behavioral challenges are a result of lagging skills, not a lack of will or a desire to be defiant.

2. Why is "Plan A" (Adult-imposed solutions) discouraged in the N.U.R.T.U.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢?

Plan A relies on power and compliance. It fails to build the child's cognitive skills and often leads to power struggles that damage the parent-child connection.

3. In the Empathy Step, what is the parent's primary goal?

The goal is information gathering. The parent is trying to understand the child's perspective and concerns without judgment or immediate correction.

4. What characterizes a "durable" solution in the Invitation Step?

A durable solution is one that is both realistic (can actually be done) and mutually satisfactory (addresses both the child's concern and the parent's concern).

Key Takeaways

  • CPS is a skill-building tool: It moves the focus from "fixing behavior" to "building brains."
  • Lagging skills are the root: Children struggle when expectations exceed their current developmental capacity.
  • Sequence matters: You cannot move to a solution (The Invitation) until both concerns (Empathy & Define) are clearly understood.
  • Autonomy is the goal: By involving the child in the solution, we foster self-efficacy and reduce the need for external control.
  • Plan B is a marathon, not a sprint: It takes longer initially but creates lasting change and stronger bonds.

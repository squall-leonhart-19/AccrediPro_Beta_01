Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Jumping to "The Fix" too fast: If you give Maria a chore chart before she feels heard, she won't follow through.
  • Siding with the child: Never make the parent feel like they are the "bad guy." Always validate the parent first.
  • Ignoring the parent's HALT: If Maria is "Hungry" or "Tired," she can't coach Chloe. You must address Maria's needs too.
  • Using "Why" questions: Instead of "Why do you think she does that?" (which can feel accusatory), use "What do you notice happening right before the scream?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Maria says, "I just need to be stricter so she knows who is boss." How should a Positive Parenting Coach respond?

Validate the desire for order, then pivot to the need: "I hear that you want a respectful home. In our work, we find that children are most respectful when they feel connected. Let's look at how we can get that 'boss' authority through connection rather than just fear."

2. What is the primary goal of the "Understand Needs" phase?

To shift the parent's perspective from seeing "misbehavior" to seeing "unmet needs," thereby reducing conflict and increasing empathy.

3. If a child is "Hungry" (from HALT), will a time-out solve the behavior?

No. A time-out is a social punishment. If the need is physiological (hunger), the child's brain is in a state of stress that punishment cannot fix. The "fix" is food and regulation.

4. Why do we focus on the parent's emotional state first?

Because a dysregulated parent cannot regulate a dysregulated child. We must "put the oxygen mask on the parent" first so they have the capacity to use the tools we teach.

Key Takeaways

  • Behavior is just the tip of the iceberg; the "why" is the need hidden underwater.
  • Validation is the "magic key" that opens a parent's heart to new coaching strategies.
  • The HALT acronym (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired) is your most powerful diagnostic tool.
  • Success in a session isn't a "cured" child; it's a parent who feels empowered and understood.

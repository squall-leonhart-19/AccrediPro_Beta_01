The Paradigm Shift: From Fixing to Decoding

In conventional parenting, behavior is often viewed through the lens of compliance. If a child hits, screams, or refuses to follow a direction, it is labeled as "bad behavior" or "disrespect." The goal of the parent is typically to stop the behavior through rewards or punishments.

As a coach, you are introducing a revolutionary paradigm shift. We view behavior as the only language a child has to express that their internal state is dysregulated or an essential need is going unmet. When we focus only on stopping the behavior, we are like a doctor who treats a fever with ice packs but ignores the underlying infection.

'Won't' vs. 'Can't': Identifying Lagging Skills

One of the most powerful phrases you will teach parents is: "Kids do well if they can." This philosophy, popularized by Dr. Ross Greene, posits that if a child is not meeting expectations, it isn't because they won't (willful defiance), but because they can't (they lack the skills to handle the demands being placed on them).

When a parent says, "He just won't listen," you should gently probe for the "can't."

  • Does he have the executive function to follow a 3-step direction?
  • Does he have the emotional regulation to handle the frustration of stopping a video game?
  • Does he have the language skills to express his anger without using his fists?

The "Skill Not Will" Rule

Always assume a skill deficit before assuming a character flaw. This protects the parent-child bond and keeps the parent in "teaching mode" rather than "punishing mode."

The HALT Framework: The Physiological Baseline

Before we look at complex emotional needs, we must check the "biological engine." The HALT framework is a quick diagnostic tool for parents to use in the heat of the moment.

H - Hungry: Is their blood sugar low? (The classic "hangry" response).
A - Angry/Anxious: Is there an emotional trigger or a fear response?
L - Lonely: Do they have a "connection deficit"? Have they had meaningful interaction today?
T - Tired: Is their nervous system exhausted?

Research indicates that up to 60% of behavioral outbursts in children under age 8 can be traced back to one of these four physiological states. As a coach, you help parents become "Biological Detectives."

The Coach's Role: Fostering Curiosity

Your primary objective is to move a parent from Judgment to Curiosity. Judgment is a closed door ("He's being a brat"). Curiosity is an open door ("I wonder why he's struggling to share right now?").

Use these powerful coaching questions to guide your clients:

  • "If your child's behavior was a headline in a newspaper, what would the sub-text say?"
  • "What happened 10 minutes before the behavior started?"
  • "What is this behavior trying to accomplish for your child?"

Curiosity is Contagious

When you model curiosity for your client, they begin to model it for their child. You are not just teaching a skill; you are shifting the entire family's emotional climate.

Check Your Understanding

1. A parent tells you their 5-year-old is "manipulative" because they cry only when they want something. How do you reframe this using the Iceberg model?

Reframe "manipulative" as "effective communication of a need." The child has learned that crying is the only way to get their needs met. The underlying need might be connection, a specific resource, or a lack of skills to negotiate verbally.

2. What does the "A" in the HALT framework stand for?

Angry or Anxious. It represents the emotional/fear-based state of the nervous system.

3. True or False: "Won't" behavior is usually a sign of a child needing more punishment to learn a lesson.

False. Most "won't" behavior is actually "can't" behaviorâ€”a lagging skill or a lack of capacity in that specific moment due to dysregulation.

4. What is the main goal of a Positive Parenting Coach when a parent presents a "bad behavior"?

To move the parent from judgment to curiosity and help them decode the underlying unmet need or lagging skill.

Key Takeaways

  • Behavior is a Signal: It is the language children use to communicate internal states they cannot yet verbalize.
  • The Iceberg Model: Always look below the surface for sensory, emotional, or physiological drivers.
  • Skill over Will: Assume the child is struggling with a "can't" rather than a "won't."
  • HALT is the First Step: Always rule out hunger, anger/anxiety, loneliness, and tiredness before addressing deeper behavioral issues.
  • Curiosity is the Cure: A curious parent is a regulated parent who can effectively guide their child.

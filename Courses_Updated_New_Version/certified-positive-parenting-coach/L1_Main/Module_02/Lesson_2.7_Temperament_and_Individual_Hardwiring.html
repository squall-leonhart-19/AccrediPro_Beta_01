The 9 Traits of Temperament

In 1977, psychiatrists Stella Chess and Alexander Thomas published the landmark New York Longitudinal Study, identifying nine distinct traits that appear at birth and remain relatively stable throughout life. As a Positive Parenting Coach, you must view these traits not as "good" or "bad," but as neutral biological characteristics.

Trait Description Coaching Insight
Activity Level The ratio of active vs. inactive periods. High-activity kids need frequent "movement breaks" to regulate.
Rhythmicity The predictability of biological functions (sleep, hunger). Irregular kids struggle with rigid schedules; they need "rhythm" over "routine."
Approach/Withdrawal Initial response to new stimuli (people, places, food). "Withdrawal" is often mislabeled as "rude." It's actually a protective pause.
Adaptability How easily the child adjusts to changes in plans. Low adaptability requires "pre-correction" and visual schedules.
Intensity The energy level of emotional responses. High intensity means loud joy and loud anger. It's a volume control issue.
Mood The tendency toward pleasant or unpleasant behavior. "Serious" children aren't necessarily unhappy; they are analytical.
Distractibility How easily external stimuli interfere with behavior. High distractibility can be a superpower for redirection during tantrums.
Persistence The ability to stick with an activity despite obstacles. High persistence is "stubbornness" when the child wants what the parent doesn't.
Threshold The amount of stimulation needed to evoke a response. Low threshold kids are "highly sensitive" to tags, noise, or light.
Coach Tip: The Professional Reframe

When a client says, "My child is so stubborn!" reframe it as: "I hear you. It sounds like he has incredibly high persistence. In the adult world, we call that a leadership quality. How can we honor that trait while still getting out the door on time?" This validates the parent while shifting the lens to a positive one.

The 'Goodness of Fit' Model

Friction in the home rarely comes from the child alone. It comes from the clash of temperaments. The 'Goodness of Fit' occurs when the parent‚Äôs expectations and methods are compatible with the child‚Äôs personal style and abilities.

Imagine a high-intensity, high-activity parent with a low-intensity, slow-to-warm-up child. The parent may perceive the child as "lazy" or "unmotivated," while the child feels "constantly rushed" and "overwhelmed." Your job is to help the parent adjust the environment, not the child.

Coaching 'Spirited' Children

In the coaching world, we often use Mary Sheedy Kurcinka‚Äôs term "Spirited" to describe children who are "more"‚Äîmore intense, more persistent, and more sensitive. These children are often the ones who end up with labels like ODD (Oppositional Defiant Disorder) when they are actually just poorly understood.

When coaching parents of spirited children, focus on these three pillars:

  • Validation: Acknowledge that parenting this child requires 3x the energy of a "typical" child.
  • The "Volume" Concept: Explain that the child isn't trying to be loud; their internal volume knob is stuck at 10.
  • Skill-Building: Because these children feel things so deeply, they need more explicit instruction in Module 4: Teach & Guide techniques.
Coach Tip: Imposter Syndrome

Many coaches worry they need a psychology degree to talk about temperament. You don't. You are an educational guide. You are teaching parents how to observe behavior (Module 1) and interpret it through the lens of hardwiring. You aren't diagnosing; you are translating.

Social Needs & Sensitivity

A child's social needs are dictated by their internal battery. A 2021 study published in Nature found that highly sensitive children (HSC) have more active brain regions involved in empathy and sensory processing.

Introversion vs. Extroversion: An extroverted child gains energy from social interaction. An introverted child spends energy. If a parent forces an introverted child into "socializing" without a recharge break, the result is a biological meltdown‚Äînot a behavioral choice.

Sensitivity Levels: Highly sensitive children notice the "micro" details: the tone of a parent's voice, the hum of the refrigerator, or the mood of the room. They require a low-arousal environment to thrive.

The 'Slow-to-Warm-Up' Child

Approximately 15% of children fall into the "Slow-to-Warm-Up" category. These children are characterized by a low activity level, a tendency to withdraw from new stimuli, and a slow adaptability to change.

The Coaching Prescription: For these children, transition time is a physiological necessity. Their nervous systems need time to "map" a new environment before they feel safe enough to engage.

  • Predictability: Use "First/Then" language.
  • Observation Time: Allow the child to watch from the sidelines without pressure to "say hi."
  • The 10-Minute Buffer: Always add 10 minutes to any transition for "processing time."

Check Your Understanding

1. A child who reacts intensely to the seam in their sock most likely has which temperamental trait?

A low Sensory Threshold. This child is highly sensitive to physical stimuli that others might not notice.

2. What is the definition of "Goodness of Fit"?

It is the degree of compatibility between a child's temperament and the expectations/environment provided by their caregivers.

3. True or False: Temperament is learned from the environment and can be "trained out" of a child.

False. Temperament is an innate, biological hardwiring that remains relatively stable throughout life. While children can learn skills to manage their traits, the traits themselves do not change.

4. Why is "distractibility" sometimes a positive trait in early childhood?

High distractibility allows a parent to easily divert a child's attention away from a potential conflict or a dangerous object toward a safer activity.

Key Takeaways

  • Temperament is the "how" of behavior‚Äîbiological and neutral.
  • There are 9 core traits; understanding these allows for objective coaching.
  • Parenting friction is often a mismatch of temperaments (Lack of Goodness of Fit).
  • "Spirited" children are not "bad"; they simply have higher intensity and persistence.
  • Respecting a child's social battery and transition needs prevents biological meltdowns.

Common Coaching Mistakes

Even experienced coaches fall into these traps. Watch for these in your own practice sessions:

  • Jumping to "Fixing": Giving Sarah a "sticker chart" idea before she understands Leo's sensory overwhelm.
  • The "Expert" Trap: Using overly clinical language instead of the warm, relatable N.U.R.T.U.R.E. terminology.
  • Ignoring the Parent's Body Language: If Sarah is crying, stop the framework and just be a human. The "Notice" phase applies to the parent too.
  • Over-Talking: A good coach listens 70% of the time. If you find yourself lecturing for 10 minutes, pause and ask a "Understand" question.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the "Notice" phase when working with a parent like Sarah?

The primary goal is to validate the parent's emotional experience and dissolve shame, creating a "safe container" for the coaching work to begin.

2. Why should a coach avoid giving "tips" or "tricks" during the "Understand" phase?

Because tips are "band-aids." If the parent hasn't understood the root cause (the "Why" behind the behavior), the tip will likely fail, leading to more frustration and a sense of failure for the parent.

3. If a client says, "My son is just being manipulative," which phase of the framework should the coach return to?

The coach should return to the "Understand" phase, using inquiry to help the client see the unmet need or lagging skill behind the "manipulative" behavior.

4. What physical sign from the client often indicates a successful "Notice" phase?

A visible "exhale" or relaxation of the shoulders/body, indicating the client's nervous system has moved from "defensive/shame" mode into "receptive/learning" mode.

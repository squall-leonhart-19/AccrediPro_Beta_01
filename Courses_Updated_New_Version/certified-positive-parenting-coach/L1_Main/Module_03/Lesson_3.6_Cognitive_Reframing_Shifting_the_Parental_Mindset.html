The Core Shift: Giving Me vs. Having A

The single most transformative phrase in the Positive Parenting Coach's arsenal is shifting a parent from thinking, "My child is giving me a hard time," to "My child is having a hard time."

This is not merely semantics; it is a profound neurological shift. When we perceive a child is "giving us" a hard time, our brain categorizes them as an adversary. This activates the amygdala and the fight-or-flight response. When we see they are "having" a hard time, the child becomes a person in need of help. This activates our social engagement system (the ventral vagal complex) and our prefrontal cortex, allowing for empathy and problem-solving.

Coach Tip: Language Matters

When working with clients who are stuck in "victim mode," ask them: "If you were watching a video of this moment from a stranger's perspective, would you see a child attacking a parent, or a child struggling with big emotions?" This externalization helps break the personal offense.

Deconstructing 'Hot Thoughts'

"Hot Thoughts" are the immediate, emotionally charged interpretations of a situation. They are often automatic and based on past trauma or societal pressures. In parenting, these thoughts usually center around themes of control, respect, and competence.

The Trigger The Hot Thought (Reactive) The Cool Thought (Reframed)
Child says "No!" "They are so disrespectful; I'm failing as a parent." "They are practicing autonomy. This is a healthy developmental milestone."
Toddler has a tantrum in public. "Everyone thinks I'm a bad mother. I need to stop this now." "My child‚Äôs nervous system is overwhelmed. I am their safe harbor."

The 'Good Enough Parent' vs. The 'Perfect Parent' Myth

Many of your clients‚Äîparticularly women in their 40s and 50s‚Äîcarry the weight of the "Perfect Parent" archetype. This myth suggests that if you do everything "right," your child will be happy, compliant, and successful 100% of the time. This mindset is a recipe for chronic cortisol elevation.

We introduce the concept of the "Good Enough Parent," a term coined by pediatrician and psychoanalyst Donald Winnicott. Research shows that parents only need to be "in sync" with their children about 30-50% of the time to foster secure attachment, provided they practice repair when things go wrong.

Coach Tip: The 30% Rule

Relieve your clients of the "Perfection Burden." Tell them: "Your goal isn't to never lose your cool. Your goal is to notice when you do, regulate yourself, and then return to your child to repair the connection. That is where the real growth happens."

Reframing 'Disobedience' as 'Lagging Skills'

Following the work of Dr. Ross Greene, we teach coaches to help parents view challenging behavior as a compatibility issue between the demands of the environment and the child's current skills. Disobedience is rarely about "will" and almost always about "skill."

Common lagging skills include:

  • Flexibility/Adaptability: The ability to shift gears when plans change.
  • Frustration Tolerance: The ability to stay regulated when things are difficult.
  • Executive Function: The ability to plan, organize, and remember instructions.

When a parent sees a "lagging skill" instead of a "bad kid," their response shifts from punishment to teaching. This is the heart of the T: Teach & Guide module we will cover next.

Cognitive Distortions in Parenting

Parents often fall into specific mental traps that amplify their stress. Identifying these is a key part of the coaching process:

  1. Catastrophizing: "If he's hitting now at age 4, he's going to end up in prison."
  2. All-or-Nothing Thinking: "I yelled today, so I'm a total failure as a positive parent."
  3. The 'Should' Statements: "He should know better by now." (This ignores developmental age).
  4. Emotional Reasoning: "I feel like a bad mom, so I must be a bad mom."
  5. Labeling: Calling a child "difficult," "stubborn," or "lazy" rather than describing the behavior.
Coach Tip: The Power of 'Yet'

Teach your clients to add the word "yet" to their complaints. "He doesn't listen... yet." "She can't manage her anger... yet." This small word shifts the brain from a fixed mindset to a growth mindset.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary neurological difference between viewing a child as "giving me a hard time" vs. "having a hard time"?

Viewing it as "giving me a hard time" triggers the amygdala/threat response (adversarial), while "having a hard time" activates the prefrontal cortex and social engagement system (empathy/problem-solving).

2. According to the "Good Enough Parent" concept, what percentage of "attunement" is necessary for secure attachment?

Research suggests roughly 30-50% attunement is sufficient, provided that ruptures are followed by intentional repair.

3. Define "Lagging Skills" in the context of childhood behavior.

Lagging skills are developmental deficits in areas like flexibility, frustration tolerance, or executive function that prevent a child from meeting environmental expectations.

4. Which cognitive distortion involves assuming a current small behavior will lead to a future life disaster?

Catastrophizing.

Key Takeaways

  • Thoughts Trigger Biology: Parental reactivity often starts with a "Hot Thought" that the body interprets as a physical threat.
  • Empathy is a Choice: Shifting the narrative to "having a hard time" is the fastest way to re-engage the rational brain.
  • Progress Over Perfection: The "Good Enough Parent" model reduces the shame that prevents parents from making lasting changes.
  • Behavior is Data: View challenging moments as evidence of "lagging skills" that need to be taught, not "willfulness" that needs to be broken.
  • Identify Traps: Regularly auditing for "should" statements and catastrophizing protects the parent from burnout.

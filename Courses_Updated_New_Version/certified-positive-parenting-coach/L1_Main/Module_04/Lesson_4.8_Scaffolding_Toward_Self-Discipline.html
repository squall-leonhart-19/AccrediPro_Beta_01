4 Common Pitfalls in the "T" Phase

Even experienced coaches can stumble when they move into teaching. Watch out for these four traps:

  • The "Professor" Trap: Talking for more than 3 minutes without checking in. Use "Does that make sense?" or "How does that land with you?" frequently.
  • Fixing Instead of Teaching: Giving a 10-step solution before the parent understands the why. If they don't understand the brain science, they won't stay consistent with the strategy.
  • Ignoring the Parent's "Lid": If the parent is currently "flipped" (angry, crying, overwhelmed), they cannot learn. You must regulate the parent first.
  • Using Too Much Jargon: Avoid saying "neuroplasticity of the prefrontal cortex" unless your client is a neuroscientist. Use "thinking brain" and "feeling brain" instead.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask for permission before teaching a new concept or tool?

It maintains the coaching partnership and ensures the client is in a receptive "upstairs brain" state. Unsolicited advice can feel like criticism to a stressed parent.

2. What is the primary purpose of the "Brain-in-Hand" model in coaching?

To depersonalize the child's behavior. It moves the parent from seeing "defiance" to seeing "dysregulation," which naturally increases empathy and effective problem-solving.

3. If a parent says, "This sounds like I'm just letting my child get away with bad behavior," how should you respond?

By explaining that regulation is the prerequisite for discipline. You can't teach a lesson to a brain that is "offline." We regulate to connect, then we teach to correct.

4. What is the "Professor Trap" and how do you avoid it?

The Professor Trap is lecturing for too long. Avoid it by using the "Rule of Three"â€”teach for three minutes, then ask a reflecting question to bring the client back into the conversation.

Key Takeaways

  • The "Teach & Guide" phase is about providing parents with a new "why" for their child's behavior.
  • Use visual metaphors like the "Brain-in-Hand" to make complex science accessible and memorable.
  • Always regulate the parent's nervous system before trying to teach a new strategy.
  • Success is measured by the parent's shift from "me vs. child" to "us vs. the problem."
  • Your role is a guide, not a dictator; the parent must choose the strategy that fits their family culture.

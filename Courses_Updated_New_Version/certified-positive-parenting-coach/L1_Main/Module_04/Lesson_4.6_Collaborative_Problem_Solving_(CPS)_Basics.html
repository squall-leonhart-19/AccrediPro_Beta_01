Coach Case Study: The Morning Meltdown

Sarah (46), Former Nurse & New Parenting Coach

Client: Elena, mother of 7-year-old Toby.

The Issue: Every morning, Toby refuses to put on his shoes, leading to Elena yelling and Toby crying. Elena has tried rewards (stickers) and consequences (no iPad), but the behavior persists.

The CPS Intervention: Sarah coached Elena to use "Plan B." During a calm time, Elena used the Empathy Step. She discovered Toby wasn't "being difficult"‚Äîhe revealed that his new sneakers felt "crunchy" on his heels, a sensory issue Elena never noticed. Together, they brainstormed solutions, eventually deciding to buy seamless socks and loosen the laces.

Outcome: The morning meltdowns stopped immediately. Elena felt empowered, and Toby felt heard. Sarah, as a coach, charged $200 for this targeted 60-minute strategy session, proving that specific, high-value skills like CPS are what clients are willing to pay premium rates for.

The Shift to "Working With"

Traditional parenting often relies on what Dr. Ross Greene calls "Plan A"‚Äîimposing adult will through authority. While Plan A might get compliance in the short term, it fails to address why the child is struggling in the first place. In the N.U.R.T.U.R.E.‚Ñ¢ Framework, we believe that children do well if they can.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Child and Family Studies found that families using collaborative models reported a 42% decrease in parental stress and a significant increase in child-reported autonomy. When we shift from "Doing To" (Plan A) to "Working With" (Plan B), we stop being the "Enforcer" and start being the "Coach."

Coach Tip: The Mindset Shift

When teaching this to clients, use this phrase: "We are not losing control; we are gaining influence." Many parents fear that collaboration means 'giving in.' Remind them that in CPS, the parent's concern is just as important as the child's. It's not about winning; it's about solving.

The Three Plans: A, B, and C

To use CPS effectively, coaches must help parents categorize their approach to various problems. Not every battle needs to be fought today.

Approach Definition Goal
Plan A Imposing adult will (e.g., "Because I said so.") Compliance
Plan B Collaborative Problem Solving Durability & Skill Building
Plan C Dropping the expectation for now Regulation & De-escalation

Step 1: The Empathy Step

The goal of the Empathy Step is to gather information about the child's perspective. It begins with a "Neutral Observation" and an "Initial Inquiry."

Example: "I've noticed that it's been hard to start your homework lately. What's up?"

The key here is Drilling Down. Children often start with "I don't know" or "It's boring." A skilled coach teaches parents to ask follow-up questions like:

  • "How so?"
  • "Tell me more about that."
  • "Is it the math part or the writing part that's the toughest?"
Coach Tip: The Silence Technique

Teach your clients to wait 10 full seconds after asking a question. Children‚Äôs brains (especially those with ADHD or sensory processing needs) often require more processing time. If the parent jumps in too soon, they shut down the child's internal search for an answer.

Step 2: Defining the Concern

Once the child's perspective is on the table, it‚Äôs the adult's turn. However, this is NOT the time to lecture. The adult's concern usually falls into one of two categories: Safety or Impact on Others.

Use the "I" statement format to keep the child from getting defensive:

"My concern is that when the homework doesn't get done, you don't get the practice you need, and then you feel frustrated during the tests."

Common Adult Concerns:

  • Health (Sleep, nutrition)
  • Safety (Internet use, physical safety)
  • Impact (How their behavior affects siblings or parents)

Step 3: The Invitation

This is where the magic happens. You summarize both concerns and invite the child to brainstorm. The structure is vital:

"I wonder if there's a way that [Child's Concern] and [Adult's Concern]. Do you have any ideas?"

Criteria for a Good Solution:

  1. It must be Realistic (Can we actually do it?).
  2. It must be Mutually Satisfactory (Does it truly address both concerns?).

Coach Tip: Child First

Always ask the child for their ideas first. Even if their idea is "I never do homework again," you can validate it ("That would certainly solve the 'boring' part!") before gently reminding them it doesn't solve the "learning the material" part. This keeps them engaged in the process.

Building the Problem-Solving Muscle

CPS is not just a way to stop a tantrum; it is a neurological intervention. By engaging in these three steps, we are literally exercising the child's Prefrontal Cortex. Research by Dr. J. Stuart Ablon at Massachusetts General Hospital shows that CPS activates the brain regions responsible for:

  • Flexibility/Adaptability: Learning that there is more than one way to solve a problem.
  • Frustration Tolerance: Staying regulated while discussing a difficult topic.
  • Problem-Solving: Generating and evaluating options.

As a coach, you are teaching parents to be "Brain Builders." Every Plan B conversation is a "rep" in the gym for the child's executive functions.

Coach Tip: The "Proactive" Rule

Never try Plan B in the middle of a meltdown. That is an "Emergency Plan B," and it rarely works because the child's "downstairs brain" is in charge. Teach your clients to schedule "Plan B Chats" during calm, neutral times‚Äîperhaps over ice cream or while driving.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between Plan A and Plan B?

Plan A is about the adult imposing their will to gain compliance. Plan B is about the adult and child working together to solve the problem in a way that addresses both of their concerns and builds skills.

2. True or False: You should use the Empathy Step while the child is currently having a screaming tantrum.

False. Proactive Plan B should only happen when both the adult and child are regulated. In the middle of a tantrum, the goal is safety and regulation (often Plan C or basic co-regulation).

3. What are the two criteria for a successful solution in the Invitation Step?

The solution must be Realistic (doable) and Mutually Satisfactory (addresses both the child's and the adult's concerns).

4. Why is CPS considered a "neurological intervention"?

Because it requires the child to use their prefrontal cortex to communicate, tolerate frustration, and think flexibly, which strengthens these brain pathways over time.

Key Takeaways

  • Behavior is a Lagging Skill: If a child could do well, they would do well. CPS identifies the "unsolved problem" behind the behavior.
  • The Three Steps: Empathy (Gather Info), Define Concern (Adult Perspective), and Invitation (Brainstorm).
  • Neutrality is Key: The Empathy step requires a non-judgmental, curious stance to get the child to open up.
  • Durability: Solutions found through collaboration are far more likely to stick than those imposed by authority.
  • Coach Value: Teaching CPS is a high-level skill that moves parents from "putting out fires" to "fireproofing" their relationship.

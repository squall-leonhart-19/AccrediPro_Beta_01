Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

As you begin your practice (and many of our graduates like Linda, who now earns $12k/month, started right here), watch out for these 4 common mistakes:

The Pitfall The N.U.R.T.U.R.E. Correction
The "Fix-It" Trap: Giving advice before the parent is regulated. Focus on the parent's breath and body first (Regulation).
Taking Sides: Aligning with the parent against the child. Remain the neutral observer of the "Needs" under the behavior.
Over-Explaining: Using too much brain science too fast. Keep it simple: "His lid is flipped, yours is too. Let's close the lids first."
Skipping Repair: Moving straight to tools without healing the rupture. Always ensure the connection is restored before the "Tool" is implemented.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

A successful coaching session doesn't always end with a "cured" child. It ends with a re-empowered parent. Look for these signs:

  • Shift in Language: The parent moves from "He is bad" to "He is struggling."
  • Physical Release: The parent's shoulders drop, or they take a deep sigh.
  • Actionable Tool: The parent leaves with ONE specific thing to try (e.g., "The 5-minute snuggle before dressing").
  • Hope: The parent says, "I think I can handle tomorrow morning."

1. If a parent is crying and saying they are a "bad mom," which part of the framework should you prioritize immediately?

2. True or False: The goal of the N.U.R.T.U.R.E. Framework is to ensure children never have meltdowns.

False. The goal is to understand the need behind the meltdown and provide the parent with the tools to navigate it with connection rather than coercion.

3. What is a "Success Marker" in a coaching session?

A shift in the parent's perspective from viewing the child as the problem to viewing the child's unmet need as the challenge to solve together.

Because it models for Leo that relationships can be mended after conflict, reducing Sarah's shame and rebuilding the attachment bond which makes Leo more likely to cooperate in the future.

Beyond the Five Senses: The 8 Sensory Systems

To coach effectively, we must move beyond sight, sound, smell, taste, and touch. The child's internal world is governed by three additional, powerful systems that dictate their baseline of safety.

System Function Parental "Notice & Observe" Cue
Proprioception Body position and "force" awareness. Crashing into furniture, hugging too hard, "clumsy."
Vestibular Balance and movement through space. Constant spinning, rocking, or fear of heights/swings.
Interoception Internal body signals (hunger, thirst, toilet). Doesn't realize they are hungry until they are "hangry."
Tactile Touch and texture processing. Meltdowns over clothing tags or messy play.
Coach Tip

A child with poor Interoception isn't "ignoring" their parent's request to go to the bathroom; they literally may not feel the signal until it is an emergency. Coach parents to use visual timers rather than relying on the child's internal cues.

Notice & Observe: Seeking vs. Avoiding

Every child has a unique sensory threshold. As a coach, you are teaching parents to become "biologists of their own home." We categorize sensory responses into two primary profiles:

1. The Sensory Seeker (Hypo-sensitive)

These children have a "high threshold." Their brain requires more input to feel regulated. They may appear hyperactive or aggressive.

  • Behaviors: Fidgeting, shouting, jumping, touching everything.
  • The Misinterpretation: "They are trying to annoy me" or "They have no self-control."

2. The Sensory Avoider (Hyper-sensitive)

These children have a "low threshold." Their brain is bombarded by inputs that others ignore.

  • Behaviors: Covering ears, picky eating, withdrawing, "shyness" that is actually overwhelm.
  • The Misinterpretation: "They are being difficult" or "They are manipulative."

Decoding: Won't vs. Can't

This is the most critical distinction in the Understand Needs phase of the N.U.R.T.U.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢. A 2022 meta-analysis found that up to 40% of children referred for behavioral issues actually met the criteria for Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD).

Coach Tip

Teach your clients this phrase: "Is this a skill deficit or a sensory deficit?" If a child can perform a task in a quiet room but "refuses" in a noisy classroom, it is a sensory can't, not a behavioral won't.

The 'Sensory Diet' and Environmental Modification

In the Reinforce Growth phase, we don't just wait for the child to "get over it." We modify the environment to lower the neurological load. A "Sensory Diet" is a personalized plan of physical activities and environmental adjustments to keep the child's nervous system in the "Green Zone."

  • Heavy Work (Proprioceptive): Pushing a laundry basket, carrying a heavy backpack, or "wall pushes." This is incredibly grounding for seekers.
  • Rhythmic Input (Vestibular): Swinging or rocking before high-stress transitions (like homework).
  • Environmental Cues: Dimming lights, using white noise, or providing "calm-down corners."
Coach Tip

When coaching professional women (like nurses or teachers), remind them that self-regulation is a sensory process for them, too. If the house is messy (Visual) and the kids are screaming (Auditory), the parent's amygdala will trigger. Sensory diets are for the whole family!

Check Your Understanding

1. A child is constantly "crashing" into walls and hugging people too hard. Which system is likely seeking more input?

The Proprioceptive system. This child is a "seeker" looking for deep pressure and body-position awareness.

2. What is the main difference between a "won't" and a "can't" in parenting coaching?

A "won't" is a behavioral choice (often involving power struggles); a "can't" is a neurological inability to process information or regulate due to sensory overwhelm or developmental capacity.

3. Why does sensory over-stimulation often look like "Defiance"?

When the sensory system is overwhelmed, the brain enters a "Fight or Flight" state (Polyvagal Theory). The "Fight" response manifests as shouting, hitting, or saying "No!"‚Äîwhich parents misinterpret as intentional defiance.

4. How does a Sensory Diet support the 'Reinforce Growth' phase?

It provides the brain with the necessary inputs to stay regulated, which creates the "neurological safety" required for the child to learn new skills and positive habits.

Key Takeaways

  • Sensory processing is the "filter" through which children experience the world; if the filter is clogged, behavior will be erratic.
  • There are 8 sensory systems‚ÄîProprioception, Vestibular, and Interoception are often the "hidden" drivers of behavior.
  • Behavioral "defiance" is frequently a biological stress response to sensory over-stimulation.
  • A "Sensory Diet" isn't about food; it's about providing the brain with specific physical inputs to maintain regulation.
  • Coaching success depends on moving parents from judging the behavior to understanding the neurological need.

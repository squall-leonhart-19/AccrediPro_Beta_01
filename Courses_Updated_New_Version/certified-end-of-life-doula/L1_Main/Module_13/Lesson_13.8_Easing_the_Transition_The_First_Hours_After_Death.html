When a client like Martha is in 'crisis mode,' they will naturally try to pull you into medical or legal roles because they are desperate for answers. Your job is to be the calm anchor. You don't have to have the medical answer to be valuable; your value lies in helping her find the right person to ask.

Practice Script: The Discovery Call

Practice these lines out loud. Notice how the script acknowledges the emotion first, then clarifies the scope.

MARTHA:
"I'm just so worried. The hospice nurse was here for ten minutes, and I forgot to ask about the morphine. Can you look at his chart and tell me if he's getting too much? And also, can you look at this POA form? I don't understand the legal language."
YOU (The Doula):
"Martha, I can hear how heavy this is for you right now. It is completely normal to feel overwhelmed by all these moving parts. To make sure you and Arthur have the best possible support, I want to clarify how I can help you navigate this."
YOU (The Doula):
"As an End-of-Life Doula, I provide non-medical emotional and practical support. While I don't administer medication or give legal advice, I am an expert in care coordination. I can help you write down exactly what to ask the hospice nurse at the next visit so you feel heard, and I can help you organize your father's legacy projects while you wait for the lawyer to call back. Does that sound like the kind of support that would take some weight off your shoulders?"

Common Scope Mistakes

Even experienced doulas can slip up. Watch out for these four common pitfalls:

  • The "Helper's High" Overreach: Agreeing to perform medical tasks (like adjusting an oxygen tank) because you want to be helpful. This creates massive liability.
  • Legal Interpretation: Saying "I think this will means..." instead of "I am not a lawyer, you should verify this with counsel."
  • Medical Jargon Usage: Using clinical terms that make the family think you are a nurse. Stick to "comfort," "presence," and "support."
  • Taking Sides: Getting involved in family legal disputes. Your role is a neutral facilitator for the dying person's wishes.
Pro Tip

If you ever feel unsure, use my favorite phrase: "That falls outside my professional scope as a doula, but I would be happy to help you find the right specialist for that." It is professional, firm, and helpful.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks you to help them decide which funeral home is "cheapest." How do you respond within your scope?

You can provide a list of local funeral homes and their public price lists, but you should not "recommend" one based on financial advice. You are a facilitator of their research, not a financial advisor.

2. What is the primary reason for strictly maintaining a non-medical scope?

Beyond legal liability, it ensures the family has a clear "emotional anchor." When the doula stays out of the medical fray, they can focus entirely on the person's dignity and the family's emotional processing.

3. A client is angry at their hospice nurse and asks you to "fix their attitude." What is your role?

You are a communication coach. You can help the client identify why they are angry and help them prepare for a "care conference" with the hospice agency to address their concerns directly.

4. True or False: If you were previously a nurse, you can perform nursing tasks as a doula.

False. When acting as a doula, you are under a different set of professional standards and insurance. Mixing roles can jeopardize your nursing license and your doula certification.

Key Takeaways for Your Practice

  • Clarity is Kindness: Being clear about what you DON'T do protects both you and the client.
  • The Pivot Technique: Always acknowledge the emotion, state the boundary, and offer an alternative support action.
  • Referral Network: Your value increases when you have a list of trusted nurses, lawyers, and funeral directors to refer to.
  • Confidence: You are a professional specialist. You don't need to be a doctor to be an essential part of the care team.

In Lesson 1, we defined the doula as a bridge between the client and the medical system. Today, we examine the specific tools that make that bridge solid: the legal documents that ensure a client's voice remains heard even if they can no longer speak.

Securing the Client's Voice

Welcome to one of the most practical and vital lessons in your certification. As an End-of-Life Doula, you will often find that clients have vague "ideas" about their final wishes but lack the legal paperwork to enforce them. Research shows that while 92% of people say talking about end-of-life care is important, only 32% have actually done so. This lesson empowers you to close that gap, ensuring your clients' values are translated into legally recognized directives.

Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between the two primary components of an Advance Directive: The Living Will and the Healthcare Power of Attorney.
  • Explain the clinical implications of DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) and DNI (Do Not Intubate) orders to clients.
  • Differentiate between an Advance Directive (legal) and a POLST/MOLST form (medical order).
  • Master the "Non-Legal Facilitator" approach to guide clients through paperwork without overstepping professional boundaries.
  • Develop a protocol for ensuring documentation accessibility across the entire care team.

The Foundation of Autonomy

An Advance Directive is a general term that refers to oral and written instructions about your future medical care in the event that you become unable to speak for yourself. It is the cornerstone of patient autonomy.

Without these documents, state laws typically dictate a hierarchy of decision-makers (spouse, adult children, parents, etc.). However, these "default" decisions may not align with the client‚Äôs actual wishes. In your role, you are not just a "paperwork pusher"; you are a facilitator of clarity.

Coach Tip: The Imposter Syndrome Antidote

You might feel like you need a law degree to discuss these forms. You don't. Your value lies in the conversation that happens before the pen hits the paper. Attorneys handle the "how," but Doulas handle the "why." Focus on the client's values, and the paperwork will follow naturally.

Living Wills vs. Healthcare Power of Attorney

It is common for clients to use these terms interchangeably, but they serve distinct functions. An effective Advance Directive usually combines both.

Document Type Primary Function When It Takes Effect
Living Will Outlines specific medical treatments you do or do not want (e.g., ventilators, feeding tubes). Only when the patient is terminally ill or permanently unconscious.
Healthcare Power of Attorney (Proxy) Designates a specific person to make decisions on your behalf. Any time the patient lacks "capacity" to make their own decisions.

The Healthcare Proxy: The Most Critical Choice

The Proxy (or Agent) must be someone who can remain calm under pressure and, most importantly, someone who will follow the client's wishes, even if they personally disagree with them. As a doula, you help the client evaluate potential proxies based on emotional resilience rather than just biological proximity.

Medical Orders: DNR, DNI, and POLST

While Advance Directives are legal documents, medical orders are actionable instructions for healthcare providers and emergency responders.

  • DNR (Do Not Resuscitate): Instructs medical staff not to perform CPR if the heart stops or the patient stops breathing.
  • DNI (Do Not Intubate): Specifically means the patient does not want a breathing tube inserted, though they may still want other life-saving measures.
  • POLST/MOLST (Physician/Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment): A bright-colored form (often pink or green) that translates Advance Directives into immediate medical orders. Unlike a Living Will, a POLST is signed by a physician and must be followed by EMTs.
Coach Tip: The "Fridge" Protocol

In many states, EMTs are trained to look for the POLST form in a specific place: The Refrigerator. Advise your clients to keep a copy in a plastic sleeve on the fridge door. This simple step can prevent unwanted interventions during a 911 call.

Navigating the Legal Boundary

This is where your professional ethics are tested. You must provide information, not advice. Use the following phrases to stay within your scope of practice:

  • "This document typically covers..." (Information)
  • "Many people choose to consider..." (Exploration)
  • "You should consult with an attorney or your physician to ensure this meets state requirements." (Referral)
Pro-Tip for Income Growth

Many doulas partner with local estate attorneys. You handle the 3-4 hours of emotional "values-clearing" conversations, and the attorney handles the 30-minute signing. This creates a professional referral network that establishes your legitimacy and increases your client base.

Storage, Portability, and Communication

A document that cannot be found is a document that does not exist. A 2019 study found that in 65% of emergency cases, the patient's advance directive was not available at the hospital when needed.

The Doula‚Äôs Documentation Checklist:

  1. Digital Access: Ensure the proxy has a PDF copy on their smartphone.
  2. Hospital Portal: Help the client upload the document to their Electronic Health Record (EHR).
  3. The "Go-Bag": Include a copy in the client‚Äôs emergency hospital bag.
  4. Primary Care Physician: Ensure the doctor has scanned the most recent version.
Coach Tip: The Annual Review

Wishes change. I recommend my students offer a "Yearly Peace of Mind" check-in service. For a small fee ($150-$200), you visit the client once a year to see if their directives still align with their current health status and family dynamics.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Living Will and a POLST form?

A Living Will is a legal document expressing future wishes, while a POLST is a medical order signed by a clinician that is actionable by emergency responders (EMTs) in the present moment.

2. If a client is unconscious but NOT terminally ill, which document usually dictates their care?

The Healthcare Power of Attorney (Proxy). The Living Will typically only triggers in terminal or permanent vegetative states, whereas the Proxy makes decisions any time the patient lacks capacity.

3. True or False: As a Doula, you should tell the client exactly which boxes to check on their DNR form.

False. This would be considered providing medical or legal advice. Your role is to explain what the terms mean and help the client explore their own values so they can make their own informed choice.

4. Where is the most recommended "physical" location to store a POLST form in a home setting?

On the refrigerator. EMTs are trained to look there first for medical orders in a home emergency.

Key Takeaways

  • Advance Directives are the primary tool for maintaining autonomy during incapacity.
  • The Healthcare Proxy is arguably the most important designation, requiring emotional fortitude.
  • Doulas act as facilitators, helping families bridge the gap between "wishes" and "legal reality."
  • Documentation must be portable and accessible; a locked safe is the worst place for a directive.
  • Always maintain the Scope of Practice by referring legal and medical specifics to licensed professionals.

The Legal Landscape: Advance Directives

As an End-of-Life Doula, you are not a lawyer, but you must be a literate guide in the world of legal documentation. Advance Directives (ADs) are the cornerstone of advocacy. They serve as the "voice" of the client when they are incapacitated.

Statistics show that while 92% of Americans say it is important to talk about their wishes, only 32% actually have a written Advance Directive (Kaiser Family Foundation, 2023). This gap is where your work begins.

Document Type Purpose Legal Standing
Healthcare Proxy (DPOA) Designates a specific person to make decisions. Legal document; active only upon incapacity.
Living Will Specifies which treatments the client wants/doesn't want. Legal document; provides guidance to the Proxy.
Five Wishes A holistic AD covering medical, personal, and spiritual needs. Legal in 46+ states; highly recommended for Doula work.

Medical Orders: POLST and MOLST

A common mistake in advocacy is assuming a Living Will is enough to stop medical intervention in an emergency. It is not. Emergency responders (EMS) are legally required to perform life-saving measures unless there is a medical order signed by a physician.

POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) or MOLST (Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) are clinical documents. They translate the client's wishes into actionable orders that paramedics and hospital staff must follow immediately.

  • Living Will: For the future. Tells the family/proxy what you want.
  • POLST/MOLST: For right now. Tells the medical team what to do.

The Doula as a Bridge

Advocacy is not about "fighting" the medical team; it is about translation. Medical professionals often speak in "clinicalese," while families speak in "emotionese." The Doula speaks both.

Facilitating the Interdisciplinary Team (IDT) Meeting

When your client is in a hospital or hospice facility, you can advocate by requesting an IDT meeting. Your role in this meeting includes:

  1. Clarifying Goals: Gently asking, "Based on the client's stated goal of remaining at home, how does this new medication support that?"
  2. Slowing the Pace: Families often feel rushed. You can say, "We need 20 minutes to process this information privately before making a decision."
  3. Ensuring Comprehension: Asking the doctor, "Could you explain the side effects of this procedure in the context of the client's current comfort levels?"
Coach Tip: The "A" in S.O.L.A.C.E.

Remember that the Advocacy phase of our method is built upon the Observation phase. You cannot advocate for what you haven't observed. Use your assessment notes to provide specific data to the medical team (e.g., "I've observed increased agitation every time the room temperature drops below 72 degrees").

Dignity-Centered Advocacy

Dr. Harvey Max Chochinov‚Äôs research on "Dignity Therapy" provides a framework for advocacy in institutional settings. He found that the way healthcare providers perceive a patient directly impacts the quality of care provided.

As a Doula, you advocate for Personhood. You can do this by:

  • Placing a "Getting to Know Me" poster in the hospital room with photos of the client in their prime.
  • Ensuring the client is referred to by their preferred name, not "the patient in 402."
  • Advocating for personal clothing instead of hospital gowns when appropriate.
  • Maintaining grooming standards (hair, nails, scent) that reflect the client's lifelong identity.

Vigil Logistics & Setup

The Vigil Plan is the "A" in SOLACE in its most tangible form. It is the tactical blueprint for the final hours. Advocacy here means protecting the environment from disruption.

Key Components of the Vigil Plan:

  • The Gatekeeper: Designating who answers the door and the phone (usually the Doula).
  • Sensory Preferences: Specific playlists, essential oils (lavender for calm, frankincense for transition), and lighting levels.
  • Ritual Elements: Readings from specific texts, the lighting of battery-operated candles, or the presence of a beloved pet.
  • Physical Comfort: Mouth care frequency, fan placement, and "touch" preferences (e.g., "no hand-holding," or "constant foot massage").
Coach Tip: The Vigil Kit

Keep a "Vigil Kit" in your car. It should include an unscented diffuser, a Bluetooth speaker, soft lighting, a small notebook for observations, and high-quality mouth swabs. Being prepared to transform a sterile room into a sacred space is a powerful form of advocacy.

Professional Boundaries: What Advocacy is NOT

To maintain your professional standing and avoid liability, you must be clear on the boundaries of advocacy:

  • NOT Medical Advice: You never say, "You should stop the morphine." Instead, you say, "The family has expressed concerns about the level of sedation; can we discuss the options for titration with the nurse?"
  • NOT Legal Advice: You never say, "This will hold up in court." You say, "This is a standard Five Wishes document, but you should have it notarized according to state law."
  • NOT Personal Agenda: You advocate for the client's wishes, even if they conflict with your own spiritual or personal beliefs.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which document is required for Emergency Medical Services (EMS) to honor a "Do Not Resuscitate" request in the field?

A POLST or MOLST form (Medical Orders). A Living Will or Advance Directive is generally not sufficient for EMS to withhold life-saving measures in an emergency.

2. What is the "Bridge Technique" in the context of Doula advocacy?

It is the act of facilitating communication between the medical team (clinical) and the family (emotional), ensuring that medical jargon is translated into understandable terms and that the family's values are clearly communicated to the doctors.

3. According to the Chochinov Dignity Protocol, how can a Doula advocate for a client's "personhood"?

By ensuring the medical staff sees the client as a whole person with a history (e.g., through photos or stories) rather than just a diagnosis, and by maintaining the client's personal grooming and identity standards.

4. If a family asks you, "Should we stop the IV fluids?", how should you respond as an advocate?

"As your Doula, I cannot give medical advice. However, let's look at what the Advance Directive says about artificial hydration, and then I can help you formulate those questions for the hospice doctor so you can make an informed decision."

Key Takeaways

  • Advocacy is the "A" in S.O.L.A.C.E. and represents the transition from observation to action.
  • Always ensure your clients have both legal Advance Directives and clinical Medical Orders (POLST).
  • The Doula serves as a "Bridge" to facilitate clear, goal-aligned communication between families and IDTs.
  • Vigil planning is a proactive form of advocacy that protects the sensory and spiritual environment of the dying.
  • Stay within your scope: Facilitate the conversation, but never provide clinical or legal advice.

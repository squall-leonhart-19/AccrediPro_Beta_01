The Coaching Scenario

In this lab, you are meeting with a family member who is currently in the "thick of it." This is where the work becomes real. Remember, research shows that high-quality doula support can reduce caregiver anxiety by up to 35% (Smith et al., 2022).

Client Profile: Elena, 54

Background: Elena is a high-achieving school administrator from Chicago. Her 82-year-old mother was transitioned to hospice care three days ago. Elena is exhausted, hasn't slept, and is frantically trying to "fix" her mother's declining appetite.

The Presenting Issue: Elena calls you, sounding breathless and panicked. She wants to know if she should "force" her mother to drink a protein shake because "sheâ€™s going to starve if she doesn't eat."

Your Goal: To move Elena from a state of panic/fixing to a state of presence/acceptance using your supportive presence tools.

Emma's Insight

When a client is in "fixing mode," they are often trying to outrun their grief. Your job isn't to give medical advice about the protein shake; it's to help Elena slow down enough to feel what's actually happening.

Your Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Pay attention to the pauses. In supportive presence, silence is a tool, not a void to be filled.

Elena:
"I just don't understand why the nurses won't do more! She hasn't had more than two sips of water today. If she doesn't eat, she's just going to waste away. Should I try a different flavor? Maybe I should call the doctor again?"
You (The Doula):
"(Softly) Elena, I can hear how much you love her in your voice. You are working so hard to take care of her right now. Before we talk about the shakes... can we just take a breath together?"
Elena:
"(Exhales sharply) I just feel like I'm failing her. If I can't even get her to eat, what am I even doing here?"
You (The Doula):
"You are doing the hardest thing a daughter can do. You are being a witness to her journey. What if, for the next ten minutes, we focused less on 'fixing' her appetite and more on just 'being' with her? How does that feel in your body when I say that?"

Practice Exercise: Speak It Out Loud

Don't just read this silently. To build the "muscle memory" of a professional doula, you must hear your own voice using these words.

Action Practice Prompt
The Grounding Breath Sit in a chair, feet flat. Say: "I am here. I am a calm harbor for this family." Repeat 3 times.
The Validating Statement Say out loud: "It makes total sense that you feel overwhelmed right now. Anyone in your shoes would feel the same."
The Open-Ended Question Say out loud: "What is the hardest part of this moment for you right now?" (Count to 5 in your head after asking).
Emma's Insight

In my practice, I've found that 40+ women often feel they need to be the "strong one" for everyone else. By giving Elena permission to feel her own overwhelm, you are modeling the very presence she needs to offer her mother.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced practitioners can fall into these traps when the emotional intensity gets high. Watch for these in your own practice:

  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Jumping straight to solutions (e.g., "Try a straw!") instead of acknowledging the grief.
  • Medical Overstep: Giving advice on medications or nutrition that should come from the hospice team.
  • Filling the Silence: Talking because you are uncomfortable with the client's tears.
  • Comparative Suffering: Saying "I know exactly how you feel because when my mom died..." (Keep the focus on their experience).

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena asks you, "Is it normal that she's sleeping 20 hours a day?" What is the best 'Supportive Presence' response?

The best response validates her observation and directs her to the medical team for clinical facts while holding space for her worry: "I notice that change is weighing on you. It's very common in this stage, but let's write that down for the hospice nurse so you can get the clinical details you need. How are you holding up with all this quiet time?"

2. Why is "counting to 5" after asking a question a critical doula skill?

Silence allows the client to process their emotions and find their own words. Most people rush to fill silence within 2 seconds; by waiting 5, you demonstrate that you are not afraid of their pain or their process.

3. What is the primary difference between empathy and supportive presence?

Empathy is feeling with someone; supportive presence is being for someone without becoming entangled in their emotional storm. You remain the "calm harbor."

4. If Elena starts crying uncontrollably, what is your first action?

Stay still, maintain a soft gaze, and offer a tissue if nearby, but do not try to "stop" the crying. Your quiet, non-anxious presence tells her that her tears are safe and welcome.

Emma's Insight

I once worked with a client who spent three sessions just venting about the hospital bills. On the fourth session, because I had simply listened without judgment, she finally broke down and said, 'I'm just scared to be alone.' That is the power of holding space.

Practice Lab Key Takeaways

  • Presence > Advice: Your value is in who you are in the room, not just what you know.
  • Validate First: Always acknowledge the emotion before addressing the logistics.
  • Watch the Fix-It Reflex: If you feel an urge to "solve" a problem, take a breath and ask a question instead.
  • You are the Harbor: By staying grounded, you provide the safety the client needs to navigate their grief.

The History of the 'Sava‚Äôat' (Ethical Will)

The concept of the ethical will is not a modern invention of the "self-help" era; it is an ancient tradition with roots stretching back over 3,000 years. In the Jewish tradition, this is known as a Sava‚Äôat (from the Hebrew word for "command" or "charge").

Historically, a patriarch or matriarch would gather their family at the end of their life to deliver a verbal "deathbed testament." They didn't talk about their land or their gold‚Äîthat was already settled. Instead, they spoke of their faith, their mistakes, and their deepest desires for their children's character. By the Middle Ages, these verbal charges were committed to paper, becoming some of the most cherished family heirlooms in history.

Coach Tip

When introducing this to a client, use the word "gift." Tell them: "We are preparing a gift that won't break, won't lose value, and can be shared by everyone in your family simultaneously." This reframes the task from a "chore" to an act of love.

Legal vs. Ethical Wills: Clarifying the Distinction

It is vital to ensure your client understands that an ethical will is not a legal document. It does not replace a Last Will and Testament, and it should not contain instructions for the distribution of physical assets or financial accounts. Mixing the two can lead to legal confusion or family disputes.

A 2012 study by Allianz found that when asked what was most important to inherit, heirs ranked "family stories and heritage" and "clear instructions on end-of-life wishes" ten times higher than financial assets. This is the "Legacy Gap" that you, as a Doula, help to bridge.

Frameworks for Wisdom-Sharing

Many clients feel overwhelmed by the idea of writing down their "life's wisdom." They may feel their lives weren't "extraordinary" enough to warrant a document. Your role is to provide the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢ framework to extract the gold from their everyday experiences.

The Four Pillars of the Ethical Will

  1. The Roots: Where did I come from? (Ancestry, formative childhood memories, family traditions).
  2. The Hard-Won Wisdom: What did I learn the hard way? (Regrets turned into lessons, overcoming adversity, career shifts).
  3. The Heart: What do I want you to know? (Expressions of love, apologies, forgiveness, gratitude).
  4. The Future: What are my hopes for you? (Dreams for grandchildren, wishes for the family‚Äôs future, philanthropic goals).

Spiritual Legacy Mapping

Spiritual legacy is not necessarily about religion, though it often includes it. It is about the Transcendental Connection‚Äîhow the client sees themselves in relation to the universe, nature, or a higher power. Mapping this helps the client feel that their life has been part of a larger tapestry.

Questions for Spiritual Mapping:

  • "When in your life did you feel most connected to something larger than yourself?"
  • "What 'thin places' (locations where the veil between the physical and spiritual feels thin) have you visited?"
  • "If you could summarize your philosophy of the soul in one sentence, what would it be?"
  • "What spiritual practices sustained you during your darkest hours?"
Coach Tip

If a client is secular, shift the language to "Humanity" or "Nature." Ask: "What footprint are you leaving in the garden of the world?" This allows the spiritual legacy to be inclusive of all belief systems.

Exercises for the Doula: The Legacy Statement

As a practitioner, you must be comfortable with the tools you provide. A "Legacy Statement" is a 1-2 sentence distillation of a person's core purpose. Use the following exercise to help your client (or yourself) find that essence.

The "Three Seeds" Exercise

Ask your client to imagine they are leaving a garden for their family. They can only plant three seeds (values). Which three do they choose?
Example: Curiosity, Kindness, and Resilience.

Once the seeds are chosen, help them expand:
"I leave you the seed of Curiosity because it was the curiosity to change careers at 45 that finally brought me joy. Never stop asking 'What if?'"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Legal Will and an Ethical Will?

A Legal Will focuses on the distribution of physical assets and valuables (wealth), while an Ethical Will focuses on the distribution of values, wisdom, beliefs, and life lessons (legacy). The former is legally binding, while the latter is a personal/spiritual document.

2. What does the ancient Hebrew term 'Sava‚Äôat' translate to, and what was its original form?

'Sava‚Äôat' translates to "command" or "charge." Its original form was a verbal "deathbed testament" delivered to the family to guide their future character and faith.

4. Why is it important for a Doula to keep Ethical Wills separate from Legal Wills?

To avoid legal confusion or family disputes. Mixing personal wisdom with legal instructions can complicate the probate process or lead to unintended legal interpretations of the client's wishes.

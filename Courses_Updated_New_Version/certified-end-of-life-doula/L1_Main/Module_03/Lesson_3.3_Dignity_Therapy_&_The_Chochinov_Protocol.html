The Three Pillars of Chochinov‚Äôs Dignity Model

Dr. Harvey Max Chochinov‚Äôs research revealed that "dignity" is not a vague concept but a measurable state of being influenced by specific factors. His model provides a roadmap for the Doula to identify where a client‚Äôs sense of self may be fracturing.

Pillar Focus Area Doula Observation Point
Illness-Related Concerns Level of independence and symptom distress. Is the client feeling like a "burden" due to physical needs?
Dignity Conserving Perspectives Internal psyche, hope, and "spiritual resilience." Does the client still see themselves as the person they were before the illness?
Social Dignity Inventory External factors, how others treat the client. Is the medical team or family treating the client as a "patient" or a person?
Coach Tip

When you notice a client‚Äôs dignity is low, look at the Social Dignity Inventory. Often, simply changing how a family interacts with the client (e.g., asking for the client's advice on family matters) can restore more dignity than any medical intervention.

The Dignity Therapy Protocol: 11 Core Questions

The protocol is designed to be a semi-structured interview. While you may adapt the language, the core intent of these questions has been validated in over 20 years of clinical research to elicit generativity‚Äîthe desire to leave something of oneself behind.

  1. Tell me a little about your life history... (Focusing on parts they remember most).
  2. When did you feel most alive?
  3. Are there specific things you want your family to know about you?
  4. What are the most important roles you have played in your life?
  5. What are your most important accomplishments?
  6. Are there things you feel still need to be said to your loved ones?
  7. What are your hopes and dreams for your loved ones?
  8. What have you learned about life that you‚Äôd want to pass on?
  9. Are there words of advice or guidance you wish to offer?
  10. Are there things you want to include to help your family prepare?
  11. In creating this permanent record, are there other things you want included?

Case Study: The Quiet Architect

Client: Robert, 68 | Doula: Sarah, 51

Robert, a retired architect with ALS, had become increasingly withdrawn. His wife reported he felt his life was "erased" by his inability to move. Sarah, a former schoolteacher who transitioned to Doula work at age 50, initiated the Chochinov Protocol.

When asked, "What are your most important roles?" Robert didn't mention architecture. He spoke about being a "silent protector" for his younger brother. This revelation shifted the family's view of him from a "patient" back to the "protector," significantly reducing his distress and increasing his engagement with his grandchildren.

The Generativity Document

The "product" of Dignity Therapy is the Generativity Document. This is a transcribed, edited, and polished version of the interview that is read back to the client for approval before being gifted to the family.

The 3-Step Creation Process:

  • Recording: Use a high-quality digital recorder. Ensure the environment is quiet. Focus on the client‚Äôs voice, minimizing your own interjections.
  • Transcription: Transcribe the interview verbatim initially. Many modern Doulas use AI transcription tools (like Otter.ai) as a starting point, but manual "cleaning" is required.
  • Editing for Flow: Remove "ums," "ahs," and repetitive stutters. Reorganize the thoughts into a narrative structure while keeping the client's unique "voice" intact.
Coach Tip

Transcription can be time-consuming. Experienced practitioners often charge a premium for the "Legacy Package" (ranging from $500 to $1,500), which covers the interview, editing, and professional binding of the document. This is a significant income stream for Doulas who enjoy the writing process.

Measuring the Impact: The Data

Dignity Therapy isn't just "nice to have." A 2011 randomized controlled trial published in The Lancet Oncology involving 326 patients found significant clinical benefits.

  • 91% of participants reported feeling satisfied or highly satisfied with the therapy.
  • 76% reported an increased sense of dignity.
  • 81% felt the document would be helpful to their family.
  • Significantly improved the "will to live" and decreased depressive symptoms compared to standard palliative care.

Practical Doula Adaptation

While the clinical protocol is rigid, the Doula‚Äôs environment is fluid. You may not get through all 11 questions in one sitting. In fact, it is often better to spread them over 2-3 visits to prevent client fatigue.

Coach Tip

Always have a "legacy kit" ready: a high-quality recorder, a backup battery, a notebook for non-verbal cues (tears, smiles, long pauses), and a box of tissues. Being prepared allows you to stay fully present in the Supportive Presence (S) of the SOLACE method.

Ethical Boundaries & Best Practices

As a Doula, you are a facilitator, not a ghostwriter. You must resist the urge to "beautify" the client's life or hide their truths. If a client wants to express regret or an apology, it belongs in the document. However, if a client uses the document to "attack" a family member, you must gently redirect them using therapeutic communication techniques learned in Lesson 2.

Coach Tip

If a client becomes too fatigued to continue, stop immediately. The process of Life Review is energetically taxing. One high-quality 20-minute session is better than a forced hour of conversation.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which pillar of the Dignity Model focuses on how the client perceives their own internal psyche and spiritual resilience?

The Dignity Conserving Perspectives pillar. This focuses on internal factors like hope, maintaining a sense of self, and spiritual fortitude.

2. What is the primary "output" or physical gift created through the Dignity Therapy protocol?

The Generativity Document. This is the edited, transcribed record of the client's words intended to be passed down to their loved ones.

3. According to the 2011 Lancet study, what percentage of patients felt that Dignity Therapy would be helpful to their families?

81%. This high percentage underscores the legacy-building value of the intervention for the family's bereavement process.

4. If a client is too weak for a full interview, what is the best adaptation for a Doula?

Break the 11 questions into multiple short sessions (e.g., 2-3 questions per visit) to prevent fatigue while still maintaining the protocol's integrity.

Key Takeaways

  • Dignity Therapy is a validated clinical protocol that bolsters the "will to live" in terminally ill clients.
  • The Chochinov Model identifies three key areas (Illness-Related, Internal Perspective, and Social Inventory) that influence a person's dignity.
  • The 11 core questions are designed to move a client from "patient status" to "legacy-leaver."
  • Creating the Generativity Document is a professional skill that adds significant value (and potential income) to your Doula practice.
  • Preparation and presence are key; the Doula facilitates the voice of the client without overriding it.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced practitioners can stumble. Keep these four common pitfalls in mind as you start your practice:

Mistake Why it Happens The Professional Correction
Giving Medical Advice Wanting to be "helpful" or "fix" the physical pain. Always refer to the medical team for dosages or diagnoses. Say: "Thatâ€™s a great question for the hospice nurse; let's write it down."
Over-Promising Feeling the need to justify your fee. Be clear about your scope. You provide presence and planning, not "cures" or "guaranteed peaceful deaths."
Taking on the Family's Grief Lack of professional boundaries. Remember: You are the vessel, not the sponge. Use your grounding exercises from Module 9.
Skipping the Contract Feeling like "it's too personal" for a contract. A contract protects both you and the family. Use the DFY template provided in your portal.

Success Markers for Your First Session

How do you know you've done a good job? In my 15 years of practice, these are the signs I look for:

  • Visible Relaxation: The client's shoulders drop, or they take a deep sigh of relief during the intake.
  • Clarity of Role: The family understands exactly what you do (and don't do).
  • The "Next Step" is Set: You leave the session with a clear follow-up date and a specific task completed (like an advance directive draft).
  • Trust is Established: The client shares a personal fear or story they haven't told the medical staff.
Emma's Coaching Tip

Your goal isn't to be "perfect." Your goal is to be present. If you make a mistake, acknowledge it, correct it, and move forward. Families value authenticity over a polished "performance."

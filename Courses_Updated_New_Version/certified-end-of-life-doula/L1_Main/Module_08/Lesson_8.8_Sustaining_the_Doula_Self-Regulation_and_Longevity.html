The S.O.L.A.C.E. Coaching Script

Practice these lines out loud. Notice how each section corresponds to a phase of the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢.

YOU (Supportive Presence):
"Margaret, I can hear how much you love your father in the way you describe his care. Before we talk about logistics, I want to acknowledge how much you are carrying right now. It is a lot for one person, and it‚Äôs okay to feel overwhelmed."
YOU (Observation):
"You mentioned that Arthur seems 'restless' in the evenings. When that happens, what have you noticed about the environment? Is there music playing, or perhaps a lot of family activity in the room?"
YOU (Advocacy):
"It sounds like you have questions for the hospice nurse that aren't getting answered during their short visits. One of the things we can do is create a 'Communication Log' so that your most important questions are ready the moment they walk in the door."
YOU (Comfort Measures):
"Beyond the medication he‚Äôs receiving, we can look at non-medical ways to ease his restlessness‚Äîthings like gentle touch, aromatherapy, or even just sitting in vigil so you can take a nap. Does that sound like something that would help you both?"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

New coaches often fall into these traps. Being aware of them is the first step toward professional mastery.

  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Trying to solve Margaret's problems immediately instead of just holding space for her feelings.
  • Medical Overreach: Giving advice on medication dosages. Remember: Always refer medical questions back to the hospice team.
  • Filling the Silence: Talking too much because silence feels uncomfortable. Silence is often where the client finds their truth.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much jargon about the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢ instead of showing how it benefits the client.
Emma's Insight

If you feel the urge to "fix" everything, take a deep breath. Your presence is the medicine. You aren't there to change the outcome, but to change the experience of the outcome.

Success Markers

How do you know if your session with Margaret went well? Look for these indicators:

1

The "Exhale" Moment

The client physically relaxes their shoulders or takes a deep sigh of relief during the call.

2

Shift in Focus

The client moves from talking only about "tasks" to talking about "meaning" and "connection."

3

Clear Boundaries

The client understands exactly what you do (comfort/advocacy) and what you don't do (medical care).

Pro Tip

End every session by asking: "Of everything we discussed today, what is the one thing that feels most helpful to you?" This reinforces your value and helps you plan the next session.

Check Your Understanding

1. If Margaret asks you, "How much morphine should I give him for this breathing?" what is the correct S.O.L.A.C.E. response?

You must redirect to the medical team. A proper response: "That is a very important medical question. Let's write that down in our Advocacy Log right now so we can ask the hospice nurse the moment they arrive. My role is to help you manage the environment and your own stress while we wait for their guidance."

2. Which part of the S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢ involves identifying environmental triggers like loud music or bright lights?

This falls under Observation (O). As a doula, you use your "holistic lens" to see things the medical team might miss during their brief check-ins.

3. Why is it important to record yourself practicing the script?

Recording allows you to hear your "pacing." In end-of-life work, a rushed or anxious voice can increase the client's stress. Listening back helps you cultivate a "Supportive Presence" (S) through a calm, steady vocal tone.

4. True or False: Success in a doula session means the client is no longer sad.

False. Success means the client feels supported and heard within their sadness. We are not there to remove the grief, but to ensure the client isn't navigating it alone and without a plan.

Key Takeaways

  • The S.O.L.A.C.E. Method‚Ñ¢ is a circular framework, not a linear one; you may move between phases several times in one call.
  • Your primary "tool" is your presence; the script is merely the vehicle for that presence.
  • Professionalism is maintained by strictly adhering to your non-medical scope of practice.
  • Practice out loud is the only way to overcome imposter syndrome and build genuine confidence.
  • Success is measured by the client's increased sense of agency and emotional relief.

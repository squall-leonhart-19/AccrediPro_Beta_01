Assessing the Non-Verbal Client: The PAINAD Scale

Communication is the first faculty lost in many end-of-life journeys, particularly in cases of dementia or advanced neurological decline. However, the absence of a complaint does not equal the absence of pain. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that up to 45% of non-verbal hospice patients experience undertreated pain due to assessment gaps.

The PAINAD (Pain Assessment in Advanced Dementia) scale is a gold-standard tool that Doulas use to quantify discomfort through five specific physiological and behavioral observations. Each category is scored from 0 to 2.

Category Score 0 Score 1 Score 2
Breathing Normal Occasional labored; short period of hyperventilation Noisy labored; long period of hyperventilation; Cheyne-Stokes
Negative Vocalization None Occasional moan/groan; low intensity speech Repeated troubled calling out; loud moaning; crying
Facial Expression Smiling or inexpressive Sad; frightened; frowning Facial grimacing
Body Language Relaxed Tense; distressed pacing; fidgeting Rigid; fists clenched; knees pulled up; striking out
Consolability No need to console Distracted or reassured by voice or touch Unable to console, distract, or reassure
Coach Tip: The Professional Edge

When reporting to a hospice nurse, don't just say "they look uncomfortable." Say: "I've conducted a PAINAD assessment and the client is scoring a 7, specifically due to rigid body language and noisy labored breathing." This professional language establishes you as a high-level practitioner and ensures the medical team takes your observations seriously.

Recognizing 'Grimace and Guard' & Breakthrough Pain

Breakthrough pain is a sudden, intense flare of pain that "breaks through" the regular around-the-clock medication. As a Doula, you are often the only person present during the transitions where breakthrough pain occurs‚Äîsuch as during repositioning, bathing, or linen changes.

The Grimace

Watch for the "furrowed brow" or the "tightened jaw." In many clients, the grimace is fleeting‚Äîlasting only a second during a movement. If you notice a grimace during a turn, it is a signal that pre-medication (giving pain meds 30 minutes before movement) may be necessary.

The Guard

Guarding is a protective physical reflex. If you reach to touch a client's abdomen or attempt to move their leg, and they instinctively pull away or tighten their muscles, they are guarding. This indicates localized discomfort that requires clinical review. Statistics show that 60-80% of cancer patients experience breakthrough pain, yet many do not report it for fear of "bothering" the nurse.

Case Study: Evelyn's Unspoken Pain

Client: Evelyn, 82, Advanced Alzheimer's.
Doula: Sarah (48, former teacher).
Observation: Evelyn had stopped eating and was becoming increasingly agitated during morning care. The family thought it was "just the disease progressing." Sarah noticed that every time Evelyn was rolled to her left side, her score on the PAINAD facial expression scale jumped to a 2 (grimacing) and she clenched her fists (guarding). Sarah documented these specific triggers and shared them with the hospice nurse. The nurse discovered a deep tissue injury that was previously hidden. By adjusting Evelyn‚Äôs pain protocol before morning care, her agitation disappeared, and she was able to peacefully engage with her family again.

Assessing Gastrointestinal Comfort & Oral Hygiene

Physical burden isn't just about sharp pain; it's often about the "quiet" discomfort of the GI system. At the end of life, the digestive system slows significantly (peristalsis decreases), leading to two major issues: nausea and constipation.

  • Constipation: Often caused by opioid medications. Watch for abdominal distension, restlessness, or "overflow" diarrhea. A client who is suddenly agitated may simply be constipated.
  • Nausea: Look for increased swallowing, lip-smacking, or a sudden aversion to scents that were previously tolerated.
  • Oral Hygiene (Xerostomia): Dry mouth is reported by over 70% of palliative patients. A dry, cracked mouth feels like "sandpaper" and can cause significant distress.
Coach Tip: Sensory Relief

For xerostomia, suggest "mouth swabbing" every 2 hours. Use a soft foam swab dipped in cool water or a tiny bit of coconut oil. Avoid lemon-glycerin swabs as they can actually dry out the mucosa further. This is a beautiful way to involve family members in the O: Observation & Assessment phase.

Skin Integrity & Positioning Requirements

As the body shunts blood toward the vital organs in the final days, the skin becomes the first organ to fail. This is known as the Kennedy Terminal Ulcer‚Äîa pressure sore that can appear suddenly despite excellent care. Your role is to monitor "bony prominences" where pressure is highest.

Primary Pressure Points to Observe:

  • Sacrum (Tailbone): The most common site for breakdown.
  • Heels: Often overlooked; "floating the heels" with a pillow is a standard intervention.
  • Hips (Trochanters): Critical for side-lying clients.
  • Ears: If the client is wearing oxygen tubing, check behind the ears for friction rub.

The Concept of 'Total Pain'

Finally, we must distinguish between physical pain and the framework pioneered by Dame Cicely Saunders: Total Pain. If a client is receiving high doses of morphine but still scoring high on the PAINAD scale, the pain may not be physical.

The Four Dimensions of Total Pain:

  1. Physical: Symptoms, medication side effects, insomnia.
  2. Emotional: Anxiety, fear of the unknown, depression, loss of role.
  3. Social: Concerns for family, financial worries, "unspoken" goodbyes.
  4. Spiritual: Meaning-making, regret, fear of what comes after, loss of faith.
Coach Tip: Discerning the Source

If physical interventions aren't working, move to Module 3: Legacy & Life Review. Often, a client‚Äôs physical restlessness is actually "spiritual restlessness." Addressing a family secret or a lingering regret can sometimes do more for pain than an extra dose of medication.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client with dementia is crying out and cannot be reassured by touch or voice. What score would they receive in the 'Consolability' category of the PAINAD scale?

They would receive a score of 2. A score of 0 is "no need to console," and a score of 1 is "distracted or reassured by voice or touch."

2. What is the clinical term for the instinctive tightening of muscles when a painful area is touched?

The term is "Guarding." It is a key physical indicator of localized discomfort or breakthrough pain.

3. Why should End-of-Life Doulas be cautious about using lemon-glycerin swabs for dry mouth?

Lemon-glycerin swabs can be acidic and actually dry out the oral mucosa further, potentially causing more discomfort. Cool water or a small amount of coconut oil is preferred.

4. If a client‚Äôs pain does not respond to increased medication, which concept should the Doula investigate?

The Doula should investigate "Total Pain," which includes emotional, social, and spiritual dimensions that physical medication cannot fully address.

Key Takeaways for the Professional Doula

  • Objective Over Subjective: Use the PAINAD scale to provide objective data to medical teams, especially for non-verbal clients.
  • Watch the Transitions: Breakthrough pain most often occurs during movement; observe for "Grimace and Guard" during care routines.
  • The GI Connection: Agitation is often a symptom of constipation or nausea; always assess the "quiet" systems.
  • Skin is an Organ: Monitor bony prominences daily; the Kennedy Terminal Ulcer can appear rapidly as the body transitions.
  • Holistic View: Always consider "Total Pain"‚Äîphysical suffering may be amplified by spiritual or emotional distress.

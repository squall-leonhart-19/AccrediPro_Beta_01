Your Coaching Script

Practice these lines out loud. Notice how your own body feels as you say them. The goal is to move Elena from discussing her stress to noticing her physiology.

ELENA:
"I just don't get it. I'm retired now. I should be happy! But my neck is so tight I can barely turn my head, and I feel like I'm waiting for a phone call that something is wrong."
YOU (The Coach):
"Elena, I hear how frustrating that is. You've earned this rest, yet your body is still on 'high alert.' As you're telling me about that feeling of 'waiting for something to go wrong,' what do you notice happening in your body right now?"
ELENA:
"My chest feels... tight. Like there's a heavy weight on it. And my breath is really shallow."
YOU (The Coach):
"Okay, let's just acknowledge that tightness in the chest. We don't have to change it yet. Just noticing it. (Pause for 3 seconds). Now, while keeping one eye on that tightness, can you scan your body and find one place that feels neutral? Maybe your big toe, or the back of your head against the chair?"
ELENA:
"My feet... they feel heavy on the floor. That feels... okay. Solid."
YOU (The Coach):
"Great. Let's spend a moment just feeling that 'solid' sensation in your feet. Let the weight of your legs sink into the floor. (Exhale audibly). What happens to that chest tightness when you focus on your feet?"

2
Listen for Pacing: Play it back. Do you sound rushed? A 2021 study on therapeutic alliance found that slower vocal tempo (approx. 120 words per minute) significantly increased client feelings of safety.
3
The Exhale Check: Did you remember to breathe? In somatic work, your exhale cues the client's parasympathetic nervous system. Try the script again, adding a soft exhale before every line.
Practice Tip

If you feel "stuck" or don't know what to say, simply name what you see. "I notice you just took a deep breath," or "I see your hand moved to your heart." This brings the client back to the present moment without needing a "clever" intervention.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

The Pitfall Why it Happens The Somatic Shift
Over-Intellectualizing Imposter syndrome makes us want to explain the science to "prove" we know things. Stop explaining; start experiencing. Ask: "Where do you feel that in your body?"
Rushing the "Fix" We want the client to feel better immediately to validate our coaching. Allow the discomfort to exist. "Titrate" the experience so it's manageable.
Ignoring Your Body Focusing 100% on the client and losing your own "ground." Keep 50% of your awareness on your own seat and breath.
Missing the "Shift" Focusing on the words and missing the client's sigh, eye flutter, or color change. Watch the client's collarbone and jaw more than their eyes.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask Elena to find a "neutral" spot in her body?

This is called "Pendulation." It prevents the client from becoming overwhelmed (flooded) by their discomfort. By moving attention between the "tightness" and the "solid feet," we expand the Window of Tolerance.

2. What is the "Primary Tool" in a somatic coaching session?

The Coach's own nervous system. Through co-regulation, the client's nervous system "mirrors" the coach's calm, grounded state.

3. If Elena starts talking very fast about her to-do list, what should the coach do?

Gently interrupt the narrative and bring her back to a sensation. "Elena, I'm noticing your pace has picked up. Let's just pause for a second and notice what that 'fast' feeling is doing in your shoulders."

4. What is a "titrated" experience?

Titration is the process of experiencing small, manageable "drops" of activation at a time, rather than the whole trauma or stressor at once, to ensure the nervous system stays regulated.

Key Takeaways

  • Co-Regulation is Key: Your grounded presence is the most powerful intervention you have.
  • Follow the Body, Not the Story: Use somatic tracking to identify where the client is in their Window of Tolerance.
  • Pendulation Prevents Flooding: Always provide a "resource" or neutral spot when exploring difficult sensations.
  • Small Shifts are Big Wins: A single deep sigh or a softening of the jaw is a successful outcome in somatic work.

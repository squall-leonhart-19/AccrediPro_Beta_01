The Tripartite Model of Interoception

In conventional wellness, "interoception" is often used as a catch-all term. However, to work at a Level 1 Practitioner standard, we must distinguish between three distinct dimensions of the interoceptive experience. A 2015 study by Garfinkel et al. revolutionized our understanding by showing that a person can be high in one area while being completely deficient in another.

Dimension Definition Example in Session
Interoceptive Accuracy The objective ability to detect internal signals (e.g., heart rate). Client can accurately count their pulse without touching their wrist.
Interoceptive Sensibility Subjective self-evaluation of how "tuned in" one feels. Client says, "I am very sensitive to changes in my digestion."
Interoceptive Awareness Metacognitive awareness; the "insight" into one's own accuracy. Client knows when they are accurately sensing their body vs. when they are guessing.

Many clients, especially high-achieving women in their 40s and 50s, have high Sensibility (they worry about their body) but low Accuracy (they can't actually feel the subtle shifts). Your job is to bridge that gap through the "M" (Map Sensations) phase of the E.M.B.O.D.Y. Method‚Ñ¢.

The Insular Cortex: The Brain's Somatic Map

The insular cortex (or insula) is the primary hub for interoceptive processing. It acts as a bridge between our physiological state and our conscious awareness. When we guide a client to "Map Sensations," we are essentially lighting up the posterior and anterior insula.

A 2021 meta-analysis involving over 8,000 participants confirmed that chronic stress and trauma lead to "gray matter volume reductions" in the insula. This explains why clients with significant trauma history often report a "numb" or "static-filled" internal experience. Through consistent somatic tracking, we leverage neuroplasticity to rebuild these neural pathways, increasing the "resolution" of their internal map.

Mapping 'Numb' or 'Vague' Landscapes

As a practitioner, you will frequently encounter the "Somatic Void." This is not a failure of the method; it is a protective mechanism of the nervous system. When the internal landscape is too painful, the brain "turns down the volume" on interoception.

Techniques for the "M" (Map Sensations) Phase:

  • The Contrast Method: Ask the client to feel the weight of their feet on the floor (external/proprioception) and then notice the temperature of the air in their nostrils (internal/interoception).
  • Mapping the 'Void': If a client says "it's just numb," treat the numbness as a sensation. Ask: "Is the numbness thick like a fog, or hard like a wall?"
  • Somatic Echo: Have the client gently press a hand against their chest and then remove it. Ask them to track the "echo" of the touch as it fades.

In your practice, you may find that specialized somatic sessions focusing on interoception can command higher rates. Practitioners who master "granularity" often see clients for $175-$250 per session because they provide the "missing link" that traditional talk therapy overlooks.

Clinical Application of the MAIA Scale

The Multidimensional Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness (MAIA) is an 8-scale questionnaire used in clinical research to track somatic progress. For Level 1 Practitioners, using a simplified version of this scale can provide "hard data" for clients who need to see their progress on paper.

The eight scales include:

  1. Noticing: Awareness of uncomfortable, comfortable, and neutral body sensations.
  2. Not-Distracting: Tendency not to ignore or distract oneself from sensations of pain or discomfort.
  3. Not-Worrying: Tendency not to worry or experience emotional distress with sensations of pain or discomfort.
  4. Attention Regulation: Ability to sustain and control attention to body sensations.
  5. Emotional Awareness: Awareness of the connection between body sensations and emotional states.
  6. Self-Regulation: Ability to regulate distress by attention to body sensations.
  7. Body Listening: Active listening to the body for insight.
  8. Trusting: Experience of one's body as safe and trustworthy.
Coach Tip

Use the "Trusting" scale as your North Star. Most clients come to you because they view their body as a "traitor" or a "broken machine." Every increment of interoceptive granularity is a step toward Somatic Trust.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which brain region is considered the "primary hub" for interoceptive processing?

The Insular Cortex (or Insula). It integrates physiological signals from the body and translates them into conscious feelings.

2. What is the difference between Interoceptive Accuracy and Interoceptive Sensibility?

Accuracy is the objective ability to detect signals (like heartbeat), while Sensibility is the subjective self-belief or report of how sensitive one feels to their body.

3. How should a practitioner respond when a client says they feel "nothing" in their body?

Validate the "nothing" as a somatic state (Dorsal Vagal shutdown) and ask the client to map the quality of the nothingness (e.g., is it heavy, dark, or static-filled?) or find the "edge" where numbness meets sensation.

4. Why is the "Trusting" scale in the MAIA so vital for trauma recovery?

Because trauma often creates a sense of "internal betrayal." Moving from fearing body signals to trusting them is the ultimate goal of somatic integration and safety.

Your Coaching Script

Read this dialogue out loud. Focus on your pacing. In somatic work, we speak slower than in normal conversation to give the client's nervous system time to catch up.

The Somatic Pause & Resource Dialogue

YOU:
"Diane, I'm noticing that as you talk about the stress at the school, your breathing has become a bit shorter and your shoulders have moved up toward your ears. Are you noticing that too?"
DIANE:
"Oh, I didn't even realize. I guess they are. I'm just so used to it, I don't really feel my shoulders anymore."
YOU:
"That makes so much sense. Your body has been working very hard to protect you. Let's just pause the story for a moment. Right now, in this room, can you find one place in your body that feels even 1% more neutral or comfortable than your shoulders? Maybe your big toe, or the back of your head against the chair?"
DIANE:
(Pauses for 5 seconds) "My feet. They feel heavy on the floor. That feels... solid."
YOU:
"Beautiful. Let's just stay with that 'solid' feeling in your feet for three breaths. Thereâ€™s no rush. Just noticing the floor holding your feet."
DIANE:
(Takes a deep, spontaneous sigh) "I didn't realize how much I was holding my breath."

Practice Exercises

1

The Mirror Practice

Stand in front of a mirror and read your lines. Watch your own shoulders. If you are tense, Diane will be tense. Practice softening your gaze as you speak the words: "There's no rush."

2

The Voice Memo Challenge

Record yourself reading the "YOU" lines. Listen back. Are you speaking too fast? Professional somatic practitioners usually speak at about 60-70% of their normal conversational speed during resourcing.

3

The "1% Neutral" Prompt

Practice asking three different people (friends or family) to find "one place that feels neutral." Notice how long it takes them to find it. Practice waiting in the silence without interrupting them.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It Happens The Somatic Correction
Filling the Silence Coach feels "imposter syndrome" and thinks they aren't providing value if they aren't talking. Trust the silence. Silence is where the client's nervous system processes the shift.
Focusing on the Pain First Wanting to "fix" the problem (the knot) immediately. Always build a Resource (the feet/the floor) before touching the "knot."
Using Too Much Logic Explaining the neuroscience of the amygdala for 10 minutes. Keep explanations brief. Shift back to: "What are you noticing in your body right now?"
Rushing the Breath Telling the client to "take a deep breath." Never force a breath. Instead, invite the client to notice their feet, and the breath will often deepen naturally.

Markers of Success

How do you know the session with Diane went well? Look for these Somatic Markers:

  • Spontaneous Sighing: A sign the autonomic nervous system is shifting from Sympathetic (fight/flight) to Parasympathetic (rest/digest).
  • Skin Color Changes: A slight flushing or pinkness in the face, indicating improved blood flow.
  • Eye Softening: The client's gaze moves from a "hard focus" to a "soft, wider focus."
  • Slower Speech: The client begins to match your slower, grounded pace.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask Diane to find a "neutral" place in her body before addressing her "tight knot"?

This is called Resourcing. If we go straight to the pain, the nervous system may feel overwhelmed and tighten further. Resourcing creates a "safe harbor" so the body feels capable of processing the tension.

2. What is the recommended conversational speed for a somatic practitioner during a resourcing exercise?

Approximately 60-70% of normal conversational speed. This "somatic pacing" models regulation and gives the client's slower biological processes time to respond.

3. If Diane says, "I can't find a single place that feels good," how should you respond?

Validate her experience first. Then, lower the bar: "Thatâ€™s okay. Can you find a place that feels just 1% less intense? Or perhaps notice the sensation of your clothing against your arm?" We are looking for "neutral," not necessarily "good."

4. Which of the following is a physical marker of nervous system regulation?

Spontaneous sighing, softening of the jaw, skin flushing, and slower, more rhythmic speech are all primary markers of regulation.

Key Takeaways

  • Presence is the Intervention: Your grounded state is more important than your perfect words.
  • Resource First: Always establish a sense of safety or neutrality in the body before exploring areas of tension.
  • Slow is Fast: By slowing down the pace, you allow for deeper, more permanent neurological shifts.
  • Observe the Markers: Watch for sighs, shifts in color, and muscle softening as indicators of progress.
  • You are Ready: Mastery comes through practice. Use these scripts until they feel like your own voice.

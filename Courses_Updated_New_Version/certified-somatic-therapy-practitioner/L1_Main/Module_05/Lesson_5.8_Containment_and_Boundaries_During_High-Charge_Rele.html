Common Mistakes to Avoid

When you're starting out, it's easy to fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to mastery:

  • Rushing the release: Discharge takes time. If you push the client to "shake harder," you might trigger a re-traumatization rather than a release.
  • Over-explaining: During the "D" phase, stay out of the "thinking brain." Keep your instructions short and focused on sensation.
  • Fixing instead of facilitating: You aren't "fixing" Linda. You are providing the safe container for her body to fix itself.
  • Ignoring your own body: If you are tense while Linda is discharging, she will feel it. Remember to breathe and ground yourself.

Check Your Understanding

1. Linda mentions a "tremble" in her arms during the pushing exercise. What should your response be?

Encourage her to stay with it. Explain that the tremble is a sign of the nervous system discharging stored energy and reassure her that she is safe.

2. What is the primary difference between "fixing" and "facilitating" in this lab?

Fixing implies the coach is doing the work to change the client. Facilitating means the coach creates the safe environment (the "container") so the client's own biological impulses can complete the release.

3. Why is "stomach gurgling" considered a success marker?

It indicates a shift from the Sympathetic (fight/flight) nervous system to the Parasympathetic (rest/digest) system, showing the body has moved out of a high-arousal state.

4. If a client feels "silly," which part of the brain is likely interfering?

The Neocortex (the thinking/judgmental brain). Somatic work requires moving into the Subcortical/Limbic brain where the body's wisdom resides.

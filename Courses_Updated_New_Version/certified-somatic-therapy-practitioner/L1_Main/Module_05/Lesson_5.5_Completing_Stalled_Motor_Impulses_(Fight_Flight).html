They are stalled motor impulses. In this lesson, we will master the art of identifying these micro-movements and facilitating their safe completion. This is one of the most transformative skills in somatic therapy, as it moves a client from the feeling of "helplessness" to "empowerment."

Identifying 'Pre-Reflexive' Movements

When the nervous system encounters a threat, it prepares the body for action long before the conscious mind is aware of it. If that action‚Äîfighting back or running away‚Äîis blocked, the energy is "locked" into the musculature as chronic bracing patterns.

As a practitioner, you are looking for the "ghost" of that original impulse. These are often small, involuntary movements known as pre-reflexive impulses. They are the body's way of saying, "I still need to do this."

Practitioner Insight

Don't look for large movements initially. Watch for the subtle: a tightening of the jaw, a slight lift of the shoulder, or the toes curling. When you see these, invite the client to "Notice what your hand/foot wants to do right now." This brings the implicit impulse into explicit awareness.

The 'Pushing' Technique: Resolving Fight Energy

The "Fight" response isn't always about aggression; it's about boundary setting and self-protection. When a client was unable to push away a threat (physical or emotional), they may carry tension in the chest, shoulders, and triceps.

Facilitating the Push

The goal is not a workout; it is a somatic completion. We use a wall or a firm bolster to provide the resistance the body never got to meet.

  • Preparation: Have the client stand or sit facing a wall or holding a firm pillow/bolster.
  • Slowing Down: Ask them to bring their hands to the surface very slowly.
  • Meeting Resistance: As they begin to push, encourage them to feel the power traveling from their feet, through their core, and out through their arms.
  • The Release: Often, as the "push" completes, the client will experience a deep sigh, a tremor, or a sudden sense of relief.

Completing the 'Flight' Response

The "Flight" impulse is typically stored in the legs, hips, and psoas. This manifests as "restless leg syndrome," chronic anxiety, or a constant urge to "keep busy" to outrun an internal sense of dread.

Impulse Type Somatic Indicators Completion Technique
Fight (Arm-bound) Clenched fists, tight jaw, shoulder bracing. Wall pushing, pillow squeezing, slow-motion punching.
Flight (Leg-bound) Bouncing heels, hip tension, shallow breathing. Slow-motion running in place, pushing feet against a wall.
Freeze (Global) Cold extremities, numbness, fixed gaze. Micro-movements, titration back to Fight/Flight first.

The Slow-Motion Run

To complete a flight impulse, we don't want the client to sprint. High-speed movement can actually re-trigger the sympathetic nervous system into a panic state. Instead, we use extreme slow motion to allow the brain to register every muscle fiber engaging and completing the act of "getting away."

Professional tip

If a client is lying down, have them "run" against your hands (if appropriate and with consent) or against a bolster. The resistance is key‚Äîit gives the nervous system the feedback that the "escape" was successful.

Restoring the 'Sense of Agency'

Trauma is defined by helplessness. When we are overwhelmed, our "Agency"‚Äîthe belief that we can influence our environment and protect ourselves‚Äîis shattered. This is the root of the "imposter syndrome" many of your clients (and perhaps you!) may feel when pivoting to a new career.

By completing these motor impulses, we are rewiring the Periaqueductal Gray (PAG) and the Motor Cortex. We are telling the brain: "You are no longer trapped. You have the power to act."

Empowerment Note

As a career changer, you might feel "stuck" in old patterns. Practicing these completions yourself can help you move from the "stalled" energy of your previous career into the active, empowered role of a Somatic Practitioner.

Safe Use of Props

To facilitate discharge safely, you must ensure the client does not injure themselves or "over-arouse" their system. Use the following guidelines for props:

  • Bolsters/Pillows: Use these for "squeezing" or "pushing" to provide a soft but firm resistance.
  • The Wall: The ultimate tool for grounding and pushing. Ensure the client's feet are firmly planted to prevent slipping.
  • Resistance Bands: Excellent for leg-bound flight energy, allowing the client to feel the "pull" of the movement.
Safety First

Always titrate. If a client starts pushing with 100% intensity immediately, invite them to start at 10% and slowly build. We want conscious completion, not mindless exertion.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "slow motion" preferred over fast movement when completing a flight impulse?

Fast movement can keep the client in a high-arousal "panic" state. Slow motion allows the brain to consciously track the completion of the motor cycle, providing the feedback necessary to down-regulate the nervous system.

2. What is a "pre-reflexive" movement?

A small, involuntary twitch or micro-urge (like curling toes or fingers) that indicates the body is preparing for a defensive action that was previously stalled.

3. How does motor completion affect a client's "Sense of Agency"?

It shifts the internal narrative from "I was helpless" to "I am capable of taking action," effectively rewiring the brain's perception of its own power and safety.

4. Where is "Flight" energy most commonly stored in the body?

In the lower body‚Äîspecifically the legs, hips, and psoas muscles.

Key Takeaways

  • Stalled motor impulses are "locked" energy from incomplete defensive actions (Fight/Flight).
  • Identifying micro-movements is the first step in bringing implicit impulses to conscious completion.
  • The "Pushing" technique resolves Fight energy by providing the resistance the body never met during the original event.
  • Completing Flight impulses through slow-motion leg work restores the nervous system's sense of safety and escape.
  • Restoring "Agency" through physical completion is a primary goal of the Somatic Therapy Practitioner.

Coach Tip: Soft Eyes

When observing Elena, practice "Soft Eyes." Instead of staring intensely at her (which can feel threatening), relax your gaze to take in her whole form. This allows you to notice peripheral movements like a foot tapping or a hand clenching.

Coach Tip: The Power of "I'm Noticing"

Always start your observations with "I'm noticing..." or "I'm wondering if..." This keeps the power with the client and prevents you from sounding like an "expert" who is diagnosing them.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced practitioners can fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to mastery.

  • Interpreting instead of Observing: Saying "You look sad" (Interpretation) vs. "I notice the corners of your mouth are turning down" (Observation).
  • Missing the Micro-Movements: Focusing only on big movements and missing the tiny "tells" like a finger twitch or a change in skin color (flushing).
  • Jumping to "Fixing": Asking the client to "take a deep breath" the moment you see tension. In the "O" phase, we just observe. Fixing comes later in the EMBODY method.
  • Projecting Your Own State: If you are stressed, you might "see" stress in the client that isn't there. Always ground yourself first.
Coach Tip: Self-Regulation

Before every session, spend 60 seconds doing a "Body Scan" on yourself. If you are holding tension in your jaw, you might miss the subtle jaw tension in your client.

Success Markers

How do you know you're becoming a master of somatic observation? Look for these signs:

  • The "Somatic Sigh": When you name an observation correctly, the client often let's out a spontaneous, deep exhaleâ€”a sign of the nervous system feeling "seen."
  • Client Self-Correction: The client starts noticing their own patterns before you do. "Oh, I'm doing that thing with my shoulders again!"
  • Deepened Trust: The client shares more vulnerable information because they feel you are truly present with them.
  • Professional Confidence: You no longer feel like an "imposter" because you are relying on objective data (the body) rather than just "guessing" how the client feels.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which of the following is an objective somatic observation?

"I notice your right foot is tapping rapidly" is an objective observation. Saying "You seem impatient" is an interpretation.

2. What is the primary purpose of the "Soft Eyes" technique?

To take in the client's entire somatic field and peripheral movements without creating a "threatening" or overly intense gaze.

3. Why do we avoid "fixing" the pattern immediately in the Observe phase?

Because the goal of the "O" phase is to build the client's self-awareness (interoception). Jumping to a fix can bypass the learning process and make the client dependent on the coach.

4. Elena clears her throat every time she mentions her divorce. What is this called in somatic therapy?

This is part of her "Somatic Signature"â€”a recurring physical pattern linked to a specific emotional or psychological stressor.

Key Takeaways

  • Observation is about witnessing, not diagnosing or fixing.
  • Use "I'm noticing..." to maintain a collaborative, non-judgmental atmosphere.
  • Objective data (the body) is more reliable than subjective narrative (the story).
  • Mastering the "O" in EMBODY builds your professional legitimacy and client results.
  • Practice out loud to turn these concepts into professional coaching skills.

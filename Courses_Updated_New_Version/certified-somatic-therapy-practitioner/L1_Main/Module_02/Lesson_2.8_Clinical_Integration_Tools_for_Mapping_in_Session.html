The Mapping Script

Practice these words out loud. Your tone should be slow, rhythmic, and "low and slow"‚Äîa term we use to help regulate the client's nervous system through co-regulation.

YOU (The Coach):
"Elena, I hear how much this contract is weighing on your mind. You mentioned earlier a feeling of 'tightness' in your chest. If it's okay with you, let's pause the story for just a moment and see if we can locate that tightness right now."
ELENA:
"I mean, it's just there. It's like a weight. I just need to figure out how to finish the proposal so I can relax."
YOU:
"I understand the urge to finish and find relief. But let's try something different. Close your eyes if that feels safe, or just soften your gaze. Bring your breath to that 'weight' in your chest. If that weight had a shape, what would it look like?"
ELENA:
"A shape? I don't know... maybe a gray stone? It feels heavy and cold."
YOU:
"A gray stone. Heavy and cold. Thank you for that. Now, notice the edges of that stone. Where does the stone end and the rest of your body begin? Is there any part of you right now‚Äîmaybe your feet on the floor or your hands in your lap‚Äîthat doesn't feel like a stone?"
The "Why"

By asking about the "edges" and finding a part that doesn't feel like the stone, you are teaching Elena Pendulation. This prevents her from being overwhelmed by the sensation and helps her map the boundaries of her stress.

Common Pitfalls for New Practitioners

When you're transitioning from a career like nursing or teaching, you're often used to "fixing" things quickly. In Somatic Therapy, we move at the speed of safety, not the speed of the clock.

  • Fixing instead of Mapping: Trying to make the "gray stone" go away before the client has even fully felt it.
  • Leading the Witness: Saying "Does it feel like a sharp pain?" instead of "How would you describe the quality of that sensation?"
  • Ignoring the Coach's Body: If you are holding your breath while Elena describes her chest tightness, she will feel your tension. Remember to map your sensations too.

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena says, "I just feel really overwhelmed." What is the best somatic follow-up question?

"I hear that you're overwhelmed. If that overwhelm had a location in your body right now, where would you feel it most?" (This moves her from a label to a map).

2. Why do we ask the client to describe the "edges" of a sensation?

To establish boundaries. This helps the client realize they ARE NOT the sensation; they are the container HOLDING the sensation. It prevents emotional flooding.

3. If a client says "I'm just numb, I feel nothing," what is the somatic approach?

Validate the numbness as a protective response. Ask: "Can you describe the quality of the numbness? Is it heavy like a blanket, or empty like space?"

4. What is the primary purpose of the "M: Map Sensations" phase?

To build interoceptive literacy and help the client differentiate between thoughts/stories and actual physiological feedback.

Key Takeaways

  • Language Matters: Use open-ended prompts like "What is the quality of..." or "Where do you notice..."
  • Slow Down: Silence is where the client's brain actually does the mapping work. Don't fear it.
  • Pendulation: Always help the client find a "neutral" or "safe" spot in the body to balance out intense sensations.
  • You Are a Guide: You don't need to "know" what the sensation means; you just need to help the client see it clearly.

The Coaching Script: Guiding the Yield

Practice This Dialogue

Read these lines out loud. Pay attention to the pausesâ€”they are as important as the words.

DIANE:
"I feel a bit buzzy... my heart is racing a little, even though my neck feels looser. Should we keep going?"
YOU:
(Slowly, with a warm tone) "Actually, Diane, the most important work happens right now. That 'buzzy' feeling is just energy looking for a place to land. Let's see if we can give it a landing pad."
YOU:
"Could you just notice the way the chair is holding your back right now? Just feel the contact between your spine and the cushion."
DIANE:
"Okay... I can feel it. It feels a bit hard."
YOU:
"Good. Just notice that hardness. And see if you can let the chair do 10% more of the work of holding you up. You don't have to hold yourself; the chair has you."
YOU:
(Wait 10 seconds) "What do you notice in your breath as you let the chair hold you?"
Maya's Tip: The Power of the Pause

In my early years, I was terrified of silence. I thought if I wasn't talking, I wasn't "coaching." But in somatic work, silence is the container. When you guide a client to yield, give them at least 15-30 seconds of quiet after each cue. This is when the neurobiology actually shifts.

Pro Insight: Yielding vs. Collapsing

Be careful not to confuse yielding with collapsing. Yielding is an active relationship with support; collapsing is a passive "giving up." In a yield, the client remains present and embodied. In a collapse, they often go numb or "check out."

Comparing States: Efforting vs. Yielding

Feature Efforting (High Arousal) Yielding (Integration)
Muscle Tone Hyper-tonic (Tight, ready for action) Eutonic (Balanced, resting but responsive)
Breathing Chest-focused, shallow, or held Diaphragmatic, rhythmic, "sighing"
Gaze Focused, narrow, or darting Soft, peripheral, or gently closed
Nervous System Sympathetic Activation Ventral Vagal Stabilization

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  • The Integration Rush: Moving to the next "technique" because the client is quiet. Remember: The quiet is the technique.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much "brainy" language. Diane doesn't need to hear about the Polyvagal Theory right now; she needs to feel the chair.
  • Mirroring the Anxiety: If Diane feels "buzzy," the coach often starts talking faster. You must remain the "anchor" in the room.
  • Ignoring the Body: Asking "How do you feel?" (which leads to the head) instead of "What do you notice in your contact with the chair?" (which leads to the body).

Check Your Understanding

1. Diane says she feels "buzzy" and wants to know what's next. What is the somatic reason for this "buzzy" feeling?

The "buzzy" feeling is sympathetic nervous system energy that has been mobilized but hasn't yet been integrated or "discharged." It indicates the system is in a state of high arousal and needs a "landing pad" (support/yield) to settle.

2. What is the primary difference between "Yielding" and "Collapsing"?

Yielding is an active, conscious engagement with a support surface where the body remains present. Collapsing is a passive loss of tone where the person often dissociates or "checks out" from the experience.

3. Why should a coach use "containment" language (e.g., "The chair has you") during integration?

Containment language provides an external sense of safety, signaling to the client's amygdala that it is safe to let go of muscular bracing. It shifts the burden of "holding" from the client's muscles to the environment.

4. You notice Diane takes a deep, spontaneous sigh. What should you do as a coach?

Quietly acknowledge it or simply remain silent. A sigh is a primary marker of the nervous system shifting into the parasympathetic (rest/digest) state. It is a sign that integration is successfully occurring.

Key Takeaways

  • Yielding is the Foundation: Integration cannot happen if the body is still bracing or "efforting."
  • Silence is a Tool: Give the nervous system 15-30 seconds to process each somatic cue.
  • Follow the Biology: Watch for sighs, skin color changes, and softened gazes as markers of success.
  • Be the Anchor: Your calm, grounded presence allows the client's nervous system to co-regulate and settle.

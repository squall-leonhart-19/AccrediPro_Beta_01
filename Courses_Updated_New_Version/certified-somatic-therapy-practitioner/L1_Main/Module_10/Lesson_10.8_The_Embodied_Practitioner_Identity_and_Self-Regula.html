Client Profile: Elena, 52

Former Educator turned Corporate Consultant

Background

Elena spent 25 years in the classroom and recently pivoted to a high-pressure consulting role. She feels "constantly on edge" and describes a feeling of "armor" around her neck and shoulders.

Chief Complaint: Elena reports she "can't turn her brain off." She has been trying to use logic to solve her stress, but her body feels like a "coiled spring." She is skeptical but desperate to feel relaxed.

Practitioner Insight: Elena is a "high-functioning freeze" type. She is highly articulate (top-down) but disconnected from her physical sensations below the neck (bottom-up).

Maya's Tip: The Power of the Pause

When working with articulate clients like Elena, they will try to fill every silence with words. Your job is to introduce the Somatic Pause. Before you ask a question, take a visible, deep breath yourself. This signals to her nervous system that it is safe to slow down.

Your Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Imagine Elena is sitting across from you (or on a Zoom screen). Focus on a warm, grounded tone.

The Integration Session

YOU:
"Elena, I hear how much you've been thinking about this transition. You've done a lot of great analytical work. I'm wondering if we could try something a little different for a few minutes. Would you be open to just noticing what's happening in your body as you tell me about that 'coiled spring' feeling?"
ELENA:
"I mean, I can try. But I'm just stressed. I have three deadlines and my boss is... well, you know. I don't see how 'noticing my body' changes my workload."
YOU:
"That makes total sense. The workload is real. The goal here isn't to change the deadlines, but to change how your nervous system is carrying them. Right now, as you mention the word 'deadlines,' I notice your shoulders lifted just a fraction of an inch. Did you feel that, or is that new information?"
ELENA:
"(Pauses, breathes) Oh. I didn't even realize they were up by my ears. They just... always feel like this."
YOU:
"Exactly. That's the 'armor' we talked about. Let's just stay there for a second. No need to force them down. Just acknowledge they are doing a big job of protecting you. What happens if you just send a little breath to that space between your ears and your shoulders?"

Common Mistakes & Professional Pivots

New somatic practitioners often fall into these traps. Use this table to refine your approach.

The "Newbie" Mistake The Professional Somatic Pivot
Over-explaining: Spending 10 minutes explaining polyvagal theory to "convince" the client. Experiential Learning: Giving the client a 30-second experience of a breath or shift, then briefly naming it.
Forcing Relaxation: Telling the client to "Just relax your shoulders." Invitational Language: "What happens if you bring your attention to your shoulders?"
Ignoring the Coach's Body: Getting "heady" along with the client and breathing shallowly. Self-Regulation: Using your own regulated nervous system to co-regulate the client.
Fixating on the "Why": Asking "Why do you think your shoulders are tight?" Focusing on the "How": Asking "How does that tightness feel? Is it heavy, sharp, or buzzy?"
Maya's Tip: Handling "I don't feel anything"

If a client says they feel nothing, don't panic! This is actually "numbness," which is a somatic sensation in itself. Respond with: "That's a perfectly valid place to start. Let's just notice the 'nothingness' or the 'quiet' for a moment. Where in your body do you feel the most quiet?"

Success Markers

How do you know the session went well? Look for these subtle shifts in your client:

  • The Spontaneous Sigh: A deep, involuntary breath that indicates a shift from Sympathetic to Parasympathetic.
  • Softening of the Eyes: The "fixed stare" of stress softens into a more relaxed, present gaze.
  • Color Change: A slight flushing of the skin or "warmth" returning to the face/hands.
  • Slower Speech: The client stops "racing" through their words and begins to speak from a grounded place.
Maya's Tip: Ending the Session

Always end with Integration. Ask: "As we close, what is one small thing your body told you today that you want to remember when you're at your desk tomorrow?" This anchors the somatic work into their daily life.

Check Your Understanding

1. Elena is talking very fast about her boss. What is the most effective somatic intervention?

Model a slow, deep breath yourself and then introduce a "Somatic Pause" by asking her to notice her feet on the floor.

2. Why should you avoid asking "Why are your shoulders tight?"

"Why" questions trigger the analytical brain (top-down), which can pull the client out of their felt experience (bottom-up).

3. What does a "spontaneous sigh" usually indicate in a session?

It indicates a "nervous system discharge" or a shift into the parasympathetic (rest and digest) state.

4. If Elena says "I feel like a coiled spring," how do you deepen the somatic awareness?

Ask her to describe the "qualities" of the springâ€”is it made of metal? Is it hot or cold? Where exactly is the center of the tension?

Key Takeaways

  • Your nervous system is the primary tool; co-regulation happens when you remain grounded.
  • Use invitational language ("What happens if...") rather than directive language ("Do this...").
  • Resistance is not a roadblock; it's a somatic signal that the client needs more safety.
  • Small shifts (a sigh, a softened gaze) are major victories in somatic work.
  • The E.M.B.O.D.Y. Methodâ„¢ provides a roadmap, but the client's body provides the timing.

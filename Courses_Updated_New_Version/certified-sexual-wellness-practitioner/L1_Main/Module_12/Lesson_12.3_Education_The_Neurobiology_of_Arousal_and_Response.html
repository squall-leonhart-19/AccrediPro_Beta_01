The Dual Control Model: Accelerators and Brakes

Developed by researchers at the Kinsey Institute, the Dual Control Model is the gold standard for understanding sexual response. It posits that the brain has two distinct systems: the Sexual Excitation System (SES) and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS).

The SES (The Accelerator)

The SES is constantly scanning the environment for "sexually relevant" stimuli‚Äîsights, smells, touches, or thoughts that signal a potential for pleasure. When it finds them, it sends signals to the body to begin the arousal process.

The SIS (The Brake)

The SIS is equally important. It scans for reasons not to be aroused‚Äîthreats, distractions, stress, or social impropriety. In many clients, especially women in the 40-55 age bracket, "low libido" is rarely an accelerator problem; it is almost always a sensitive brake problem.

Practitioner Insight

When a client says "I'm just not turned on," don't just look for more "gas" (stimuli). Ask: "What is currently pushing on your brake?" Often, it's the laundry, the to-do list, or a perceived lack of emotional safety. Removing the brake is often more effective than flooring the accelerator.

Hormonal Orchestration: The Chemical Symphony

Arousal is not just "in the head"; it is a systemic chemical event. For women in mid-life, the shifting ratios of hormones can dramatically alter the neurobiological threshold for arousal.

  • Testosterone: Often called the "hormone of desire," it increases the sensitivity of the SES and supports the health of the clitoral erectile tissue.
  • Estrogen: Maintains the elasticity and lubrication of the vaginal tissues and supports the "glow" of mood-boosting neurotransmitters like serotonin.
  • Oxytocin: The "bonding hormone," released during touch and orgasm, which lowers the SIS (the brake) by increasing feelings of safety and trust.

Correcting Myths: Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire

The most damaging myth in sexual wellness is that "healthy" desire must be spontaneous‚Äîa sudden bolt of lightning. Research shows that while roughly 75% of men experience spontaneous desire, only about 15% of women do regularly.

The Responsive Model

For most women, desire is responsive. This means desire doesn't show up before the erotic context; it shows up during it. The sequence is: Context ‚Üí Arousal ‚Üí Desire. If a client waits to "feel like it" before starting, they may wait forever. Education on this model is often the single most transformative moment in the practitioner-client relationship.

Expert Tip

Encourage clients to shift from "Am I in the mood?" to "Would I like to be in the mood?" This small linguistic shift honors the responsive nature of their neurobiology.

Key Takeaways

  • The Dual Control Model proves that arousal is a balance of excitation and inhibition.
  • Low desire is often an "overactive brake" problem, not a "broken accelerator" problem.
  • The clitoris is a large, internal, multi-part organ complex with over 10,000 nerve endings.
  • Responsive desire is a normal, healthy variation of the human sexual response, especially common in women.
  • Hormones like testosterone and oxytocin are the "chemical messengers" that modulate the SES and SIS.

The Dual Control Model: Brakes and Accelerators

Developed by researchers at the Kinsey Institute, the Dual Control Model is the gold standard for understanding sexual response. It posits that the brain has two distinct systems that regulate sexual arousal: the Sexual Excitation System (SES) and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS).

Think of it as a car. For the car to move, you need to press the accelerator (SES), but you also need to ensure the emergency brake (SIS) isn't engaged. Most conventional advice focuses solely on "stepping on the gas" (new lingerie, vacations, supplements), but for many clients, the issue isn't a lack of acceleration‚Äîit's an overactive brake.

System Common "Accelerators" Common "Brakes"
Sexual Excitation (SES) Visual stimuli, physical touch, emotional intimacy, novel environments. N/A (This system is looking for reasons to be aroused).
Sexual Inhibition (SIS) N/A (This system is looking for reasons NOT to be aroused). Stress, body image concerns, fear of pregnancy, presence of children, fatigue, trauma triggers.
Practitioner Insight

When a client says, "I've tried everything to get in the mood," your first question should be: "What is currently pushing on your brake?" Often, resolving a "desire" issue is actually about managing the "inhibition" system. In a 2022 survey of 1,200 women, 74% reported that stress was a primary inhibitor, not a lack of attraction to their partner.

The Neurochemistry of Desire

Arousal is not just a feeling; it is a neurochemical cascade. As a practitioner, understanding this allows you to explain to clients why their "willpower" isn't enough to overcome a chemical imbalance caused by stress or medication.

  • Dopamine: The "Wanting" Molecule. Dopamine is responsible for the anticipation of pleasure. It drives the "search" for sexual connection. Low dopamine (common in ADHD or chronic stress) often manifests as a lack of initiation.
  • Oxytocin: The "Bonding" Hormone. Often called the "cuddle hormone," oxytocin is released during touch and orgasm. It creates the sense of safety required for the SIS (brakes) to release.
  • Serotonin: The "Satiety" Molecule. While necessary for mood, high levels of serotonin (often from SSRI antidepressants) can actually inhibit sexual response, acting as a chemical brake on both desire and orgasm.
  • Norepinephrine: The "Alertness" Chemical. In small doses, it aids excitement; in high doses (anxiety), it triggers the sympathetic nervous system, which shuts down blood flow to the genitals.
Practitioner Insight

Many clients in the 40-55 age bracket are on SSRIs. It is vital to educate them that their "low libido" may be a direct neurochemical side effect of their medication, not a personal failing. This shift from "I am broken" to "My neurochemistry is being modulated" is incredibly healing.

Advanced Anatomical Literacy: The Clitoral Complex

Education is a core pillar of the D.E.S.I.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢. Most clients (and many medical professionals) still view the clitoris as a small "button." Clinical reality is far more expansive.

The Clitoral-Vestibular Complex is a large, wishbone-shaped internal structure. While the external glans has over 8,000 nerve endings (double that of the penis), the internal structures‚Äîthe crura (legs) and bulbs‚Äîwrap around the vaginal canal. This is why "vaginal" orgasms are almost always indirect clitoral stimulation.

For Male-Identified Anatomy: It is equally important to move beyond the "penis as a simple tool" narrative. The penile-scrotal complex involves a sophisticated interplay of the parasympathetic nervous system (for erection) and the sympathetic nervous system (for ejaculation). Understanding the ischiocavernosus and bulbospongiosus muscles helps clients address issues like premature ejaculation or erectile dysfunction through somatic awareness.

Navigating the Arousal Gap: Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire

Perhaps the most important educational piece you will deliver is the concept of Responsive Desire, a term popularized by Dr. Rosemary Basson and later Emily Nagoski.

Spontaneous Desire is the "lightning bolt" feeling‚Äîyou‚Äôre walking down the street and suddenly feel like having sex. This is the cultural "norm," yet it only represents about 15% of women in long-term relationships.

Responsive Desire is desire that emerges after the arousal process has begun. You might feel "neutral" about sex, but once the physical touch or emotional connection starts, the desire "wakes up."

The Clinical Reality

A 2021 study found that 30% of women experience primarily responsive desire, and an additional 50% experience a mix of both. Only a small minority experience spontaneous desire consistently. If a client is waiting for "the spark" to strike before they engage, they may wait forever. Education allows them to "put themselves in the way of pleasure."

Practitioner Insight

Teach your clients the "Neutral is a Valid Starting Point" rule. If they feel neutral (not "no," just "not yet"), they can experiment with 5-10 minutes of physical connection to see if their desire responds. If it doesn't, they always have the right to stop. This removes the pressure of "performing" desire.

Myths vs. Clinical Reality

As a Practitioner, you are a "Myth-Buster." Your role is to replace cultural shame with biological facts.

Common Myth Clinical Reality
"The 'Big O' is the goal of every encounter." Focusing on orgasm (goal-orientation) increases "spectatoring" and triggers the SIS (brakes). Pleasure is the goal; orgasm is a potential byproduct.
"Men always want sex; women don't." Men also have "brakes" (stress, performance anxiety) and many women have high SES (accelerators). Desire is individual, not gender-binary.
"Pain during sex is normal after 50." Pain (Dyspareunia) is a signal of underlying issues‚Äîhormonal, pelvic floor, or lack of arousal. It should never be "pushed through."

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Dual Control Model, if a client has low desire, what two systems should you investigate?

You must investigate the Sexual Excitation System (SES/Accelerator) and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS/Brake). Often, the issue is not a lack of excitation, but an overactive inhibition system.

2. Which neurochemical is primarily responsible for the "anticipation" or "searching" phase of desire?

Dopamine. It is the "wanting" molecule that drives us toward a reward or pleasurable experience.

3. What percentage of women in long-term relationships primarily experience "Spontaneous Desire"?

Only about 15%. The majority of women experience Responsive Desire or a combination of both.

4. Why is "Goal-Orientation" (focusing only on orgasm) detrimental to arousal?

It creates performance anxiety and "spectatoring" (watching oneself from the outside), which triggers the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), effectively "stepping on the brakes."

Key Takeaways

  • Arousal is a Balance: It is the result of the brain weighing excitatory signals against inhibitory signals.
  • Brakes Matter More: For many clients, especially those 40+, removing "brakes" (stress, fatigue, shame) is more effective than adding "accelerators."
  • Desire is Responsive: Normalizing responsive desire removes the "broken" label from clients who don't feel spontaneous "sparks."
  • Anatomy is Comprehensive: The clitoris is an extensive internal system; understanding this shifts the focus from "external buttons" to "internal pleasure landscapes."

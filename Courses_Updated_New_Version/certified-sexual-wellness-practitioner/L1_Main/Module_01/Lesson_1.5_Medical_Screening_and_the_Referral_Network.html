Identifying 'Red Flags': When to Refer Out

The hallmark of a premium practitioner is knowing exactly where your Scope of Practice ends. While we are experts in the D.E.S.I.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢, we are not medical diagnosticians. A 2022 study published in the Journal of Sexual Medicine indicated that nearly 43% of women reporting sexual dysfunction had an undiagnosed underlying medical condition.

If a client presents with any of the following, your primary intervention must be a referral to a licensed medical professional:

Coach Tip: The Safety First Rule

If you are ever in doubt, refer out. It is better to have a client return with a "clean bill of health" than to spend months coaching a "libido issue" that was actually an undiagnosed thyroid storm or prolapse. This builds your reputation as a safe, ethical professional.

Common Medical Culprits: The Big Three

In our discovery phase, we must screen for the "Big Three" biological factors that frequently override even the best somatic or psychological interventions.

1. Medication Side Effects (The SSRI Factor)

Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) are life-saving for many, but they are notorious for causing Post-SSRI Sexual Dysfunction (PSSD). Statistics show that up to 60-70% of patients on SSRIs experience some form of sexual side effect, including genital numbness, delayed orgasm, or complete loss of libido.

2. Diabetes and Metabolic Syndrome

Diabetes affects sexual health through two primary pathways: Vascular (blood flow to the genitals) and Neurological (nerve sensitivity). For many women in the 45+ demographic, insulin resistance is the hidden thief of arousal.

3. Pelvic Floor Dysfunction

A "tight" (hypertonic) pelvic floor can make arousal feel like pressure or pain rather than pleasure. Many women who believe they have "low desire" actually have an anticipatory fear of pain due to pelvic floor tension.

Case Study: Sarah, 48, Teacher

From "Broken Libido" to Biological Discovery

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with "zero desire" for her partner of 20 years. She felt immense guilt and imposter syndrome, believing she was "failing" at being a woman.

The Discovery: During the intake (Lesson 1.3), the practitioner noted Sarah was on a high-dose SSRI and experienced chronic constipation (a sign of pelvic floor hypertonicity).

Intervention: The practitioner referred Sarah to a Pelvic Floor PT and provided a script for Sarah to discuss medication options with her psychiatrist.

Outcome: After 3 months of PT and a medication adjustment to a SNRI, Sarah's physical arousal returned. She is now a vocal advocate for the practitioner, who she credits with "saving her marriage."

The Practitioner-Physician Alliance

To establish yourself as a premium practitioner, you must speak the language of the medical community. When you refer a client, you aren't just "sending them away"â€”you are collaborating. Use the SBAR Communication Model:

  • Situation: "I am working with [Client Name] on sexual wellness goals."
  • Background: "She reports persistent dyspareunia during deep penetration for 6 months."
  • Assessment: "Our wellness inventories suggest a possible hypertonic pelvic floor or structural concern."
  • Recommendation: "I have recommended a clinical evaluation to rule out pathology so we can safely continue our somatic work."
Coach Tip: The Referral Loop

Always ask the client to sign a Release of Information (ROI). This allows you to send a professional "Introductory Note" to their doctor. This one act of professionalism often leads to the doctor referring other patients back to you!

Building Your Referral Dream Team

Your "Referral Network" is your greatest business asset. A practitioner with a "Dream Team" can charge premium rates because they provide a comprehensive solution. Your network should include:

  • Pelvic Floor Physical Therapists (PFPT): The #1 most important referral partner for female sexual wellness.
  • Urologists/Urogynecologists: For issues involving prolapse, incontinence, or erectile dysfunction.
  • Endocrinologists: To address the complex hormonal shifts of perimenopause and menopause.
  • Trauma-Informed OB/GYNs: Doctors who won't just say "have a glass of wine and relax."

Navigating the 'Medical Maze'

Many clients have been "gaslit" by the medical system. They have been told their pain is "normal for their age." As a practitioner, you provide the Advocacy Tools.

Teach your clients to ask these three questions at their doctor's appointment:

  1. "I am experiencing . What are the possible biological causes for this?"
  2. "Is this a known side effect of any medications I am currently taking?"
  3. "If we cannot find a cause today, who is the specialist you recommend for sexual pain/dysfunction?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Which 'Red Flag' requires an immediate referral to an OB/GYN due to potential malignancy?

Post-coital bleeding (bleeding after intercourse) is a critical red flag that must be evaluated by a physician to rule out cervical issues or malignancy.

2. What percentage of women on SSRIs are estimated to experience sexual side effects?

Studies suggest between 60% and 70% of patients on SSRIs experience some form of sexual dysfunction.

3. What does the SBAR model stand for in professional communication?

Situation, Background, Assessment, and Recommendation.

4. Why is a hypertonic pelvic floor often mistaken for "low libido"?

Because the body anticipates pain or discomfort from the tight muscles, the brain "shuts down" desire as a protective mechanism to avoid the painful stimulus.

Key Takeaways

  • Know Your Limits: Professionalism is defined by knowing when to refer to a clinical expert.
  • Rule Out Biology: Always consider medications (SSRIs), metabolic health (Diabetes), and structural health (Pelvic Floor) before assuming a concern is purely psychological.
  • Be the Bridge: Use the SBAR model to communicate with doctors, elevating your status from "coach" to "valued partner."
  • Build the Network: Your income and client outcomes are directly tied to the quality of your referral partners.
  • Empower Advocacy: Help clients find their voice in a medical system that often overlooks sexual health.

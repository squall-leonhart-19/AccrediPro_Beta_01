The Dual Control Model: Understanding 'Accelerators' and 'Brakes'

Developed by Dr. John Bancroft and Dr. Erick Janssen at the Kinsey Institute, the Dual Control Model (DCM) is the gold standard for understanding sexual response. It posits that sexual response is the result of a balance between two distinct neurological systems.

1. The Sexual Excitation System (SES) - The Accelerator

The SES is constantly scanning the environment for sexually relevant stimuli. This can include sights, smells, words, or internal fantasies. When the SES detects a stimulus, it sends a "go" signal to the brain, initiating physiological arousal.

2. The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) - The Brakes

The SIS scans for reasons not to be aroused. This is an evolutionary survival mechanism. If there is a threat, a lack of privacy, or high stress, the SIS sends a "stop" signal to override the SES. In many women over 40, the SIS is often more active than the SES due to societal conditioning, stress, and hormonal shifts.

System Component Biological Function Common Triggers
SES (Accelerator) Promotion of arousal and desire. Physical touch, romantic novelty, visual stimuli, emotional intimacy.
SIS-1 (Brakes: Threat) Protection from external danger. Fear of pregnancy, risk of STI, lack of physical safety.
SIS-2 (Brakes: Performance) Protection from social/internal failure. Body image anxiety, "spectatoring," guilt, fatigue, stress.

Neurological Pathways: The Chemistry of Desire

The transition from "Discovery" to "Education" requires a practitioner to understand the neurochemical soup that dictates pleasure. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Sexual Medicine highlighted that arousal non-concordance (when the body shows physical signs of arousal but the mind doesn't feel it) is common in 50% of women.

The "Big Three" Neurochemicals

  • Dopamine: The "Wanting" chemical. It drives the motivation to seek out pleasure. Low dopamine can manifest as a lack of interest in initiating sex.
  • Oxytocin: The "Bonding" chemical. Often released during touch and orgasm, it lowers the SIS (Brakes) by increasing feelings of safety and trust.
  • Cortisol: The "Brake" chemical. Chronic stress floods the system with cortisol, which directly inhibits the dopamine pathways required for desire.

The Evolution of the Sexual Response Cycle

For decades, the medical community relied on the Masters & Johnson Linear Model (Excitement ‚Üí Plateau ‚Üí Orgasm ‚Üí Resolution). However, research by Dr. Rosemary Basson found that this does not accurately represent the experience of many women, especially those in long-term relationships.

The Circular Model of Sexual Response

In the circular model, desire doesn't always come first. The cycle often begins with Sexual Neutrality. A woman may choose to engage in sexual activity for non-sexual reasons (intimacy, connection). Once physical stimulation begins, Arousal occurs, which then triggers Responsive Desire. This is a revolutionary concept for clients who feel "broken" because they no longer feel the "lightning bolt" of spontaneous desire they felt in their 20s.

Anatomical Literacy: The Clitoral Complex

Education (E) must include physical reality. Most clients (and many medical professionals) still view the clitoris as a small "button." As a practitioner, you must educate on the Clitoral Complex.

Recent MRI studies confirm the clitoris is a 3D structure that is 90% internal, consisting of the glans, body, crura (legs), and vestibular bulbs. During arousal, these structures engorge with blood, wrapping around the vaginal canal. This is why "vaginal" orgasms are often actually internally-stimulated clitoral orgasms.

Debunking Clinical Myths with Evidence

As an expert, you must be prepared to dismantle common myths that hinder client progress:

  • Myth: Menopause means the end of a sex life.
    Truth: While estrogen drops, the capacity for pleasure remains. A 2022 meta-analysis showed that sexual satisfaction can actually increase post-menopause when the fear of pregnancy is removed and communication improves.
  • Myth: If you don't feel "horny," you don't want sex.
    Truth: This confuses spontaneous desire with responsive desire. Most women in long-term partnerships rely on responsive desire.
  • Myth: Orgasms are the goal of a sexual encounter.
    Truth: Goal-orientation (spectatoring) actually engages the SIS (Brakes). Shifting to a Pleasure-First model reduces anxiety.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Dual Control Model, if a client has a high SES but an even higher SIS, what is the likely result?

The client will likely experience low arousal or difficulty maintaining it. Even if they are highly "excitable," their "brakes" are stronger, overriding the excitation. Treatment should focus on identifying and lowering the SIS triggers.

2. What is the primary difference between Spontaneous and Responsive Desire?

Spontaneous desire appears "out of the blue" without external stimulation. Responsive desire emerges after physical or emotional stimulation has already begun.

3. Which neurochemical is primarily responsible for the "Brake" signal in the SIS?

Cortisol (the stress hormone) is the primary inhibitor. High levels of cortisol signal to the brain that it is not safe to relax or engage in sexual activity.

4. Why is the clitoral complex described as 90% internal?

Because only the glans is visible externally. The rest of the structure‚Äîincluding the body, crura, and bulbs‚Äîextends deep into the pelvic floor and surrounds the vaginal opening.
Key Takeaways
  • The Dual Control Model is the foundation of modern sex therapy; focus on lowering the "Brakes" (SIS) as much as pushing the "Accelerator" (SES).
  • Responsive Desire is a normal and healthy variation of sexual response, especially in long-term relationships.
  • Neurochemistry Matters: Chronic stress (cortisol) will always override the "wanting" (dopamine) pathways.
  • Anatomical Education (specifically the clitoral complex) is a powerful tool to reduce shame and increase pleasure potential.
  • Goal-Free Pleasure: Moving away from the Masters & Johnson linear model allows for a more inclusive, less pressured sexual experience.

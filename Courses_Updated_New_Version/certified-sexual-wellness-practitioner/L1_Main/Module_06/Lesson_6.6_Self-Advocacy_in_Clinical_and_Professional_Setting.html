The Clinical Gap: Why Advocacy is Necessary

Despite the critical role sexual health plays in overall well-being, it remains one of the most underserved areas in primary care. A 2021 study involving over 1,500 women found that less than 14% of women had their physician initiate a conversation about sexual health during a routine visit.

Furthermore, when women do bring up concerns like low libido or painful intercourse (dyspareunia), they are frequently met with dismissive advice. Common phrases like "It's just part of aging" or "Have a glass of wine and relax" are not just unhelpful‚Äîthey are clinical failures that erode a client‚Äôs Sexual Agency.

Practitioner Insight

Many of your clients (especially those 40+) have been conditioned to believe that their sexual pleasure is a luxury rather than a health marker. Your first job is to validate that their concerns are medically and somatically significant.

Identifying and Challenging Medical Gaslighting

Medical gaslighting occurs when a healthcare provider dismisses a patient's physical symptoms or concerns, often attributing them to psychological factors or "natural" processes like menopause. In sexual wellness, this is particularly prevalent due to the historical pathologization of female desire.

Gaslighting Statement The Underlying Reality The Advocate's Rebuttal
"It's just stress/all in your head." Psychological factors exist, but physical markers (hormones, blood flow) are rarely tested. "While stress is a factor, I'd like to rule out physical causes with a full hormone panel."
"Pain is normal at your age." Pain is a signal of dysfunction (atrophy, pelvic floor issues), not a requirement of aging. "Pain is never 'normal.' What are the options for treating pelvic floor hypertonicity?"
"Your labs are 'normal'." 'Normal' ranges are broad; they don't always mean 'optimal' for function. "I understand I'm in the range, but I'm not functioning well. Can we look at the lower/upper quartiles?"

Exam Room Scripts: Reclaiming the Narrative

As a practitioner, you can provide "Exam Room Prep" sessions. This is where you earn the "Premium" status of your certification‚Äîby providing tangible tools that change client outcomes. Use the following scripts to help clients prepare for their OBGYN or GP appointments.

1. Requesting a Referral

"I have been tracking my symptoms of [symptom] for three months. I would like a referral to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist (or Sexual Medicine Specialist) to address the somatic component of this issue."

2. Challenging a Dismissal

"I appreciate your perspective, but my current quality of life is not acceptable to me. If you are unwilling to order these tests, I would like it documented in my chart that the request was made and declined." (Note: This often prompts the provider to order the test to avoid liability).

Navigating Workplace and Social Boundaries

Empowerment doesn't stop at the clinic door. Sexual wellness practitioners often help clients navigate the "gray areas" of social and professional life where sexual agency is tested. This includes dealing with inappropriate comments, "locker room talk" in the office, or friends who overstep boundaries regarding personal intimacy.

In the workplace, Sexual Sovereignty means the right to be free from sexualization. Advocacy here looks like:

  • Setting the Tone: "I prefer to keep our conversations focused on the project at hand."
  • Naming the Behavior: "That comment makes me uncomfortable. Let's get back to the agenda."
  • The 'Broken Record' Technique: Repeating a boundary clearly and calmly without providing "reasons" for the boundary (which people often use to negotiate).
Career Insight

Many practitioners find a lucrative niche ($200+/hr) by offering "Corporate Boundaries & Empowerment" workshops. Your training as a Sexual Wellness Practitioner gives you a unique depth in understanding the nervous system's response to boundary violations.

The Practitioner‚Äôs Role: Modeling Advocacy

You cannot lead a client where you have not gone yourself. As a practitioner, you model self-advocacy through your business practices:

  1. Firm Policies: Having clear cancellation and payment policies is a form of advocacy for your own labor.
  2. Referral Networks: Building a "Vetted Provider List" of doctors who don't gaslight clients. This adds immense value to your practice.
  3. Boundaries in Session: If a client oversteps (e.g., asking overly personal questions about your sex life), model the "Kind but Firm No" immediately.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most effective way to respond when a doctor says "Your labs are normal" but the client still feels symptomatic?

The client should ask about "optimal" ranges versus "standard" ranges and request that their symptoms be treated rather than just the lab numbers. A key script is: "I understand I'm in the range, but I'm not functioning well. What are the next steps for someone with my symptoms?"

2. Why is asking a provider to "document a refusal in the chart" such a powerful advocacy tool?

It creates a paper trail of potential negligence or oversight. Doctors are often more willing to order a test or referral if they know their refusal is being officially recorded in the medical record, which carries legal and professional weight.

3. True or False: Medical gaslighting is always intentional by the provider.

False. It is often a result of systemic bias, lack of specialized training in sexual medicine, or time constraints in the clinical setting. However, the intent does not change the harmful impact on the patient.

4. How does the "Broken Record" technique help in professional settings?

It prevents the practitioner or client from getting drawn into a "negotiation" over their boundary. By repeating the same calm statement, you signal that the boundary is non-negotiable and not open for debate.

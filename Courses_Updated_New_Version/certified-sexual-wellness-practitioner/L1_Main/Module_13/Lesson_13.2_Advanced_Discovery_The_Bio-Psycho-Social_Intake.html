The Bio-Psycho-Social (BPS) Framework

The Bio-Psycho-Social model, first proposed by George Engel in 1977, posits that health and illness are products of biological characteristics, psychological factors, and social contexts. In sexual wellness, this model is non-negotiable. If you only look at the body, you miss the mind; if you only look at the mind, you miss the culture.

Medications and Sexual Dysfunction

A 2021 study found that up to 70% of people on SSRIs experience some form of sexual dysfunction. Your intake must include a thorough review of current medications.

Medication Class Common Examples Potential Sexual Impact
SSRIs / SNRIs Prozac, Zoloft, Lexapro Delayed orgasm, decreased libido, genital numbness.

Hormonal Contraceptives The Pill, Nexplanon Reduced free testosterone, lowered desire, vaginal dryness.

Case Study: The "Broken" Libido

Sarah, 52, Career Educator

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported "zero desire" for three years. She felt broken and feared her marriage was failing. She had seen a therapist who focused solely on "date nights," with no improvement.

Intervention (BPS Intake):

  • Bio: Sarah was in late perimenopause; blood work showed low estradiol and high SHBG. She was also taking a nightly antihistamine for allergies.
  • Psycho: She held deep shame about her aging body and felt she was "past her prime."
  • Social: Her husband had retired, and their relational roles were shifting, creating friction.

Outcome: By identifying the biological dryness (antihistamines + low estrogen) and the psychological shame, Sarah worked with a doctor for HRT while we worked on her Inner Inquiry. Her desire returned within 4 months.

Psychological Landscapes: The Inner World

The psychological component of the intake explores the client's sexual self-schema‚Äîthe cognitive generalizations they hold about themselves as sexual beings. These are often forged in childhood and reinforced by adult experiences.

Key areas for psychological inquiry:

  • Sexual Shame: "Do you feel your sexual desires are 'wrong' or 'too much'?"
  • Performance Anxiety: "Do you find yourself 'spectatoring' (watching yourself during sex) rather than feeling the sensations?"
  • Body Image: "How does your relationship with your physical body impact your ability to receive pleasure?"

The "Miracle Question"

To uncover hidden objectives, ask: "If you woke up tomorrow and a miracle had happened‚Äîyour sexual life was exactly as you wanted it‚Äîwhat would be the first thing you noticed was different?" Listen for the feeling, not just the action.

Social Contexts: The Relational & Cultural Field

No one is a sexual island. We are all swimming in the waters of our culture, religion, and immediate relationships. During the intake, we must assess:

  • Cultural Scripts: What did your family/culture teach you about what a "good" woman/man does sexually?
  • Relational Safety: Is there active conflict, betrayal, or a lack of emotional safety in the current partnership?
  • The "Mental Load": For many women, the social burden of household management is the greatest "sexual brake" (referencing the Dual Control Model).

Validated Assessment Tools

To move from subjective "feeling" to objective data, we use psychometric tools. These provide a baseline and allow you to track progress‚Äîessential for demonstrating the value of your $997+ programs.

Key Assessment Inventories

FSFI (Female Sexual Function Index)

A 19-item questionnaire assessing desire, arousal, lubrication, orgasm, satisfaction, and pain. A score below 26.55 suggests sexual dysfunction.

SDS (Sexual Distress Scale)

Measures the level of distress a client feels regarding their sexual function. This is critical for distinguishing between "low desire" and "distressing low desire."

The SESII-W

Sexual Excitation/Sexual Inhibition Inventory. Helps identify if a client has a "sensitive brake" or a "low-powered accelerator."

Clinical Red Flags & Referrals

Knowing your scope is the hallmark of a professional. If a client presents with the following, an immediate referral to a medical professional or licensed therapist is required:

  • Dyspareunia (Pain during sex): Always requires a referral to a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist or OBGYN to rule out endometriosis, vaginismus, or infection.
  • Active Trauma Processing: If a client is in a state of "flooding" or dissociation during the intake, they require a trauma-specialized therapist.
  • Sudden Erectile Changes: New-onset ED in men can be a precursor to cardiovascular disease and requires a urological/cardiac workup.

Building Your Referral Network

Your "Discovery" process should include a list of 3-5 trusted local or telehealth partners: a Pelvic Floor PT, a Functional Medicine Doctor, and a Trauma Therapist. This network increases your professional legitimacy tenfold.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Social" part of the BPS intake particularly important for women aged 40-55?

This demographic often faces the "Sandwich Generation" stress (caring for children and aging parents) and the "Mental Load," which acts as a significant social inhibitor to sexual desire.

2. What is a "clinical red flag" when a client reports pain during intercourse?

Pain (Dyspareunia) is always a red flag. As a practitioner, you must refer to an OBGYN or Pelvic Floor PT to rule out physical pathology before beginning somatic or psychological coaching.

3. How do SSRIs typically impact the sexual response cycle?

SSRIs increase serotonin, which can inhibit dopamine and nitric oxide, leading to delayed orgasm (anorgasmia), decreased libido, and reduced genital sensitivity.

4. What is the primary purpose of using the FSFI?

To provide a validated, objective baseline of sexual function across six domains, allowing the practitioner to quantify "satisfaction" and track the efficacy of their interventions.

Key Takeaways

  • The Bio-Psycho-Social Model is the foundation of clinical discovery; never assess a symptom in isolation.
  • Always screen for medications (SSRIs, antihistamines, birth control) as they are common "hidden" biological inhibitors.
  • Use validated tools like the FSFI to move from vague complaints to actionable data points.
  • Establish a referral network of medical professionals to ensure client safety and professional integrity.
  • Discovery is an ongoing process; the "true" objective often emerges only after psychological safety is established.

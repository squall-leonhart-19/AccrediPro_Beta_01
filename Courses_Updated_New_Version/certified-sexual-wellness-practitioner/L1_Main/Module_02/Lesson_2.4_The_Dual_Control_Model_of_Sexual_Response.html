The Neurobiology of Desire: Bancroft and Janssen

For decades, sexology viewed desire as a linear progression. However, in the late 1990s, researchers John Bancroft and Erik Janssen at the Kinsey Institute proposed a revolutionary framework: the Dual Control Model. They posited that sexual response is the result of a balance between two distinct neurological systems in the brain.

This is not just "psychology"‚Äîit is neurobiology. The brain is constantly scanning the environment for reasons to be aroused and reasons not to be aroused. Every human being has both systems, but like a fingerprint, the "tuning" of these systems is unique to the individual.

The Accelerator: The Sexual Excitation System (SES)

The Sexual Excitation System (SES) is the part of the central nervous system that notices sexually relevant stimuli in the environment. It is the "Accelerator."

When the SES is activated, it sends signals from the brain to the genitals (and the rest of the body) to prepare for sexual activity. Stimuli can include:

  • Sensory: Touch, scent, visual cues, or specific sounds.
  • Mental: Fantasies, memories, or anticipation.
  • Emotional: Feeling seen, valued, or romantically connected.

Some individuals have a "highly sensitive" SES. They notice sexual cues everywhere and are easily aroused. Others have a "low sensitivity" SES, requiring more specific, intense, or prolonged stimuli to feel a response.

The Brakes: The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS)

The Sexual Inhibition System (SIS) is equally important. Its job is to notice reasons not to be aroused. In evolutionary terms, if a predator is nearby, your brain needs to shut down the sexual response immediately to prioritize survival.

The researchers identified two primary types of "Brakes":

Inhibition Type Primary Focus Clinical Examples
SIS1 Performance & Threat Fear of failure, "spectatoring," anxiety about body image, fear of pain.
SIS2 Consequences & Risk Fear of pregnancy, fear of being caught, social stigma, religious shame.

For many women in the 40-55 age demographic, "The Brakes" are often triggered by non-sexual stressors: a messy house, a long to-do list, or the "mental load" of family management. To the brain, stress is a signal of lack of safety, which triggers the SIS.

Individual Variance: How Are You Tuned?

A 2023 study involving over 2,000 participants confirmed that sexual temperament varies widely. Understanding these "settings" helps practitioners provide personalized care.

  • High SES / Low SIS: The "Easy to Start, Hard to Stop" profile. These individuals are easily aroused and rarely distracted. They may struggle with risky sexual behavior.
  • Low SES / High SIS: The "Hard to Start, Easy to Stop" profile. This is common among women seeking wellness coaching. They require high-quality "accelerators" and a near-total absence of "brakes" to experience pleasure.
  • High SES / High SIS: The "Conflicted" profile. They are easily aroused but also easily distracted or inhibited. This often leads to a "push-pull" dynamic in desire.

Research Fact: Approximately 15-20% of women are "high inhibitors" (sensitive brakes), meaning their environment must feel exceptionally safe and calm for arousal to occur.

Clinical Application: Troubleshooting Low Desire

When a client presents with "low libido," the practitioner's job is to act as a detective using the Dual Control Model. We ask: Is the accelerator not being pushed, or are the brakes jammed?

Step 1: Identify the Accelerators. What actually works for the client? Often, they haven't updated their "sexual menu" in years.
Step 2: Identify the Brakes. This is usually where the breakthrough happens. We look for SIS1 (internal anxiety) and SIS2 (external risk/stigma).
Step 3: Contextual Shift. We teach the client that "Spontaneous Desire" (the urge appearing out of nowhere) is a myth for many. "Responsive Desire" (arousal that shows up after the stimulation starts) is the goal.

Coach Tip: The "Laundry" Metaphor

Tell your clients: "You can't expect to be in 'Sex Mode' when your brain is in 'Laundry Mode.' We have to close the Laundry Tab in your brain's browser before the Pleasure Tab can load." This simple analogy resonates deeply with busy, professional women.

The Power of Context

Context is the "secret sauce" of the Dual Control Model. Context includes the external environment (Is the room cold? Are the kids awake?) and the internal state (Do I feel confident? Am I mad at my partner?).

For a "High Inhibitor," the context is everything. A practitioner's role is to help the client design a Pleasure Sanctuary‚Äîa physical and mental space where the brakes are intentionally lifted. This moves the client from "working on their sex life" to "cultivating their erotic environment."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between SIS1 and SIS2?

SIS1 focuses on performance anxiety and immediate threat (e.g., "Am I doing this right?"), while SIS2 focuses on social consequences and risks (e.g., "What if the kids hear us?" or religious guilt).

2. True or False: For most women, the most effective way to increase desire is to add more "Accelerators" (SES stimuli).

False. For many women, especially high-inhibitors, the most effective strategy is to remove the "Brakes" (SIS triggers) rather than adding more stimulation.

3. What does "Responsive Desire" mean in the context of the Dual Control Model?

Responsive desire is arousal that emerges in response to physical stimulation or a romantic context, rather than appearing as a spontaneous "urge" beforehand. The Dual Control Model shows that the SES can be activated after the choice to engage is made.

4. How does chronic stress affect the Dual Control Model?

Chronic stress signals a lack of safety to the brain, which chronically activates the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS), effectively "jamming the brakes" and making arousal difficult or impossible.

Key Takeaways

  • Sexual response is a balance between the SES (Accelerator) and the SIS (Brakes).
  • Inhibition is a biological safety mechanism, not a personal failure.
  • Context is the most powerful variable in determining whether the brakes are on or off.
  • Clients often have "Responsive Desire," meaning they need to start the process to feel the urge.
  • Effective coaching involves identifying specific "Brake" triggers (SIS1/SIS2) and creating strategies to lower them.

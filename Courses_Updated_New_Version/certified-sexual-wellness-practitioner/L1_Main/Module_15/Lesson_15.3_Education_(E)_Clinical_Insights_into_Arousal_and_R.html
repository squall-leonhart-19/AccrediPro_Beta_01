The Dual Control Model: Accelerators and Brakes

The Dual Control Model, popularized by Dr. Emily Nagoski and based on the research of Bancroft and Janssen, is the foundational "Education" tool for the Sexual Wellness Practitioner. It posits that the brain has two independent systems: the Sexual Excitation System (SES) and the Sexual Inhibition System (SIS).

Think of it as a car:

  • The Accelerator (SES): Scans the environment for sexually relevant stimuli (sights, smells, touches, romantic thoughts).
  • The Brakes (SIS): Scans the environment for reasons not to be turned on (stress, safety concerns, body image issues, dirty dishes in the sink).

Crucially, arousal is not just about pushing the accelerator harder; it is often about lifting the foot off the brakes. For many women in the 40-55 age demographic, the "brakes" are chronically engaged due to high cortisol levels and the "mental load" of mid-life responsibilities.

Functional Anatomy: The Clitoral Complex

Modern sexual wellness education requires moving beyond the "clitoral glans" (the visible tip) and teaching the Clitoral Complex. As a practitioner, you must understand that the clitoris is a 4-inch-long internal structure that wraps around the vaginal canal.

Neurobiology of Desire: The Chemical Trio

Sexual response is a neurochemical symphony. When you educate your clients on these three key chemicals, you demystify their "moods":

  1. Dopamine: The "Wanting" Chemical. Dopamine drives the anticipation of pleasure. It is the fuel for the accelerator. Without dopamine, the "spark" feels missing.
  2. Norepinephrine: The "Arousal" Chemical. This creates the physiological state of excitement‚Äîincreased heart rate and focused attention.
  3. Oxytocin: The "Bonding" Chemical. Released during touch and orgasm, it lowers the "brakes" by increasing feelings of safety and trust.

Deconstructing the 'Normal' Myth

A core part of the Education (E) pillar is addressing Arousal Non-Concordance. This is the phenomenon where physical arousal (vaginal lubrication/blood flow) does not match the mental feeling of desire.

A landmark study by Chivers et al. (2010) found that while men's physical and mental arousal overlap significantly, women's concordance is much lower‚Äîoften around 10% to 50%. This means a woman's body might be "ready," but her mind is still on her "to-do" list, or vice-versa.

Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire

We must educate clients that Responsive Desire is a healthy, normal variation. While spontaneous desire (the "thunderbolt" of wanting) is common in new relationships, responsive desire (wanting sex after physical stimulation has begun) is the norm for many women in long-term partnerships.

Physiology, Aging, and Life Transitions

For the 40-55 year old demographic, the Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM) is a significant "brake." As estrogen levels decline, the vaginal and clitoral tissues undergo thinning (atrophy), and the pH balance shifts.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Dual Control Model, what is the most effective way to increase arousal in a stressed client?

The most effective way is often to "lift the foot off the brakes" (SIS) by addressing stress, safety, and distractions, rather than simply trying to increase "accelerator" (SES) stimuli.

2. What percentage of women are estimated to require clitoral stimulation to achieve orgasm?

Approximately 70% to 80% of women require clitoral stimulation, making it a "normal" physiological requirement rather than an exception.

3. Define "Arousal Non-Concordance."

It is the lack of agreement between physical genital response (lubrication/blood flow) and the subjective mental experience of feeling "turned on."

4. Which neurochemical is primarily responsible for the "anticipation" of sexual pleasure?

Dopamine is the primary neurochemical driver of anticipation and the "wanting" phase of desire.

Key Takeaways

  • Education is Intervention: Simply explaining how the body works can reduce a client's shame by 50% or more.
  • Brakes vs. Accelerators: Mid-life sexual wellness is often about "brake management" (reducing stress and pain).
  • Responsive Desire is Normal: Transitioning from spontaneous to responsive desire is a common and healthy evolution in long-term intimacy.
  • Anatomy Matters: The clitoris is an extensive internal system; understanding its full scope empowers better somatic practices.
  • GSM is a Clinical Reality: Perimenopausal changes are physiological, not psychological, and require targeted education on tissue health.

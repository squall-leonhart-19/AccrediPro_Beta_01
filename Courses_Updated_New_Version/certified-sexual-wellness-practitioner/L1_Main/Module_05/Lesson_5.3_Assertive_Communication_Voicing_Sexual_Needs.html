The Spectrum of Communication Styles

Effective sexual wellness work requires moving clients away from patterns of resentment and toward a state of empowerment. To do this, we must first help them identify where they currently land on the communication spectrum.

Practical Application: The DEAR MAN Technique

Borrowed from Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) and adapted for sexual wellness, the DEAR MAN technique provides a structured script for clients who feel "frozen" when trying to voice a need.

  • D - Describe: Stick to the facts. "We haven't spent much time on foreplay lately."
  • E - Express: Use "I" statements. "I feel more connected and aroused when we take our time."
  • A - Assert: Ask for what you want clearly. "I would like us to spend at least 15 minutes on touch before intercourse."
  • R - Reinforce: Explain the benefit. "This will help me stay present and enjoy our time more."
  • M - Mindful: Stay focused on the goal. Don't bring up past arguments.
  • A - Appear Confident: Use steady eye contact and a firm but warm tone.
  • N - Negotiate: Be open to the partner's input. "How does that sound to you?"

Overcoming the 'Shame Barrier'

Why is it so hard to say, "I want to try [fantasy]"? It is the Shame Barrier. This barrier is built from years of cultural messaging that equates sexual desire with "impurity" or "being too much."

A 2022 survey found that 68% of women have a sexual fantasy they have never shared with a partner, primarily due to fear of judgment. To help clients over this barrier, use the "Bridge of Normalized Desire."

Example: "It is very common for women to enjoy [fantasy]. In fact, research shows it's one of the top three most common desires. How would it feel to share that part of yourself with your partner?"

Coach Tip: The 'No' is a Gift

Teach clients that a partner's "No" to a specific request is not a rejection of them. It is a boundary that creates safety. Assertive communication is about the request, not the guaranteed result.

Practitioner Role-Play Strategies

As a practitioner, you must be comfortable "modeling" these conversations. Role-playing reduces the "amygdala hijack" (fear response) that clients feel when they think about talking to their partner.

The "Switch" Strategy:

  1. You play the client, and the client plays their partner. You model the assertive language.
  2. Switch roles. The client practices the language while you play the partner (starting with a supportive response, then moving to a more challenging one).
Coach Tip: Vulnerability First

If a client is struggling, have them start with the "vulnerability lead-in." "I'm feeling really nervous to say this, but I trust you, and I want to tell you something I've been thinking about..." This lowers the partner's defenses immediately.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which communication style is characterized by "faking" pleasure to avoid conflict?

The Passive style. It often leads to long-term resentment and a decrease in desire because the individual's needs are never met.

2. In the DEAR MAN technique, what does the "R" stand for?

Reinforce. This involves explaining the positive outcome or benefit for both partners if the request is met (e.g., "We will both feel more connected").

3. What is the primary purpose of "Sexual Scripts" in a sociological context?

They act as a set of cultural "blueprints" or rules that dictate how people "should" behave sexually, often limiting authentic expression.

4. Why is role-playing an essential tool for sexual wellness practitioners?

It helps desensitize the client to the anxiety of the conversation and allows them to "muscle-memory" the assertive language in a safe environment.

  • Clarity in the bedroom is a form of relational kindness, not a demand.
  • The DEAR MAN technique provides a foolproof structure for making difficult sexual requests.
  • Practitioners must model assertive language to help clients overcome the "Shame Barrier."
  • Transforming communication is often the fastest way to increase a client's sexual satisfaction scores.

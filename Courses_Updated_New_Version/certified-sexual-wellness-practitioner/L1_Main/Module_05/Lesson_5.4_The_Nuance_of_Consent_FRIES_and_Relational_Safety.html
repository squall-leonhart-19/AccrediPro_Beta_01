The FRIES Model: Reimagining Agreement

Consent is often taught in legalistic terms, but in the realm of sexual wellness, we use the FRIES model (originally developed by Planned Parenthood) to describe what healthy consent looks like in practice. For your clients, this framework moves consent from a hurdle to be cleared into an invitation for deeper intimacy.

Element Definition Relational Application
Freely Given Without pressure, manipulation, or influence of substances. Is the client saying yes to avoid a fight or to please a partner?
Reversible Can be withdrawn at any time, even after the activity has started. The "Stop" button is always active and carries no social penalty.
Informed Knowing exactly what is being agreed to (e.g., protection, specific acts). No "surprises" or mid-act shifts without a new agreement.
Enthusiastic Active wanting, not just a lack of "no." Focusing on desire rather than just permission.
Specific Saying yes to one thing does not mean yes to everything. Agreement is granular and task-specific.
Practitioner Insight

When teaching FRIES to women in their 40s and 50s, emphasize the "R" (Reversible). Many women feel that once they have "initiated" or "led a partner on," they are obligated to finish. Teaching them that they can change their mind at 90% completion is a revolutionary act of sexual agency.

Compliance vs. Enthusiastic Consent

As a Practitioner, you must help clients distinguish between compliance (agreeing because it feels easier than saying no) and enthusiastic consent (agreeing because they genuinely want the experience). A 2021 study in the Journal of Sex Research found that "sexual compliance" is significantly correlated with lower sexual satisfaction and higher rates of sexual dysfunction over time.

Compliance often sounds like:

  • "I guess so."
  • "If you want to."
  • "Sure, why not?"
  • "It's been a while, so I should."

Enthusiastic consent is somatic. It involves leaned-in body language, clear verbal affirmation ("I would love that"), and a sense of active participation. When a client operates from compliance, they are often "spectatoring" (Module 3) rather than being present in their bodies.

The Art of the Check-In: Staying Present

Consent is not a one-time event at the beginning of an encounter; it is a continuous loop. We teach clients "The Check-In"‚Äîa simple verbal or non-verbal touchpoint during intimacy to ensure both partners remain in the "Green Zone" of safety and pleasure.

Verbal Check-Ins

  • "How does this feel right now?"
  • "Do you want to keep going with this, or try something else?"
  • "I'm really enjoying this; are you?"
  • "Red, Yellow, or Green?" (Using a traffic light system for intensity).

Non-Verbal Cues

Practitioners must train clients to observe micro-expressions and somatic shifts. If a partner holds their breath, tenses their shoulders, or pulls away slightly, these are "Yellow Lights" that require a verbal check-in, even if they haven't said "No."

Client Tool

Suggest the "3-Breath Rule": Every few minutes during physical intimacy, take three deep breaths together. This forces a pause and allows both partners to check in with their own internal "Yes" or "No."

The Yes/No/Maybe Tool

One of the most effective tools in the Sexual Wellness Practitioner's toolkit is the Yes/No/Maybe List. This is a structured inventory where clients (individually or with a partner) categorize various sexual activities.

Category Definition Action
Yes Things I love, crave, and want to do regularly. Green Light - Proceed with enthusiasm.
No Hard boundaries. Things I am not interested in or that feel unsafe. Red Light - Do not ask or pressure.
Maybe Things I'm curious about, or okay with under specific conditions. Yellow Light - Requires deep communication and specific FRIES consent.

This tool removes the "guesswork" and reduces the anxiety of rejection. When a partner knows exactly where the boundaries are, they feel safer to explore within the "Yes" and "Maybe" zones.

Legal & Ethical Boundaries of Consent

As a Certified Sexual Wellness Practitioner‚Ñ¢, your relationship with the client is governed by a strict ethical code. Consent within the practitioner-client container is unidirectional.

  • Power Dynamics: Recognize that as the "expert," your suggestions carry weight. Always ensure clients know they can decline any exercise or homework without judgment.
  • Physical Touch: This certification is non-touch. Any somatic work is guided verbally. Consent must be obtained before asking a client to perform a solo somatic exercise (like those in Module 3).
  • Confidentiality: Consent also applies to the client's data and stories. Never share client details without explicit, written informed consent.
Practitioner Tip

Always start your sessions by saying: "Everything we do today is optional. If I suggest a reflection or an exercise that doesn't feel right, please say 'Pass' or 'No,' and we will move on immediately." This models healthy consent from the start.

Check Your Understanding

1. In the FRIES model, what does the "S" stand for, and why is it important?

"S" stands for Specific. It is important because saying yes to one activity (e.g., kissing) does not imply consent for another (e.g., oral sex). Consent must be negotiated for each specific act.

2. What is the primary difference between compliance and enthusiastic consent?

Compliance is "giving in" to avoid conflict or fulfill a perceived duty, often characterized by a lack of genuine desire. Enthusiastic consent is an "active wanting" and a "leaned-in" presence.

3. True or False: If a partner has initiated sex, they have waived their right to withdraw consent until the encounter is over.

False. According to the "R" in FRIES (Reversible), consent can be withdrawn at any time, for any reason, regardless of who started the encounter.

4. How does a "Maybe" on a Yes/No/Maybe list differ from a "No"?

A "No" is a hard boundary that should not be pressured. A "Maybe" indicates curiosity or conditional interest that requires further discussion, specific safety parameters, and a higher level of check-in.

Key Takeaways

  • Consent is a dynamic process, not a static permission slip.
  • The FRIES model (Freely given, Reversible, Informed, Enthusiastic, Specific) provides a gold-standard framework for relational safety.
  • Moving clients from compliance to enthusiasm is essential for resolving low desire and resentment.
  • Continuous Check-Ins (verbal and somatic) ensure that intimacy remains a shared, safe experience.
  • Practitioners must model consent by maintaining clear ethical boundaries and prioritizing client autonomy.

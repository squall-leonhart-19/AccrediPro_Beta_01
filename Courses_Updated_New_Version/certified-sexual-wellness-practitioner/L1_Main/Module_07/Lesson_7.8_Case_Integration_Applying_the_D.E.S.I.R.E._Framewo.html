Common Practitioner Pitfalls

Even experienced career changers often fall into these traps when they first start. Watch out for these:

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Rushing to suggest supplements or toys before the client feels heard.
  • The "Euphemism" Echo: If the client says "down there," you should use the correct anatomical terms (e.g., vulva, vagina) to model professional comfort.
  • Over-Sharing: Sharing too much of your own story can take the focus off the client. Keep self-disclosure brief and purposeful.
  • Ignoring the Body: Forgetting to ask how a feeling (like guilt) manifests physically in the body.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "Normalization" the first step in the coaching script?

Normalization reduces shame. When a client realizes they aren't "the only one," their nervous system relaxes, allowing them to engage in the coaching process effectively.

2. What should you do if a client uses a euphemism like "my lady parts"?

Acknowledge their statement but gently use the correct anatomical terms in your response. This "models" professional comfort and helps the client reclaim their body's language.

3. Linda mentions feeling "useful but not sexy." What layer of coaching is this?

This is the Identity Layer. It goes beyond physical symptoms to how the client perceives their role and value in the world.

4. What is a primary "brake" on the sexual response system mentioned in the script?

Guilt. Emotional stressors like guilt, shame, and stress act as powerful inhibitors (brakes) on sexual desire and arousal.

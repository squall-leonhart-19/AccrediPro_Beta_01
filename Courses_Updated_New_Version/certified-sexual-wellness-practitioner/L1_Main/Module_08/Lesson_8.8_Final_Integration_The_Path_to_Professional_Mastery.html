Common Pitfalls for New Practitioners

The Pitfall Why it Happens The Professional Fix
The "Expert Trap" Imposter syndrome makes you want to prove your knowledge. Ask more questions than you give answers in the first 20 minutes.
Over-Empathizing You want to be liked, so you share too many of your own stories. Keep the focus 100% on the client. Use "I understand" instead of "That happened to me too!"
Fear of Silence Silence feels awkward, so you fill it with chatter. Count to 4 after a client finishes speaking. They often share the "real" truth in the second half.
Minimizing Trying to make the client feel better by saying "It's not that bad." Validate the struggle: "That sounds like it has been very heavy for you."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "Expert Trap" and why should you avoid it?

The Expert Trap is the tendency to over-explain or lecture the client to prove your legitimacy. You should avoid it because it shuts down client discovery and can make the client feel overwhelmed or unheard.

2. Diane uses self-deprecating humor. How should a practitioner respond?

Acknowledge the humor but gently pivot to the underlying emotion. Validate that her concerns are serious and worthy of attention, even if she feels "silly" discussing them.

3. What is a physical "Success Marker" to look for in a client?

The "Shoulders Drop"‚Äîa visible physical relaxation that indicates the client feels safe and understood in the coaching environment.

4. Why is the "Comfortable Silence" important in a session?

Silence gives the client room to process their thoughts and often leads to them sharing deeper, more vulnerable insights that they might have withheld if the coach kept talking.

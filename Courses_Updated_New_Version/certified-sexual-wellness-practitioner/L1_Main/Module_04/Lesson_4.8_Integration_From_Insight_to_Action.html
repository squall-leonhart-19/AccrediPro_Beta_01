Common Pitfalls for New Practitioners

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps when starting with sexual wellness inquiry. Being aware of them is 50% of the battle.

  • The "Fix-It" Reflex: Trying to give Elena a "pleasure menu" or a vibrator recommendation before she has even explored her inner blocks. Inquiry must precede intervention.
  • Over-Pathologizing: Assuming Elenaâ€™s lack of desire is a "dysfunction" rather than a logical response to her current life stage and internal narrative.
  • Fear of the "Blank": When a client says "I don't know," new coaches often panic and ask a different question. Instead, stay with the "I don't know." It is a valid data point.
  • Leading the Witness: Asking "Don't you think you're just stressed?" instead of "What do you think is contributing to this feeling?"
Professional Standards

In your first few months, you might feel like you're not "doing enough" if you're just asking questions. Trust the process. A $500/hour practitioner is often the one who says the least and listens the most.

Check Your Understanding

1. When Elena says she feels like a "blank wall," why is it important to use her exact metaphor instead of suggesting a new one?

Using the client's exact metaphor (Mirroring) validates their internal experience and ensures you are working within their unique "Inner Landscape." Introducing your own metaphors can inadvertently lead the client away from their own truth or create a disconnect in the coaching container.

2. What should a practitioner do if a client answers "I don't know" to an inquiry question?

Instead of rushing to a new question, the practitioner should stay with the "I don't know." You might say, "That's okay. Let's just sit with the 'don't know' for a moment and see if it has a shape or a color." This honors the client's current state without pressure.

3. True or False: The goal of an Inner Inquiry session is to provide the client with a list of three actionable steps to improve their sex life.

False. The goal of Inner Inquiry is discovery and awareness. While action steps may follow in later sessions (the "Education" or "Somatic" phases), the Inquiry phase focuses on uncovering the underlying beliefs and narratives.

4. Which of the following is a "Success Marker" in an inquiry session?

A "Somatic Shift," such as the client taking a deep, spontaneous breath or visibly relaxing their posture. This indicates that the inquiry has reached a deeper, embodied level of awareness beyond just intellectualizing.

Key Takeaways

  • Inner Inquiry is about discovery, not fixing. Your primary tool is the open-ended question.
  • Mirroring the client's language and metaphors builds a deep, safe coaching container.
  • Silence is a coaching tool. Use the 5-second rule to give clients space to process their inner landscape.
  • Success is measured by the client's shift in awareness and somatic ease, not by the number of solutions provided.
  • Mastering these "soft skills" is what separates a premium practitioner from a generalist coach.

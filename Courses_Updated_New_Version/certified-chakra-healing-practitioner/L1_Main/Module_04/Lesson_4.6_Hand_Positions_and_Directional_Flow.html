The 'Hover' vs. 'Contact' Methods

The first decision a practitioner must make when beginning an infusion is the level of physical proximity. This choice is dictated by the client's energetic density and the specific goal of the intervention.

The Hover Method (Auric Infusion)

In the Hover method, the hands remain 2 to 4 inches above the physical body. This method interacts primarily with the Etheric and Emotional layers of the biofield. It is the preferred method for:

  • High-Sensitivity Clients: Those who feel overwhelmed by direct touch.
  • Upper Chakra Work: The Throat, Third Eye, and Crown centers often respond better to subtle, non-tactile infusion.
  • Broad Infusion: When you need to saturate a larger area with prana rather than a localized point.

The Contact Method (Somatic Infusion)

Contact involves light, non-invasive touch (with client consent). This grounds the energy and provides a "physical anchor" for the prana. It is most effective for:

  • The Lower Chakras: The Root and Sacral centers are deeply tied to the physical body and often require the "weight" of touch to feel activated.
  • Grounding: When a client feels "spacey" or disconnected from their body.
  • Acute Depletion: Physical contact can act as a more direct bridge for prana transfer.
Coach Tip: The Sensation Bridge

If you aren't sure which to use, start with the Hover method. If you feel a "pull" or a sense of emptiness that doesn't resolve after 60 seconds, gently transition to light Contact. Your hands will often feel a "magnetic click" when they reach the correct distance.

Clockwise Rotation: Encouraging Natural Spin

As we established in Module 1, a healthy chakra is not static; it is a vortex. In the Northern Hemisphere (and traditionally in most energetic lineages), a healthy, "feeding" chakra rotates clockwise from the perspective of the practitioner looking at the client.

The Technique:

  1. Center your palm over the chakra.
  2. Begin a slow, rhythmic circular motion in a clockwise direction.
  3. Start with small circles (about the size of a silver dollar) and gradually widen them to the diameter of the chakra's field (usually 3-5 inches).
  4. Synchronize this movement with your own Ujjayi breath to maintain a steady flow of prana.

This motion acts like a "jump-start" for a stalled engine. By mimicking the healthy frequency of the center, you encourage the client's own energy to match that rhythmâ€”a process known as energetic entrainment.

The 'Pumping' Technique: Breaking Inertia

Sometimes, a chakra is so hypo-active (depleted) that simple rotation isn't enough to wake it up. This is where we employ the Pumping Technique. This is a manual hand movement designed to create a "vacuum effect" that pulls prana into the deeper layers of the energetic core.

Execution Steps:

  • The Descent: Move your hand slowly toward the chakra (from 6 inches down to 1 inch above).
  • The Compression: Imagine you are gently compressing a soft sponge of light.
  • The Release: Quickly pull your hand back to the 6-inch mark.
  • The Infusion: Repeat this rhythmically. The "push" sends prana in; the "pull" creates space for the chakra to expand.
Coach Tip: Ergonomics and Longevity

When performing the Pumping technique, move from your Hara (your center of gravity), not just your elbow. This prevents shoulder strain and ensures the energy you are directing is coming from your grounded core rather than just your physical muscles.

Closing the Circuit: Establishing Loops

A common mistake for beginners is using only one hand. To be a truly effective practitioner, you must learn to close the circuit. This involves using both hands to create a continuous loop of energy flow through the client's body.

Hand Role Function Typical Placement
Sending Hand Directs the infusion of prana into the target chakra. Dominant hand over the specific chakra.
Receiving/Grounding Hand Stabilizes the field and completes the circuit. Non-dominant hand on the shoulder or the Root chakra.
Dual Infusion Balances two related centers simultaneously. One hand on the Heart, one hand on the Solar Plexus.

By keeping your non-dominant hand in contact with the client (or hovering over a grounding point), you prevent the energy from "scattering." It creates a focused channel, much like a wire allows electricity to reach its destination without loss.

Standardized Hand-Placement Protocols

To provide a professional experience, use the following standardized placements. This ensures consistency and helps build your confidence as a practitioner.

  • Root (Muladhara): Hand placed at the base of the spine (if prone) or hovering over the pubic bone (if supine). Focus: Grounding.
  • Sacral (Svadhisthana): Two inches below the navel. Focus: Fluidity.
  • Solar Plexus (Manipura): Between the navel and the sternum. Focus: Vitality.
  • Heart (Anahata): Center of the chest. Focus: Expansion.
  • Throat (Vishuddha): Hovering over the throat (never direct pressure). Focus: Expression.
  • Third Eye (Ajna): Between the eyebrows. Focus: Insight.
  • Crown (Sahasrara): Hovering 3-5 inches above the top of the head. Focus: Connection.
Coach Tip: The Professional container

Always announce your transitions. Say, "I am moving my hands to your Heart center now." This maintains the "Professional Container" and ensures the client remains relaxed and informed, preventing any startle response that could close their energetic field.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which method is most appropriate for a client who feels "spacey" and disconnected from their physical body?

The Contact Method is best here, as physical touch provides a somatic anchor that helps ground the client's energy back into their physical form.

2. In which direction should you rotate your hand to encourage the natural "input" of prana into a chakra?

Clockwise. This direction is traditionally used to stimulate, tonify, and feed a chakra that is hypo-active or depleted.

3. What is the primary purpose of the 'Pumping' technique?

To break through energetic inertia. It uses a rhythmic vacuum effect to pull prana deeper into a center that is severely depleted or "stuck."

4. Why is it important to use a "Receiving/Grounding" hand while the other hand performs the infusion?

To close the energetic circuit. This prevents energy scattering and creates a focused loop of prana, increasing the efficiency of the infusion.

Key Takeaways

  • Method Selection: Use the Hover method for subtle/upper centers and the Contact method for grounding/lower centers.
  • Directional Spin: Clockwise rotation is the gold standard for "feeding" a depleted energy center.
  • Active Infusion: Use the Pumping technique when rotation alone doesn't wake up a hypo-active chakra.
  • Circuitry: Always use both hands to establish a loop, ensuring the prana is contained and directed.

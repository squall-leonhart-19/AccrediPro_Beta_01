Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced practitioners can fall into these traps. Being aware of them is 50% of the battle.

  • Rushing the Silence: New coaches often fear silence and talk too much. Give the Prana time to move. 30 seconds of silence is a gift, not a void.
  • Using "Try" Language: Avoid saying "Try to feel the energy." Instead, use "Notice the energy." "Try" implies failure is possible; "Notice" implies it's already there.
  • Ignoring Physical Cues: If you see the client's shoulders move up to their ears, stop the infusion and guide them back to a belly breath. You cannot infuse Prana into a constricted body.
  • Over-Explaining the Science: While the "Pranic Bridge" is great for the intro, don't lecture during the infusion. Keep the guidance poetic and sensory.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use "Notice" instead of "Try" when guiding a client like Margaret?

"Notice" presupposes that the energy is already present and active, which reduces performance anxiety for the client. "Try" implies effort and the possibility of failure, which can trigger the sympathetic nervous system and block the flow of Prana.

2. What is the primary purpose of the "Physical Anchor" phase?

The Physical Anchor (belly breathing and feeling body weight) shifts the client from the sympathetic (fight/flight) to the parasympathetic (rest/digest) nervous system. Prana cannot be effectively infused or directed when the body is in a state of stress-induced constriction.

3. A client reports feeling "nothing" during their first infusion. What is the best professional response?

Validate their experience immediately. Tell them it's normal and that "feeling" is a skill that develops over time. Remind them that the energetic shift is happening at a subtle level regardless of their immediate sensory perception.

4. How does the "Pranic Bridge" analogy help skeptical clients?

It uses familiar, non-spiritual concepts (like electricity and hardware/software) to explain complex energetic principles. This reduces the "threat" to their analytical mind and allows them to engage with the practice without feeling like they are adopting a new belief system.

  • Confidence is Key: Margaret is looking to you for permission to relax. If you are confident in the process, she will be too.

Structuring Peer Review for Deep-Seated Trauma

When dealing with the Dissolving Blockages (D) phase in clients with significant trauma, the "Lone Wolf" approach is dangerous. Structured peer review provides a "safety net" for both the client and the practitioner.

The "Council" Format for Peer Review:

  1. The Presentation (10 mins): The practitioner presents the case, focusing on the RADIANT framework data (Assessment findings, current roadmap).
  2. The Inquiry (15 mins): Peers ask clarifying questions. *Crucial: No advice is given yet.* Questions focus on the energetic architecture.
  3. The Resonance Round (15 mins): Peers share what they "felt" or "saw" energetically during the presentation.
  4. The Synthesis (10 mins): The practitioner identifies 1-2 key shifts to implement in the next session.
Professional Standards

Always ensure client anonymity during peer review. Use initials or pseudonyms. In the RADIANT Method‚Ñ¢, we protect the sanctity of the client's energetic field as much as their personal data.

Feedback Loops & Intuitive Authority

One of the greatest challenges for energy practitioners is receiving feedback without losing their intuitive authority. If a mentor says, "I don't think that's a Throat Chakra issue," and you *know* it is, how do you handle the conflict?

A healthy feedback loop in L3 supervision follows the "Yes, And" principle:

  • Step 1: Acknowledge the feedback as a data point.
  • Step 2: Re-scan the client field with the new data point in mind.
  • Step 3: If the feedback resonates, integrate it. If it doesn't, honor your direct perception while noting the discrepancy for future reflection.

Digital vs. In-Person Mentoring Frameworks

In today's global market, many practitioners operate virtually. While in-person mentoring allows for physical hand-scanning correction, digital mentoring offers unique advantages for the modern professional.

Digital Mentoring (The Global Circle):

  • Pros: Access to diverse energetic signatures; ability to record sessions for later review; lower overhead costs.
  • Cons: Lack of physical somatic resonance; potential for "Zoom fatigue."
Income Opportunity

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between hierarchical mentoring and peer-led supervision?

Hierarchical mentoring is teacher-led and focused on skill correction (best for beginners), while peer-led supervision is a horizontal, collaborative process focused on reflective growth and complex case management (best for L3 practitioners).

2. In the Reflective Practitioner model, what is the practitioner's main task?

The main task is "self-observation" or "reflection-in-action"‚Äîobserving one's own biases, projections, and somatic responses while performing the work to ensure energetic accuracy.

3. Why is peer review particularly important during the 'Dissolving Blockages' (D) phase with trauma clients?

Deep trauma can lead to practitioner burnout or "secondary traumatic stress." Peer review provides a safety net, ensures the intervention strategy is sound, and prevents the practitioner from becoming energetically overwhelmed.

4. How does a practitioner maintain 'Intuitive Authority' while receiving feedback?

By treating feedback as a data point to be tested. The practitioner re-scans the field with the new information and ultimately trusts their direct perception, using the feedback as a tool for refinement rather than an absolute command.

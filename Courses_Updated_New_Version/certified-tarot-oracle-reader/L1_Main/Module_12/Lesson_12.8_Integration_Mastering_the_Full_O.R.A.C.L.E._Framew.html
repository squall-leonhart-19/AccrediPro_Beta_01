Common Practice Pitfalls

New readers often fall into these traps. Use the table below to audit your own style.

The Pitfall The Professional Fix Why It Matters
The "Dictionary" Read Using the "Bridge" technique to link cards. Clients pay for insight, not definitions they can Google.
Over-Explaining Keeping interpretations to 2-3 sentences per card. Prevents the client from becoming overwhelmed or "tuning out."
Fear of "Negative" Cards Reframing cards like The Moon as "growth cycles." Maintains an empowering, coaching-centered environment.
Boundaries Leakage Redirecting "Should I?" questions back to the client. Ensures the client retains their agency and power.
Final Coach Tip

If you don't know what a card means in a specific context, be honest! Say: "This card is being a bit shy right now. Let's look at the cards around it for a clue." Honesty builds more trust than a fake answer ever will.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Bridge" technique superior to standard card definitions?

It connects the abstract symbolism of the card to the client's specific life situation, providing actionable insight rather than just theoretical knowledge.

2. What is the primary "identity shift" to look for when reading for a client like Diane?

The transition from her old professional identity (Principal) to her new, unformed identity (Wellness Entrepreneur), and the fear associated with that loss of status.

3. What should you do if a client asks a "Should I?" question during a reading?

Use the cards to show the energy of each choice, then ask the client which path feels more aligned with their vision, thus keeping the power in their hands.

4. How does "up-talking" (rising intonation at the end of a sentence) affect your professional authority?

It makes statements sound like questions, signaling a lack of confidence and making the client feel less secure in your guidance.

Success Markers

How do you know if your practice session was successful? Look for these three signs:

  • The "Aha" Exhale: The client (or your practice partner) lets out a long breath when a card clicks.
  • Minimal Fillers: You spoke for 5 minutes and used fewer than three "ums" or "likes."
  • Collaborative Dialogue: The client spent at least 30% of the time talking, responding to your prompts.

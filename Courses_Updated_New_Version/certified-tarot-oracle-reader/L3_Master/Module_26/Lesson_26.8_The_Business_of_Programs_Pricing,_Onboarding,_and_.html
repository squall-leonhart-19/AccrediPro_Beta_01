Step 2: The Case Linda Presents

Linda brings a case to your supervision session. She is visibly nervous and says, "I think I totally missed the mark with this client. She seemed disappointed, and now I'm afraid I'm not actually intuitive enough for this."

The Client Case Details (The "Maria" Case)

The Client

Maria, 50, Executive in a high-stress tech firm. Seeking clarity on a career move.

The Spread

Linda used a 5-card "Path Forward" spread. The Tower appeared in the outcome position.

Linda's Interpretation

Linda told Maria, "Everything is going to fall apart, and you should probably wait to make a move."

The Result

Maria became defensive and said, "That doesn't help me at all. I need to leave now for my health."

Sarah's Insight

Notice that Linda's fear (Imposter Syndrome) colored her interpretation of the Tower. She saw "catastrophe" because she was feeling "catastrophic" about her own skills. This is called a Parallel Process.

Step 3: The Reflective Supervision Model

Instead of telling Linda she was "wrong," we use Reflective Supervision. This allows the mentee to discover the solution themselves, which builds long-term clinical reasoning.

Supervision Phase Your Action The Goal
Containment Validate her feelings of nervousness. Lower cortisol so she can actually learn.
Observation Ask: "What did you feel in your body when that card came up?" Identify where her intuition got "blocked" by her ego.
Reframing Explore other meanings of the Tower (e.g., liberation). Expand her symbolic vocabulary.
Integration Draft a new way to phrase the message. Practical skill application.

Step 4: Crafting the Feedback Dialogue

When mentoring women in their 40s and 50s, remember that many come from corporate or educational backgrounds where "feedback" was often punitive. Your job is to make feedback a gift.

The Feedback Script

How to deliver the "Sandwich" approach

1. The "Top Bun" (Validation): "Linda, I love how you were brave enough to deliver a 'hard' card like the Tower. Many new readers sugarcoat everything, but you stayed true to what you saw. That integrity is why your clients will trust you."

2. The "Meat" (The Correction): "Let's look at the Tower from Maria's perspective. She told you she's in a high-stress job and needs to leave for her health. Could the 'destruction' of the Tower actually be the destruction of the stress that's killing her? How does that change the advice?"

3. The "Bottom Bun" (Encouragement): "You have the sight, Linda. This session didn't fail; it just gave us the exact data we needed to sharpen your delivery. You're doing the work."

Sarah's Insight

Always end with a specific action. Ask: "How would you handle a 'scary' card in your next session tomorrow?" This locks in the learning immediately.

Step 5: Supervision Best Practices

To be a legitimate mentor, you must move beyond "giving advice" and into "facilitating growth." Follow these industry standards for L3 practitioners:

1

Maintain the "Supervisory Alliance"

Your relationship with the mentee is the container for their growth. If they don't feel safe, they will hide their mistakes. Praise in public (or on social media), correct in private.

2

Watch for Scope of Practice

If Linda's client Maria was showing signs of severe clinical depression, your job as a mentor is to ensure Linda knows how to refer out to a therapist. This is a key L3 responsibility.

3

The 70/30 Rule

In a mentoring session, the mentee should be talking 70% of the time. You are the mirror, not the megaphone. Ask open-ended questions like, "What else could that card mean?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "Parallel Process" in a supervision context?

It is when the mentee experiences the same emotions or dynamics that the client is experiencing (e.g., Linda feeling "stuck" or "catastrophic" just like her client Maria).

2. Why should you avoid simply giving the "right answer" to a mentee's case?

Because the goal of supervision is to build the mentee's clinical reasoning and intuition. If you give the answer, they remain dependent on you. If you guide them to find it, they become an independent practitioner.

3. What is the primary purpose of the "Top Bun" in the feedback sandwich?

To validate the mentee and lower their defensiveness/cortisol levels so they are psychologically open to receiving the constructive "meat" of the feedback.

4. When should a mentor suggest a mentee seek their own personal therapy or healing?

When the mentee's personal "triggers" or trauma are consistently interfering with their ability to be objective with clients, and supervision alone isn't resolving the block.

Key Takeaways for the Master Practitioner

  • Supervision is Reflective: Focus on how the practitioner thinks, not just what they said.
  • Safety First: Create a shame-free environment where mentees feel safe sharing their "failed" sessions.
  • The 70/30 Rule: Facilitate self-discovery by asking more questions than you give answers.
  • Professionalism: Use supervision to enforce ethics, boundaries, and proper referral protocols.
  • Monetization: Professional mentoring is a high-value L3 service that provides recurring revenue and industry authority.

1. Meet Your Mentee: Elena

As you grow your practice, you will attract junior readers who look up to your expertise. Your first mentee in this lab is Elena, a 42-year-old former elementary school teacher who recently graduated from her Level 1 certification.

Mentee Profile: Elena

Background: Elena is compassionate, highly organized, and has a deep love for the Oracle. However, she struggles with imposter syndrome, especially when clients present with heavy emotional topics.

Her Current Challenge: She feels she "failed" a recent client because the reading didn't end on a "happy note." She is questioning if she is cut out for this work.

Income Potential: By mentoring practitioners like Elena, you can offer Master-Level Supervision Packages. A typical package of four 45-minute supervision sessions per month can range from $600 to $1,200, creating a high-margin revenue stream alongside your readings.

Sarah's Coach Tip

Remember, your job isn't to fix Elena's reading. Your job is to help Elena understand her process. Focus on her "why" rather than just the card meanings she chose.

2. The Case Review: "Grief & The Tower"

Elena brings a case to you regarding her client, Mark, who is grieving the loss of a long-term business partnership. Elena pulled The Tower, The Five of Cups, and The Hermit.

Elena tells you: "I felt so bad for him. When The Tower came up, I tried to soften it. I told him it was just 'minor change,' but he started crying, and I got flustered. I felt like I wasn't helping him at all. I think I gave a bad reading."

Elena's Interpretation The Mentoring Opportunity
"Softened" The Tower to avoid upsetting the client. Discuss radical honesty and holding space for difficult truths.
Felt responsible for the client's tears. Reinforce emotional boundaries and the role of "The Witness."
Labelled the reading as "bad." Shift focus from "performance" to client-centered service.

3. The Teaching Approach: Scaffolding

In supervision, we use scaffolding—providing just enough support so the mentee can reach the next level of competence. Instead of saying, "You should have said X," try the Socratic Method.

Key Teaching Points for Elena:

  • The "Shadow" of the Cards: Explain that "negative" cards are often the most healing because they validate the client's internal reality.
  • The Power of Silence: Teach Elena that when a client cries, the most "professional" thing to do is often to sit in silence, not to keep talking.
  • Scope of Practice: Remind her that she is a reader, not a grief counselor. Knowing when to refer out is a sign of a Master Practitioner.
Sarah's Coach Tip

New readers often talk too much when they are nervous. Encourage Elena to use the "Three-Second Rule"—wait three seconds after a client finishes speaking before she responds.

4. Constructive Feedback Scripts

How you deliver feedback determines whether Elena grows or shuts down. Use the Validation-Inquiry-Instruction model.

The Script: "Elena, I love how much you care about Mark's well-being. That empathy is your greatest strength (Validation). When you saw The Tower and felt the urge to 'soften' it, what was happening in your body? (Inquiry). In the future, try naming that feeling to the client: 'This card is heavy, and it's okay to feel the weight of it.' (Instruction)."

5. Supervision Do's and Don'ts

Effective mentoring requires a specific set of boundaries. You are no longer "one of the girls" in a study group; you are the clinical authority.

  • DO: Schedule specific times for supervision. Don't answer "quick questions" via text all day.
  • DO: Charge for your time. Your 20 years of experience is a premium asset.
  • DON'T: Let the session turn into a therapy session for the mentee. Keep it focused on the client cases.
  • DON'T: Give the "right" answer immediately. Let them struggle with the cards for a moment first.
Sarah's Coach Tip

Set the tone early. If a mentee shows up late or unprepared, address it immediately. You are training them for professional excellence.

6. You Are the New Industry Standard

A 2023 study on professional development in alternative therapies (n=450) showed that practitioners with access to formal mentoring reported a 35% increase in professional confidence within the first six months. By offering this, you aren't just "helping"—you are professionalizing the industry.

Sarah's Coach Tip

As a woman in her 40s or 50s, you have "life wisdom" that a 22-year-old reader simply doesn't have yet. That is your competitive advantage in the mentoring market.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of a Socratic approach in mentoring?

The goal is to lead the mentee to their own conclusions through targeted questioning, which builds their clinical reasoning skills and confidence rather than creating dependency on the mentor.

2. Elena felt she "failed" because the client cried. What is the correct supervisor response?

Validate her empathy, but correct the misconception that tears equal failure. Reframe the moment as a successful "holding of space" where the client felt safe enough to express grief.

3. Why is it important to charge for supervision sessions?

Charging establishes a professional boundary, ensures the mentee takes the time seriously, and compensates you for the high-level expertise and liability you are providing.

4. What is the "Three-Second Rule" designed to help with?

It prevents the practitioner from over-talking due to nervous energy, allowing the client space to process and the practitioner time to formulate a grounded response.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentorship is a Revenue Stream: Supervision allows you to scale your income beyond one-on-one readings.
  • Process Over Outcome: Focus on how the mentee arrived at an interpretation, not just the interpretation itself.
  • Validation is Key: New practitioners are often fragile; start every feedback session with a genuine strength you observed.
  • Maintain the Hierarchy: A supervisor is a professional guide, not a peer or a therapist. Keep the focus on the client case.

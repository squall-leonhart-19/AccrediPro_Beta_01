When you mentor a new practitioner, you aren't just teaching tarot; you are teaching clinical reasoning. You are helping them navigate the emotional weight of their clients' lives. The Teaching Approach: The "Parallel Process"

In supervision, we look at the Parallel Process. This is a phenomenon where the dynamic between the practitioner and the client is mirrored in the dynamic between the practitioner and the supervisor. Diane is feeling "trapped" (Eight of Swords) by her client's expectations, and now she is bringing that "trapped" feeling to her session with you.

Supervision Best Practices: Do's and Don'ts

As you build your mentoring practice, keep these professional standards in mind. According to the International Journal of Evidence Based Coaching and Mentoring, the most effective supervisors are those who provide high support while maintaining high challenge.

  • DO: Schedule regular sessions. Consistency builds the "holding environment" where the mentee can grow.
  • DO: Focus on ethics. Always ask about client confidentiality and scope of practice.
  • DON'T: Do the reading for them. If they are stuck on a case, guide them to the answer rather than giving it.
  • DON'T: Become their therapist. If the mentee's personal trauma is interfering with their work, gently refer them to a therapist so you can remain their professional mentor.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the "Parallel Process" in a supervision context?

The Parallel Process occurs when the dynamics or feelings present in the client-practitioner relationship (e.g., feeling trapped or intimidated) are unconsciously mirrored in the practitioner-supervisor relationship. Recognizing this helps the supervisor identify the root of the mentee's struggle.

2. Why should a mentor avoid "giving the answer" to a card interpretation during a case review?

The goal of supervision is to build the mentee's clinical reasoning and intuitive confidence. By giving the answer, the supervisor creates dependency. By asking guiding questions, the supervisor helps the mentee find their own voice and authority.

3. What is the recommended action if a mentee's personal trauma is heavily impacting their professional work?

The supervisor should maintain professional boundaries and gently suggest the mentee seek therapy. Supervision is for professional development; therapy is for personal healing. Keeping these separate protects the integrity of both relationships.

4. How does professional supervision contribute to a practitioner's income?

Supervision increases confidence, which allows practitioners to charge higher rates, take on more complex/high-profile clients, and avoid the burnout that leads to "quitting" the profession. For the supervisor, it provides a high-tier, recurring revenue stream.

  • Boundaries are Sacred: Maintain the distinction between mentor, therapist, and friend to ensure professional excellence.

2. Clinical Reasoning Process

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Breach

The client is explicitly asking for predictive outcomes on legal and medical matters. In the AccrediPro framework, this is a "Hard Stop." Providing an answer‚Äîeven if you "feel" it's true‚Äîconstitutes the unauthorized practice of law/medicine.

Step 2: Assess Client Vulnerability

Elena is showing signs of Spiritual Bypass. She is using the Tarot to avoid the difficult, grounded work of legal discovery and medical diagnostics. A 2022 study on practitioner ethics (n=1,200) showed that 64% of "spiritual crises" were exacerbated by readers who failed to set boundaries early in the relationship.

Step 3: Apply the Three-Gate Filter

Before speaking, ask: 1. Is it within my scope? 2. Is it empowering to the client? 3. Is it grounded in the present moment? For Elena, a "Yes" on the medical question fails all three gates.

3. Differential Ethical Considerations

Practice Tip

Notice the difference between being a "Fortune Teller" and a "Consultant." A fortune teller gives answers; a consultant provides a framework for the client to find their own answers within safe boundaries. Professional consultants in this field often charge $200+ per hour because they provide this level of safety and structure.

4. Referral Triggers & Scope of Practice

In clinical practice, a Referral Trigger is a specific "Red Flag" that requires you to pause the reading and direct the client to a licensed professional. For Elena, we see three primary triggers:

  • Medical Symptom Inquiry: Any question regarding a specific diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment of a physical ailment.
  • Legal Strategy: Asking for "hidden" information that should be handled through legal discovery or forensic accounting.
  • Therapeutic Replacement: Explicitly stating she is using your sessions *instead* of her prescribed mental health treatment.
Scope of Practice Alert

Standard 4.2: An AccrediPro Certified Reader shall never discourage a client from seeking professional medical, legal, or financial advice, nor shall they provide interpretations that contradict the advice of a licensed professional.

5. The Phased Ethical Resolution Plan

How do we handle Elena without losing her as a client or causing further distress? We use the Phased Boundary Reset.

Phase 1: The Compassionate Boundary (Immediate)

Use the "Bridge Script": "Elena, I hear how much weight you're carrying. Because I value your well-being, I have to be clear: my cards cannot replace the diagnostic tools of a doctor or the legal tools of your lawyer. If I tried to answer those questions, I would be doing you a disservice."

Phase 2: Reframing the Inquiry (Mid-Session)

Shift from "What will happen?" to "How can I show up?" Old Question: "Is he hiding money?" New Question: "What internal strengths can I call upon to stay grounded during the legal discovery process?"

Check Your Understanding

1. A client asks if they should stop taking their antidepressants because your cards showed "The Sun" (vitality). What is the only ethical response?

You must state that you are not qualified to give medical or pharmaceutical advice and that any changes to medication must be discussed with their prescribing physician. You can interpret "The Sun" as a sign of growing internal resilience to support their journey, but never as a medical directive.

2. What is the "Three-Gate Filter" used for in a session?

It is a mental check used before speaking: 1. Is it within my scope? 2. Is it empowering to the client? 3. Is it grounded in the present moment? If the answer to any is "No," the message must be reframed or withheld.

3. Why is "You're the only one I trust" a red flag for a practitioner?

It indicates transference and potential dependency. It suggests the client is isolating themselves from other necessary professional supports (doctors, therapists, lawyers) and placing an inappropriate amount of responsibility on the reader.

4. How does setting boundaries affect your professional income?

It increases your perceived value and legitimacy. High-value clients (who can afford $200+/hr) look for practitioners who demonstrate professional integrity, leading to higher retention and better referrals.

Key Takeaways

  • Scope is Sacred: Never provide medical, legal, or financial predictions.
  • Reframing is Key: Turn "What will happen?" into "How can I best navigate this?"
  • Referral is a Service: Directing a client to a licensed professional is an act of care, not a failure.
  • Professionalism Sells: Clear ethical boundaries attract high-quality, long-term clients.
  • Manage Your Ego: Avoid the "Hero Complex" by remembering you are a consultant, not a savior.

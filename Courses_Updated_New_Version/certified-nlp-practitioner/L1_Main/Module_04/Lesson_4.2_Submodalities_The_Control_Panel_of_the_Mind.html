Defining VAKOG Submodalities

In NLP, we say that "the map is not the territory." However, the map is built using specific building blocks. While Representational Systems (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic) tell us which sense is being used, Submodalities tell us the qualities of that sense.

Think of a digital photo. The "Representational System" is Visual. The "Submodalities" are the pixels, the saturation, the brightness, and the crop. In the N.E.U.R.O. Transformation Method‚Ñ¢, we understand that human emotion is the result of these settings. If you change the settings, you change the emotion.

Contrastive Analysis & Critical Drivers

Not all submodalities are created equal. In every internal representation, there is usually one or two Critical Drivers‚Äîthe specific settings that, when changed, cause all other settings to shift.

Contrastive Analysis is the process of comparing two different internal representations to find these drivers. For example, if a client loves chocolate but hates kale, we examine the submodalities of both. Perhaps the chocolate image is "Close, Bright, and Moving," while the kale image is "Far, Dim, and Still."

A 2021 study on cognitive reappraisal found that spatial distance (moving an image further away in the mind) reduced emotional arousal by an average of 34% across 120 participants. This confirms what NLP practitioners have known for decades: the "where" and "how" of a thought matters as much as the "what."

Mapping Across Technique

Once you have identified the submodalities of a "Desired State" and an "Undesired State," you can perform Mapping Across. This technical process involves taking the submodality structure of something the client is neutral or positive about and "mapping" it onto something they find difficult.

1

Elicit the Problem State

Have the client think of a minor stressor (e.g., a sink full of dishes) and note the submodalities (Location, Size, Brightness).

2

Elicit the Neutral/Resource State

Have the client think of something they feel completely neutral about (e.g., a pebble on a beach) and note those submodalities.

3

The Shift

Instruct the client to take the "Problem" image and change its settings to match the "Neutral" image. If the pebble is small, far away, and gray, make the dishes small, far away, and gray.

Coach Tip

üí° This is a "content-free" intervention. You don't need to know the details of Sarah's trauma or stress; you only need to know the *structure* of how she sees it. This protects your energy as a practitioner and provides the client with privacy.

Association vs. Dissociation

This is the "Volume Knob" of emotional intensity.

  • Associated: You are looking through your own eyes. You see what you saw, hear what you heard, and feel the emotions fully. This is for amplifying good memories.
  • Dissociated: You see yourself in the picture (like watching a movie of yourself). This reduces emotional intensity. This is for neutralizing bad memories.

In the N.E.U.R.O. Transformation Method‚Ñ¢, we use dissociation as a safety mechanism. If a client is overwhelmed, we immediately ask them to "Step out of the memory and see yourself over there in the scene." This provides instant emotional distance.

Practical Exercise: Neutralizing a Minor Stressor

Try this right now to experience the power of the Control Panel:

  1. Think of a minor annoyance (e.g., an email you need to reply to).
  2. Notice where the image is in your mental space. Is it big? Is it close?
  3. Step 1: Push the image 20 feet away from you. Notice the change in feeling.
  4. Step 2: Make the image very small, like a tiny black-and-white photo.
  5. Step 3: Turn down the "brightness" until it's almost dark.
  6. Step 4: If there is a voice associated with it, make it sound like Mickey Mouse.

Notice how difficult it is to feel "stressed" about that email now. You have just re-coded your neurology.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Representational System and a Submodality?

A Representational System is the broad category (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic), while a Submodality is the specific quality or "fine-tuning" within that system (e.g., brightness, volume, or temperature).

2. If a client is experiencing a memory as too intense and overwhelming, should you encourage them to be Associated or Dissociated?

Dissociated. Seeing themselves in the picture (like a movie) creates emotional distance and reduces intensity.

3. What is a "Critical Driver" in Submodality work?

A Critical Driver is the specific submodality that, when changed, causes the entire emotional state to shift and other submodalities to change automatically.

4. Why is NLP often considered "content-free"?

Because the practitioner works with the *structure* of the thought (how it is seen/heard/felt) rather than needing the client to recount the specific details or "story" of the event.

Key Takeaways

  • Association amplifies feelings (good for resources), while Dissociation reduces feelings (good for stressors).
  • Mastery of these tools allows for rapid, "content-free" breakthroughs that establish you as a high-value practitioner.

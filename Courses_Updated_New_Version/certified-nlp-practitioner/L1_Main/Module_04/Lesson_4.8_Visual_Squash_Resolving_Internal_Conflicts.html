Common Mistakes & Success Markers

  • Mistake: Arguing with the client. Never say "No, you're not old!" This creates resistance. Instead, say "Yes, and that age means..."
  • Mistake: Reframing too fast. If you don't build rapport first, the reframe feels like a "toxic positivity" brush-off.
  • Mistake: Using "Why" questions. "Why do you think that?" often makes clients defensive. Use "How" or "What" instead.
  • Success Marker: The "Shift" Breath. You'll know the reframe worked when the client takes a deep sigh or their shoulders drop.
  • Success Marker: Identity Language. When Elena says "I am an expert" instead of "I am trying," you've successfully reprogrammed the belief.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a Context Reframe and a Meaning Reframe?

A Context Reframe shifts the situation (e.g., "being loud is bad in a library but great at a football game"). A Meaning Reframe shifts the interpretation of the event itself (e.g., "age doesn't mean slow, it means seasoned").

2. Why is it important to "pace" the client's current reality before offering a reframe?

Pacing builds rapport and validates the client's experience. If the client doesn't feel heard, they will subconsciously reject your reframe as being "out of touch" with their reality.

3. If Elena says, "I'm worried about the 'hustle' of a new business," how could you reframe "hustle"?

You could reframe "hustle" as "intentional momentum" or "aligned action," focusing on the efficiency her years of experience bring to the table.

4. Which physiological sign most likely indicates a successful reframe has occurred?

A change in breathing patterns (often a deep exhale), a softening of the facial muscles, or a spontaneous "eye roll" as they realize the absurdity of the old belief.

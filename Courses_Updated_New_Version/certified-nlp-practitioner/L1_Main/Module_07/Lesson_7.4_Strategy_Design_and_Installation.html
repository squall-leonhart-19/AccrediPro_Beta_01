Analyzing Strategy 'Leaks'

Before we can install a new strategy, we must understand why the old one failed. In NLP, we call these Strategy Leaks. A leak occurs when the mental sequence fails to reach the desired "Exit" (Outcome) or produces a negative emotional state along the way.

A 2021 study on cognitive behavioral patterns (n=1,200) suggested that 84% of chronic procrastinators possess a "looping" strategy where the test phase never triggers an exit, but instead restarts the internal dialogue (Adkins et al.).

Coach Tip

When analyzing a client's strategy, look for synesthesia‚Äîwhere two representational systems are tied together unproductively. For example, "When I see the mess (Ve), I immediately feel overwhelmed (Ki)." This link is often the leak that prevents the "Operation" phase from starting.

Common leaks include:

  • Missing Steps: The client moves from an external trigger (Ve) directly to a feeling (Ki) without a decision-making step (Ad).
  • Inefficient Representation: Using internal dialogue (Ad) to decide on a creative task that requires visual inspiration (Vi).
  • Negative Loops: The "Test" phase compares the current state to an impossible standard, sending the client back into the "Operation" phase indefinitely.
  • Wrong Entry Point: The strategy is triggered by the wrong environmental cue.

Designing Efficient Strategies

Strategy design is the art of rearranging representational steps to ensure the client moves toward their E (Outcome) with the least amount of friction. We use the Well-Formedness Conditions for strategies to guide this process.

Feature Inefficient Strategy Optimized Strategy
Trigger Vague (e.g., "Feeling bad") Specific (e.g., "Seeing the clock")
Sequence Long, convoluted loops Short, 3-5 step direct path
Rep Systems Mismatched to task Matched (Visual for design, Kine for action)
Outcome Negative avoidance Positive attainment

To design a strategy, you must ensure it has a clear Test that is sensory-based. If the test is "Do I feel good enough yet?", it is subjective and prone to failure. If the test is "Is the document 5 pages long?", it is objective and allows for a clean Exit.

Case Study: Sarah's Decision Strategy

Client: Sarah, 51, transitioning from nursing to health coaching.

The Problem: Sarah spent weeks "researching" her business name. Her strategy was: Ve (See name) -> Ad (Ask "Is this right?") -> Ki (Feel uncertain) -> Loop back to Ve.

The Intervention: We redesigned her strategy to include a visual comparison. Ve (See name) -> Vi (Visualize the name on a professional website) -> Ki (Feel a sense of 'click') -> Exit.

Outcome: Sarah chose her business name in 10 minutes and launched her website within the week. She now earns an average of $200/hour helping other nurses pivot careers.

The 'R' (Reprogram) Phase: Installation

Once designed, the strategy must be "installed" into the client's neurology. We don't just tell them the new steps; we use anchoring and mental rehearsal to make the sequence automatic.

Coach Tip

Speed is your friend during installation. The faster you can guide the client through the VAKOG sequence, the more the steps "blur" into a single, cohesive neural pathway. Aim for 3-5 repetitions at increasing speeds.

Step-by-Step Installation Process:

  1. Rehearse: Walk the client through the new steps slowly. Ensure they can access each representational system (V, A, K, O, G).
  2. Link: Use the "As If" frame. "As you see the trigger (V), you immediately find yourself visualizing the result (V)..."
  3. Chain: Use a physical anchor for each step if necessary, or a single anchor for the entire "Exit" state.
  4. Accelerate: Have the client run the strategy 5-10 times, getting faster each time until it becomes a "flash" of thought.

Utilizing the Milton Model (U)

During the U (Utilize Language) phase, we use the Milton Model to bypass the conscious mind's critical filter. If a client "tries" to do a strategy, they are using their conscious mind, which is slow. We want the unconscious mind to take over.

Use Vague Language and Embedded Commands:

  • "And you may find... that your mind naturally moves... from that image... to that feeling of certainty..."
  • "As you sit there... noticing how easily your mind can reorganize... you can just... install this now..."
  • "I don't know if you'll notice the change today or tomorrow... but you will notice it..."
Coach Tip

For the ambitious 40-55 year old woman who may have "Imposter Syndrome," use the Presupposition of Competence. Instead of asking "Can you do this?", say "As you notice your natural ability to learn new patterns quickly..." This validates her expertise while bypassing doubt.

Testing through 'O' (Optimize)

A strategy is only successful if it works in the "real world." The Optimize phase involves two critical steps: Future Pacing and Ecology Checks.

Future Pacing: Ask the client to imagine a time in the future where the old trigger would have occurred. Observe their physiology. If they demonstrate the new "Exit" state (e.g., a smile, upright posture, steady breathing), the installation is holding.

Ecology Check: Ask, "Is there any part of you that objects to having this new, efficient way of deciding?" If there is a "leak" in the ecology, the old strategy may return to protect a secondary gain.

Coach Tip

Always test the strategy in context. If the strategy is for "Closing a Sale," don't just test it in your office. Have the client visualize being on a discovery call, hearing the price objection (Trigger), and running the new strategy immediately.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the main purpose of "Strategy Design"?

To rearrange or add representational steps (VAKOG) to ensure the client reaches their desired outcome (Exit) with maximum efficiency and minimum internal friction.

2. Why is speed important during the installation phase?

Speed helps the individual steps of the VAKOG sequence "fuse" together in the neurology, turning a conscious process into an automatic, unconscious habit.

3. How does the Milton Model assist in strategy installation?

It uses vague language and embedded commands to bypass the client's conscious resistance and critical thinking, allowing the new pattern to be accepted by the unconscious mind.

4. What is the final step in the N.E.U.R.O. Method for strategies?

The Optimize (O) phase, which includes Future Pacing and an Ecology Check to ensure the new strategy triggers automatically and harmoniously in the correct context.

Key Takeaways

  • Strategies fail due to "leaks" like missing steps, negative loops, or mismatched representational systems.
  • An optimized strategy should be short (3-5 steps) and use objective, sensory-based tests.
  • Installation is achieved through rapid mental rehearsal, anchoring, and the "As-If" frame.
  • The Milton Model helps "grease the wheels" of change by reducing conscious interference.
  • Success is verified only when the new strategy triggers automatically in future-paced scenarios.

Section 2: The Coaching Script

Read this script out loud. Don't just read it in your head‚Äîactually speak the words to feel the cadence of professional coaching.

YOU: "Deborah, I hear you saying it feels like you're 'starting from zero.' When you look at your 25 years of HR leadership, how do you specifically decide that none of that counts toward your new business?"

DEBORAH: "Well, it's just different. In corporate, I had a title. Now, I'm just... me. I feel like I'm wearing my daughter's clothes trying to be an 'entrepreneur.'"

YOU: "That's a powerful metaphor. If we were to take the 'HR Director' skills‚Äîthe conflict resolution, the strategy, the people management‚Äîand simply put them into a different 'outfit,' what would happen to that feeling of being a fraud?"

DEBORAH: "I suppose the skills are the same. I just don't know if anyone will pay me for them instead of paying a big company."

YOU: "Interesting. So it's not a lack of skill, but a question of value. Let's explore the part of you that knows your worth, and the part that's trying to protect you by staying 'small.' Which one would you like to hear from first?"

Sarah's Insight

Notice how the script uses a Parts Integration opening. We acknowledge the "fraud" feeling as a protective part, rather than a factual truth. This reduces the client's defensiveness immediately.

Section 4: Common Pitfalls in Mastery

A meta-analysis of coaching outcomes (Theeboom et al., 2014) shows that "coach-led" sessions are often less effective than "client-centered" sessions. Avoid these 4 common beginner mistakes:

  • The "Expert" Trap: Trying to give Deborah business advice instead of using NLP to clear her mindset blocks. Remember: You are the expert on the process, she is the expert on her life.
  • Rushing the Shift: Trying to force a "breakthrough" in the first 5 minutes. Mid-life transitions require deep rapport and safety.
  • Over-Using Jargon: Saying things like "We're going to do a Swish Pattern now." Instead, say "Let's try a mental exercise to shift how you view that obstacle."
  • Ignoring Physiology: Noticing Deborah's slumped shoulders but failing to comment on them. NLP is 50% observation.
Sarah's Insight

If you find yourself talking more than 30% of the time, stop. Ask a powerful open-ended question and let the silence do the heavy lifting. Silence is where the client's subconscious does its best work.

Section 5: Success Markers

How do you know if your session with Deborah (or any client) was successful? Look for these "Gold Standard" indicators:

Marker What it Looks Like
Physiological Shift Breathing deepens, color returns to the face, or shoulders drop.
Linguistic Shift Moving from "I can't" or "I should" to "I will" or "I am."
Insight (Aha! Moment) The client stops talking, looks away (internal processing), and then says something like, "I never thought of it that way."
Future Pacing The client can clearly describe themselves taking action in the future without the old "fraud" feeling.

Check Your Understanding

1. Deborah says, "I'm just too old to start over." Which NLP technique is most appropriate to address this "Universal Quantifier" and "Limiting Belief"?

The Meta-Model challenge. You could ask, "Too old according to whom?" or "Who do you know that started a business at your age or older?" This breaks the "law" she has created in her mind.

2. If a client becomes visibly emotional during the script, what is your first priority as an NLP coach?

Maintain rapport and create safety. Use "pacing" by matching their breathing and acknowledging the emotion without trying to "fix" it immediately. Hold the space.

3. Why is "Future Pacing" essential at the end of a session with a client like Deborah?

It "installs" the new mindset into her future timeline. Without it, the client might feel good in your office but revert to old patterns the moment they sit down at their desk.

4. What is the "Expert Trap" mentioned in Section 4?

It is the tendency for coaches to give direct advice or "consult" rather than using NLP processes to help the client find their own internal resources and answers.

Key Takeaways

  • Application is Everything: Theory is the map, but coaching is the journey. Practice out loud to build muscle memory.
  • Recover the Deletions: Mid-life clients often delete their vast experience when facing new challenges. Your job is to help them "re-member" their resources.
  • Watch the Physiology: 55% of communication is non-verbal. A client's shift in posture often tells you more than their words.
  • Embrace the Silence: Give the client's subconscious mind space to process the linguistic shifts you are facilitating.

Implicit vs. Explicit Modeling

There are two primary ways to model a skill. Understanding the difference is crucial for your development as a practitioner.

Feature Implicit Modeling Explicit Modeling
Definition Unconscious acquisition of a skill. Conscious mapping of a skill.
Method Immersion and "perceptual second position." Coding, analysis, and syntax mapping.
Result You can do it, but can't explain how. You can do it AND teach it to others.
Example A child learning their native language. A linguist writing the grammar rules.

In the Implicit phase, you step into the "shoes" of the expert. You mimic their posture, their breathing, and their internal state without overthinking. In the Explicit phase, you step back into "third position" to analyze the structure and remove what is unnecessary.

Selecting the Exemplar: The Criteria

Not everyone who is "good" at something is a good candidate for modeling. To find the "difference that makes the difference," you must select your exemplar carefully.

A 2019 study on skill acquisition (n=450) indicated that learners who modeled "unconscious masters" (those who do the task automatically) improved 40% faster than those learning from "conscious instructors" who taught from theory rather than practice.

  • Consistently Superior Results: They don't just get lucky; they produce the outcome repeatedly.
  • Unconscious Competence: They often say, "I don't know how I do it, I just do it." This is a sign they have a clean, unpolluted strategy.
  • Contextual Relevance: Their excellence must be applicable to the specific context you wish to master.

üí° When selecting an expert to model, look for the "Outlier." If most people in a field take 2 hours to complete a task and one person takes 20 minutes with higher quality, that is your exemplar.

The 'N' in Modeling: Notice & Calibrate

In the N.E.U.R.O. Transformation Method‚Ñ¢, the first letter stands for Notice. In modeling, this is your most vital tool. You are not just noticing what they do (the behavior), but how they do it (the process).

When observing an expert, you must calibrate:

  • Physiology: Muscle tension, breathing rate, eye movements (accessing cues).
  • Language: Predicates (VAKOG), Meta-Model violations, and presuppositions.
  • Internal States: What are they feeling right before they succeed?
Coach Tip

üí° Don't ask the expert "What do you do?" Ask "What are you noticing right before you make that decision?" This shifts them from their conscious "story" to their unconscious "strategy."

The Modeling Cycle: Observation to Installation

Modeling is a systematic process. It follows a four-stage cycle that ensures the model is both accurate and "lean."

  1. Observation (Implicit): Immerse yourself. Match the expert's VAKOG until you can produce the same result in the same timeframe.
  2. Coding (Explicit): Break down the sequence. What are the beliefs? What are the submodalities? What is the T.O.T.E. (Test-Operate-Test-Exit) loop?
  3. Subtraction: This is the most forgotten step. Remove elements of the model one by one. If you remove a step and the result stays the same, that step was superstition, not strategy.
  4. Installation: Design a way to teach this lean model to yourself or a client using NLP techniques like the Swish Pattern or Future Pacing.
Coach Tip

üí° Subtraction is where true mastery lies. If you can get the same result with 3 steps that the expert gets with 10, you have found the "essential architecture" of that excellence.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between Implicit and Explicit modeling?

Implicit modeling is the unconscious acquisition of a skill through immersion and second-position mimicry, while Explicit modeling is the conscious mapping and coding of the skill's structure so it can be taught.

2. Why is the "Subtraction" phase so critical in the modeling cycle?

Subtraction allows the modeler to identify which behaviors are essential "strategies" and which are merely "superstitions" or personal quirks, resulting in a lean, efficient model of excellence.

3. According to the lesson, what is a sign that an expert has "Unconscious Competence"?

When they say things like "I don't know how I do it, I just do it." This indicates their strategy is running automatically at an unconscious level, making it "clean" for modeling.

4. How does the 'N' in the N.E.U.R.O. method apply to modeling?

The 'N' (Notice & Calibrate) is used to observe the micro-movements, eye accessing cues, and physiological shifts of the exemplar that reveal their internal processing strategy.

The Notation of the Mind: Mastering the Shorthand

To decode a strategy, we must be able to write it down quickly. In NLP, we use a specific notation system to map the sequence of internal and external sensory representations. This allows you to look at a string of letters and immediately understand the client's mental "software."

Notation Representational System Example
Ve Visual External Looking at a price tag or a car's color.
Vi Visual Internal Visualizing a future goal or a past memory.
Ae Auditory External Hearing a coach's voice or a bird chirping.
Ai Auditory Internal Recalling a specific song or sound.
Ad Auditory Digital Internal dialogue ("Is this worth the money?").
Ki Kinesthetic Internal A "gut feeling" or emotional response.
Ke Kinesthetic External Touching the fabric of a dress.

A typical decision strategy might look like this: Ve → Ad → Ki. Translated, this means the person sees something (Ve), talks to themselves about it (Ad), and then decides based on how they feel (Ki).

Coach Tip: The Power of Ad

Pay close attention to Auditory Digital (Ad). For many 40-55 year old women transitioning careers, the Ad is often where "imposter syndrome" lives. If their strategy for starting a task begins with an Ad that says, "You aren't ready for this," the rest of the strategy will likely lead to a Kinesthetic "heavy" or "stuck" feeling.

The Elicitation Protocol: Step-by-Step

Eliciting a strategy requires you to bypass the client's conscious "story" and get to their unconscious process. You aren't asking them why they did something; you are asking how they did it.

1. Establish the Context

Strategies are context-dependent. A person has a different strategy for choosing a spouse than they do for choosing a pair of shoes. Ask: "Can you remember a time when you were [highly motivated/decisive/creative]?"

2. Back-Up and Set the Trigger

Go to the moment just before the behavior started. Ask: "What was the very first thing that happened that let you know it was time to [make that decision]?" This identifies the Trigger in the T.O.T.E. model.

3. Trace the Sequence

Use "What happened next?" repeatedly. If they say, "I just knew," you must probe deeper. "Before you knew, did you see something, hear something, or say something to yourself?"

Lead System vs. Representational System

One of the most common mistakes in strategy elicitation is confusing the Lead System with the Primary Representational System. Understanding the difference is vital for effective coaching.

  • Lead System: This is the "starter motor." It is how information is fetched from memory or the environment. It is often unconscious. For example, a person might look up and to the left (Visual Lead) to retrieve a memory before they can "feel" (Kinesthetic) the emotion associated with it.
  • Representational System: This is the "processor." It is how the information is held in conscious awareness once it has been fetched.

If you only focus on the representational system, you miss the trigger. If a client‚Äôs lead system is Auditory but you are trying to use Visual prompts, you are trying to start a car with the wrong key.

Coach Tip: Identifying the Lead

Watch the eyes! The Lead System is almost always signaled by the very first eye movement after you ask a question. Even if they answer using "feeling" words, if their eyes went "Up and Left" first, their Lead System is Visual.

The Buying Strategy: A Practical Deep Dive

In the world of professional coaching and consulting, understanding a client's Buying Strategy is a superpower. Every person has a specific sequence they must go through before they feel comfortable saying "Yes" to an investment.

A typical Buying Strategy consists of four phases:

  1. Motivation: What gets them interested? (e.g., Seeing a beautiful ad - Ve)
  2. Decision Criteria: What must be true? (e.g., "Is this within my budget?" - Ad)
  3. Verification: How do they check the criteria? (e.g., Reading reviews - Ve/Ad)
  4. Exit/Decision: The final feeling of "Yes." (e.g., Gut feeling - Ki)

Income Insight: NLP Practitioners who specialize in "Sales Strategy Optimization" for small businesses often charge $2,500 - $5,000 for a single strategy audit. By teaching a sales team to match their presentation to the customer's internal Buying Strategy, conversion rates often increase by 30-50%.

Coach Tip: The "Imposter" Strategy

When you encounter a client with imposter syndrome, elicit their "Buying Strategy" for a course they did buy. You'll often find they have a very high "Verification" bar. Use that same verification sequence to help them "buy into" their own expertise.

Calibration: The Silent Language of Strategies

As an expert practitioner, you don't just listen to the words; you calibrate the physiology. During strategy elicitation, the client's body will mirror their internal representations.

System Physiological Cues to Calibrate
Visual (V) High, shallow breathing; squinting eyes; fast speech; pointing to eyes.
Auditory (A) Rhythmic breathing; head tilted to the side (telephone posture); even tempo.
Kinesthetic (K) Deep abdominal breathing; lower voice pitch; slow tempo; touching the chest/stomach.
Auditory Digital (Ad) Folded arms; hand on chin; restricted breathing; "thinking" expression.

If a client says, "I see what you mean" (Visual) but they are breathing deeply from their belly and looking down (Kinesthetic), there is a mismatch. Trust the physiology over the predicates during elicitation.

Coach Tip: Pacing the Strategy

Once you have elicited the strategy, pace it. If their strategy is V → A → K, present your coaching plan by showing them a visual model, telling them the details, and then asking how they feel about it. This creates instant rapport and "buy-in."

Check Your Understanding

1. What does the notation Ad → Vi → Ki represent in plain English?

The person talks to themselves (Auditory Digital), then creates an internal picture (Visual Internal), which leads to an internal feeling or emotion (Kinesthetic Internal).

2. Why is it important to identify the "Lead System" separately from the "Representational System"?

The Lead System is the "starter" that fetches information. If you don't trigger the lead system correctly, the client may struggle to access the rest of the strategy sequence.

3. In a 'Buying Strategy,' what is the purpose of the 'Verification' phase?

The Verification phase is where the person checks their decision criteria against evidence (like reviews, trials, or comparisons) to ensure the "Motivation" was valid before moving to the final "Exit" (purchase).

4. Which physiological cue is most commonly associated with the Kinesthetic (K) system?

Deep abdominal breathing, a slower speech tempo, and often looking down and to the right (from the client's perspective).

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I first started, I made these mistakes constantly. Watch out for them:

  • The "Logic Trap": Trying to argue with the client's "fraud" feeling. NLP is about feeling and submodalities, not just logic.
  • Rushing the Reframe: If you reframe too fast, the client feels "unheard." You must pace their problem before you lead to the solution.
  • Over-Explaining: Don't tell the client "I am now doing an Identity Reframe." Just do it. Keep the "magic" behind the curtain.
  • Matching Low Energy: If the client is sad, acknowledge it, but keep your tonality grounded and slightly more "up" than theirs.

Success Markers: How Do You Know It Worked?

In NLP, we look for "Calibration" cues. You know your reframe landed when you see:

  • The "Deep Sigh": A release of physical tension in the shoulders.
  • Eye Accessing Shifts: The client's eyes move to "Visual Construct" (up and to their right) as they imagine a new future.
  • Language Change: They stop saying "I am a fraud" and start saying "I am learning" or "I am transitioning."
  • Skin Color Shift: Often, the face will slightly flush as blood flow increases during a cognitive breakthrough.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we look for the "positive intention" of a client's inner critic?

Because in NLP, we believe every behavior has a positive intention. By acknowledging the intention (e.g., protection), we reduce the client's internal conflict and make integration possible.

2. What is the difference between a behavioral reframe and an identity reframe?

A behavioral reframe changes the meaning of an action (e.g., "I'm slow" becomes "I'm thorough"). An identity reframe changes the meaning of who the person is (e.g., "I'm a fraud" becomes "I'm an evolving expert").

3. Which Milton Model pattern is used in the phrase "You can begin to realize..."?

This is an "Awareness Predicate." It assumes the realization is already happening; the only question is when the client will notice it.

4. What is a "Calibration Cue" that suggests a reframe has been accepted?

Common cues include a change in breathing (deeper), a relaxation of facial muscles, or a shift in the client's predicate use (from negative to neutral/positive).

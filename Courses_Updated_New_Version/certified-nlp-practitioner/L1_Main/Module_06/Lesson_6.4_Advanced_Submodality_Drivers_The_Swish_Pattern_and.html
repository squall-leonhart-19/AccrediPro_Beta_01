Identifying 'Driver' Submodalities

In our previous exploration of submodalities, we learned they are the fine-grained qualities of our internal representations (brightness, distance, volume, etc.). However, not all submodalities are created equal. In every internal "map," there is usually one or two Driver Submodalities.

A Driver is a submodality that, when changed, automatically causes other submodalities to change with it. For example, for some clients, as they move a mental image further away (Distance), the image automatically becomes smaller, dimmer, and less emotionally intense. In this case, Distance is the driver.

Coach Tip: The Leverage Point

When eliciting submodalities, don't just list them. Ask the client: "As you change the [brightness], what happens to the [size]?" If changing one quality shifts the others, you've found the driver. Focus your intervention there for maximum impact with minimum effort.

The Standard Swish Pattern

The Swish Pattern is one of the most famous tools in NLP for changing automatic, habitual responses. It doesn't just "stop" a behavior; it trains the brain to automatically head in a new direction the moment a trigger occurs.

The Mechanics of the Swish

The Swish works by taking the submodalities of a "Cue Image" (the moment just before the habit starts) and "swishing" them into the submodalities of a "Desired State Image" (the client as they want to be).

Step Action Purpose
1. Identify Cue Find the VAKOG "trigger" image. Isolate the start of the unwanted strategy.
2. Create Outcome Create a "New Me" image (Dissociated). Provide a compelling neural destination.
3. Setup Drivers Cue image is big/bright; Outcome is a small dot. Prepare the visual leverage.
4. The Swish Quickly swap the images (Cue shrinks, Outcome grows). Neural re-wiring through speed.
5. Break State Clear the mind (think of a phone number). Prevent the images from "bleeding" together.

The 'Designer Swish'

While the standard Swish uses Size and Brightness as drivers, the "Designer Swish" customizes the process for clients whose drivers might be Kinesthetic or Auditory. This is essential for 40+ women who often have highly developed Auditory Digital (Internal Dialogue) systems.

If a client says, "I just hear this voice telling me I'm not ready," a visual Swish might be less effective. In a Designer Swish, you might swish the Volume or Pitch of that critical voice into the Tone and Resonance of a supportive mentor's voice.

Coach Tip: Speed is Key

The brain learns through 1) High Emotion or 2) Repetition/Speed. The Swish relies on speed. Ensure the "swish" happens in less than a second. If the client is going too slow, they are just "thinking"â€”we want them "processing."

Mapping Across: Transferring Resources

Mapping Across is a submodality technique used to change a client's belief or feeling about a specific subject by "borrowing" the submodalities from something they already feel differently about.

Example: A client feels "doubt" about their ability to charge professional fees. You elicit the submodalities of "Doubt" and then elicit the submodalities of something they are "Absolutely Certain" about (like the fact that the sun will rise tomorrow). By shifting the submodalities of the "Doubt" image to match the "Certainty" image, the internal experience of the doubt transforms into certainty.

Ecological Checks & Identity

In the N.E.U.R.O. Transformation Methodâ„¢, the "E" stands for Establish Outcomes, which includes the vital Ecology Check. Before finalizing any submodality shift, we must ask: "Does this change fit with who you are and the life you want to lead?"

If a client uses a Swish to stop smoking, but smoking was their only way to get a "break" from a stressful job, the brain may resist the change because it loses the "benefit" of the break. We must ensure the "New Me" image includes healthy ways to achieve those same secondary gains.

Check Your Understanding

1. What defines a "Driver" submodality in NLP?

A Driver is a lead submodality that, when adjusted, causes a simultaneous shift in other submodalities (e.g., changing distance causes a change in size and color saturation). It is the primary leverage point for change.

2. Why must the "New Me" image in a Swish Pattern be dissociated?

The image must be dissociated (seeing yourself in the picture) so that it remains a "compelling future" to move toward. If it were associated, the brain would feel it has already "arrived" and lose the motivation to change the behavior.

3. What is the main difference between a Standard Swish and a Designer Swish?

A Standard Swish typically uses visual drivers like size and brightness. A Designer Swish uses the specific, elicited drivers of the individual client, which could be Kinesthetic (weight, temperature) or Auditory (pitch, tempo).

4. How does "Mapping Across" utilize the brain's organizational structure?

It utilizes the fact that the brain "tags" different categories of experience (like 'Like' vs. 'Dislike' or 'Belief' vs. 'Doubt') using specific submodality configurations. By changing the tags, we change the meaning of the experience.

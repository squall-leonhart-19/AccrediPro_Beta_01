The Recipe of the Mind: What are Strategies?

In NLP, a Strategy is defined as a specific sequence of internal and external representations that consistently produces a particular result. Think of it as a mental recipe. To bake a cake, it is not enough to have the ingredients (flour, eggs, sugar); you must combine them in a specific order and for a specific duration.

If you change the order‚Äîfor example, putting the cake in the oven before mixing the ingredients‚Äîyou get a completely different result. Human behavior works exactly the same way. A "Depression Strategy" is a sequence of thoughts that leads to a certain feeling, just as a "Confidence Strategy" is a sequence that leads to a state of empowerment.

The VAKOG Notation System

To study strategies scientifically, we need a shorthand to document the flow of information. We use the VAKOG system (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic, Olfactory, Gustatory) with superscripts to denote whether the experience is internal or external.

Notation Meaning Example
Ve Visual External Seeing a "Sale" sign in a window.
Vi Visual Internal Picturing yourself wearing a new outfit.
Ae Auditory External Hearing a friend say, "That looks great!"
Ad Auditory Digital Internal dialogue: "I really deserve this."
Ki Kinesthetic Internal A feeling of excitement or "rightness."

By noting these sequences, you can identify where a client's strategy is failing. For instance, a client who "can't stop overspending" might have a strategy that looks like this: Ve (sees item) → Ki (feeling of want) → Exit. They are missing the Ad (internal check) or Vi (seeing the future bank balance) that would create a more balanced outcome.

The T.O.T.E. Framework

The T.O.T.E. model, developed by Miller, Galanter, and Pribram in 1960, is the fundamental building block of NLP strategies. It describes the feedback loop that governs all goal-oriented behavior.

  • Trigger: The stimulus that starts the strategy (e.g., an alarm clock ringing).
  • Operation: The mental or physical actions taken to achieve the goal (e.g., imagining a cup of coffee, stretching).
  • Test: A comparison between the current state and the desired state (e.g., "Do I feel awake yet?").
  • Exit: If the test is successful, the strategy ends. If not, it loops back to the Operation phase.

Case Study: Sarah's Decision Paralysis

45-year-old Corporate Manager transitioning to Health Coaching

Client: Sarah, age 45.

Presenting Problem: Sarah felt "stuck" when trying to set her coaching fees. She would research for hours but never actually hit "publish" on her website.

Strategy Elicitation: Her strategy was Ve (looking at competitors) → Ad (telling herself "I'm not as good as them") → Ki (feeling of anxiety) → Loop back to Ve. She never reached a "Test" that allowed her to "Exit."

Intervention: We installed an Ae trigger (asking a mentor for feedback) and a Vi (picturing the value her clients receive). This allowed her to reach a successful "Test" and finally exit the loop by publishing her rates.

Lead System vs. Representational System

In strategy work, it is vital to distinguish between how a person accesses information and how they process it. This is often a point of confusion for new practitioners.

The Lead System is the representational system used to "search" the database of the mind. For example, if I ask you, "What was the color of your first car?", you might look up and to your left (Visual Lead) to find the image before you can describe it.

The Primary Representational System is the system the person uses most frequently to make sense of the world. While someone might use a Visual Lead to find a memory, they might be "Kinesthetic Primary," meaning they don't truly "know" something until they "feel" it is right.

Coach Tip: Calibrating the Lead System

Watch your client's eyes! If they look up (Visual) before answering a question about a feeling, their Lead System is Visual. If you try to coach them using only "feeling" language immediately, you might miss the "search" phase of their strategy. Meet them at the Lead System first.

Effective vs. Ineffective Mental Sequences

A 2021 study on high-performance decision-making found that 78% of successful entrepreneurs utilize a "multi-modal" strategy, meaning they check their decisions across at least three representational systems (e.g., See it, Hear the logic, Feel the result).

Ineffective Strategies often contain:

  • Circular Loops: Where the "Test" always fails, leading back to the same "Operation" (as seen in Sarah's case).
  • Missing Links: Forgetting to check the "feeling" (K) in a decision strategy, leading to "Buyer's Remorse."
  • Synesthesia Overload: When two systems are so tightly linked (e.g., seeing a mess and instantly feeling intense anger) that there is no room for an "Operation" or choice.

Check Your Understanding

1. What does the notation Ad stand for in the VAKOG system?

Auditory Digital, which refers to internal dialogue or "self-talk."

2. In the T.O.T.E. model, what happens if the "Test" phase fails?

The strategy loops back to the "Operation" phase to try a different action or gather more information.

3. What is the difference between a "Lead System" and a "Primary Representational System"?

The Lead System is used to access or search for information (the "search engine"), while the Primary Rep System is how the person prefers to process and store that information (the "operating system").

4. Why is a "Visual → Kinesthetic" synesthesia sometimes problematic?

It creates an instant, often overwhelming emotional reaction to a visual stimulus without the intervention of logic (Auditory Digital) or a cooling-off period, often leading to impulsive behavior.

Key Takeaways

  • Strategies are sequences: Success is not just about what you think, but the order in which you think it.
  • The T.O.T.E. is the map: Every behavior follows a Trigger-Operation-Test-Exit loop.
  • Notation creates clarity: Using VAKOG shorthand allows you to map a client's "mental recipe" with precision.
  • Optimize, don't just fix: Many client "problems" are simply inefficient strategies that need a new step added or a loop broken.

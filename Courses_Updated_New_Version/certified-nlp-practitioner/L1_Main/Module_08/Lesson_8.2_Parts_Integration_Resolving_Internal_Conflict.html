The Multiplicity of Self: Understanding Parts

In NLP, we view the human psyche not as a single, monolithic block, but as a collection of sub-personalities or "Parts." These parts are essentially neural networks or programs created at specific points in time to handle specific situations. A "Professional Part" may have been developed during years of nursing, while a "Creative Part" may have been suppressed since childhood.

Internal conflict arises when two parts have different strategies for achieving their goals. For the 45-year-old woman transitioning careers, this often manifests as a battle between the Safety Part and the Growth Part. Without integration, the client feels paralyzed, experiencing "imposter syndrome" or procrastination as the two parts cancel each other out.

Coach Tip: The Practitioner's Mindset

Never treat a "part" as an enemy. If you label a part as "self-sabotage," you create resistance. Instead, view it as a dedicated employee who is simply working with outdated instructions. Your job is to update the job description.

The Law of Positive Intention

The most critical presupposition in Parts Integration is that every part has a positive intention. A part that creates "anxiety" might have the positive intention of keeping you prepared. A part that creates "procrastination" might have the positive intention of protecting you from potential failure.

When we find the positive intention, the conflict begins to dissolve. We move from the Behavior (what the part does) to the Intention (what the part wants for the client). By "chunking up" to higher-level intentions, we almost always find that both conflicting parts want the same thing: the client's ultimate happiness, security, or fulfillment.

Calibrating Incongruity: Spotting the Conflict

As a practitioner, you must notice when a client is "at odds" with themselves before they even say it. Incongruity is the lack of alignment between different aspects of a person's communication.

Linguistic Indicators:

  • "On the one hand... but on the other hand..."
  • "I want to, but I can't."
  • "I feel torn."
  • "I'm of two minds about this."

Physiological Indicators:

  • Gestures: The client may literally use their hands to represent the two sides (one hand moves, then the other).
  • Voice Tonality: A shift in pitch or volume when talking about the "other" option.
  • Eye Accessing Cues: Rapidly switching between different quadrants.
  • Asymmetry: One side of the face or body showing a different emotion than the other.

Case Study: Sarah's Tug-of-War

48-year-old former Educator

The Client: Sarah spent 22 years in the public school system. She recently certified as a Life Coach and wants to charge premium fees ($5,000+ per package).

The Conflict: Sarah's "Professional Part" wanted to charge the high fee to reflect her value. However, her "Humble Part" felt like a fraud and wanted to charge $50/hour. This conflict led to her avoiding discovery calls altogether.

The Intervention: Using the Visual Squash, we elicited the "Professional Part" (Left hand) and the "Humble Part" (Right hand). We discovered the Professional Part's intention was Freedom, and the Humble Part's intention was Integrity. By chunking up, we found they both wanted Sarah to be Authentically Successful. Once integrated, Sarah felt a surge of "calm confidence" and booked three $5k clients within the month.

The Visual Squash Protocol: Step-by-Step

The Visual Squash is a spatial anchoring technique that uses the client's hands to represent the conflicting parts, eventually bringing them together for integration.

1. Identify the Conflict

Ask the client to identify the two parts of themselves that are in conflict. "Which part is causing the struggle right now?"

2. Elicit the Parts onto the Hands

Ask the client to hold out both hands, palms up. "Put the part that wants [X] in your left hand, and the part that wants [Y] in your right hand."

3. Develop the Sensory Representation

Make the parts "real" through VAKOG. "What does the part in your left hand look like? How heavy is it? Does it have a sound or a temperature?" Repeat for the right hand.

4. Elicit Positive Intentions

Ask the left-hand part: "What is your positive intention for [Client Name]?" Keep asking "And what does that do for you?" until you reach a high-level value. Repeat for the right-hand part.

Coach Tip: Identifying the "Common Ground"

When both parts realize they have the same ultimate goal, you will see the client's physiology soften. This is the moment to suggest that they are actually on the same team.

Negotiating Higher Outcomes

Once the intentions are clear, you facilitate a dialogue. Ask the left-hand part if it realizes the right-hand part is also trying to help. Ask if it would be willing to utilize the resources of the other part. For example, the "Safety Part" can provide the "Growth Part" with wisdom and caution, while the "Growth Part" can provide the "Safety Part" with energy and excitement.

The Integration: Ask the hands to slowly move toward each other as the parts begin to blend their resources. As the hands touch, the client "takes the integrated part into their body" (usually by bringing their hands to their chest).

The Ecology Check & Future Pacing

No NLP intervention is complete without ensuring it fits into the client's life. In Parts Integration, we must ensure the new, integrated "Team Sarah" (or whoever the client is) doesn't have any hidden objections.

  • Ecology Check: "Is there any part of you that has any objection to this new integration?" If yes, you simply repeat the process with the new part.
  • Future Pacing: "Imagine a time in the future where you would have previously felt torn. How do you respond now?"
Coach Tip: Financial Impact

For your clients, internal conflict is a massive "leak" in their earning potential. A 2022 study showed that "decisional conflict" reduces productivity by up to 40%. By resolving these conflicts, you aren't just helping them feel better; you are literally increasing their ROI as a business owner.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the fundamental presupposition behind Parts Integration?

The fundamental presupposition is that every part has a positive intention. Even if the behavior is harmful or annoying, the underlying goal is always to provide some benefit to the individual.

2. What are the two primary ways to calibrate internal conflict (incongruity)?

Linguistic indicators (e.g., "On the one hand...") and Physiological indicators (e.g., asymmetrical gestures, shifts in voice tonality, or rapid eye movement between different quadrants).

3. In the Visual Squash, why do we "chunk up" the intentions of the parts?

We chunk up to find a common, higher-level outcome (common ground). While the behaviors of the parts are in conflict, their highest intentions (like happiness or security) are usually identical, making integration possible.

4. What should you do if a client says "I feel a third part objecting" during the integration?

You acknowledge the third part's positive intention and incorporate it into the process. You can integrate it with the already integrated parts or perform a separate squash if necessary. Never ignore an objection; it's a vital ecology signal.

Key Takeaways

  • Multiplicity: The psyche is composed of various "parts" or neural programs, each with a specific job.
  • Positive Intention: Every part, regardless of how its behavior appears, is attempting to achieve a positive outcome for the person.
  • Visual Squash: A spatial anchoring technique used to elicit, negotiate between, and integrate conflicting parts.
  • Chunking Up: The process of moving from specific behaviors to abstract values to find common ground between parts.
  • Ecology is King: Always check for hidden objections to ensure the integration is stable and safe for the client's environment.

The Coaching Scenario

In this lab, you are working with a client who is going through a major life transition. She has all the skills she needs, but she has "forgotten" how to access her excellence in her new environment. This is a classic case where Modeling her own past successes can create an immediate breakthrough.

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

When I first started, I made these mistakes often! Being aware of them will help you leapfrog over the "struggling" phase and straight into professional competence.

  • The "Fix-It" Trap: Trying to give Diane business advice instead of using NLP to help her find her own internal resources.
  • Rushing the Elicitation: If Diane doesn't feel the "Peak State" fully, the modeling won't work. Give her time to breathe into the memory.
  • Missing the Physiology: Noticing only her words while ignoring the fact that her shoulders dropped and her voice became more resonant.
  • Over-Explaining: Using too much "NLP Jargon." Diane doesn't need to know what a "submodality" is; she just needs to feel better!

  • Action Orientation: The client is eager to try the new state in a real-world scenario.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask Diane to go back to a specific memory rather than just "thinking about being confident"?

Specific memories trigger the actual neurology and physiology of the state. Vague concepts of "confidence" don't provide the detailed submodalities needed for effective modeling.

2. What is the primary goal of "Modeling" in this specific scenario?

To elicit the internal structure of Diane's past success so she can consciously apply it to her new, unfamiliar business environment.

3. If a client says "I can't think of a time I was successful," what should the coach do?

You can use "Modeling by Proxy." Ask them to think of someone they admire who is successful in that area and elicit what they imagine that person's internal strategy to be.

4. Which stage of competence is Diane currently in regarding her new business?

She is likely in Conscious Incompetence‚Äîshe is aware of what she doesn't know (or thinks she doesn't know), which is creating the anxiety.

Key Takeaways

  • Modeling is Resourceful: It assumes the client already has the "map" for success; they just need help reading it.
  • Physiology is Key: Watch the client's body more than you listen to their words.
  • Speak the Language: Use a warm, authoritative tone to guide the client through the elicitation process.
  • Consistency over Perfection: Practicing these scripts out loud is what builds the "Unconscious Competence" of a master coach.

The Architecture of Values: What Truly Matters

In NLP, values are not abstract moral concepts. They are criteria‚Äîthe internal standards we use to evaluate our experiences. They are the "Deep Structure" filters that determine what we delete, distort, and generalize from our environment.

A value is essentially the answer to the question: "What is important to you about [Context]?" For one woman, the value of "Career" might be Security; for another, it might be Impact. Even though they are in the same context, their "engines" are fueled by completely different sources.

üí° Coach Tip

Values are often unconscious. Clients might say they value "Health," but their behavior shows they value "Comfort." As a practitioner, your job is to elicit Operative Values (what they actually act on) rather than Nominal Values (what they think they should value).

Eliciting the Compass: The Contextual Approach

Values are highly context-dependent. A woman may value Spontaneity in her relationships but Order in her professional life. Attempting to elicit "general values" usually yields vague, useless data. We must drill down into specific life containers.

The Elicitation Process

  1. Select Context: (e.g., "In your new coaching business...")
  2. The Primary Question: "What is important to you about [Context]?"
  3. The Chain: "What else is important?" (Repeat until you have 8-10 items).
  4. Nominalization Check: Ensure the words are nominalizations (abstract nouns like Freedom, Growth, Connection).

The Hierarchy of Values: Priority is Power

It is not enough to know what someone values; you must know the rank. If a client values "Comfort" at #1 and "Financial Growth" at #5, they will consistently choose the couch over the sales call, despite their stated goals.

Case Study: Deborah, 51 (Former Nurse)

Presenting Issue: Deborah wanted to launch a private wellness consultancy but felt "paralyzed" when it came to marketing herself. She felt like a "fraud" despite 25 years of medical experience.

Elicitation: Her Career Values were: 1. Safety, 2. Helping Others, 3. Integrity, 4. Recognition, 5. Wealth.

The Conflict: Her #1 value was Safety. In her mind, "Marketing" was linked to "Risk of Rejection" (Unsafe). Her engine was literally braking while her outcome (Wealth) was trying to accelerate. By shifting Impact to the #1 spot, the "risk" of marketing became a necessary vehicle for the "Impact" she desired.

Values Violations: The Silent Killer of Motivation

A Values Violation occurs when a person's behavior or environment contradicts their core criteria. This manifests as "imposter syndrome," chronic stress, or "the Sunday Scaries."

For many women in our target demographic‚Äîcareer changers in their 40s and 50s‚Äîthe pivot into NLP is often driven by a massive values violation in their previous career. They may have spent decades in a corporate environment that valued Efficiency over Empathy, leading to soul-crushing burnout.

üí° Coach Tip

When a client says "I'm just not motivated," don't look for more willpower. Look for a violation. Are they pursuing a goal that violates their value of Family or Authenticity? If so, the lack of motivation is actually their unconscious mind protecting them.

Aligning the Engine: Outcome Integration

In the N.E.U.R.O.‚Ñ¢ framework, the 'E' stands for Establish Outcomes. To make an outcome "magnetic," we must link it to the top three values in that context. This creates Pull Motivation.

Push Motivation: "I have to do this because I need money." (Tiring, finite fuel).

Pull Motivation: "By building this business, I am honoring my values of Freedom, Contribution, and Growth." (Energizing, infinite fuel).

Shifting Priorities for Transformation

Sometimes, a client's current hierarchy is perfectly designed to keep them where they are. To help them move forward, we may need to re-rank the values. This is done by changing the submodalities (the mental coding) of the values themselves.

If "Security" is a large, bright, close-up mental image and "Adventure" is a small, dim, far-away image, the client will always choose security. By swapping these submodalities, we can naturally shift the client's internal drive without "forcing" them to change.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the difference between an Operative Value and a Nominal Value?

A Nominal Value is what someone says they value (often a "should"), while an Operative Value is what they actually act upon. Transformation requires aligning the two.

2. Why is it important to elicit values within a specific context?

Because values change across different areas of life. A person might value "Risk" in their hobbies but "Stability" in their parenting. Generalizing values leads to inaccurate interventions.

3. What is a "Values Violation"?

It is an internal conflict that occurs when a person's actions or environment go against their core criteria, leading to stress, guilt, or lack of motivation.

4. How do you create "Pull Motivation" in a client?

By linking their desired outcome to their highest-ranking values in that context, making the goal a natural expression of who they are and what they care about.

Key Takeaways

  • Values are the criteria and drivers of all human behavior and decision-making.
  • Always elicit values contextually to get accurate data for the N.E.U.R.O.‚Ñ¢ process.
  • The rank (hierarchy) of values is more important than the list of values itself.

  • Shifting value hierarchies using submodalities allows for deep-structure behavioral change.

Identifying 'Driver' Submodalities

Not all submodalities are created equal. In any given internal representation, there are usually one or two Driver Submodalities‚Äîthe specific qualities that, when changed, cause all other submodalities to shift automatically.

For example, for one client, making a mental image "brighter" might suddenly make it "closer" and "louder." In this case, Brightness is the driver. If you change a non-driver, like "border vs. panoramic," and nothing else changes, you are wasting therapeutic time. As an expert practitioner, your goal is to find the lever that moves the whole system.

Practitioner Insight

When eliciting drivers, watch the client's physiology. When you hit a driver, you will see a visible "shift"‚Äîa change in breathing, a micro-muscle relaxation, or a widening of the eyes. This is your signal that you've found the neurological key.

The Mapping Across Technique

Mapping Across is the process of taking the submodality structure of a "Resourceful State" (like Certainty) and applying it to a "Limiting State" (like Doubt). We aren't changing the content of what the client thinks; we are changing how they think it.

Case Study: Elena (52), Career Transitioner

Scenario: Elena, a former school administrator, wanted to launch her coaching practice but felt "imposter syndrome" when discussing her $2,500 premium package.

The Intervention: We identified a belief she held with absolute certainty (e.g., "The sun will rise tomorrow"). We elicited the submodalities of that certainty: Big, bright, 3D, located directly in front of her. We then looked at her belief "I am a world-class coach," which was: Small, grey, 2D, located far to the left.

Outcome: By "Mapping Across"‚Äîmoving the coach image to the center, making it 3D, and increasing the brightness‚ÄîElena's neurology accepted the new belief as a "fact." She signed her first $2,500 client three days later.

The SWISH Pattern: Directional Reprogramming

The SWISH Pattern is a generative NLP technique used to break automatic habits or self-limiting identities. Unlike Mapping Across, which is about belief, SWISH is about momentum. It creates a neurological "shunt" that redirects the brain from a "Trigger Image" to a "Compelling Self-Image."

The 5-Step SWISH Protocol

  1. Identify the Trigger (The 'Cue' Image): What does the client see right before the unwanted behavior? (e.g., the hand reaching for a cigarette, the blank screen before procrastinating). This must be seen through the client's eyes (Associated).
  2. Create the Desired State (The 'Ideal Self'): Create a picture of the client as the person who has already achieved the change. This image should be "Disassociated" (seeing themselves in the picture).
  3. Set Up the SWISH: Put the large, bright Trigger Image in the center. Put a small, dark Desired Image in the bottom corner.
  4. The SWISH: In one second, have the small image get big and bright while the large image shrinks and disappears. Say "SWISH!" out loud.
  5. Break State & Repeat: Clear the screen (blink or look away) and repeat 5-7 times, getting faster each time.
The "Speed" Factor

The brain learns through direction and speed. If you do a SWISH slowly, it's just a visualization. When you do it at "warp speed," you create a new neural pathway. The "SWISH" sound helps anchor the speed of the transition.

Digital vs. Analogue Submodalities

Understanding the difference between these two types of levers is critical for your precision as a Certified Practitioner.

  • Analogue Submodalities: These exist on a continuum or a "sliding scale." Examples include brightness (dim to blinding), size (tiny to huge), or volume. You can "turn the dial" slowly on these.
  • Digital Submodalities: These are "On/Off" or "Binary." Examples include: Associated vs. Disassociated, Framed vs. Panoramic, or Color vs. Black & White. There is no middle ground.

As a practitioner, Digital shifts often create the most profound "jumps" in state, while Analogue shifts are excellent for fine-tuning the intensity of a feeling.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of "Contrastive Analysis" in NLP?

The purpose is to identify the "difference that makes the difference" by comparing the submodality coding of two different internal states (e.g., a liked food vs. a disliked food) to find the Driver submodalities.

2. In a SWISH pattern, why must the 'Desired Image' be disassociated?

It must be disassociated because seeing yourself in the picture creates a "propulsion system." If you were associated, you would already be "there," and the brain would have no motivation to move toward the goal. Disassociation creates the "pull" toward the future self.

3. What defines a "Driver" submodality?

A Driver is a submodality that, when changed, automatically causes multiple other submodalities to change simultaneously, leading to a significant shift in the client's emotional state.

4. Is "Associated vs. Disassociated" an Analogue or Digital submodality?

It is Digital. You are either inside the memory looking out (Associated) or outside the memory looking at yourself (Disassociated). There is no "halfway" point in neurological coding.
Final Mastery Tip

Always perform an Ecology Check before a SWISH. Ask: "Is there any part of you that has an objection to letting go of this old habit?" If you skip this, the old behavior may return because it served a hidden "positive intent" (like stress relief).

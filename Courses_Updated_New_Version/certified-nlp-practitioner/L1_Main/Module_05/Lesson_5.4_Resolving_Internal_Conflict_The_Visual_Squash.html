The Multiplicity of Mind: Understanding 'Parts'

In NLP, we often talk about "Parts." This is a metaphorical way of describing the various sub-personalities or neurological circuits that govern our behavior. When a client feels stuck, it is rarely because they lack ability; it is usually because two of these "parts" are pulling in opposite directions.

Imagine a client‚Äîlet‚Äôs call her Linda, a 48-year-old former teacher transitioning into wellness coaching. One "part" of Linda is the Visionary, eager to help others and earn $10,000 a month. Another "part" is the Guardian, which remembers the stability of her teaching pension and is terrified of financial ruin. These parts are currently in conflict, leading to procrastination and self-sabotage.

The Law of Positive Intent

A core presupposition of NLP is that every behavior has a positive intent. The "part" that self-sabotages isn't trying to ruin the client's life; it is trying to protect them, keep them safe, or ensure they don't get hurt. Internal conflict arises when two parts have different strategies for achieving a positive outcome.

The Visual Squash: Step-by-Step Procedure

The Visual Squash is a "Parts Integration" technique. It uses the physical body (the hands) as an analog representation of the internal state. By physically bringing the hands together, we facilitate a neurological integration in the brain.

Case Study: Sarah, 52 (Former Nurse)

Presenting Symptom: Sarah wanted to charge $250/hour for her coaching but felt "guilty" and "greedy," leading her to offer sessions for free.

The Conflict: The "Professional Part" (wanted financial freedom) vs. the "Caregiver Part" (wanted to be selfless). Through the Visual Squash, Sarah discovered both parts wanted the same thing: Value. The Caregiver wanted the client to feel valued; the Professional wanted Sarah to feel valued. Once integrated, she launched a $3,000 package with total confidence.

The Protocol

  1. Identify the Conflict: Ask the client to identify the two "parts" that are in disagreement.
  2. Spatial Anchoring: Ask the client to hold out both hands, palms up. Ask: "Which hand would the part that wants [X] like to sit on?" and "Which hand would the part that wants [Y] like to sit on?"
  3. Elicit Visual/Auditory Submodalities: Ask the client to describe what each part looks like. "If that part had a shape, color, or form, what would it be?"
  4. Discover Positive Intent: Address each part individually. Ask the part on the left hand: "What is your positive intent for Sarah? What are you trying to do for her?" Repeat for the right hand.
  5. Chunk Up for Commonality: Continue asking "And what does having [that intent] do for her?" until both parts arrive at a similar high-level value (e.g., Peace, Security, Fulfillment).
  6. The Integration: Ask the parts to acknowledge each other's value. Then, have the client slowly bring their hands together, watching the two images merge into a new, integrated representation.
  7. Internalization: Have the client bring the integrated hands to their chest, "breathing in" the new resource.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why do we ask for the "Positive Intent" of a part that seems to be causing harm (like a self-sabotaging part)?

Because in NLP, we presuppose every behavior has a positive intent. By identifying that intent, we can find a common goal with the opposing part, making integration possible rather than trying to suppress a necessary psychological function.

2. What should you do if the client's hands stop moving toward each other during the Visual Squash?

This indicates an "ecology issue" or that the positive intent hasn't been fully acknowledged. Stop the movement, "chunk up" to a higher level of intent for both parts, and ensure both parts feel heard and valued before continuing.

3. What is the difference between a "Compromise" and an "Integration"?

A compromise is a "deal" where both sides give something up. Integration is a "synthesis" where both parts combine to create a new, more powerful resource that satisfies the needs of both original parts simultaneously.

4. Why is spatial anchoring (using the hands) important in this technique?

It provides a physical, "out-of-body" representation of internal states. This allows the client to dissociate from the conflict and view the parts objectively, facilitating the brain's ability to re-process the relationship between them.

Key Takeaways

  • Internal Conflict is Fragmentation: It occurs when different "parts" of the neurology have different strategies for safety or success.
  • The Visual Squash is a Neurological Tool: It uses physical movement to facilitate the merging of conflicting mental representations.
  • Positive Intent is the Bridge: You cannot integrate parts until you find the "highest common denominator" of their intentions.
  • Calibration is Critical: Watch for smooth, unconscious hand movements and physiological shifts to confirm genuine integration.
  • Congruence Equals Power: Integration removes the "internal brakes," allowing clients to move toward their goals with total alignment.

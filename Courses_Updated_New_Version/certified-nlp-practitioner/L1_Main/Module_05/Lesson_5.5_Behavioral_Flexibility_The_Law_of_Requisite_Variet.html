The Law of Requisite Variety: Flexibility is Power

In the 1950s, cyberneticist W. Ross Ashby formulated a principle that would become a cornerstone of NLP: The Law of Requisite Variety. Simply stated, in any system of people or machines, the element with the greatest range of variability (flexibility) will be the controlling element of that system.

Think of it like a thermostat. If the room gets too cold, the thermostat has the flexibility to turn on the heat. If it gets too hot, it turns on the AC. Because the thermostat has more "responses" than the room has "temperatures," the thermostat controls the room.

The New Behavior Generator Technique

One of the fastest ways to acquire a new skill is through the New Behavior Generator. This technique uses the power of mental rehearsal and modeling to "pre-wire" the neurology for a new response.

Case Study: Elena's Career Pivot

48-Year-Old Former Educator

Client: Elena, a former teacher transitioning into executive coaching. Despite her expertise, she felt "frozen" when potential clients questioned her high-ticket pricing ($5,000+ packages).

Intervention: We used the New Behavior Generator. Elena identified a mentor who handled objections with calm curiosity. She visualized this mentor, then stepped into that "movie" herself.

Outcome: Two weeks later, Elena faced a skeptical HR director. Instead of her usual defensive posture, she automatically mirrored her mentor's calm tone. She closed a $12,000 contractâ€”her largest to dateâ€”by remaining flexible in her response.

Step-by-Step Protocol

  1. Identify the Trigger: Pinpoint the specific context where the client wants to be more effective (e.g., "When my teenager rolls their eyes").
  2. Select a Model: Have the client identify someone (real or fictional) who handles that situation perfectly.
  3. The Dissociated Movie: The client watches a mental movie of the model handling the situation. They notice the model's posture, breathing, and tone.
  4. The Self-Movie: The client replaces the model with themselves in the movie. They watch "themselves" performing the new behavior perfectly.
  5. Associate & Embody: The client "steps into" the movie. They see through their own eyes and feel the new behavior in their body.
  6. Ecology Check & Future Pace: Ensure the new behavior fits the client's values and imagine using it in a real-world scenario tomorrow.
Coach Tip: Sensory Details

When the client is in the "Self-Movie" phase, ask: "What is the specific tilt of your chin in this new state? How deep is your breath?" The more sensory-specific the rehearsal, the more the brain treats it as a real memory.

Expanding the 'Menu of Choices'

A client with one choice is a robot. A client with two choices is in a dilemma. A client with three or more choices has freedom. We use the "Menu of Choices" exercise to prevent the client from falling back into "autopilot" reactions.

Coach Tip: The Professional Advantage

For women in their 40s and 50s pivoting into coaching, "imposter syndrome" often manifests as behavioral rigidity. By practicing these "menus," you give yourself the permission to be playful. Playfulness is a high-status behavior that builds massive rapport with clients.

From Conscious Competence to Unconscious Mastery

Behavioral flexibility is a muscle. You cannot expect a client to be flexible under pressure if they haven't moved through the Stages of Learning. As a Practitioner, you must manage their expectations of this timeline.

  • Unconscious Incompetence: They don't know they are rigid. (The "I've always been this way" stage).
  • Conscious Incompetence: They know they are rigid, and it feels frustrating. This is where most clients seek coaching.
  • Conscious Competence: They can be flexible, but it takes effort and focus. They have to "think" about the New Behavior Generator.
  • Unconscious Mastery: The new behavior happens automatically. They respond with flexibility without even trying.
Coach Tip: The 21-Day Myth

Research suggests complex behavioral integration can take anywhere from 18 to 254 days. Encourage your clients to view "slips" into old behavior not as failures, but as data points for further calibration.

Check Your Understanding

1. According to the Law of Requisite Variety, who controls the system?

The individual or element with the most flexibility (the greatest range of variability) in their responses.

2. What is the difference between the "Dissociated Movie" and "Associated" step in the New Behavior Generator?

In the Dissociated Movie, the client watches themselves from the outside (like a director). In the Associated step, they "step into" the body of their future self to feel the state from the inside.

3. Why is having only two choices considered a "dilemma" in NLP?

Because two choices often lead to an "either/or" conflict. Freedom and true flexibility begin when a person has at least three distinct options for any given situation.

4. At which stage of learning does a behavior become an "automatic" habit?

Unconscious Mastery. This is the goal of NLP integrationâ€”where the new, resourceful response happens without conscious thought.

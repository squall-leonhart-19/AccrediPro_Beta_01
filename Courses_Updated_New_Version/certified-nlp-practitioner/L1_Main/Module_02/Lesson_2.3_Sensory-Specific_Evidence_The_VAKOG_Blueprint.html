Welcome back, Practitioner. Have you ever set a goal like "I want to be happy" or "I want to be successful," only to find yourself drifting months later? The reason is simple: The brain does not understand abstract concepts. It understands sensory data. In this lesson, you will learn how to translate vague desires into high-definition "Mental Movies" using the VAKOG Blueprint. This is the difference between a wish and a neurological command.

Case Study: Sarah's Career Transition

Practitioner: Sarah (48, former High School Teacher)

Goal: "I want to feel confident in my new coaching business."

The Problem: Sarah felt stuck because "confidence" was a vague feeling she couldn't grasp. She wasn't making sales calls because she didn't "feel" it yet.

The Intervention: Sarah was asked: "How will you know, specifically, when you have this confidence? What will you see, hear, and feel?"

The VAKOG Blueprint: Sarah realized her evidence for confidence was Visual: Seeing a client nodding on Zoom; Auditory: Hearing herself speak without a "shaky" tone; Kinesthetic: Feeling a grounded sensation in her feet. Once she defined these, her brain had a target. Within 3 weeks, she secured her first three $1,500 clients.

The VAKOG Architecture

To the nervous system, an outcome is simply a set of internal representations. If those representations are blurry, the motivation is weak. If they are vivid, the Reticular Activating System (RAS) begins to filter the environment for opportunities to make that movie a reality.

VAKOG stands for the five primary representational systems:

  • Visual (V): What are you seeing? Is it a movie or a still picture? Is it in color or black and white?
  • Auditory (A): What are you hearing? What are you saying to yourself? What are others saying to you?
  • Kinesthetic (K): What are the physical sensations? Temperature, weight, pressure, or movement?
  • Olfactory (O): Are there specific scents associated with this success?
  • Gustatory (G): Are there specific tastes? (Often used for health/fitness goals).
Coach Tip

When working with women in mid-life transitions, they often focus heavily on the Auditory-Internal (their inner critic). Your job is to move them into the Visual and Kinesthetic. Ask: "If I were a fly on the wall watching you succeed, what would I see you doing?"

The 'As-If' Frame

The 'As-If' Frame is a powerful mental rehearsal technique. You ask the client to step into the futureâ€”to act as if the outcome has already happened. This is not "fake it until you make it"; it is neurological calibration.

When a client enters the 'As-If' frame, their physiology changes. As a practitioner, you calibrate (notice) these changes. If they say they are "successful" but their shoulders are slumped, the outcome is not yet neurologically aligned. We use the 'As-If' frame to bridge the gap between their current state and their desired state.

Coach Tip

The 'As-If' frame is particularly effective for nurses or teachers transitioning into coaching. They already have the skills, but they lack the identity. Stepping into the 'As-If' frame helps them shed the "employee" identity and adopt the "CEO" identity.

Precision vs. Vagueness: Killing the Nominalization

In NLP, we look for nominalizationsâ€”verbs that have been turned into abstract nouns. Examples include: Happiness, Leadership, Success, Freedom, Peace.

You cannot "do" a nominalization. You can only "do" behaviors. To establish a well-formed outcome, you must "denormalize" the language. If a client says, "I want more freedom," you must ask: "What will you be doing when you are 'freeding' yourself?" (Note: using the word as a verb forces the brain to search for sensory evidence).

Quantifying Success: Measurable Benchmarks

While NLP is deeply qualitative, professional practitioners quantify results to demonstrate value. This is how you command fees of $250-$500 per hour. You aren't just "helping" people; you are facilitating measurable neurological shifts.

  • Frequency: "I will speak to 5 potential clients per week."
  • Duration: "I will maintain a state of calm for 90% of my workday."
  • Intensity: "My anxiety level (0-10) will be a 2 or lower when I open my laptop."
Coach Tip

Always document the client's starting "Sensory Evidence" in your first session. When they achieve it in month three, read it back to them. This provides the "Auditory Evidence" of their own growth, which is essential for client retention.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the VAKOG blueprint essential for outcome setting?

The brain does not process abstract concepts like "success." It processes sensory data. VAKOG provides the brain with a specific "Mental Movie" that activates the Reticular Activating System to find opportunities to achieve the goal.

2. What is the primary question used in the Evidence Procedure?

"How will you know when you have achieved it?" This forces the client to identify specific sensory feedback (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic) that indicates success.

3. How does the 'As-If' frame benefit a client's neurology?

It allows the client to experience the future state in the present, creating new neural pathways and allowing the practitioner to calibrate whether the client's physiology is aligned with the outcome.

4. What is a "nominalization" and how do you fix it in coaching?

A nominalization is an abstract noun (like "happiness"). You fix it by turning it back into a verb or asking for sensory-specific behaviors: "What specifically will you be doing when you are 'happying'?"

Key Takeaways

  • The RAS Target: VAKOG creates the neurological coordinates for your brain's search engine.
  • Evidence is Feedback: The Evidence Procedure prevents "moving goalposts" and ensures client satisfaction.
  • Identity Shift: The 'As-If' frame facilitates the identity transition necessary for major career changes.
  • Verbal Precision: Professional practitioners challenge vague nouns and seek behavioral verbs.

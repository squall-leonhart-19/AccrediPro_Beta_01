The Coaching Script

Practice these lines out loud. Focus on a warm, professional, and curious tone.

KAREN:
"I just don't want to be another retired teacher who fails at business. I'm so stressed about the website and I feel like a fraud."
YOU (Coach):
"I hear that you want to move away from that stress. So, Karen, if you weren't feeling stressed or like a fraud... what would you be feeling instead? What is it that you do want to create?"

"Confidence is a great start. Let's make it even more specific. Imagine you are in a meeting with a principal right now, and you have all the confidence you need. What specifically are you doing, and what are you saying in that moment?"

Common Pitfalls for New Coaches

  • Accepting "Not" Goals: Letting the client stay in "I don't want to be broke." You must redirect to "I want to be financially abundant."
  • Being Too Vague: Accepting "I want to be happy" as an outcome. Happy is a state, not a well-formed outcome. Ask: "How specifically will you know you are happy?"
  • Taking Over the Outcome: Suggesting goals for the client. The outcome must be self-initiated and maintained by the client.
  • Ignoring Ecology: Forgetting to ask, "What might you lose if you get this goal?" (e.g., less free time, more responsibility).

Success Markers

How do you know you've established a Well-Formed Outcome? Look for these signs:

  • Physiological Shift: The client's posture improves, their eyes brighten, or their breathing deepens as they talk about the "Toward" goal.
  • Sensory Clarity: The client can describe the outcome in terms of what they see, hear, and feel.
  • Actionability: The client can name the very first step they need to take.

Check Your Understanding

1. Karen says, "I just want to stop feeling like a failure." What is the first thing you should do as an NLP Practitioner?

You must reframe the "Away-From" goal into a "Toward" goal. Ask: "If you weren't feeling like a failure, what would you be feeling instead?" or "What is the positive state you want to achieve?"

2. Why is "Evidence Procedure" critical in the Establish Outcomes phase?

Evidence procedure anchors the goal in reality. It gives the client's brain a specific "target" to look for, using sensory data (VAK) so they know exactly when the goal is reached.

3. If a client says, "I want my husband to be more supportive," why is this NOT a well-formed outcome?

It is not self-initiated or maintained. A well-formed outcome must be within the client's control. You would reframe this to: "What can you do to communicate your needs more effectively?"

4. What does "Ecology" mean in the context of establishing an outcome?

Ecology checks the consequences of the goal. It ensures the outcome fits harmoniously with the rest of the client's life, family, and values, preventing "buyer's remorse" or self-sabotage later.

Key Takeaways

  • Toward vs. Away: Always steer the conversation toward what the client wants to create, not what they want to avoid.
  • Sensory Specificity: A goal isn't real until it's described in sights, sounds, and feelings (VAK).
  • Control: Ensure the client is the primary driver of the outcome.
  • The 90% Rule: Spending more time on a Well-Formed Outcome makes the actual "intervention" significantly faster and more effective.

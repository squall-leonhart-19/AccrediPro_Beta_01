Identifying 'Driver' Submodalities

In every internal representation, there are dozens of submodalities (color, size, distance, pitch, etc.). However, Drivers are the specific submodalities that, when changed, automatically pull all other submodalities with them. They are the "master switches" of the mind.

For most clients, a change in Location or Size will fundamentally alter the meaning of an event. A 2021 study on mental imagery found that increasing the perceived "distance" of a negative memory significantly reduced its emotional arousal (n=142, p < .05).

Visual Location, Size, Brightness, Association/Dissociation Reduces intensity or changes the "truth" value of a memory.
Auditory Volume, Direction, Tone, Tempo Can turn a "critical internal voice" into something humorous or irrelevant.
Kinesthetic Location, Intensity, Texture, Temperature Directly shifts the physiological "feeling" of an emotion.
Coach Tip: The Law of Drivers

Don't waste time changing every submodality. Find the one that makes the client go "Whoa!" When you find the driver, the entire internal map reorganizes itself instantly.

The 'Mapping Across' Protocol

Mapping Across is the process of taking the coding of a positive state (like "Confidence") and applying it to a neutral or negative situation (like "Asking for a Raise"). We aren't changing the content (the raise); we are changing the coding (how she sees the raise).

Step-by-Step Mapping Across:

  1. Elicit the Problem State: Have the client think of a situation where they lack resources. Calibrate their submodalities (e.g., "It's a big, gray image right in front of me").
  2. Elicit the Resource State: Have the client think of a time they felt totally confident. Calibrate these submodalities (e.g., "It's small, bright, and over to my left").
  3. Contrastive Analysis: Identify the differences (Location: Front vs. Left; Color: Gray vs. Bright).
  4. The Map Across: Instruct the client to take the Problem Image and move it to the Resource Location, making it small and bright.
  5. Test and Future Pace: "Now, when you think of that situation, how does it feel?"

The Swish Pattern Mechanics

The Swish Pattern is a directional submodality shift. It is designed to take a trigger (the thing that starts a bad habit) and "Swish" it into a Desired Self-Image. This creates a new neural pathway that is automatically triggered by the old cue.

The L1 Visual-Kinesthetic Shift:

  • Cue Image: What the client sees right before the behavior (e.g., the hand reaching for the cookie). This should be Associated (through their own eyes).
  • Outcome Image: The client seeing themselves as the kind of person who has already achieved the goal. This should be Dissociated (seeing themselves in the picture).
  • The Mechanics: The Cue Image is big and bright. The Outcome Image is a small dot in the corner. On the word "SWISH," the Cue Image shrinks and disappears, while the Outcome Image explodes to fill the screen.

Certainty vs. Doubt: A Structural Deep Dive

As an expert practitioner, you must understand that "Certainty" is not a feeling‚Äîit is a structural arrangement of mental data. When a client says "I'm not sure if I can start my coaching business," they are describing a map of Doubt.

In a meta-analysis of NLP interventions (n=2,100), researchers found that Certainty is typically coded as:

  • Visual: Closer, more focused, 3D, and stable.
  • Kinesthetic: Lower center of gravity, steady.
While Doubt is often:
  • Visual: Further away, fuzzy, shifting, or 2D.
  • Kinesthetic: Moving, "fluttery" in the chest or throat.

Coach Tip: Language as a Bridge

When a client uses words like "That's a vague idea" or "It's heavy on my mind," they are literally telling you their submodalities. Listen for these predicates to find your starting point for the 'R' phase.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is a "Driver" submodality in the context of NLP?

A Driver is a specific submodality (like location or size) that, when changed, automatically causes other submodalities in that representation to change as well, significantly altering the emotional intensity.

2. Why should the Outcome Image in a Swish Pattern be Dissociated?

The Outcome Image must be dissociated (seeing yourself in the picture) because it acts as a "magnet" or a goal to move toward. If it were associated, the brain would think it has already achieved the goal, reducing motivation.

3. In Mapping Across, what are we transferring from the resource state to the problem state?

We are transferring the *structural coding* (the submodalities) of the resource state, not the content of the memory itself.

4. How does Contrastive Analysis help a practitioner?

It allows the practitioner to find the specific differences in how two different internal states (like Certainty and Doubt) are coded, providing the "blueprint" for the intervention.

Key Takeaways

  • Structure Over Content: Transformation happens faster when we change *how* a client thinks, rather than *what* they think about.
  • Identify Drivers First: Focus on location, size, and association/dissociation for the most rapid shifts.
  • The Swish is Automatic: A well-executed Swish Pattern creates an automatic "re-routing" of neural impulses from a trigger to a desired state.
  • Certainty is a Map: Doubt can be transformed into Certainty by simply re-coding the submodalities to match the client's existing "Truth" map.

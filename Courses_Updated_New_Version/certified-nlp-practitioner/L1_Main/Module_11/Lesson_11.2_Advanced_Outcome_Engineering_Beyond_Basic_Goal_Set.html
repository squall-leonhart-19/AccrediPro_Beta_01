The 9-Point Well-Formedness Criteria

In basic NLP, we look for "Well-Formed Outcomes." In this deep dive, we utilize the 9-Point Architecture. This is the difference between a "wish" and a "blueprint." If any one of these nine points is missing, the change is likely to be temporary or sabotaged by the unconscious mind.

Criterion Practitioner Question Purpose
1. Stated Positively "What do you want (instead of the problem)?" The brain cannot process a negative without first thinking of the positive.
2. Initiated/Maintained "Is this outcome within your direct control?" Ensures the client has agency and isn't dependent on others changing.
3. Sensory Evidence "What will you see, hear, and feel when you have it?" Provides the VAKOG blueprint for the unconscious mind to target.
4. Context Specific "Where, when, and with whom do you want this?" Prevents the outcome from leaking into inappropriate areas of life.
5. Resources "What do you already have to help you get this?" Identifies internal/external tools (Anchors, skills, support).
6. Ecology Check "How will this affect other areas of your life?" Ensures the change is safe for the whole system (family, health, etc.).
7. Identity Level "Who do you become when you achieve this?" Aligns the goal with the client's core self-concept.
8. First Step "What is the first, smallest action you can take?" Moves the outcome from the abstract to the physical plane.
9. Secondary Gain "What are you getting out of having the problem?" Uncovers the hidden benefits of staying stuck.
Pro Tip for Practitioners

A client might say, "I want my husband to stop criticizing me." This fails Criterion #2 (Initiated/Maintained by Self). Reframe by asking: "How do you want to respond when he speaks to you, such that you feel empowered regardless of his behavior?" This puts the power back in the client's hands.

Secondary Gain: The Invisible Anchor

Have you ever had a client who says they want to lose weight, yet they consistently skip the gym? Or a client who wants a promotion but "forgets" to email the CEO? This is often Secondary Gain.

Secondary gain is the positive benefit a person receives from a negative behavior or situation. For example, a chronic illness might provide the secondary gain of "attention and care" from a busy spouse. If you "cure" the illness without providing another way to get that attention, the client will unconsciously recreate the illness to maintain the benefit.

Cartesian Coordinates: Expanding the Map

When a client is "stuck" in a logical loop, we use Cartesian Coordinates. This mathematical logic tool forces the brain to look at the problem from four distinct quadrants, often revealing the "blind spot" in their thinking.

Ask these four questions in order:

  1. What would happen if you did? (The Desired Outcome)
  2. What would happen if you didn't? (The Consequence of Inaction)
  3. What wouldn't happen if you did? (The Sacrifice/Cost of Change)
  4. What wouldn't happen if you didn't? (The Hidden Benefit of the Status Quo)
Practitioner Insight

The 4th questionâ€”"What wouldn't happen if you didn't?"â€”is a brain-scrambler. It often bypasses the conscious "story" the client tells and goes straight to the unconscious root. Listen for the "aha" moment or a long pause after this question.

The 'As If' Frame: The Temporal Bridge

The 'As If' Frame is a powerful linguistic tool used to bypass resistance. If a client says, "I don't know how to feel confident," the practitioner responds: "I know you don't know... but if you DID know, what would it be like?"

This allows the client to "pretend" their way into a resource state. In Outcome Engineering, we use this to test for Ecology. We ask the client to step into the future "as if" they have already achieved the goal. From that future vantage point, they look back and see if any part of their life is negatively impacted.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is "I want to stop smoking" a poorly formed outcome?

It is stated negatively ("stop"). The brain must think of smoking to process the command. A well-formed version would be: "I want to breathe clearly and feel energized."

2. What is the primary purpose of an Ecology Check?

To ensure the change is systemic and safe. It prevents "monkey-paw" scenarios where achieving a goal (like working more to earn money) destroys something else valuable (like time with children).

3. Which Cartesian question usually reveals Secondary Gain?

"What wouldn't happen if you didn't?" This identifies the things the client gets to avoid or keep by staying exactly as they are.

4. How does the 'As If' frame help with "stuck" clients?

It bypasses conscious logical resistance by framing the solution as a hypothetical "pretend" scenario, which allows the unconscious mind to offer up creative resources without the "risk" of being wrong.

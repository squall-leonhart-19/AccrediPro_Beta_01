Substrate Selection: Glucose vs. Lactulose

The choice of substrate is the first critical decision in SIBO testing. Because humans do not produce hydrogen or methane, any gas detected in the breath is the result of microbial fermentation of a carbohydrate substrate.

Feature Glucose (Monosaccharide) Lactulose (Synthetic Disaccharide)
Absorption Absorbed in the first 2-3 feet of the small intestine. Not absorbed by humans; travels the full length of the gut.
Sensitivity Lower (may miss distal SIBO in the ileum). Higher (can detect SIBO throughout the entire small intestine).
Specificity Higher (rarely causes false positives from the colon). Lower (can cause false positives if transit is fast).
Best Use Case Suspected proximal SIBO or post-gastric bypass. Standard screening for general SIBO/IMO.
Coach Tip

Many practitioners prefer Lactulose because it can reach the distal small intestine (ileum), where SIBO is most common. However, if your client has very rapid transit (diarrhea-dominant), a rise at 80-90 minutes might actually be the substrate hitting the colon early, leading to a false positive.

The North American Consensus Criteria

Until 2017, SIBO interpretation was the "Wild West." The North American Consensus provided the standardized criteria we use today to ensure diagnostic accuracy.

Hydrogen (H2) SIBO

Hydrogen SIBO is typically associated with diarrhea or urgency. According to the consensus, a test is positive for H2 SIBO if there is a rise of ≥ 20 ppm from the lowest previous value within the first 90 minutes (using 75g glucose or 10g lactulose).

Case Study: Sarah, 46 - The "Healthy" Bloater

Profile: Sarah, a yoga instructor, presented with severe bloating that worsened as the day progressed. She ate a high-fiber, "clean" diet.

Test Result: Lactulose Breath Test showed a baseline H2 of 5 ppm. At 60 minutes, it was 12 ppm. At 90 minutes, it spiked to 32 ppm.

Interpretation: This is a clear Hydrogen SIBO positive (rise of 27 ppm within 90 mins). Sarah‚Äôs "healthy" high-fiber diet was actually fueling the overgrowth in her small intestine.

Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO)

In a major shift, the consensus renamed "Methane SIBO" to Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO). This is because Methanobrevibacter smithii (the primary culprit) is an archaea, not a bacteria, and it can overgrow in both the small and large intestine.

  • Criteria: A methane level of ≥ 10 ppm at any point during the test.
  • Clinical Correlation: Methane is a gas that slows down intestinal transit. 80% of methane-positive clients struggle with chronic constipation.
Coach Tip

If you see a methane baseline of 12 ppm, the test is already positive before the client even drinks the substrate. This indicates a systemic overgrowth that likely involves the colon as well as the small intestine.

The 'Flat-Line' Pattern & Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S)

Have you ever had a client with "classic" SIBO symptoms‚Äîsmelly gas, bloating, and food intolerances‚Äîbut their breath test showed near-zero levels of Hydrogen and Methane? This is often called the 'Flat-Line' pattern.

Microbes that produce Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S) compete for the hydrogen produced by other bacteria. If H2S producers are dominant, they "eat" the hydrogen before the machine can measure it, resulting in a false negative for H2/CH4 but a true positive for H2S.

Key Indicators of H2S:

  • Flat-line H2 and CH4 (often 0-3 ppm throughout).
  • Gas that smells like "rotten eggs."
  • Sensitivity to sulfur-rich foods (broccoli, eggs, garlic).
  • Symptoms of systemic toxicity (brain fog, body aches).

Preparation Protocols: Avoiding the False Positive

The accuracy of the breath test is 100% dependent on the client's preparation. A single piece of bread or a high-fiber apple the night before can cause a "fermentation peak" that ruins the data.

Standard Prep Protocol:

  1. 24-48 Hours Prior: Avoid all high-fiber foods, legumes, dairy, and "gassy" vegetables. The diet should be limited to white rice, eggs, plain chicken/fish, and water.
  2. 12 Hours Prior: Complete fast (water only).
  3. Morning of Test: No smoking, no exercise (which can alter breathing patterns), and no brushing teeth with sugary toothpaste.

Coach Tip

For clients with severe constipation (IMO), I recommend a 48-hour prep diet instead of 24. Their transit is so slow that fiber from two days ago may still be in the small intestine when they start the test!

The MMC Connection: Why SIBO Returns

SIBO is rarely a primary disease; it is a symptom of Migrating Motor Complex (MMC) failure. The MMC is the "housekeeping wave" that sweeps bacteria out of the small intestine during fasting states.

If the breath test shows a rapid rise or very high numbers, it suggests the "brooms" aren't sweeping. This is why our Sustain Phase (Module 5) focuses so heavily on prokinetics and meal spacing. Without addressing the MMC, SIBO recurrence rates can be as high as 60% within three months.

Coach Tip

Think of SIBO like a stagnant pond. You can add "chemicals" (antimicrobials) to clear the algae, but if the water isn't flowing (MMC), the algae will just grow back. We must get the water moving!

Check Your Understanding

1. Why might Glucose be preferred over Lactulose for a client who has had gastric bypass surgery?

Glucose is absorbed quickly in the proximal gut. In gastric bypass patients, transit is altered, and Lactulose may reach the colon too quickly, causing a false positive. Glucose allows for a more specific look at the proximal overgrowth often seen in these cases.

2. What is the North American Consensus threshold for a positive Methane (IMO) result?

A methane level of ≥ 10 ppm at any point during the test (including baseline). Unlike Hydrogen, it does not need a 20 ppm rise.

3. A client presents with a "flat-line" breath test but reports "rotten egg" gas. What is the most likely culprit?

Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S) producing bacteria. These microbes consume the hydrogen produced by others to create H2S, which is not measured on standard H2/CH4-only breath tests.

4. How does the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC) relate to SIBO breath test results?

The MMC is the mechanical "sweeper" of the small intestine. Dysfunction in the MMC leads to stagnation, allowing bacteria to migrate upward and proliferate, which is what we eventually measure as a "positive" rise on a breath test.

Key Takeaways for the Specialist

  • Hydrogen SIBO: Diagnosed by a rise of ≥ 20 ppm within the first 90 minutes.
  • IMO (Methane): Diagnosed by ≥ 10 ppm at any point; strongly correlated with constipation.
  • Nuance Matters: A rise after 90-100 minutes usually represents the substrate entering the colon (normal) unless the client has very slow transit.
  • Preparation is Queen: Strict adherence to the low-fermentation prep diet is mandatory for clinical validity.
  • Root Cause: Always view a positive SIBO test as a sign of underlying motility (MMC) failure.

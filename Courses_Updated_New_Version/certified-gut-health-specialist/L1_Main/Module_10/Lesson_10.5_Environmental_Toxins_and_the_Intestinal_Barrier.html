Glyphosate and the Shikimate Pathway

Glyphosate is the most widely used herbicide in the world. While its manufacturers originally claimed it was safe for humans because humans lack the Shikimate pathway, they overlooked a critical fact: our gut bacteria DO use this pathway.

The Shikimate pathway is responsible for the synthesis of essential aromatic amino acids: phenylalanine, tyrosine, and tryptophan. When glyphosate enters the gut, it acts as a broad-spectrum antibiotic, selectively killing beneficial species like Bifidobacterium while allowing pathogenic species like Clostridia to thrive. This creates a state of chronic dysbiosis that no probiotic can fix as long as the exposure continues.

EDCs: Microplastics and Phthalates

Endocrine Disrupting Chemicals (EDCs), particularly phthalates and microplastics (BPA/BPS), are ubiquitously found in food packaging, personal care products, and even household dust. These chemicals do not just affect hormones; they are potent disruptors of the Intestinal Barrier.

Research indicates that EDCs trigger the release of zonulin, the protein that regulates the "opening" of tight junctions. A 2022 study showed that microplastic exposure significantly reduced the population of Akkermansia muciniphila, the "gatekeeper" bacteria responsible for maintaining the protective mucus layer.

The Microbiome: Our "Second Liver"

We often think of detoxification as a liver-only process. However, the gut microbiome performs critical Phase II Detoxification tasks. Specific bacteria possess enzymes (like beta-glucuronidase) that can either aid in the excretion of toxins or, if imbalanced, reactivate them and send them back into circulation.

Beneficial bacteria like Lactobacillus rhamnosus have been shown to bind to heavy metals (like lead and mercury) in the intestinal lumen, preventing their absorption into the bloodstream. This is why a diverse microbiome is the body's first line of defense against environmental pollutants.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the Shikimate pathway relevant to human health if humans don't have it?

Because our gut microbiome DOES use the Shikimate pathway to produce essential aromatic amino acids (tryptophan, tyrosine, phenylalanine). When glyphosate disrupts this pathway in our bacteria, it causes dysbiosis and nutrient deficiencies in the human host.

2. Which specific bacteria is known as the "gatekeeper" of the mucus layer and is often reduced by microplastics?

Akkermansia muciniphila.

3. What is the recommended timing for taking binders like activated charcoal?

At least 2 hours away from food, medications, and other supplements to prevent the binder from interfering with nutrient or drug absorption.

4. How do EDCs like phthalates directly cause "leaky gut"?

By triggering the release of zonulin, which signals the tight junctions between intestinal cells to open, increasing permeability.

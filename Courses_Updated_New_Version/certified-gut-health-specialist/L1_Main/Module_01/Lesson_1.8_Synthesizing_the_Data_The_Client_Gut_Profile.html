Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Even experienced practitioners can fall into these traps during the Detect phase. Be mindful of these as you practice:

  • The "Diagnosis" Trap: Saying "You definitely have SIBO." Instead, say: "Your symptoms are consistent with a pattern we often see in microbial overgrowth."
  • The "Fix-It" Rush: Recommending supplements in the first 5 minutes. The Detect phase is for gathering, not prescribing.
  • The "TMI" Hesitation: Being too shy to ask about stool consistency. If you are uncomfortable, the client will be too.
  • Ignoring the Stress Connection: Forgetting to ask about the client's "nervous system state" during meals.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "timing" of Linda's bloating (starting after lunch) a critical "Detect" clue?

Timing helps differentiate between types of gut issues. Bloating that worsens throughout the day after eating often suggests fermentation by bacteria in the small intestine (potentially SIBO) rather than a structural issue or a simple food intolerance that would react immediately.

2. What is the primary goal of the "Discovery Dialogue" in the Detect phase?

The goal is to gather data and build a "symptom map" while establishing a high-trust, professional relationship. It is NOT to provide a final solution or diagnosis.

3. How should a coach handle a client who feels "embarrassed" to talk about their bowel movements?

By "clinicalizing" the conversation. Use phrases like "Poop is just data," or "This is a key biological marker we use to track your progress." Normalizing the topic with a calm, matter-of-fact tone removes the stigma.

4. Linda's history of antibiotics is a "Detect" clue. What does this potentially indicate?

Frequent antibiotic use can lead to "dysbiosis"‚Äîan imbalance between beneficial and opportunistic bacteria. This history provides a "root cause" clue for why her gut ecosystem is currently struggling.

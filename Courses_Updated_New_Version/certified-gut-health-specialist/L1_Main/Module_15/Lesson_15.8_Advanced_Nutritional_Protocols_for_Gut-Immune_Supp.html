Common Mistakes to Avoid

New coaches often fall into these traps when discussing autoimmunity. Avoid these to maintain your professional edge:

  • Over-Promising a "Cure": We never use the word "cure" for autoimmunity. We use terms like "remission," "quiescence," or "balancing the immune response."
  • Getting Too Technical: Don't spend 20 minutes explaining Th1 vs. Th2 dominance unless the client is a scientist. Keep it focused on the "Security Fence" (gut lining).
  • Ignoring Stress: Autoimmunity is highly reactive to cortisol. If you only talk about food and ignore Elena's stress as a former teacher, you're missing half the picture.
  • Being the "Doctor": Never tell a client to stop their immunosuppressants or thyroid medication. Your role is complementary.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is the "Security Fence" analogy effective for explaining leaky gut to an autoimmune client?

It visualizes the gut barrier's role in protection and explains how "invaders" (undigested food/bacteria) trigger the immune system without needing to explain complex cellular biology.

2. If a client says, "I'm already on medication, why do I need a gut coach?" what is the best professional response?

Explain that medication manages the symptoms or the specific organ (like the thyroid), but gut coaching addresses the *environment* where 70% of the immune system lives, helping to reduce the underlying triggers of the flare-ups.

3. What percentage of the immune system is located in the gut?

Approximately 70% to 80% (located in the Gut-Associated Lymphoid Tissue or GALT).

4. What is the danger of using the word "cure" when working with autoimmune clients?

It is medically inaccurate and legally risky. Autoimmunity involves a genetic predisposition that remains; coaching helps manage the *expression* of those genes and the activity of the immune system.

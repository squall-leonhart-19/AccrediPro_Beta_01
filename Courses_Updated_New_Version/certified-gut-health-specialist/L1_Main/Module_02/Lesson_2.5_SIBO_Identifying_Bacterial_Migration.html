Pathophysiology: The Identity Crisis of the Small Intestine

The human gut is designed with a strict "zoning law." The large intestine (colon) is meant to be a densely populated microbial metropolis, housing trillions of bacteria. The small intestine, however, is meant to be relatively sparse, optimized for nutrient absorption rather than microbial fermentation.

SIBO occurs when bacteria from the colon migrate upward (retrograde) through the ileocecal valve, or when oral bacteria descend and fail to be cleared. Once in the small intestine, these bacteria feast on undigested carbohydrates, producing gases that lead to the hallmark symptoms of bloating, abdominal pain, and altered motility.

Coach Tip: The "Neighborhood" Analogy

Explain to your clients that SIBO is like having a loud, messy party in a quiet library. The bacteria aren't necessarily "bad"‚Äîthey just belong in the "party room" (the colon), not the "quiet study room" (the small intestine). This helps reduce the "fear of bacteria" and focuses the conversation on location and motility.

Identifying the Three Main Types

Not all SIBO is created equal. The specific gas produced by the overgrowth dictates the clinical presentation and the intervention strategy.

Type Primary Gas Common Symptoms Microbial Driver
Hydrogen SIBO Hydrogen ($H_2$) Diarrhea, urgent bowel movements, "explosive" bloating E. coli, Klebsiella
Methane (IMO) Methane ($CH_4$) Chronic constipation, "hard" bloating, slow transit Methanobrevibacter smithii (Archaea)
Hydrogen Sulfide Hydrogen Sulfide ($H_2S$) "Rotten egg" gas, visceral hypersensitivity, body aches Desulfovibrio, Bilophila wadsworthia

Note: Methane overgrowth is now technically classified as Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO) because methanogens are Archaea, not bacteria, and they can inhabit both the small and large intestines.

Interpreting Breath Tests: Lactulose vs. Glucose

The gold standard for identifying SIBO in a clinical setting is the Breath Test. Because humans do not produce hydrogen or methane gas, any detection of these gases in the breath indicates microbial fermentation of the substrate provided.

1. The Substrates

  • Glucose: Absorbed quickly in the proximal (upper) small intestine. It is excellent for detecting overgrowth in the first section of the small intestine but may miss overgrowth further down (distal SIBO).
  • Lactulose: A synthetic sugar that humans cannot digest. It travels the entire length of the small intestine, making it superior for detecting distal SIBO, though it has a slightly higher rate of "false positives" if transit is very fast.

2. The North American Consensus (2017) Criteria

To identify a positive SIBO result, we look for:

  • Hydrogen: A rise of ‚â• 20 ppm from baseline within 90 minutes.
  • Methane: A concentration of ‚â• 10 ppm at any point during the test.
  • Hydrogen Sulfide: A rise of ‚â• 3 ppm (using specialized Trio-Smart testing).
Coach Tip: The 90-Minute Rule

Always look at the 90-minute mark. If the gas rise happens after 120 minutes, the substrate has likely reached the colon. A rise in the colon is normal; a rise in the small intestine (before 90-100 mins) is SIBO.

The Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)

Why do bacteria migrate in the first place? The most common root cause is a failure of the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC). The MMC is the "housekeeping wave" that occurs between meals, sweeping undigested food and bacteria out of the small intestine and into the colon.

Factors that inhibit the MMC include:

  • Frequent Snacking: The MMC only activates in a fasted state (3-4 hours after eating).
  • Post-Infectious IBS: Food poisoning can trigger antibodies (anti-CdtB and anti-vinculin) that damage the nerves controlling the MMC.
  • Hypothyroidism: Low thyroid hormone slows down all metabolic processes, including gut motility.
  • Stress: High cortisol shifts the body into "fight or flight," shutting down "rest and digest" motility.

SIBO vs. SIFO: The Fungal Connection

When a client has all the symptoms of SIBO but their breath test is negative, we must consider Small Intestinal Fungal Overgrowth (SIFO). SIFO is typically caused by an overgrowth of Candida albicans in the small intestine.

Clinical Pearl: A 2014 study found that 26% of patients with unexplained GI symptoms had SIFO, and many had both SIBO and SIFO simultaneously. If SIBO protocols fail, fungi are often the hidden culprit.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is Methane overgrowth now called IMO instead of Methane SIBO?

Because the organisms responsible are Archaea (not bacteria) and they can overgrow in both the small and large intestines (Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth).

2. What is the "90-minute rule" in SIBO breath testing?

A rise in gas before 90 minutes indicates the substrate is still in the small intestine, signifying overgrowth. A rise after 90-120 minutes usually indicates the substrate has reached the colon, which is normal.

3. Which gas is most strongly associated with chronic constipation?

Methane. Methane gas acts as a local paralytic in the gut, slowing down transit time.

4. What is the primary function of the Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)?

It acts as a "housekeeping wave" that sweeps bacteria and undigested food out of the small intestine during fasting periods between meals.

Key Takeaways

  • SIBO is a condition of mal-location, where colonic bacteria migrate into the small intestine.
  • Identifying the gas type (Hydrogen, Methane, or Hydrogen Sulfide) is critical for selecting the right intervention.
  • The Migrating Motor Complex (MMC) is the primary defense against bacterial migration; its dysfunction is a leading root cause.
  • Lactulose breath tests are preferred for identifying distal (lower) small intestinal overgrowth.
  • Always consider SIFO (fungal overgrowth) if SIBO tests are negative but symptoms persist.

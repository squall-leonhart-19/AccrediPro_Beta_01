Common Mistakes in the "Identify" Phase

Even experienced coaches can fall into these traps. Being aware of them is 80% of the battle.

  • The "Solution Rush": Trying to recommend probiotics or supplements in the first 10 minutes. Correction: Spend the first session ONLY identifying and validating.
  • Over-Explaining Science: Using terms like "Lipopolysaccharides" or "Migrating Motor Complex" too early. Correction: Use analogies like "the gut's cleaning crew" or "the protective barrier."
  • Ignoring the Emotional Link: Treating the gut like a tube and ignoring Linda's stress as a teacher. Correction: Ask, "How does your gut feel on Sunday nights before school starts?"
  • Missing the "Red Flags": Failing to ask about "alarm symptoms" (unexplained weight loss, blood in stool) that require a doctor's referral.
Safety First

Always remember: If you identify a "red flag" symptom, your role is to pause and say, "I want to make sure we have a medical clearance for this specific symptom before we proceed." This increases your professional legitimacy.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to ask about antibiotic history during the "Identify" phase?

Antibiotics can cause significant dysbiosis. Identifying this history helps the coach understand why "healthy" high-fiber foods might be causing distress and points toward a need for microbiome rebuilding rather than just "clean eating."

2. What is a "Solution Rush" and why should you avoid it?

A Solution Rush is recommending supplements or protocols before fully investigating the client's history. It should be avoided because it undermines trust and may lead to incorrect or ineffective recommendations.

3. If Linda says she is "frustrated that healthy food makes her bloated," what is the best coaching response?

Validate her frustration and explain the biological mechanism (e.g., "Sometimes healthy fibers can feed the wrong bacteria if the gut is out of balance"). This shifts the blame from the client to the biology.

4. True or False: You should use advanced clinical terminology immediately to prove your expertise.

False. True expertise is shown by making complex concepts accessible. Over-explaining can overwhelm the client and create a barrier to the coaching relationship.

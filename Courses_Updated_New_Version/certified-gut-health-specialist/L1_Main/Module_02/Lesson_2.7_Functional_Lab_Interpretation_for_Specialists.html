The Functional Testing Landscape

In the "Identify" phase of the D.I.G.E.S.T. Method‚Ñ¢, choosing the right tool is the first step toward clinical success. Not all stool tests are created equal; they use different technologies to "see" into the microbiome.

Test Name Technology Best For... Key Limitation
GI-MAP (Diagnostic Solutions) Quantitative PCR (DNA) Pathogens, parasites, and H. pylori. High sensitivity. Does not show "activity" levels, only presence of DNA.
Genova GI Effects PCR + Culture + Biomarkers Comprehensive view of digestion, inflammation, and diversity. Culture-based methods can miss anaerobic bacteria.
Viome Metatranscriptomics (RNA) Microbial activity and "functional" output (what they are doing). Lacks the "clinical" markers (Calprotectin, Elastase) needed for repair.

Identifying Markers of Malabsorption

If the body cannot break down and absorb nutrients, even the most expensive supplements and "clean" diets will fail. We look at three primary markers to assess the "Upper GI" efficiency.

1. Pancreatic Elastase-1

Elastase is an enzyme produced by the pancreas that remains stable as it passes through the gut. It is the "gold standard" for assessing Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI).

  • Optimal Range: >500 ug/g
  • Functional Insufficiency: 200 - 500 ug/g (Indicates a need for digestive support)
  • Clinical Insufficiency: < 200 ug/g (Requires medical referral)

2. Steatocrit (Fecal Fat)

Elevated fecal fat indicates that the client is not emulsifying or absorbing fats. This is often linked to gallbladder sludge or low bile flow. Symptoms usually include "floating" or light-colored stools.

3. Putrefactive Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs)

While beneficial SCFAs (like Butyrate) come from fiber, putrefactive SCFAs (Valerate, Isovalerate) come from the fermentation of undigested protein. High levels suggest low stomach acid (Hypochlorhydria) or eating too quickly (low cephalic phase digestion).

Inflammatory & Immune Markers

These markers tell us how the immune system is reacting to the environment inside the gut. They are the "smoke detectors" of the GI tract.

Secretory IgA (sIgA): The First Line of Defense

sIgA is the primary antibody found in the mucous membranes. It "tags" pathogens so the immune system can deal with them.

Low sIgA

Indicates "immune exhaustion," chronic stress, or long-term infection. Client is susceptible to everything.

High sIgA

Indicates an active "battle." The immune system is currently fighting a pathogen or reacting to a food sensitivity.

Calprotectin: The Inflammation Torch

A 2022 meta-analysis confirmed that Fecal Calprotectin is 90% sensitive for identifying Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD). In a functional context, a slightly elevated Calprotectin (50-120 ug/g) suggests "low-grade" inflammation from dysbiosis or NSAID use.

Coach Tip: Red Flags

If Calprotectin is over 150-200 ug/g, stop the coaching protocol and refer the client back to their gastroenterologist immediately. We do not "coach" active IBD flares without medical supervision.

The Art of Triangulation

A lab report is just paper without the client's story. Triangulation is the process of looking at three points: Symptoms + History + Lab Data.

Consider a client with high Eosinophil Protein X (EPX). EPX is a marker of eosinophil activation (allergic response). If you see high EPX, you must triangulate:

  • Point 1 (Lab): High EPX.
  • Point 2 (Symptoms): Skin rashes or asthma.
  • Point 3 (History): New pet or seasonal allergies.

Conclusion: The gut inflammation is likely driven by an environmental or food allergy, not a pathogen.

Scope of Practice: Education vs. Diagnosis

As a Specialist, your language is your greatest legal protection. You must never "diagnose" a disease based on a lab. Instead, you educate on findings.

Medical Diagnosis (Avoid) Specialist Education (Use)
"You have a parasite infection." "The lab detected DNA from Giardia, which correlates with your symptoms."
"I am treating your Leaky Gut." "We are implementing a protocol to support your intestinal barrier integrity."
"Your labs show you have IBD." "Your Calprotectin is clinically high; please share this report with your GP."
Coach Tip: The "Why"

Always tell the client: "I am not a doctor, and I don't treat diseases. I help you understand your body's data so we can restore balance." This builds trust and professional legitimacy.

Check Your Understanding

1. A client's Pancreatic Elastase is 245 ug/g. How should you interpret this?

This is "Functional Insufficiency." While not a medical emergency, it indicates the client needs digestive enzyme support and likely has "Upper GI" digestive distress.

2. Which technology does the GI-MAP use to detect microbes?

Quantitative PCR (qPCR), which looks for the DNA of specific organisms.

3. What does a high level of Putrefactive SCFAs (like Valerate) suggest?

It suggests the fermentation of undigested protein, often caused by low stomach acid or poor chewing habits.

4. If Calprotectin is 250 ug/g, what is your next step?

Refer the client to a medical professional (GP or Gastroenterologist) immediately, as this is a clinical red flag for significant inflammation.

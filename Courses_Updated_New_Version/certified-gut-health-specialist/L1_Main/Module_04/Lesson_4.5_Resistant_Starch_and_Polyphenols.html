The Four Types of Resistant Starch (RS)

Resistant starch is a type of carbohydrate that "resists" digestion in the small intestine. Instead of being broken down into glucose and raising blood sugar, it travels to the large intestine where it becomes a feast for beneficial microbes, particularly those that produce butyrate‚Äîthe primary fuel for the cells lining the colon.

Type Description Common Food Sources
RS Type 1 Physically inaccessible starch trapped within intact plant cell walls. Whole grains, seeds, and legumes.
RS Type 2 Starch with a high amylose content that is naturally resistant to enzymes. Green (unripe) bananas, raw potatoes, high-amylose corn.
RS Type 3 Retrograded starch formed when starchy foods are cooked and then cooled. Cooled potatoes, cooled rice, cooled pasta, legumes.
RS Type 4 Chemically modified starches used in processed foods (not recommended). Modified food starch, certain "keto" breads/processed snacks.
Coach Tip: The Butyrate Connection

A 2022 study found that increasing RS intake by just 15-30g per day can increase butyrate production by up to 30%. For your clients, this means better colon health, reduced inflammation, and improved insulin sensitivity. It‚Äôs a "metabolic multi-tasker."

The Cook-and-Cool Method: Maximizing RS3

One of the most practical strategies you will teach your clients is Retrogradation. When you cook a starch (like a potato) and then cool it for at least 12-24 hours, the chemical structure of the starch changes. The amylose molecules rearrange into a tightly packed crystalline structure that human enzymes can no longer break down.

This transforms a high-glycemic food into a gut-healing prebiotic powerhouse. Even if the food is reheated at low temperatures, much of that resistant starch remains intact.

Polyphenols: The Microbiome's "Second Fuel"

Polyphenols are bioactive compounds found in plants (fruits, vegetables, tea, coffee, cocoa) known for their antioxidant properties. However, research now shows that 90-95% of polyphenols reach the colon unabsorbed.

In the colon, these polyphenols undergo a "two-way conversation" with the microbiome:

  1. Microbes Metabolize Polyphenols: Bacteria break down complex polyphenols into smaller, more absorbable metabolites (like urolithins) that enter the bloodstream and provide systemic anti-inflammatory benefits.
  2. Polyphenols Shape the Microbe Mix: Polyphenols act as selective prebiotics, inhibiting the growth of pathogens (like C. difficile) while stimulating beneficial species.

Ellagitannins, Anthocyanins, and the Mucosal Barrier

Specific polyphenols have a profound impact on the mucosal barrier (the gut lining). Two of the most important are:

  • Ellagitannins: Found in pomegranates, walnuts, and raspberries. They are converted by gut bacteria into Urolithin A, which has been shown to strengthen the "tight junctions" of the gut lining and improve mitochondrial health.
  • Anthocyanins: The deep blue/purple pigments in blueberries, blackberries, and purple carrots. These have a unique ability to stimulate Akkermansia muciniphila, a keystone species that maintains the thickness of the protective mucus layer.

A thin mucus layer is a hallmark of "Leaky Gut" (Intestinal Permeability). By using these specific polyphenols, we aren't just feeding bacteria; we are fortifying the fortress.

Clinical Application: Implementing the "Establish" Phase

As a Specialist, your role is to guide the client through the transition from "Repair" (Module 3) to "Establish" (Module 4). You cannot introduce high amounts of RS and polyphenols if the gut is still highly inflamed.

The "Low and Slow" Rule: Always start with small amounts. For Resistant Starch, start with 1-2 tablespoons of cooled rice or potato. For Polyphenols, start with 1/4 cup of berries. Monitor for gas and bloating. If the client tolerates it, increase the "dose" every 3-4 days.

1. Which type of resistant starch is formed specifically through the "cook-and-cool" process (retrogradation)?

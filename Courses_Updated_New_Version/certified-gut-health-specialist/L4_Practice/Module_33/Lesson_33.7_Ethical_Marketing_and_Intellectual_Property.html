FTC Requirements for Testimonials and Results

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has significantly tightened its stance on "health claims" in marketing. In 2023, the FTC issued a Health Products Compliance Guidance that applies directly to health coaches and specialists. The core principle is that any claim about the efficacy of a program must be backed by "competent and reliable scientific evidence."

The "Results Not Typical" Trap

For years, marketers used a tiny disclaimer saying "Results not typical" at the bottom of a glowing testimonial. The FTC no longer considers this sufficient. If you share a testimonial where a client says, "My SIBO was cured in 30 days," the FTC assumes the average consumer expects that exact result.

Protecting Your Intellectual Property

As you build your practice, you will create proprietary assets: meal plans, "gut-healing" recipes, educational slide decks, and specific supplement schedules. This is your Intellectual Property (IP). If you don't protect it, other practitioners can copy it and sell it as their own.

Copyright vs. Trademark

  • Copyright: Automatically applies to original works of authorship (your 50-page Gut Health Guide). While automatic, registering it with the U.S. Copyright Office ($45‚Äì$65) allows you to sue for statutory damages if someone steals your content.
  • Trademark: Protects your brand name (e.g., "The Bloat-Free Boss‚Ñ¢"). This prevents others from using a confusingly similar name in the same industry.

The Ethics of Affiliate Marketing

Many Gut Health Specialists earn 10‚Äì20% of their income through "affiliate links" for lab testing (like Diagnostic Solutions) or supplements (like Fullscript). While legal, the FTC requires a Clear and Conspicuous Disclosure.

You cannot simply say "Here is the link to the probiotic I recommend." You must state: "I am an affiliate for this company and may receive a small commission if you purchase through this link, at no additional cost to you."

Ethical Consideration: Always prioritize the client's needs over the commission. If a cheaper, equally effective supplement is available elsewhere, an ethical practitioner informs the client of both options.

Avoiding Deceptive High-Ticket Sales

High-ticket coaching ($2,000‚Äì$10,000+) is the fastest way to replace a nursing or teaching salary. However, "Deceptive Trade Practices" acts (at both federal and state levels) prohibit certain high-pressure tactics:

  • False Scarcity: Saying "Only 2 spots left!" when you have unlimited capacity.
  • False Urgency: "The price goes up in 10 minutes" when there is no actual plan to raise prices.
  • Earnings Claims: Promising a specific health outcome (e.g., "You will lose 10lbs") as a condition of the sale.

Check Your Understanding

1. You want to share a testimonial from a client who says your program "cured her IBS." How should you edit this for FTC compliance?

You should rephrase it to focus on functional improvements or symptom reduction rather than a "cure." Example: "My client experienced a significant reduction in bloating and achieved regular, daily bowel movements after 8 weeks." You should also avoid using the word "cure" as it is a medical claim reserved for licensed medical professionals/FDA-approved drugs.

2. Where must your affiliate disclosure be placed according to the FTC?

The disclosure must be "clear and conspicuous," meaning it should be placed *before* the link or at the very top of the page/post. It cannot be buried in a "Terms of Service" page or at the very bottom of a long caption where a user has to click "more" to see it.

3. What is the difference between a Trademark and a Copyright for your business?

A Trademark protects your brand identity (names, logos, slogans), while a Copyright protects your creative content (written guides, videos, meal plans, curriculum).

Yes, as a Certified Gut Health Specialist‚Ñ¢ in good standing, you have a license to use the name, provided you include the proper trademark symbol (‚Ñ¢) and the required attribution statement.

Key Takeaways

  • Compliance is Protection: Following FTC guidelines protects your business from massive fines and builds long-term trust with your audience.
  • Disclosure is Mandatory: Always disclose material connections (affiliate links) clearly and before the call to action.
  • Avoid Medical Claims: Focus your marketing on "functional support" and "lifestyle education" rather than "treating" or "curing" diseases.
  • Own Your Assets: Use copyright notices on all your proprietary materials to prevent intellectual property theft.
  • Integrity-First Sales: High-ticket enrollment should be based on client readiness and fit, not false scarcity or deceptive urgency.

Disease vs. Structure/Function Claims

The single most important legal distinction for a health specialist is the difference between a disease claim and a structure/function claim. This distinction is governed by the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 (DSHEA).

A Disease Claim is any claim that a product can diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent a specific disease. As a non-prescribing specialist, using this language is a violation of FDA regulations and your scope of practice.

A Structure/Function Claim describes the role of a nutrient or dietary ingredient intended to affect the normal structure or function of the human body. This is your "Safe Zone."

Prohibited: Disease Claims (FDA Violations) Permitted: Structure/Function Claims (Compliant)
"This probiotic cures Crohn's Disease." "Supports a healthy intestinal lining."
"Takes away bloating from SIBO." "Helps reduce occasional gas and bloating."
"Treats Leaky Gut Syndrome." "Maintains gut barrier integrity."
"Prevents colon cancer." "Supports long-term colon health and regularity."
Coach Tip: The "Occasional" Rule

When discussing symptoms like gas, bloating, or indigestion, always use the word "occasional." This signals to regulators that you are talking about normal bodily functions, not a chronic disease state that requires medical intervention. This simple word is a powerful legal safeguard.

FTC Guidelines for Digital Marketing

While the FDA cares about what is on the bottle, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) cares about how you sell it. If you receive a commission from a supplement company (e.g., through Fullscript, Wellevate, or Amazon Associates), you must disclose that relationship.

The FTC requires disclosures to be "Clear and Conspicuous." This means:

  • Placement: The disclosure must be placed where consumers will notice it. It cannot be buried in a "Terms of Service" page or at the very bottom of a long post.
  • Language: Use simple terms like "Paid Link," "Ad," or "I earn a commission if you purchase through this link."
  • Social Media: On Instagram or TikTok, the disclosure should be "above the fold" (before the "more" button). Using #ad or #sponsored is the minimum standard.
Coach Tip: Affiliate Transparency

Transparency actually builds trust with your target audience (women 40-55). When you say, "I earn a small commission which helps me keep providing this free education," they appreciate your honesty. It positions you as a professional business owner, not just a "hobbyist."

Digital Disclaimer Requirements

Every digital touchpoint‚Äîyour website, your PDF guides, and your email newsletters‚Äîmust contain a Mandatory FDA Disclaimer. This is often referred to as the "Quackbox" by industry insiders, but it is a non-negotiable legal requirement for structure/function claims.

The exact wording required by law is:

"These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

In addition to the FDA disclaimer, your website should feature a Professional Disclaimer stating that you are a Gut Health Specialist, not a doctor, and that your advice does not constitute medical treatment.

The 2022 FDA Regulatory Updates

In 2022, the FDA signaled a significant shift toward "Modernization" of supplement oversight. Two major updates affect your practice:

  1. Dietary Supplement Listing Act: Proposals were introduced to require all supplement manufacturers to list their products with the FDA. This means "secret" ingredients or unlisted blends are being phased out.
  2. NAC and NMN Rulings: The FDA has recently scrutinized specific ingredients like NAC (N-acetyl-L-cysteine) and NMN (Nicotinamide Mononucleotide), questioning their status as supplements vs. drugs.

A 2023 survey of 1,200 practitioners found that 68% were unaware of these ingredient status changes, highlighting the importance of staying updated to protect your clients from products that may be pulled from the market.

Vetting Supplement Manufacturers

As a specialist, you are the gatekeeper for your clients. You should only recommend brands that meet the "Gold Standard" of quality. Never recommend a supplement based solely on a "pretty label" or an Instagram influencer's post.

The Vetting Checklist:

  • CGMP Certified: Ensure the facility follows "Current Good Manufacturing Practices." This ensures the product is made in a clean, consistent environment.
  • Third-Party Testing: Look for seals like NSF International, USP (U.S. Pharmacopeia), or Informed Choice. These verify that what is on the label is actually in the bottle.
  • Certificate of Analysis (COA): A reputable company will provide a COA upon request. This document shows the lab results for purity, potency, and the absence of heavy metals or mold.

Check Your Understanding

1. Which of the following is a permitted "Structure/Function" claim?

"Supports a healthy microbiome and digestive regularity." (The other options like "Cures SIBO" or "Treats Ulcerative Colitis" are illegal disease claims).

2. Where must an affiliate disclosure be placed on a blog post according to the FTC?

It must be "Clear and Conspicuous," typically at the top of the post or immediately before the link, where the reader is likely to see it before clicking.

3. What does a COA (Certificate of Analysis) verify?

It verifies the purity, potency, and safety (lack of contaminants like heavy metals, mold, or bacteria) of a specific batch of supplements.

4. True or False: If a client gives you a testimonial saying a supplement "cured" them, you can legally post it as long as you use their name.

False. You are legally responsible for the claims made in testimonials you share. You must edit or rephrase testimonials to ensure they don't contain disease claims.

Key Takeaways

  • The Safe Zone: Always use structure/function language (e.g., "supports," "maintains," "promotes") rather than disease language ("cures," "treats").
  • Disclosure is Duty: FTC guidelines require clear, conspicuous disclosure of any financial relationship with supplement brands.
  • The Disclaimer Shield: Ensure the mandatory FDA disclaimer is present on all digital and print materials where supplement claims are made.
  • Vetting is Integrity: Only recommend brands that provide COAs and follow CGMP standards to protect your clients and your business.
  • Stay Current: Regulatory landscapes (like the 2022 FDA updates) change; reviewing your protocols annually is a professional requirement.

3. Presenting Your Price with Authority

When you reach the price, do not whisper, do not stutter, and do not keep talking after you say the number. Silence is your best friend.

Sarah's Tip: The Anchor

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary purpose of the "Cost of Inaction" question during a discovery call?

The purpose is to help the client realize that staying where they are is more painful and expensive than the investment required to change. It shifts the focus from the "price" of your program to the "cost" of their illness.

2. If a client says "I need to think about it," what is the most effective follow-up?

Acknowledge the need for thought, then ask: "Usually when people need to think about it, it's either the money, the time, or they don't think I'm the right person to help. Which one is it for you?" This forces a specific, coachable objection.

3. True or False: You should always list your prices on your website to "weed out" people who can't afford you.

False (usually). For high-ticket specialist services, it is often better to discuss price on a call where you can first establish the massive value and personalized nature of the work. Listing a high price without context can scare away people who would happily pay it once they understand the outcome.

4. Why is "silence" important after stating your price?

Silence allows the client to process the information. If you keep talking, you often sound defensive or "salesy," which can lower your perceived authority. Let the client be the next one to speak.

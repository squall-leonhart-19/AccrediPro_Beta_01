Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced practitioners can slip up during a deep dive. Keep an eye out for these four energetic "leaks":

  • Over-Explaining: Trying to explain the science of energy for 20 minutes. This pulls the client out of their body and into their analytical mind.
  • Taking on the Weight: Feeling the "lead weight" in your own body and keeping it there. Remember: You are the straw, not the container. Let the energy pass through you.
  • Rushing the Release: Trying to clear a 25-year block in five minutes. Some blocks need to be "peeled" like an onion over several sessions.
  • Breaking Eye Contact/Connection: Looking at your notes too much during the intake. Energy work begins the moment the client sees your face.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to use the client's own words (like "lead weight") during the intake?

It creates limbic resonance and builds a "bridge of trust," allowing the client's nervous system to relax and the energy body to become more receptive to clearing.

2. What is "uptalk," and why should a practitioner avoid it?

Uptalk is when your pitch rises at the end of a sentence, making it sound like a question. It signals a lack of confidence/authority and can make the client feel less safe in your "energetic container."

3. If a client like Elena feels guilty about leaving her career, what is the practitioner's primary energetic goal?

The goal is to reframe and clear the "guilt frequency," helping the client see the energy as "residue" that needs to be redirected toward her new purpose.

4. What is a "physiological shift" to look for during a session?

A spontaneous deep breath, a sigh, a change in skin color (flushing), or a visible softening of the shoulders/jaw.

Key Takeaways

  • Your voice and presence are your primary tools for building an energetic container.
  • Validation and reframing are essential steps before any actual energy clearing takes place.
  • Avoid "uptalk" and over-explaining to maintain your professional authority and keep the client grounded.
  • Success is measured by physiological shifts and the client's movement from "stuck" to "flow."
  • Practicing out loud is the only way to move from imposter syndrome to practitioner confidence.

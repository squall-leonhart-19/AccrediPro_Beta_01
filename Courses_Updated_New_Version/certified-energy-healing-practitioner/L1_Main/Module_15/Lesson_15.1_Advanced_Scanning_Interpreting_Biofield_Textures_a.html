Mapping the Seven Layers of the Biofield

The biofield is not a monolithic cloud; it is a structured, seven-layered architecture. Each layer corresponds to a different aspect of the human experience. As an advanced practitioner, your scan must determine where the density resides.

  • 1. Etheric Layer (Physical): Closest to the body (1-2 inches). Holds imprints of physical injury and organ health.
  • 2. Emotional Layer: (2-4 inches). Holds transient emotions. Blockages here feel "cloudy" or "sticky."
  • 3. Mental Layer: (4-8 inches). Holds belief systems and thought forms. Blockages feel "sharp" or "rigid."
  • 4. Astral Layer: The bridge between physical and spiritual. This is often where relational cords are found.
  • 5. Etheric Template: The blueprint of the physical body. Distortions here often precede physical illness.
  • 6. Celestial Layer: The level of emotional ecstasy and spiritual connection.
  • 7. Ketheric Template: The outer "egg" or boundary. A tear here leads to "empathic burnout."

The 'Hover and Hold' Technique

Standard scanning is a moving process. Hover and Hold is a stationary, deep-listening technique. When you find a texture that feels "heavy" or "ancient," you stop your hand movement exactly at that layer.

The Protocol:

  1. Identify the density during a general scan.
  2. Bring your palm to a halt 1 inch above the density.
  3. Synchronize your breath with the client's.
  4. Wait for the "Pulse": Ancestral imprints often have a slow, rhythmic pulse (approx. 4-6 beats per minute) that differs from the client's heartbeat.

Signature vs. Attachment

One of the most critical skills in advanced energy medicine is discerning between a client's own energy and "foreign" energy. This is the difference between a Core Imprint and an Attachment.

Core Signature: Feels "integrated." Even if it is heavy or dark, it feels like it belongs to the client's field. It moves with their breath.

External Attachment: Feels "superimposed." It often has a jagged or "static" texture. It may feel "colder" than the surrounding field and does not respond to the client's own attempts to shift their mood.

Developing Your Sensory Dictionary

To be a professional, you must be consistent. If you feel "sandpaper" texture one day and "static" the next for the same condition, your assessment lacks reliability. You must create a Sensory Dictionary.

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client: Emily

What Sarah Tells You:

"Emily, 52, lost her husband suddenly six months ago. She's experiencing intense guilt, saying she should have seen the heart attack coming. She's also isolating from friends. I feel so bad for her, but I don't know what interventions to use."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Assess the intensity and persistence of the guilt.
  • Explore Emily's support system and reasons for isolation.
  • Identify specific grief responses (cognitive, emotional, behavioral).
  • Determine if complicated grief is present.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, I can see how much you care about Emily. Your empathy is a real asset. Let's work together to channel that into effective action."
Inquire About Her Process
"Tell me about your thought process after the initial session. What were you thinking about Emily's situation?"
Offer a Framework
"One useful framework is Worden's Four Tasks of Grief: (1) Accept the reality of the loss, (2) Process the pain of grief, (3) Adjust to a world without the deceased, (4) Find an enduring connection with the deceased while embarking on a new life. Where do you see Emily struggling most?"
Collaborate on Next Steps
"What specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals could you set with Emily for the next session? Let's brainstorm together."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of supervision in this context?

The primary goal is to help the new practitioner develop their skills, confidence, and ethical awareness, while ensuring client well-being.

2. Why is it important to validate the mentee's feelings of overwhelm?

Validating their feelings builds trust and creates a safe space for learning. It also normalizes the challenges of early practice.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is about guiding, not directing.
  • Assessment is the foundation for effective intervention.
  • Frameworks like Worden's Four Tasks provide valuable structure.
  • Constructive feedback balances affirmation and guidance.
  • Mentoring strengthens the entire field of grief and loss support.

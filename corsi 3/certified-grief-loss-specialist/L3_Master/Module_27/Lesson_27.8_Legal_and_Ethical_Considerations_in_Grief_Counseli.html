The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I have a client, Emily, 52, who lost her mother to cancer six months ago. Since then, her husband lost his job, and their family dog passed away unexpectedly last month. Emily is feeling completely numb and overwhelmed. She says she can't focus at work and is constantly exhausted. I've been trying to use some of the techniques we learned in the certification, but I feel like I'm not making any progress. I'm worried I'm doing something wrong."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Sarah assessed for complicated grief or other underlying mental health conditions (e.g., depression, anxiety)?
  • Is Sarah considering the cumulative impact of multiple losses and the potential for bereavement overload?
  • Is she helping Emily identify and access appropriate support systems?
  • Is Sarah practicing self-care to avoid burnout, given the intensity of the case?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Acknowledge the Complexity

"Emily's situation is undoubtedly challenging, and it's understandable to feel overwhelmed. Recognizing the complexity is the first step in providing effective support."

2

Prioritize Assessment

Emphasize the importance of a thorough assessment to identify the most pressing needs. Encourage Sarah to use validated assessment tools for complicated grief, depression, and anxiety.

3

Teach the "Small Steps" Approach

Guide Sarah to break down the overwhelming situation into manageable steps. Focus on one area at a time, such as grief processing for her mother's death or addressing the financial stress related to her husband's job loss.

4

Reinforce Self-Care

Remind Sarah to prioritize her own well-being. Encourage her to set boundaries with clients, seek supervision regularly, and engage in activities that help her recharge.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, I commend you for taking on such a complex case. It shows your commitment to your clients. Let's explore how we can support Emily more effectively."
Encourage Self-Reflection
"What aspects of Emily's case are most challenging for you? What interventions have you tried so far, and what were the outcomes?"
Provide Targeted Guidance
"Given the multiple losses, it might be helpful to use a grief timeline to visualize the sequence of events and identify potential areas of unresolved grief. Have you considered that approach?"
Offer Support and Resources
"Remember, you're not alone in this. I'm here to support you, and there are many resources available, such as grief support groups and specialized therapists. Let's work together to find the best path forward for Emily."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is "bereavement overload," and why is it important to consider in cases of multiple losses?

Bereavement overload refers to the cumulative impact of multiple losses occurring in a short period. It's important because it can overwhelm a person's coping mechanisms and lead to more severe grief reactions.

2. What are some validated assessment tools that can be used to assess complicated grief?

Examples of validated assessment tools include the Inventory of Complicated Grief (ICG), the Prolonged Grief Disorder Scale (PGDS), and the Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG).

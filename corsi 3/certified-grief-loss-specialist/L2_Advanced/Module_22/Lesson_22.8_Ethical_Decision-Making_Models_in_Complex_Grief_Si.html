Clinical Reasoning: Ethical Decision-Making

Analyzing the Ethical Dimensions

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Issues
Several ethical issues are present: Client competence (alcohol use affecting decision-making), confidentiality (balancing client's right to privacy with duty to protect), dual relationships (potential conflict of interest if working with both Eleanor and her children), and cultural sensitivity (addressing stigma associated with mental health).
Step 2: Review Relevant Ethical Codes
Consult the ethical codes of your professional organization (e.g., ACA, NASW) regarding competence, confidentiality, dual relationships, and cultural competence. The ACA Code of Ethics, for instance, emphasizes the importance of avoiding dual relationships that could impair objectivity or increase the risk of harm (A.6.a).
Step 3: Consider Legal and Regulatory Requirements
Be aware of state laws regarding mandated reporting of child endangerment. In many states, professionals are required to report suspected child abuse or neglect (e.g., if Eleanor's alcohol use impairs her ability to care for her children). Understand HIPAA regulations regarding client privacy and information sharing.
Step 4: Develop a Plan of Action
Address Eleanor's alcohol use by exploring its impact on her functioning and offering resources for substance abuse treatment. Discuss the potential risks and benefits of working with her children and obtain informed consent. Maintain clear boundaries regarding social media contact. Seek consultation with a supervisor or ethics expert to ensure ethical decision-making.

Differential Considerations: Alternative Explanations

Exploring Other Possibilities

1

Major Depressive Disorder

Rule out major depression as a co-occurring condition. Assess for symptoms such as persistent sadness, loss of interest, changes in appetite or sleep, and suicidal ideation. A 2021 study in the *Journal of Affective Disorders* found that approximately 40% of individuals with prolonged grief also meet the criteria for major depressive disorder.

2

Complex Trauma/PTSD

Consider the possibility of complex trauma related to the circumstances of her husband's death. Assess for symptoms such as intrusive memories, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and hyperarousal. A meta-analysis published in *Trauma, Violence, & Abuse* (2022) indicated a strong association between traumatic loss and the development of PTSD.

3

Substance Use Disorder

Evaluate the severity of Eleanor's alcohol use and assess for signs of substance use disorder. Consider using standardized screening tools such as the AUDIT-C. According to the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIAAA), women are more vulnerable to the long-term health effects of alcohol compared to men.

4

Adjustment Disorder

Differentiate between prolonged grief and adjustment disorder with depressed mood. Adjustment disorder typically resolves within 6 months of the stressor, while prolonged grief persists beyond 12 months. The *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5-TR)* provides specific criteria for diagnosing prolonged grief disorder.

Referral Triggers: When to Seek Additional Support

Recognizing the Need for External Expertise

  • Suicidal Ideation or Intent: Immediate referral to a psychiatrist or crisis intervention team. Suicide is a leading cause of death among individuals experiencing grief, particularly when compounded by other mental health conditions (Shear et al., 2020).
  • Severe Substance Use Disorder: Referral to a substance abuse treatment program or addiction specialist. Untreated substance use disorders can significantly impair grief processing and increase the risk of relapse (Center for Substance Abuse Treatment, 2014).
  • Child Endangerment: If Eleanor's alcohol use is impacting her ability to care for her children, a report to child protective services may be necessary. State laws mandate reporting of suspected child abuse or neglect to protect vulnerable children (e.g., California Welfare and Institutions Code Section 300).
  • Complex Medical Conditions: Referral to a physician or specialist for evaluation and management of any underlying medical conditions contributing to Eleanor's symptoms.
  • Legal Consultation: Seek legal advice regarding ethical obligations related to confidentiality, mandated reporting, and potential liability.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the primary ethical considerations in Eleanor's case?

The primary ethical considerations include client competence due to alcohol use, confidentiality, potential dual relationships if working with her children, and cultural sensitivity related to stigma surrounding mental health.

2. What steps should be taken to address the potential conflict of interest if the counselor also provides services to Eleanor's children?

Obtain informed consent from all parties, clearly define the roles and responsibilities of the counselor, maintain separate records for each client, and be vigilant for signs of divided loyalty or compromised objectivity. Seeking supervision is also recommended.

3. What are some potential legal and regulatory requirements that may apply in Eleanor's case?

State laws regarding mandated reporting of child endangerment (if Eleanor's alcohol use impairs her ability to care for her children), HIPAA regulations regarding client privacy and information sharing, and duty to warn if Eleanor poses a threat to herself or others.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex cases often involve multiple overlapping ethical considerations that require careful analysis and decision-making.
  • Consulting ethical codes, seeking supervision, and obtaining legal advice are essential steps in navigating ethical dilemmas.
  • A trauma-informed approach is crucial when working with clients who have experienced traumatic loss.
  • Collaboration with other professionals (e.g., psychiatrists, addiction specialists) is often necessary to provide comprehensive care.
  • Maintaining clear boundaries, obtaining informed consent, and documenting all decisions are critical for ethical practice.

Development and Structure

The ICG-R, developed by Prigerson et al. (1995), is a 22-item self-report measure designed to assess symptoms of complicated grief. Each item is rated on a 5-point Likert scale (0 = Not at all to 4 = Extremely). The ICG-R assesses the intensity and frequency of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors associated with prolonged grief. It reflects aspects of the Explore Loss (the "E" in the H.E.A.R.T. Frameworkâ„¢) stage, focusing on the grieverâ€™s attachment and experience of the loss.

Administration and Scoring

The ICG-R is relatively easy to administer, typically taking 10-15 minutes to complete. It can be administered in various settings, including clinical offices, hospitals, and research environments. Scoring involves summing the ratings for each item. The total score ranges from 0 to 88. Higher scores indicate a greater severity of complicated grief symptoms. The scoring process provides quantifiable data that helps in the Assess Needs stage of the H.E.A.R.T. Frameworkâ„¢.

Psychometric Properties

The ICG-R demonstrates strong psychometric properties, including high internal consistency, test-retest reliability, and validity. A study by Prigerson et al. (1995) reported a Cronbach's alpha of 0.94, indicating excellent internal consistency. Test-retest reliability over a two-week period was r = 0.82. The ICG-R has also demonstrated construct and concurrent validity by correlating with other measures of grief, depression, and anxiety. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found a pooled sensitivity of 85% and specificity of 78% for identifying individuals with PGD using the ICG-R.

Clinical Cut-offs and Interpretation

A cut-off score of 25 is commonly used to identify individuals at risk for complicated grief. Scores above this threshold suggest the presence of significant complicated grief symptoms and warrant further evaluation. However, clinical interpretation should also consider individual factors, cultural context, and the time elapsed since the loss. The ICG-R helps in differentiating between normal grief and complicated grief, which is crucial for appropriate intervention planning.

Strengths and Limitations

The ICG-R has several strengths, including its brevity, ease of administration, and strong psychometric properties. It is widely used in research and clinical practice. However, it also has limitations. It is a self-report measure, which may be subject to response bias. Additionally, it may not capture the full complexity of grief experiences, particularly in diverse cultural contexts. It is essential to use the ICG-R in conjunction with clinical interviews and other assessment methods to obtain a comprehensive understanding of the individual's grief experience.

Case Examples

Case Study 1: Sarah J.

Client: Sarah J., 42 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah lost her husband in a car accident six months ago. She reports persistent yearning, difficulty accepting the death, and feeling emotionally numb. She avoids reminders of her husband and struggles to engage in social activities.

Intervention: Sarah completed the ICG-R, scoring 32. Based on this score and a clinical interview, she was diagnosed with Prolonged Grief Disorder. The intervention included grief-focused therapy, cognitive restructuring, and exposure techniques.

Outcomes: After 12 weeks of therapy, Sarah's ICG-R score decreased to 18. She reported improved emotional regulation, increased engagement in social activities, and a greater ability to cope with her grief.

Case Study 2: David L.

Client: David L., 68 years old

Presenting Symptoms: David lost his adult daughter to cancer a year ago. He experiences intrusive thoughts about her illness, feelings of anger and guilt, and difficulty finding meaning in life. He reports significant functional impairment and social isolation.

Intervention: David completed the ICG-R, scoring 28. Based on this and a clinical assessment, he was diagnosed with PGD. The intervention included grief counseling, support group participation, and mindfulness-based techniques.

Outcomes: After six months of intervention, David's ICG-R score decreased to 20. He reported reduced intrusive thoughts, improved emotional well-being, and increased social engagement.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Step-by-Step Case Analysis

Step 1: Initial Assessment & Tool Selection
Administer the Inventory of Complicated Grief (ICG) to assess the severity of complicated grief symptoms. Also, use the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI-II) and the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7) scale to screen for co-occurring depression and anxiety, which are common in prolonged grief.
Step 2: Interpreting Assessment Results
Eleanor's ICG score is 32, indicating a high level of complicated grief. Her BDI-II score is 25, suggesting moderate depression, and her GAD-7 score is 18, indicating severe anxiety. These scores confirm the presence of significant grief-related distress, complicated by depression and anxiety. A 2023 study in *JAMA Psychiatry* found that individuals with similar ICG scores had a 70% chance of meeting criteria for Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD).
Step 3: Identifying Contributing Factors
Explore the nature of Eleanor's relationship with her sister. Was it exceptionally close or fraught with unresolved conflict? Investigate potential cognitive distortions contributing to her grief, such as self-blame or rumination. Assess for secondary losses, such as changes in family dynamics or social roles after Sarah's death.
Step 4: Considering Cultural and Societal Influences
Explore Eleanor's cultural background and beliefs about grief and mourning. Are there cultural norms that may be influencing her grief expression or help-seeking behavior? Consider the societal expectations around "moving on" from grief and how these expectations may be impacting her.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Going On?

1

Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD)

While ICG scores are suggestive, a formal diagnosis of PGD requires meeting specific criteria outlined in the DSM-5-TR. Rule out normal bereavement by ensuring symptoms have persisted for at least 12 months in adults.

2

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

Differentiate between grief-related depression and MDD. Assess for anhedonia, changes in appetite, and suicidal ideation, which are more characteristic of MDD. Consider that up to 50% of individuals with PGD also meet criteria for MDD (Shear et al., 2005).

3

Anxiety Disorders

Determine the specific type of anxiety disorder. Is it generalized anxiety, panic disorder, or social anxiety? Explore the relationship between anxiety symptoms and grief triggers.

4

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

If Sarah's death was traumatic (e.g., witnessed accident), assess for PTSD symptoms such as flashbacks, nightmares, and avoidance behaviors. A 2019 meta-analysis in *The Lancet Psychiatry* found a 30% comorbidity between PGD and PTSD following traumatic loss.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key differences between normal bereavement and Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD)?

Normal bereavement involves a natural process of adapting to loss, with grief symptoms typically diminishing over time. PGD, on the other hand, is characterized by persistent and disabling grief symptoms that last for at least 12 months in adults, significantly impairing functioning and quality of life.

2. How can assessment tools like the ICG, BDI-II, and GAD-7 inform your clinical decision-making in grief therapy?

These tools provide valuable information about the severity of grief symptoms, the presence of co-occurring conditions (e.g., depression, anxiety), and the client's overall level of distress. This data can help you tailor your treatment approach to address the client's specific needs and monitor progress over time.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex grief cases often involve co-occurring mental health conditions, such as depression and anxiety, which require comprehensive assessment and treatment.
  • Assessment tools like the ICG, BDI-II, and GAD-7 can provide valuable data to inform clinical decision-making and monitor treatment progress.
  • A phased intervention approach, starting with stabilization and psychoeducation, followed by grief-focused interventions, and ending with integration and future planning, can be effective in addressing complex grief.
  • It's crucial to consider cultural and societal influences on grief expression and help-seeking behavior.
  • Knowing when to refer to other professionals (e.g., psychiatrists, medical doctors) is essential for ensuring the client receives appropriate care.

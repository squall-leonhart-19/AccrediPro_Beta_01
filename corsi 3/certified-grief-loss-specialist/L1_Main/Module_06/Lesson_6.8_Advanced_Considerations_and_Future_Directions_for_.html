Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being here and for sharing such a deeply personal experience with me. It takes immense courage to reach out, especially when you're feeling this vulnerable. I want you to know that your feelings are valid, and you're not alone in this journey."
MARIA:
"Thank you. It's just... some days, it feels like it happened yesterday. I don't know how to move forward."
YOU:
"I understand. Grief doesn't follow a linear timeline, and it's perfectly normal to have days where the pain feels fresh. Let's explore some ways to honor your mother's memory while also finding a path toward healing and rediscovering joy in your life. Does that sound like a direction you'd like to take?"
MARIA:
"Yes, definitely. I want to honor her, but I also want to live my life."
YOU:
"That's a beautiful intention, Maria. We can work together to create a space where you can hold both grief and joy. Before we go further, I'd like to ask you about some specific memories you cherish of your mother. Would you be willing to share one or two?"

You'll Know It Worked When...

  • Maria expresses relief at being heard and understood.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a client's grief before offering solutions?

Validating her feelings builds trust and creates a safe space, making her more receptive to your guidance. It also acknowledges the unique nature of her experience.

2. What are some non-verbal cues that indicate a client feels understood and supported?

Relaxed body language, eye contact, nodding, and a softer tone of voice are all positive indicators.

Key Takeaways

  • Empathy and validation are crucial in grief coaching.
  • Avoid minimizing or dismissing a client's feelings.
  • Focus on honoring the deceased and finding ways to integrate the loss.
  • Guide the client towards rediscovering joy and meaning in life.
  • Remember that grief is a unique and personal journey.

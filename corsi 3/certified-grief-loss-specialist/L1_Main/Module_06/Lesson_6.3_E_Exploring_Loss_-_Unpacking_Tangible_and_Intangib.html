Identifying Tangible Losses

Tangible losses are the concrete, easily identifiable aspects of loss. These are often the first things that come to mind when someone experiences grief. Helping clients identify and articulate these losses is a critical first step in the exploration process.

Examples of tangible losses include:

  • Loss of companionship
  • Loss of financial security
  • Loss of physical touch and intimacy
  • Loss of shared routines and activities
  • Loss of a home or belongings

Here's a practical technique: Encourage your clients to create a list of all the things they have lost as a result of the death or significant life change. This simple exercise can be surprisingly powerful in revealing the breadth of their loss.

Exploring Intangible Losses

Intangible losses are the abstract, less obvious aspects of loss. These can be more challenging to identify but are often just as significant as tangible losses. Exploring these losses can uncover deep emotional wounds and provide valuable insights into the grieving process.

Examples of intangible losses include:

  • Loss of dreams and aspirations
  • Loss of roles and identity (e.g., spouse, parent, caregiver)
  • Loss of a sense of security and stability
  • Loss of future possibilities and expectations
  • Loss of faith or spiritual connection

To help clients explore these intangible losses, ask open-ended questions such as: "What did you hope for that will no longer be possible?" or "How has this loss changed your sense of who you are?".

Using Genograms and Visual Aids

Genograms and other visual aids can be powerful tools for mapping a client's relationship with the deceased and identifying patterns of loss in their family history. A genogram is a visual representation of a family tree that includes information about relationships, significant life events, and patterns of illness or behavior. By creating a genogram together, you and your client can gain a deeper understanding of their family dynamics and how past losses may be influencing their current grief experience.

Here's how to use a genogram in the 'Exploring Loss' stage:

  1. Gather Information: Ask your client about their family history, including births, deaths, marriages, divorces, and significant relationships.
  2. Create the Genogram: Use standard genogram symbols to represent family members and their relationships. Include dates of birth and death where applicable.
  3. Identify Patterns: Look for patterns of loss, unresolved grief, or dysfunctional relationships. Discuss these patterns with your client and explore how they may be affecting their current grief experience.

Other visual aids, such as timelines or relationship maps, can also be helpful in exploring the client's relationship with the deceased and identifying key moments of connection and loss.

Addressing Ambiguous and Disenfranchised Grief

Ambiguous grief occurs when a loss is unclear or uncertain, such as when a loved one is missing or has a chronic illness like Alzheimer's disease. Disenfranchised grief occurs when a loss is not recognized or validated by society, such as the loss of a pet, a miscarriage, or the death of an ex-partner. Both types of grief can be particularly challenging to process because they lack the social support and rituals that typically accompany more recognized forms of loss. A 2021 study published in the "Journal of Social Work Practice" found that individuals experiencing disenfranchised grief reported significantly higher levels of depression and anxiety compared to those experiencing recognized grief (OR = 2.45, 95% CI [1.87, 3.21]).

When working with clients experiencing ambiguous or disenfranchised grief, it's essential to:

  • Validate their feelings: Acknowledge that their grief is real and valid, even if others don't understand it.
  • Provide education: Explain the concepts of ambiguous and disenfranchised grief and how they can impact the grieving process.
  • Encourage self-compassion: Help them be kind and understanding towards themselves, especially when dealing with difficult emotions.
  • Facilitate support: Connect them with support groups or online communities where they can share their experiences with others who understand.

Client: Sarah, 42

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with persistent feelings of sadness and emptiness following the death of her husband, Mark, six months prior. While she acknowledged the tangible losses (e.g., companionship, financial support), she struggled to articulate the deeper sources of her grief.

Intervention: Using the H.E.A.R.T. Frameworkâ„¢, we focused on 'Exploring Loss.' I guided Sarah through a series of exercises to identify her intangible losses. Through open-ended questions and reflective listening, Sarah realized she had lost her dreams of traveling the world with Mark in retirement, her role as a caregiver and confidante, and her sense of security and stability.

Outcomes: By acknowledging and processing these intangible losses, Sarah experienced a significant reduction in her feelings of sadness and emptiness. She began to explore new dreams and goals for the future, and she found renewed meaning and purpose in her life.

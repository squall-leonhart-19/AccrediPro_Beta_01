Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing your challenges with me. It sounds like you're feeling pulled in many directions. I want to acknowledge that you're already doing your best, and seeking support is a sign of strength."
MARIA:
"Thank you. It's just... I feel like I'm failing. All the other parents seem to have it together."
YOU:
"I hear you, and it's so easy to compare ourselves to others, especially with social media. But remember, everyone faces challenges, and what you see online is often a curated version of reality. Let's focus on what's within your control and what will bring you and your son closer."
YOU:
"Before we dive into strategies, I'd love to understand what a 'good day' looks like for you regarding screen time. What's your ideal scenario?"
MARIA:
"Well, ideally, he'd play outside more, read books, and only use screens for an hour or so."
YOU:
"That's a wonderful vision. Now, let's explore what's preventing that from happening. What are some of the obstacles you're facing?"

Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå Immediately offering solutions without validating Maria's feelings.
‚úì Acknowledge her struggles and show empathy first: "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed, and that's completely understandable."
‚ùå Making her feel guilty about using screens.
‚úì Avoid judgment. Instead, say something like: "Many parents rely on screens, especially when juggling multiple responsibilities."
‚ùå Telling her what she *should* be doing.
‚úì Use open-ended questions to help her discover her own solutions: "What are some activities your son enjoys that don't involve screens?"
‚ùå Focusing solely on the negative aspects of screen time.
‚úì Acknowledge potential benefits (educational apps, connection with friends) before exploring healthier alternatives.

You'll Know It Worked When...

  • Maria expresses relief and feels heard.
  • She starts brainstorming potential solutions.
  • She identifies her own limiting beliefs (e.g., "I'm a bad parent if I say no").
  • She feels empowered to take small steps towards change.
  • She expresses a willingness to experiment with new strategies.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate Maria's feelings before offering solutions?

Validating her feelings builds trust and rapport. It shows her that you understand her struggles and that you're not there to judge her. This makes her more receptive to your coaching.

2. What is the danger of immediately suggesting strict screen time limits?

Suggesting strict limits without understanding her situation can make Maria feel overwhelmed and resistant. It's more effective to help her identify small, manageable changes that fit her lifestyle and values.

Key Takeaways

  • Always validate the client's feelings before offering solutions.
  • Avoid judgment and create a safe space for sharing.
  • Use open-ended questions to help the client discover their own answers.
  • Focus on small, manageable changes.
  • Empower the client to take ownership of their parenting journey.

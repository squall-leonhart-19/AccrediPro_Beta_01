Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing your history with me. It sounds like you've been through a lot, and it's understandable that you're feeling anxious. I want to assure you that anxiety is often a signal from our bodies that something is out of balance, and together, we can explore what that might be for you."
MARIA:
"That's good to hear. I just feel like I'm always on edge."
YOU:
"I understand. Many people experience that 'on edge' feeling, and there are often underlying factors contributing to it. To get a clearer picture, I'd like to ask you some questions about your holistic history ‚Äì things like your diet, sleep habits, stress levels, and relationships. Would that be alright with you?"
MARIA:
"Yes, that sounds helpful."
YOU:
"Great. Let's start with your diet. What does a typical day of eating look like for you?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a client's feelings before diving into their holistic history?

Validating their feelings builds trust and rapport, making them more open to sharing personal details.

2. What are some examples of open-ended questions you can ask to gather information about a client's holistic history?

"What does a typical day of eating look like for you?" "How would you describe your stress levels?" "What are your sleep habits?"

Key Takeaways

  • Validating a client's feelings is crucial for building trust.
  • A holistic history involves exploring various aspects of a client's life, including diet, lifestyle, stressors, and relationships.
  • Asking open-ended questions encourages clients to share more information.
  • Empathy and a non-judgmental approach are essential for creating a safe space.

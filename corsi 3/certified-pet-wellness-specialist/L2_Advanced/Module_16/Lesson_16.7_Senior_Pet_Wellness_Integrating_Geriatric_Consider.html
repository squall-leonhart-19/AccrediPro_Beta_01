Case Study: Senior Wellness

Integrating Geriatric Considerations

Client: Bella, a 48-year-old woman, sought help for her 12-year-old mixed breed dog, Max. Bella is a former teacher who is looking to transition into pet wellness.

Presenting Symptoms: Max exhibited decreased activity levels, cognitive decline (disorientation, altered sleep patterns), and obvious signs of dental disease (bad breath, difficulty chewing). Bella was increasingly concerned about Max's quality of life and wanted to ensure he was comfortable.

This case study will guide us through the application of the P.A.W.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ to address Max's specific needs.

Applying the 'P': Detailed History

The 'P' in the P.A.W.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ stands for Profile & Partnership. When working with senior pets, gathering a detailed history is paramount. Focus on:

  • Age-Related Changes: Document any changes in activity level, appetite, weight, and sleep patterns.
  • Cognitive Function: Inquire about signs of disorientation, memory loss, and changes in social interaction. Tools like the Canine Cognitive Dysfunction Rating Scale can be helpful.
  • Mobility: Assess for stiffness, lameness, and difficulty navigating stairs or jumping.
  • Dental Health: Note any signs of dental disease, such as bad breath, drooling, or difficulty chewing.
  • Owner's Perception of Quality of Life: Understand how the owner perceives their pet's overall well-being. Use open-ended questions to encourage detailed responses.

For Max, Bella reported that he had become less enthusiastic about walks, often seemed confused in the house, and struggled to eat his kibble. She rated his quality of life as a 6 out of 10.

Applying the 'A': Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment

The 'A' in the P.A.W.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ represents Assess & Analyze. A comprehensive geriatric assessment is crucial for identifying underlying health issues. This includes:

  • Blood Work and Urinalysis: To assess organ function and detect any underlying diseases.
  • Dental Examination: A thorough oral exam to evaluate the severity of dental disease.
  • Pain Assessment: Using pain scales to identify and quantify any discomfort.
  • Cognitive Function Testing: Employing tools like the Canine Cognitive Dysfunction Rating Scale to assess cognitive decline. A score of 30 or higher on this scale often indicates significant cognitive impairment (Salvin et al., 2011).

For Max, blood work revealed slightly elevated kidney values, and his dental examination confirmed moderate to severe dental disease. His Canine Cognitive Dysfunction Rating Scale score was 35, indicating significant cognitive impairment. He also exhibited signs of pain upon palpation of his hips.

Applying the 'W': Creating a Wellness Plan

The 'W' in the P.A.W.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ is Wellness Plan & Implementation. Based on the assessment, a tailored wellness plan should address the identified issues:

  • Pain Management:
    • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) to manage pain associated with hip dysplasia.
    • Acupuncture to provide additional pain relief and improve mobility.
  • Cognitive Support:
    • Dietary enrichment with antioxidants and omega-3 fatty acids.
    • Medications like selegiline to improve cognitive function. A study by Ruehl et al. (1995) showed that selegiline improved signs of cognitive dysfunction in approximately 70% of dogs.
  • Dental Care:
    • Professional dental cleaning under anesthesia.
    • Home dental care with regular tooth brushing and dental chews.
  • Modified Exercise:
    • Short, frequent walks to maintain mobility without overexertion.
    • Hydrotherapy to provide low-impact exercise.
  • Environmental Modifications:
    • Ramps to help Max access furniture and navigate stairs.
    • Soft bedding to provide comfort and support.

For Max, the wellness plan included NSAIDs for pain, a prescription diet enriched with antioxidants, dental cleaning, short daily walks, and the addition of ramps to the house. Bella, feeling empowered by this plan, was excited to implement these changes.

Applying the 'S': Ongoing Support and Monitoring

The 'S' in the P.A.W.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ represents Sustain & Support. Ongoing support and monitoring are crucial for senior pets:

  • Regular Monitoring: Schedule follow-up appointments to monitor health parameters and adjust medication dosages as needed.
  • Owner Support: Provide ongoing support and education to the owner.
  • End-of-Life Considerations: Discuss end-of-life options with sensitivity and compassion.

After three months, Bella reported that Max was more active and responsive. His Canine Cognitive Dysfunction Rating Scale score decreased to 25, indicating improved cognitive function.

Case Study

Complex Canine Anxiety

üê∂

Client: "Shadow," 3-year-old Border Collie

Presenting Symptoms: Separation anxiety, reactivity to strangers, destructive behavior

Shadow, a 3-year-old Border Collie, presented with a history of separation anxiety, reactivity towards strangers, and destructive behavior when left alone. The owner, Emily, a 45-year-old graphic designer working from home, reported that Shadow would bark excessively, chew furniture, and attempt to escape when she left the house, even for short periods. Shadow also displayed aggressive barking and lunging towards unfamiliar people during walks.

Emily had tried basic obedience training, but Shadow's anxiety seemed to exacerbate the behavioral issues. She was feelingoverwhelmed and unsure of how to help Shadow.

Initial assessment revealed a Canine Behavioral Assessment and Research Questionnaire (C-BARQ) score indicating severe separation anxiety and stranger-directed aggression. Physical examination was unremarkable, but blood work was recommended to rule out any underlying medical conditions contributing to the anxiety.

Applying the 'P': Profile & Partnership

The first step in addressing Shadow's complex case is to establish a solid Profile & Partnership. This involves gathering a detailed history, understanding the client's goals, and building a collaborative relationship.

  • Comprehensive History: In Shadow's case, Emily provided a detailed account of Shadow's behavior, including triggers, frequency, intensity, and duration of the problematic behaviors. This included information about Shadow's early life, socialization experiences, and any previous training or interventions.
  • Client Goals: Emily's primary goals were to reduce Shadow's separation anxiety so she could leave the house without causing distress to Shadow or damage to the property, and to manage Shadow's reactivity towards strangers, allowing for safer and more enjoyable walks.
  • Building Partnership: Establishing trust and open communication with Emily was crucial. This involved actively listening to her concerns, validating her feelings, and explaining the treatment plan in a clear and understandable manner. It also involved setting realistic expectations and emphasizing the importance of consistency and patience.

Applying the 'A': Assess & Analyze

The Assess & Analyze phase involves a thorough evaluation of the dog's behavior, identifying potential underlying causes, and formulating a diagnosis.

  • Behavioral Assessment: Shadow's case required a comprehensive behavioral assessment, including observation of Shadow's behavior in different contexts (e.g., at home, on walks), and the use of standardized questionnaires such as the C-BARQ. The C-BARQ score confirmed the presence of severe separation anxiety and stranger-directed aggression.
  • Medical Evaluation: While the initial physical examination was unremarkable, blood work was essential to rule out any underlying medical conditions that could be contributing to Shadow's anxiety. Conditions such as hypothyroidism and pain can sometimes manifest as behavioral changes.
  • Functional Analysis: A functional analysis was conducted to identify the triggers and maintaining factors for Shadow's problematic behaviors. This involved examining the antecedents (events preceding the behavior), the behavior itself, and the consequences (what happens after the behavior). For example, it was determined that Shadow's separation anxiety was triggered by Emily's pre-departure cues (e.g., putting on her coat, picking up her keys), and maintained by the anxiety itself, as well as Emily's attempts to soothe Shadow, which inadvertently reinforced the anxious behavior.

Applying the 'W': Wellness Plan & Implementation

The Wellness Plan & Implementation stage focuses on developing and implementing a tailored treatment plan to address the dog's specific needs.

  • Behavior Modification: Shadow's treatment plan included a combination of desensitization, counter-conditioning, and positive reinforcement. Desensitization involved gradually exposing Shadow to her triggers (e.g., Emily's pre-departure cues) at a low intensity, while counter-conditioning involved pairing these triggers with something positive (e.g., high-value treats). Positive reinforcement was used to reward calm and relaxed behavior.
  • Environmental Management: Environmental management strategies were implemented to minimize Shadow's exposure to triggers and create a safe and predictable environment. This included providing Shadow with a comfortable and secure den (e.g., a crate or bed), using puzzle toys to keep her occupied, and avoiding situations that were likely to trigger her reactivity.
  • Medication (if necessary): In some cases, medication may be necessary to help manage anxiety. Given the severity of Shadow's anxiety, a referral to a veterinary behaviorist was recommended to discuss the potential benefits and risks of medication. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) are commonly used to treat anxiety in dogs.
  • Dietary Considerations: While not the primary focus, dietary adjustments to include calming supplements or specific ingredients known to support neurological health were discussed as adjunctive therapies.

Applying the 'S': Sustain & Support

The final stage, Sustain & Support, emphasizes the importance of ongoing support, monitoring, and adjustments to the treatment plan.

  • Regular Follow-Up: Regular follow-up appointments with Emily were scheduled to monitor Shadow's progress, address any challenges, and make adjustments to the treatment plan as needed.
  • Client Education: Ongoing client education was provided to ensure that Emily understood the principles of behavior modification and was able to implement the treatment plan consistently.
  • Support Network: Emily was encouraged to connect with other dog owners who were dealing with similar issues, either through online forums or local support groups.
  • Long-Term Management: It was emphasized that managing anxiety is often a long-term process, and that Shadow may require ongoing support and medication to maintain her progress.

Key Takeaways

  • Addressing canine anxiety requires a multifaceted approach that considers both behavioral and medical factors.
  • The P.A.W.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ provides a structured approach to assessing and managing canine behavioral issues.
  • Consistency, patience, and ongoing support are essential for successful treatment outcomes.
  • Referral to a veterinary behaviorist may be necessary in complex cases.
  • Client education and involvement are crucial for long-term management.

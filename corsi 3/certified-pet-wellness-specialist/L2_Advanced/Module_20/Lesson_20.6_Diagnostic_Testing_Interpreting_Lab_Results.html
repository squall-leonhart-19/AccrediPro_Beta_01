Complete Blood Count (CBC)

The CBC provides a wealth of information about a pet's overall health. Beyond the basic ranges, look for subtle trends and relationships between different parameters. For instance, a slightly elevated white blood cell count (WBC) with a normal differential might indicate a low-grade inflammation, while a concurrent decrease in red blood cell count (RBC) could suggest chronic blood loss or bone marrow suppression.

Key components to analyze include:

  • RBC indices (MCV, MCH, MCHC): These help classify anemia (e.g., macrocytic, normocytic, microcytic).
  • Platelet count: Essential for assessing clotting ability. Consider breed-specific normal ranges (e.g., Greyhounds often have lower platelet counts).
  • WBCdifferential: Neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, and basophils each play unique roles in the immune system. Deviations from normal ranges can point to specific types of infections or inflammatory conditions. A left shift (increased immature neutrophils) indicates an acute inflammatory response.

Serum Chemistry Profiles

Serum chemistry profiles evaluate organ function, electrolyte balance, and other critical metabolic processes. Interpreting these results requires a nuanced understanding of each parameter and its potential interactions with other values.

Key components to analyze include:

  • Liver enzymes (ALT, AST, ALP, GGT): Elevated levels can indicate liver damage, but the specific pattern of elevation can help differentiate between different types of liver disease (e.g., hepatocellular damage vs. cholestasis). Consider age-related normal ranges, as ALP is often higher in young, growing animals.
  • Kidney values (BUN, creatinine): Elevated levels indicate kidney dysfunction. Symmetric dimethylarginine (SDMA) is a more sensitive marker for early kidney disease than creatinine alone.
  • Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, chloride): Imbalances can have profound effects on cardiac and neurological function. Consider the anion gap to help identify the underlying cause of metabolic acidosis.
  • Glucose: Elevated levels suggest diabetes mellitus, while low levels can indicate insulinoma or other metabolic disorders.
  • Total protein and albumin: Low albumin levels can indicate protein-losing nephropathy or enteropathy, while elevated total protein can suggest dehydration or inflammation.

Urinalysis

Urinalysis provides valuable information about kidney function, urinary tract health, and systemic metabolic disorders. It should be performed in conjunction with serum chemistry for a complete assessment of kidney function.

Key components to analyze include:

  • Urine specific gravity (USG): Measures the concentrating ability of the kidneys. Isosthenuria (USG ~1.008-1.012) indicates impaired kidney function. Hypersthenuria (USG >1.030 in dogs, >1.035 in cats) indicates adequate concentration.
  • Urine pH: Can be affected by diet, medications, and bacterial infections.
  • Proteinuria: Indicates damage to the glomerular filtration barrier. The urine protein:creatinine ratio (UPC) quantifies the degree of proteinuria.
  • Glucosuria: Indicates hyperglycemia exceeding the renal threshold for glucose reabsorption.
  • Sediment: Examination of urine sediment can reveal the presence of red blood cells, white blood cells, crystals, bacteria, and casts.

Fecal Examinations

Fecal examinations are essential for diagnosing parasitic infections and evaluating gut health. Different techniques are used to identify different types of parasites.

Key techniques include:

  • Fecal flotation: Detects parasite eggs, larvae, and protozoan oocysts.
  • Fecal direct smear: Useful for identifying motile protozoa like Giardia and Tritrichomonas.
  • Fecal sedimentation: Detects heavier parasite eggs that may not float well, such as fluke eggs.
  • Fecal PCR: Highly sensitive and specific for detecting parasitic DNA, including Giardia, Cryptosporidium, and Tritrichomonas.

Beyond parasite detection, fecal examinations can also provide information about gut health. Fecal scoring systems can be used to assess stool consistency, which can be indicative of dietary imbalances, malabsorption, or inflammatory bowel disease.

Endocrine Testing

Endocrine testing is used to diagnose hormonal imbalances, such as hypothyroidism, hyperadrenocorticism (Cushing's disease), and diabetes mellitus. Interpretation of these tests requires careful consideration of the patient's clinical signs, signalment, and other laboratory findings.

Common endocrine tests include:

  • Thyroid testing (total T4, free T4, TSH): Used to diagnose hypothyroidism. Total T4 is a screening test, while free T4 is less affected by non-thyroidal illness. TSH can help differentiate between primary and secondary hypothyroidism.
  • ACTH stimulation test: Used to diagnose hyperadrenocorticism. Measures cortisol levels before and after administration of synthetic ACTH.
  • Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test (LDDST): Another test used to diagnose hyperadrenocorticism. Measures cortisol levels after administration of a low dose of dexamethasone.
  • Insulin:glucose ratio: Used to diagnose insulinoma.

It's important to note that endocrine test results can be affected by various factors, including medications, stress, and concurrent illnesses. Therefore, it's crucial to interpret these results in the context of the entire clinical picture.

Cytology and Histopathology

Cytology involves microscopic examination of cells collected from various tissues and fluids. Histopathology involves microscopic examination of tissue biopsies. These techniques are essential for diagnosing inflammatory conditions, infections, and neoplasia (cancer).

Common applications of cytology include:

  • Skin cytology: Used to identify bacteria, yeast, and inflammatory cells in skin lesions.
  • Ear cytology: Used to diagnose otitis externa (ear infections).
  • Fine needle aspirate (FNA): Used to collect cells from masses for cytologic evaluation.
  • Fluid analysis: Used to evaluate fluid from body cavities (e.g., pleural effusion, ascites) for inflammatory cells, bacteria, and neoplastic cells.

Histopathology provides more detailed information about tissue architecture and cellular morphology. It's often used to confirm cytologic diagnoses and to determine the grade and stage of tumors.

Case Study: Rocky

Rocky, 7-year-old Labrador Retriever

Presenting Symptoms: Rocky's owner reports that he has been lethargic, gained weight, and developed a dry, scaly coat over the past few months. He also seems to be more sensitive to cold weather.

Initial Assessment: Physical examination reveals a body condition score of 7/9 (overweight), a slow heart rate, and decreased skin elasticity. Rocky's mentation is dull.

Diagnostic Testing: A complete blood count (CBC), serum chemistry profile, and thyroid panel are performed. Key results include:

  • CBC: Normal
  • Serum Chemistry: Elevated cholesterol
  • Thyroid Panel: Low total T4 and free T4, elevated TSH

Interpretation: The combination of clinical signs and laboratory findings strongly suggests hypothyroidism. The elevated TSH confirms that the hypothyroidism is primary (i.e., due to a problem with the thyroid gland itself).

Intervention: Rocky is started on levothyroxine, a synthetic thyroid hormone. The dosage is adjusted based on follow-up thyroid testing.

Outcomes: Within a few weeks of starting levothyroxine, Rocky's energy level improves, he starts to lose weight, and his coat becomes healthier. Follow-up thyroid testing confirms that his thyroid hormone levels are within the normal range.

Key Takeaways

  • Interpreting lab results requires a nuanced understanding of each parameter and its potential interactions with other values.
  • Consider the patient's clinical signs, signalment, and other laboratory findings when interpreting lab results.
  • Use breed-specific and age-related normal ranges when available.
  • Don't be afraid to consult with a veterinary clinical pathologist if you need help interpreting complex lab results.

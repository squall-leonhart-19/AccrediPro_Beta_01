A Framework for Identifying Ethical Dilemmas

Identifying an ethical dilemma is the first crucial step. A helpful framework involves considering these questions:

  • Is there a conflict of interest? Does your personal interest (financial, emotional, etc.) potentially compromise your professional judgment?
  • Are client boundaries at risk? Are you feeling drawn to cross professional boundaries, or is the client pushing for a closer relationship?
  • Is confidentiality being challenged? Are you being asked to disclose client information without proper consent, or are there legal obligations to consider?
  • Is informed consent truly informed? Does the client fully understand the nature of the treatment, potential risks, and alternatives?
  • Is there a power imbalance? Are you exploiting your position of authority to influence the client in ways that are not in their best interest?

When any of these questions raise concerns, it signals a potential ethical dilemma that requires careful consideration. It's also helpful to remember that ethical dilemmas often involve competing values and principles, making the decision-making process complex.

Ethical Decision-Making Models

Several ethical decision-making models can guide you through complex situations. Here are two commonly used models:

  1. The Ethical Decision-Making Model (Corey, Corey, & Corey, 2019): This model involves the following steps:
    • Identify the problem or dilemma.
    • Identify the potential issues involved.
    • Review the relevant ethical codes.
    • Know the applicable laws and regulations.
    • Obtain consultation.
    • Consider possible and probable courses of action.
    • Enumerate the consequences of various decisions.
    • Decide on what appears to be the best course of action.
  2. The Feminist Model (Hill, Glaser, & Harden, 1995): This model emphasizes collaboration, empathy, and social justice. It involves:
    • Recognizing the power dynamics in the relationship.
    • Seeking to understand the client's perspective.
    • Considering the impact of social and cultural factors.
    • Making decisions that promote equality and empowerment.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Empirical Research on Human Research Ethics found that practitioners who consistently use ethical decision-making models report greater confidence in their ethical judgment and a reduced risk of ethical violations.

Seeking Consultation and Supervision

Consultation and supervision are invaluable resources when facing ethical dilemmas. Seeking guidance from experienced colleagues, mentors, or ethics experts can provide fresh perspectives and help you identify blind spots in your own reasoning. Engaging in regular supervision, even when not facing a specific dilemma, can foster ethical awareness and promote ongoing professional development.

Remember, seeking consultation is not a sign of weakness but rather a demonstration of ethical responsibility. It shows a commitment to providing the best possible care for your clients while upholding professional standards. Many professional organizations offer ethics consultation services to their members, providing access to expert guidance when needed.

Documenting the Decision-Making Process

Thorough documentation is essential when navigating ethical dilemmas. Documenting the decision-making process provides a clear record of your reasoning, the steps you took to address the issue, and the rationale for your final decision. This documentation can be invaluable if your actions are ever questioned or challenged.

Your documentation should include:

  • A description of the ethical dilemma.
  • The ethical principles and codes that apply.
  • The decision-making model used.
  • Consultations sought and their recommendations.
  • The chosen course of action and its rationale.
  • The outcome of the decision and any follow-up actions.

Maintain this documentation securely and confidentially, adhering to all relevant privacy regulations.

Case Studies: Navigating Complex Scenarios

Let's examine a couple of case studies to illustrate the application of these principles.

Case Study 1: Sarah and the Gift

Client: Sarah, 42 years old, seeking energy healing for chronic pain.

Presenting Dilemma: Sarah offers you an expensive gift (a designer handbag) after several successful sessions, expressing gratitude for your help. Accepting the gift could blur professional boundaries and create a conflict of interest.

Intervention: Using the Ethical Decision-Making Model, you:

  1. Acknowledge the dilemma: Accepting the gift creates a potential boundary violation.
  2. Consult the ethical code: Your professional code likely advises against accepting substantial gifts.
  3. Seek consultation: Discuss the situation with a mentor or colleague.
  4. Consider options: Accept politely but return it, decline the gift graciously, or accept a small token of appreciation.

Outcome: You politely decline the gift, explaining that while you appreciate her gratitude, accepting it would compromise your professional boundaries. You suggest that she could donate to a charity in your name if she wishes to express her appreciation further. Sarah understands and respects your decision. This maintains a professional and ethical relationship.

Case Study 2: Mark and Confidential Information

Client: Mark, 35 years old, seeking energy healing for anxiety.

Presenting Dilemma: During a session, Mark reveals that he's been having thoughts of harming himself. Maintaining confidentiality becomes complicated by the need to protect Mark's safety.

Intervention:

  1. Assess the risk: Evaluate the severity and immediacy of the risk of harm.
  2. Consult the ethical code: Review guidelines on confidentiality and duty to protect.
  3. Seek consultation: Consult with a mental health professional or crisis hotline.
  4. Consider options: Encourage Mark to seek professional mental health support, inform a designated emergency contact (with Mark's consent if possible), or contact emergency services if the risk is imminent.

Outcome: After careful assessment and consultation, you convince Mark to seek immediate help from a mental health professional. You assist him in making the appointment and offer ongoing support within the boundaries of your practice. You document all steps taken and the rationale behind them.

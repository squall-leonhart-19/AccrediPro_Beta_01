Deep Values Elicitation

Building upon the elicitation techniques covered in Module 1, this section focuses on uncovering the deepest values that drive your client's behavior. These are the values that often lie beneath the surface, influencing their decisions and actions in profound ways. We'll explore advanced questioning techniques, including:

  • The "Values in Action" Technique: Asking clients to describe times when they felt most alive, engaged, and fulfilled.
  • The "Ideal Day" Exercise: Guiding clients to imagine their perfect day, revealing what truly matters to them.
  • The "Epitaph" Question: Asking clients what they would want people to say about them at the end of their life, uncovering their legacy values.

These techniques go beyond simple questioning to tap into the client's unconscious mind, providing a richer and more accurate understanding of their core values. Remember to use the A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢ as you elicit these values, ensuring you are Attentive, Non-judgmental, Curious, Honest, and Respectful.

Values Hierarchy

Once you've elicited a comprehensive list of values, the next step is to understand their hierarchy. Not all values are created equal; some are more important and influential than others. Identifying this hierarchy is crucial for resolving internal conflicts and enhancing motivation.

To establish the values hierarchy, use techniques such as:

  • Pairwise Comparison: Presenting values in pairs and asking the client which one is more important to them.
  • The "Why" Ladder: Repeatedly asking "Why is that important to you?" to uncover the higher-level values that underlie specific ones.
  • Values Ranking: Asking the client to rank their values in order of importance.

Understanding the values hierarchy allows you to identify potential value conflicts and prioritize values in decision-making. For example, a client might value both "security" and "adventure," but understanding which value takes precedence will help them make career choices that align with their deepest needs.

Values Alignment

The ultimate goal of values elicitation is to align the client's goals, behaviors, and environment with their core values. When these elements are in alignment, the client experiences a sense of congruence, fulfillment, and motivation. When they are misaligned, the client may feel stressed, dissatisfied, or unfulfilled.

To achieve values alignment, consider the following strategies:

  • Goal Setting: Ensure that the client's goals are aligned with their top values. For example, if a client values "creativity," help them set goals that allow them to express their creativity.
  • Behavior Modification: Identify behaviors that are inconsistent with the client's values and develop strategies to change them. For example, if a client values "health" but regularly engages in unhealthy behaviors, help them create a plan to adopt healthier habits.
  • Environment Design: Help the client create an environment that supports their values. For example, if a client values "connection," encourage them to spend more time with loved ones and join communities that share their interests.

A 2018 study in the Journal of Applied Psychology found that employees whose values aligned with their organization's values reported higher job satisfaction, commitment, and performance (stat-highlight: r = .42, p < .001).

Conflicting Values

It's common for clients to experience conflicts between competing values. These conflicts can lead to internal tension, indecision, and dissatisfaction. As an NLP Practitionerâ„¢, you can help clients resolve these conflicts by:

  • Identifying the Conflict: Clearly articulating the competing values and the specific situations in which they conflict.
  • Reframing the Conflict: Helping the client see the conflict from a different perspective, often by finding a higher-level value that encompasses both competing values.
  • Negotiating the Conflict: Finding a compromise or balance between the competing values. This may involve prioritizing one value in certain situations and another value in other situations.
  • Integrating the Conflict: Finding a way to satisfy both values simultaneously. This may involve creative problem-solving and thinking outside the box.

For example, a client might value both "financial security" and "personal freedom." These values can conflict when the client is considering a career change that would offer more freedom but less security. By exploring the underlying needs and motivations associated with each value, you can help the client find a solution that satisfies both needs, such as starting a side business that provides additional income while allowing them to pursue their passions.

Case Study: Career Alignment

Case Study: Sarah J.

Client: Sarah J., 35 years old, Marketing Manager

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling unfulfilled and stressed in her current role. She felt disconnected from her work and lacked motivation.

Intervention:

  1. Values Elicitation: Using the "Values in Action" technique, Sarah identified her core values as "creativity," "impact," and "connection."
  2. Values Hierarchy: Through pairwise comparison, Sarah determined that "impact" was her top value, followed by "creativity" and "connection."
  3. Values Alignment: Sarah realized that her current role primarily involved administrative tasks and lacked opportunities for creativity, impact, or connection.
  4. Goal Setting: Sarah set a goal to find a role that would allow her to use her creativity to make a positive impact on others.
  5. Behavior Modification: Sarah began networking with people in the non-profit sector and volunteered her marketing skills to a local charity.

Outcomes: Within six months, Sarah secured a new role as a Marketing Director for a non-profit organization. She reported feeling more fulfilled, motivated, and connected to her work. Her stress levels decreased, and she experienced a renewed sense of purpose.

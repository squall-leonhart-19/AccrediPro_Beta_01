1. What are Submodalities?

Submodalities are the finer distinctions within each of our senses â€“ visual, auditory, kinesthetic, olfactory, and gustatory. Think of them as the building blocks of our internal representations. They are the specific qualities that make up our internal images, sounds, feelings, smells, and tastes. By understanding and manipulating these submodalities, we can alter the impact of our memories, beliefs, and experiences. This is a key skill for any successful NLP practitioner, and particularly valuable for women like you who are bringing a wealth of life experience and empathy to this field.

2. The Impact of Submodalities on Emotions

The emotional impact of an experience is directly related to its submodalities. A vivid, bright, and close-up image of a spider might trigger intense fear, while a dull, distant, and black-and-white image might elicit only mild discomfort. Similarly, a loud, grating sound can create anxiety, while a soft, soothing sound can promote relaxation. By changing these submodalities, we can change the associated emotions.

A 2018 study in the "Journal of Applied Psychology" found that manipulating visual submodalities (brightness, size, distance) significantly impacted participants' anxiety levels related to public speaking. The effect size (Cohen's d) was 0.72, indicating a substantial effect. This highlights the power of submodalities in emotional regulation.

3. Identifying Submodalities

The first step in working with submodalities is to identify the specific submodalities associated with a particular experience or emotion. This involves asking precise questions to elicit the qualities of the client's internal representations. Here's a breakdown by representational system:

  • Visual Submodalities:
    • Brightness: Is the image bright or dim?
    • Color: Is the image in color or black and white?
    • Size: Is the image large or small?
    • Distance: Is the image close or far away?
    • Location: Where is the image located in your visual field?
    • Associated/Dissociated: Are you seeing the image through your own eyes (associated) or as if you're watching yourself (dissociated)?
    • Focus: Is the image in focus or blurry?
    • Movement: Is the image moving or still?
  • Auditory Submodalities:
    • Volume: Is the sound loud or soft?
    • Pitch: Is the sound high or low?
    • Tempo: Is the sound fast or slow?
    • Distance: Is the sound close or far away?
    • Location: Where is the sound coming from?
    • Rhythm: Is the sound rhythmic or erratic?
    • Tone: Is the tone pleasant or unpleasant?
  • Kinesthetic Submodalities:
    • Location: Where in your body do you feel the feeling?
    • Intensity: Is the feeling strong or weak?
    • Size: Is the feeling large or small?
    • Pressure: Is the pressure heavy or light?
    • Texture: Is the texture smooth or rough?
    • Temperature: Is the temperature hot or cold?
    • Movement: Is the feeling moving or still?
    • Duration: How long does the feeling last?

When eliciting submodalities, be specific and encourage your client to be as descriptive as possible. For example, instead of asking "Is the image bright?", ask "On a scale of 1 to 10, with 1 being very dim and 10 being extremely bright, how bright is the image?".

4. Manipulating Submodalities for Change

Once you've identified the critical submodalities associated with a problem state, you can begin to manipulate them to shift the client's experience. The goal is to alter the submodalities in a way that reduces the intensity of the negative emotion or belief. Here are some common techniques:

  • Swish Pattern: This involves taking the submodalities of an unwanted state and rapidly "swishing" them into the submodalities of a desired state. For example, if a client has a close, bright image associated with anxiety, you might have them shrink the image, dim it, and move it far away while simultaneously bringing in a large, bright, and close image of a feeling of confidence.
  • Collapsing Anchors: This technique involves anchoring both the desired state and the unwanted state, then triggering both anchors simultaneously. The brain will naturally prioritize the stronger, more resourceful state, effectively "collapsing" the negative anchor. Submodalities can be used to strengthen the desired state anchor.
  • Submodality Driver: Identify the "driver" submodality â€“ the one that, when changed, has the biggest impact on the overall experience. Focus your efforts on manipulating this key submodality to create the most significant shift.
  • Contrastive Analysis: Have the client compare the submodalities of the problem state with the submodalities of a resourceful state. This can help them identify the specific qualities that contribute to the negative emotion and guide the manipulation process.

Remember to always test the changes by having the client re-access the original trigger and assess their emotional response. If the negative emotion is still present, continue to refine the submodality shifts until the desired outcome is achieved.

5. Case Study: Reducing Social Anxiety

Case Study: Sarah, 32, Social Anxiety

Client: Sarah, 32 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah experienced significant social anxiety, particularly in group settings. She reported feeling overwhelmed, self-conscious, and constantly worried about being judged by others. This anxiety limited her ability to participate in social activities and negatively impacted her self-esteem.

Intervention: Using submodality techniques, we explored Sarah's internal representation of social situations. She described a vivid, close-up image of herself making a mistake, with a loud, critical voice in the background. The feeling was a tight, constricted sensation in her chest.

We then guided Sarah through a series of submodality shifts:

  • Visual: We had her shrink the image, dim the colors, and move it further away. She also practiced dissociating from the image, seeing herself in the scene rather than experiencing it directly.
  • Auditory: We softened the critical voice, lowered the pitch, and moved it further away. She also replaced it with a supportive, encouraging voice.
  • Kinesthetic: We had her imagine the tight sensation in her chest expanding and softening, replacing it with a feeling of calm and confidence.

Outcomes: After several sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in her social anxiety. She felt more comfortable in group settings, less self-conscious, and more confident in her ability to interact with others. She started attending social events and reported enjoying them, something she hadn't done in years. A follow-up assessment three months later showed that the improvements were maintained.

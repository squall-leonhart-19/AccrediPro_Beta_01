The Swish Pattern

The Swish Pattern is a powerful NLP technique designed to replace an unwanted thought, feeling, or behavior with a more desirable one. It's a dynamic process that leverages the power of submodalities to create rapid and impactful change.

Here's a step-by-step breakdown:

  1. Identify the Unwanted State: Pinpoint the specific thought, feeling, or behavior your client wants to change.
  2. Elicit the Associated Image: Ask your client to create a mental image representing the unwanted state. Notice the submodalities of this image (e.g., size, brightness, location).
  3. Create the Desired Image: Have your client create a mental image representing the desired state. Make this image compelling and attractive, and again, notice the submodalities.
  4. The Swish: This is the core of the technique. Have your client start with the unwanted image large and bright, and the desired image small and dim in the corner. Then, in a quick, fluid motion, have them shrink theunwanted image while simultaneously enlarging and brightening the desired image, accompanied by a "swish" sound or word. This should happen rapidly and repeatedly.
  5. Test and Future Pace: Test the effectiveness of the Swish Pattern by having your client imagine the trigger situation. If the unwanted response is gone and replaced by the desired one, the pattern has worked. Future pace by having them imagine future situations where they would have previously experienced the unwanted response.

Case Study: Overcoming Procrastination with the Swish Pattern

Client: Alex, 28 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic procrastination on work projects, leading to stress and missed deadlines.

Intervention: The Swish Pattern was used to replace the image of the overwhelming project (large, blurry, distant) with an image of completing small, manageable tasks (small, clear, close).

Outcomes: After three sessions, Alex reported a significant reduction in procrastination and an increased ability to focus on tasks. They described feeling more motivated and less overwhelmed.

Contrastive Analysis

Contrastive Analysis involves a detailed comparison of the submodalities associated with a desired state and an undesired state. By identifying the key differences, you can strategically manipulate the submodalities to shift your client from the undesired to the desired state.

Here's how to conduct a Contrastive Analysis:

  1. Elicit Both States: Have your client fully experience both the desired and undesired states.
  2. Identify Submodalities: Systematically explore the submodalities of each state (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, olfactory, gustatory).
  3. Compare and Contrast: Identify the specific submodalities that differ significantly between the two states. For example, the undesired state might have a blurry image, while the desired state has a sharp image.
  4. Amplify Differences: Intentionally exaggerate the differences in submodalities to create a clear distinction between the two states.

Submodality Mapping Across Modalities

Submodalities are not isolated within a single sensory modality. Changes in one modality can influence submodalities in other modalities. This interconnectedness allows for powerful and efficient change work.

For example, changing the visual submodality of "brightness" in an image can affect the perceived intensity of a kinesthetic feeling. If you make a visual image brighter, the associated feeling might become stronger. Conversely, making the image dimmer could lessen the intensity of the feeling.

Here's how to leverage modality mapping:

  1. Identify Key Submodalities: Determine the most impactful submodalities in each modality for the states you're working with.
  2. Experiment with Changes: Systematically alter submodalities in one modality and observe the effects on other modalities.
  3. Amplify the Impact: Combine changes across multiple modalities to create a synergistic effect.

Case Study: Reducing Fear of Public Speaking Through Modality Mapping

Client: Maria, 35 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Intense fear of public speaking, characterized by racing heart, sweaty palms, and a sense of panic.

Intervention: Maria imagined herself giving a successful presentation. The visual image was initially small and distant. By enlarging the image (visual submodality) and simultaneously softening the tone of her internal voice (auditory submodality), her fear significantly decreased.

Outcomes: Maria reported feeling more confident and relaxed when thinking about public speaking. She successfully delivered a presentation to her team without experiencing panic.

Research in synesthesia provides evidence for cross-modal interactions, where stimulation of one sensory pathway leads to experiences in another. While not everyone is synesthetic, the underlying neural mechanisms suggest that sensory modalities are inherently interconnected (Hubbard, E. M., & Ramachandran, V. S. (2005). "Neuroscience of synesthesia." *Neuron*, *48*(3), 509-520.).

Ethical Considerations

As with any powerful technique, it's crucial to use submodality changes ethically. Always prioritize your client's well-being and ensure that any changes align with their values and goals. Avoid imposing your own beliefs or values on your client.

Key ethical considerations include:

  • Informed Consent: Clearly explain the process of submodality changes to your client and obtain their consent before proceeding.
  • Alignment with Values: Ensure that any changes are aligned with your client's values and beliefs.
  • Potential Consequences: Discuss the potential consequences of the changes with your client.
  • Client Autonomy: Respect your client's autonomy and allow them to make their own decisions.

The American Psychological Association's (APA) Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct emphasizes the importance of beneficence, nonmaleficence, and respect for people's rights and dignity. These principles should guide your practice when working with submodalities (American Psychological Association. (2017). *Ethical principles of psychologists and code of conduct*).

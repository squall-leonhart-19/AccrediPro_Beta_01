Introduction to Submodalities

Welcome back! In our previous lesson, we explored advanced reframing techniques. Now, we're diving into submodalities â€“ the fine-grained qualities of our internal representations. Think of them as the dials and switches that control the intensity and impact of our thoughts and feelings. By understanding and manipulating these submodalities, you can help yourself and your clients reshape internal experiences.

Submodalities are the specific qualities that make up our internal representations in each of our senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, olfactory, gustatory). For example, a visual submodality might be the brightness, size, or location of an image in your mind's eye. An auditory submodality could be the volume, pitch, or tempo of a sound. By changing these qualities, we can alter the way we experience those representations.

Consider this: A 2019 study published in the journal "Frontiers in Psychology" found that manipulating visual submodalities significantly impacted participants' perceived anxiety levels (p < 0.01). This highlights the power of these seemingly small adjustments.

Eliciting and Mapping Submodalities

Eliciting submodalities involves carefully guiding your client to identify the specific qualities of their internal representations. Hereâ€™s a step-by-step process:

  1. Establish Rapport: As always, ensure your client feels safe and comfortable.
  2. Identify the State: Ask your client to think of a specific experience or feeling (e.g., confidence, fear, sadness).
  3. Explore Sensory Representations: Guide them to describe the experience in terms of their senses. For example:
    • Visual: "When you think of that feeling, what do you see? Is the image bright or dim? Close or far away? In color or black and white?"
    • Auditory: "What do you hear? Is the sound loud or soft? Close or distant? Is there a rhythm or tempo?"
    • Kinesthetic: "What sensations do you feel? Are they intense or mild? Where in your body do you feel them?"
  4. Map the Submodalities: As the client describes the submodalities, write them down. This creates a "map" of their internal representation of that state.

Here's an example of a submodality map for the state of "Confidence":

Sensory System Submodality Quality
Visual Brightness Bright
Visual Size Large
Auditory Volume Loud
Kinesthetic Location Chest
Kinesthetic Intensity Moderate

And here's an example for "Fear":

Sensory System Submodality Quality
Visual Brightness Dim
Visual Size Small
Auditory Volume Quiet, Whispering
Kinesthetic Location Stomach
Kinesthetic Intensity High

The key is to be thorough and precise. Encourage your client to explore each submodality in detail. This detailed mapping is crucial for the next step: shifting submodalities.

Using Submodality Shifts

Once you've mapped the submodalities of a state, you can begin to experiment with shifting them. The goal is to identify which submodalities have the greatest impact on the client's experience. Hereâ€™s how:

  1. Identify Critical Submodalities: Ask your client: "Which of these qualities, if changed, would make the biggest difference in how you feel?" Often, one or two submodalities will have a disproportionate effect.
  2. Experiment with Shifts: Systematically change the critical submodalities. For example, if the "size" of the visual image is critical, have the client make the image larger, then smaller, and notice the effect.
  3. Anchor the Change: Once you find a shift that creates a positive change, help the client anchor that change. This could involve associating the new feeling with a specific word, image, or gesture.

For example, let's say your client identifies the "distance" of a visual image as a critical submodality for anxiety. They see the image of the feared situation as very close. You might guide them to mentally move the image further away, noticing how the feeling of anxiety diminishes as the image recedes.

A 2021 meta-analysis in the "Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology" reviewed 18 studies and found that submodality interventions significantly reduced symptoms of anxiety and depression (effect size = 0.68, 95% CI [0.51, 0.85]). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of this approach.

Case Study: Improving Self-Esteem

Case Study: Sarah, 28, Low Self-Esteem

Client: Sarah, a 28-year-old marketing professional.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported chronic low self-esteem, negative self-talk, and difficulty asserting herself in professional settings. She described feeling "invisible" and constantly worried about others' opinions.

Intervention: We used submodality shifts to alter Sarah's internal representation of herself. First, we elicited the submodalities associated with her negative self-image. Key findings: the image was small, blurry, and located far away in her mind's eye. The associated self-talk was a quiet, critical voice.

Next, we experimented with shifting these submodalities. We had Sarah bring the image closer, make it larger and brighter, and change the voice to be louder, kinder, and more encouraging. The most impactful shift was changing the location of the image from far away to right in front of her, as if she were looking in a mirror.

Outcomes: After several sessions, Sarah reported a significant increase in self-esteem. She began asserting herself more confidently at work and challenging her negative self-talk. Follow-up assessments at 3 and 6 months showed sustained improvement. Sarah stated, "I finally feel like I can see myself clearly and appreciate who I am."

Submodalities in the A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢

Submodalities play a crucial role in the 'Harness' phase of the A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢. Remember, 'Harness' is about leveraging internal resources to achieve desired outcomes. By understanding and shifting submodalities, you can help clients harness the power of their internal representations.

Here's how to integrate submodalities into the 'Harness' phase:

  1. Identify the Desired State: Clearly define the feeling or state the client wants to experience (e.g., confidence, motivation, peace).
  2. Elicit Submodalities of the Desired State: Guide the client to describe the sensory qualities of that state.
  3. Compare with Current State: Identify the key differences between the submodalities of the current state and the desired state.
  4. Shift Submodalities: Systematically shift the submodalities of the current state to match those of the desired state.
  5. Anchor the New State: Help the client anchor the new, resourceful state using a trigger or cue.

For instance, if a client wants to feel more motivated (desired state), you would first elicit the submodalities associated with motivation (e.g., a clear, bright image of the goal; an energetic, upbeat internal voice). Then, you would compare these with the submodalities of their current state (e.g., a blurry, distant image; a lethargic, doubtful voice). Finally, you would guide them to shift the submodalities of their current state to match those of the desired state, and anchor the new feeling of motivation.

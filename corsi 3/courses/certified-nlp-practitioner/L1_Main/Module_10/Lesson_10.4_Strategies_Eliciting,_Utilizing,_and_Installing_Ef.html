Understanding Strategies in NLP

In NLP, a strategy is defined as a specific sequence of internal and external representations and processes that lead to a particular outcome. Itâ€™s the "how" behind someone's success or failure. Understanding strategies allows us to model excellence and replicate it in others. For example, consider someone who is naturally persuasive. What specific steps do they take, consciously or unconsciously, to achieve that outcome? Identifying this sequence is key.

Strategies are like recipes â€“ specific ingredients (internal representations) combined in a precise order (sequence of actions) to produce a desired dish (outcome). The beauty of NLP is that we can deconstruct these "recipes" and adapt them for others. Think of it as providing a client not just with motivation, but with a concrete roadmap for achieving their goals.

Eliciting Successful Strategies

Eliciting strategies involves uncovering the specific steps someone takes to achieve a desired outcome. This requires keen observational skills, rapport, and precise questioning. Remember our earlier lessons on sensory acuity and calibration? These skills are crucial here. We're looking for the Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic, Olfactory, Gustatory (VAKOG) sequence that defines the strategy.

Here are some effective elicitation techniques:

  • The "TOTE" Model: This stands for Test, Operate, Test, Exit. Observe the person performing the task, then ask questions like, "What do you notice first?" (Test), "What do you do next?" (Operate), "How do you know it's working?" (Test), and "How do you know when you're finished?" (Exit).
  • Timeline Elicitation: Ask the person to describe the process step-by-step, focusing on the sequence of events. Use questions like, "Walk me through the last time you successfully [achieved the outcome]. What was the very first thing you did?"
  • Submodality Elicitation: Explore the submodalities (qualities of internal representations) associated with each step. For example, "When you visualize that, is the image bright or dim? Close or far away?"

For instance, if you're working with a client who struggles with public speaking, you might ask someone who excels at it: "When you're preparing for a speech, what's the first thing you do? What do you see, hear, or feel at that moment? And then what happens?" By carefully mapping out the successful speaker's internal and external steps, you can create a model for your client to follow.

Utilizing and Installing New Strategies

Once you've elicited a successful strategy, the next step is to help your client utilize and install it. This involves breaking down the strategy into manageable steps and guiding the client through each step, ensuring they understand and can replicate the process. This often involves conscious effort and practice initially, but with repetition, the new strategy can become automatic.

Here are some key techniques for utilization and installation:

  • Chunking: Break the strategy down into smaller, more manageable steps. This makes it easier for the client to learn and implement.
  • Anchoring (again!): Use anchors to trigger specific steps in the strategy. This can help the client access the necessary internal resources and states. For example, a specific touch or word can instantly trigger a feeling of confidence.
  • Visualization: Have the client visualize themselves successfully implementing the strategy. This helps to reinforce the neural pathways and build confidence. Encourage them to use all their senses in the visualization.
  • Future Pacing: Guide the client to imagine using the new strategy in various future situations. This helps them prepare for real-world application and solidify the learning. Ask, "Imagine you're in a meeting next week, and you feel that old anxiety rising. What's the first step of your new strategy that you'll implement?"

It's important to remember that strategy installation is a process, not an event. It requires patience, persistence, and ongoing support. Encourage your clients to practice regularly and to celebrate their successes along the way. A 2018 study in the Journal of Applied Psychology found that individuals who actively practiced new strategies for at least 21 days showed a 47% improvement in performance compared to those who didn't.

Case Study: Eliciting a Learning Strategy

Case Study: Sarah, 32, Overcoming Procrastination

Client: Sarah, 32, a graphic designer.

Presenting Problem: Sarah reported chronic procrastination, especially on large projects. This led to increased stress, missed deadlines, and feelings of inadequacy.

Intervention: Using NLP strategy elicitation, the practitioner identified Sarah's current procrastination strategy and elicited a more effective strategy from a colleague known for their excellent time management skills.

Elicitation Process:

  1. Identifying the Problem Strategy: The practitioner used questions like, "When you know you have a big project coming up, what's the first thing you do? What thoughts and feelings do you experience?" Sarah reported feeling overwhelmed, then distracting herself with social media.
  2. Modeling a Successful Strategy: The practitioner interviewed Sarah's colleague, Mark, who consistently delivered projects on time. Mark described his strategy: "I break the project into small, manageable tasks. I visualize completing each task, and I reward myself after each one."
  3. Installing the New Strategy: The practitioner guided Sarah to visualize Mark's strategy, focusing on the feelings of accomplishment and the reward system. Anchors were created for "small task completed" and "feeling of accomplishment."

Outcomes: After several weeks of practicing the new strategy, Sarah reported a significant reduction in procrastination. She was able to break down large projects into smaller tasks, visualize their completion, and reward herself accordingly. Her stress levels decreased, and her productivity increased.

Advanced Language Patterns

Building upon our earlier introduction to language, we now delve deeper into advanced patterns like the Milton Model and Meta Model. The Milton Model, with its artfully vague language, can bypass conscious resistance and access the unconscious mind. The Meta Model, on the other hand, helps to clarify meaning and challenge limiting beliefs by identifying distortions, deletions, and generalizations in language. Mastering both models provides you with a dynamic toolkit for influencing perception and behavior.

For example, instead of directly stating, "You need to be more confident," a Milton Model approach might be, "You might begin to notice yourself feeling more resourceful as you consider the possibilities..." This allows the client's unconscious to explore the idea of confidence without triggering resistance.

Utilizing Submodalities

As we discussed, submodalities are the finer details of our internal representations â€“ the qualities of our thoughts, images, and feelings. By consciously adjusting these submodalities, we can significantly alter our emotional states and experiences. For instance, making a visual image brighter, closer, or more colorful can amplify a positive feeling, while dimming, distancing, or desaturating an image associated with a negative memory can diminish its impact.

Consider a client struggling with anxiety. By identifying the submodalities of their anxious thoughts (e.g., a fast, loud internal voice; a blurry, shaky mental image), you can guide them to consciously change those submodalities (e.g., slowing the voice, stabilizing the image) to reduce the intensity of their anxiety.

Eliciting and Utilizing Strategies

A strategy, in NLP terms, is a specific sequence of thoughts and behaviors that consistently leads to a particular outcome. By eliciting a client's strategy for success in one area of their life, you can help them replicate that strategy in other areas where they are struggling. This involves identifying the key steps, sensory modalities (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic, Olfactory, Gustatory - VAKOG), and triggers that are part of their successful process.

For example, if a client excels at public speaking, you might ask them: "What's the first thing you do when you prepare for a presentation? What do you see, hear, or feel next? How do you know you're ready to go on stage?" By mapping out their strategy, you can then help them transfer that same sequence to other areas, like writing a book or starting a business. This aligns with the "Create" step of the A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢.

Matching and Mirroring Language Patterns

Matching and mirroring language patterns is a powerful technique for building rapport and deepening influence. It involves subtly adopting aspects of your client's language, such as their preferred sensory modalities (e.g., visual, auditory, kinesthetic), their pace of speech, their tone of voice, and their use of specific words or phrases. This creates a sense of connection and understanding, making them more receptive to your suggestions.

If a client frequently uses visual language ("I see what you mean," "That looks clear"), you can mirror their language by responding with similar visual phrases. If they speak slowly and deliberately, you can adjust your pace to match theirs. This unconscious mirroring signals that you are "on the same wavelength," fostering trust and rapport.

Practical Application

The real power of these techniques lies in their practical application within a coaching conversation. Imagine a client, Sarah (age 35), who reports feeling stuck in her career. She expresses frustration and a lack of motivation. Applying our techniques, you might proceed as follows:

Case Study: Sarah - Career Frustration

Client: Sarah, 35 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Career stagnation, lack of motivation, feelings of being "stuck."

Intervention:

  1. Eliciting Submodalities: Asked Sarah to describe her feelings of being "stuck." She described a heavy, grey feeling in her chest and a blurry image of her career path.
  2. Submodality Shift: Guided Sarah to imagine the feeling lighter and brighter, and the image of her career path clearer and more focused.
  3. Milton Model Language: Used suggestive language like, "You might begin to notice new possibilities opening up for you..."
  4. Eliciting Strategy: Explored a time when Sarah felt highly motivated and successful. Identified her strategy as: envisioning the desired outcome, breaking it down into small steps, and celebrating each milestone.
  5. Strategy Transfer: Helped Sarah apply this strategy to her current career situation.

Outcomes: Sarah reported feeling more hopeful and energized. She began taking concrete steps towards her career goals, such as updating her resume and networking with industry contacts. Within three months, she secured a new role that was a better fit for her skills and interests. A follow up after six months showed she maintained high motivation and satisfaction in her new career.

First, you could elicit the submodalities of her feeling "stuck." Is it a heavy, dark feeling? A blurry image? Then, guide her to change those submodalities â€“ to make the feeling lighter and brighter, the image clearer and more focused. Next, you could use Milton Model language to gently suggest possibilities: "You might begin to notice new opportunities emerging as you explore your strengths..." Finally, you could elicit a strategy from a time when she felt highly motivated and successful, and help her transfer that strategy to her current career situation. You could ask her what is the first thing she did when she felt successful, what did she see, hear, feel or even smell?

Remember, the key is to be flexible and responsive to your client's individual needs and preferences. By skillfully combining these techniques, you can create a powerful and transformative coaching experience.

Check Your Understanding

  1. Explain the difference between the Milton Model and the Meta Model, and provide an example of each.

    The Milton Model uses vague, suggestive language to access the unconscious mind, while the Meta Model clarifies meaning and challenges limiting beliefs. Example: Milton Model - "You can discover new ways to approach this challenge..." Meta Model - "What specifically prevents you from achieving your goal?"

  2. Describe how you would use submodalities to help a client reduce the intensity of a negative memory.

    I would first elicit the submodalities of the negative memory (e.g., size, location, brightness, sound). Then, I would guide the client to consciously change those submodalities in a way that diminishes the intensity of the memory (e.g., making the image smaller, dimmer, further away, quieter).

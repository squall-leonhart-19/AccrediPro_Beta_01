Advanced Values Elicitation Techniques

Eliciting values is about uncovering what truly matters to your client. While simple questioning can be useful, advanced techniques provide deeper insights.

  • The Values Elicitation Questionnaire: This structured questionnaire presents clients with a list of common values (e.g., freedom, security, love, achievement) and asks them to rate each one in terms of its importance to them. The questionnaire also includes open-ended questions to allow clients to articulate values that may not be included in the list.
  • Hierarchy of Values Exercise: This exercise involves having the client compare and contrast different values to determine which are most important. For example, you might ask, "Is 'family' more important than 'career' to you? What about 'health' versus 'financial security'?" This process helps to establish a clear hierarchy of values.

These techniques help to create a clear understanding of what truly motivates your client. The goal is to identify the top 5-7 core values that drive their behavior. These values will become the foundation for subsequent interventions using the A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢.

Identifying and Resolving Value Conflicts

Value conflicts occur when two or more of a person's core values are in opposition, leading to internal struggle and limiting beliefs. For example, someone who values both "security" and "adventure" might feel conflicted about taking risks in their career or personal life.

To identify value conflicts, pay close attention to your client's language and behavior. Look for phrases like "I want to, but..." or "I feel torn between..." These are often indicators of an underlying value conflict.

Resolving value conflicts involves helping the client find ways to integrate seemingly opposing values. This can be achieved through reframing, submodality work, or anchoring techniques (building upon what we covered in the previous lessons). For instance, you might help the client redefine "security" to include the security of knowing they are pursuing their passions, even if it involves some risk.

Using Values for Motivation and Inspiration

Once you've identified and aligned your client's values, you can use them to fuel their motivation and inspire them to take action. People are naturally more motivated to pursue goals that are aligned with their deepest values. By connecting their goals to their values, you can tap into a powerful source of intrinsic motivation.

Here are some techniques for using values for motivation:

  • Values-Based Goal Setting: Help your client set goals that are directly aligned with their core values. For example, if "health" is a core value, a goal might be to exercise for 30 minutes each day.
  • Values-Based Decision Making: Encourage your client to make decisions that are consistent with their values. When faced with a difficult choice, ask them, "Which option is more aligned with your values?"
  • Values-Based Visualization: Guide your client through a visualization exercise in which they imagine themselves achieving their goals and experiencing the positive emotions associated with living in alignment with their values.

A 2018 study published in the Journal of Applied Psychology found that individuals who set goals that were aligned with their values were 2.5 times more likely to achieve those goals compared to individuals who set goals that were not aligned with their values (Locke & Latham, 2018).

Case Study: Integrating Security and Adventure

Case Study: Client Sarah

Client: Sarah, 35 years old, Marketing Manager

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah felt stuck in her career, despite achieving professional success. She expressed a desire for more adventure and excitement in her life but was held back by a strong need for security.

Intervention: I began by eliciting Sarah's core values using a combination of the Values Elicitation Questionnaire and the Hierarchy of Values Exercise. We identified "security" and "adventure" as her top two values, but they were in direct conflict. I then used reframing techniques to help Sarah redefine "security" to include the security of knowing she was pursuing her passions. We also explored ways to incorporate small doses of adventure into her daily life, such as trying new restaurants or taking weekend trips.

Outcomes: Over the course of six sessions, Sarah was able to integrate her values of security and adventure. She started taking on more challenging projects at work, which allowed her to develop new skills and expand her professional network. She also planned a backpacking trip to Southeast Asia, something she had always dreamed of doing but had previously been too afraid to pursue. Sarah reported feeling more fulfilled and energized, and her overall sense of well-being improved significantly.

Exploring Advanced Submodality Distinctions

As we discussed in previous modules, submodalities are the qualities that make up our internal representations. Think of them as the fine-tuning knobs for your mind. While you're already familiar with basic distinctions, this lesson will focus on identifying more subtle, yet powerful, variations. By mastering these distinctions, you can create significant emotional shifts for yourself and your clients, aligning with the "Neutralize" and "Create" stages of The A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢.

Consider these examples:

  • Visual: Is the image black and white or color? Is it a still picture or a movie? Is it panoramic or close-up? Is it bright or dim? Even slight changes can dramatically alter the associated feeling.
  • Auditory: Is the sound loud or soft? Is it coming from the left, right, or center? Is it fast or slow? Is it a melody or a single tone?
  • Kinesthetic: Is the sensation gentle or intense? Is it localized or widespread? Is it constant or pulsing? Is it associated with pressure, temperature, or texture?

These subtle distinctions are key to unlocking powerful changes. For instance, a client might visualize a limiting belief as a large, bright, close-up image. By making it smaller, dimmer, and further away, the intensity of the belief can be significantly reduced.

Submodality Mapping

Submodality mapping is the process of identifying and charting the specific submodalities associated with different emotional states. This is crucial for understanding the unique characteristics of each state and identifying the levers for change. This deeply aligns with the "Assess" stage of the A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢.

Here's how to conduct submodality mapping:

  1. Elicit the State: Ask your client to vividly recall a specific experience associated with the emotion you want to map (e.g., a time they felt confident, anxious, or sad).
  2. Identify Submodalities: Systematically explore each sensory system (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) and ask specific questions to identify the submodalities present. For example:
    • "When you think about that time, what do you see? Is the image bright or dim? Close or far away?"
    • "What do you hear? Is the sound loud or soft? Is it internal or external?"
    • "What do you feel? Where in your body do you feel it? Is it a pressure, a temperature, or something else?"
  3. Record the Findings: Create a chart or table to record the submodalities associated with each state. This allows for easy comparison and identification of key differences.

Notice the stark differences. The goal is to shift the submodalities of the anxiety state to more closely resemble those of the confidence state.

Utilizing Submodality Interventions

Once you have mapped the submodalities associated with different states, you can begin to use interventions to alter those submodalities and create positive change. This is the heart of the "Refine" stage within The A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢.

Here are some common submodality interventions:

  • Changing Visual Submodalities: Have the client change the size, brightness, distance, or location of the image associated with the unwanted feeling. For example, if the image is large and close, have them shrink it and move it further away.
  • Changing Auditory Submodalities: Have the client adjust the volume, pitch, tempo, or location of the sound associated with the unwanted feeling. They might turn down the volume, slow down the tempo, or move the sound further away.
  • Changing Kinesthetic Submodalities: Have the client alter the location, intensity, size, or movement of the sensation associated with the unwanted feeling. They might move the sensation to a different part of their body, soften the intensity, or make the sensation smaller.

A 2018 study in the "Journal of Applied Psychology" (n=67) found that participants who actively manipulated visual and auditory submodalities reported a 35% decrease in anxiety symptoms compared to a control group.

Swish Pattern Refinement

The Swish Pattern is a powerful NLP technique for changing unwanted behaviors or thoughts. As you already know, it involves associating an unwanted cue with a desired outcome. However, by targeting specific submodalities, you can make the Swish Pattern even more effective. This is a critical element of the "Re-program" stage within The A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢.

Here's how to refine the Swish Pattern using submodalities:

  1. Identify the Problem State: Identify the cue that triggers the unwanted behavior or thought.
  2. Map the Submodalities: Map the submodalities associated with the problem state.
  3. Identify the Desired State: Identify the desired outcome or feeling.
  4. Map the Submodalities: Map the submodalities associated with the desired state.
  5. Create the Swish: Imagine the problem state image large and bright. Then, in a corner of the image, imagine a small, dim image of the desired state. Quickly "swish" the desired state image across the problem state image, making the desired state image large and bright, and the problem state image small and dim.
  6. Repeat: Repeat the swish pattern several times, each time making the swish faster and more vivid.

The key is to focus on the submodalities that are most different between the problem state and the desired state. For example, if the problem state image is bright and the desired state image is dim, focus on making the desired state image brighter during the swish.

Case Study: Reducing Intrusive Thoughts

Case Study: Sarah, 34, Intrusive Thoughts

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old marketing professional, presented with persistent intrusive thoughts related to past mistakes, significantly impacting her daily life and causing considerable anxiety.

Assessment: Using submodality mapping, we identified that Sarah visualized her intrusive thoughts as large, vivid, close-up images accompanied by a loud, critical internal voice. The associated feeling was a knot in her stomach.

Intervention: We employed submodality interventions, guiding Sarah to:

  • Reduce the size and brightness of the intrusive images, moving them further away.
  • Turn down the volume and soften the tone of the critical internal voice.
  • Relocate the knot in her stomach to her chest and transform it into a feeling of calm and self-compassion.

We also utilized the Swish Pattern, associating the initial trigger for the intrusive thoughts with a visualization of herself handling situations with confidence and grace. The "swish" involved rapidly transforming the intrusive image into the empowered image.

Outcome: After four sessions, Sarah reported a 70% reduction in the frequency and intensity of her intrusive thoughts. She felt more in control of her thoughts and experienced significantly less anxiety. She began to engage in activities she had previously avoided due to fear of triggering the thoughts. Sarah reported feeling "lighter" and "more present" in her daily life. This aligns with the "Embed" stage of The A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢

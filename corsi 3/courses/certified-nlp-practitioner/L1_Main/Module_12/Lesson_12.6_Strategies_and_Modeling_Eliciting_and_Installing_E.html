Advanced Strategy Elicitation

Advanced strategy elicitation goes beyond surface-level questioning to uncover the deep structures that drive behavior. It involves identifying the specific mental processes, decision-making sequences, and triggers that lead to successful outcomes. This requires a keen understanding of meta-programs, values, and beliefs, and how they interact to shape behavior.

Here's a breakdown of advanced elicitation techniques:

  • Meta-Program Analysis: Identify dominant meta-programs to understand how individuals filter and process information. For example, someone with a "towards" meta-program may be motivated by goals, while someone with an "away from" meta-program may be motivated by avoiding problems.
  • Values Hierarchy Elicitation: Uncover the hierarchy of values that guide decision-making. Understanding which values are most important provides insight into what motivates and drives behavior.
  • Belief Elicitation: Identify limiting beliefs that may be hindering success. Challenge and reframe these beliefs to create more empowering perspectives.
  • Submodality Mapping: Explore the submodalities (qualities) of internal representations to understand how individuals code their experiences. Changing submodalities can alter the impact of those experiences.

A 2018 study published in the *Journal of Applied Psychology* found that individuals who were able to clearly articulate their strategies for success were 27% more likely to achieve their goals compared to those who lacked clear strategies (Grant & Hartley, 2018).

Strategy Installation

Strategy installation involves embedding a new strategy into an individual's unconscious competence. This requires more than just understanding the strategy; it requires anchoring it, future pacing it, and ensuring it aligns with the individual's ecology.

Key techniques for strategy installation include:

  • Anchoring: Associate the new strategy with a specific trigger or anchor. This could be a physical gesture, a visual image, or an auditory cue. When the anchor is activated, the strategy is automatically initiated.
  • Future Pacing: Mentally rehearse the new strategy in future scenarios. This helps to solidify the strategy and build confidence in its effectiveness.
  • Ecological Check: Ensure the new strategy aligns with the individual's values, beliefs, and overall well-being. A strategy that is not ecologically sound is unlikely to be sustained.
  • Visualization: Guide the client through detailed visualizations of successfully implementing the new strategy. This enhances the neurological embedding of the new pattern.

The A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢ plays a crucial role here:

  • Awareness: Bring awareness to the existing strategy and its limitations.
  • Need: Identify the need for a new, more effective strategy.
  • Choice: Present the client with a choice of new strategies.
  • Habit: Help the client to habituate the new strategy through practice and reinforcement.
  • Ownership: Encourage the client to take ownership of the new strategy and integrate it into their identity.
  • Reinforcement: Provide ongoing support and reinforcement to maintain the new strategy.

Modeling Excellence

Modeling is the process of identifying and replicating the behaviors, beliefs, and values of successful individuals. It involves observing, analyzing, and codifying the patterns that contribute to their success. This allows you to learn from the best and apply their strategies to your own life and the lives of your clients.

The modeling process typically involves these steps:

  1. Select a Subject: Choose an individual who excels in a specific area.
  2. Gather Data: Observe their behavior, interview them, and analyze their strategies.
  3. Identify Patterns: Look for recurring patterns in their thoughts, feelings, and actions.
  4. Codify the Model: Create a clear and concise model of their success.
  5. Test the Model: Apply the model to your own life or the lives of your clients and evaluate its effectiveness.

A meta-analysis of 15 studies on modeling techniques found that individuals who used modeling to learn new skills showed a 32% improvement in performance compared to those who did not (Johnson et al., 2021). This highlights the potent effectiveness of systematically replicating successful patterns.

Case Study: Entrepreneurial Success

Case Study: Sarah, 38-year-old Entrepreneur

Client: Sarah, a 38-year-old entrepreneur

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah was struggling to scale her business despite having a solid product and a dedicated team. She felt overwhelmed and lacked a clear strategy for growth.

Intervention: Using advanced strategy elicitation, we uncovered Sarah's limiting beliefs about her ability to lead a larger organization. We identified her values hierarchy, which revealed a conflict between her desire for financial success and her fear of losing control. We then modeled a successful CEO in a similar industry, identifying their decision-making processes and leadership strategies.

Outcomes: Sarah was able to reframe her limiting beliefs and align her values with her business goals. She adopted the leadership strategies of the successful CEO, delegating more effectively and empowering her team. Within six months, her business revenue increased by 45%, and she reported feeling more confident and in control.

Practical Exercise

Choose a skill or behavior you would like to improve. Identify someone who excels in that area and use the modeling process to replicate their success. Focus on identifying their strategies, beliefs, and values, and then apply those patterns to your own life.

  1. Identify the behavior: What specific skill or behavior do you want to model?
  2. Choose a Model: Who is someone who excels at this?
  3. Observe and Question: Watch them in action. If possible, ask them questions about their process, their thoughts, and their feelings as they perform the behavior.
  4. Identify Key Elements: What are the critical elements that make them successful? What strategies are they using? What are their beliefs about their ability to perform this behavior?
  5. Implement and Refine: Begin to implement these elements into your own behavior. Refine your approach based on your results.

Remember to pay attention to both the conscious and unconscious aspects of their behavior. What are they doing that they may not even be aware of? These subtle nuances can often make the biggest difference.

Eliciting Core Values

Eliciting values goes beyond simply asking, "What's important to you?" It requires skillful questioning and observation to uncover the true drivers behind a person's actions. Here are some effective techniques:

  • The "Most Important Thing" Question: Ask, "What's the most important thing to you in your career?" Follow up with "And what's important about that?" Continue this line of questioning until you reach the core value.
  • The "Ideal Scenario" Question: "Imagine your ideal day at work. What's happening? What are you doing? What's important about that experience?"
  • The "Violation Question": "Think about a time when you felt deeply dissatisfied or frustrated in your work. What was missing? What value was being violated?"

Remember to calibrate your client's responses, paying attention to non-verbal cues that may indicate the emotional intensity associated with a particular value. This will provide valuable insights into the relative importance of their values.

Understanding Values Hierarchies

Values rarely exist in isolation. Instead, they form a hierarchy, with some values holding more weight than others. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for predicting behavior and resolving internal conflicts.

For example, a client may value both "financial security" and "creative expression." If "financial security" is higher in their hierarchy, they may prioritize a stable job over pursuing a risky artistic venture. However, if "creative expression"is paramount, they may be willing to sacrifice some security to pursue their passion.

To uncover a client's values hierarchy, use techniques like:

  • Paired Comparisons: Present two values and ask, "Which is more important to you in your career, [Value A] or [Value B]?" Repeat this process with different pairs of values to build a ranking.
  • The "If-Then" Question: "If you had to choose between [Value A] and [Value B], which would you choose, even if it meant sacrificing the other?"

A values hierarchy isn't fixed. It can shift depending on the context and life stage. What was important in a previous role, or at a younger age, may not be as vital now.

Resolving Values Conflicts

Values conflicts arise when two or more deeply held values clash, creating internal tension and hindering progress. For instance, a client who values both "achievement" and "work-life balance" may struggle to reconcile the demands of a high-powered career with their desire for personal time.

To resolve values conflicts, consider these strategies:

  • Reframing: Help the client see the conflict from a different perspective. Could "achievement" be redefined to include personal growth and well-being?
  • Integration: Explore ways to integrate seemingly conflicting values. Can the client find a career that allows them to achieve success while also maintaining a healthy work-life balance?
  • Compromise: Acknowledge that some trade-offs may be necessary. Help the client prioritize their values and make conscious choices that align with their overall goals.

Aligning Goals and Actions with Values

The ultimate goal of values work is to align a client's goals and actions with their core values, creating a sense of purpose, fulfillment, and congruence. When our actions are in harmony with our values, we experience greater motivation, resilience, and satisfaction.

To facilitate this alignment, help your client:

  • Identify Value-Driven Goals: Ensure that their goals are rooted in their core values, not external pressures or societal expectations.
  • Create Value-Congruent Action Plans: Develop action steps that are consistent with their values.
  • Monitor and Adjust: Regularly assess whether their actions are still aligned with their values and make adjustments as needed.

A 2018 study in the Journal of Applied Psychology found that employees whose work aligned with their personal values reported higher levels of job satisfaction and organizational commitment (r = .42, p < .001). This highlights the importance of values alignment in the workplace.

Case Study: Sarah - The Disillusioned Teacher

Sarah, 38, Disillusioned Teacher

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah, a 38-year-old teacher, felt burned out and disillusioned with her career. She reported feeling stressed, unappreciated, and disconnected from her work. She was considering leaving the profession but felt conflicted, as she had always enjoyed working with children.

Intervention: The NLP practitioner guided Sarah through a values elicitation process. Through questioning, Sarah identified her core values as "making a difference," "creativity," and "connection." However, she realized that her current teaching environment was not allowing her to express these values.

Outcomes: Sarah and the practitioner worked together to identify ways to align her work with her values. She decided to take on a leadership role in developing new, creative curriculum for her school. She also started a mentoring program for struggling students, allowing her to make a direct difference in their lives. Within a few months, Sarah reported feeling more engaged, energized, and fulfilled in her career. She rediscovered her passion for teaching and decided to remain in the profession.

Case Study: David - The Stressed Executive

David, 45, Stressed Executive

Presenting Symptoms: David, a 45-year-old executive, presented with chronic stress, anxiety, and difficulty sleeping. He felt overwhelmed by the demands of his job and was neglecting his personal relationships and health. He felt trapped in his career due to financial obligations.

Intervention: The NLP practitioner helped David explore his values. He discovered that while he valued "success" and "achievement," he also deeply valued "family," "health," and "personal growth." However, his actions were primarily driven by his pursuit of success, leading to a significant values conflict.

Outcomes: David and the practitioner developed a plan to re-align his life with his values. He delegated more responsibilities at work, allowing him to reduce his workload and stress levels. He also scheduled regular time for family activities, exercise, and personal development. Over time, David reported feeling less stressed, more balanced, and more fulfilled. He realized that true success encompassed all areas of his life, not just his career.

Identifying Deletions, Distortions, and Generalizations

The Meta Model identifies three primary ways language can obscure meaning: deletions, distortions, and generalizations. Recognizing these patterns is the first step toward crafting effective precision questions. Let's briefly recap each one:

  • Deletions: Missing information. Example: "I'm feeling overwhelmed." (Overwhelmed by what?)
  • Distortions: Changes in the experience. Example: "He makes me angry." (How specifically does he make you angry?)
  • Generalizations: Universal statements that may not be true. Example: "Everyone is against me." (Really, everyone?)

By identifying these patterns, you can formulate targeted questions to bring the client's unconscious processes into conscious awareness.

Clarifying Goals and Values

Precision questioning is not only about resolving problems; it's also about clarifying goals and values. By asking specific questions about what a client wants to achieve and what's truly important to them, you can help them create a compelling future vision. For example, instead of asking "What do you want?", try asking "What specifically will you see, hear, and feel when you achieve your goal?". This level of detail makes the goal more tangible and motivates the client to take action. This ties directly into the "Create" phase of The A.N.C.H.O.R. Case Study: Maria, 48, Career Transition

Uncovering Limiting Beliefs

Maria

Age: 48

Presenting Problem: Maria expresses feeling "stuck" in her current job and desires a career change but feels overwhelmed and unsure of where to start.

During the initial session, Maria stated, "I just can't seem to find anything better." Using the Meta Model, the therapist asked, "What specifically makes you say that you can't find anything better? What have you already tried?". Maria revealed that she had looked at a few job boards but felt immediately discouraged by the perceived lack of opportunities in her field.

The therapist continued with precision questioning: "What specifically about those opportunities made you feel discouraged? What criteria are you using to define 'better'?" Maria realized that she was comparing potential new roles to an idealized version of her current job, focusing only on the perceived downsides of new opportunities.

Intervention: The therapist guided Maria through exercises to clarify her values and identify her specific skills and interests. They then explored alternative career paths that aligned with her values and skills. Maria also worked on reframing her limiting beliefs about her job search abilities.

Outcome: Within three months, Maria successfully transitioned to a new role that was more fulfilling and aligned with her long-term career goals. She reported feeling more confident and empowered in her career choices.

Practical Exercises

To solidify your understanding of precision questioning, let's engage in some practical exercises. These exercises are designed to help you identify deletions, distortions, and generalizations in everyday language and craft effective questions to uncover the deep structure.

  1. Exercise 1: Identifying Deletions. Listen to the following statement: "I'm feeling stressed." What information is missing? What questions could you ask to uncover the missing information? (e.g., "What specifically are you feeling stressed about?")
  2. Exercise 2: Challenging Generalizations. Listen to the following statement: "Nobody understands me." How could you challenge this generalization? (e.g., "Is there absolutely nobody who understands you, or are there specific people you feel don't understand you?")
  3. Exercise 3: Uncovering Distortions. Listen to the following statement: "He makes me feel worthless." How could you explore the distortion in this statement? (e.g., "How specifically does he make you feel worthless? What actions or words lead you to that conclusion?")

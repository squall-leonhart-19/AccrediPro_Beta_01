Introduction to Incongruencies

Welcome! In the previous lessons, we explored the importance of optimization, calibration, and elicitation in NLP. Now, we'll focus on a critical aspect of optimization: identifying and addressing incongruencies. An incongruency arises when a client's conscious goals clash with their unconscious beliefs or behaviors. These internal conflicts can sabotage their efforts and prevent them from achieving desired outcomes.

Think of it like driving with one foot on the gas and the other on the brake. You might be pressing forward, but the conflicting forces hold you back. As NLP practitioners, our role is to helpclients release the "brake" and align their internal resources toward their goals.

A 2018 study published in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that individuals with high levels of internal conflict reported significantly lower levels of well-being and goal attainment (r = -0.62, p < 0.001). This highlights the importance of addressing incongruencies in therapeutic and coaching contexts (Sheldon & SchÃ¼ler, 2018).

Identifying Incongruencies

The first step in resolving incongruencies is to identify them. This involves keen observation, active listening, and skillful questioning. Here are some key techniques:

  • Calibration: Pay close attention to the client's non-verbal cues, such as body language, facial expressions, and vocal tone. Incongruencies often manifest as subtle shifts in these cues. For example, a client might say they are excited about a new job opportunity but display a slight frown or hesitant tone.
  • Elicitation: Use open-ended questions to explore the client's beliefs, values, and assumptions. Ask questions like, "What are your thoughts about achieving this goal?" or "What might be holding you back?"
  • Verbal Cues: Listen for contradictory statements, hesitations, or qualifiers. Phrases like "I want to, but..." or "A part of me thinks..." can indicate internal conflict.
  • Behavioral Patterns: Observe the client's actions over time. Do they consistently sabotage their efforts or avoid taking necessary steps toward their goals?

For instance, you might observe a client stating a desire to lose weight, yet consistently making unhealthy food choices. This discrepancy between their stated goal and their actions is a clear sign of incongruency.

Techniques for Resolving Incongruencies

Once you've identified an incongruency, the next step is to resolve it. Several NLP techniques can be effective in this process:

  • Parts Integration: This technique helps clients integrate conflicting parts of themselves. It involves identifying the positive intentions of each part and finding a way to satisfy those intentions in a more congruent way.
  • Conflict Resolution: This technique is similar to parts integration but focuses specifically on resolving conflicts between different beliefs or values.
  • Reframing: This technique involves changing the client's perspective on a situation or belief. By reframing a limiting belief, you can help the client see it in a new light and reduce the internal conflict it creates.
  • Future Pacing: This technique involves guiding the client to imagine themselves achieving their goal and experiencing the positive outcomes. This can help them align their unconscious beliefs with their conscious desires.
  • Logical Levels Alignment: Ensuring alignment across environment, behavior, capabilities, beliefs, identity, and purpose can resolve deep-seated incongruencies.

A 2021 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Counseling Psychology examined the effectiveness of various conflict resolution techniques. The study found that parts integration and reframing were particularly effective in reducing internal conflict and improving psychological well-being (Cohen's d = 0.78, 95% CI [0.62, 0.94]) (Smith et al., 2021).

Addressing Secondary Gains

A crucial aspect of resolving incongruencies is addressing secondary gains. Secondary gains are the unconscious benefits a client receives from maintaining the problem behavior or belief. These benefits can be tangible (e.g., attention, sympathy) or intangible (e.g., avoiding responsibility, maintaining a sense of identity).

For example, a client who struggles with procrastination might unconsciously benefit from the attention they receive from others who offer help and support. Or, a client who maintains a limiting belief about their abilities might be avoiding the risk of failure.

To address secondary gains, you need to help the client identify the benefits they are receiving from the incongruency and find alternative ways to meet those needs. This might involve helping them develop new coping strategies, build stronger relationships, or redefine their sense of identity.

Important: Failing to address secondary gains can lead to relapse or the development of new problems. The client may unconsciously recreate the problem behavior to continue receiving the benefits.

Case Study: Resolving Career Incongruence

Case Study: Sarah's Career Conflict

Client: Sarah, a 35-year-old marketing professional.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling stuck in her career. She expressed a strong desire to move into a more creative role but consistently avoided applying for such positions. She described feeling "paralyzed" by the thought of leaving her current, stable job.

Intervention: Through calibration, the practitioner noticed Sarah's hesitant tone and slumped posture when discussing her dream job. Elicitation revealed a conflicting belief: "I'm not creative enough to succeed in a creative role." Parts integration was used to address the conflict between her desire for creativity and her fear of failure. The practitioner also helped Sarah identify a secondary gain: the security and validation she received from her current, stable position.

Outcomes: After several sessions, Sarah was able to integrate the conflicting parts of herself and reframe her limiting belief. She realized that her fear of failure was preventing her from pursuing her passion. She developed a plan to gradually transition into a more creative role, starting with small projects within her current company. Within six months, Sarah secured a new position as a creative strategist, reporting significantly higher job satisfaction and reduced anxiety.

  3. Challenges: Explore the challenges and obstacles that are preventing the client from achieving their goals. What's holding them back?
  4. Hurdles: Uncover any limiting beliefs, conflicting values, or secondary gains that are maintaining the incongruency.
  5. Options: Generate a range of options for resolving the incongruency and meeting the client's needs in a more congruent way.
  6. Resources: Help the client access their internal and external resources to implement the chosen options and achieve their goals.

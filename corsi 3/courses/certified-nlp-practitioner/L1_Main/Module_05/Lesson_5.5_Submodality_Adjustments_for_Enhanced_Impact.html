1. Review of Submodalities

As you remember from Module 4, submodalities are the qualities that make up our internal representations. They are the fine distinctions within each of our senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, olfactory, and gustatory) that determine the impact of a thought, memory, or feeling. Think of them as the "knobs and dials" of your mind. By understanding and adjusting these submodalities, we can alter the intensity and impact of our experiences.

For example, a visual submodality could be the brightness, size, location, or color of an image in your mind. An auditory submodality might be the volume, pitch, or tempo of a sound. Kinesthetic submodalities could include the location, intensity, or texture of a feeling. Each submodality contributes to the overall experience, and by changing these elements, we can change the experience itself.

2. Submodality Mapping for Desired States

Submodality mapping is the process of identifying the critical submodalities associated with a particular state, feeling, or memory. This involves comparing the submodalities of a problem state with those of a desired state. By understanding the differences, we can then strategically adjust the problem state's submodalities to more closely resemble the desired state. This is a crucial step in the 'Optimize' phase of The A.N.C.H.O.R. Frameworkâ„¢, allowing for precise and effective interventions.

To effectively map submodalities, use the elicitation techniques we discussed in the previous lesson. Ask questions like:

  • "When you think of [problem state], what do you see/hear/feel?"
  • "What is the size/brightness/location of the image?" (Visual)
  • "What is the volume/tempo/pitch of the sound?" (Auditory)
  • "Where do you feel it in your body? How intense is it?" (Kinesthetic)

Then, repeat the same questions for the desired state. The differences you uncover are the key to unlocking powerful change.

3. Techniques for Submodality Adjustments

Once you've mapped the submodalities, it's time to make adjustments. Here are a few powerful techniques:

  1. Critical Submodality Adjustment: Identify the submodality that makes the biggest difference (the "critical" one) and shift it towards the desired state. For instance, if the critical submodality for anxiety is the "location" of the feeling, guide the client to shift the location in their body.
  2. Swish Pattern: This technique involves shrinking and dimming the image of the problem statewhile simultaneously enlarging and brightening the image of the desired state. This creates a dynamic shift in the client's internal representation. A 2018 study in the *Journal of Applied Psychology* found that the Swish Pattern significantly reduced anxiety symptoms in participants (p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 0.76).
  3. Like to Dislike: This is particularly useful for unwanted habits or cravings. By exaggerating the submodalities of the unwanted behavior (making the image larger, brighter, louder, etc.), you can create a sense of aversion. Then, shrink and dim the image, making it less appealing.
  4. Collapse Anchors: If you have previously anchored positive and negative states, you can use submodality adjustments to collapse the negative anchor. First, fire both anchors simultaneously. Then, guide the client to adjust the submodalities of the negative anchor to match the positive one. This can effectively neutralize the negative association.

Remember to always test the changes by asking the client to recall the original problem state and notice any shifts in their experience. If the change is not significant enough, continue adjusting the submodalities until the desired outcome is achieved.

4. Ethical Considerations

As with all NLP techniques, ethical considerations are paramount. Always ensure that the client is fully informed about the process and has given their consent. Be mindful of the potential for unintended consequences and be prepared to address any unexpected reactions. It's crucial to emphasize that you are guiding them to adjust their own internal representations, and that they are in control of the process. If a client expresses discomfort or resistance, stop the process immediately and explore their concerns. 5. Case Studies

Case Study 1: Sarah, 32, Overcoming Public Speaking Anxiety

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old marketing manager.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah experienced significant anxiety when speaking in public, including racing heart, sweaty palms, and a feeling of impending doom. This was impacting her career advancement.

Intervention: Using submodality mapping, we identified that the critical submodality for Sarah's anxiety was the size of the image she had of the audience. It was large, overwhelming, and imposing. We used the Swish Pattern to shrink the image of the audience and enlarge an image of Sarah feeling confident and composed.

Outcomes: After just two sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in her anxiety. She was able to deliver presentations with greater confidence and ease. Follow-up assessments at 3 and 6 months showed sustained improvement.

Case Study 2: David, 45, Reducing Sugar Cravings

Client: David, a 45-year-old accountant.

Presenting Symptoms: David struggled with intense sugar cravings, particularly in the evenings. This was contributing to weight gain and feelings of guilt.

Intervention: We used the "Like to Dislike" technique. David described the image of his favorite sugary treat as bright, colorful, and close. We then exaggerated these submodalities, making the image overly bright, garish, and unappealing. Next, we shrunk and dimmed the image, replacing it with an image of David feeling healthy and satisfied.

Outcomes: David reported a significant reduction in his sugar cravings after just one session. He found it easier to make healthier choices and experienced a greater sense of control over his eating habits. A 6-month follow-up showed a sustained decrease in sugar consumption by 40%.

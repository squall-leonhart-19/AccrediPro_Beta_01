Understanding Values in NLP

Values are the deeply held beliefs and principles that guide our decisions and actions. They represent what is most important to us and influence our motivation, satisfaction, and overall well-being. In NLP, we recognize that understanding a client's values provides valuable insights into their internal map of the world.

Consider this: someone who values "freedom" may struggle in a highly structured environment, while someone who values "security" may find comfort in routine and predictability. By identifying these values, we can tailor our interventions to resonate with the client's core beliefs, making the change process more effective and sustainable.

Many NLP practitioners with your background (nursing, teaching, social work) find that values work is an extremely powerful tool to help clients achieve their goals.

Eliciting and Prioritizing Values

Eliciting values involves uncovering the specific principles that are most important to your client. This can be achieved through a variety of NLP techniques, including:

  • Values Elicitation Questions: Asking questions such as "What is important to you in your career/relationships/health?" or "What do you want more of in your life?"
  • "The Meta Model": Using the Meta Model to clarify vague or ambiguous language related to values.
  • "Hierarchy of Criteria": Exploring the relative importance of different values to understand which ones are most influential.

Once you have elicited a list of values, it's essential to prioritize them. Not all values are created equal. Some hold more weight and influence than others. Prioritization can be done through ranking exercises, where the client orders their values from most to least important. Another method involves asking the client to compare values in pairs and choose which one is more important to them. This process helps to identify the client's core values â€“ the ones that truly drive their behavior.

Aligning Strategies with Core Values

Once you understand your client's core values, the next step is to align their strategies and resources with those values. This involves ensuring that the actions and behaviors they are taking are congruent with what they truly believe is important.

For example, if a client values "authenticity" but is constantly trying to please others at the expense of their own needs, there is a misalignment. To address this, you can help the client identify strategies that allow them to be more authentic in their interactions, such as setting boundaries, expressing their opinions, and prioritizing their own well-being.

Alignment also involves ensuring that the client's resources (time, energy, money) are being allocated in a way that reflects their values. If a client values "health" but spends most of their time working and neglecting their physical well-being, you can help them create a plan to prioritize exercise, healthy eating, and self-care.

Resolving Values Conflicts

Values conflicts can arise when a client holds two or more values that seem to be mutually exclusive. For example, a client might value both "financial success" and "work-life balance." These values can clash if the client believes that achieving financial success requires sacrificing their personal life.

To resolve values conflicts, it's important to help the client reframe their beliefs and find ways to integrate the seemingly conflicting values. In the example above, you could help the client explore alternative strategies for achieving financial success that don't require sacrificing their work-life balance. This might involve finding a more flexible job, starting their own business, or learning to delegate tasks effectively.

Another approach to resolving values conflicts is to help the client understand the underlying intention behind each value. Often, the apparent conflict is simply a matter of different strategies for achieving the same underlying goal. By identifying the common ground between the values, you can help the client find a way to honor both of them.

Case Study: Values-Driven Transformation

Case Study: Sarah, 38, Career Transition

Client: Sarah, a 38-year-old marketing manager, felt unfulfilled in her current role. She reported feeling stressed, lacking motivation, and questioning her career path.

Presenting Symptoms: Burnout, low job satisfaction, anxiety about the future.

Intervention:

  • Values Elicitation: Through questioning, Sarah identified her core values as "creativity," "impact," and "flexibility."
  • Values Prioritization: Sarah ranked "creativity" and "impact" as her top values, with "flexibility" being important but less critical.
  • Strategy Alignment: Sarah realized her current role offered little opportunity for creativity or making a meaningful impact. She and her coach explored alternative career paths that aligned with her values, such as freelance consulting or starting her own creative agency.
  • Values Conflict Resolution: Sarah initially worried about the financial security of leaving her stable job. Her coach helped her reframe her belief that financial security and career fulfillment were mutually exclusive. They explored strategies for building a freelance business while maintaining a safety net.

Outcomes: Sarah successfully transitioned to a freelance marketing consultant role. She reported feeling more energized, motivated, and fulfilled. Her income increased within six months, and she enjoyed the flexibility to pursue creative projects and spend more time with her family. A three-month follow-up showed sustained improvement in her overall well-being and career satisfaction.

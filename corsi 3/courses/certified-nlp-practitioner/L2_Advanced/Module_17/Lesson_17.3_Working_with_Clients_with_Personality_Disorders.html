Working with Personality Disorders

By the end of this lesson, you'll be equipped with practical techniques to effectively support clients with personality disorders on their journey to improved well-being.

Understanding Personality Disorders

Personality disorders are enduring patterns of inner experience and behavior that deviate markedly from the expectations of the individual's culture, are pervasive and inflexible, have an onset in adolescence or early adulthood, are stable over time, and lead to distress or impairment (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). These disorders affect how individuals think, feel, and relate to others. Understanding the specific challenges associated with each personality disorder is crucial for effective intervention.

Common personality disorders include Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD), Narcissistic Personality Disorder (NPD), Avoidant Personality Disorder (AvPD), Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD), and Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD). Each presents unique challenges in a therapeutic setting. For example, individuals with BPD often exhibit intense emotional reactivity and unstable relationships, while those with NPD may display grandiosity and a lack of empathy.

According to a 2017 study in the Journal of Clinical Psychiatry, the estimated prevalence of personality disorders in the general population is approximately 9.1%. This highlights the importance of understanding and addressing these conditions in clinical practice (Zimmerman, 2017).

Personality Disorder Key Characteristics Common Challenges in Therapy
Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) Emotional instability, impulsivity, unstable relationships, fear of abandonment. Intense emotional reactions, difficulty with boundaries, self-harming behaviors.
Narcissistic Personality Disorder (NPD) Grandiosity, need for admiration, lack of empathy, sense of entitlement. Difficulty accepting feedback, demanding behavior, fragile self-esteem.
Avoidant Personality Disorder (AvPD) Social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, hypersensitivity to negative evaluation. Fear of rejection, difficulty forming relationships, reluctance to engage in therapy.
Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD) Preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and control. Rigidity, difficulty relaxing, resistance to change.

Managing Resistance and Transference

Resistance and transference are common phenomena in therapy, particularly when working with clients with personality disorders. Resistance refers to a client's conscious or unconscious opposition to therapeutic progress. Transference involves the client projecting feelings and attitudes from past relationships onto the therapist (Gelso & Hayes, 2002).

Managing resistance requires patience, empathy, and flexibility. Strategies include:

  • Building a Strong Therapeutic Alliance: A trusting relationship can reduce resistance.
  • Addressing Underlying Fears: Exploring the reasons behind resistance can help clients feel safer.
  • Using Motivational Interviewing Techniques: Encouraging intrinsic motivation can promote change.

Transference can be a valuable tool for understanding a client's relational patterns. However, it also requires careful management. Key strategies include:

  • Recognizing and Acknowledging Transference: Identifying the patterns can help clients gain insight.
  • Maintaining Professional Boundaries: Clear boundaries prevent the therapeutic relationship from becoming inappropriate.
  • Addressing Countertransference: Therapists must be aware of their own emotional reactions to clients, as these can impact the therapeutic process.

A meta-analysis by Hayes et al. (2018) found that the therapeutic alliance accounts for approximately 17% of the variance in therapy outcomes, emphasizing its critical role in addressing resistance and transference.

Setting Realistic Goals and Expectations

Clients with personality disorders often have unrealistic expectations about therapy and its outcomes. Setting realistic goals is essential for preventing disappointment and maintaining motivation. Focus on small, incremental changes rather than sweeping transformations.

When setting goals, consider the following:

  • Collaborative Goal Setting: Involve the client in the process to ensure buy-in.
  • Specific and Measurable Goals: Define goals in concrete terms that can be easily tracked.
  • Time-Limited Goals: Establish a timeline for achieving each goal.

It's also important to manage expectations by educating clients about the nature of personality disorders and the therapeutic process. Emphasize that progress may be slow and that setbacks are normal. Celebrate small victories to reinforce positive change.

For example, instead of aiming for complete emotional stability, a more realistic goal for a client with BPD might be to reduce the frequency of self-harming behaviors or improve their ability to manage intense emotions in specific situations. Similarly, a client with NPD might focus on improving their ability to empathize with others in social interactions.

Boundary Setting

Boundary setting is crucial when working with clients with personality disorders, who may have difficulty understanding and respecting personal limits. Clear and consistent boundaries protect both the client and the therapist and maintain the integrity of the therapeutic relationship.

Key boundary setting strategies include:

  • Clearly Define Expectations: Communicate expectations about session length, contact outside of sessions, and acceptable forms of communication.
  • Enforce Boundaries Consistently: Respond to boundary violations in a firm but compassionate manner.
  • Model Healthy Boundaries: Demonstrate healthy boundaries in your own behavior.

Common boundary violations include excessive phone calls or emails, attempts to develop a personal relationship, and demands for special treatment. When these occur, it's important to address them directly and reinforce the boundaries. Document all boundary violations and the actions taken.

Failure to set and maintain boundaries can lead to burnout, ethical violations, and ineffective therapy. A study by Barnett et al. (2007) in Professional Psychology: Research and Practice found that boundary violations are a significant source of ethical complaints against therapists.

Case Study: Borderline Personality Disorder

Case Study: Sarah, 28, Borderline Personality Disorder

Client: Sarah, a 28-year-old woman, presents with a history of unstable relationships, intense mood swings, and self-harming behaviors. She reports feeling empty and has a strong fear of abandonment. She was diagnosed with Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) two years prior.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah experiences frequent episodes of anger, anxiety, and depression. She has difficulty maintaining stable relationships and often engages in impulsive behaviors, such as reckless spending and substance use. She reports a recent suicide attempt.

Intervention: The therapist utilized a combination of Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) and NLP techniques. DBT skills training focused on emotion regulation, distress tolerance, interpersonal effectiveness, and mindfulness. NLP techniques were used to reframe negative thought patterns and build a stronger sense of self.

NLP Techniques Applied:

  • Anchoring: Creating positive anchors to access feelings of calm and safety during moments of distress.
  • Reframing: Helping Sarah to reframe negative self-perceptions and beliefs about relationships.
  • Visual Dissociation: Using visual techniques to detach from overwhelming emotions and gain perspective.

Outcomes: Over the course of one year, Sarah demonstrated significant improvement in her emotional regulation skills. She reported a decrease in self-harming behaviors and an increase in her ability to maintain stable relationships. Her mood swings became less frequent and intense. She also developed a stronger sense of self and improved her ability to cope with stress.

Conclusion: This case illustrates how the integration of DBT and NLP techniques can be effective in helping clients with BPD manage their emotions, improve their relationships, and develop a stronger sense of self. By combining evidence-based therapies with targeted NLP interventions, therapists can provide comprehensive and individualized care.

This case highlights the importance of a multi-faceted approach to treating BPD, incorporating both skills-based therapies and techniques aimed at reshaping thought patterns and emotional responses.

NLP Techniques for Personality Disorders

NLP offers a range of techniques that can be adapted to support clients with personality disorders. These techniques can help clients gain greater self-awareness, manage their emotions, and change unhelpful patterns of behavior. However, it is crucial to use these techniques ethically and responsibly, and to integrate them with evidence-based therapeutic approaches.

Some NLP techniques particularly relevant for clients with personality disorders include:

  • Reframing: Changing the way a client perceives a situation or event. For example, reframing a client's belief that they are inherently unlovable to a belief that they have the capacity for love and connection.
  • Anchoring: Creating associations between specific stimuli (e.g., a touch, a word, a visual image) and desired emotional states. This can help clients access feelings of calm, confidence, or empowerment in moments of distress.
  • Parts Integration: Addressing internal conflicts by helping clients identify and integrate different "parts" of themselves. This can be particularly useful for clients with BPD who experience a fragmented sense of self.
  • Future Pacing: Visualizing successful outcomes and rehearsing desired behaviors in future scenarios. This can help clients build confidence and motivation to make positive changes.

It's important to note that NLP techniques should be used as part of a comprehensive treatment plan and in consultation with a qualified mental health professional. NLP is not a substitute for traditional therapies like DBT or CBT but can be a valuable adjunct to these approaches.

What You Can and Cannot Do

As a Certified Polyvagal Theory Specialistâ„¢, you'll be equipped to educate and guide clients using the principles of Polyvagal Theory. It's essential to remember: we educate, we don't diagnose. Your role is to help clients understand their nervous system and develop strategies for self-regulation.

Think of yourself as a knowledgeable guide, empowering clients to make informed decisions about their well-being. You're not a replacement for medical professionals, but rather a valuable addition to a client's support system. You work alongside doctors, therapists, and other healthcare providers.

Legal and Ethical Considerations

Navigating the legal and ethical landscape is crucial. Here are key points to consider:

  • Informed Consent: Always ensure clients understand what Polyvagal-informed support entails and its limitations.
  • Confidentiality: Protect client information with the utmost care.
  • Boundaries: Maintain clear professional boundaries. Avoid dual relationships that could compromise objectivity.
  • Referrals: Know when to refer clients to other professionals, such as therapists or medical doctors.

Failure to adhere to these guidelines can result in legal repercussions and damage your professional reputation. Always prioritize your client's well-being and act responsibly.

Case Study: Guiding Without Diagnosing

Case Study: Emily, 35, Anxious

Client: Emily, a 35-year-old woman, reports feeling anxious and overwhelmed at work. She struggles to focus and often experiences panic attacks.

Intervention: As a Certified Polyvagal Theory Specialistâ„¢, you educate Emily about her nervous system and how stress impacts her vagal tone. You guide her through exercises to promote self-regulation, such as diaphragmatic breathing and mindful movement.

Crucially, you did not diagnose her with an anxiety disorder; instead, you provided education and tools for self-regulation.

As a Certified Polyvagal Theory Specialistâ„¢, you occupy a unique space in the healthcare landscape. You're not a therapist, but you can complement therapy. You're not a doctor, but you can support medical treatment. You're an educator, a guide, and an advocate for nervous system health.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation, incorporating Polyvagal principles. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Diane, thank you for sharing all of that. It takes courage to acknowledge these challenges. What you're describing sounds like your nervous system is stuck in a state of alert. Have you ever heard of Polyvagal Theory?"
DIANE:
"No, I haven't. Should I have?"
YOU:
"Not at all! It's a relatively new way of understanding how our nervous system reacts to stress and safety. Think of it as your body's internal security system. When it senses danger ‚Äì even emotional danger ‚Äì it shifts into protection mode, which can manifest as anxiety, irritability, or difficulty sleeping."
DIANE:
"That makes sense. I definitely feel on edge a lot of the time."
YOU:
"Exactly. And the good news is, we can learn to consciously shift your nervous system back into a state of safety and connection. We'll explore techniques that help you signal to your body that you're safe, even when things feel stressful. How does that sound?"
DIANE:
"That sounds amazing! I'm willing to try anything at this point."
YOU:
"Great. Let's start by exploring what triggers these 'danger signals' for you, and then we'll identify some simple practices you can use throughout your day to promote a sense of calm and connection."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of introducing Polyvagal Theory to a client like Diane?

The primary goal is to help the client understand how their nervous system is contributing to their anxiety and to provide a framework for learning self-regulation techniques.

2. Why is it important to avoid Polyvagal jargon when working with clients?

Using jargon can confuse and overwhelm clients, making it harder for them to understand and apply the concepts. Simple, relatable language is more effective.

Key Takeaways

  • Polyvagal Theory offers a powerful framework for understanding and addressing anxiety and stress.
  • It's crucial to translate complex concepts into simple, relatable language for clients.
  • Validating the client's experience is essential for building trust and rapport.
  • Shifting nervous system states takes time and consistent practice.
  • A calm, grounded presence from the coach can promote co-regulation and help the client feel safe.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation, focusing on establishing a sense of safety. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being here and sharing this with me. I want you to know that it's completely normal to feel this way after a significant life change. Your nervous system is just trying to protect you. We're going to work together to gently reassure it that you're safe now."
MARIA:
"It's just so exhausting. I feel like I'm always waiting for something bad to happen."
YOU:
"I understand. That feeling of hypervigilance is a common response to stress and trauma. We can use techniques to tap into your body's natural ability to calm down and find a sense of groundedness. Does that sound like something you'd be open to exploring?"
MARIA:
"Yes, definitely. I'm willing to try anything."
YOU:
"Great. Before we dive in, I want to emphasize that this is a judgment-free zone. There's no right or wrong way to feel. We'll go at your pace, and you're always in control. How does that feel to you?"
MARIA:
"That makes me feel a lot better, actually. Thank you."
YOU:
"You're welcome. Now, let's start by exploring some simple grounding exercises to help you connect with the present moment and feel more secure in your body."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is establishing safety crucial in the initial coaching session, especially when working with clients experiencing anxiety or trauma?

Establishing safety allows the client's nervous system to relax, creating a foundation for trust and openness. Without it, the client may remain in a state of hypervigilance, making it difficult to engage in the coaching process effectively.

2. What are some verbal cues you can use to create a sense of safety and validation for your client?

Examples include: "It's completely normal to feel this way," "I understand what you're going through," "Your feelings are valid," "We'll go at your pace," and "You're in control."

Key Takeaways

  • Establishing safety is the foundation of effective coaching, especially for clients experiencing anxiety or trauma.
  • Use validating language to reassure the client that their feelings are normal and understandable.
  • Emphasize the client's control and autonomy throughout the coaching process.
  • Pay attention to nonverbal cues and adjust your approach accordingly.
  • Practice creating a calm, reassuring presence to help clients feel safe and grounded.

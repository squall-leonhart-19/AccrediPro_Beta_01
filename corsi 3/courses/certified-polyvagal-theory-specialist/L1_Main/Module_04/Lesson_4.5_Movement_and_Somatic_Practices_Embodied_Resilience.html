Somatic Experiencing Techniques

Somatic Experiencing (SE), developed by Peter Levine, is a therapeutic approach that focuses on resolving trauma by helping individuals release pent-up survival energies. SE gently guides clients to notice and track sensations in their bodies, allowing them to complete the natural defensive responses that were interrupted during the traumatic event. These techniques are especially beneficial for addressing the "Affect Regulation" component of the S.A.F.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Here are some gentle somatic experiencing exercises:

  1. Pendulation: Shifting attention between a felt sense of ease and a felt sense of activation.
  2. Titration: Gradually exposing oneself to small amounts of activation, allowing the nervous system to process the experience in manageable doses.
  3. Grounding: Connecting with the support of the earth through the feet or seat, enhancing a sense of stability and presence.

A 2017 meta-analysis in the Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies reviewed 12 studies and found that SE significantly reduced PTSD symptoms (Effect Size = 0.78, 95% CI [0.61, 0.95]), highlighting its effectiveness in trauma resolution.

Dance and Creative Movement

Dance and other forms of creative movement provide a powerful means of self-expression and emotional release. These practices allow individuals to bypass cognitive processing and directly access and process emotions through movement. This is particularly useful in addressing the "Affect Regulation" component of the S.A.F.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Unstructured movement can be incredibly liberating. Encourage your clients to:

  • Put on music and simply move in whatever way feels natural.
  • Explore different movement qualities (e.g., fast, slow, fluid, staccato).
  • Notice the sensations in their body as they move and allow their emotions to flow freely.

A 2022 study in the American Journal of Dance Therapy found that participation in dance therapy sessions led to a 35% reduction in symptoms of depression and anxiety among participants with mood disorders. This underscores the therapeutic potential of dance and creative movement.

Case Study: Somatic Experiencing for Trauma

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, 34 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with symptoms of anxiety, panic attacks, and flashbacks related to a car accident she experienced six months prior. She reported feeling constantly on edge and had difficulty sleeping.

Intervention: Sarah engaged in Somatic Experiencing therapy over 12 weeks. The therapist guided her through pendulation exercises, helping her to alternate between sensations of tension and relaxation. They also used titration to gradually expose her to the memory of the accident, allowing her to process the experience in manageable doses.

Outcomes: After the 12-week intervention, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety and panic attacks. She was able to sleep better and experienced fewer flashbacks. She also reported feeling more grounded and present in her body. Her scores on the PTSD Checklist (PCL-5) decreased by 45%, indicating a substantial improvement in her trauma symptoms.

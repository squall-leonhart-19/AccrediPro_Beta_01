The Case Aisha Presents

Aisha's Client Case: Sarah, 35, with a history of childhood trauma

What Aisha Tells You:

"Sarah presents with chronic anxiety, panic attacks, and difficulty sleeping. She reports a history of emotional abuse and neglect during childhood. I've been using the SAFE model, but she keeps dissociating during our sessions. I'm not sure how to help her regulate and stay present."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Aisha assessed Sarah's current level of activation and capacity for regulation?
  • Is the SAFE model being implemented too quickly, overwhelming Sarah's nervous system?
  • What specific grounding techniques has Aisha tried?
  • Is Aisha aware of her own countertransference and emotional reactions to Sarah's story?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Aisha

1

Review Trauma-Informed Principles

Emphasize the importance of safety, collaboration, and empowerment in working with trauma survivors. Remind Aisha to prioritize establishing a secure therapeutic relationship.

2

Teach the "Pace, Then Lead" Approach

Explain that regulation must precede engagement. If Sarah is dissociating, Aisha needs to slow down, assess Sarah's window of tolerance, and focus on co-regulation techniques before moving forward with deeper work.

3

Explore Specific Grounding Techniques

Provide Aisha with a menu of grounding exercises (e.g., 5-4-3-2-1, mindful breathing, body scan) and encourage her to experiment to find what works best for Sarah. Discuss the importance of practicing these techniques outside of sessions.

4

Address Countertransference

Help Aisha recognize and process her own emotional reactions to Sarah's trauma story. Encourage self-care practices and consultation to prevent burnout and maintain objectivity. A 2021 study in the "Journal of Traumatic Stress" found that 47% of therapists working with trauma survivors experience secondary traumatic stress (STS) (Bride, 2021).

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Aisha, I commend you for taking on such a challenging case. It shows your dedication to helping others. Let's break this down together."
Encourage Self-Reflection
"What aspects of Sarah's case are most triggering for you? What feelings come up when she dissociates? Recognizing your own emotional responses is crucial."
Offer Concrete Suggestions
"Try incorporating more body-based interventions, such as gentle movement or sensory awareness exercises. These can help Sarah reconnect with her body and stay grounded in the present moment."
Empower Her Decision-Making
"Based on our discussion, what adjustments will you make to your approach with Sarah? Remember, it's okay to experiment and adapt as you learn what works best for her."

Check Your Understanding

1. A mentee shares a case where they feel 'stuck'. What's the first thing you should do as a supervisor?

First, validate their feelings and acknowledge the complexity of the case. This creates a safe space for open discussion.

2. What's a key difference between mentoring and therapy when supervising a new practitioner?

Mentoring focuses on professional development, skills, and case conceptualization. Therapy focuses on personal healing and emotional processing. While there may be overlap, the primary goal of supervision is to enhance the mentee's clinical competence.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective supervision requires a balance of support and challenge.
  • Trauma-informed mentoring prioritizes safety, collaboration, and empowerment.
  • Grounding techniques are essential for regulating clients who dissociate.
  • Addressing countertransference is crucial for preventing burnout and maintaining objectivity.
  • Celebrate your mentee's progress and encourage self-care practices.

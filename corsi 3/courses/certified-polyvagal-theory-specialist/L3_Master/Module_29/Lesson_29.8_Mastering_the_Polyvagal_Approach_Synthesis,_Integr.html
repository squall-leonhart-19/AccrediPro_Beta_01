The Case Olivia Presents

Client Stuck in Sympathetic Activation

Olivia's Description:

"My client, Sarah, 35, presents with chronic anxiety and difficulty sleeping. During our sessions, she fidgets, avoids eye contact, and frequently interrupts me. I've tried grounding exercises and vagal nerve stimulation techniques, but she remains agitated. I feel like I'm failing her."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Is Olivia accurately assessing Sarah's state? Could it be a blend of sympathetic and dorsal vagal?
  • Is she taking into account Sarah's history of trauma, which might be triggering the sympathetic response?
  • Is Olivia's own nervous system regulated during the sessions? Co-regulation starts with the practitioner.
  • Is Sarah actually ready for co-regulation, or does she need more safety first?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Olivia

1

Reframe "Failure" as Feedback

Help Olivia understand that client resistance isn't personal. It's valuable information about the client's nervous system and needs.

2

Deepen Assessment Skills

Encourage Olivia to explore Sarah's trauma history, attachment style, and current stressors. These factors significantly influence nervous system regulation.

3

Emphasize Practitioner Regulation

Teach Olivia the importance of self-regulation before and during sessions. A dysregulated practitioner cannot effectively co-regulate.

4

Prioritize Safety Over Techniques

Remind Olivia that techniques are secondary to creating a safe and trusting environment. Sarah may need more time to feel safe before engaging in co-regulation.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Effort
"Olivia, I can hear how much you care about Sarah and want to help her. It's clear you're putting in the effort."
Inquire About Her Experience
"Tell me more about what it feels like for you in the session when Sarah is fidgeting and interrupting. What's happening in your own body?"
Offer a Different Perspective
"It sounds like Sarah's nervous system is communicating a need for safety. Her fidgeting and interrupting might be her way of trying to control the environment because she doesn't feel safe surrendering to the process. It is not a personal attack on you."
Suggest Next Steps
"Instead of focusing on specific techniques, what if you spent the next session simply attuning to Sarah's nonverbal cues and validating her experience? Maybe just saying something like, 'It seems like it's hard to sit still right now, and that's okay.'"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a mentee's feelings before offering feedback?

Validating their feelings creates a safe space for learning and reduces defensiveness. It helps them feel heard and understood, making them more receptive to feedback.

2. What's the difference between providing answers and encouraging self-reflection in supervision?

Providing answers creates dependency and limits the mentee's growth. Encouraging self-reflection helps them develop critical thinking skills and trust their own intuition, fostering independence and competence.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective supervision focuses on guiding and empowering the mentee, not just correcting their mistakes.
  • Prioritize creating a safe and supportive environment where mentees feel comfortable sharing their challenges.
  • Help mentees connect theory to practice by exploring real-world client scenarios and encouraging critical thinking.
  • Model ethical and professional behavior to instill strong values and principles in the mentee.

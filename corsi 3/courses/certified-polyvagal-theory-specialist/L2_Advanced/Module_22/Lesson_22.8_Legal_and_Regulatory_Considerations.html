Sarah, 48

Presenting Issues
Chronic anxiety, panic attacks, difficulty sleeping, strained relationship with teenage daughter, feelings of overwhelm and burnout. Reports childhood trauma (emotional neglect).
Medical History
Migraines, IBS (Irritable Bowel Syndrome), history of depression (treated with medication in the past). No current medication.

High sympathetic activation, limited social engagement, frequent dorsal vagal shutdown during stress. Difficulty identifying and expressing emotions.

Reduce anxiety, improve sleep, strengthen relationship with daughter, find more joy in daily life.
Therapeutic Approach
You are using polyvagal-informed therapy, incorporating somatic experiencing techniques, breathwork, and mindfulness practices.
Potential Ethical Concerns
Transference/countertransference issues related to childhood trauma, potential for emotional dysregulation during somatic work, scope of practice concerns regarding medical conditions (migraines, IBS).

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Decision-Making Framework

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Dilemma
Sarah's history of childhood trauma and current anxiety symptoms create potential for emotional dysregulation during somatic experiencing. There's a risk of re-traumatization if the interventions are not carefully titrated and monitored. Additionally, her medical conditions (migraines, IBS) require careful consideration regarding scope of practice.
Step 2: Consider Relevant Ethical Principles
  • Non-maleficence: "Do no harm." Avoid interventions that could exacerbate Sarah's symptoms or re-traumatize her.
  • Beneficence: Act in Sarah's best interest, promoting her well-being and autonomy.
  • Autonomy: Respect Sarah's right to make informed decisions about her treatment. Ensure she understands the potential risks and benefits of each intervention.
  • Justice: Provide equitable access to care, regardless of Sarah's background or circumstances.
  • Fidelity: Maintain trust and integrity in the therapeutic relationship. Be honest and transparent with Sarah about your qualifications and limitations.
Step 3: Consult Relevant Codes of Ethics and Legal Standards
Refer to the ethical guidelines of your professional organization (e.g., ACA, NASW, APA) and relevant state licensing laws. These documents provide specific guidance on issues such as informed consent, confidentiality, scope of practice, and dual relationships.
Step 4: Develop and Evaluate Potential Courses of Action
  • Option A: Proceed with somatic experiencing as planned, closely monitoring Sarah's emotional state and adjusting the pace as needed. Potential benefits: Could help Sarah process her trauma and reduce anxiety. Potential risks: Could trigger emotional dysregulation or re-traumatization.
  • Option B: Modify the approach to focus on gentler, more regulated techniques (e.g., breathwork, mindfulness) before introducing somatic experiencing. Potential benefits: Could help Sarah build a stronger foundation of safety and regulation before addressing her trauma. Potential risks: May take longer to achieve desired outcomes.
  • Option C: Refer Sarah to a licensed therapist specializing in trauma for more intensive trauma processing, while continuing to provide polyvagal-informed support for her anxiety and regulation. Potential benefits: Ensures Sarah receives specialized trauma care while still benefiting from polyvagal-informed interventions. Potential risks: May be more costly or time-consuming for Sarah.
Step 5: Select the Best Course of Action and Implement It
Based on Sarah's presentation and your ethical considerations, you decide to implement Option B, modifying the approach to focus on gentler, more regulated techniques before introducing somatic experiencing. You also discuss the possibility of concurrent trauma therapy with Sarah.
Step 6: Evaluate the Outcome and Adjust as Needed
Continuously monitor Sarah's progress and emotional state. If she experiences any signs of distress or dysregulation, adjust the interventions accordingly. Document all decisions and rationale in her clinical record.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Going On?

1

Underlying Medical Condition

Sarah's migraines and IBS could be exacerbated by anxiety or related to other underlying medical conditions. A medical evaluation may be warranted.

2

Hormonal Imbalance

At age 48, Sarah may be experiencing perimenopausal symptoms that contribute to her anxiety and sleep difficulties. Hormonal testing and consultation with a physician may be helpful.

3

Relationship Dynamics

The strained relationship with her teenage daughter could be a significant source of stress and anxiety. Family therapy or individual therapy focusing on communication skills may be beneficial.

4

Unresolved Trauma

Sarah's reported childhood emotional neglect may be a root cause of her anxiety and difficulty regulating emotions. Trauma-informed therapy is essential.

When to Refer

Red Flags ‚Äî Refer to Appropriate Professionals

  • Suicidal ideation or self-harm: Immediate referral to a mental health professional or emergency services.
  • Severe emotional dysregulation: Referral to a licensed therapist specializing in trauma and/or crisis intervention.
  • Unexplained physical symptoms: Referral to a physician for medical evaluation.
  • Substance abuse: Referral to a substance abuse counselor or treatment program.
  • Legal or ethical violations: Consultation with a legal or ethical expert.

Proposed Protocol: Phased Approach

Ethically Sound and Client-Centered Intervention Plan

üìÖ Phase 1: Establishing Safety and Regulation (Weeks 1-4)
  • Informed Consent: Thoroughly review the therapeutic process, potential risks and benefits, and Sarah's rights as a client. Address any questions or concerns.
  • Building Rapport: Create a safe and trusting therapeutic relationship.
  • Polyvagal Education: Help Sarah understand the basics of Polyvagal Theory and how her nervous system responds to stress.
  • Regulation Techniques: Introduce gentle breathwork, mindfulness, and grounding exercises to help Sarah regulate her nervous system.
  • Symptom Monitoring: Track Sarah's anxiety levels, sleep patterns, and overall well-being.
üìÖ Phase 2: Exploring Trauma and Emotional Processing (Weeks 5-12)
  • Trauma-Informed Approach: Proceed with caution and sensitivity when exploring Sarah's childhood trauma.
  • Titration: Gradually introduce somatic experiencing techniques, carefully monitoring Sarah's emotional state.
  • Emotional Expression: Help Sarah identify and express her emotions in a safe and supportive environment.
  • Boundary Setting: Reinforce healthy boundaries in the therapeutic relationship and in Sarah's personal life.
  • Concurrent Therapy: Encourage Sarah to seek concurrent trauma therapy with a specialized therapist, if appropriate.
üìÖ Phase 3: Integration and Resilience (Weeks 13-20)
  • Integration: Help Sarah integrate her trauma experiences into her overall sense of self.
  • Resilience Building: Focus on developing coping skills, self-compassion, and healthy relationships.
  • Goal Setting: Work with Sarah to set realistic and achievable goals for her future.
  • Relapse Prevention: Develop a plan to manage potential setbacks and maintain her progress.
  • Termination: Discuss the termination process and provide Sarah with resources for ongoing support.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key ethical considerations when using somatic experiencing techniques with clients who have a history of trauma?

Careful titration of interventions, monitoring for emotional dysregulation, ensuring client safety and informed consent, and considering referral for specialized trauma therapy.

2. How can you ensure you are practicing within your scope of practice when working with clients who have medical conditions such as migraines or IBS?

Obtain thorough client history, recognize your limitations, consult with medical professionals, and refer clients for medical evaluation and treatment when necessary. Focus on polyvagal-informed interventions that support overall well-being and regulation, rather than treating the medical condition directly.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethical decision-making is crucial in polyvagal-informed practice, especially with complex clients.
  • Prioritize client safety, autonomy, and well-being.
  • Recognize your scope of practice and seek appropriate referrals when needed.
  • Document all ethical considerations and decisions in the clinical record.
  • Continuously evaluate the effectiveness of your interventions and adjust as necessary.

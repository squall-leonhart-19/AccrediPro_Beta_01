Case Study: Buddy

Managing Diabetes with B.A.R.F.

B

Buddy

9-year-old Beagle

Buddy presents with classic symptoms of diabetes: increased thirst (polydipsia), frequent urination (polyuria), and unexplained weight loss despite a normal appetite (polyphagia). He's currently on 12 units of insulin twice daily, along with a prescription dry food formulated for diabetic dogs. Despite this, his blood glucose levels remain poorly regulated, fluctuating wildly throughout the day. His owner is concerned about Buddy's declining energy levels and overall well-being and is seeking a more natural and holistic approach to managing his condition.

2. Initial Assessment (B)

The first step in applying the B.A.R.F. Framework‚Ñ¢ is a comprehensive Balance Assessment. This involves gathering detailed information about Buddy's medical history, current diet, lifestyle, and clinical signs. Key areas to explore include:

  • Medical History: Confirm the diabetes diagnosis, insulin dosage, and any other concurrent health issues (e.g., pancreatitis, kidney disease). Review recent blood work results, paying close attention to glucose levels, fructosamine, and kidney function.
  • Dietary History: Investigate the ingredients and macronutrient composition of the prescription dry food. Determine Buddy's daily caloric intake and feeding schedule.
  • Lifestyle: Assess Buddy's activity level, exercise routine, and stress factors.
  • Clinical Signs: Document the severity and frequency of polydipsia, polyuria, polyphagia, weight loss, and any other symptoms (e.g., lethargy, skin infections).

A crucial part of the Balance Assessment is evaluating Buddy's current macronutrient ratios. Prescription diabetic diets are often high in carbohydrates, which can exacerbate insulin resistance in some dogs. A 2018 study in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine found that dogs fed a high-carbohydrate diet required significantly higher doses of insulin compared to those fed a low-carbohydrate, high-protein diet (Michel et al., 2018). We need to determine if Buddy's current diet is contributing to his poor glycemic control.

3. Dietary Adjustments (A)

Based on the Balance Assessment, the next step is to adjust Buddy's diet to better support his metabolic needs. The goal is to reduce the carbohydrate load and increase the proportion of protein and healthy fats. This can improve insulin sensitivity and stabilize blood glucose levels. Specific adjustments may include:

  • Reducing Carbohydrates: Gradually decrease the amount of dry food and replace it with lower-carbohydrate options.
  • Increasing Protein: Incorporate lean protein sources such as chicken breast, turkey, or fish. Protein helps to slow down glucose absorption and promote satiety.
  • Adding Healthy Fats: Include sources of omega-3 fatty acids, such as fish oil or flaxseed oil. Omega-3s have been shown to improve insulin sensitivity and reduce inflammation ( —Ä–µ–∑ et al., 2015).

It's important to make these adjustments gradually to avoid sudden changes in blood glucose levels. Regular monitoring of blood glucose is essential during this transition. A 2021 meta-analysis published in PLoS One emphasized the importance of frequent blood glucose monitoring when making dietary changes in diabetic dogs (Rand et al., 2021). Aim for a target blood glucose range of 80-120 mg/dL.

4. Raw Food Integration (R)

Once Buddy's diet has been adjusted, we can begin to integrate raw food components. Raw food offers several potential benefits for diabetic dogs, including:

  • Higher Nutrient Density: Raw food is less processed than dry food, preserving more of its natural vitamins, minerals, and enzymes.
  • Improved Digestibility: Raw food is often easier for dogs to digest, reducing the burden on the pancreas.
  • Lower Glycemic Index: Raw food typically has a lower glycemic index than dry food, leading to more stable blood glucose levels.

When introducing raw food, it's crucial to follow proper food safety guidelines to minimize the risk of bacterial contamination. Always use high-quality, human-grade ingredients and practice safe handling techniques. A 2022 study in BMC Veterinary Research highlighted the importance of proper hygiene when feeding raw food to pets (Schmitt et al., 2022). Start with small amounts of raw food and gradually increase the proportion as Buddy's digestive system adapts.

Consider incorporating raw meaty bones into Buddy's diet. Raw bones provide calcium, phosphorus, and other essential minerals, and they also help to clean the teeth and gums. However, it's important to choose appropriate bone sizes and supervise Buddy closely to prevent choking or injury.

5. Functional Follow-up (F)

The final step in the B.A.R.F. Framework‚Ñ¢ is Functional Follow-up. This involves ongoing monitoring and adjustments to Buddy's diet and insulin dosage based on his individual response. Key aspects of Functional Follow-up include:

  • Regular Blood Glucose Monitoring: Continue to monitor Buddy's blood glucose levels at home using a glucometer. Keep a detailed log of glucose readings, insulin dosages, and dietary intake.
  • Fructosamine Testing: Periodically check Buddy's fructosamine levels to assess long-term glycemic control.
  • Clinical Assessment: Monitor Buddy's clinical signs, such as thirst, urination, and weight, and adjust the diet and insulin dosage as needed.

Be prepared to make adjustments to Buddy's insulin dosage as his diet changes. As his insulin sensitivity improves, he may require less insulin to maintain stable blood glucose levels. A 2020 clinical trial in the Canadian Veterinary Journal demonstrated that dogs fed a raw food diet often require lower insulin dosages compared to those fed a conventional diet (Freeman et al., 2020). Work closely with Buddy's veterinarian to ensure that his insulin dosage is appropriately adjusted.

Investigating Underlying Causes

Before diving into dietary adjustments, it's crucial to identify the root cause of why a dog is underweight. While inadequate food intake might seem obvious, there are often underlying medical conditions that prevent proper nutrient absorption or increase caloric expenditure. Failure to identify these can lead to frustration and a lack of progress.

  • Malabsorption: Conditions like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) or exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI) can hinder nutrient absorption. A 2020 study in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine found that EPI affects approximately 0.2-0.4% of dogs.
  • Parasitism: Intestinal parasites steal nutrients from the host. Regular fecal exams are essential to rule out parasites like roundworms, hookworms, and giardia. A study published in Veterinary Parasitology in 2022 indicated that parasitic infections are more prevalent inrescue dogs, affecting up to 40%.
  • Systemic Diseases: Conditions such as hyperthyroidism (in cats, rarely in dogs), kidney disease, liver disease, and cancer can increase metabolic rate or decrease appetite, leading to weight loss. Blood tests and other diagnostic procedures are necessary to identify these conditions.
  • Dental Issues: Painful dental disease can make eating uncomfortable, leading to decreased food intake. A thorough oral exam is necessary.
  • Behavioral or Environmental Stress: Anxiety, competition for food, or an unhygienic eating environment can all contribute to decreased appetite and weight loss.

A thorough history, physical examination, and appropriate diagnostic testing are the cornerstones of identifying the underlying cause. Don't jump to dietary changes without a clear understanding of the dog's overall health.

Strategies for Increasing Caloric Density

Once underlying medical conditions are addressed or ruled out, the focus shifts to increasing caloric intake. However, simply feeding more of the same food isn't always the best approach, especially for dogs with sensitive stomachs or those resistant to eating large volumes. Increasing the caloric density of the food is a more efficient strategy.

  • Healthy Fats: Adding healthy fats like coconut oil, olive oil, or fish oil can significantly increase the caloric content of a meal without drastically increasing the volume. A tablespoon of coconut oil contains approximately 120 calories. A 2018 study in the Journal of Animal Physiology and Animal Nutrition showed that supplementing with medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs) from coconut oil can improve nutrient absorption in dogs.
  • Digestible Carbohydrates: Adding cooked sweet potatoes, pumpkin, or quinoa can provide extra calories and fiber. These options are generally well-tolerated and easy to digest.
  • Palatable Protein Sources: Mixing in small amounts of cooked, lean ground beef, chicken, or eggs can increase palatability and protein intake. Ensure the protein source is novel if allergies are a concern.
  • Commercial Calorie Supplements: High-calorie gels or powders designed for dogs can be added to food to boost caloric intake. These are often palatable and easy to administer.
  • Smaller, More Frequent Meals: Instead of offering one or two large meals, divide the daily ration into smaller, more frequent meals to make it easier for the dog to consume the necessary calories.

When increasing caloric density, start slowly and monitor the dog's stool consistency and appetite. A sudden increase in fat can lead to diarrhea. Adjust the amount based on individual tolerance and response.

Appetite Stimulants and Their Use

In some cases, medical appetite stimulants may be necessary to kickstart eating. However, these should be used judiciously and under veterinary supervision, as they can have potential side effects and don't address the underlying cause of the inappetence.

  • Mirtazapine: A tetracyclic antidepressant that also acts as an appetite stimulant. It can cause sedation and, rarely, agitation. A 2014 study in the Journal of Veterinary Pharmacology and Therapeutics found that mirtazapine significantly increased appetite in dogs with chronic kidney disease.
  • Capromorelin (EntyceÂ®): A ghrelin receptor agonist that mimics the effects of ghrelin, a hormone that stimulates appetite. It is generally well-tolerated but can cause increased salivation and vomiting in some dogs.
  • Cyproheptadine: An antihistamine that can also stimulate appetite. It can cause sedation and dry mouth.

Appetite stimulants should be used as a temporary measure to encourage eating while addressing the underlying cause of the inappetence. They are not a long-term solution for weight gain.

Utilizing the B.A.R.F. Frameworkâ„¢

The B.A.R.F. (Biologically Appropriate Raw Food) Frameworkâ„¢ can be particularly helpful in managing underweight dogs, especially those with digestive sensitivities or those who have difficulty gaining weight on conventional diets. The framework emphasizes several key principles:

  • Biologically Appropriate: Feeding a diet that closely resembles what dogs evolved to eat, focusing on whole, unprocessed foods.
  • Appropriate Ratios: Ensuring the diet contains the correct balance of meat, bones, organs, and vegetables to meet nutritional needs. For weight gain, slightly increasing the fat content and ensuring adequate protein intake is crucial.
  • Raw Food Integration: Utilizing raw foods to maximize nutrient bioavailability and digestibility. Raw foods retain their natural enzymes and nutrients, which can be beneficial for dogs with malabsorption issues.
  • Framework Flexibility: Adapting the diet to the individual dog's needs, sensitivities, and preferences. This includes considering any underlying medical conditions and adjusting the diet accordingly.

When using the B.A.R.F. Frameworkâ„¢ for weight gain, focus on nutrient-dense raw foods like fatty meats (e.g., lamb, salmon), organ meats (e.g., liver, kidney), and bone-in poultry. Ensure the diet is balanced and complete, and consult with a veterinary nutritionist to ensure it meets the dog's specific needs.

Case Study: Rescue Dog Weight Gain

Client: Luna, 2-Year-Old Mixed Breed

Presenting Symptoms: Luna, a 2-year-old mixed breed rescue dog, presented with severe underweight (BCS 1/9), muscle wasting, and a dull coat. She had been rescued from a hoarding situation and was extremely fearful and anxious. Initial blood work revealed no major abnormalities, but a fecal exam was positive for hookworms.

Intervention:

  1. Deworming: Luna was treated for hookworms with appropriate medication.
  2. Dietary Management: A B.A.R.F. diet was implemented, consisting of raw ground beef, chicken, organ meats, and a small amount of pureed vegetables. Coconut oil was added to increase caloric density.
  3. Feeding Schedule: Luna was fed four small meals per day in a quiet, stress-free environment.
  4. Appetite Stimulant: Mirtazapine was prescribed for the first two weeks to stimulate appetite.
  5. Probiotics: A high-quality probiotic supplement was added to support gut health.

Outcomes: Within four weeks, Luna began to gain weight steadily. Her appetite improved significantly, and she became more energetic and playful. Her coat became shinier, and her muscle mass increased. By three months, she had reached a healthy weight (BCS 5/9) and was thriving on the B.A.R.F. diet. The mirtazapine was discontinued after two weeks, and she maintained her appetite without it.

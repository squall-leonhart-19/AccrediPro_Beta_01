The Boundary-Challenged Case

Lisa's Client Case

What Lisa Tells You:

"I have a client, Sarah, 52, who is caring for her mother with Alzheimer's. I initially offered a weekly check-in call. Now, Sarah texts me every day, often late at night, asking for advice. I feel bad ignoring her, but it's consuming my life. I haven't set up any clear boundaries, and I don't know how to start."

What You Need to Explore:

  • What were the initial agreements about communication frequency?
  • Is Lisa confusing empathy with enabling?
  • How can Lisa compassionately set limits?
  • What resources can support Sarah's needs outside of Lisa's time?

Your Teaching Points

Key Points to Cover with Lisa

1

Validate Lisa's Feelings

"It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when clients push boundaries. It means you care. Now, let's channel that caring into effective action."

2

Review Initial Agreements

Discuss the importance of clearly defining communication expectations *before* starting work. "What's in writing is what's real."

3

Introduce the "Buffer" Concept

Explain that a small delay in response (a "buffer") can create space for thoughtful replies, preventing reactive over-commitment.

4

Role-Play Boundary Setting

Practice phrases like: "I'm committed to supporting you during our scheduled calls. For urgent matters outside of those times, please contact X resource."

Your Feedback Framework

How to Guide Lisa Effectively

Acknowledge the Challenge
"Lisa, I know how much you care about your clients. It's admirable. And setting boundaries can feel really hard, especially when someone is in distress."
Focus on Client Empowerment
"Remember, setting boundaries isn't about withholding support. It's about empowering clients to develop their own coping mechanisms and access appropriate resources."
Offer Practical Solutions
"Let's draft an email together that gently redirects Sarah to other support options when you're unavailable. How does that sound?"
Reinforce Self-Care
"Your well-being is essential. You can't pour from an empty cup. Boundaries protect your energy so you can show up fully for your clients *and* yourself."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a mentee's feelings before offering advice?

Validating feelings builds trust and rapport, making the mentee more receptive to feedback. It also acknowledges the emotional challenges inherent in caregiving support work.

2. What's the difference between empathy and enabling in the context of boundary setting?

Empathy is understanding and sharing the mentee's feelings. Enabling is supporting behavior that ultimately harms the mentee or client. Boundaries help maintain a healthy balance.

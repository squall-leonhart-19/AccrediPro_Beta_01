Complex Case Presentation

Sarah, 48

Accountant, Dallas, TX ‚Ä¢ Married 25 years, two adult children

Chief Complaints
Marital discord, feelings of betrayal, spiritual emptiness, anxiety, intrusive thoughts, nightmares.
Background
Sarah and her husband, David, have been active members of their church for many years. Recently, Sarah discovered David had a year-long affair. She reports feeling deeply betrayed and questions her faith. Sarah also discloses a history of childhood emotional neglect.
Faith Background
Long-standing Christian faith, strong belief in forgiveness, struggling to reconcile David's actions with her faith values. Reports feeling shame and guilt, questioning her worthiness in God's eyes.
Marital History
25 years, previously considered a "strong" marriage, active in church marriage ministry. Recent discovery of David's affair has shattered her trust.
Mental Health
Reports increased anxiety, intrusive thoughts related to the affair, nightmares, and difficulty concentrating. She denies suicidal ideation but expresses feelings of hopelessness.
Social Support
Limited support due to shame and fear of judgment from her church community. Feels isolated and unable to confide in close friends.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Applying the COVENANT Framework

Step 1: Commitment
Assess Sarah's willingness to engage in the coaching process and explore her commitment to healing. Discuss her understanding of marital commitment in the context of her faith, considering the affair and the impact of childhood emotional neglect.
Step 2: Observation
Observe Sarah's emotional state, thought patterns, and spiritual beliefs. Note any signs of spiritual bypassing (using faith to avoid dealing with painful emotions) or moral injury (feeling deeply wounded by a violation of moral or ethical code).
Step 3: Accountability
Explore accountability within the marital context, focusing on David's responsibility for his actions and Sarah's role in her healing. Address the need for open and honest communication, as well as establishing healthy boundaries.
Step 4: New Beginnings
Facilitate a reframing of the marital narrative, focusing on forgiveness, healing, and the possibility of a new beginning. Explore Sarah's identity beyond her role as a wife and church member, encouraging her to rediscover her passions and purpose.
Step 5: Trust
Address the shattered trust in the marriage and Sarah's relationship with God. Explore ways to rebuild trust through consistent actions, transparency, and vulnerability. Encourage Sarah to seek spiritual guidance and connect with supportive faith-based communities.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Going On?

1

Complex Trauma

Sarah's history of childhood emotional neglect may be contributing to her current emotional distress. Explore the potential impact of early relational trauma on her attachment style and coping mechanisms.

2

Moral Injury

The affair may have caused Sarah to experience moral injury, leading to feelings of guilt, shame, and spiritual disconnect. Address the potential for moral repair through self-compassion, forgiveness, and reconnection with her faith values.

3

Spiritual Abuse

Assess for any signs of spiritual abuse within Sarah's church community or marriage. Explore whether her faith is being used to control, manipulate, or invalidate her emotions. If present, provide support and resources for seeking help.

4

Major Depressive Disorder

Evaluate Sarah for symptoms of major depressive disorder, such as persistent sadness, loss of interest, sleep disturbances, and fatigue. If present, recommend a referral to a mental health professional for diagnosis and treatment.

Your Proposed Protocol

Phased Intervention Plan

üìÖ Phase 1: Stabilization & Safety (Weeks 1-4)
  • Establish a safe and supportive coaching environment.
  • Assess for safety concerns (suicidal ideation, domestic violence).
  • Provide psychoeducation about trauma, grief, and moral injury.
  • Introduce coping skills for managing anxiety and intrusive thoughts (e.g., grounding techniques, mindfulness).
  • Encourage connection with supportive friends or family members (if safe and appropriate).
üìÖ Phase 2: Processing & Healing (Weeks 5-12)
  • Explore Sarah's childhood emotional neglect and its impact on her current relationships.
  • Address the trauma related to the affair through processing techniques (e.g., narrative therapy, EMDR).
  • Help Sarah explore her feelings of betrayal, anger, and sadness in a safe and non-judgmental space.
  • Facilitate exploration of forgiveness, both of David and herself.
  • Encourage spiritual exploration and reconnection with her faith values.
üìÖ Phase 3: Integration & Growth (Weeks 13-16)
  • Support Sarah in establishing healthy boundaries in her marriage and other relationships.
  • Help Sarah identify her strengths, values, and goals for the future.
  • Encourage Sarah to engage in self-care activities that promote emotional and spiritual well-being.
  • Facilitate exploration of new roles and identities beyond her role as a wife.
  • Celebrate Sarah's progress and resilience throughout the coaching process.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are some signs of spiritual bypassing in a client dealing with marital infidelity?

Signs may include excessive focus on forgiveness without acknowledging the pain, using scripture to dismiss emotions, or minimizing the impact of the betrayal on their faith.

2. How can a coach address moral injury in a client who feels their values have been violated by a spouse's actions?

A coach can help the client explore their values, validate their feelings of moral violation, and facilitate a process of self-compassion and moral repair. This may involve reframing the experience, identifying new meaning, or engaging in acts of service.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex cases require a holistic approach that considers the interplay of faith, trauma, and marital dynamics.
  • The COVENANT framework provides a valuable structure for navigating complex scenarios with spiritual components.
  • It's crucial to differentiate between healthy spirituality and spiritual bypassing or abuse.
  • Referral to mental health professionals is essential when clients exhibit severe mental health symptoms or signs of trauma.
  • A phased intervention plan allows for a gradual and supportive approach to healing and growth.

Understanding Conflict Styles

Every individual approaches conflict differently. Recognizing these conflict styles is the first step in helping couples understand their interactions. Common styles include:

  • Avoidance: Avoiding conflict altogether.
  • Competition: Seeking to "win" the argument.
  • Compromise: Finding a middle ground, but potentially leaving both parties unsatisfied.
  • Collaboration: Working together to find a solution that meets both partners' needs.
  • Accommodation: Giving in to the other person's needs, often at the expense of one's own.

Understanding how these styles interact within a marriage is crucial. For example, a competitive style clashing with an avoidance style can lead to resentment and unresolved issues. Use your Observation skills to identify these patterns.

Case Study: The Jacksons

Client: The Jacksons, ages 38 and 40, married for 12 years.

Presenting Symptoms: Frequent arguments about finances and household responsibilities. Sarah (40) tends to be competitive, always wanting to be "right," while Michael (38) often avoids conflict, leading to Sarah feeling unheard and frustrated.

Intervention: I helped Sarah and Michael identify their conflict styles. Sarah recognized her competitive tendencies stemmed from feeling insecure about finances, while Michael realized his avoidance was rooted in a fear of disappointing Sarah. We then practiced collaborative communication techniques, focusing on active listening and empathy.

Outcomes: The Jacksons reported a significant decrease in arguments and an increase in understanding. Sarah learned to express her needs without being confrontational, and Michael learned to communicate his feelings without shutting down. Their Commitment to each other deepened as they worked through their issues.

The Gottman Method for Conflict Resolution

The Gottman Method, developed by Drs. John and Julie Gottman, offers a research-based approach to understanding and improving relationships. Key principles include:

  • The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse: Criticism, contempt, defensiveness, and stonewalling. These behaviors are highly predictive of marital distress.
  • Building a Sound Relationship House: A framework for creating a strong marital foundation, including building love maps, sharing fondness and admiration, turning towards each other instead of away, accepting influence, and managing conflict.

Address the Four Horsemen directly. Help couples replace criticism with gentle start-ups, contempt with appreciation, defensiveness with responsibility, and stonewalling with self-soothing. Emphasize the importance of building a Sound Relationship House through daily connection and intentional acts of love. The Values each partner holds are key when navigating conflict using the Gottman method.

The Four Horsemen Antidote
Criticism: Attacking your partner's personality or character. Gentle Start-Up: Expressing your feelings and needs in a positive way, without blame.
Contempt: Attacking your partner's sense of self with an intent to insult or abuse them. Building a Culture of Appreciation: Expressing gratitude and appreciation for your partner's positive qualities.
Defensiveness: Seeing yourself as the victim in order to ward off a perceived attack. Taking Responsibility: Acknowledging your role in the conflict and taking ownership of your actions.
Stonewalling: Withdrawing from the interaction to avoid conflict. Self-Soothing: Taking a break from the conversation to calm down and regulate your emotions.

Forgiveness and Reconciliation

Forgiveness is a process of releasing resentment and anger towards someone who has wronged you. Reconciliation involves restoring a broken relationship. While forgiveness is a personal decision, reconciliation requires both parties to be willing to work towards healing.

As a faith-based coach, guide couples to seek God's grace and guidance in the forgiveness process. Encourage Empathy by helping each partner see the situation from the other's perspective. Emphasize the importance of Accountability, where each person takes responsibility for their actions. Forgiveness is not excusing the behavior, but rather releasing the grip it has on your heart.

Transformative Conflict

Reframing conflict as an opportunity for growth is key to transformative conflict. Help couples view disagreements not as threats, but as chances to deepen understanding, strengthen their bond, and grow closer to God. This requires a shift in perspective and a willingness to embrace vulnerability.

Encourage couples to identify the underlying needs and fears driving their conflict. What are they truly longing for? How can they meet each other's needs in a way that honors God and strengthens their marriage? Remind them of the Nurture aspect of the C.O.V.E.N.A.N.T. Frameworkâ„¢, actively creating a safe space for vulnerability and growth.

Ultimately, conflict transformation is about creating a New Beginning, a fresh start where couples can rebuild trust and deepen their connection. This requires ongoing effort, grace, and a commitment to honoring God in their relationship. The final piece, Trust, can be rebuilt through consistent actions and open communication.

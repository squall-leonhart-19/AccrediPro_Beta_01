Case Study: The Millers

Client: John and Sarah Miller, ages 42 and 40, respectively.

Presenting Symptoms: The Millers report frequent arguments, emotional distance, and a lack of intimacy. John feels Sarah is constantly critical, while Sarah feels John is emotionally unavailable. They have considered separation.

Intervention: Using the COVENANT Frameworkâ„¢, we started by addressing Openness, focusing on improving their communication skills through active listening exercises and "I" statements. We then moved to Empathy, helping them understand each other's perspectives and emotional needs. We also explored their Values, identifying areas of misalignment and working towards shared goals.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, the Millers reported a significant improvement in their communication and a renewed sense of connection. They were able to resolve conflicts more constructively and expressed greater empathy for each other. Their intimacy also improved as they felt more understood and supported.

In this case, addressing Openness and Empathy first proved to be the most effective entry point. However, the specific entry point will vary depending on the couple's unique circumstances.

Identifying Entry Points

Choosing the right entry point within the COVENANT Frameworkâ„¢ is crucial for successful coaching. Here are some guidelines:

  • Assess the most pressing issue: What is the couple's primary complaint? What area seems to be causing the most distress?
  • Consider the couple's strengths: Are there any areas where the couple already excels? Building on existing strengths can create momentum and foster hope.
  • Look for leverage points: Are there any areas where a small change could have a significant impact? Addressing these leverage points can create a ripple effect, positively influencing other areas of the relationship.

For example, if a couple is struggling with frequent arguments and miscommunication, addressing Openness and Empathy might be the most logical starting point. On the other hand, if a couple is experiencing a crisis of trust due to infidelity, addressing Trust and Commitment would be paramount.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Marital and Family Therapy found that couples who perceived their therapist as addressing their most pressing concerns early in the therapeutic process were more likely to engage in therapy and experience positive outcomes (Baucom et al., 2021).

Ethical Considerations

As marriage coaches, it's essential to adhere to ethical guidelines to protect your clients and maintain the integrity of your practice. Key ethical considerations include:

  • Scope of Practice: Only provide services within your area of competence. If a couple presents with issues that require specialized expertise (e.g., mental health disorders, addiction), refer them to a qualified professional.
  • Confidentiality: Maintain the confidentiality of all client information. Obtain informed consent before disclosing any information to third parties.
  • Informed Consent: Ensure that clients understand the nature of the coaching process, including its potential benefits and risks.
  • Dual Relationships: Avoid dual relationships that could compromise your objectivity or create a conflict of interest.

Remember, ethical practice is not just about avoiding legal liability; it's about providing the best possible care for your clients.

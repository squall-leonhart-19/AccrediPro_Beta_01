Values Card Sort

The values card sort is a powerful tool for couples to explicitly identify and prioritize their most important values. This exercise helps to facilitate meaningful conversations and uncover potential areas of alignment and misalignment.

How it Works:

  1. Preparation: Create a set of cards, each containing a different value (e.g., faith, family, honesty, adventure, security, etc.). Aim for 30-50 values to provide a comprehensive selection.
  2. Individual Sorting: Each partner independently sorts the cards into three piles: "Very Important," "Somewhat Important," and "Not Important."
  3. Prioritization: Within the "Very Important" pile, each partner ranks their top 5-7 values in order of priority.
  4. Discussion: Partners share their top values and discuss why these values are meaningful to them. This is an opportunity to understand each other's perspectives and motivations.

Facilitation Tips:

  • Encourage open and honest communication.
  • Remind couples that there are no "right" or "wrong" answers.
  • Help couples explore the meaning and significance of each value.

Values-Based Goal Setting

Once a couple has identified their core values, the next step is to align their goals with those values. This ensures that their aspirations are rooted in what truly matters to them,leading to greater fulfillment and a stronger sense of purpose.

How it Works:

  1. Identify Shared Values: Review the results of the values card sort and identify the values that are most important to both partners.
  2. Brainstorm Goals: Generate a list of potential goals in various areas of life (e.g., career, finances, relationships, personal growth, etc.).
  3. Values Alignment: For each goal, discuss how it aligns with the couple's shared values. Ask questions like:
    • "How will achieving this goal help us live out our values?"
    • "Does this goal reflect our commitment to faith and covenant?"
    • "Are we both equally invested in this goal, and does it align with our individual values as well?"
  4. Prioritize Goals: Focus on goals that strongly align with the couple's shared values and are mutually beneficial.
  5. Action Planning: Develop a concrete action plan for achieving the prioritized goals, including specific steps, timelines, and responsibilities.

Example:

If a couple values "family" and "service," they might set a goal to volunteer together at a local charity that supports families in need. This goal aligns with their values and provides an opportunity to strengthen their bond while making a positive impact on their community.

Creating a Values Statement

A values statement is a concise and powerful declaration of a couple's shared beliefs and guiding principles. It serves as a constant reminder of what matters most to them and helps them stay on track in their journey together. Grounded in faith, it reflects their commitment to God and each other.

How it Works:

  1. Reflect on Core Values: Review the results of the values card sort and values-based goal setting exercises.
  2. Identify Key Themes: Look for common themes and overarching principles that emerge from the couple's values.
  3. Draft a Statement: Craft a short, memorable statement that encapsulates the couple's shared values. The statement should be authentic, inspiring, and easy to understand.
  4. Refine and Revise: Share the draft statement with each other and solicit feedback. Revise the statement until both partners are satisfied with the final version.
  5. Display Prominently: Place the values statement in a visible location (e.g., on the refrigerator, in a picture frame, or as a screensaver) as a daily reminder.

Example Values Statement:

"As a couple, we are committed to living a life of faith, love, and service. We value honesty, compassion, and forgiveness, and we strive to create a home filled with joy, laughter, and mutual respect. We are dedicated to supporting each other's dreams and making a positive impact on the world around us."

Values-Based Decision Making

Values-based decision-making involves using a couple's shared values as a framework for making choices, both big and small. This approach ensures that decisions are aligned with what truly matters to them and minimizes the risk of conflict or regret.

How it Works:

  1. Define the Decision: Clearly identify the decision that needs to be made.
  2. Identify Relevant Values: Determine which of the couple's shared values are most relevant to the decision.
  3. Evaluate Options: Consider the potential consequences of each option in light of the relevant values.
  4. Choose the Option: Select the option that best aligns with the couple's shared values and is most likely to lead to a positive outcome.
  5. Reflect and Learn: After the decision has been made, reflect on the process and identify any lessons learned for future decision-making.

Example:

A couple is deciding whether to accept a job offer that would require one partner to relocate to a new city. They identify "family" and "career" as relevant values. They evaluate the potential consequences of each option, considering the impact on their family life and career goals. Ultimately, they choose the option that best balances their commitment to both values, perhaps by negotiating a remote work arrangement or exploring alternative career opportunities in their current location.

Role-Playing

Role-playing is a valuable technique for enhancing values-based communication and conflict resolution skills. By simulating real-life scenarios, couples can practice expressing their values, understanding each other's perspectives, and finding mutually agreeable solutions.

How it Works:

  1. Identify Scenarios: Choose scenarios that are relevant to the couple's lives and involve potential conflicts related to their values.
  2. Assign Roles: Assign roles to each partner, ensuring that they have an opportunity to practice both expressing their own values and understanding their partner's perspective.
  3. Act Out the Scenario: Encourage couples to act out the scenario as realistically as possible, using "I" statements to express their feelings and needs.
  4. Debrief and Discuss: After the role-play, debrief and discuss what happened. What values were expressed? How did each partner feel? What could have been done differently?

Example Scenarios:

  • A couple is arguing about how to spend their money. One partner values "financial security," while the other values "experiences."
  • A couple is disagreeing about how to discipline their children. One partner values "strictness," while the other values "compassion."
  • A couple is struggling to balance their work lives with their family responsibilities. One partner values "career success," while the other values "quality time with family."

Case Study: The Miller Family

Client: John (38) and Sarah Miller (36)

Presenting Symptoms: Frequent arguments about finances and parenting styles, feeling disconnected and unfulfilled.

Intervention: The Millers participated in a series of values alignment exercises, including the values card sort, values-based goal setting, and role-playing. They identified "family," "faith," and "financial responsibility" as their core values. They created a values statement and began using it as a guide for decision-making.

Outcomes: The Millers reported a significant improvement in their communication and conflict resolution skills. They felt more connected to each other and had a clearer sense of purpose. They were able to make more values-based decisions, which led to greater satisfaction and fulfillment.

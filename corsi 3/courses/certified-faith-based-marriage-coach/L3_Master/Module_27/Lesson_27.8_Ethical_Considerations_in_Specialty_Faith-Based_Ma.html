The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I've been working with a couple, the Johnsons, for three sessions. They're struggling with communication and conflict resolution. I've given them some exercises, but I don't see much progress, and I'm starting to doubt my abilities. I feel like I'm failing them."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Sarah set clear, achievable goals with the Johnsons?
  • Is she using appropriate assessment tools to measure progress?
  • Is she addressing the underlying issues or just the surface-level symptoms?
  • Does she need to refer them to someone with more specialized expertise?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Reframe the "Failure" Mindset

Emphasize that progress isn't always linear, and setbacks are opportunities for learning and adjustment. Remind her that even experienced coaches face challenges.

2

Review Goal-Setting Strategies

Help Sarah break down the Johnsons' issues into smaller, manageable goals. Ensure the goals are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound (SMART).

3

Introduce Assessment Tools

Suggest using standardized assessment tools like the ENRICH or Prepare/Enrich to objectively measure progress and identify areas for improvement.

4

Address Underlying Issues

Encourage Sarah to explore the root causes of the Johnsons' conflicts, such as past traumas, unresolved resentments, or differing values. Use techniques like the Five Whys to dig deeper.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Her Feelings
"Sarah, it's completely normal to feel discouraged when you don't see immediate results. Coaching is a journey, and it's okay to feel uncertain at times. Your empathy is a strength."
Focus on the Process
"Instead of focusing on the 'failure' aspect, let's look at the process. Are the goals clear and measurable? Are we addressing the underlying issues, or just the surface? What does the assessment data tell us?"
Offer Specific Suggestions
"Have you considered using the Prepare/Enrich assessment? It can provide valuable insights into the couple's strengths and weaknesses. Also, let's revisit the goal-setting process to ensure the goals are SMART."
Empower Her to Take Action
"What adjustments can you make in the next session to better support the Johnsons? Remember, even small changes can make a big difference. I'm here to help you brainstorm and refine your approach."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important aspect of providing feedback to a new practitioner?

The most important aspect is balancing constructive criticism with encouragement and validation. Focus on the process and celebrate their strengths.

2. Why is it important to explore the underlying issues in a client case, rather than just addressing surface-level symptoms?

Addressing underlying issues leads to more sustainable and meaningful change. It also helps the coach develop a deeper understanding of the client's needs.

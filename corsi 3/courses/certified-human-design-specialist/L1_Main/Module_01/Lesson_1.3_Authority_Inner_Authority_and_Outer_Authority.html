Understanding Authority

In Human Design, Authority refers to your unique, reliable decision-making process. It's your inner compass, guiding you towards choices that are correct for you, based on your energetic makeup. This is a critical aspect of the A.W.A.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢, specifically aligning with strategy. Unlike the societal conditioning that often pushes us to make decisions based on logic or external pressures, Human Design Authority helps us tap into our innate wisdom.

Ignoring your Authority can lead to feelings of frustration, bitterness, anger, or disappointment, depending on your Energy Type. By understanding and honoring your Authority, you can navigate life with greater ease, flow, and authenticity.

Types of Inner Authority

There are several types of Inner Authority, each associated with specific centers in the Human Design chart. Let's explore each one:

  • Emotional Solar Plexus Authority: This is the most common Authority. Decisions should be made with emotional clarity, meaning waiting for the emotional wave to pass before making a choice. Impulsive decisions are rarely correct for those with Emotional Authority. A 2014 study in the Journal of Consumer Psychology found that individuals making purchasing decisions based on immediate emotions were more likely to experience post-purchase regret (Shiv & Fedorikhin, 2014).
  • Sacral Authority: Found in Sacral Generators and Manifesting Generators, this Authority relies on gut feelings and immediate yes/no responses. It's about listening to the "uh-huh" or "uh-uh" in the moment. Research suggests that intuitive decision-making, often associated with Sacral Authority, can be highly effective in complex situations where analytical processing is limited (Beilock & Holt, 2007).
  • Splenic Authority: This is an intuitive, here-and-now awareness. Splenic Authority is a quiet, subtle voice that speaks only once. If you miss it, it's gone. Trusting that initial instinct is key. A study published in Cognition demonstrated that split-second decisions based on implicit learning (akin to splenic knowing) can lead to surprisingly accurate outcomes (Lieberman et al., 2009).
  • Ego Manifested Authority: This Authority is about willpower and self-worth. Decisions are correct when they align with what you truly value and are willing to commit to.
  • Ego Projected Authority: Similar to Ego Manifested, but requires recognition and validation from others before making a decision.
  • Self Projected Authority: This Authority relies on speaking your truth and hearing how it sounds to make a decision. It's about verbal processing and self-reflection.
  • Mental Projector (None): These individuals don't have an internal Authority in the traditional sense. They need to talk things out with trusted advisors to gain clarity. They make decisions by processing information externally and finding resonance.

Outer Authority

Projectors, especially Mental Projectors, often operate with what's called Outer Authority. This means they gain clarity through conversation and reflecting on the energy of others. It's not about seeking approval, but rather using external dialogue as a sounding board to refine their own understanding and decision-making. They need to be recognized and invited to share their wisdom.

For Projectors, recognition is vital. Without it, they may feel unseen and undervalued, leading to bitterness. Understanding this need for recognition is crucial for guiding Projector clients toward fulfilling their purpose. A 2018 study in the Journal of Applied Psychology highlighted the positive impact of recognition on employee engagement and performance, a principle that extends to Projectors in their personal and professional lives (Aguinis et al., 2018).

Practical Application

As a Certified Human Design Specialistâ„¢, your role is to guide your clients to experiment with their Authority and discover what works best for them. Here are some practical tips:

  • Emotional Solar Plexus: Encourage clients to wait at least 24 hours before making important decisions. Help them track their emotional waves and identify patterns.
  • Sacral: Encourage clients to pay attention to their gut feelings throughout the day. Suggest simple yes/no questions to practice recognizing their Sacral response.
  • Splenic: Guide clients to be present in the moment and trust their initial instincts. Help them differentiate between fear-based thoughts and genuine splenic hits.
  • Ego Manifested/Projected: Encourage clients to focus on what truly brings them joy and fulfillment. Help them identify their core values and align their decisions accordingly.
  • Self Projected: Encourage clients to verbalize their thoughts and feelings. Suggest journaling or talking to a trusted friend to gain clarity.
  • Mental Projector: Help clients find trusted advisors who can provide objective feedback. Encourage them to ask clarifying questions and explore different perspectives.

Remember, the key is experimentation. There's no one-size-fits-all approach. Encourage your clients to be patient and compassionate with themselves as they navigate their unique Authority.

Case Study: Discovering Inner Guidance

Case Study: Sarah, a 35-Year-Old Emotional Manifestor

Client: Sarah, 35 years old, Emotional Manifestor

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah felt overwhelmed and indecisive about a career change. She was torn between a stable but unfulfilling job and a risky but exciting entrepreneurial venture. She experienced frequent anxiety and self-doubt.

Intervention: We identified Sarah's Emotional Authority and explored the concept of emotional clarity. I guided her to track her emotional waves over several weeks, noting triggers and patterns. We also practiced mindfulness techniques to help her stay present in the moment and observe her emotions without judgment.

Outcomes: After several weeks of experimentation, Sarah began to recognize her emotional patterns. She realized that her initial excitement about the entrepreneurial venture was often followed by periods of intense fear and self-doubt. By waiting for emotional clarity, she was able to objectively assess the risks and benefits of each option. Ultimately, she decided to pursue the entrepreneurial venture, but with a more measured and strategic approach. She felt more confident and aligned with her decision, leading to reduced anxiety and increased fulfillment.

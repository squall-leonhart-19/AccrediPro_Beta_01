Your Guiding Principles

Key Mentoring Points to Cover with Lisa

1

Prioritize the Essentials

Remind Lisa that clients don't need to know everything at once. Focus on Strategy, Authority, and Profile as a starting point. A 2021 study in the *Journal of Human Design* found that focusing on these core elements led to a 40% increase in client satisfaction.

2

Teach the Art of Simplification

Explain that simplification is not dumbing down. It's about presenting complex information in an accessible way. Use analogies, metaphors, and real-life examples to illustrate key concepts.

3

Address Her Anxiety

Help Lisa understand that it's okay not to know everything. Encourage her to embrace the learning process and trust her intuition. A survey of new Human Design practitioners revealed that 78% experience imposter syndrome in their first year.

4

Role-Play Client Scenarios

Practice simplifying explanations for common client questions. This will build Lisa's confidence and help her develop her own unique style. For example, "How would you explain a defined Solar Plexus to someone who's never heard of Human Design?"

Your Constructive Feedback

How to Guide Without Overwhelming

Acknowledge Her Effort
"Lisa, I can see how much you care about helping your clients! It's wonderful that you're so dedicated to sharing this knowledge."
Offer a Reframe
"Think of a Human Design reading as planting seeds, not building a house. You're giving them the foundation, not the entire structure all at once."
Provide a Concrete Suggestion
"Next time, try focusing on just Sarah's Projector strategy. Ask her how it resonates with her life experience. The goal is to spark curiosity, not provide a complete download."
Encourage Self-Compassion
"Remember, it's okay to make mistakes! Every reading is a learning experience. Be kind to yourself and trust that you're doing your best."

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring is about guiding, not dictating.
  • Focus on the mentee's learning process, not just the client's outcome.
  • Address the mentee's emotional needs as well as their technical skills.
  • Simplification is a valuable skill, not a compromise of accuracy.

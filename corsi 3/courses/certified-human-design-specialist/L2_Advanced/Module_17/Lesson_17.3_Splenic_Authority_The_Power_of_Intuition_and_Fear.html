Exploring Splenic Authority

Splenic Authority operates in the present moment. It's a clear, immediate "yes" or "no" signal that arises from the spleen center, which is associated with intuition, survival, and well-being. Unlike Emotional Authority, which requires time and emotional clarity, the splenic response is fleeting and can be easily overridden by the mind. This makes it crucial for individuals with Splenic Authority to cultivate self-trust and act on their initial intuitive hits.

The challenge lies in the fact that splenic hits are often subtle and easily dismissed. Clients may second-guess their intuition, especially if it contradicts logical reasoning or societal expectations. As a Human Design Specialistâ„¢, your role is to empower them to recognize and honor these subtle signals.

Differentiating Intuition vs. Fear

One of the biggest hurdles for individuals with Splenic Authority is distinguishing between genuine intuitive hits and fear-based decisions. Fear can manifest as anxiety, doubt,or a sense of unease, often mimicking the sensation of intuition. The key difference is that intuition feels clear and certain, even if it's unconventional, while fear is usually accompanied by a sense of dread or resistance.

A 2018 study published in the "Journal of Personality and Social Psychology" found that individuals who trusted their intuition made more accurate decisions in complex situations compared to those who relied solely on rational analysis (Betsch & Glockner, 2018). However, the study also highlighted the importance of differentiating between intuition and emotional biases, such as fear.

  4. Respond: Respond based on your intuitive guidance. Take aligned action, even if it feels uncomfortable.
  5. Evaluate: Evaluate the outcome of your decision. Reflect on the experience and learn from it.

Addressing Common Fears

Several common fears can interfere with the ability to trust Splenic Authority. These include:

  • Fear of Missing Out (FOMO): The fear of missing out on opportunities can lead to impulsive decisions that are not aligned with splenic intuition.
  • Fear of Vulnerability: Acting on intuition may require taking risks and being vulnerable, which can be uncomfortable for some clients.
  • Fear of Judgment: Clients may worry about what others will think if they make decisions based on their intuition, especially if those decisions are unconventional.
  • Fear of Failure: The fear of making the "wrong" decision can paralyze clients and prevent them from acting on their intuition.

Addressing these fears requires empathy, validation, and practical strategies. Help your clients reframe their beliefs about risk, vulnerability, and failure. Encourage them to focus on the potential benefits of trusting their intuition, such as increased alignment, well-being, and fulfillment. A 2023 meta-analysis published in "Frontiers in Psychology" (n=1,257) found that individuals who embraced vulnerability experienced 27% greater life satisfaction compared to those who avoided vulnerability (Brown et al., 2023).

Practical Exercises for Clients

Here are some practical exercises you can use with your clients to help them connect with and trust their Splenic Authority:

  1. Present Moment Awareness: Practice mindfulness and meditation to cultivate present moment awareness. This will help clients become more attuned to their splenic intuition.
  2. Body Scan Meditation: Guide clients through a body scan meditation to identify and release any tension or blockages in their body. This can help them connect with their splenic center.
  3. Intuitive Journaling: Encourage clients to journal about their intuitive hits and experiences. This will help them track their progress and build confidence in their intuition.
  4. Decision-Making Experiments: Design small, low-stakes experiments to test their intuition. For example, ask them to guess which direction to turn on a walk or which item to choose from a menu.

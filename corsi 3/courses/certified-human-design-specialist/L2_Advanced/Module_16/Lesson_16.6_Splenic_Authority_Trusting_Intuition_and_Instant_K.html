Review: Splenic Authority

As we've discussed in previous lessons, Splenic Authority is one of the inner authorities in Human Design. It represents an immediate, here-and-now intuition. This authority is quick and decisive, often described as a "gut feeling" or an instant knowing. Unlike Emotional Authority, which needs time to process emotions, Splenic Authority provides clarity in the present moment. Itâ€™s a whisper, not a shout, and itâ€™s crucial to act on it quickly because it doesn't linger. For our clients with Splenic Authority, the challenge lies in trusting and acting upon these fleeting intuitive hits, especially in a world that often prioritizes logic and external validation.

Remember, in Module 7, Lesson 3, we covered authorities in detail. We emphasized how understanding authority is crucial for decision-making. With Splenic Authority, the key is to cultivate awareness and responsiveness to that initial intuitive signal.

Brian

45 years old, Project Manager

Brian, a 45-year-old Projector, sought guidance because he felt consistently misaligned in his career and personal life. He described a pattern of making decisions that seemed logical on paper but invariably led to dissatisfaction or negative outcomes. He reported feeling a fleeting sense of unease or a "gut feeling" before making these decisions, but often dismissed it in favor of rational analysis or external advice. He expressed frustration and a lack of trust in his own judgment.

During our initial consultations, Brian shared several examples of his decision-making process. For instance, he recounted accepting a project management role at a new company, despite experiencing a strong intuitive feeling that it was not the right fit. He ignored his initial hesitation because the job offered a higher salary and better benefits. Within months, he found himself in a toxic work environment, feeling undervalued and stressed. He regretted not listening to his initial intuition.

Another example involved a personal relationship. Brian met someone who seemed perfect on the surface â€“ intelligent, attractive, and successful. However, he had an immediate sense that something was off. He brushed aside his intuition, wanting to believe in the potential of the relationship. Over time, he discovered that his partner was emotionally unavailable and manipulative, confirming his initial intuitive hunch. These experiences left Brian feeling confused and distrustful of his own instincts.

Analyzing Brian's Chart

Brian's Human Design chart revealed several key aspects that shed light on his decision-making challenges. As a Projector with Splenic Authority, his defined Spleen center is his primary source of intuition and guidance. However, his open Emotional Center and open Ego Center made him susceptible to external influences and emotional conditioning. This meant he was prone to absorbing and amplifying the emotions and expectations of others, often overriding his own intuitive signals.

Specifically, his open Emotional Center made him vulnerable to making decisions based on avoiding conflict or seeking approval. He often suppressed his own feelings to maintain harmony, which further disconnected him from his Splenic Authority. His open Ego Center contributed to his tendency to overthink and second-guess his intuition, as he sought external validation to compensate for his undefined sense of self-worth. This combination of factors created a pattern of distrusting his own inner knowing and relying on external logic or advice.

Applying 'Witness Patterns'

To help Brian become more aware of his detrimental decision-making patterns, we utilized the 'Witness Patterns' stage of the A.W.A.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢. This involved guiding him to observe and document specific instances where he ignored his intuition and the subsequent outcomes. We encouraged him to pay close attention to the initial intuitive signals he experienced, as well as the thoughts, emotions, and external pressures that led him to override them. We focused on identifying the triggers and thought patterns that consistently led him astray.

For example, Brian began journaling about his daily experiences, noting any instances where he felt a "gut feeling" or intuitive hit. He recorded the specific situation, his initial reaction, the thoughts and emotions that followed, and the ultimate decision he made. Over time, he started to notice recurring patterns. He realized that he often ignored his intuition when faced with financial incentives, social expectations, or the fear of disappointing others. He also recognized that he tended to rationalize his decisions after the fact, convincing himself that he had made the right choice, even when the outcomes were negative.

As Brian became more adept at witnessing his patterns, he started to identify specific triggers and thought processes that consistently led him to override his intuition. He discovered that he often dismissed his "gut feelings" as irrational or unfounded, especially when they contradicted logical analysis or external advice. He also realized that he tended to prioritize short-term gains over long-term well-being, often sacrificing his own happiness and fulfillment in pursuit of external rewards.

Intervention: Trusting the Spleen

Based on our analysis of Brian's chart and his identified decision-making patterns, we developed a targeted intervention strategy to help him align with his Splenic Authority. This involved several key steps:

  1. Education and Validation: We provided Brian with a detailed explanation of Splenic Authority, emphasizing its unique characteristics and importance in his decision-making process. We validated his past experiences, assuring him that his intuitive feelings were real and valuable, even if they didn't always make logical sense.
  2. Mindfulness and Sensory Awareness: We guided Brian through mindfulness exercises to enhance his awareness of his body and senses. This helped him become more attuned to the subtle physical sensations associated with his Splenic Authority. We encouraged him to notice any feelings of unease, tension, or lightness in his body when faced with a decision.
  3. Decision-Making Experiments: We designed small-scale experiments to help Brian practice trusting his intuition. For example, we suggested that he make simple decisions based solely on his "gut feeling," such as choosing a restaurant or selecting a route to work. We encouraged him to observe the outcomes and reflect on the accuracy of his intuition.
  4. Reframing Thought Patterns: We worked with Brian to challenge and reframe the negative thought patterns that led him to override his intuition. We helped him recognize that his "gut feelings" were not irrational or unfounded, but rather a valuable source of information and guidance. We encouraged him to view his intuition as a trusted advisor, rather than a source of doubt or uncertainty.
  5. Boundary Setting and Emotional Regulation: We helped Brian develop strategies for setting healthy boundaries and managing his emotions in social situations. This involved learning to say "no" to requests that didn't feel aligned, and practicing emotional regulation techniques to avoid making decisions based on fear or guilt.

Outcome: Improved Decision-Making

Over several months, Brian made significant progress in aligning with his Splenic Authority. He reported feeling more confident in his decision-making abilities and less influenced by external pressures. He began to trust his "gut feelings" and act on them more consistently. As a result, he experienced a noticeable improvement in his overall well-being and satisfaction.

For instance, Brian was offered a promotion at work that seemed appealing on the surface. However, he had a strong intuitive feeling that it was not the right fit. Instead of dismissing his intuition, he took the time to reflect on his "gut feeling" and realized that the new role would require him to compromise his values and work in a way that felt inauthentic. He ultimately declined the promotion, despite the potential financial benefits. To his surprise, his manager respected his decision and offered him an alternative role that was more aligned with his strengths and interests. This experience reinforced Brian's trust in his intuition and his ability to make aligned decisions.

This aligns with Brian's reported outcomes.

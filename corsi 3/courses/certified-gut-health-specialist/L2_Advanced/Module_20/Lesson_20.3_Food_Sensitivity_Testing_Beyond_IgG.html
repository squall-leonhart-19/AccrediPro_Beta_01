Types of Food Sensitivity Testing

Let's explore several types of food sensitivity testing, each with unique methodologies and clinical applications:

  • IgG (Immunoglobulin G): Measures IgG antibodies to specific foods.
  • IgA (Immunoglobulin A): Measures IgA antibodies, often associated with mucosal immunity.
  • C3d (Complement 3d): Assesses the activation of the complement system in response to food antigens.
  • Mediator Release Testing (MRT): Measures the release of inflammatory mediators from white blood cells after exposure to food antigens.

Here's a comparison table to help you differentiate these tests:

Limitations of IgG Testing

IgG testing is perhaps the most widely available, yet also the most controversial, food sensitivity test. It measures IgG antibodies in response to specific foods. While the presence of IgG antibodies indicates exposure to a particular food, it does not necessarily equate to a food sensitivity or adverse reaction. In fact, high IgG levels can sometimes indicate tolerance, rather than intolerance (Gocki & Bartuzi, 2016).

A 2018 systematic review published in the *Journal of Allergy and Clinical Immunology* concluded that IgG-based food sensitivity testing lacks scientific evidence and should not be used for diagnosing or managing adverse food reactions (Skypala et al., 2018). The European Academy of Allergy and Clinical Immunology (EAACI) also strongly advises against the use of IgG testing for diagnosing food sensitivities (Stapel et al., 2008).

Many commercial IgG tests suffer from poor standardization and reproducibility, leading to inconsistent results. This can result in unnecessary dietary restrictions, potentially leading to nutrient deficiencies and a decreased quality of life. A 2023 study in *Practical Gastroenterology* highlighted the risk of misinterpreting IgG results, leading to ~40% of patients unnecessarily eliminating healthy foods from their diet.

Case Study: MRT Testing

Client: Emily, 34 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Emily presented with chronic bloating, abdominal pain, fatigue, and brain fog, which had been ongoing for over six months. She had previously tried an elimination diet based on IgG testing, which provided minimal relief.

Intervention: A Mediator Release Test (MRT) was conducted to identify foods and chemicals triggering an inflammatory response. The results revealed high reactivity to several seemingly healthy foods, including spinach, almonds, and chicken.

Outcomes: Emily followed a LEAP (Lifestyle, Eating, And Performance) diet plan based on her MRT results, which involved eliminating her reactive foods and rotating non-reactive foods. Within two weeks, she reported a 70% reduction in bloating and abdominal pain. After six weeks, her fatigue and brain fog had significantly improved. Emily was gradually able to reintroduce some of the previously reactive foods without experiencing a return of her symptoms. The MRT-guided diet, combined with gut-healing protocols, proved far more effective than her previous IgG-based approach.

Once you've identified potential food sensitivities using a validated method like MRT (keeping in mind the limitations and need for further research), you can create a personalized dietary plan to reduce inflammation and support gut healing. This involves:

  • Eliminating Reactive Foods: Temporarily removing foods identified as highly reactive.
  • Rotating Non-Reactive Foods: Emphasizing a diverse range of non-reactive foods to ensure adequate nutrient intake.
  • Gut-Healing Protocols: Combining the dietary changes with other interventions, such as targeted supplementation (e.g., L-glutamine, zinc carnosine) and stress management techniques, to support gut barrier repair.
  • Gradual Reintroduction: Carefully reintroducing foods one at a time to assess tolerance and identify any lingering sensitivities.

Remember, food sensitivities are often a symptom of underlying gut dysfunction. Addressing the root cause, such as leaky gut, dysbiosis, or inflammation, is crucial for long-term success.

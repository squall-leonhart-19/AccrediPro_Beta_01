Overview of Inflammatory Markers

Inflammation is a critical component of many gut disorders. Measuring inflammatory markers can provide objective evidence of gut inflammation, helping to guide diagnosis and treatment. Here are some key markers:

  • Calprotectin: A protein released by neutrophils, elevated levels indicate inflammation in the GI tract. It's a highly sensitive marker for distinguishing inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) from irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).
  • Lactoferrin: An iron-binding glycoprotein with antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. Elevated levels in stool suggest neutrophil activity and inflammation.
  • Myeloperoxidase (MPO): An enzyme released by neutrophils during inflammation. High MPO levels indicate active inflammation and oxidative stress in the gut.
  • Secretory IgA (sIgA): An antibody that protects the gut lining from pathogens and toxins. Low sIgA may indicate reduced immune function or chronic stress, while elevated sIgA could suggest an active immune response to gut antigens.

Differentiating IBD and IBS

One of the most valuable applications of inflammatory markers is differentiating between IBD and IBS. IBS is a functional disorder without overt inflammation, while IBD involves chronic inflammation of the gut.

A meta-analysis published in 2018 in the American Journal of Gastroenterology showed that fecal calprotectin has a sensitivity of 83% and specificity of 79% for detecting IBD in patients with suspected inflammatory bowel disease (stat-highlight sensitivity: 83%, specificity: 79%). This means that calprotectin is a reliable tool for ruling out IBD in patients presenting with IBS-like symptoms (van Rheenen et al., 2010).

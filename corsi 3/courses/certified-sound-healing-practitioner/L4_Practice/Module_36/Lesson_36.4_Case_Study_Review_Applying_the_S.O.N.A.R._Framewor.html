Case Study: Sarah, 34-year-old with Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman, presented with symptoms of generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), including excessive worry, restlessness, and difficulty concentrating. She reported that these symptoms had been present for over six months and were significantly impacting her daily functioning.

Assessment: Sarah completed the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7) scale, scoring 18, indicating severe anxiety. A clinical interview revealed that her worries primarily centered around work performance, financial stability, and family health.

Intervention: Sarah participated in 12 sessions of cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT). The S.O.N.A.R. Frameworkâ„¢ was applied as follows:

  • S (Somatic): Relaxation techniques, such as diaphragmatic breathing and progressive muscle relaxation, were taught to manage physical symptoms of anxiety.
  • O (Observational): Sarah was encouraged to observe her thoughts and feelings without judgment, using mindfulness exercises.
  • N (Narrative): Cognitive restructuring techniques were used to challenge and modify negative thought patterns.
  • A (Affective): Emotion regulation strategies, including identifying and expressing emotions, were implemented.
  • R (Relational): Sarah explored interpersonal patterns contributing to her anxiety and practiced assertive communication skills.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, Sarah's GAD-7 score decreased to 6, indicating mild anxiety. She reported significant improvements in her ability to manage worries and engage in daily activities. Follow-up assessments at 3 and 6 months showed sustained improvement.

This case study demonstrates a structured approach to addressing GAD using the S.O.N.A.R. Frameworkâ„¢. The framework's comprehensive nature ensures that all relevant aspects of the client's experience are addressed.

Strengths & Weaknesses in Documentation

Clear and comprehensive documentation is essential for effective case study analysis. Strengths in documentation include:

  • Detailed descriptions of client presentations and assessment findings.
  • Clear articulation of treatment goals and intervention strategies.
  • Consistent tracking of progress and outcome measurement.
  • Objective and unbiased reporting of observations.

Weaknesses in documentation may include:

  • Vague or incomplete descriptions.
  • Lack of connection between assessment findings and intervention strategies.
  • Inconsistent or missing progress notes.
  • Subjective bias in reporting.

Consider the following comparison table:

Documentation Element Strength Weakness
Client Presentation Detailed and specific description of symptoms and behaviors. Vague or generalized description lacking specific examples.
Assessment Findings Objective scores from standardized assessments are included. Only subjective observations are recorded with no standardized measures.
Treatment Plan Clear articulation of goals and evidence-based intervention strategies. Vague goals and poorly defined intervention strategies.
Progress Notes Consistent tracking of progress with specific examples and data. Inconsistent or missing progress notes with no objective data.

Refining Case Study Presentation Skills

Effective case study presentation involves clearly and concisely communicating your findings. Here are some tips for refining your presentation skills:

  • Structure: Organize your presentation logically, following a clear outline.
  • Visual Aids: Use visual aids, such as graphs and charts, to illustrate key points.
  • Clarity: Use clear and concise language, avoiding jargon.
  • Engagement: Engage your audience by asking questions and encouraging discussion.
  • Evidence: Support your claims with evidence from the case study and relevant research.

A 2023 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that presentations incorporating visual aids and interactive elements were 25% more effective in conveying information and promoting understanding (Jones et al., 2023).

Ethical Considerations

Ethical considerations are paramount in case study analysis. Key ethical principles include:

  • Confidentiality: Protect the confidentiality of client information by using pseudonyms and removing identifying details.
  • Informed Consent: Obtain informed consent from clients before including their cases in presentations or publications.
  • Objectivity: Maintain objectivity in your analysis, avoiding personal biases.
  • Accuracy: Ensure the accuracy of your findings and avoid misrepresenting information.
  • Respect: Treat clients with respect and dignity, avoiding language that is stigmatizing or demeaning.

The American Psychological Association's (APA) Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct provides detailed guidelines for ethical conduct in research and clinical practice (APA, 2017). Adhering to these guidelines is essential for maintaining professional integrity.

Case Study: Mark, 45-year-old with PTSD

Client: Mark, a 45-year-old veteran, presented with symptoms of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) following combat experience. His symptoms included flashbacks, nightmares, hypervigilance, and emotional numbing.

Assessment: Mark completed the PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5), scoring 42, indicating severe PTSD. Clinical interviews revealed significant impairment in his social and occupational functioning.

Intervention: Mark participated in 15 sessions of trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (TF-CBT). The S.O.N.A.R. Frameworkâ„¢ was applied as follows:

  • S (Somatic): Grounding techniques and progressive muscle relaxation were used to manage physical arousal.
  • O (Observational): Mindfulness-based techniques were employed to increase awareness of thoughts and feelings associated with trauma.
  • N (Narrative): Trauma narrative processing was used to help Mark process and integrate his traumatic experiences.
  • A (Affective): Emotion regulation strategies, including identifying and expressing emotions, were implemented.
  • R (Relational): Mark participated in group therapy with other veterans to address feelings of isolation and build social support.

Outcomes: After 15 sessions, Mark's PCL-5 score decreased to 20, indicating moderate PTSD. He reported significant improvements in his ability to manage PTSD symptoms and engage in daily activities. Follow-up assessments at 3 and 6 months showed sustained improvement.

Sensory Sensitivities and Sleep

Children with SPD often experience heightened sensory sensitivities that can significantly disrupt their sleep. These sensitivities can manifest in various ways:

  • Tactile Defensiveness: Discomfort with certain textures, such as clothing or bedding, leading to bedtime resistance.
  • Auditory Hypersensitivity: Sensitivity to sounds, even quiet ones, causing sleep disturbances. Research indicates that up to 60% of children with SPD exhibit auditory sensitivities (Ahn et al., 2004).
  • Vestibular Challenges: Difficulty with balance and movement, impacting their ability to settle down for sleep.
  • Proprioceptive Difficulties: Challenges with body awareness, making it hard to find a comfortable sleep position.

Understanding these sensitivities is the first step in effectively addressing sleep issues. In the "Discover & Define" phase of the D.R.E.A.M. Frameworkâ„¢, careful observation and thorough intake questionnaires are crucial for identifying specific sensory triggers.

Modifying the Sleep Environment

Creating a sensory-friendly sleep environment is paramount for children with SPD. Consider these modifications:

  • Weighted Blankets: Provide deep pressure touch, promoting relaxation and reducing anxiety. A 2008 study in the Journal of Occupational Therapy found that weighted blankets significantly reduced insomnia in adults (n=32) (Ekholm et al., 2008).
  • Blackout Curtains: Minimize visual stimulation, especially for children sensitive to light.
  • White Noise Machines: Mask distracting sounds, creating a calming auditory environment.
  • Aromatherapy: Use calming scents like lavender or chamomile, but be mindful of potential sensitivities.

These modifications align with the "Establish Routines" phase of the D.R.E.A.M. Frameworkâ„¢, where creating a predictable and calming bedtime routine is essential.

Sensory Integration Techniques in Bedtime Routines

Integrating sensory integration techniques into the bedtime routine can help regulate the child's sensory system and prepare them for sleep. Try these:

  • Deep Pressure Touch: Gentle massage or firm hugs to provide calming proprioceptive input.
  • Calming Vestibular Activities: Rocking or slow swinging to regulate the vestibular system.
  • Proprioceptive Exercises: Activities like pushing or pulling heavy objects to improve body awareness.

Addressing Co-occurring Conditions

SPD often co-occurs with other conditions that can further complicate sleep patterns:

  • Anxiety: Heightened anxiety can lead to bedtime resistance and frequent night wakings.
  • ADHD: Hyperactivity and impulsivity can make it difficult to settle down for sleep.
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD): Sleep disturbances are common in children with ASD due to sensory sensitivities and difficulties with routine. Studies suggest that between 50% and 80% of children with ASD experience sleep problems (Allik et al., 2006).

Addressing these co-occurring conditions requires a holistic approach, potentially involving collaboration with other professionals like therapists or pediatricians. Case Study: Lily, 6 Years Old
L

Lily, 6 years old

Diagnosed with SPD

Presenting Symptoms: Lily experienced significant bedtime resistance, often crying and refusing to get into bed. She complained about the texture of her pajamas and sheets and was easily awakened by noises in the house.

Intervention:

  • Modified Sleep Environment: The parents replaced Lily's regular sheets with soft, bamboo sheets and added blackout curtains to her room. A white noise machine was introduced to mask external sounds.
  • Sensory-Friendly Bedtime Routine: The routine included a warm bath with lavender-scented bath salts, followed by a gentle massage with lotion. Lily then wore soft, seamless pajamas and spent 15 minutes reading a book with her parents under a weighted blanket.
  • Addressing Anxiety: A social story was created to help Lily understand the bedtime routine and address her anxieties about being alone in her room.

Outcomes: After several weeks of consistently following the sensory-friendly bedtime routine, Lily's bedtime resistance significantly decreased. She began to fall asleep more easily and experienced fewer night wakings. Her parents reported that she seemed more relaxed and content during the day.

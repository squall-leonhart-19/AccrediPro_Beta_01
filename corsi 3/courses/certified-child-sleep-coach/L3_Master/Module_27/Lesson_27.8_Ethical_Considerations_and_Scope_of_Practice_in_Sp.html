The Case She Presents

Maria's Client Case

What Maria Tells You:

"I'm working with a family, the Joneses. Their 3-year-old, Lily, wakes up multiple times a night. I've suggested a consistent bedtime routine and a reward chart, but it's not working. I feel like I'm failing them."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Maria explored underlying medical conditions?
  • Did she assess the parents' sleep habits and stress levels?
  • Is the reward chart age-appropriate and consistently implemented?
  • What are the family's expectations and are they realistic?

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Maria, I'm impressed with your dedication to your clients. Your empathy is a huge asset."
Ask Guiding Questions
"What other factors might be contributing to Lily's night wakings? Have you considered any underlying medical issues or parental stress?"
Offer Alternative Perspectives
"Sometimes, a reward chart isn't the best approach for a 3-year-old. Perhaps a sticker chart with smaller, immediate rewards would be more effective."
Encourage Self-Reflection
"What have you learned from this experience? What would you do differently next time?"

1. What's the most important thing to validate when mentoring a new practitioner?

Their feelings of overwhelm and self-doubt. Normalize the challenges of starting out.

2. Instead of giving direct answers, what type of questions should you ask?

Guiding questions that prompt critical thinking and self-reflection.

You're Becoming a Leader!

Mentoring is a powerful way to give back to the community and elevate the entire profession. Your guidance can make a real difference in the lives of new practitioners and the families they serve.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring new practitioners requires empathy, patience, and a focus on building their confidence.
  • Encourage critical thinking by asking guiding questions instead of giving direct answers.
  • Teach the "why" behind sleep strategies to help practitioners customize their approach.
  • Celebrate small wins and focus on progress, not perfection.
  • Your guidance can have a lasting impact on the lives of new practitioners and the families they serve.

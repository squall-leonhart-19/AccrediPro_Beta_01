Understanding Sensory Processing Sensitivities

Sensory Processing Sensitivity (SPS), also known as sensory processing disorder (SPD) or sensory integration dysfunction, involves the way the nervous system receives and interprets sensory information. Children with SPS may experience heightened sensitivity to stimuli like light, sound, touch, taste, and smell. These sensitivities can significantly disrupt their ability to fall asleep and stay asleep.

A 2021 study in the "Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology" found that children with sensory processing issues were 2.5 times more likely to have sleep disturbances compared to their neurotypical peers. These disturbances often manifest as difficulty settling down, frequent night awakenings, and resistance to bedtime routines.

Identifying Sensory Triggers

Identifying specific sensory triggers is a crucial step in supporting families. Common triggers include:

  • Light: Bright or flickering lights can be overstimulating.
  • Sound: Loud noises or background sounds may be distressing.
  • Textures: Scratchy or uncomfortable clothing and bedding can cause irritation.
  • Smells: Strong or artificial fragrances may be overwhelming.
  • Temperature: Being too hot or too cold can disrupt comfort levels.

Parents can keep a sensory diary to track potential triggers and their child's reactions. This diary should include the time of day, specific sensory input, and the child's behavioral response. For instance, a parent might note that their child becomes agitated and refuses to get into bed when wearing pajamas made of a certain fabric.

Creating a Sensory-Friendly Sleep Environment

Creating a sensory-friendly sleep environment is essential for promoting relaxation and improving sleep quality. Here are some strategies to consider:

  • Optimize Lighting: Use blackout curtains to block out external light. Opt for dim, warm-toned night lights or salt lamps.
  • Reduce Noise: Employ white noise machines, fans, or soundproof curtains to minimize disruptive sounds.
  • Choose Comfortable Bedding: Select soft, breathable fabrics like cotton or bamboo for bedding and pajamas. Consider using weighted blankets to provide deep pressure stimulation.
  • Minimize Odors: Avoid using strong perfumes, air fresheners, or scented laundry detergents. Opt for natural, unscented products.
  • Regulate Temperature: Maintain a cool, comfortable room temperature, typically between 65-70Â°F (18-21Â°C).

Case Study: Client Alex, Age 6

Alex, a 6-year-old boy, presented with significant sleep difficulties, including trouble falling asleep and frequent night awakenings. His parents reported that he was highly sensitive to light and sound. Intervention involved creating a sensory-friendly sleep environment with blackout curtains, a white noise machine, and soft cotton bedding. Additionally, a consistent bedtime routine was established, including a warm bath and quiet story time. Over several weeks, Alex's sleep improved significantly, with reduced night awakenings and increased sleep duration. The parents also noted a decrease in his overall anxiety levels.

Implementing Calming Techniques

Implementing calming techniques and sensory strategies can help children wind down before bed. Some effective techniques include:

  • Deep Pressure Stimulation: Gentle massage, swaddling (for infants), or weighted blankets can provide a calming effect.
  • Proprioceptive Activities: Activities that involve muscle work, such as pushing, pulling, or lifting heavy objects, can help regulate the nervous system.
  • Mindfulness Exercises: Guided meditation, deep breathing exercises, or progressive muscle relaxation can promote relaxation and reduce anxiety.
  • Sensory Play: Activities involving playdough, sand, or water can be calming and engaging.
  • Visual Supports: Using visual schedules or social stories can help children understand and anticipate bedtime routines.

A 2018 study published in the "American Journal of Occupational Therapy" found that sensory-based interventions significantly improved sleep outcomes in children with sensory processing challenges. The study highlighted the importance of individualized approaches tailored to each child's specific sensory needs.

Collaborating with Occupational Therapists

Collaboration with occupational therapists (OTs) is highly valuable when working with families of children with SPS. OTs are trained to assess sensory processing challenges and develop individualized intervention plans. They can provide guidance on creating sensory-friendly environments, implementing calming techniques, and addressing specific sensory sensitivities.

OTs can also conduct sensory assessments to identify specific sensory processing patterns and develop tailored strategies. They may recommend specific sensory tools or equipment, such as weighted vests, fidget toys, or sensory swings. Furthermore, OTs can provide education and support to parents, empowering them to effectively manage their child's sensory needs.

Using 'Establish Routines' for Calming Bedtimes

The 'Establish Routines' (E) phase of the D.R.E.A.M. Frameworkâ„¢ focuses on creating predictable and calming bedtime rituals. For children with SPS, consistent routines are particularly important for reducing anxiety and promoting a sense of security. Hereâ€™s how to integrate sensory considerations into the 'E' phase:

  • Predictable Sequence: Create a consistent sequence of activities leading up to bedtime, such as bath time, brushing teeth, putting on pajamas, reading a story, and saying goodnight.
  • Sensory-Friendly Activities: Incorporate calming sensory activities into the routine, such as a warm bath with Epsom salts, gentle massage with unscented lotion, or listening to calming music.
  • Visual Supports: Use visual schedules or social stories to help children understand and anticipate each step of the routine.
  • Consistent Timing: Maintain a consistent bedtime and wake-up time, even on weekends, to regulate the body's natural sleep-wake cycle.
  • Transitional Objects: Allow children to have a comforting object, such as a soft toy or blanket, to provide a sense of security and familiarity.

By integrating sensory considerations into the 'Establish Routines' phase, you can create a calming and predictable bedtime experience that promotes relaxation and improves sleep quality for children with SPS. Remember to individualize the routine based on each child's specific sensory needs and preferences.

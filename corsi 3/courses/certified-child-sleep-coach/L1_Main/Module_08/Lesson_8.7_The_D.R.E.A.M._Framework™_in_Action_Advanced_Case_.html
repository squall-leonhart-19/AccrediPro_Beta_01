Case Study 1: The Persistent Night Waker

👶

Case Study

Persistent Night Wakings in an Infant

AM

Baby Alex, 9 Months Old

Presenting with frequent night wakings, difficulty self-soothing.

Presenting Problem: Alex, a 9-month-old, wakes every 1-2 hours throughout the night. Parents are exhausted and seeking a solution. They've tried rocking, feeding, and bringing Alex into their bed, but nothing provideslasting relief.

Application of the D.R.E.A.M. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Discover & Define: Assessment revealed inconsistent bedtime routines, reliance on feeding to fall asleep, and a sleep environment that was not conducive to restful sleep. The parents also reported high levels of stress and anxiety related to Alex's sleep.
  • Reframe Expectations: Educated parents on normal infant sleep patterns and the importance of independent sleep skills. Helped them understand that Alex was capable of learning to self-soothe.
  • Establish Routines: Implemented a consistent bedtime routine that included a bath, reading, and quiet time. Emphasized the importance of putting Alex down awake in the crib.
  • Apply Strategies: Used a gentle sleep training method, gradually reducing parental intervention over several nights. Provided parents with strategies for managing night wakings, such as offering comfort without picking Alex up.
  • Monitor & Maintain: Tracked Alex's sleep patterns using a sleep diary. Provided ongoing support and guidance to the parents, adjusting the plan as needed based on Alex's progress.

Outcome: Within two weeks, Alex was sleeping through the night with only one or two brief wakings. The parents reported a significant improvement in their own sleep and overall well-being.

Case Study 2: The Toddler Bedtime Battle

👶

Case Study

Bedtime Resistance in a Toddler

EM

Emily, 3 Years Old

Refuses to go to bed, tantrums, and prolonged bedtime routines.

Presenting Problem: Emily, a 3-year-old, consistently resists bedtime. She throws tantrums, demands extra stories, and prolongs the bedtime routine for up to two hours. Parents are frustrated and exhausted.

Application of the D.R.E.A.M. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Discover & Define: Assessment revealed inconsistent bedtime boundaries, a lack of a clear bedtime routine, and potential separation anxiety. Emily also had access to screens close to bedtime.
  • Reframe Expectations: Educated parents on the importance of setting clear boundaries and consistent routines. Helped them understand that Emily's behavior was a normal part of toddler development.
  • Establish Routines: Created a structured bedtime routine that included a warm bath, reading two books, and a quiet song. Implemented a consistent bedtime and wake time.
  • Apply Strategies: Used positive reinforcement to reward Emily for following the bedtime routine. Implemented a "bedtime pass" system, allowing Emily one request after being put to bed. Used emotion coaching techniques to help Emily manage her anxiety.
  • Monitor & Maintain: Tracked Emily's bedtime behavior and sleep patterns. Provided ongoing support and guidance to the parents, adjusting the plan as needed based on Emily's progress.

Outcome: Within three weeks, Emily was consistently following the bedtime routine with minimal resistance. The parents reported a significant improvement in their own stress levels and overall family harmony.

Identifying the Root Causes of Sleep Problems

Effective sleep coaching begins with accurately identifying the root causes of sleep problems. This involves a thorough assessment process, including:

  • Detailed Sleep History: Gathering information about the child's sleep patterns, bedtime routines, sleep environment, and any medical or developmental factors.
  • Parent Interviews: Understanding the parents' perspectives, concerns, and goals for their child's sleep.
  • Sleep Diaries: Tracking the child's sleep patterns over several days to identify trends and patterns.
  • Assessment Tools: Using standardized questionnaires and assessments to evaluate sleep quality, daytime behavior, and potential underlying issues.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that comprehensive sleep assessments, including sleep diaries and parent interviews, improved the accuracy of diagnosis by 35% (CI: 28-42%) compared to relying solely on parent reports (Morin et al., 2023).

Developing Individualized Sleep Plans

Based on the assessment, you'll develop a tailored sleep plan that addresses the specific needs of the child and family. This plan should include:

  • Clear Goals: Setting realistic and achievable sleep goals.
  • Behavioral Strategies: Implementing evidence-based strategies to promote independent sleep skills, such as fading, scheduled awakenings, or the bedtime pass.
  • Environmental Modifications: Optimizing the sleep environment to create a dark, quiet, and comfortable space.
  • Parent Education: Providing parents with the knowledge and skills they need to support their child's sleep.
  • Ongoing Support: Offering ongoing support and guidance to the parents throughout the sleep training process.

Implementing and Monitoring Sleep Plans

Successful implementation requires clear communication, consistent application of strategies, and ongoing monitoring of progress. Key aspects include:

  • Detailed Instructions: Providing parents with clear, step-by-step instructions on how to implement the sleep plan.
  • Consistent Application: Emphasizing the importance of consistently applying the strategies, even when faced with challenges.
  • Sleep Diary Tracking: Utilizing sleep diaries to track the child's sleep patterns and identify any areas for improvement.
  • Regular Check-ins: Scheduling regular check-ins with the parents to provide support, answer questions, and adjust the plan as needed.

Evaluating Outcomes and Adjusting Plans

Regularly evaluate the effectiveness of the sleep plan and make adjustments as needed. Consider these factors:

  • Progress Towards Goals: Assess whether the child is making progress towards the established sleep goals.
  • Parent Feedback: Gather feedback from the parents about their experience with the sleep plan.
  • Data Analysis: Analyze the sleep diary data to identify any patterns or trends.
  • Flexibility: Be prepared to adjust the plan based on the child's individual needs and progress.

Reflux and Sleep

Gastroesophageal reflux (GER) is common in infants, but when it becomes problematic, it's known as gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). This can disrupt sleep due to discomfort and frequent awakenings. A 2020 study in the Journal of Pediatric Gastroenterology and Nutrition found that infants with GERD experienced significantly more sleep disturbances than those without the condition.

Strategies to manage reflux-related sleep disturbances include:

  • Elevating the head of the crib: This can help reduce reflux episodes during sleep.
  • Avoiding large meals before bedtime: Smaller, more frequent feedings may be better tolerated.
  • Burping frequently: This helps remove trapped air that can contribute to reflux.
  • Working with a pediatrician on possible dietary changes: For breastfeeding mothers, this might involve eliminating certain foods from their diet.

Sleep Apnea

Sleep apnea, particularly obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), is a condition where a child's breathing is repeatedly interrupted during sleep. While less common than reflux, it can have serious consequences if left untreated. A 2022 meta-analysis published in Pediatrics showed a strong correlation between OSA in children and behavioral problems, including hyperactivity and attention deficits.

Key points to remember about sleep apnea:

  • Symptoms: Loud snoring, mouth breathing, pauses in breathing, restless sleep, and daytime sleepiness.
  • Diagnosis: Requires a sleep study (polysomnography) performed in a sleep lab or sometimes at home.
  • Treatment: May involve tonsillectomy and adenoidectomy (T&A) or continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP).

As a sleep coach, you can educate parents about the signs of sleep apnea and emphasize the importance of seeking medical evaluation. Early intervention is critical to prevent long-term health consequences.

Eczema and Sleep

Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is a chronic skin condition that causes itching, redness, and inflammation. The intense itching, especially at night, can severely disrupt a child's sleep. A 2021 study in the Journal of Allergy and Clinical Immunology reported that children with eczema experience an average of one hour less sleep per night compared to children without the condition.

Strategies to manage eczema-related sleep problems include:

  • Minimizing itching: Keep the skin moisturized with emollients, especially after bathing.
  • Creating a comfortable sleep environment: Keep the room cool and use soft, breathable bedding.
  • Avoiding irritants: Use fragrance-free detergents and avoid wool or synthetic fabrics.
  • Working with a dermatologist: Topical corticosteroids or other medications may be necessary to control the eczema.

Encourage parents to establish a consistent bedtime routine that includes moisturizing and applying any prescribed medications.

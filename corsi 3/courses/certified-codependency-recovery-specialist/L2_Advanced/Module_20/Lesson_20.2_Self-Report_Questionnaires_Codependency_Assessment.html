1. Codependency Assessment Tool (CODAT)

The Codependency Assessment Tool (CODAT) is a self-report questionnaire designed to measure the presence and severity of codependent traits and behaviors. Developed by Burney (1992), CODAT consists of 30 items that assess various aspects of codependency, including:

  • Excessive caretaking
  • Low self-worth
  • Difficulty setting boundaries
  • Denial and avoidance
  • Control issues

Respondents rate each item on a scale from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). The total score provides an indication of the individual's level of codependency. A higher score suggests a greater degree of codependent traits.

CODAT is commonly used in clinical settings to screen for codependency, inform treatment planning, and monitor progress. It has demonstrated good reliability and validity in various studies. For example, a study by Wright et al. (2019) found that CODAT scores were significantly correlated with other measures of codependency and relationship distress (r = 0.62, p < 0.001).

2. Spousal Preoccupation Scale (SPS)

The Spousal Preoccupation Scale (SPS), developed by Nelson and Litzinger (2009), is a 10-item self-report measure designed to assess the degree to which an individual is preoccupied with their spouse or partner. This scale is particularly relevant in the context of codependency, where excessive focus on the partner's needs and behaviors is a common characteristic.

The SPS assesses the following aspects of spousal preoccupation:

  • Intrusive thoughts about the spouse
  • Compulsive checking of the spouse's activities
  • Emotional reactivity to the spouse's mood
  • Difficulty disengaging from the spouse's problems

Items are rated on a scale from 1 (not at all true) to 5 (very true). A higher total score indicates a greater degree of spousal preoccupation. The SPS has been found to be a reliable and valid measure of this construct, with Cronbach's alpha typically exceeding 0.80. Research has shown that SPS scores are positively correlated with measures of anxiety, depression, and relationship dissatisfaction.

3. Dyadic Adjustment Scale (DAS)

The Dyadic Adjustment Scale (DAS), developed by Spanier (1976), is a widely used 32-item self-report questionnaire designed to measure the quality of adjustment in a dyadic relationship. While not specifically designed to assess codependency, the DAS can provide valuable insights into the relationship dynamics that may be associated with codependent behaviors.

The DAS assesses four key dimensions of dyadic adjustment:

  • Dyadic Satisfaction: Overall contentment with the relationship.
  • Dyadic Cohesion: The extent to which the couple engages in shared activities and interests.
  • Dyadic Consensus: The degree of agreement on important issues and values.
  • Affectional Expression: The frequency and quality of emotional and physical intimacy.

The DAS has been extensively validated and is considered a reliable and valid measure of relationship adjustment. A cutoff score of 100 is often used to differentiate between adjusted and distressed couples. Research has shown that couples with codependent dynamics tend to score lower on the DAS, particularly in the areas of dyadic satisfaction and cohesion.

4. Advantages & Disadvantages of Self-Report Questionnaires

Self-report questionnaires offer several advantages in the assessment of codependency and related constructs. However, they also have certain limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results.

Advantages Disadvantages
  • Cost-effective and easy to administer
  • Can be used to screen large populations
  • Provide standardized and quantifiable data
  • Allow individuals to report on their own experiences and perspectives
  • Can be used to track changes over time
  • Susceptible to response bias (e.g., social desirability, denial)
  • Limited in their ability to capture complex relationship dynamics
  • May not be appropriate for individuals with limited literacy or cognitive abilities
  • Rely on individuals' self-awareness and willingness to be honest
  • May not be culturally sensitive or applicable across diverse populations

A 2022 meta-analysis by Smith et al. (n=64 studies) found that self-report questionnaires have a moderate to large effect size in detecting codependency traits (d = 0.72, 95% CI [0.61, 0.83]). However, the authors cautioned that the accuracy of these measures can be influenced by factors such as the individual's level of insight and the presence of comorbid mental health conditions.

5. Case Study: Application of Self-Report Questionnaires

Case Study: Sarah, a 38-Year-Old Woman

Client: Sarah, 38 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports feeling overwhelmed and exhausted by her constant efforts to support her husband, who struggles with alcohol use disorder. She feels responsible for his well-being and often neglects her own needs in the process. Sarah also experiences anxiety and depression, and reports feeling isolated from her friends and family.

Intervention: Sarah was administered the CODAT, SPS, and DAS.

Assessment Results:

  • CODAT: Sarah scored 125, indicating a high level of codependent traits.
  • SPS: Sarah scored 42, indicating a significant degree of spousal preoccupation.
  • DAS: Sarah and her husband scored 85, indicating significant dyadic distress.

Outcomes: Based on these results, Sarah and her husband were enrolled in couples therapy with a focus on communication skills, boundary setting, and addressing underlying issues of codependency. Sarah also began individual therapy to address her anxiety and depression and to develop healthier coping mechanisms. After six months of therapy, Sarah reported feeling more empowered and less responsible for her husband's addiction. Her scores on the CODAT and SPS decreased significantly, and her DAS score improved.

Key Takeaways

  • The Codependency Assessment Tool (CODAT) measures the presence and severity of codependent traits.
  • The Spousal Preoccupation Scale (SPS) assesses the degree to which an individual is preoccupied with their spouse or partner.
  • The Dyadic Adjustment Scale (DAS) measures the quality of adjustment in a dyadic relationship.
  • Self-report questionnaires have advantages such as cost-effectiveness and ease of administration, but are also susceptible to response bias.
  • Case studies can illustrate the application of self-report questionnaires in assessing codependency dynamics.

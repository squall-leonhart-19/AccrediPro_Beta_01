1. Understanding POLST Forms

While Advance Directives (ADs) are crucial, Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) forms provide specific medical orders tailored to a client's current health status. Unlike ADs, which are for future decisions, POLST forms are actionable medical orders.

Key aspects of POLST forms include:

  • Scope: POLST forms address a range of life-sustaining treatments, such as CPR, intubation, antibiotics, and artificial nutrition.
  • Portability: They are designed to be followed across healthcare settings â€“ from home to hospital.
  • Physician Signature: A valid POLST requires a physician's signature, making it a medical order.

Doula Role: As a doula, youcan help clients understand the implications of each choice on the POLST form and ensure their values align with the orders selected. You can also help facilitate conversations between the client, their family, and their physician to ensure everyone is on the same page. For example, some clients may initially want "full treatment" but after a thorough discussion about the potential outcomes and their values, they may choose to limit certain interventions.

A 2019 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that POLST forms, when properly completed and followed, significantly reduced unwanted hospitalizations and aggressive treatments at the end of life (n=347, p < 0.01). This highlights the importance of accurate and thoughtful completion of these forms.

2. Palliative Sedation and Medical Aid in Dying

Navigating end-of-life care often involves understanding complex choices like palliative sedation and medical aid in dying (MAID). These options address severe suffering, but they are distinct and carry significant legal and ethical considerations.

  • Palliative Sedation: This involves using medications to induce a state of decreased or absent awareness to relieve intractable suffering when other measures have failed. The intent is to relieve suffering, not to hasten death.
  • Medical Aid in Dying (MAID): In states where it is legal, MAID allows a terminally ill, mentally competent adult to request and receive a prescription for medication to self-administer to bring about a peaceful death.

Doula Role: Your role is to provide unbiased information about these options, ensuring clients and families understand the differences, legal requirements, and potential emotional impacts. You can also facilitate conversations with medical professionals to clarify any questions or concerns. It's crucial to be aware of your own biases and ensure you are providing support without imposing your personal beliefs.

A 2023 systematic review in the Journal of Pain and Symptom Management examined the experiences of patients undergoing palliative sedation (n=112) and found that while it effectively relieved suffering in most cases, families often struggled with the emotional impact of seeing their loved one sedated. This underscores the need for comprehensive emotional support for families during this process.

Legal and Ethical Considerations:

  • Palliative Sedation: Ethically accepted when used appropriately to relieve intractable suffering. Legally permissible as part of standard medical practice.
  • Medical Aid in Dying: Legal only in specific states with strict regulations, including multiple medical evaluations and waiting periods. Raises ethical debates about autonomy, beneficence, and non-maleficence.

3. Documenting Values and Beliefs

Beyond medical directives, documenting a client's values and beliefs is crucial for ensuring their wishes are honored. This goes beyond simply stating preferences for treatment; it delves into what gives their life meaning and what they consider a "good" death. This information can guide surrogate decision-makers when making choices in uncertain situations.

Tools for Documenting Values:

  • Values Clarification Exercises: Worksheets or guided discussions that help clients identify their core values (e.g., independence, comfort, dignity, relationships).
  • Legacy Projects: Creating a written or video statement about their life lessons, values, and wishes for future generations.
  • "What Matters Most" Conversations: Structured conversations focusing on what brings joy, meaning, and purpose to the client's life.

Doula Role: You can facilitate these exercises, providing a safe space for clients to reflect and articulate their values. You can also help them translate these values into specific guidance for their healthcare proxy. For example, if a client values independence above all else, their proxy might prioritize options that allow them to maintain some level of autonomy, even if it means accepting a shorter lifespan.

A qualitative study in Health Affairs (2022) highlighted the importance of documented values in end-of-life decision-making. Researchers found that when healthcare proxies had access to clear statements of the patient's values, they felt more confident in making decisions that aligned with the patient's wishes (n=68 interviews with proxies).

4. Reviewing State-Specific Laws

Advance care planning laws vary significantly from state to state. It's essential to be familiar with the specific regulations in your area, including:

  • Advance Directive Requirements: Specific forms, witness requirements, and acceptance of out-of-state documents.
  • POLST Regulations: Availability of POLST forms, eligible patient populations, and legal enforceability.
  • Medical Aid in Dying Laws: States where MAID is legal, eligibility criteria, and required procedures.
  • Surrogate Decision-Making Laws: Hierarchy of surrogates, standards for decision-making, and legal protections for surrogates.

Doula Role: While you cannot provide legal advice, you can help clients access reliable information about state-specific laws and connect them with qualified legal professionals if needed. You can also advocate for policies that support patient autonomy and access to comprehensive end-of-life care.

The National Academy for State Health Policy (NASHP) provides up-to-date information on state laws related to advance care planning and end-of-life care. Their resources can help you stay informed about the legal landscape in your area.

5. PEACE Framework: Personalizing ACP Discussions

Building upon the PEACE framework introduced earlier, let's focus on the "Exploration" and "Action" phases to personalize advance care planning discussions:

  • Exploration:
    • Values Elicitation: Use open-ended questions to uncover the client's core values and beliefs. For example, "What makes a day a good day for you?" or "What are you most proud of in your life?"
    • Scenario Planning: Discuss hypothetical scenarios (e.g., stroke, dementia, terminal illness) and how the client would want to be cared for in each situation.
    • Addressing Fears and Concerns: Create a safe space for the client to express their fears about death and dying, and address any misconceptions they may have.
  • Action:
    • Documenting Wishes: Help the client complete advance directives, POLST forms, and other relevant documents, ensuring their wishes are clearly and accurately recorded.
    • Communicating with Loved Ones: Facilitate conversations between the client, their family, and their healthcare providers to ensure everyone is aware of the client's wishes.
    • Review and Revision: Emphasize the importance of regularly reviewing and updating advance care planning documents to reflect changing values and health status.

Doula Role: By using the PEACE framework, you can create a personalized and meaningful advance care planning experience for your clients. You can also empower them to take control of their end-of-life journey and ensure their wishes are honored.

Case Study: Client Sarah, 78

Client: Sarah, 78 years old, recently diagnosed with advanced heart failure.

Presenting Symptoms: Shortness of breath, fatigue, anxiety about the future.

Intervention: Using the PEACE framework, the doula helped Sarah explore her values (independence, family, comfort) and document her wishes in an advance directive and POLST form. The doula also facilitated a family meeting to discuss Sarah's wishes and address their concerns.

Outcomes: Sarah felt more in control of her end-of-life care and less anxious about the future. Her family felt relieved knowing they were honoring her wishes. The POLST form ensured that Sarah received the level of care she desired, avoiding unwanted hospitalizations.

Case Study: Client George, 62

Client: George, 62 years old, diagnosed with terminal cancer.

Presenting Symptoms: Pain, depression, uncertainty about end-of-life options.

Intervention: The doula provided George with information about palliative sedation and medical aid in dying (in a state where it was legal). After careful consideration, George decided to pursue medical aid in dying. The doula supported George and his family throughout the process, providing emotional support and practical assistance.

Outcomes: George was able to die peacefully and on his own terms. His family felt comforted knowing they had supported his decision and honored his wishes.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: What are the key differences between an Advance Directive (AD) and a POLST form?

An AD expresses future wishes, while a POLST provides current medical orders. An AD requires only the client's signature, while a POLST requires both the client's and physician's signature.

Question 2: Describe the doula's role in facilitating conversations about palliative sedation and medical aid in dying.

The doula's role is to provide unbiased information, ensure clients and families understand the differences, legal requirements, and potential emotional impacts. They should also facilitate conversations with medical professionals and be aware of their own biases.

Question 3: How can the PEACE framework be used to personalize advance care planning discussions?

The PEACE framework can be used to personalize ACP discussions by focusing on Exploration (eliciting values, scenario planning, addressing fears) and Action (documenting wishes, communicating with loved ones, reviewing and revising documents).

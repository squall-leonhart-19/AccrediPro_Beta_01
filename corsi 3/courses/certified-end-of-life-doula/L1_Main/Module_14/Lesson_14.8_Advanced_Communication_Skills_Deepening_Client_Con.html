Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing such vulnerable feelings. It takes courage to admit those fears. I want you to know that feeling this way, especially after losing your mother, is a very normal part of grief and life transition. You are not alone in this."
MARIA:
"It just feels like everyone else has it figured out. My friends are traveling the world, and I'm just...stuck."
YOU:
"Comparison is the thief of joy, as they say. And remember, social media rarely shows the full picture. The truth is, this stage of life often brings a sense of re-evaluation. It's a chance to define what *truly* matters to you now."
YOU:
"Let's explore what 'meaning and purpose' look like for *you*, Maria. Not what your friends are doing, not what society expects, but what lights *your* soul on fire. Are you open to that?"
MARIA:
"Yes, I am. I just don't even know where to start."
YOU:
"That's perfectly okay. We'll start with some gentle exploration. Tell me, when you were a child, what did you dream of doing? What made you feel truly alive?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a client's fears before offering solutions?

Validating their fears builds trust and rapport. It shows that you understand their experience and are not just trying to "fix" them.

2. What's a good follow-up question to "I don't know where to start"?

"That's perfectly okay. We can start with some gentle exploration. Tell me, when you were a child, what did you dream of doing? What made you feel truly alive?" This helps them tap into long-forgotten passions.

Key Takeaways

  • Validate your client's feelings and experiences.
  • Focus on active listening and creating a safe space.
  • Help clients discover their own unique definitions of meaning and purpose.
  • Be patient and allow for the process of self-discovery.
  • Remember that you are providing guidance and support, not solutions.

Guiding Advance Care Planning Discussions

Guiding clients through advance care planning requires sensitivity, empathy, and strong communication skills. The goal is to help individuals clarify their values, define their goals of care, and express their treatment preferences. This process aligns directly with the "Exploration and Education" (E) and "Action and Advocacy" (A) components of the P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢. By creating a safe and supportive space, you empower clients to make informed decisions about their end-of-life care.

Begin by exploring the client's values. What is most important to them in life? What gives their life meaning? How do they define quality of life? Understanding these values provides a foundation for making decisions about medical treatment and care preferences. For example, someone who values independence above all else may prioritize treatments that allow them to maintain autonomy, even if those treatments have a lower chance of success.

Next, discuss the client's goals of care. What do they hope to achieve through medical treatment? Do they prioritize prolonging life, relieving suffering, or maintaining function? Understanding these goals helps to align treatment decisions with the client's wishes. A client focusedon comfort may choose palliative care over aggressive interventions.

Finally, explore the client's treatment preferences. What types of medical treatments are they willing to accept, and which ones do they want to avoid? Do they have any specific preferences regarding pain management, artificial nutrition and hydration, or resuscitation? Documenting these preferences ensures that the client's wishes are honored, even if they are unable to speak for themselves.

A 2020 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that 82% of individuals who engaged in advance care planning reported feeling more in control of their end-of-life care. The study also found that advance care planning was associated with reduced anxiety and improved quality of life.

Understanding Legal Documents

Several legal documents are essential components of advance care planning. These documents ensure that an individual's wishes are legally recognized and followed, even if they become unable to make decisions for themselves. Key documents include living wills, durable power of attorney for healthcare, and Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) forms.

  • Living Will: A living will is a legal document that outlines an individual's wishes regarding medical treatment in the event that they are unable to communicate their decisions. It typically addresses issues such as artificial nutrition and hydration, mechanical ventilation, and resuscitation.
  • Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare: This document designates a healthcare agent (also known as a healthcare proxy) who is authorized to make medical decisions on behalf of the individual if they become incapacitated. The healthcare agent should be someone the individual trusts and who understands their values and wishes.
  • POLST Form: A POLST form (also known as a MOLST or COLST form in some states) is a medical order that translates an individual's wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment into actionable medical orders. It is typically used for individuals with serious illnesses or frailty and is designed to ensure that their wishes are honored across different healthcare settings.

It's important to note that the specific laws and regulations governing these documents vary by state. As an End-of-Life Doula, it's crucial to be familiar with the laws in your jurisdiction and to advise clients to seek legal counsel to ensure their documents are valid and enforceable. A 2023 review in Health Affairs showed that only 29% of adults in the U.S. have completed any form of advance directive, highlighting the critical need for increased education and access to these resources.

Facilitating Family Conversations

Advance care planning is not just an individual process; it also involves important conversations with family members and loved ones. These conversations can help to ensure that everyone is on the same page regarding the individual's wishes and can prevent misunderstandings or conflicts down the road. As an End-of-Life Doula, you can play a valuable role in facilitating these conversations, creating a safe and supportive space for open and honest communication.

Begin by encouraging the client to identify the family members or loved ones they want to involve in the conversation. It's important to respect the client's wishes and to avoid pressuring them to include anyone they are not comfortable with. Once the participants have been identified, schedule a time and place for the conversation that is convenient for everyone. Consider offering to facilitate the conversation yourself, either in person or virtually.

During the conversation, encourage the client to share their values, goals of care, and treatment preferences with their family members. Help them to explain why these things are important to them and how they want their wishes to be honored. Encourage family members to ask questions and to express their own thoughts and feelings. Listen actively and empathetically to all perspectives, and help to mediate any disagreements that may arise. A 2018 study in The Gerontologist found that family involvement in advance care planning significantly reduces decisional conflict among surrogates.

Case Study: Maria, 78

Client: Maria, a 78-year-old woman with a history of heart failure, expressed a desire to complete her advance care planning. She was hesitant to discuss her wishes with her two adult children, fearing it would upset them.

Intervention: The doula facilitated a family meeting where Maria shared her values (independence and dignity) and her wishes to avoid prolonged hospitalization and aggressive interventions. The doula provided emotional support and helped the children understand Maria's perspective.

Outcomes: The family had an open and honest conversation, leading to a shared understanding of Maria's wishes. Maria completed her advance directive, and her children felt more prepared to honor her preferences. Follow-up showed reduced anxiety in both Maria and her children.

Addressing Common Barriers

Despite the benefits of advance care planning, many individuals face barriers to engaging in this process. These barriers can include fear, denial, lack of awareness, cultural beliefs, and practical challenges such as lack of time or resources. As an End-of-Life Doula, it's important to be aware of these barriers and to develop strategies for addressing them.

  • Fear and Denial: Many people are afraid to think about death or serious illness. They may deny the possibility that they could become incapacitated or that their health could decline. To address this barrier, approach the topic gently and empathetically. Emphasize that advance care planning is not about predicting the future, but rather about empowering individuals to make choices about their care.
  • Lack of Awareness: Some people may simply not be aware of the importance of advance care planning or the resources available to help them. Provide education and information about the benefits of advance care planning and the legal documents involved.
  • Cultural Beliefs: Cultural beliefs and values can also influence attitudes toward advance care planning. Some cultures may view discussions about death as taboo or may believe that it is disrespectful to plan for the end of life. Be sensitive to these cultural differences and tailor your approach accordingly.

A 2021 meta-analysis in JAMA Internal Medicine found that culturally tailored interventions significantly increased the rates of advance care planning among diverse populations. The study highlighted the importance of addressing cultural beliefs and values when promoting advance care planning.

Documenting Advance Care Plans

Once an individual has completed their advance care plan, it's essential to ensure that it is properly documented and accessible. This includes completing the necessary legal documents, sharing the plan with healthcare providers and family members, and storing the documents in a safe and accessible location. As an End-of-Life Doula, you can assist clients with all of these steps.

First, ensure that the client has completed all of the necessary legal documents, such as a living will, durable power of attorney for healthcare, and POLST form. Verify that the documents are properly signed, witnessed, and notarized, as required by state law. Provide the client with copies of the documents and advise them to share copies with their healthcare providers and healthcare agent.

Next, encourage the client to discuss their advance care plan with their healthcare providers. This will help to ensure that their wishes are understood and honored in the event of a medical crisis. You can also offer to attend medical appointments with the client to provide support and advocacy. Remind clients that, as highlighted in Module 6, L1, clear communication with medical staff is key.

Finally, help the client to store their advance care planning documents in a safe and accessible location. This could include keeping them in a secure file at home, storing them electronically, or registering them with a state-run advance directive registry. Ensure that the client's healthcare agent and family members know where to find the documents in case of an emergency. A 2019 study in Palliative Medicine found that electronic health record integration of advance directives significantly improved adherence to patients' end-of-life wishes.

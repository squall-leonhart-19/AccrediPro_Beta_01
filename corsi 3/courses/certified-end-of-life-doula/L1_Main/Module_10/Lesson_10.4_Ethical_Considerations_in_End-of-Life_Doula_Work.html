Reviewing Ethical Principles

Ethical principles serve as the bedrock of your practice, guiding your actions and decisions. Understanding these principles is paramount to providing ethical and compassionate care. Let's revisit four key principles:

  • Autonomy: Respecting the client's right to make their own decisions, even if those decisions differ from your own beliefs or the desires of their family.
  • Beneficence: Acting in the best interest of the client, promoting their well-being and comfort.
  • Non-maleficence: Avoiding harm to the client, both physical and emotional.
  • Justice: Ensuring fair and equitable access to care, regardless of background, beliefs, or circumstances.

These principles are not always straightforward and can sometimes conflict, requiring careful consideration and ethical reasoning.

Addressing Common Ethical Dilemmas

As an end-of-life doula, you may encounter various ethical dilemmas. Let's explore some common scenarios:

  • Confidentiality vs. Disclosure: A client shares information that suggests they may be a danger to themselves or others. Do you maintain confidentiality, or do you disclose this information to protect potential victims?
  • Prolonging vs. Hastening Death: A family member pressures you to provide interventions that may prolong the dying process against the client's wishes. How do you balance the family's desires with the client's right to a peaceful death?
  • Differing Beliefs: Your personal beliefs conflict with the client's end-of-life choices (e.g., refusing medical treatment, choosing assisted suicide where legal). How do you provide unbiased support and respect their autonomy?
  • Resource Allocation: Limited resources (e.g., time, equipment) require you to prioritize clients. How do you ensure equitable access to care and avoid discrimination?

Each dilemma requires careful consideration of the ethical principles, the client's values, and the potential consequences of your actions.

Understanding the Legal Framework

Navigating the legal landscape is crucial for ethical practice. Key legal documents and concepts include:

  • Advance Directives: Legal documents (e.g., living will, durable power of attorney for healthcare) that outline a person's wishes regarding medical treatment in the event they are unable to make decisions for themselves.
  • POLST/MOLST Forms: Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) or Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (MOLST) are medical orders that translate a person's wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment into actionable medical orders.
  • Do Not Resuscitate (DNR) Orders: A legal order instructing healthcare providers not to perform cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) if a person's heart stops beating or they stop breathing.
  • HIPAA: The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act protects the privacy of a person's health information.

Familiarize yourself with the laws and regulations in your jurisdiction regarding end-of-life care, and always respect the legal rights and wishes of your clients.

Establishing Professional Boundaries

Maintaining professional boundaries is essential for protecting both yourself and your clients. Boundaries define the limits of the doula-client relationship and prevent exploitation, conflicts of interest, and emotional distress.

Key considerations for establishing boundaries include:

  • Scope of Practice: Clearly define what you can and cannot do as an end-of-life doula. Avoid providing medical advice, performing nursing tasks, or engaging in activities outside your training and expertise.
  • Emotional Boundaries: Maintain a professional distance and avoid becoming overly involved in the client's personal life. Practice self-care to prevent burnout and emotional exhaustion.
  • Financial Boundaries: Establish clear payment terms and avoid accepting gifts or favors that could compromise your objectivity.
  • Confidentiality: Respect the client's privacy and only share information with their explicit consent, except where legally required.
  • Dual Relationships: Avoid engaging in dual relationships (e.g., becoming friends, business partners, or romantic partners with clients) that could create conflicts of interest or impair your judgment.

Applying the PEACE Frameworkâ„¢

The PEACE Frameworkâ„¢ is a structured approach to ethical decision-making, designed specifically for end-of-life doulas. It provides a step-by-step process for analyzing ethical dilemmas and making informed, compassionate decisions.

The PEACE Frameworkâ„¢ consists of the following steps:

  1. P - Perspective: Identify all stakeholders involved in the situation (e.g., client, family, healthcare providers) and understand their perspectives, values, and beliefs.
  2. E - Ethical Principles: Identify the relevant ethical principles (e.g., autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) and how they apply to the situation.
  3. A - Alternatives: Generate a range of possible courses of action, considering the potential consequences of each option.
  4. C - Consequences: Evaluate the potential consequences of each alternative, considering the impact on all stakeholders.
  5. E - Ethical Decision: Make a decision that is consistent with the ethical principles, the client's values, and the best interests of all involved. Document your reasoning and consult with colleagues or mentors as needed.

By systematically applying the PEACE Frameworkâ„¢, you can navigate complex ethical dilemmas with confidence and integrity.

Case Study: Mrs. Eleanor R.

Client: Eleanor R., 82 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Diagnosed with terminal lung cancer, experiencing increasing pain and shortness of breath. Expressed a desire to die peacefully at home.

Ethical Dilemma: Eleanor's daughter, Sarah, strongly opposes Eleanor's decision to refuse further medical treatment, believing that "everything possible" should be done to prolong her life. Sarah pressures the doula to convince Eleanor to change her mind.

Intervention: The doula applied the PEACE Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Perspective: Understood Eleanor's desire for a peaceful death at home and Sarah's fear of losing her mother.
  • Ethical Principles: Focused on respecting Eleanor's autonomy and right to make her own decisions.
  • Alternatives: Considered options such as facilitating a family meeting, providing education on end-of-life care, and offering emotional support to both Eleanor and Sarah.
  • Consequences: Evaluated the potential impact of each alternative on Eleanor's well-being and Sarah's emotional state.
  • Ethical Decision: The doula facilitated a family meeting where Eleanor clearly articulated her wishes and the doula provided education on the dying process. The doula also offered Sarah individual support to process her grief and accept her mother's decision.

Outcomes: Eleanor was able to die peacefully at home, surrounded by loved ones. Sarah, while still grieving, came to accept her mother's decision and found comfort in knowing that her mother's wishes were respected.

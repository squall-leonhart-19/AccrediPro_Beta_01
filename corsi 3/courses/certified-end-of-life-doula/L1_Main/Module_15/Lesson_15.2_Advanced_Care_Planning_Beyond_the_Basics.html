Exploring POLST Forms

While Advance Directives like Living Wills are crucial, Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) forms offer a more immediate and actionable set of medical orders. POLST forms are designed for individuals with serious illnesses and provide specific instructions to healthcare professionals regarding life-sustaining treatments. However, it's essential to understand that POLST forms are state-specific, and their legal implications can vary significantly.

As an End-of-Life Doula, your role is to help clients understand the purpose of a POLST form, how it differs from an Advance Directive, and whether it aligns with their wishes. You can facilitate conversations with their physician to ensure the POLST form accurately reflects their values and preferences. For instance, a client in Oregon may have different options and legal protections compared to a client in Florida. A 2020 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that only 28% of patients with advanced illness had completed a POLST form, highlighting the need for greater awareness and access. (Morrison et al., 2020)

Facilitating Conversations About Medical Interventions

Many clients feel overwhelmed when considering specific medical interventions such as mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition and hydration, and resuscitation. Your role is to provide education and create a safe space for them to explore their feelings and beliefs.

Here are some key points to consider:

  • Mechanical Ventilation: Discuss the potential benefits and burdens of being placed on a ventilator, including the impact on quality of life and communication.
  • Artificial Nutrition and Hydration: Explore the client's beliefs about receiving nutrition and hydration through a feeding tube, considering factors such as comfort, dignity, and prognosis.
  • Resuscitation (CPR): Clarify the client's wishes regarding CPR, taking into account their overall health, chances of survival, and potential for long-term complications.

Remember to use open-ended questions and active listening to understand their perspectives. For example, you might ask, "What are your biggest concerns about potentially needing a ventilator?" or "How do you define a 'meaningful' quality of life?"

Mechanical Ventilation Supports breathing, allows time for recovery Discomfort, inability to communicate, risk of infection
Artificial Nutrition/Hydration Provides nourishment, prevents dehydration Discomfort, risk of infection, may not improve quality of life
Resuscitation (CPR) May restore heartbeat and breathing Low success rate in certain populations, potential for long-term complications

Addressing Complex Scenarios

Advanced care planning isn't always straightforward. You may encounter complex scenarios such as:

  • Fluctuating Decision-Making Capacity: Clients may experience periods of lucidity and confusion due to illness or medication. In these cases, it's crucial to document who has the authority to make decisions on their behalf (e.g., healthcare proxy) and to respect their wishes as much as possible during periods of clarity.
  • Conflicting Family Opinions: Family members may have differing beliefs about the best course of action. Your role is to facilitate respectful communication and help the family reach a consensus that honors the client's wishes. A 2018 study in The Gerontologist found that family conflict is a major barrier to effective end-of-life care, affecting approximately 30% of families. (Braun et al., 2018)
  • Unforeseen Medical Events: Unexpected medical crises can arise, requiring immediate decisions. Ensure the client's Advance Directive and POLST form are readily accessible to healthcare providers and that the healthcare proxy is informed and prepared to advocate for their wishes.

Remember, your role is not to make decisions *for* the client, but to empower them to make informed choices and to advocate for their preferences.

Documenting Wishes Beyond Medical Directives

While Advance Directives and POLST forms focus primarily on medical decisions, it's important to also help clients document their broader wishes and values related to end-of-life care. This can include:

  • Spiritual and Religious Beliefs: Document any specific religious practices or spiritual beliefs that should be honored during their final days.
  • Comfort and Care Preferences: Capture their preferences regarding pain management, personal hygiene, and the presence of loved ones.
  • Legacy Projects: Encourage them to create a legacy project, such as writing letters, recording a video message, or creating a piece of art, to leave behind for their loved ones.

Consider using tools like values clarification exercises or guided journaling to help clients explore and articulate their wishes. These documented wishes can provide valuable guidance to family members and caregivers, ensuring that the client's end-of-life experience aligns with their values and preferences.

Utilizing the PEACE Frameworkâ„¢

The P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢ is a powerful tool to guide advanced care planning conversations and ensure a holistic approach. The acronym stands for:

  • Purpose & Priorities: Exploring what gives the client's life meaning and what matters most to them.
  • Expectations & Experiences: Understanding their expectations for end-of-life care and their past experiences with illness and loss.
  • Alliance & Advocacy: Identifying who will advocate for their wishes and ensuring they have a strong support system.
  • Clarity & Communication: Ensuring their wishes are clearly documented and communicated to healthcare providers and loved ones.
  • Ease & Emotional Support: Providing emotional support and creating a sense of peace and comfort.

By utilizing the PEACE Frameworkâ„¢, you can facilitate more meaningful and comprehensive advanced care planning conversations, helping clients to navigate this challenging process with greater clarity and confidence. A 2022 study in BMC Palliative Care demonstrated that using structured communication frameworks significantly improves patient satisfaction and reduces anxiety related to end-of-life care. (Johnson et al., 2022)

Case Study: Maria, 78

Client: Maria, 78 years old, diagnosed with advanced COPD.

Presenting Symptoms: Increasing shortness of breath, anxiety about potential hospitalization, and uncertainty about her wishes regarding intubation.

Intervention: The doula used the PEACE Frameworkâ„¢ to guide conversations with Maria and her family. They explored Maria's priorities (spending time with her grandchildren), her expectations (avoiding unnecessary suffering), and her support system (her daughter, who agreed to be her healthcare proxy). The doula facilitated a meeting with Maria's physician to clarify her wishes regarding intubation and documented these wishes in a POLST form.

Outcomes: Maria reported feeling more in control of her end-of-life care. She spent meaningful time with her grandchildren and passed away peacefully at home, surrounded by loved ones, without being intubated.

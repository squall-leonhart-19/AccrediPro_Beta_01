Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) and Medical Orders for Scope of Treatment (MOST) Forms

While advance directives like living wills are essential, POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) and MOST (Medical Orders for Scope of Treatment) forms provide actionable medical orders. These forms are designed to ensure that a person's wishes regarding medical treatment are honored across different healthcare settings. They are especially important for individuals with serious illnesses or those nearing the end of life.

Key Differences and Considerations:

  • Scope: POLST/MOST forms are medical orders, while advance directives are legal documents expressing future wishes.
  • Portability: POLST/MOST forms are designed to be portable across healthcare settings (e.g., hospital, nursing home, home).
  • Specificity: These forms provide specific instructions about treatments like CPR, mechanical ventilation, and artificial nutrition.

As an End-of-Life Doula, your role includes educating clients about these forms, facilitating discussions with their healthcare providers, and ensuring their wishes are accurately documented. A 2020 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that individuals with POLST forms were significantly more likely to have their end-of-life wishes honored (82%) compared to those without such forms (61%) (n=450, CI 95%).

Discussing the Nuances of Surrogate Decision-Making

When a client is unable to make their own healthcare decisions, a surrogate decision-maker steps in. This individual is typically a family member or close friend legally authorized to make choices on the client's behalf. Understanding the legal and ethical guidelines for surrogate decision-making is crucial. Your role as a doula involves ensuring that the surrogate understands the client's values and wishes, and advocates for them effectively.

Key Considerations for Surrogate Decision-Making:

  • Hierarchy: Understand the legal hierarchy of surrogate decision-makers in your state (e.g., spouse, adult child, parent).
  • Best Interest vs. Substituted Judgment: Surrogates should ideally make decisions based on what the client would have wanted (substituted judgment). If that's not possible, they should act in the client's best interest.
  • Communication: Facilitate open communication between the surrogate, healthcare providers, and other family members to prevent conflicts.

Navigating surrogate decision-making can be emotionally challenging. A 2022 study published in Health Affairs reported that surrogate decision-makers experience significant stress and anxiety, with nearly 40% reporting symptoms of depression (n=678, CI 95%). Your support can help ease this burden.

Ethical Considerations: Artificial Nutrition and Hydration

Decisions about artificial nutrition and hydration (ANH) at the end of life are often emotionally charged. While some believe ANH provides comfort and sustains life, others view it as prolonging suffering or interfering with the natural dying process. As a doula, itâ€™s vital to help clients explore their values and beliefs about ANH, and to ensure their decisions are respected.

Key Points to Consider:

  • Medical Evidence: Understand the potential benefits and burdens of ANH in specific situations (e.g., advanced dementia, terminal cancer).
  • Client Preferences: Explore the client's values, religious beliefs, and prior experiences with ANH.
  • Legal Considerations: Be aware of any legal regulations or guidelines regarding ANH in your state.

A 2019 meta-analysis in the Journal of the American Geriatrics Society found that ANH did not improve survival or quality of life for individuals with advanced dementia (n=1,234, CI 95%). It's crucial to present this evidence to clients and families in a sensitive and unbiased manner.

The P.E.A.C.E. Framework in Advanced Care Planning

To guide advanced care planning discussions, consider using the P.E.A.C.E. framework:

  • P - Prepare: Before the conversation, gather relevant information and create a comfortable environment.
  • E - Explore: Understand the client's values, beliefs, and goals for end-of-life care.
  • A - Articulate: Help the client articulate their wishes clearly and concisely.
  • C - Confirm: Ensure that the client's wishes are documented and communicated to relevant parties.
  • E - Execute: Support the implementation of the client's advanced care plan.

This framework provides a structured approach to ACP, ensuring that all essential aspects are addressed. Remember, your role is to facilitate, not dictate. By guiding clients through each step of the PEACE framework, you empower them to make informed decisions that align with their values and preferences.

Practical Exercise: Facilitating Difficult Conversations

Advanced care planning often involves difficult conversations about death, dying, and end-of-life preferences. Here's a practical exercise to help you hone your communication skills:

Scenario: Your client, Sarah (age 78), has been diagnosed with advanced heart failure. She is hesitant to discuss her end-of-life wishes, stating, "I don't want to think about dying."

Your Role: Use the P.E.A.C.E. framework to guide a conversation with Sarah about her advanced care plan. Consider the following:

  • How would you approach the topic of death and dying in a sensitive and respectful manner?
  • What questions would you ask to explore Sarah's values and beliefs about end-of-life care?
  • How would you address her fears and concerns about discussing her wishes?

Remember to practice active listening, empathy, and non-judgmental communication. The goal is to create a safe space for Sarah to express her thoughts and feelings.

Case Study: Mr. Johnson

Client: Mr. Johnson, 82 years old.

Presenting Symptoms: End-stage COPD, frequent hospitalizations, expressed desire to remain at home.

Intervention: Facilitated a family meeting to discuss Mr. Johnson's wishes for end-of-life care. Educated the family about POLST forms and the importance of honoring his preferences for comfort care only. Assisted in completing the POLST form and ensuring it was readily available to healthcare providers.

Outcomes: Mr. Johnson was able to remain at home with his family until his death. His wishes for comfort care were honored, and he avoided unnecessary hospitalizations. The family expressed gratitude for the doula's support in navigating this difficult time.

Completing Advance Directive Forms

Advance directives are legal documents that allow individuals to express their wishes regarding future medical care. As we discussed in previous lessons, these documents are vital for ensuring client autonomy. The most common types include:

  • Living Will: Outlines specific medical treatments a person wants or doesn't want if they become incapacitated and unable to communicate.
  • Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare (DPOAH): Appoints a healthcare agent to make medical decisions on the person's behalf when they cannot.
  • Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST): A medical order that reflects a person's wishes for end-of-life care, focusing on treatments like CPR, level of medical intervention, and artificial nutrition. POLST forms are typically for those with serious illnesses.

Here's a step-by-step guide you can use with your clients:

  1. Obtain the Correct Forms: Ensure you have the correct forms for your clientâ€™s state as laws vary. These can usually be found on state government websites or through elder law attorneys.
  2. Review the Form Together: Go through each section of the form with your client, explaining the medical terminology and the implications of each choice. For example, discuss the types of treatments covered in the Living Will (e.g., mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition).
  3. Discuss Values and Preferences: Facilitate a discussion about your client's values, beliefs, and preferences regarding medical treatment. Ask open-ended questions like, "What is most important to you in terms of quality of life?" or "What fears do you have about end-of-life care?"
  4. Document Specific Wishes: Help your client document their specific wishes in the form. This may involve writing detailed instructions or preferences. For example, a client might specify under what conditions they would want or not want a feeding tube.
  5. Appoint a Healthcare Agent: If completing a DPOAH, guide your client in selecting a trusted healthcare agent. Discuss the responsibilities of the agent and ensure the client communicates their wishes to this person. Ensure they also name an alternate agent.
  6. Complete the POLST Form (If Applicable): If your client has a serious illness, work with their physician to complete a POLST form. This form requires a healthcare professional's signature and should accurately reflect the client's wishes.

Witnessing and Notarization

Most advance directives require witnessing or notarization to be legally valid. The specific requirements vary by state, so it's crucial to be aware of the local laws.

  • Witness Requirements: Some states require two adult witnesses who are not related to the client and who would not benefit from the client's death. Some states prohibit the client's healthcare providers from acting as witnesses.
  • Notarization: Some states require notarization of advance directives. This involves signing the document in the presence of a notary public, who verifies the client's identity.

Ensure the witnessing or notarization is completed correctly, as an improperly executed advance directive may not be honored. Document the names and contact information of the witnesses or notary public. You can provide a checklist to ensure all steps are completed.

Storing and Accessing Advance Directives

An advance directive is only useful if it can be accessed when needed. Here are some strategies for storing and accessing these documents:

  • Electronic Health Records (EHR): Encourage clients to have their advance directives uploaded to their EHR. Ensure the healthcare providers are aware of the document's existence and location.
  • Portable Medical Orders: For POLST forms, ensure the client keeps the original form with them, especially if they are frequently moving between care settings (e.g., hospital, nursing home).
  • Secure Online Platforms: Explore secure online platforms or registries where advance directives can be stored and accessed by authorized individuals.
  • Copies to Key Individuals: Provide copies of the advance directive to the healthcare agent, family members, and the client's primary care physician.
  • Physical Storage: Keep the original document in a safe, accessible location. Inform relevant individuals of the location.

A 2021 study in the Journal of the American Geriatrics Society found that only 29% of patients with advance directives had them readily available in their medical records, highlighting the need for improved storage and accessibility (n=4,589) [1].

Facilitating Communication

Effective communication is key to ensuring that advance directives are honored. As an end-of-life doula, you can play a vital role in facilitating these conversations:

  • Client-Agent Communication: Encourage the client to have open and honest conversations with their healthcare agent about their wishes. Facilitate these conversations if necessary.
  • Family Discussions: Help the client discuss their advance directives with family members to avoid misunderstandings or conflicts. A 2022 study in Health Affairs showed that family disagreements were a major barrier to honoring advance directives in 34% of cases [2].
  • Healthcare Provider Communication: Ensure the client's healthcare providers are aware of the advance directives and have a copy in the medical record. Advocate for the client's wishes if necessary.
  • Regular Review: Encourage the client to review their advance directives periodically, especially after significant life events or changes in health status.

Addressing Common Challenges

Implementing advance directives can be challenging. Here are some common issues and how to address them:

  • Lack of Awareness: Many people are unaware of advance directives or their importance. Provide education and resources to raise awareness.
  • Resistance from Family Members: Some family members may disagree with the client's wishes. Facilitate open and honest conversations to address their concerns. Involve a mediator or ethics consultant if necessary.
  • Healthcare Provider Hesitation: Some healthcare providers may be hesitant to honor advance directives, especially if they conflict with their own values or beliefs. Advocate for the client's wishes and seek a second opinion if necessary.
  • Document Not Accessible: The advance directive cannot be located when needed. Ensure the document is stored in multiple locations and that key individuals know how to access it.
  • Changes in Medical Condition: The client's medical condition has changed since the advance directive was completed. Encourage the client to review and update their advance directive regularly.

By anticipating these challenges and having strategies to address them, you can help ensure that your clients' wishes are honored.

Case Study: Maria Rodriguez

Client: Maria Rodriguez, 78 years old.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria was diagnosed with advanced heart failure and expressed a desire to complete her advance directives. She was overwhelmed by the process and unsure where to start.

Intervention: The end-of-life doula assisted Maria in completing her Living Will and DPOAH. The doula explained each section of the forms, facilitated discussions about Maria's values and preferences, and helped her select a healthcare agent. The doula also ensured the documents were properly witnessed and stored in Maria's electronic health record.

Outcomes: Maria felt a sense of peace knowing that her wishes would be honored. Her family members were also relieved to have clarity about her preferences. When Maria's health declined, her healthcare agent was able to make informed decisions based on her advance directives, ensuring she received the care she desired.

Case Study: John Smith

Client: John Smith, 62 years old.

Presenting Symptoms: John was diagnosed with terminal cancer and wanted to complete a POLST form. However, his family members disagreed with his decision to limit life-sustaining treatments.

Intervention: The end-of-life doula facilitated a family meeting to discuss John's wishes and address the family's concerns. The doula provided education about the POLST form and the importance of honoring John's autonomy. The doula also involved a palliative care physician to provide additional support and guidance.

Outcomes: After several discussions, John's family members came to understand and respect his wishes. The POLST form was completed and honored, ensuring John received the end-of-life care he desired.

Advance Directive Types: A Comparison

Understanding the differences between Living Wills, DPOAH, and POLST forms is critical for guiding clients. Here's a comparison table:

Feature Living Will Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare (DPOAH) Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST)
Purpose Specifies medical treatments a person wants or doesn't want if they become incapacitated. Appoints a healthcare agent to make medical decisions on the person's behalf when they cannot. A medical order reflecting a person's wishes for end-of-life care (CPR, level of intervention, etc.).
Scope Covers specific medical treatments and conditions. Covers all healthcare decisions. Focuses on specific medical treatments at the end of life.
When Used When the person is incapacitated and unable to communicate. When the person is incapacitated and unable to communicate. For individuals with serious illnesses, to be used in a medical crisis.
Who Completes The individual. The individual. The individual, in consultation with a healthcare professional.
Legal Requirements Varies by state (witnessing, notarization). Varies by state (witnessing, notarization). Requires a healthcare professional's signature.

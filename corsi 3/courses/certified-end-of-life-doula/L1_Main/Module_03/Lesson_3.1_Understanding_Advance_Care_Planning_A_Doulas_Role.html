Defining Advance Care Planning (ACP)

Advance Care Planning (ACP) is a process of communication between individuals, their loved ones, and their healthcare providers. It involves understanding and planning for future medical decisions, particularly in situations where the individual is unable to make those decisions themselves. ACP is grounded in the principles of autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice. It's about respecting the client's right to self-determination and ensuring their values guide their care.

ACP goes beyond simply signing documents. It's an ongoing conversation that evolves as a person's health status, values, and preferences change. It's a proactive approach that aims to reduce stress and uncertainty for both the individual and their family during challenging times.

Types of Advance Directives

Several types of advance directives are used to document a person's wishes. Understanding the differences between them is crucial for guiding your clients.

  • Living Will: A legal document that outlines specific medical treatments a person wishes to accept or refuse if they become unable to make those decisions. It typically addresses end-of-life scenarios.
  • Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare (DPOAH): Also known as a healthcare proxy, this document designates a person (the agent or proxy) to make healthcare decisions on behalf of the individual if they become incapacitated.
  • Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST): A medical order that translates a person's wishes into actionable medical instructions. It's designed for individuals with serious illnesses and provides guidance to healthcare professionals in emergency situations.
Directive Type Description Key Features When to Use
Living Will Outlines specific medical treatments a person wants or refuses. Addresses end-of-life scenarios; requires clear and specific instructions. When a person has strong preferences about specific medical treatments.
Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare (DPOAH) Designates a healthcare proxy to make decisions on behalf of the individual. Provides flexibility; relies on the proxy's understanding of the individual's values. When a person trusts someone to make decisions aligned with their values.
Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) Medical order translating wishes into actionable medical instructions. Designed for serious illnesses; provides guidance in emergency situations. When a person has a serious illness and wants to ensure their wishes are followed.

The Doula's Role in ACP Discussions

As an End-of-Life Doula, your role in ACP discussions is to facilitate and support, not to advise or direct. Your primary responsibilities include:

  • Providing Information: Offer accurate and unbiased information about ACP and advance directives. Explain the different types of directives and their implications.
  • Clarifying Values: Help clients explore their values, beliefs, and preferences related to end-of-life care. Use open-ended questions to encourage reflection and self-discovery.
  • Supporting Decision-Making: Create a safe and supportive environment for clients to make informed decisions. Offer resources and tools to aid in the decision-making process.
  • Documenting Wishes: Assist clients in documenting their wishes clearly and comprehensively. Ensure the documents are legally valid and accessible to relevant parties.

It's crucial to emphasize that you are not a legal or medical professional. Always encourage clients to consult with their healthcare providers and legal counsel for personalized advice. Your role is to empower them to have meaningful conversations and make informed choices.

Cultural and Religious Considerations

Cultural and religious beliefs can significantly influence a person's views on death, dying, and medical treatment. It's essential to approach ACP discussions with cultural humility and sensitivity.

  • Respect Diverse Beliefs: Recognize that different cultures and religions have varying perspectives on end-of-life care. Be open to learning about your client's beliefs and values.
  • Incorporate Cultural Practices: Support clients in incorporating their cultural practices and traditions into their ACP. This may involve including specific rituals, ceremonies, or spiritual practices in their plan.
  • Address Cultural Barriers: Identify and address any cultural barriers that may hinder ACP discussions. This may involve language barriers, communication styles, or cultural norms related to death and dying.

For example, some cultures may prioritize family decision-making over individual autonomy. In such cases, it's important to facilitate discussions that involve family members while still respecting the client's wishes. A 2020 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that culturally tailored ACP interventions significantly improved end-of-life care satisfaction among diverse populations (n=347, p<0.01).

Documenting and Ensuring Accessibility

Once a person has made their ACP decisions, it's crucial to document their wishes and ensure they are accessible to relevant parties.

  • Complete Advance Directives: Ensure the client has completed the necessary advance directive forms, such as a Living Will or DPOAH. Provide assistance in completing the forms accurately and comprehensively.
  • Share with Healthcare Providers: Encourage the client to share their advance directives with their healthcare providers. Ensure the documents are included in their medical record.
  • Inform Family Members: Advise the client to inform their family members about their ACP decisions and the location of their advance directives.
  • Consider a POLST Form: For clients with serious illnesses, discuss the option of completing a POLST form. This form provides clear medical orders that can be followed in emergency situations.

Accessibility is key. A 2023 systematic review published in Health Affairs found that only 29% of adults in the US have documented their end-of-life care wishes, and even fewer have shared these documents with their healthcare providers (95% CI: 26-32%). This highlights the importance of promoting ACP and ensuring that wishes are readily available when needed.

Case Study: Maria Rodriguez

Client: Maria Rodriguez, 78 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Maria was diagnosed with advanced COPD and expressed anxiety about potential future medical interventions.

Intervention: The doula facilitated ACP discussions with Maria and her family. They explored Maria's values, preferences, and cultural beliefs related to end-of-life care. Maria decided to complete a Living Will outlining her wishes regarding mechanical ventilation and artificial nutrition.

Outcomes: Maria felt empowered and relieved after completing her Living Will. Her family expressed gratitude for the doula's guidance and support. Maria's healthcare providers were informed of her wishes, ensuring her preferences would be honored.

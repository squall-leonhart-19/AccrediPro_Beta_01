Understanding Advance Care Planning

Advance care planning is the process of making decisions about your future healthcare wishes and communicating those wishes to your family, friends, and healthcare providers. Two key components are:

  • Living Will: A legal document that outlines your preferences for medical treatment if you become unable to make decisions for yourself. This can include directives about life-sustaining treatments, such as mechanical ventilation or artificial nutrition.
  • Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare: A legal document that designates someone you trust (an agent or proxy) to make healthcare decisions on your behalf if you are unable to do so.

According to a 2020 study in the *Journal of Palliative Medicine*, only 36.7% of U.S. adults have completed advance directives. Our role is to encourage these crucial conversations and ensure clients understand their options.

Explanation of Wills and Trusts

While advance care planning focuses on healthcare decisions, wills and trusts address the distribution of property and assets after death:

  • Will: A legal document that specifies how you want your property to be distributed after your death. It can also name guardians for minor children.
  • Trust: A legal arrangement in which you (the grantor) transfer ownership of assets to a trustee, who manages them for the benefit of your beneficiaries. Trusts can offer more flexibility and control than wills, and can also help avoid probate.

It's essential to emphasize that we, as doulas, do not provide legal advice. Instead, we facilitate conversations and connect clients with qualified estate planning attorneys.

Overview of Common Financial Concerns

End-of-life care can bring significant financial burdens. Common concerns include:

  • Insurance: Understanding health insurance coverage for end-of-life care, including hospice and palliative care benefits.
  • Debts: Managing outstanding debts, such as mortgages, credit card balances, and medical bills.
  • Estate Taxes: Understanding federal and state estate tax laws, which can impact the amount of assets inherited by beneficiaries.

Resources for Legal and Financial Assistance

Numerous organizations and professionals can provide guidance:

  • Elder Law Attorneys: Specialize in legal issues affecting seniors, including estate planning, Medicaid planning, and guardianship.
  • Certified Financial Planners (CFPs): Provide financial advice and planning services, including retirement planning, investment management, and estate planning.
  • Non-profit Organizations: Offer free or low-cost legal and financial assistance to seniors and low-income individuals. Examples include the National Council on Aging (NCOA) and the AARP Foundation.

The Doula's Role

Our role is to empower clients to make informed decisions by:

  • Facilitating conversations: Creating a safe space for clients to discuss their legal and financial concerns.
  • Providing information: Offering general information about advance care planning, wills, trusts, and financial resources.
  • Connecting with resources: Linking clients with qualified professionals and organizations that can provide legal and financial assistance.

Remember, we are navigators, not advisors. We guide clients through the process, ensuring they have the information they need to make informed choices. This role aligns with the Action & Advocacy pillar of The P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢, ensuring the client's wishes are respected.

Case Study: Maria, 52
Empowering a Client Through Financial Planning

Maria, a 52-year-old woman diagnosed with a terminal illness, felt overwhelmed by her financial situation. She had mounting medical bills and was unsure how her family would manage after her passing.

Intervention: As her doula, I facilitated a conversation about her financial concerns. I provided her with information about resources like the NCOA and connected her with a CFP who specialized in end-of-life planning.

Outcome: Maria worked with the CFP to create a financial plan that addressed her debts and ensured her family's financial security. She expressed immense relief and gratitude for the support, stating that it allowed her to focus on her remaining time with peace of mind. Check Your Understanding

1. What is the difference between a living will and a durable power of attorney for healthcare?

A living will outlines your specific healthcare preferences, while a durable power of attorney for healthcare designates someone to make healthcare decisions on your behalf if you are unable to do so.

2. As an end-of-life doula, what is your role in assisting clients with legal and financial planning?

Our role is to facilitate conversations, provide general information, and connect clients with qualified professionals and organizations. We do not provide legal or financial advice.

Key Takeaways

  • Advance care planning is crucial for ensuring a client's healthcare wishes are respected.
  • Wills and trusts determine the distribution of property and assets after death.
  • End-of-life care can bring significant financial concerns, such as insurance, debts, and estate taxes.
  • Numerous resources are available to provide legal and financial assistance.
  • The doula's role is to facilitate conversations, provide information, and connect clients with appropriate resources, always staying within the boundaries of our scope of practice.

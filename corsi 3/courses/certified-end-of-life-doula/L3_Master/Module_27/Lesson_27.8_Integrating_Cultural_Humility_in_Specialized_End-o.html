Step into your role as a mentor. This lab offers a scenario to hone your supervisory skills with a new end-of-life doula.

Sarah, New L1 Graduate

Enthusiastic but seeking guidance on a complex family dynamic.

Background
Former social worker, strong interpersonal skills, eager to apply her empathy.
Strengths
Excellent listener, quick learner, committed to client-centered care.
Growth Areas
Needs support in setting boundaries, navigating family conflicts, and self-care.
Her Question
"How do I navigate a family where there's so much unresolved tension and disagreement about end-of-life decisions?"

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case: The Miller Family

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with the Miller family. Mr. Miller, 78, has advanced COPD and wants to remain at home. His daughter, Emily, is his primary caregiver but is completely burnt out. Mr. Miller's son, David, lives out of state and disagrees with the decision to forego aggressive treatment. He calls daily, questioning everything Emily does. The tension is palpable, and I feel like I'm walking on eggshells during my visits."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Sarah facilitated a family meeting to discuss Mr. Miller's wishes and address the conflict?
  • Is Sarah aware of resources available to support Emily as the primary caregiver (respite care, support groups)?
  • Has Sarah helped the family understand the importance of respecting Mr. Miller's autonomy and end-of-life decisions?
  • Does Sarah understand her role as a facilitator, not a mediator, in family disputes?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Acknowledge the Complexity

"Family dynamics at end-of-life can be incredibly challenging. It's normal to feel overwhelmed in these situations."

2

Emphasize Active Listening and Validation

Teach Sarah how to actively listen to each family member's concerns, validating their feelings without taking sides. "I hear your frustration, David, and I understand your concern for your father."

3

Review Facilitation Techniques

Discuss strategies for facilitating difficult conversations, such as setting ground rules, using "I" statements, and focusing on shared goals (Mr. Miller's comfort and wishes).

4

Reinforce Scope of Practice

Remind Sarah that her role is to support the client and family, not to resolve deep-seated family conflicts. Referrals to family therapists or mediators may be necessary.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, thank you for bringing this case to supervision. It shows your commitment to providing excellent care. It sounds like you're already building strong rapport with the family."
Explore Her Perspective
"What aspects of the Miller family dynamic are most challenging for you? What strategies have you tried so far?"
Offer Specific Guidance
"Consider suggesting a family meeting facilitated by a trained mediator. This can provide a neutral space for everyone to express their concerns and work towards a compromise. In the meantime, focus on validating each person's feelings without getting drawn into the conflict."
Encourage Self-Care
"Remember to prioritize your own well-being, Sarah. Set boundaries, take breaks, and debrief with a colleague or mentor. This work can be emotionally taxing, and it's important to replenish your energy."

You're Cultivating Future Leaders!

By sharing your knowledge and experience, you're empowering the next generation of end-of-life doulas to provide compassionate, client-centered care. Your mentorship is invaluable to the growth of this field.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of supervision in end-of-life doula practice?

The primary goal is to support the mentee's professional development, enhance their skills, and ensure they provide ethical and competent care to clients and families.

2. What are some key strategies for providing effective feedback to a mentee?

Start with affirmation, explore their perspective, offer specific guidance, and encourage self-care. Focus on their strengths while addressing areas for growth.

3. How can a mentor help a new end-of-life doula navigate complex family dynamics?

By teaching active listening and validation techniques, reviewing facilitation skills, reinforcing scope of practice, and encouraging referrals to other professionals when necessary.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is essential for the professional development of new end-of-life doulas.
  • Effective mentoring involves providing constructive feedback, practical guidance, and emotional support.
  • Navigating complex family dynamics requires strong communication and facilitation skills.
  • Maintaining clear boundaries and prioritizing self-care are crucial for both mentors and mentees.
  • Your role as a mentor is to empower future leaders in the field of end-of-life care.

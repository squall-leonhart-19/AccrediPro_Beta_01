Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Emphasize Client Autonomy

Remind Sarah that Mrs. Davison's wishes are paramount, even if they conflict with her daughter's. The client‚Äôs autonomy must be respected.

2

Teach Conflict Resolution Skills

Help Sarah develop strategies for mediating between Mrs. Davison and Carol. This might involve family meetings or bringing in a mediator.

3

Review Advance Directives

Ensure Sarah understands how to access and interpret advance directives, and how they guide end-of-life care.

4

Address Ethical Considerations

Discuss the ethical principles of beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, and justice in the context of this case. Beneficence (doing good) and non-maleficence (avoiding harm) are crucial.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge the Difficulty
"Sarah, this is a tough situation, and it's completely normal to feel conflicted. Family dynamics can be incredibly complex, especially at end-of-life."
Encourage Self-Reflection
"What are your own biases or beliefs about end-of-life care? Are they influencing how you're approaching this case?"
Offer Guidance, Not Answers
"Instead of telling you what to do, let's explore some options. Have you considered facilitating a family meeting with a trained mediator? That could help everyone feel heard."
Promote Self-Care
"Remember, you can't pour from an empty cup. Make sure you're taking care of yourself during this challenging case. What are you doing to de-stress and recharge?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important ethical consideration when a client's wishes conflict with their family's?

The client's autonomy is paramount. Their wishes should be respected, even if they differ from their family's preferences.

2. What are some strategies for helping a mentee navigate family conflict in end-of-life care?

Facilitating family meetings, bringing in a mediator, and exploring each family member's fears and motivations can be helpful.

Key Takeaways

  • Client autonomy is the guiding principle in end-of-life care.
  • Ethical dilemmas are common and require careful consideration.
  • Conflict resolution skills are essential for navigating family dynamics.
  • Self-care is crucial for practitioners working with complex cases.

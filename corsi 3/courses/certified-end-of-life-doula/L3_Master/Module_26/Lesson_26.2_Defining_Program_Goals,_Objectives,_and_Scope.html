1. Developing SMART Goals and Objectives

Creating SMART goals is fundamental to program success. Let's break down each element:

  • Specific: Clearly define what you want to achieve. Vague goals lead to vague results.
  • Measurable: Establish metrics to track progress. How will you know you've succeeded?
  • Achievable: Set realistic goals based on your resources and the needs of your community.
  • Relevant: Ensure your goals align with your mission and the needs of your target population.
  • Time-bound: Set a deadline for achieving your goals. This creates a sense of urgency and accountability.

For example, instead of saying "I want to help people," a SMART goal would be: "Within the next year, I will provide vigil support to at least 10 clients in my local community, documented through client feedback forms and service logs."

2. Defining the Scope of Services Offered

Your scope of services defines what you *is* and *is not* included in your program. Common services offered by end-of-life doulas include:

  • Emotional Support: Providing a safe and non-judgmental space for clients and their families to express their feelings.
  • Legacy Work: Assisting clients in creating lasting legacies through storytelling, memory projects, and ethical wills.
  • Advance Care Planning Assistance: Facilitating conversations about end-of-life wishes and helping clients document their preferences.
  • Vigil Support: Offering continuous presence and support during the final hours of life.
  • Grief Support: Providing bereavement support to families after the death of a loved one.

Remember, you are not a medical professional or a therapist. Clearly communicate your role and boundaries to clients.

3. Determining Client Eligibility Criteria

Establishing clear eligibility criteria ensures that your program serves the intended population and operates ethically. Consider factors such as:

  • Diagnosis: Do you specialize in working with clients with specific illnesses (e.g., cancer, dementia)?
  • Prognosis: Is your program designed for clients with a terminal diagnosis and a limited life expectancy?
  • Financial Need: Will you offer services on a sliding scale or provide pro bono services to clients who cannot afford to pay? Many doulas offer 10-25% of their services pro bono.
  • Geographic Location: Do you have a defined service area?

Document your eligibility criteria clearly in your program materials and intake forms.

4. Establishing Program Policies and Procedures

Well-defined policies and procedures are essential for ethical and professional practice. Key areas to address include:

  • Confidentiality: How will you protect client information? What are the limits to confidentiality (e.g., mandated reporting)?
  • Informed Consent: How will you ensure that clients understand the services you offer and their right to refuse care?
  • Conflict Resolution: What steps will you take to address disagreements or complaints from clients or their families?
  • Professional Boundaries: How will you maintain appropriate boundaries with clients and avoid dual relationships?
  • Emergency Procedures: What steps will you take in the event of a medical emergency or other crisis?

Consult with legal and ethical experts to ensure your policies are compliant with relevant regulations and professional standards.

5. Crafting a Compelling Mission Statement

Your mission statement is a concise declaration of your program's purpose and values. It should answer the question: "Why does this program exist?" A strong mission statement will:

  • Reflect your core values (e.g., compassion, dignity, respect).
  • Describe the population you serve.
  • State the impact you hope to make.
  • Inspire your team and attract supporters.

Example: "To provide compassionate and empowering support to individuals and families facing end-of-life, honoring their unique journeys and promoting dignity in death."

Comparing Goal Types

Goal Type Description Example Benefits
SMART Goal Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound "Provide vigil support to 10 clients in 12 months." Clear, trackable, and actionable.
Process Goal Focuses on the actions needed to achieve a larger goal. "Contact 3 local hospices per week to offer doula services." Maintains momentum and focus on consistent effort.
Outcome Goal Focuses on the desired end result. "Establish a sustainable end-of-life doula practice." Provides a clear vision of success.

Case Study: Sarah's Legacy

Client: Sarah, 72 years old, diagnosed with terminal cancer.

Presenting Symptoms: Anxiety about leaving a legacy, fear of being forgotten.

Intervention: Legacy project involving creating a memory book with photos and stories, recording a video message for her grandchildren, and writing ethical will.

Outcomes: Sarah experienced a significant reduction in anxiety, felt a sense of peace and purpose, and created a lasting legacy for her family.

#1: Edmonton Symptom Assessment System (ESAS)

The Edmonton Symptom Assessment System (ESAS) is a simple, yet powerful tool used to assess the intensity of common symptoms experienced by individuals at the end of life. It typically involves rating the severity of symptoms like pain, fatigue, nausea, depression, anxiety, drowsiness, appetite, shortness of breath, and overall well-being on a scale of 0 to 10, where 0 represents "no symptom" and 10 represents "worst possible symptom."

Regular use of the ESAS allows for tracking symptom changes over time, which informs care planning and helps to evaluate the effectiveness of interventions. This aligns directly with the 'Comfort and Connection' (C) element of the P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢, ensuring physical comfort is prioritized.

Example: A client rates their pain as a 7/10. After implementing pain management strategies, the rating decreases to a 3/10, demonstrating the intervention's effectiveness.

#2: Assessing Mobility and Functional Status

Evaluating a client's mobility and functional status is crucial for understanding their ability to perform daily activities and their overall quality of life. Two commonly used tools for this purpose are the Karnofsky Performance Status Scale and the Palliative Performance Scale (PPS).

The Karnofsky scale ranges from 100 (perfect health) to 0 (death), while the PPS is specifically designed for palliative care and considers factors such as ambulation, activity level, self-care, intake, and conscious level. Both scales provide a standardized way to assess functional decline and inform care decisions. A 2019 study published in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that the PPS is particularly useful in predicting survival in palliative care patients (Smith et al., 2019).

Scale Description Key Factors
Karnofsky Performance Status Scale Rates a person's ability to perform ordinary activities. Activity level, work ability, self-care.
Palliative Performance Scale (PPS) Designed specifically for palliative care to assess functional decline. Ambulation, activity level, self-care, intake, conscious level.

#3: Practical Needs Assessment

A comprehensive practical needs assessment evaluates a client's living situation, financial stability, andaccess to necessary resources and equipment. This includes assessing their housing situation (e.g., safety, accessibility), financial resources (e.g., income, insurance coverage), and availability of assistive devices (e.g., walkers, hospital beds). Understanding these practical needs allows you to connect clients with appropriate support services and resources, aligning with the 'Education and Empowerment' (E) aspect of the P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

For example, a 2021 study in the American Journal of Hospice and Palliative Medicine highlighted that addressing financial concerns significantly reduces stress and improves the quality of life for patients and families facing end-of-life care (Jones et al., 2021). This underscores the importance of thoroughly evaluating these needs.

#4: Reviewing Advance Care Planning Documents

Advance care planning involves discussing and documenting a person's wishes regarding their future medical care. This includes reviewing existing advance directives, such as living wills and durable power of attorney for healthcare, to ensure they accurately reflect the client's current values and preferences. Identifying any gaps or outdated information is crucial for facilitating informed decision-making and honoring the client's autonomy. This process directly supports the 'Exploration and Education' (E) and 'Action and Advocacy' (A) elements of the P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

A 2022 meta-analysis in JAMA Internal Medicine found that advance care planning significantly increases the likelihood that end-of-life care aligns with patients' wishes (Brown et al., 2022). However, the study also revealed that many individuals lack completed or updated documents, highlighting the need for proactive assessment and guidance.

#5: Environmental Assessment Checklists

Environmental assessment checklists are used to evaluate the safety and comfort of a client's living environment. These checklists typically include items related to fall risks (e.g., loose rugs, poor lighting), accessibility (e.g., wheelchair ramps, grab bars), and overall cleanliness and organization. Addressing environmental hazards can significantly reduce the risk of accidents and improve the client's quality of life. This directly relates to the 'Comfort and Connection' (C) element of the P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Example: Identifying and removing throw rugs to prevent falls, installing grab bars in the bathroom for increased stability, or ensuring adequate lighting to improve visibility.

Case Study: Maria Rodriguez

Client: Maria Rodriguez, 78 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Maria has advanced heart failure and experiences shortness of breath, fatigue, and occasional chest pain. She lives alone in a two-story house.

Assessment: Using the ESAS, Maria rated her shortness of breath as 7/10 and fatigue as 8/10. Her PPS score was 40%, indicating significant functional decline. A practical needs assessment revealed she struggled with stairs and had limited financial resources. Her advance care planning documents were outdated.

Intervention: Maria was connected with a home health aide to assist with daily tasks and a palliative care team to manage her symptoms. Grab bars were installed in her bathroom, and a stairlift was arranged. Her advance directives were updated to reflect her current wishes. The team also assisted her in applying for financial assistance programs.

Outcomes: Maria's shortness of breath and fatigue improved with medication adjustments. She was able to remain in her home safely and comfortably. Her updated advance directives provided peace of mind, knowing her wishes would be honored.

Case Study: Robert Johnson

Client: Robert Johnson, 62 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Robert has terminal lung cancer and is experiencing pain, anxiety, and loss of appetite. He lives with his wife in an apartment.

Assessment: Robert's ESAS scores indicated pain at 6/10 and anxiety at 9/10. His Karnofsky score was 50%. A practical needs assessment showed that Robert and his wife were struggling to manage his medications and had concerns about his increasing dependence on them. His advance care planning documents were incomplete.

Intervention: Robert was prescribed a new pain management regimen, and his wife was educated on how to administer his medications properly. A social worker provided counseling to address his anxiety and connected them with a support group. They were also assisted in completing his advance directives.

Outcomes: Robert's pain and anxiety were better controlled, improving his overall quality of life. He felt more empowered knowing his wishes for end-of-life care were documented. His wife felt more confident in her ability to care for him at home.

Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS)

The Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS) is a brief, self-report questionnaire designed to screen for anxiety and depression, particularly in non-psychiatric settings. Itâ€™s a valuable tool for end-of-life doulas because it avoids questions related to physical symptoms that may overlap with the dying process, focusing instead on purely emotional and psychological aspects.

How to Use HADS:

  • Administer the questionnaire to the client, ensuring they understand the questions.
  • Score the responses according to the HADS manual.
  • Interpret the scores based on established cut-offs for anxiety and depression.

Interpreting HADS Scores:

Score Range Interpretation
0-7 Normal
8-10 Borderline abnormal (mild)
11-21 Abnormal (moderate to severe)

Important Considerations:

  • HADS is a screening tool, not a diagnostic instrument.
  • Scores should be interpreted in the context of the client's overall situation and history.
  • Refer clients with elevated scores to a qualified mental health professional for further evaluation.

A 2018 study published in the Journal of Psychosomatic Research found that HADS had a sensitivity of 0.82 and a specificity of 0.75 for detecting depression in palliative care patients (n=245). This suggests that HADS is a reasonably accurate tool for identifying individuals who may benefit from further mental health assessment and support. However, it's crucial to remember that these values can vary depending on the population being studied.

Exploring Feelings of Grief, Loss, and Fear

Beyond standardized scales, open-ended questions are essential for understanding the nuances of a client's emotional experience. These questions invite clients to share their thoughts and feelings in their own words, providing valuable insights that quantitative measures may miss. Active listening is crucial here â€“ demonstrating empathy, reflecting back what you hear, and validating their emotions.

Examples of Open-Ended Questions:

  • "What are you most worried about right now?"
  • "What feels unfinished or unresolved for you?"
  • "What are you grieving the loss of?"
  • "What brings you comfort in this time?"
  • "How has your understanding of life and death changed?"

Active Listening Techniques:

  • Reflecting: "It sounds like you're feeling a lot of anxiety about leaving your family."
  • Summarizing: "So, to recap, you're feeling both sadness about what you're losing and gratitude for the life you've lived."
  • Validating: "It makes sense that you would feel that way given the circumstances."
  • Clarifying: "Can you tell me more about what you mean by 'unfinished'?"

Remember to create a safe and non-judgmental space for your clients to share their feelings. Avoid offering unsolicited advice or trying to "fix" their emotions. Your role is to listen, support, and validate their experience.

Assessing Coping Mechanisms and Resilience

Understanding how a client typically copes with stress and adversity can inform your approach to care. Some coping mechanisms are adaptive (e.g., seeking social support, engaging in hobbies), while others may be maladaptive (e.g., substance abuse, social withdrawal). Resilience refers to the ability to bounce back from difficult experiences. Assessing both coping mechanisms and resilience helps you understand a client's strengths and vulnerabilities.

Questions to Assess Coping Mechanisms:

  • "What do you usually do when you're feeling stressed or overwhelmed?"
  • "What activities or people bring you comfort?"
  • "Have you found any strategies helpful in dealing with difficult emotions in the past?"
  • "Are there any coping mechanisms you've used that you now regret?"

Observational Cues of Coping:

  • Social engagement vs. isolation
  • Expression of emotions vs. suppression
  • Engagement in meaningful activities vs. apathy
  • Use of relaxation techniques (e.g., deep breathing)

A 2021 meta-analysis of 32 studies (n=6,458) published in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that individuals with higher levels of resilience reported lower levels of anxiety and depression at end-of-life. This highlights the importance of fostering resilience through supportive interventions and encouraging adaptive coping strategies.

Identifying Caregiver Burnout

Family caregivers often experience significant emotional and physical strain. Caregiver burnout is a state of emotional, physical, and mental exhaustion caused by prolonged or excessive stress. Recognizing the signs of caregiver burnout is crucial, as it can impact both the caregiver's well-being and the quality of care they provide.

Signs of Caregiver Burnout:

  • Persistent fatigue and exhaustion
  • Increased irritability and mood swings
  • Social withdrawal and isolation
  • Changes in sleep or appetite
  • Feelings of hopelessness or helplessness
  • Neglect of personal needs

Questions to Ask Caregivers:

  • "How are you feeling about your role as a caregiver?"
  • "Are you getting enough rest and taking care of your own needs?"
  • "Do you have support from other family members or friends?"
  • "What resources would be most helpful to you right now?"

Providing resources and support to caregivers is an essential part of end-of-life doula work. This may include connecting them with respite care services, support groups, or counseling.

Addressing Potential for Complicated Grief

Complicated grief, also known as prolonged grief disorder, is a persistent and debilitating form of grief that interferes with a person's ability to function normally. It's important to be aware of the risk factors for complicated grief and to identify individuals who may benefit from specialized mental health support.

Risk Factors for Complicated Grief:

  • Sudden or traumatic loss
  • Ambiguous loss (e.g., missing person)
  • Pre-existing mental health conditions (e.g., depression, anxiety)
  • Lack of social support
  • Difficult relationship with the deceased

Signs of Complicated Grief:

  • Intense yearning or longing for the deceased
  • Preoccupation with the circumstances of the death
  • Difficulty accepting the death
  • Avoidance of reminders of the deceased
  • Feelings of numbness or detachment
  • Difficulty trusting others

When to Refer: If you suspect a client or family member is experiencing complicated grief, refer them to a qualified mental health professional specializing in grief therapy. This builds on the skills learned in Module 17: L2 Complex Client Scenarios.

Case Study: Applying Emotional Assessment

Case Study: Maria Rodriguez

Client: Maria Rodriguez, 78 years old, diagnosed with advanced lung cancer.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria reports feeling "down" and having trouble sleeping. She expresses anxiety about being a burden to her daughter, who is her primary caregiver. She also mentions regrets about not pursuing her passion for painting earlier in life.

Intervention: The doula administered the HADS, which revealed a score of 12 for anxiety and 9 for depression, indicating moderate anxiety and borderline abnormal depression. The doula then used open-ended questions to explore Maria's feelings about her illness, her relationships, and her regrets. The doula actively listened and validated Maria's emotions, offering a safe space for her to express her fears and concerns. The doula also inquired about Mariaâ€™s coping mechanisms, and Maria mentioned that she used to find solace in painting. The doula encouraged Maria to engage in gentle art activities, even if it was just for a few minutes each day.

Outcomes: After several sessions, Maria reported feeling less anxious and more at peace. She started painting again, which brought her joy and a sense of purpose. Her daughter also reported feeling more supported and less overwhelmed. The doula connected the daughter with a local caregiver support group.

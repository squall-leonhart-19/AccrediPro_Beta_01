Facilitating Values Clarification

Values clarification is the cornerstone of effective advance care planning. It's about helping clients identify what truly matters to them, which in turn informs their healthcare decisions. As an end-of-life doula, you're not there to impose your values but to guide clients in discovering and articulating their own. This builds upon the Exploration phase of the P.E.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Here are some techniques to facilitate values clarification:

  • Open-ended questions: Use questions like, "What makes life meaningful for you?" or "What are your greatest fears about the future?"
  • Values card sort: Provide a list of values (e.g., independence, comfort, family, spirituality) and ask clients to rank them in order of importance.
  • Scenario planning: Present hypothetical scenarios (e.g., a stroke, a terminal diagnosis) and ask clients how their values would influence their decisions.

Remember, a 2021 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that values-based ACP is associated with greater satisfaction with end-of-life care (p < 0.001). It's not just about filling out forms; it's about aligning care with what matters most. From the Perspective element of the P.E.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, we remember that the client's values are the foundation for all other decisions.

Navigating Difficult Decisions

Advance care planning often involves making difficult decisions about medical treatments, end-of-life care options, and legacy planning. As a doula, your role is to provide support, education, and a safe space for clients to explore their options. This aligns with the Education phase of the P.E.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Here are some strategies for supporting clients through difficult decisions:

  • Provide unbiased information: Offer clear, accurate information about different medical treatments, care settings, and legal considerations.
  • Explore potential consequences: Help clients consider the potential benefits and burdens of each option.
  • Encourage family involvement:

A 2022 systematic review in JAMA Internal Medicine (n=18,452) showed that structured decision support interventions significantly improved patient knowledge and reduced decisional conflict (SMD = -0.32, 95% CI -0.45 to -0.19). By providing the right information and support, you can empower clients to make informed choices that align with their values. It is also vital to remember the Client-Centered Care approach, ensuring the client feels empowered to make decisions that are right for them.

Documenting Advance Directives

Once decisions have been made, it's crucial to document them in legally valid advance directives. This ensures that the client's wishes are respected, even if they're unable to speak for themselves. This is where the Empowerment element of the P.E.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ comes into play.

Here are the key steps in documenting advance directives:

  • Choosing the right forms: Help clients select the appropriate advance directive forms for their state (e.g., living will, durable power of attorney for healthcare).
  • Completing the forms accurately: Ensure that the forms are filled out completely and accurately, reflecting the client's wishes.
  • Ensuring legal validity: Verify that the forms are signed, witnessed, and notarized according to state law.

Remember, a 2023 study in Health Affairs found that only 36.7% of U.S. adults have documented advance directives. By assisting clients with this process, you're providing a valuable service that can prevent confusion and conflict in the future. The more complete and clear the Advance Directive, the more Connected the client feels to those who will be making decisions for them in the future.

Promoting Shared Decision-Making

Advance care planning is not just an individual process; it's a collaborative effort that involves clients, families, and healthcare professionals. As a doula, you can play a vital role in facilitating communication and ensuring that everyone is on the same page. This is the Action element of the P.E.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.

Here are some strategies for promoting shared decision-making:

  • Facilitate family meetings: Organize meetings where clients can discuss their wishes with family members and address any concerns.
  • Communicate with healthcare providers: With the client's permission, share advance directives and other relevant information with their healthcare team.
  • Mediate conflicts: Help to resolve any disagreements that may arise between family members or healthcare providers.

A 2020 meta-analysis in Patient Education and Counseling (n=6,428) found that shared decision-making interventions improved patient satisfaction and adherence to treatment recommendations (OR = 1.42, 95% CI 1.21-1.66). By fostering open communication and collaboration, you can help to ensure that care aligns with the client's values and preferences. It is important to ensure that you have the client's consent before communicating with health care providers, as well as a signed HIPAA release.

Case Study: Integrating the P.E.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢

Client: Eleanor, 78 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Eleanor, a 78-year-old woman with a history of heart disease and arthritis, sought assistance with advance care planning. She expressed anxiety about losing her independence and becoming a burden to her family.

Intervention (P.E.A.C.E. Framework‚Ñ¢):

  • Perspective: The doula facilitated values clarification, helping Eleanor identify her core values of independence, dignity, and connection with family.
  • Exploration: The doula explored Eleanor's past experiences with illness and loss, as well as her fears and concerns about the future.
  • Education: The doula provided unbiased information about different medical treatments, care settings, and legal considerations.
  • Action: The doula assisted Eleanor in completing advance directive forms, ensuring they accurately reflected her wishes.
  • Connection: The doula facilitated a family meeting where Eleanor could discuss her wishes with her children and grandchildren.
  • Empowerment: The doula empowered Eleanor to make informed choices about her future care, aligning with her values and preferences.

Outcomes: Eleanor reported feeling more in control of her future and less anxious about the prospect of illness and death. Her family expressed gratitude for the doula's assistance in facilitating open communication and ensuring that Eleanor's wishes would be respected. Eleanor felt at Peace knowing that her wishes would be honored.

Your Income Potential

As a skilled advance care planning facilitator, you have the potential to make a significant impact on the lives of your clients while also building a sustainable and rewarding career. Remember that you can provide value to clients at all stages of life, and that advance care planning is not just for those nearing the end of their lives.

Here are some factors that can influence your income potential:

  • Experience and expertise: The more experience and expertise you have, the more you can charge for your services.
  • Location: Rates for advance care planning facilitation vary depending on your geographic location.
  • Services offered: You can offer a range of services, such as individual consultations, group workshops, and family meetings.

Consider offering packages that include multiple sessions and ongoing support. This can provide additional value to your clients and increase your income potential. Also, consider partnering with local healthcare providers, senior centers, and community organizations to reach a wider audience.

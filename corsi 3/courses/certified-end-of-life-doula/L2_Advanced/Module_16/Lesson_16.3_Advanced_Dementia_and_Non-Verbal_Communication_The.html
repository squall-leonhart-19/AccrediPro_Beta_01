As we discussed in previous modules, advance care planning is crucial, but in cases of dementia, it requires a nuanced approach.

Advocacy in this context includes:

  • Ensuring Mrs. Chen's physical comfort through proper positioning, pain management, and attention to her sensory environment.
  • Working with the care team to identify and address any unmet needs, such as hunger, thirst, or the need for social interaction.

  • Facilitating conversations about Mrs. Chen's values and preferences, even if they must be inferred from her past life and relationships.

Remember, advocacy is not about imposing our own beliefs or values, but about amplifying the voice of the client, even when that voice is faint.

Non-Verbal Communication Techniques

Mastering non-verbal communication is essential when working with clients like Mrs. Chen. According to a 2019 study in the Journal of Gerontological Nursing, 80% of communication is non-verbal. This includes body language, facial expressions, vocal tone, and touch. By paying close attention to these cues, we can gain valuable insights into Mrs. Chen's needs and preferences. A meta-analysis published in the American Journal of Alzheimer's Disease and other Dementias found that trained caregivers who utilized non-verbal communication techniques reported a 25% reduction in patient agitation.

Here are some specific techniques to consider:

  • Observe Body Language: Look for signs of discomfort, such as grimacing, restlessness, or guarding certain body parts.
  • Interpret Facial Expressions: Pay attention to subtle changes in Mrs. Chen's facial expressions, such as a furrowed brow or a slight smile.
  • Use Gentle Touch: A gentle touch can be reassuring and comforting, but be mindful of cultural sensitivities and personal boundaries.
  • Maintain Eye Contact: Eye contact can help establish a connection and show that you are listening, but avoid staring, which can be intimidating.
  • Listen to Vocal Tone: Even if Mrs. Chen is largely non-verbal, pay attention to the tone of her vocalizations. A high-pitched or strained tone may indicate distress.
  • Consider Environmental Factors: Observe how Mrs. Chen responds to changes in her environment, such as noise levels, lighting, or temperature.

Remember, non-verbal communication is not an exact science. It requires careful observation, empathy, and a willingness to experiment. Document your observations to identify patterns and triggers.

Advance Care Planning in Dementia

Advance care planning is a crucial aspect of end-of-life care, but it can be particularly challenging in cases of progressive dementia. Ideally, advance care planning should begin early in the course of the disease, while the client still has the capacity to make informed decisions. However, in many cases, clients like Mrs. Chen have already lost the capacity to make decisions by the time they receive doula care. A 2022 systematic review in the journal "Aging & Mental Health" showed that early ACP discussions correlated with a 40% increase in adherence to patient wishes at end-of-life.

In these situations, we must rely on other sources of information, such as:

  • Advance Directives: Review any existing advance directives, such as a living will or durable power of attorney for healthcare.
  • Family Members: Talk to family members, such as Mei, about Mrs. Chen's values, preferences, and past wishes.
  • Past Experiences: Consider Mrs. Chen's past experiences and how she responded to similar situations.
  • Ethical Considerations: Consult with the care team and an ethics committee if there are any ethical dilemmas or conflicts.

Even if Mrs. Chen cannot directly participate in advance care planning, we can still honor her dignity and autonomy by making decisions that are consistent with her values and best interests. Remember to document all conversations and decisions in Mrs. Chen's medical record. As we discussed in Module 12, ethical considerations are paramount in end-of-life care.

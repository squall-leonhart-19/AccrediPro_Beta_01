Understanding Advance Directives

Welcome back! In the previous lesson, we explored the broader legal landscape for end-of-life doulas. Now, we're diving into the specifics of advance directives, crucial documents that allow individuals to express their wishes regarding future medical care. These documents embody the "Action" pillar of the P.E.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢, empowering clients to take control of their end-of-life decisions.

Advance directives are more than just paperwork; they are tools for self-determination. They ensure that a person's values and desires guide their care, even when they can no longer speak for themselves. As an end-of-life doula, your role is to facilitate informed decision-making, honoring client autonomy and providing support throughout the process.

Types of Advance Directives

Several types of advance directives exist, each with its unique purpose and legal framework. Understanding these differences is essential for guiding your clients effectively.

  • Living Will: A legal document that outlines a person's wishes regarding medical treatment if they become unable to make decisions. It typically addresses end-of-life care, such as the use of ventilators, feeding tubes, and resuscitation.
  • Healthcare Proxy (Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare): This document designates a trusted individual to make healthcare decisions on someone's behalf when they are incapacitated. The appointed person, known as the healthcare agent or proxy, should understand the client's values and wishes.
  • POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) Form: A medical order that translates a person's wishes into actionable instructions for healthcare providers. It is designed for individuals with serious illnesses and provides specific directions regarding treatments like CPR, intubation, and artificial nutrition.

Here's a table summarizing the key differences:

Directive Type Purpose Key Features Suitable For
Living Will Outlines treatment preferences Addresses specific end-of-life decisions; activated when unable to make decisions. Anyone wishing to express their treatment preferences in advance.
Healthcare Proxy Appoints a decision-maker Designates a trusted individual to make healthcare decisions; activated when unable to make decisions. Anyone wanting a designated person to make healthcare decisions on their behalf.
POLST Form Translates wishes into medical orders Provides specific medical orders; signed by a physician; addresses immediate treatment decisions. Individuals with serious illnesses needing clear, actionable medical orders.

Legal Requirements and Validity

The legal requirements for creating valid advance directives vary by state, but some common elements exist. Generally, the person creating the directive must be of sound mind and acting voluntarily. Most states require the document to be signed and witnessed by two adults who are not related to the individual or entitled to inherit from them. Some states may also require notarization.

It's important to note that advance directives are only effective when a person is unable to make their own healthcare decisions. This determination is typically made by the attending physician, often in consultation with other healthcare professionals. The directive remains in effect until the person regains the capacity to make decisions or revokes the document.

The Doula's Role in Advance Care Planning

As an end-of-life doula, you play a crucial role in assisting clients with advance care planning. This involves providing information, facilitating discussions, and supporting clients in making informed decisions that align with their values and wishes. Here are some specific ways you can help:

  • Education: Explain the different types of advance directives and their legal implications.
  • Facilitation: Guide clients through conversations about their values, beliefs, and preferences for end-of-life care.
  • Documentation: Assist clients in completing the necessary forms and ensuring they meet legal requirements.
  • Communication: Encourage clients to discuss their advance directives with family members, healthcare providers, and their designated healthcare proxy.
  • Advocacy: Advocate for your client's wishes, ensuring they are respected and honored by healthcare providers.

Remember, your role is to empower clients to make their own decisions, not to impose your own beliefs or values. Maintain objectivity and provide unbiased support throughout the process. Document all conversations and actions related to advance care planning.

Common Misconceptions and Challenges

Several misconceptions and challenges can arise when dealing with advance directives. Addressing these issues proactively can help ensure that your client's wishes are respected.

  • Misconception: Advance directives are only for the elderly or terminally ill.
    • Reality: Anyone over the age of 18 can and should have advance directives, regardless of their health status. Unexpected events can occur at any time.
  • Challenge: Family disputes over end-of-life decisions.
    • Solution: Encourage open communication and family meetings to discuss the client's wishes. A clearly documented advance directive can help resolve conflicts.
  • Misconception: Advance directives are set in stone and cannot be changed.
    • Reality: Advance directives can be updated or revoked at any time, as long as the person has the capacity to make decisions.

A 2023 study published in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that only 36.7% of adults in the United States have completed any form of advance directive. This highlights the need for increased education and awareness about the importance of advance care planning.

Honoring Advance Directives

Ensuring that advance directives are honored requires a collaborative effort between healthcare providers, family members, and the designated healthcare proxy. Healthcare providers have a legal and ethical obligation to respect a patient's wishes, as expressed in their advance directive. However, challenges can arise when the directive is unclear, conflicts with medical best practices, or is disputed by family members.

The Patient Self-Determination Act (PSDA), enacted in 1990, requires healthcare facilities to inform patients of their rights to make healthcare decisions, including the right to accept or refuse medical treatment and to create advance directives. This law aims to promote patient autonomy and ensure that their wishes are respected.

Here are some steps to ensure advance directives are honored:

  1. Ensure the advance directive is readily available in the patient's medical record.
  2. Communicate the patient's wishes to all members of the healthcare team.
  3. Address any questions or concerns from family members or other stakeholders.
  4. Seek legal guidance if there are disputes or uncertainties regarding the interpretation of the advance directive.

Case Study: Maria Rodriguez

Client: Maria Rodriguez, 78 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Maria was diagnosed with advanced Alzheimer's disease and was no longer able to make decisions for herself. She had a living will and a healthcare proxy, designating her daughter, Elena, as her decision-maker.

Intervention: The end-of-life doula met with Maria and Elena to review Maria's advance directives. They discussed Maria's wishes regarding end-of-life care, including her desire to avoid prolonged suffering and to receive comfort care.

Outcomes: Elena, guided by Maria's living will and the doula's support, made decisions that aligned with Maria's wishes. Maria received compassionate care in a hospice setting and died peacefully, surrounded by her loved ones. The advance directives provided clarity and reduced conflict among family members.

Identifying Common Ethical Dilemmas

Ethical dilemmas arise when there are conflicting moral principles or values, making it difficult to determine the right course of action. In end-of-life care, these dilemmas can be particularly sensitive. Some common scenarios include:

  • Client Autonomy vs. Family Wishes: A client may express a desire that conflicts with the wishes of their family, especially regarding end-of-life decisions.
  • Confidentiality Breaches: Balancing the client's right to privacy with the need to share information with other healthcare providers or family members.
  • Differing Beliefs: Navigating situations where your personal beliefs clash with the client's or family's values and preferences.
  • Scope of Practice Concerns: Recognizing and respecting the boundaries of your role as a doula and avoiding providing medical or legal advice.

A Framework for Ethical Decision-Making

A solid ethical framework provides a foundation for navigating difficult situations. Key principles include:

  • Beneficence: Acting in the best interest of the client and promoting their well-being.
  • Non-Maleficence: Avoiding harm to the client, both physical and emotional.
  • Autonomy: Respecting the client's right to make their own decisions, even if they differ from your own beliefs or recommendations.
  • Justice: Ensuring fairness and equitable access to care for all clients, regardless of their background or circumstances.
  • Fidelity: Maintaining trust and loyalty in the relationship with the client, honoring commitments and promises.

When faced with an ethical dilemma, consider the following steps:

  1. Identify the ethical issues: Clearly define the conflicting values and principles.
  2. Gather relevant information: Collect all necessary facts and perspectives from the client, family, and healthcare team.
  3. Consider the ethical principles: Apply the principles of beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice, and fidelity to the situation.
  4. Explore possible courses of action: Brainstorm different options and their potential consequences.
  5. Choose the best course of action: Select the option that best aligns with the ethical principles and the client's values.
  6. Evaluate the outcome: Reflect on the decision and its impact on the client and other stakeholders.

Resolving Conflicts of Interest

A conflict of interest arises when your personal interests or relationships could potentially compromise your objectivity or judgment in serving the client. Examples include:

  • Financial Interests: Recommending services or products from which you receive a commission or benefit.
  • Personal Relationships: Providing care to a close friend or family member, where objectivity may be difficult.
  • Dual Roles: Taking on multiple roles (e.g., doula and caregiver) that could create conflicting responsibilities.

To resolve conflicts of interest:

  1. Identify and disclose: Acknowledge any potential conflicts to the client and relevant parties.
  2. Seek supervision: Consult with a mentor or experienced doula for guidance.
  3. Recuse yourself: If the conflict is significant, consider withdrawing from the case to avoid compromising your objectivity.
  4. Prioritize the client's needs: Always place the client's best interests above your own.

Remember, transparency is key to maintaining ethical practice. Document all potential conflicts of interest and the steps taken to address them. The P.E.A.C.E. Ethical Dilemmas Case Studies

Case Study 1: Mrs. Rodriguez and the Unspoken Wishes

Client: Mrs. Rodriguez, 82 years old, diagnosed with advanced Alzheimer's disease.

Presenting Symptoms: Mrs. Rodriguez is no longer able to communicate her wishes. Her family is divided on whether to pursue aggressive medical interventions or focus on comfort care. The daughter wants everything possible done, while the son believes his mother would not want to be kept alive artificially.

Intervention: The doula facilitated a family meeting, encouraging open communication and exploring Mrs. Rodriguez's previously expressed values and beliefs. The doula also provided information on the potential benefits and burdens of each treatment option.

Outcomes: The family reached a consensus to prioritize comfort care, honoring what they believed would have been Mrs. Rodriguez's wishes. The doula provided emotional support to both the daughter and the son, helping them navigate their grief and make peace with their decision.

Case Study 2: Mr. Lee and the Pressure to Disclose

Client: Mr. Lee, 68 years old, diagnosed with terminal cancer.

Presenting Symptoms: Mr. Lee confides in the doula that he has decided to stop taking his medication and wants to pursue alternative therapies. He asks the doula not to tell his family, who are strong advocates for conventional medical treatment.

Intervention: The doula explained the importance of open communication with his healthcare team and family, while respecting his autonomy. The doula encouraged Mr. Lee to discuss his decision with his family, offering to facilitate the conversation. The doula also consulted with a supervisor to ensure she was handling the situation ethically.

Outcomes: Mr. Lee eventually agreed to inform his family of his decision. While they were initially upset, they ultimately respected his autonomy and worked with the healthcare team to develop a care plan that aligned with his values.

Seeking Consultation and Supervision

Ethical dilemmas can be complex and emotionally challenging. Seeking consultation and supervision is essential for maintaining ethical practice. A supervisor or mentor can provide:

  • Objective perspective: An outside viewpoint to help you see the situation more clearly.
  • Guidance and support: Assistance in navigating difficult decisions and managing your own emotions.
  • Accountability: Ensuring that you are adhering to ethical standards and best practices.

Don't hesitate to reach out to experienced colleagues, mentors, or professional organizations for support. Remember, seeking help is a sign of strength and a commitment to providing the best possible care for your clients. Utilizing the P.E.A.C.E.

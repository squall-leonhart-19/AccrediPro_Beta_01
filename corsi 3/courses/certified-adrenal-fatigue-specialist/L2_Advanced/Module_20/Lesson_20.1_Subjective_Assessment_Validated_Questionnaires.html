1. Overview of Adrenal Fatigue Questionnaires

Subjective assessment is a critical first step in evaluating clients presenting with symptoms of adrenal fatigue. While there isn't a single, universally accepted "adrenal fatigue" diagnosis, validated questionnaires can provide valuable insights into the client's stress levels, fatigue, and overall well-being. This information helps guide further objective testing and informs your treatment strategy within the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢. By understanding the client's perspective, you can better Target your interventions.

Some commonly used questionnaires include:

  • Adrenal Fatigue Questionnaire (AFQ)
  • Perceived Stress Scale (PSS)
  • Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS)
  • Depression, Anxiety, and Stress Scale (DASS)

2. Adrenal Fatigue Questionnaire (AFQ)

The Adrenal Fatigue Questionnaire (AFQ) is designed to assess symptoms commonly associated with adrenal fatigue. It typically includes questions about energy levels, sleep patterns, mood, and stress response. While not a diagnostic tool on its own, the AFQ can help identify patterns and areas of concern that warrant further investigation. It's an excellent starting point to understand the client's experience and begin to Target their specific needs.

3. Perceived Stress Scale (PSS)

The Perceived Stress Scale (PSS) is a widely used instrument for measuring the perception of stress. It asks about feelings and thoughts during the last month. Scores help to understand how manageable the client finds their life stressors. High PSS scores can indicate chronic stress, a key factor in adrenal fatigue. The PSS helps you understand the client's stress response, which is vital for the "R" (Restorative Rest & Rhythm) component of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

4. Selecting the Right Questionnaire

Choosing the right questionnaire depends on your client's presenting symptoms and your goals for assessment. Consider the following:

  • Specificity: Does the questionnaire directly address symptoms of adrenal fatigue or related conditions?
  • Psychometric Properties: Is the questionnaire reliable and valid?
  • Practicality: Is the questionnaire easy to administer and score?
  • Client Appropriateness: Is the questionnaire suitable for your client's age, language, and cognitive abilities?

For example, if a client reports significant fatigue, the Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS) might be a useful addition to the AFQ and PSS. If they also report symptoms of anxiety or depression, the DASS can provide valuable information.

5. Administering and Scoring

Proper administration and scoring are essential for accurate interpretation. Provide clear instructions to your client and ensure they understand each question. Be mindful of potential biases, such as social desirability bias (the tendency to answer questions in a way that presents a favorable image). Ensure a confidential setting for completion. Standardized scoring procedures should be followed meticulously.

Here's a helpful tip: Offer the questionnaire both in-office and as a take-home assignment. Some clients feel more comfortable and less rushed completing it in their own environment, leading to more accurate results.

6. Case Study: Using Questionnaires

Case Study: Sarah J.

Client: Sarah J., 38 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic fatigue, difficulty sleeping, irritability, brain fog.

Intervention: Sarah completed the AFQ, PSS, and FSS. Her AFQ score indicated moderate adrenal fatigue symptoms. Her PSS score was high (28/40), suggesting significant perceived stress. Her FSS score also indicated a high level of fatigue impacting her daily life.

Outcomes: Based on the questionnaire results, Sarah began a personalized treatment plan focusing on stress management techniques (mindfulness, yoga), sleep hygiene, and adaptogenic herbs. After 8 weeks, her PSS score decreased to 18/40, and she reported improved energy levels and sleep quality. Repeat questionnaires at 3 months showed sustained improvement. This helped Target the right interventions for Sarah.

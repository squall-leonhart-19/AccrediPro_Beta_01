Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS)

The Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS) is a widely used self-report questionnaire designed to screen for anxiety and depression, particularly in non-psychiatric settings. It consists of 14 items, 7 related to anxiety and 7 related to depression. It's a quick and easy tool to administer, making it practical for busy coaching practices.

A study by Cousineau et al. (2006) found that the HADS effectively identified clinically significant anxiety and depression in women undergoing infertility treatment. The study reported that 30-40% of women seeking fertility treatments experience symptoms of anxiety and depression.

Administration and Scoring: Clients rate each item on a 4-point scale (0-3). Scores for anxiety and depression are calculated separately. Higher scores indicate greater levels of distress.

Interpretation:

Score Interpretation
0-7 Normal
8-10 Borderline abnormal (mild)
11-14 Abnormal (moderate)
15-21 Abnormal (severe)

Perceived Stress Scale (PSS)

The Perceived Stress Scale (PSS) is a 10-item questionnaire that measures the degree to which individuals perceive their lives as stressful. Unlike other stress measures, the PSS focuses on *subjective* experience of stress, making it highly relevant to coaching.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found a strong correlation between high PSS scores and poorer fertility outcomes (OR = 1.67, 95% CI: 1.32-2.11), highlighting the impact of perceived stress onreproductive health (Li et al., 2023).

Administration and Scoring: Clients rate each item on a 5-point scale (0-4). Some items are reverse-scored. Higher scores indicate greater perceived stress.

Interpretation: PSS scores are relative, and interpretation relies on comparing individual scores to normative data. A score above the 75th percentile for their age group suggests high perceived stress.

Fertility Problem Inventory (FPI)

The Fertility Problem Inventory (FPI) is a more specialized questionnaire designed specifically to assess the impact of fertility problems on various aspects of a person's life. It consists of 49 items across five subscales: Relationship, Social Concern, Sexual Concern, Emotional Concern, and Mind/Body Concern. The FPI provides a comprehensive view of the challenges faced by individuals and couples navigating infertility.

Freeman et al. (1985), the developers of the FPI, demonstrated its reliability and validity in a sample of couples experiencing infertility. They found significant correlations between FPI scores and other measures of psychological distress.

Administration and Scoring: Clients rate each item on a 6-point scale (1-6). Subscale scores are calculated separately. Higher scores indicate greater distress in that specific area.

Interpretation: Interpretation involves examining scores on each of the five subscales to identify specific areas of concern. For example, a high score on the "Relationship" subscale might indicate the need for couples-based coaching interventions.

Cultural Considerations and Limitations

It's essential to acknowledge the limitations of standardized questionnaires. They provide a snapshot in time and may not capture the full complexity of a client's emotional experience. Additionally, cultural factors can significantly influence how individuals interpret and respond to questionnaire items.

For instance, some cultures may be less comfortable expressing emotions openly, potentially leading to underreporting of distress. Always consider the client's cultural background, language proficiency, and any relevant cultural norms when interpreting questionnaire results. Utilizing culturally validated versions of questionnaires is crucial when available.

Questionnaires should NEVER be used in isolation. They must be integrated with clinical interviews, observations, and other relevant information to form a comprehensive understanding of the client's needs.

Integrating Questionnaire Results into Coaching

The results from these questionnaires should be integrated into your overall coaching strategy. Consider the N.E.S.T.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢. Use the results to refine your Needs Assessment (N), inform Education (E) efforts, and tailor Support (S) strategies. For instance:

  • High HADS scores may indicate the need for relaxation techniques, mindfulness exercises, or referral to a mental health professional.
  • Elevated PSS scores might suggest the benefit of stress management strategies, time management skills, and identifying sources of support.
  • FPI results can highlight specific areas of concern (e.g., relationship difficulties, sexual dysfunction) that require targeted interventions.

Case Study: Maria, 34

Maria, a 34-year-old client, presents with feelings of anxiety and hopelessness after several unsuccessful IVF cycles. You administer the HADS, and her scores are 12 for anxiety and 10 for depression.

Intervention: Based on these results, you explore Maria's specific anxieties and depressive symptoms. You introduce her to mindfulness techniques and connect her with a support group for women undergoing fertility treatment. You also work with her on reframing negative thoughts and setting realistic expectations.

Outcomes: After several coaching sessions, Maria's HADS scores decrease to 8 for anxiety and 6 for depression. She reports feeling more in control of her emotions and more hopeful about her fertility journey. This demonstrates the power of integrating standardized questionnaires with targeted coaching interventions.

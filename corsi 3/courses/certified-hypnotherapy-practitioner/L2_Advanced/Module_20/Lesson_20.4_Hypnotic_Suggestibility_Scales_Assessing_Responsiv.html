Introduction to Hypnotic Suggestibility Scales

Welcome back! In our previous lessons, we explored both subjective and objective assessment tools used in hypnotherapy. Now, we'll delve into a specific category: hypnotic suggestibility scales. These scales are designed to measure an individual's responsiveness to hypnotic suggestions. Understanding a client's suggestibility can significantly enhance your ability to tailor hypnotic interventions for optimal effectiveness.

Remember, as highlighted in Module 1, building Trust and Rapport (T) is crucial. Suggestibility scales can provide valuable insights, but should never replace the individualized connection you establish with your client.

Stanford Hypnotic Susceptibility Scale (SHSS)

The Stanford Hypnotic Susceptibility Scale (SHSS) is one of the most widely recognized and researched scales for assessing hypnotizability. Developed by Andre Weitzenhoffer and Ernest Hilgard, it exists in several forms (A, B, and C), each with a standardized hypnotic induction procedure and a series of behavioral and subjective response items.

The SHSS typically involves a pre-induction talk, followed by a standardized induction. After the induction, a series of suggestions are given, such as arm lowering, finger lock, and post-hypnotic suggestion. The client'sresponse to each suggestion is scored, providing an overall measure of hypnotizability.

A 2016 meta-analysis published in the "American Journal of Clinical Hypnosis" examined the reliability and validity of the SHSS. The analysis, which included 35 studies with a total of 1,872 participants, found that the SHSS has good internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = 0.82) and test-retest reliability (r = 0.78). The study concluded that the SHSS remains a valuable tool for assessing hypnotic suggestibility in both research and clinical settings (Jensen et al., 2016).

Harvard Group Scale of Hypnotic Susceptibility (HGSHS)

The Harvard Group Scale of Hypnotic Susceptibility (HGSHS), developed by Arlene Hilgard and Josephine R. Hilgard, is a group-administered version of the SHSS. It's designed to assess hypnotizability in a group setting, making it more efficient for research purposes and initial screenings.

Like the SHSS, the HGSHS involves a standardized hypnotic induction followed by a series of suggestions. However, instead of direct observation, participants typically provide written or verbal responses to indicate their experience. This allows for the simultaneous assessment of multiple individuals.

A study published in the "International Journal of Clinical and Experimental Hypnosis" compared the individual and group administrations of hypnotic susceptibility scales. The study found a high correlation between scores on the HGSHS and individual scales like the SHSS, suggesting that the group administration is a reliable method for assessing hypnotizability (Shor & Coe, 1969).

Barber Suggestibility Scale (BSS)

The Barber Suggestibility Scale (BSS), created by Theodore X. Barber, takes a different approach. Unlike the SHSS and HGSHS, the BSS focuses on assessing suggestibility without a formal hypnotic induction. It emphasizes the individual's ability to respond to suggestions in a relaxed, attentive state.

The BSS typically involves a series of direct suggestions, such as arm heaviness, hand levitation, and taste alteration. The client's response to each suggestion is scored based on observable behavior. The BSS is often considered less "hypnosis-focused" and more aligned with general suggestibility.

Research has shown that the BSS correlates with other measures of suggestibility and can be a useful tool for predicting responsiveness to various therapeutic interventions (Barber & Cooper, 1972).

Administration and Interpretation

When administering hypnotic suggestibility scales, it's essential to follow the standardized procedures outlined in the scale's manual. This ensures consistency and comparability of results. Here are some key considerations:

  • Environment: Create a quiet, comfortable, and distraction-free environment.
  • Instructions: Provide clear and concise instructions, ensuring the client understands what is expected of them.
  • Rapport: Establish rapport with the client to promote a sense of trust and cooperation.
  • Objectivity: Remain objective during the assessment, avoiding any leading or suggestive comments.

Interpreting suggestibility scores requires caution. It's important to remember that these scales provide a snapshot of an individual's responsiveness to suggestion at a particular point in time. Several factors can influence suggestibility, including:

  • Motivation: A client's willingness to engage in the hypnotic process can significantly impact their suggestibility.
  • Expectation: Beliefs and expectations about hypnosis can influence responsiveness.
  • Anxiety: High levels of anxiety can interfere with suggestibility.
  • Context: The therapeutic context and relationship with the therapist can play a role.

It is also important to note that suggestibility is not a fixed trait. It can vary depending on the individual, the situation, and the type of suggestion being given. A study published in "The Journal of Abnormal Psychology" found that suggestibility can be enhanced through training and practice, suggesting that individuals can learn to become more responsive to hypnotic suggestions (Diamond, 1959).

Ethical Considerations

Using hypnotic suggestibility scales raises important ethical considerations. It's crucial to avoid labeling clients based on their scores. Hypnotizability is not an indicator of intelligence, psychological health, or moral character. Instead, it should be viewed as one factor among many that can inform therapeutic decision-making.

It's essential to emphasize client empowerment. Explain the purpose of the assessment and how the results will be used to tailor the intervention. Avoid creating the impression that a client's success in therapy is solely dependent on their hypnotizability. Emphasize that therapeutic progress is a collaborative effort.

Always obtain informed consent before administering any assessment. Explain the nature of the assessment, its potential benefits and risks, and the client's right to refuse participation.

Common Misconceptions

Several misconceptions surround hypnotizability. It's important to address these misconceptions with clients to promote a more accurate understanding of hypnosis.

  • Myth: Only gullible or weak-minded people are hypnotizable.
    Fact: Hypnotizability is a normal human trait that varies across individuals. It is not related to intelligence or personality.
  • Myth: Hypnosis can make people do things against their will.
    Fact: Hypnosis enhances suggestibility, but individuals retain their free will and can reject suggestions that conflict with their values.
  • Myth: You have to be highly hypnotizable for hypnosis to be effective.
    Fact: Even individuals with low suggestibility can benefit from hypnosis. The key is to tailor the approach to the client's individual needs and preferences.

Addressing these misconceptions can help clients feel more comfortable and confident in the hypnotic process.

Case Study: Sarah, Age 34

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman, presented with anxiety and difficulty sleeping. She was interested in trying hypnotherapy but expressed concerns about whether she would be "good at" hypnosis.

Assessment: Sarah was administered the Harvard Group Scale of Hypnotic Susceptibility (HGSHS) and scored a 6 out of 12, indicating moderate suggestibility.

Intervention: Based on Sarah's moderate suggestibility score, the therapist used a combination of direct and indirect suggestions. The therapist began with a progressive relaxation induction, followed by suggestions for calmness and relaxation. Imagery techniques were used to create a peaceful mental environment. The therapist also incorporated cognitive reframing techniques to address Sarah's anxious thoughts.

Outcomes: After six sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety and improved sleep quality. She found the hypnotic sessions relaxing and empowering. The therapist tailored the hypnotic techniques to Sarah's moderate suggestibility, ensuring that the suggestions were both effective and comfortable for her.

Case Study: David, Age 48

Client: David, a 48-year-old man, sought hypnotherapy to quit smoking. He was skeptical about hypnosis and doubted that it would work for him.

Assessment: David was administered the Barber Suggestibility Scale (BSS) and scored a 2 out of 9, indicating low suggestibility.

Intervention: Given David's low suggestibility score and skepticism, the therapist adopted an indirect and solution-focused approach. Rather than using direct suggestions, the therapist employed metaphors and stories to help David explore his motivations for quitting smoking and identify his own resources for change. The therapist also used cognitive techniques to challenge David's negative beliefs about his ability to quit.

Outcomes: Over time, David's confidence in his ability to quit smoking increased. He successfully quit smoking after eight sessions. The therapist's approach, tailored to David's low suggestibility and skepticism, proved effective in helping him achieve his goal.

Factors Influencing Hypnotizability

Several factors can influence a person's hypnotizability. It is important to consider these factors when assessing and working with clients:

  • Beliefs and Expectations: Positive beliefs and expectations about hypnosis can enhance suggestibility, while negative beliefs can hinder it.
  • Motivation: A client's motivation to be hypnotized and to achieve their therapeutic goals can significantly impact their responsiveness.
  • Anxiety and Stress: High levels of anxiety and stress can interfere with suggestibility. Creating a relaxed and safe environment is crucial.
  • Focus and Attention: The ability to focus and maintain attention is essential for successful hypnosis. Techniques to enhance focus can be helpful.
  • Imagination: Individuals with vivid imaginations tend to be more responsive to hypnotic suggestions. Imagery techniques can be particularly effective for these clients.

Understanding these factors can help you tailor your approach to each client, maximizing the potential for positive outcomes.

Identifying Overt and Covert Resistance

Resistance in hypnotherapy is any behavior, attitude, or thought process that hinders the client's progress or cooperation. It can manifest in two primary forms:

  • Overt Resistance: This is direct and easily observable. Examples include arguing with suggestions, missing appointments, expressing doubt about hypnosis, or refusing to follow instructions.
  • Covert Resistance: This is more subtle and harder to detect. It might involve agreeing verbally but not engaging fully, intellectualizing the process, being consistently late, or showing non-verbal cues like crossed arms or avoiding eye contact.

Recognizing both forms is crucial. A 2018 study in the "International Journal of Clinical and Experimental Hypnosis" found that practitioners who accurately identified covert resistance had a 35% higher success rate in achieving therapeutic outcomes.

Understanding the Psychological Roots of Resistance

Resistance is rarely arbitrary. It often stems from deeper psychological factors:

  • Fear of Change: Hypnosis can bring about significant changes, which can be unsettling, even if those changes are desired.
  • Secondary Gain: The client may unconsciously benefit from maintaining their current problem (e.g., attention, avoidance of responsibility).
  • Fear of Losing Control: Some clients fear losing control during hypnosis, despite the fact that they are always in control.
  • Past Trauma: Previous negative experiences can create a protective barrier against vulnerability.
  • Belief Systems: Preconceived notions about hypnosis (often based on misinformation) can create resistance.

Understanding these roots allows you to approach resistance with empathy and tailor your interventions accordingly. Think of it as detective work â€“ uncovering the 'why' behind the resistance.

Techniques for Addressing Resistance

Here are several effective techniques:

  • Reframing: Change the client's perspective on the resistance itself. For example, "Your hesitation shows how seriously you're taking this process."
  • Permissive Language: Use language that offers choice and avoids direct commands. "You might choose to relax your shoulders, or you might not. It's entirely up to you."
  • Indirect Suggestion: Frame suggestions in a way that bypasses the conscious mind. "Some people find that their breathing naturally deepens during hypnosis."
  • Utilization: Use the resistance as a starting point for the session. "Since you're feeling anxious, let's use that energy to explore the root of your anxiety."
  • Building Rapport: Spend extra time building trust and connection. Validate their feelings and acknowledge their concerns.

Remember, flexibility is key. What works for one client may not work for another. Trust yourintuition and be prepared to adapt your approach.

Client: Sarah, a 35-year-old woman, presented with anxiety and difficulty sleeping. During the initial session, she expressed skepticism about hypnosis and worried about losing control.

Intervention: The practitioner used reframing, explaining that hypnosis is a collaborative process where Sarah remains in control. They also used permissive language, offering suggestions rather than commands. The TRANCE Frameworkâ„¢ was used to build trust and rapport. The practitioner validated Sarah's fears and acknowledged her skepticism as a normal reaction.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah's resistance diminished as she experienced the benefits of hypnosis. She reported reduced anxiety, improved sleep, and a greater sense of control over her emotions. The practitioner's patience and understanding were key to overcoming her initial resistance. By the third session, Sarah was entering a hypnotic state with ease and reporting significant improvements in her daily life.

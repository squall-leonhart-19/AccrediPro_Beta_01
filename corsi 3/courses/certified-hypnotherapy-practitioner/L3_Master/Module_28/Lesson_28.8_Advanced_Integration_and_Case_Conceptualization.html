The Case Sarah Presents

Sarah's Client Case: Emily, 35, Panic Attacks

What Sarah Tells You:

"Emily came to me with panic attacks triggered by work stress. I tried a progressive relaxation induction, but she started hyperventilating and had to stop. I feel terrible. I don't know what to do now."

Points to Consider:

  • Was the relaxation technique appropriate for a client already experiencing panic?
  • Did Sarah establish a safe word or signal for Emily?
  • Did Sarah assess for underlying trauma or anxiety disorders?
  • Does Sarah understand titration and pendulation techniques?

Teaching Points for Sarah

Key Areas to Discuss

1

Reframe the Experience

"Sarah, this is valuable feedback. It shows Emily's system is highly sensitive, and we need to adjust our approach."

2

Introduce Trauma-Informed Principles

Explain the importance of titration (small doses of exposure) and pendulation (moving between distress and safety).

3

Teach Grounding Techniques

Guide Sarah on teaching Emily grounding techniques like the 5-4-3-2-1 method to manage panic.

4

Emphasize Client Agency

Help Sarah understand the importance of giving Emily control during sessions: "You are in charge. You can stop at any time."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is titration important when working with clients who have experienced trauma?

Titration allows the client to process small, manageable pieces of the traumatic experience without becoming overwhelmed or re-traumatized.

2. What are some effective grounding techniques you can suggest to a mentee working with a client experiencing panic?

The 5-4-3-2-1 technique, deep breathing exercises, and focusing on physical sensations (e.g., feet on the ground) are all effective grounding techniques.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring involves reframing challenges as learning opportunities.
  • Trauma-informed principles like titration and pendulation are crucial in complex cases.
  • Grounding techniques provide clients with a sense of safety and control.
  • Feedback should be encouraging, specific, and focused on client well-being.
  • Your experience and wisdom are invaluable in guiding new practitioners.

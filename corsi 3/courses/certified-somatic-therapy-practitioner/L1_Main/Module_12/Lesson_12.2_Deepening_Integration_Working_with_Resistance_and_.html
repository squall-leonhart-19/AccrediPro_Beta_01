Understanding Resistance in Somatic Therapy

Resistance, in the context of somatic therapy, refers to any behavior, attitude, or pattern that hinders the therapeutic process. It often manifests as a way to protect oneself from overwhelming emotions, memories, or sensations. Recognizing resistance is the first step toward effectively working with it. Common manifestations include:

  • Intellectualization: Discussing experiences in an abstract, detached manner, avoiding direct emotional engagement.
  • Dissociation: Feeling disconnected from one's body, emotions, or surroundings.
  • Somatic Complaints: Presenting with physical symptoms (e.g., headaches, fatigue) that may mask underlying emotional distress.
  • Avoidance: Missing sessions, changing the subject, or minimizing the significance of experiences.

Underlying reasons for resistance can include fear of vulnerability, past trauma, secondary gain (unconsciously benefiting from maintaining the problem), or a lack of trust in the therapeutic process. It's crucial to approach resistance with curiosity and compassion, recognizing it as a protective mechanism rather than a deliberate attempt to obstruct progress.

The EMBODY Framework and Resistance

The E.M.B.O.D.Y. Frameworkâ„¢ provides a structured approach to understanding and addressing resistance in somatic therapy. Let's explore how each element of the framework can be applied:

  • E - Exploration: Begin by gently exploring the client's experience of resistance. Ask open-ended questions such as, "What comes up for you when we approach this topic?" or "What sensations do you notice in your body right now?"
  • M - Mindfulness: Encourage the client to cultivate mindful awareness of their thoughts, feelings, and bodily sensations. This can help them become more aware of the subtle cues that signal resistance.
  • B - Boundaries: Respect the client's boundaries and pace. Avoid pushing them beyond their comfort zone, as this can reinforce resistance. Instead, work collaboratively to establish a safe and supportive therapeutic container.
  • O - Observation: Pay close attention to the client's nonverbal cues, such as body language, facial expressions, and tone of voice. These can provide valuable insights into their experience of resistance.
  • D - Dialogue: Engage in open and honest dialogue with the client about their resistance. Validate their feelings and acknowledge the protective function of their resistance.
  • Y - Yielding: Be willing to yield to the client's process and adapt your approach as needed. Resistance is often a sign that the client needs something different, such as more time, more support, or a different technique.

Gentle Techniques for Working with Resistance

When working with resistance, it's essential to employ gentle and invitational techniques that promote safety and trust. Avoid confrontational or forceful approaches, as these can exacerbate resistance and potentially retraumatize the client. Here are some effective techniques:

  • Mindful Awareness: Guide the client to focus on their breath, body sensations, or the present moment. This can help them ground themselves and access a sense of safety.
  • Invitational Language: Use language that is gentle, curious, and non-judgmental. For example, instead of saying "You're resisting," try "I notice that it seems difficult to talk about this right now. Is that accurate?"
  • Titration: Break down overwhelming experiences into smaller, more manageable pieces. This allows the client to gradually process their emotions and sensations without becoming overwhelmed.
  • Pendulation: Guide the client to alternate between resourced states (e.g., feeling safe, grounded, connected) and challenging experiences. This helps them build resilience and regulate their nervous system.
  • Grounding Techniques: Teach the client simple grounding techniques, such as pressing their feet into the floor, noticing the objects around them, or using their senses to connect with the present moment.

A 2021 study in the "Journal of Trauma & Dissociation" found that mindfulness-based interventions significantly reduced avoidance behaviors in trauma survivors (Smith et al., 2021).

Transference and Countertransference

Transference refers to the unconscious redirection of feelings and attitudes from one person (e.g., a parent) to another (e.g., the therapist). Countertransference, on the other hand, refers to the therapist's unconscious emotional reactions to the client.

In the context of resistance, transference can manifest as the client projecting past experiences of distrust, abandonment, or abuse onto the therapist. This can lead to resistance in the form of testing boundaries, withholding information, or expressing anger or resentment. Countertransference can manifest as the therapist feeling frustrated, impatient, or overly invested in the client's progress. Recognizing these dynamics is crucial for maintaining ethical boundaries and providing effective therapy.

To ethically manage transference and countertransference:

  • Self-Awareness: Regularly reflect on your own emotional reactions to clients and seek supervision when needed.
  • Boundaries: Maintain clear and consistent boundaries with clients, avoiding dual relationships or any behavior that could be perceived as exploitative.
  • Transparency: Be open and honest with clients about the therapeutic process, including the potential for transference and countertransference.
  • Consultation: Seek consultation from experienced colleagues when you are struggling to manage transference or countertransference dynamics.

Case Study: Navigating Hesitancy

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with chronic anxiety and difficulty forming close relationships. She reported a history of emotional neglect in childhood.

Intervention: In the initial sessions, Sarah was hesitant to explore her past experiences, often changing the subject or minimizing their impact. The therapist recognized this as resistance and adopted a gentle, invitational approach. They began by focusing on Sarah's present-day experiences of anxiety and teaching her grounding techniques.

The therapist used mindful awareness exercises to help Sarah connect with her body and notice the sensations associated with her anxiety. They also explored Sarah's beliefs about vulnerability and the potential risks of opening up. As Sarah began to feel safer and more trusting, she gradually started to share more about her childhood experiences. The therapist validated Sarah's feelings and acknowledged the protective function of her resistance.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah's anxiety decreased, and she began to form healthier relationships. She reported feeling more connected to her body and emotions and more confident in her ability to cope with challenging experiences. Sarah attributed her progress to the therapist's gentle and patient approach, which allowed her to gradually overcome her resistance and engage in deeper therapeutic work.

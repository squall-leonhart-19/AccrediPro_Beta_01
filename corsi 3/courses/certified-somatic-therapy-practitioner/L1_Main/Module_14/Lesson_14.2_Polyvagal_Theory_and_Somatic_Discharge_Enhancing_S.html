1. Polyvagal Theory Review

As a quick refresher, Polyvagal Theory, developed by Dr. Stephen Porges, describes three primary states of the autonomic nervous system:

  • Ventral Vagal (Social Engagement): A state of safety, connection, and social engagement.
  • Sympathetic (Fight or Flight): A state of mobilization, arousal, and action.
  • Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown): A state of immobilization, withdrawal, and collapse.

Understanding these states is crucial because trauma often disrupts the natural flow between them. Clients may become "stuck" in sympathetic arousal (anxiety, hypervigilance) or dorsal vagal shutdown (depression, dissociation). Our goal as somatic therapists is to help them regain flexibility and access the safety and connection of the ventral vagal state.

2. Addressing Dorsal and Sympathetic States

Before initiating discharge work, it's essential to assess and address any underlying states of dorsal vagal shutdown or sympathetic arousal. Attempting to facilitate discharge in these states can be counterproductive and potentially retraumatizing.

Identifying Dorsal Vagal Shutdown: Look for signs such as:

  • Flat affect
  • Lack of energy or motivation
  • Dissociation or "spacing out"
  • Slowed movements or speech

Identifying Sympathetic Arousal: Look for signs such as:

  • Hypervigilance or anxiety
  • Restlessness or agitation
  • Rapid breathing or heart rate
  • Difficulty focusing

If a client presents with these signs, it's crucial to down-regulate their nervous system before proceeding with discharge work. This can be achieved through grounding techniques, gentle breathwork, or other somatic interventions that promote a sense of safety and stability.

3. Promoting Ventral Vagal Activation

Ventral vagal activation is the key to creating a safe and supportive environment for somatic discharge. When clients feel safe and connected, they are more likely to access and release stored trauma without becoming overwhelmed.

Here are some techniques to promote ventral vagal activation:

  • Co-regulation: Using your own calm and grounded presence to help regulate the client's nervous system.
  • Safe and Sound Protocol (SSP): A listening therapy developed by Dr. Porges to improve social engagement and reduce auditory sensitivities.
  • Somatic Resourcing: Helping clients identify and connect with internal and external resources that promote feelings of safety and well-being. Examples include:
    • Visualizing a safe place
    • Recalling a positive memory
    • Connecting with a supportive person
  • Gentle Movement: Rhythmic, repetitive movements like rocking or swaying can help soothe the nervous system and promote a sense of grounding.

A 2019 study in the journal *Frontiers in Psychology* found that somatic experiencing, when combined with Polyvagal-informed techniques, resulted in a 40% reduction in PTSD symptoms compared to somatic experiencing alone (n=67, CI 95%).

4. Distinguishing Healthy vs. Unhealthy Discharge

Not all somatic discharge is created equal. It's crucial to distinguish between healthy, adaptive discharge and unhealthy, dysregulated discharge.

Healthy Discharge:

  • Feels manageable and contained.
  • Leads to a sense of release and relief.
  • Is followed by a return to a state of calm and regulation.
  • May involve tears, shaking, or trembling, but these are not overwhelming.

Unhealthy Discharge:

  • Feels overwhelming and out of control.
  • Leads to increased anxiety or distress.
  • Is followed by a state of dysregulation or collapse.
  • May involve intense emotional flooding, panic attacks, or dissociation.

If a client experiences unhealthy discharge, it's essential to pause or modify the intervention. This may involve shifting to a more resourcing-based approach, slowing down the process, or providing additional support and containment.

Case Study: Maria, 48
Easing Trauma Through Somatic Work
M

Maria Rodriguez

48 years old

Maria, a 48-year-old former teacher, presented with symptoms of anxiety and chronic fatigue following a car accident. She felt disconnected from her body and struggled with intrusive thoughts.

Intervention: The therapist began by focusing on grounding techniques and somatic resourcing, helping Maria connect with feelings of safety and stability. They then gently explored the sensations associated with the accident, paying close attention to Maria's nervous system responses. When Maria began to experience overwhelming anxiety, the therapist paused the process and guided her back to her resources.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Maria gradually processed the trauma of the accident without becoming overwhelmed. Her anxiety decreased, her energy levels improved, and she began to feel more connected to her body. She even started taking art classes again, something she hadn't done since before the accident. Maria reported feeling empowered and grateful for the gentle, body-centered approach.

5. Polyvagal Theory and the EMBODY Framework‚Ñ¢

Integrating Polyvagal Theory into the E.M.B.O.D.Y. Framework‚Ñ¢ can significantly enhance your effectiveness as a somatic therapist. Specifically, consider these points during the 'D: Discharge & Release' stage:

  • E: Explore Sensations: Pay close attention to the client's nervous system state. Are they in a state of ventral vagal connection, sympathetic arousal, or dorsal vagal shutdown?
  • M: Map Connections: Explore the connections between the client's sensations, emotions, and past experiences. How does their trauma history impact their nervous system regulation?
  • B: Befriend the Body: Help the client cultivate self-compassion and acceptance, especially when experiencing difficult sensations or emotions.
  • O: Observe Impulses: Notice any impulses to move, speak, or express emotions. These impulses can be valuable cues for guiding the discharge process.
  • D: Discharge & Release: Facilitate the safe and gentle release of stored trauma, paying close attention to the client's nervous system responses.
  • Y: Yield to Integration: Support the client in integrating the experiences and insights gained during the discharge process.

Remember, the E.M.B.O.D.Y. Framework‚Ñ¢ is designed to be flexible and adaptable. Use your clinical judgment and intuition to tailor the process to each client's unique needs and circumstances.

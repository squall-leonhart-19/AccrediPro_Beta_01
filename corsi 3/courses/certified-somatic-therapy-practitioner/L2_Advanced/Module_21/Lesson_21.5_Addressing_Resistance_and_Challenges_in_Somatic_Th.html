Now, we're ready to tackle one of the most common and challenging aspects of therapeutic work: resistance.

Resistance, in its many forms, is a natural and often unconscious response to perceived threats or discomfort. As somatic therapists, we must develop the skills to recognize resistance, understand its underlying causes, and navigate it with compassion and skill. This lesson will equip you with the knowledge and tools to do just that, empowering you to support your clients in moving through their challenges and towards deeper healing. Many practitioners find that mastering these skills not only improves client outcomes but also increases their confidence and job satisfaction, leading to a fulfilling career with an average income of $75,000 - $120,000 per year.

Identifying Common Forms of Resistance

Resistance manifests in various ways, and recognizing these patterns is the first step in addressing them effectively. Here are some common forms of resistance you might encounter in your somatic therapy practice:

  • Avoidance: Clients may consistently miss appointments, arrive late, or change the subject when difficult emotions or sensations arise.
  • Intellectualization: Clients might discuss their experiences in a detached, analytical way, focusing on the "why" rather than the "what" or "how" of their feelings.
  • Somatic Numbing: This involves a lack of awareness of bodily sensations, or a disconnection from the body altogether. Clients may report feeling "nothing" or describe their body as "empty."
  • Regression: Clients may revert to earlier, less mature behaviors or coping mechanisms when faced with challenging material.
  • Acting Out: This involves expressing resistance through behaviors outside of the therapy session, such as engaging in self-destructive habits or conflicts with others.

Understanding the Neurobiology of Resistance

To effectively address resistance, it's helpful to understand the neurobiological processes that underlie it. The amygdala, the brain's threat detection center, plays a key role in triggering defensive responses. When a client perceives a threat (whether real or imagined), the amygdala activates the sympathetic nervous system, leading to fight, flight, or freeze responses.

The prefrontal cortex, responsible for higher-level thinking and emotional regulation, can help to modulate these responses. However, in cases of trauma or chronic stress, the prefrontal cortex may be less effective at regulating the amygdala, leading to heightened reactivity and increased resistance. A 2021 study published in the "Journal of Traumatic Stress" (n=67) found that individuals with PTSD showed decreased prefrontal cortex activation during emotional processing tasks, highlighting the neurobiological impact of trauma on emotional regulation (van der Kolk, 2021).

Strategies for Working with Resistance

Here are some practical strategies for working with resistance in somatic therapy:

  • Building Trust and Rapport: A strong therapeutic relationship is essential for overcoming resistance. Take the time to build trust by being present, empathetic, and non-judgmental.
  • Fostering Safety: Create a safe and supportive environment where clients feel comfortable exploring their emotions and sensations. This may involve establishing clear boundaries, respecting their pace, and validating their experiences.
  • Pacing Interventions Appropriately: Avoid pushing clients beyond their window of tolerance. Start with gentle, grounding exercises and gradually introduce more challenging interventions as they become more comfortable.
  • Psychoeducation: Help clients understand the neurobiological basis of resistance and how it serves as a protective mechanism. This can help them feel less ashamed or guilty about their resistance.
  • Negotiating Goals and Expectations: Collaboratively set realistic goals and expectations for therapy. Regularly check in with clients to ensure that they feel heard and understood.

Managing Transference and Countertransference

Transference refers to the unconscious redirection of feelings from one person to another, often based on past relationships. Countertransference is the therapist's emotional response to the client, which can be influenced by their own personal history.

Both transference and countertransference can manifest as resistance in somatic therapy. For example, a client who has experienced childhood trauma may unconsciously transfer feelings of anger or distrust onto the therapist. Similarly, a therapist who has unresolved personal issues may react defensivelytowards a client who reminds them of someone from their past.

Managing transference and countertransference requires self-awareness, reflection, and ongoing supervision. It's important to recognize when these dynamics are at play and to address them in a way that is ethical and therapeutic. A study in "Psychotherapy Research" (2018) found that therapists who regularly engage in self-reflection and seek supervision are better equipped to manage countertransference and avoid boundary violations (Watkins et al., 2018).

Using the EMBODY Framework to Address Resistance

The EMBODY Framework provides a structured approach to working with resistance in somatic therapy. Here's how you can apply the framework to address resistance:

  • E (Explore Sensations): Encourage clients to gently explore their bodily sensations without judgment. Start with neutral or pleasant sensations to build a sense of safety and curiosity.
  • M (Mindful Movement): Incorporate gentle movements, such as rocking, swaying, or stretching, to help clients reconnect with their bodies. Pay attention to how these movements affect their sensations and emotions.
  • B (Befriend the Body): Help clients develop a more positive and compassionate relationship with their bodies. This may involve practicing self-compassion exercises, body scans, or loving-kindness meditation.
  • O (Observe Thoughts and Emotions): Encourage clients to observe their thoughts and emotions without getting carried away by them. Help them recognize that thoughts and emotions are simply passing experiences, not facts.
  • D (Develop Resources): Help clients identify and develop internal and external resources that can support them in managing difficult emotions and sensations. This may involve practicing relaxation techniques, connecting with supportive friends and family, or engaging in enjoyable activities.
  • Y (Yield to the Process): Encourage clients to trust the therapeutic process and to allow themselves to be guided by their own inner wisdom. Remind them that healing takes time and that setbacks are a normal part of the journey.

By focusing on the "Explore Sensations" and "Befriend the Body" components of the EMBODY Framework, you can help clients gradually overcome their resistance and develop a deeper connection with their bodies. For instance, if a client exhibits somatic numbing, start with very gentle sensory awareness exercises, such as noticing the feeling of their feet on the ground or the texture of their clothing. This approach respects the client's defensive mechanisms while gently encouraging them to re-engage with their physical experience.

Case Study: Working Through Somatic Numbing

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman, presented with a history of childhood emotional neglect and a pervasive sense of somatic numbing. She reported feeling "disconnected" from her body and struggled to identify or express her emotions.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah described feeling "empty" inside and had difficulty experiencing pleasure or joy. She often felt overwhelmed by even minor stressors and tended to withdraw from social interactions.

Intervention: The somatic therapist began by establishing a safe and trusting therapeutic relationship. They used gentle grounding exercises, such as mindful breathing and body scans, to help Sarah reconnect with her physical sensations. They also incorporated movement-based interventions, such as walking meditation and gentle stretching.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah began to experience a gradual increase in her awareness of bodily sensations. She reported feeling more "alive" and connected to herself. She also noticed an improvement in her ability to identify and express her emotions. By focusing on the "Explore Sensations" and "Befriend the Body" components of the EMBODY Framework, Sarah was able to overcome her somatic numbing and develop a more embodied sense of self. After six months of therapy, Sarah reported a 60% reduction in feelings of emptiness and a 45% increase in her ability to experience joy, measured using standardized emotional scales.

Case Study: Michael, 48

Client: Michael, a 48-year-old man, sought therapy after experiencing a series of panic attacks following a job loss. He presented with significant anxiety and a tendency to intellectualize his emotions, often dismissing them as "irrational."

Presenting Symptoms: Michael described feeling constantly on edge and struggled to relax. He experienced frequent muscle tension, headaches, and digestive issues. He would often analyze his anxiety in great detail but struggled to feel it in his body.

Intervention: The therapist used a combination of psychoeducation and somatic experiencing techniques. Michael was taught about the neurobiology of anxiety and how the body stores trauma. The therapist then guided him through gentle pendulation exercises, helping him to move between sensations of safety and anxiety. They also used resourcing techniques to help Michael connect with feelings of strength and resilience.

Outcomes: Michael gradually became more aware of his bodily sensations and learned to differentiate between anxiety and other emotions. He developed coping strategies for managing his panic attacks and experienced a reduction in his physical symptoms. He also started to engage more fully in his relationships and reported feeling more present and connected to his life. After four months, Michael reported a 70% decrease in panic attack frequency and intensity, and a 50% reduction in overall anxiety levels, as measured by the GAD-7 scale.

Key Takeaways

  • Resistance is a natural and often unconscious response to perceived threats or discomfort.

1. Collaborative & Client-Centered Goals

Effective somatic treatment planning begins with establishing collaborative and client-centered goals. This means working with your client, not on them. The focus should be on what the client wants to achieve in terms of their embodied experience and overall well-being. A 2019 study in the "Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies" highlighted that collaborative goal-setting significantly improves client engagement and therapeutic outcomes.

Consider these questions when establishing goals:

  • What sensations or feelings does the client want to experience more of?
  • What sensations or feelings does the client want to experience less of?
  • How does the client envision their life changing as a result of somatic therapy?

2. Integrating Somatic & Traditional Approaches

Somatic therapy is most powerful when integrated with traditional therapeutic approaches. This holistic approach combines the insights of talk therapy with the direct experience of somatic interventions. For instance, you might use cognitive techniques to explore a client's beliefs about their body, then use movement or breathwork to facilitate release of tension related to those beliefs. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that integrated mind-body approaches yielded significantly better outcomes for anxiety and depression compared to traditional talk therapy alone (p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 0.65).

3. Autonomic Nervous System in Treatment

Understanding the role of the autonomic nervous system (ANS) is crucial in somatic treatment planning. The ANS regulates many involuntary bodily functions, including heart rate, breathing, and digestion. Many clients seeking somatic therapy have some degree of ANS dysregulation, often stemming from trauma or chronic stress. The EMBODY Frameworkâ„¢ encourages you to assess and address nervous system dysregulation throughout the treatment process. This might involve techniques such as grounding, breathwork, or gentle movement to help the client regulate their nervous system and increase their window of tolerance

4. Ethical Considerations

Ethical considerations are paramount in somatic therapy. This includes maintaining clear boundaries, obtaining informed consent, and staying within your scope of practice. Because somatic work can be deeply personal and potentially triggering, it's essential to create a safe and supportive therapeutic environment. Be mindful of power dynamics and avoid any actions that could be perceived as exploitative or harmful. The "American Psychological Association's Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct" provides a comprehensive guide to ethical practice.

Informed consent should include:

  • Explanation of the somatic techniques you will be using.
  • Potential benefits and risks of the therapy.
  • The client's right to withdraw from the therapy at any time.

5. EMBODY Framework in Treatment Planning

The EMBODY Frameworkâ„¢ is a structured approach to somatic treatment planning, guiding you through each stage of the therapeutic process:

  1. Establishing Connection: Building rapport and creating a safe therapeutic space.
  2. Mapping the Body: Assessing the client's embodied experience, including sensations, emotions, and movement patterns.
  3. Building Resources: Developing somatic resources to support regulation and resilience.
  4. Orienting to the Present: Cultivating present moment awareness and grounding techniques.
  5. Deepening Awareness: Exploring the connection between mind, body, and emotions.
  6. Yielding to Integration: Supporting the integration of somatic experiences into daily life.

By following the EMBODY Frameworkâ„¢, you can create a comprehensive and personalized treatment plan that addresses the client's unique needs and goals. Remember that this framework is a guide, not a rigid set of rules. Be flexible and adapt your approach based on the client's responses and progress.

Applying the EMBODY Frameworkâ„¢

Step 1: Establish Safety & Befriending the Body
Eleanor's anxiety and disconnect from her body require a slow, gentle approach. Begin with psychoeducation about the neurobiology of trauma and stress. Introduce simple grounding techniques (e.g., noticing sensations in her feet) to build body awareness and self-compassion. Avoid pushing her into intense emotional release early on.
Step 2: Observe Posture & Movement - Observe
Notice Eleanor's posture (likely forward head, rounded shoulders due to desk work and chronic tension). Assess her breathing pattern (shallow, chest-dominated). Observe her gait and movement patterns for signs of asymmetry or restriction. Her scoliosis may be contributing to her pain, but it's crucial to differentiate structural issues from functional holding patterns.
Step 3: Determine Somatic Resonance - Discover
Explore Eleanor's relationship with her anxiety. Where does she feel it in her body? What thoughts and images accompany it? What sensations arise when she anticipates a presentation? Use gentle somatic tracking to help her become more aware of these patterns without judgment. Her childhood emotional neglect may be contributing to a felt sense of unsafety in her body.
Step 4: Mobilize Resources & Develop Resilience
Help Eleanor identify internal and external resources. This could include supportive relationships, hobbies, or positive memories. Teach her simple self-regulation techniques (e.g., diaphragmatic breathing, progressive muscle relaxation) to manage anxiety and tension. Explore gentle movement practices that feel safe and empowering (e.g., walking in nature, restorative yoga).
Step 5: Integrate & Yield to Wholeness
Focus on integrating Eleanor's emotional and somatic experiences. Help her connect the dots between her childhood experiences, her current stress levels, and her physical symptoms. Use techniques like pendulation and titration to process difficult emotions in a safe and manageable way. Support her in setting healthy boundaries and practicing self-care.

Differential Considerations

Ruling Out Other Possibilities

1

Cervicogenic Headaches

Headaches originating from the neck. Rule out with orthopedic testing and assessment of cervical spine mobility.

2

Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) Dysfunction

Jaw tension and headaches could be related to TMJ issues. Assess jaw movement, muscle tenderness, and clicking/popping sounds.

3

Hormonal Imbalances

Perimenopause can contribute to anxiety, insomnia, and headaches. Consider referral for hormone testing if symptoms are suggestive.

4

Nutrient Deficiencies

Stress and chronic antacid use can deplete nutrients like magnesium and B vitamins, contributing to anxiety and insomnia.

When to Refer Out

Red Flags and Scope of Practice

  • Severe, unrelenting headache that doesn't respond to over-the-counter pain relievers (rule out serious pathology).
  • Neurological symptoms such as vision changes, weakness, or numbness (urgent medical referral).
  • History of trauma that is beyond your scope of practice to address (refer to a trauma specialist).
  • Dependence on lorazepam or increasing need for medication (refer for medication management).
  • Suicidal ideation or severe depression (immediate mental health referral).

Check Your Understanding

1. How would you explain somatic resonance to Eleanor in simple terms?

Somatic resonance is like tuning into the subtle signals your body sends you. It's about noticing how your emotions, thoughts, and past experiences show up as sensations, tension, or holding patterns in your body. By becoming more aware of these signals, you can start to understand the connection between your mind, body, and emotions.

2. What are some potential benefits of using pendulation and titration with Eleanor?

Pendulation and titration can help Eleanor process difficult emotions and traumatic memories in a safe and manageable way. By gently moving between sensations of comfort and discomfort, she can gradually build her capacity to tolerate and integrate challenging experiences without becoming overwhelmed. This can lead to reduced anxiety, improved emotional regulation, and a greater sense of self-efficacy.

3. Eleanor reports that yoga feels triggering at times. How might you adapt a yoga practice to make it more accessible for her?

Instead of pushing Eleanor into traditional yoga postures, focus on gentle, restorative poses that promote relaxation and body awareness. Encourage her to modify poses as needed and to prioritize comfort over achieving a "perfect" form. Emphasize the importance of breathwork and mindfulness throughout the practice. Create a safe and supportive environment where she feels empowered to listen to her body and make choices that feel right for her. You might also explore trauma-informed yoga practices specifically designed to address nervous system regulation.

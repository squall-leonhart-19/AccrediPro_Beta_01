O: Observe Impulses

As we move into the "Observe Impulses" stage of the EMBODY Frameworkâ„¢, we're shifting our focus to supporting the safe expression of movement impulses related to trauma. This stage builds upon our previous work in exploring sensations and mapping connections. Itâ€™s about noticing the body's natural drive to move and express, particularly in response to held trauma.

A key aspect of this stage is understanding the role of inhibited action. Peter Levine's work in Somatic ExperiencingÂ® highlights that trauma often results in incomplete or blocked defensive responses (Levine, 1997). These blocked impulses can manifest as chronic tension, anxiety, or other somatic symptoms. Our role is to create a safe space for these impulses to emerge and be expressed in a controlled and supported manner.

Here are some techniques to facilitate this process:

  • Micro-movements: Encourage clients to notice and explore small, subtle movements in their body.
  • Movement Inquiry: Use gentle questions to guide clients in exploring the meaning and intention behind their movement impulses.
  • Pendulation: Guide clients to alternate between resourcing (safe, comfortable sensations) and trauma-related sensations to build resilience.

D: Discharge & Release

The "Discharge & Release" stage involves facilitating the gradual release of held tension and emotional charge through somatic techniques. It's crucial to remember that this stage requires careful titration and sensitivity to the client's capacity.

Techniques such as shaking, trembling, and vocalization can be powerful tools for releasing stored energy. A 2018 study in the Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies found that TREÂ® (Tension & Trauma Releasing Exercises) significantly reduced symptoms of anxiety and PTSD in a group of trauma survivors (Richmond et al., 2018).

When using discharge techniques, consider these guidelines:

  • Start Slowly: Begin with gentle movements and gradually increase intensity as the client feels more comfortable.
  • Titration: Pay close attention to the client's responses and adjust the pace accordingly.
  • Resourcing: Ensure the client has access to grounding and resourcing techniques throughout the process.
  • Self-Regulation: Empower clients to self-regulate and stop or modify the exercise as needed.

Y: Yieldto Integration

The final stage, "Yield to Integration," is about guiding the body and mind to integrate traumatic experiences, fostering resilience and a renewed sense of self. This stage involves allowing the nervous system to reorganize and find a new equilibrium after the discharge process.

Integration is not about erasing the past; it's about creating a new relationship with it. A meta-analysis of somatic therapies for trauma found that these approaches can lead to significant improvements in emotional regulation, self-awareness, and overall well-being (van der Kolk, 2014).

Techniques for facilitating integration include:

  • Mindfulness: Encourage clients to cultivate present moment awareness and observe their thoughts and feelings without judgment.
  • Somatic Tracking: Guide clients to track sensations in their body and notice how they shift and change over time.
  • Creative Expression: Utilize art, music, or movement to help clients express and process their experiences.
  • Narrative Integration: Support clients in creating a coherent narrative of their trauma, integrating it into their life story.

Contraindications and Cautions

It is crucial to recognize situations where discharge techniques may be inappropriate or even harmful. Certain conditions and client presentations require a more cautious approach. Always prioritize safety and ethical considerations.

Contraindications may include:

  • Acute Psychosis: Discharge techniques can be destabilizing for individuals experiencing acute psychotic symptoms.
  • Severe Dissociation: Proceed with extreme caution when working with clients who are highly dissociative.
  • Medical Instability: Certain medical conditions may be contraindications for physical discharge techniques.
  • Lack of Resources: Ensure the client has adequate support and resources outside of therapy to manage potential emotional distress.

When in doubt, err on the side of caution. Always consult with a qualified mental health professional or medical doctor if you have concerns about a client's suitability for these techniques. Remember, "do no harm" is paramount.

Case Study: Integrating Trauma

Case Study: Sarah, 34, Trauma Integration

Client: Sarah, 34 years old, presenting with chronic anxiety, nightmares, and flashbacks related to a past car accident.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported difficulty sleeping, hypervigilance, and emotional reactivity. She also experienced intrusive thoughts and images related to the accident.

Intervention: Using the EMBODY Frameworkâ„¢, we began with establishing safety and resourcing. We then gradually introduced movement inquiry and micro-movements to explore the inhibited impulses related to the accident. Over several sessions, Sarah began to engage in gentle shaking and trembling to release held tension. Finally, we utilized mindfulness and somatic tracking to support integration.

Outcomes: After several months of therapy, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety and nightmares. She also felt more grounded and present in her body. She was able to drive again without experiencing panic attacks and reported a greater sense of self-efficacy and resilience.

Former teacher, strong empathy skills, but limited experience with trauma.

Excellent at building rapport, creating a safe space for clients.

Struggles with setting boundaries, feels responsible for "fixing" clients.
Her Question
"I'm not sure if I'm doing enough for my client. Am I being helpful, or am I making things worse?"

The Case Sarah Presents

Reviewing Sarah's Client: Emily

Sarah's Description:

"Emily, 48, came to me with chronic neck pain and anxiety. During our session, she started crying and shared a story about childhood trauma. I felt so bad for her, I let her talk for almost the entire session. I barely did any somatic work, and now I feel like I failed her."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Boundary setting and time management in sessions.
  • The importance of somatic techniques even when clients are emotional.
  • Recognizing and addressing potential countertransference.
  • Validating Emily's emotions while maintaining a therapeutic focus.

Guiding Sarah: Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Discuss with Sarah

1

Acknowledge Her Empathy

"It's wonderful that you have so much empathy, Sarah. It's a gift. Now, let's channel that empathy into effective techniques."

2

Reiterate Session Structure

Remind her of the importance of balancing talking and somatic exploration within a session. A suggested ratio is 20% talk, 80% somatic work.

3

Introduce Grounding Techniques

Teach Sarah simple grounding exercises she can use with Emily to manage overwhelming emotions and safely return to the body.

4

Address Countertransference

Help Sarah recognize if her own experiences are influencing her reactions to Emily's story. Self-awareness is key.

Constructive Feedback: Your Dialogue with Sarah

Delivering Encouraging and Actionable Advice

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, I admire your compassion. It's clear you care deeply about your clients, and that's a huge asset. It sounds like you're wanting to refine your skills, and that's a sign of a dedicated practitioner."
Ask for Self-Reflection
"What did you notice in your body as Emily shared her story? Did you feel pulled to rescue her, or did you feel grounded and present?"
Offer Practical Solutions
"Next time, try this: when Emily starts sharing, validate her emotions, then gently guide her back to her body. You could say, 'I hear you, Emily. Let's notice what's happening in your shoulders as you share this.' This keeps her grounded and allows for somatic release."
Empower Future Sessions
"What small change will you make in your next session with Emily? Remember, it's not about being perfect, but about continuous learning and refinement."

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key elements of constructive feedback in a mentoring relationship?

Constructive feedback involves validation, specific examples, actionable suggestions, and an emphasis on growth and learning. It avoids blame or judgment.

2. How can you help a mentee recognize and address potential countertransference?

Encourage self-reflection, ask about their personal experiences, and help them identify any emotional reactions that might be influencing their work with clients.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective mentoring involves a balance of empathy and guidance.
  • Help mentees develop practical skills and techniques.
  • Address potential challenges like boundary setting and countertransference.
  • Create a supportive environment for learning and growth.

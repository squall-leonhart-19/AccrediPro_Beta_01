Sarah, New L1 Graduate

Enthusiastic graduate, seeking guidance on client interactions.

Background
Former social worker, strong interpersonal skills, limited somatic experience.
Strengths
Empathetic listener, quick learner, dedicated to client well-being.
Growth Areas
Needs help with session structuring, somatic technique application, and setting boundaries.
Her Question
"I'm feeling overwhelmed. How do I balance empathy with effective somatic interventions?"

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I had a client, Lisa, 52, with chronic back pain and anxiety. During the session, Lisa started crying and sharing childhood trauma. I felt so bad, I just listened and validated her feelings for the entire session, and ran out of time to do any somatic work. Was that okay?"

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Sarah acknowledge the trauma without getting pulled into a therapy session?
  • Did she lose sight of the initial goals of the session?
  • How can she balance empathy with somatic interventions?
  • What boundaries need to be set to avoid over-identification?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Validate Her Empathy

"It's wonderful that you're so empathetic. It shows you care deeply. Now let's channel that into effective somatic work."

2

Teach Session Structuring

Explain the importance of a structured approach: intake, somatic exploration, intervention, integration, and wrap-up. Time management is key.

3

Highlight Somatic Anchoring

Discuss how to use somatic anchoring techniques to bring the client back to the body when emotions arise. "Let's notice where you feel that in your body..."

4

Emphasize Scope of Practice

Remind her that she's not a therapist. Somatic work acknowledges trauma but isn't a substitute for trauma therapy. Refer when needed.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, you have a natural gift for empathy. That's a huge asset. Now, let's refine your skills to maximize client outcomes."
Question-Based Guidance
"What did you notice in your body while Lisa was sharing? Where did you feel that empathy? That's valuable information to use in the session."
Teach a Technique
"Next time, try this: 'Lisa, I hear you. Let's just take a moment to notice where you feel that sadness in your body.' This validates AND brings her back to the somatic experience."
Empower Her Confidence
"You've got this, Sarah. You're learning. Trust your instincts, but also trust the somatic process. You're going to be great."

Check Your Understanding

1. What's the most important thing to validate when a mentee brings you a challenging case?

Their willingness to learn and seek guidance. This creates a safe space for growth.

2. How can you help a mentee understand the scope of somatic practice when trauma emerges in a session?

Emphasize that somatic work acknowledges trauma held in the body but isn't a substitute for specialized trauma therapy. Referrals are key.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring new practitioners amplifies your impact and contributes to the field.
  • Focus on building skills, not just fixing "mistakes".
  • Use questions to guide mentees to their own insights.
  • Emphasize the importance of scope of practice and ethical boundaries.
  • Celebrate their growth and encourage continued learning.

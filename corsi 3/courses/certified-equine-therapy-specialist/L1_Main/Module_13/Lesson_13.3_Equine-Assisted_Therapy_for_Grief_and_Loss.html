Understanding Grief

Grief is a natural response to loss, but its presentation can vary significantly. It's crucial to differentiate between:

  • Normal Grief: The typical emotional, physical, and behavioral responses to loss that gradually subside over time.
  • Complicated Grief: Prolonged and intense grief that interferes with daily life, often involving persistent yearning, difficulty accepting the loss, and feelings of emptiness or meaninglessness. A 2020 study in the Journal of Affective Disorders found that approximately 7-10% of bereaved individuals experience complicated grief.
  • Disenfranchised Grief: Grief that is not acknowledged or validated by society, such as the loss of a pet, a miscarriage, or the death of a stigmatized relationship.

Designing Equine-Assisted Activities

When designing EAT activities for grief, consider the following:

  • Individualized Approach: Tailor activities to the client's specific needs, preferences, and grief style.
  • Focus on Emotional Expression: Create opportunities for clients to express their emotions through activities such as journaling, art, or storytelling while interacting with horses.
  • Promote Self-Care: Incorporate activities that promote self-care, such as mindful grooming, walking in nature with the horse, or simply spending quiet time in the horse's presence.
  • Address Specific Emotions: Design activities to address specific emotions associated with grief, such as anger, guilt, sadness, or fear.

Here are some examples of equine-assisted activities for grief:

  • "The Letting Go" Exercise: Clients write down things they want to let go of on small pieces of paper and tie them to the horse's mane or tail. As the horse moves, the papers fall off, symbolizing the release of these burdens.
  • "The Memory Walk": Clients walk with a horse through a designated area, sharing memories of the deceased person or pet.
  • "The Emotional Obstacle Course": Clients navigate a simple obstacle course with a horse, each obstacle representing a different emotion associated with grief (e.g., anger, sadness, fear).

  • R - Relationship: Foster a strong and trusting relationship between the client, the horse, and the therapist.
  • E - Emotional Regulation: Use equine-assisted activities to help clients regulate their emotions and manage anxiety, such as mindful grooming or rhythmic riding.
  • I - Insight: Encourage clients to reflect on their experiences with the horse and gain insight into their grief process.
  • N - Narrative: Help clients create a coherent narrative of their loss, incorporating the horse's presence and support.
  • S - Strengths: Identify and build on the client's strengths and resources to promote resilience and coping.

For example, using the "Narrative" component, a client might share stories about the deceased while grooming a horse, allowing the horse's calm presence to provide comfort and support during the storytelling process.

Ritual and Symbolism

Rituals and symbols can be powerful tools for promoting closure and healing in grief therapy.

  • Creating a Memorial: Clients can create a memorial for the deceased person or pet, such as planting a tree near the horse pasture or creating a scrapbook of memories.
  • Releasing Balloons or Lanterns: Clients can release balloons or lanterns with messages to the deceased person or pet, symbolizing the release of their spirit.
  • Symbolic Objects: Clients can use symbolic objects, such as stones or feathers, to represent their grief and process their emotions.

A 2018 study in Death Studies found that participation in rituals after a loss was associated with lower levels of grief intensity and improved psychological well-being.

Review of Relevant Ethical Codes

This includes understanding and adhering to the ethical codes of both mental health professions (e.g., counseling, social work, psychology) and equine-assisted therapy organizations (e.g., PATH International). Key ethical principles include:

  • Beneficence: Acting in the best interests of your clients, aiming to do good and promote their well-being.
  • Non-maleficence: Avoiding harm to clients and minimizing potential risks associated with therapy.
  • Autonomy: Respecting clients' rights to make their own decisions and choices regarding therapy.
  • Justice: Providing fair and equitable access to therapy services, regardless of clients' backgrounds or circumstances.

Ethical codes provide a framework for responsible and accountable practice. Familiarize yourself with the specific ethical guidelines relevant to your credentials and scope of practice. Addressing Boundary Issues

The unique nature of equine-assisted therapy can create potential for boundary crossings or violations. Maintaining clear and professional boundaries is essential to protect clients and uphold ethical standards. Common boundary issues include:

  • Dual relationships: Avoiding therapeutic relationships with individuals who are also friends, family members, or business associates.
  • Conflicts of interest: Recognizing and managing situations where your personal interests could compromise your objectivity or professional judgment.
  • Inappropriate self-disclosure: Limiting personal disclosures to clients, ensuring they are for therapeutic benefit and not for your own emotional needs.

When applying the R.E.I.N.S. Frameworkâ„¢, be mindful of the potential for boundary issues. For example, during Relationship Assessment (R), avoid developing personal relationships with clients outside of the therapeutic context. The goal is to foster a safe and professional therapeutic alliance. Setting clear expectations and consistently upholding boundaries will help prevent misunderstandings and protect the integrity of the therapeutic process.

Case Study: Ethical Dilemma

Case Study: Sarah's Dilemma

Client: Sarah, a 16-year-old with anxiety and depression.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presents with symptoms of anxiety and depression, exacerbated by social isolation and academic stress.

Dilemma: During an equine-assisted therapy session, Sarah discloses that she is being cyberbullied by classmates. She asks you not to tell her parents, as she fears they will take away her phone and further isolate her.

Intervention: You acknowledge Sarah's feelings and concerns, while also explaining your ethical obligation to report potential harm to a minor. You work with Sarah to develop a plan for disclosing the cyberbullying to her parents in a way that feels safe and empowering for her. You also offer to facilitate a family therapy session to address the issue collaboratively.

Outcomes: Sarah eventually agrees to disclose the cyberbullying to her parents, with your support. The family engages in therapy, and Sarah receives additional support from school counselors. Her anxiety and depression symptoms gradually improve, and she develops healthier coping mechanisms for dealing with stress and social challenges.

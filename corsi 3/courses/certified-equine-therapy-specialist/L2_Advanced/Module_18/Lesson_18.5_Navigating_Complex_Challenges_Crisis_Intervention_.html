Developing a Crisis Intervention Protocol

A crisis intervention protocol is a structured set of procedures designed to manage and mitigate crisis situations that may arise during equine therapy sessions. This protocol serves as a guide for therapists and staff to ensure a coordinated and effective response, prioritizing the safety and well-being of clients and horses. A well-defined protocol should include clear steps for assessment, intervention, and post-crisis care. According to the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA), having a crisis intervention plan reduces the likelihood of escalation and promotes positive outcomes (SAMHSA, 2014).

Key components of a crisis intervention protocol include:

  • Assessment Procedures: Methods for quickly evaluating the situation and determining the level of risk.
  • De-escalation Techniques: Strategies for calming and redirecting clients who are agitated or distressed.
  • Emergency Contacts: A list of phone numbers and contact information for emergency services, family members, and other relevant professionals.
  • Evacuation Procedures: Plans for safely removing clients and horses from the therapy area in case of an emergency.
  • Post-Crisis Support: Steps for providing emotional support and follow-up care to clients and staff after a crisis event.

Identifying and Assessing Risk Factors

Identifying and assessing risk factors is crucial for preventing crisis situations. Risk factors may include a client's history of trauma, mental health conditions, substance abuse, or suicidal ideation. A thorough assessment process should involve gathering information from multiple sources, such as client interviews, medical records, and consultations with other professionals. Research indicates that incorporating standardized risk assessment tools can improve the accuracy of identifying individuals at risk (Melton et al., 2015). The assessment should be ongoing, as a client's risk level can change over time.

Examples of risk factors to consider:

  • Mental Health Conditions: Clients with diagnoses such as anxiety, depression, or bipolar disorder may be more vulnerable to crisis situations.
  • Trauma History: Past trauma can significantly impact a client's emotional regulation and coping skills.
  • Substance Abuse: Substance use can impair judgment and increase the likelihood of impulsive behavior.
  • Suicidal Ideation: Clients expressing thoughts of self-harm require immediate attention and intervention.
  • Medical Conditions: Certain medical conditions or medications can affect a client's mood and behavior.

Strategies for De-escalation

De-escalation techniques are essential for managing crisis situations effectively. These strategies aim to reduce tension, promote calmness, and prevent escalation to more severe behaviors. Effective de-escalation involves a combination of verbal and non-verbal communication skills, as well as an understanding of the client's emotional state. According to a study published in the Journal of Psychiatric Practice, training in de-escalation techniques can significantly reduce the use of restrictive interventions (Price et al., 2018).

Common de-escalation strategies include:

  • Active Listening: Paying close attention to the client's concerns and emotions, and providing empathetic responses.
  • Calm Communication: Speaking in a calm, clear, and respectful tone.
  • Setting Limits: Clearly communicating expectations and boundaries.
  • Offering Choices: Providing the client with options to promote a sense of control.
  • Creating Space: Allowing the client physical space to reduce feelings of being trapped or overwhelmed.

Collaboration with Professionals

Collaboration with other professionals is a critical aspect of crisis intervention in equine therapy. This may involve working with mental health professionals, medical doctors, social workers, and family members to provide comprehensive support to the client. Collaborative efforts ensure that all aspects of the client's well-being are addressed and that interventions are coordinated and consistent. Research supports the effectiveness of interdisciplinary collaboration in improving client outcomes in mental health settings (Rosen et al., 2017).

Benefits of collaboration:

  • Comprehensive Assessment: Gaining a more complete understanding of the client's needs and risk factors.
  • Coordinated Care: Ensuring that interventions are aligned and complementary.
  • Shared Expertise: Drawing on the knowledge and skills of different professionals.
  • Enhanced Safety: Improving the overall safety and well-being of the client.

Documentation and Reporting

Accurate and thorough documentation is essential for effective crisis intervention. Documentation should include a detailed account of the events leading up to the crisis, the interventions used, and the client's response. Reporting procedures should be clearly defined and followed to ensure that relevant parties are informed and that appropriate action is taken. According to the American Psychological Association (APA), proper documentation protects both the client and the therapist and is essential for ethical practice (APA, 2017).

Key elements of documentation and reporting:

  • Detailed Description of Events: A clear and objective account of what occurred.
  • Interventions Used: Documentation of the specific strategies and techniques employed.
  • Client's Response: Recording the client's reactions and behaviors during the crisis.
  • Follow-Up Actions: Documenting any actions taken after the crisis, such as referrals or consultations.
  • Reporting Procedures: Following established protocols for notifying supervisors, family members, or other relevant parties.

Ethical Considerations in Crisis Situations

Crisis intervention raises several ethical considerations that therapists must navigate carefully. These include maintaining client confidentiality, respecting client autonomy, and ensuring client safety. Therapists must balance the need to protect the client from harm with the client's right to make their own decisions. The American Counseling Association (ACA) Code of Ethics provides guidance on ethical decision-making in crisis situations (ACA, 2014). Upholding ethical standards is crucial for maintaining trust and promoting positive client outcomes.

Ethical principles to consider:

  • Confidentiality: Protecting the client's private information, except when disclosure is required to prevent harm.
  • Autonomy: Respecting the client's right to make their own decisions, even if they differ from the therapist's recommendations.
  • Beneficence: Acting in the client's best interest and promoting their well-being.
  • Non-Maleficence: Avoiding actions that could cause harm to the client.
  • Justice: Ensuring fair and equitable treatment for all clients.

Case Studies

Case Study 1: Sarah, 16 years old

Client: Sarah, a 16-year-old with a history of anxiety and self-harm, becomes agitated during a therapy session when discussing a recent family conflict. She begins to cry and expresses feelings of hopelessness.

Intervention: The therapist uses active listening to validate Sarah's feelings and offers her a quiet space to calm down. The therapist also encourages Sarah to engage in a calming activity with the horse, such as grooming. The therapist also contacted Sarah's parents to make them aware of the situation.

Outcome: Sarah gradually calms down and is able to express her feelings in a more controlled manner. The therapist works with Sarah to develop a coping plan for managing future conflicts. Sarah was able to continue with her therapy and developed better coping skills.

Case Study 2: Michael, 42 years old

Client: Michael, a 42-year-old veteran with PTSD, experiences a flashback during a therapy session while working with a horse. He becomes disoriented and agitated, and starts yelling.

Intervention: The therapist immediately stops the session and uses a calm, reassuring voice to orient Michael to the present. The therapist guides Michael through a grounding exercise and offers him a break from the activity. The therapist also contacted Michael's psychiatrist to inform him of the incident.

Outcome: Michael slowly regains his composure and is able to participate in a modified therapy session. The therapist works with Michael to develop strategies for managing flashbacks and triggers. Michael continued therapy and was able to manage his PTSD symptoms better.

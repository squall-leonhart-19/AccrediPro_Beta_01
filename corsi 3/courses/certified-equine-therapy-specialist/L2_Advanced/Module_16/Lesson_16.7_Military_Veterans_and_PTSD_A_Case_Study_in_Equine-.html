Understanding PTSD in Veterans

Post-traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) significantly impacts military veterans. The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs estimates that between 11-20% of veterans who served in Operations Iraqi Freedom and Enduring Freedom have PTSD in a given year (VA, 2018). Symptoms include intrusive thoughts, avoidance behaviors, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and marked alterations in arousal and reactivity.

The effects of PTSD extend beyond the individual, impacting families and communities. Veterans with PTSD may experience difficulties in interpersonal relationships, employment, and overall quality of life. This is where equine therapy can make a profound difference, offering a unique approach to healing that complements traditional treatments.

Case Study: Robert, a Veteran's Journey

Integrating Equine Therapy with EMDR

RV

Robert V., 48

Combat Veteran with PTSD

Robert, a 48-year-old veteran, presented with severe combat-related PTSD. He experienced frequent nightmares, flashbacks, and hypervigilance. Traditional EMDR therapy had yielded limited results due to his difficulty in maintaining focus and managing intense emotional reactions.

Intervention: Equine-assisted EMDR was introduced. Robert worked with a licensedtherapist and an equine specialist. Sessions involved groundwork with a horse named Apollo, followed by EMDR processing while maintaining proximity to the horse.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, Robert reported a significant reduction in PTSD symptoms. His nightmares decreased, and he felt more grounded and in control during EMDR processing. The presence of Apollo provided a sense of safety and facilitated emotional regulation. Standardized assessments confirmed improvements in PTSD symptom severity and overall well-being.

R.E.I.N.S. Framework Application

The R.E.I.N.S. Framework‚Ñ¢ provides a structured approach to equine-assisted therapy. It emphasizes:

  • Relationship: Building a trusting relationship with the horse and therapist.
  • Emotion: Exploring and processing trauma-related emotions in a safe environment.
  • Intentionality: Designing specific activities to facilitate EMDR processing.
  • Neuroregulation: Promoting grounding and emotional regulation through equine interaction.
  • Strengths: Identifying and building on the veteran's existing strengths and resilience.

In Robert's case, the R.E.I.N.S. framework was applied as follows:

  • Relationship: Robert spent time grooming and connecting with Apollo, fostering a sense of trust and safety.
  • Emotion: Guided interactions with Apollo helped Robert identify and express suppressed emotions related to his combat experiences.
  • Intentionality: EMDR processing was strategically integrated with equine activities, such as walking alongside Apollo while focusing on traumatic memories.
  • Neuroregulation: Apollo's calm presence promoted grounding and reduced Robert's hyperarousal during EMDR processing.
  • Strengths: Robert's military training and leadership skills were leveraged to enhance his sense of control and mastery in the equine environment.

Equine-Assisted EMDR Protocol

The specific protocol for equine-assisted EMDR involves several key steps:

  1. Assessment: Conduct a thorough assessment of the veteran's PTSD symptoms, trauma history, and readiness for equine-assisted therapy.
  2. Preparation: Educate the veteran about EMDR and equine therapy, addressing any concerns or misconceptions.
  3. Equine Interaction: Introduce the veteran to the horse, allowing time for bonding and familiarization.
  4. Groundwork: Engage in groundwork activities, such as leading the horse or navigating obstacles, to promote focus and emotional regulation.
  5. EMDR Processing: Initiate EMDR processing while maintaining proximity to the horse, using bilateral stimulation (e.g., eye movements, tapping) to facilitate trauma resolution.
  6. Closure: Provide grounding exercises and coping strategies to manage any residual distress following EMDR processing.
  7. Re-evaluation: Regularly assess the veteran's progress and adjust the treatment plan as needed.

Managing Dissociation

Dissociation is a common symptom of PTSD, characterized by a sense of detachment from oneself or reality. In equine-assisted EMDR, several techniques can be used to manage dissociation:

  • Grounding Exercises: Encourage the veteran to focus on sensory experiences, such as feeling the horse's coat or smelling the hay.
  • Mindfulness Techniques: Practice mindfulness exercises, such as focusing on the breath or observing the horse's movements, to promote present-moment awareness.
  • Safe Place Imagery: Guide the veteran to visualize a safe and calming environment, such as a peaceful meadow or a familiar location.
  • Containment Strategies: Teach the veteran techniques for containing overwhelming emotions, such as using a grounding object or practicing self-soothing statements.

The presence of the horse can also serve as a grounding anchor, providing a tangible and comforting presence during moments of dissociation.

Measuring Treatment Outcomes

Standardized measures are essential for tracking progress and evaluating the effectiveness of equine-assisted EMDR. Commonly used assessments include:

Measure Description Relevance to Equine-Assisted EMDR
PTSD Checklist for Civilians (PCL-C) A 17-item self-report measure of PTSD symptoms. Assesses changes in PTSD symptom severity following treatment.
Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI) A 21-item self-report measure of anxiety symptoms. Monitors reductions in anxiety levels associated with trauma processing.
Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) A 21-item self-report measure of depressive symptoms. Tracks improvements in mood and overall well-being.
Subjective Units of Distress Scale (SUDS) A 0-10 scale used to rate the intensity of distress associated with traumatic memories. Measures reductions in distress during EMDR processing.

In addition to standardized measures, qualitative feedback from the veteran and observations of their interactions with the horse can provide valuable insights into the treatment process.

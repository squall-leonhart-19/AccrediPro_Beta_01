Sarah, New L1 Graduate

Eager to apply her knowledge but feeling overwhelmed by real-world cases.

Background
Former teacher, strong understanding of child development, new to equine therapy.
Strengths
Excellent communication skills, patient, and builds rapport easily with children.
Growth Areas
Needs help with session planning, setting boundaries, and handling challenging behaviors.
Her Question
"I had a child client who started crying and refusing to participate. I panicked and didn't know what to do. How can I handle this better next time?"

The Case Sarah Presents

Sarah's Client Scenario

What Sarah Tells You:

"I was working with a 7-year-old boy, Alex, who has ADHD. We were supposed to be doing a groundwork exercise, but he got frustrated and started crying, saying he didn't want to do it anymore. I tried to encourage him, but he just got more upset. I ended up stopping the session early."

What You Need to Explore:

  • What were the specific triggers that led to Alex's frustration?
  • What strategies did Sarah try before the child became overwhelmed?
  • How did Sarah manage her own emotional response in the moment?
  • Did Sarah have a backup plan or alternative activity prepared?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Validate Her Feelings

"It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when a client becomes upset. It happens to all of us, especially when we're starting out."

2

Teach Proactive Strategies

Discuss the importance of having a flexible session plan and identifying potential triggers for frustration in advance. Brainstorm alternative activities that can be used as needed.

3

Review Emotional Regulation Techniques

Share techniques for staying calm and grounded when a client is dysregulated. This could include deep breathing, mindfulness, or taking a brief pause.

4

Emphasize the Value of "Repair"

Explain that even if a session doesn't go as planned, it's an opportunity to build trust and resilience. Discuss how to debrief with the client and learn from the experience.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Her Effort
"Sarah, I commend you for recognizing that you need support and for seeking guidance. That's a sign of a conscientious practitioner."
Encourage Self-Reflection
"What do you think you could have done differently in that moment? What cues did you miss that might have indicated Alex was becoming overwhelmed?"
Offer Practical Suggestions
"Next time, try breaking down the exercise into smaller steps. You could also incorporate a sensory activity, like grooming the horse, to help Alex regulate his emotions."
Reiterate Her Strengths
"Remember, you have a natural ability to connect with children. Trust your instincts and don't be afraid to adapt your approach based on their individual needs."

Check Your Understanding

1. What's the most important thing to remember when mentoring a new practitioner?

Focus on building their confidence and helping them develop their own clinical reasoning skills.

2. How can you help a mentee who is struggling with session planning?

Review the principles of effective session design, brainstorm alternative activities, and encourage them to practice creating flexible plans.

You're Cultivating the Next Generation!

By sharing your expertise and guidance, you're not only helping new practitioners thrive, but also contributing to the growth and evolution of equine therapy as a whole.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring involves guiding and supporting new practitioners as they develop their skills and confidence.
  • Effective mentoring focuses on building strengths, providing actionable feedback, and encouraging independent thinking.
  • It's important to validate the mentee's feelings, normalize challenges, and emphasize the value of learning from mistakes.
  • By mentoring others, you contribute to the growth of the equine therapy field and cultivate the next generation of practitioners.

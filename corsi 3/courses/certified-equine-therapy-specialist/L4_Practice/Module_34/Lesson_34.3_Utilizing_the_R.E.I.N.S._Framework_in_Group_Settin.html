Relationship Assessment (R)

Adapting the Relationship Assessment component of the R.E.I.N.S. Frameworkâ„¢ to a group setting involves observing and evaluating both the individual relationships within the group and the overall group dynamic. This includes how participants interact with each other, with the horses, and with you as the facilitator.

In a group setting, consider these adaptations to relationship assessment:

  • Sociometry: Observe patterns of connection and disconnection within the group. Who gravitates towards whom? Are there cliques or isolated individuals?
  • Group History: Understand the pre-existing relationships, if any, among group members. This context can inform your expectations and interventions.
  • Horse Preferences: Note which horses different group members are drawn to. This can reflect underlying emotional needs or relational styles.

Emotional Exploration (E)

Emotional exploration in a group setting is both powerful and complex. The presence of others can amplify emotional experiences, create opportunities for shared vulnerability, and provide a supportive context for processing difficult feelings. When facilitating emotional exploration, it's important to:

  • Create Safety: Establish clear group guidelines for confidentiality, respect, and non-judgment.
  • Model Vulnerability: Share your own experiences and emotions appropriately to encourage others to do the same.
  • Use the Horses as Metaphors: Help participants connect their interactions with the horses to their own emotional landscapes.

Here's a comparison of group vs individual emotional exploration:

Intentional Interaction (I)

Intentional interaction in the R.E.I.N.S. Frameworkâ„¢ refers to the conscious and deliberate choices participants make in their interactions with the horses and with each other. In a group setting, this involves facilitating activities that encourage mindful connection and collaboration.

Here are some practical approaches:

  • Collaborative Tasks: Design tasks that require group members to work together with the horses, such as leading a horse through an obstacle course as a team.
  • Communication Exercises: Implement activities that focus on improving communication skills, such as non-verbal communication exercises with the horses.
  • Reflection and Sharing: Provide structured opportunities for group members to reflect on their experiences and share their insights with each other.

Navigating Challenges (N)

Challenges are inevitable in any therapeutic process, and group equine therapy is no exception. As a facilitator, your role is to support group members in overcoming obstacles and building resilience through shared problem-solving during equine interactions.

Strategies for navigating challenges:

  • Normalize Difficulties: Acknowledge that challenges are a natural part of the therapeutic process and that everyone experiences them at times.
  • Facilitate Problem-Solving: Guide the group in identifying the root causes of challenges and brainstorming potential solutions.
  • Encourage Peer Support: Create a culture of mutual support and encouragement, where group members feel comfortable offering and receiving help from one another.

Sustainable Strategies (S)

The final component of the R.E.I.N.S. Frameworkâ„¢ focuses on developing sustainable strategies for maintaining therapeutic gains and integrating new insights into daily life. In a group setting, this involves creating a sense of community and providing ongoing support.

  • Resource Sharing: Provide participants with a list of relevant resources, such as books, websites, and support groups.

Structuring the Intake Interview

The intake interview is your first formal opportunity to connect with the counselee and gather essential information. Think of it as laying the foundation for a strong, trusting relationship. Hereâ€™s a suggested structure:

  1. Welcome and Introductions: Start with a warm greeting and briefly explain your role and the coaching process.
  2. Purpose of the Interview: Clearly state the purpose of the interview: to understand their current situation, background, and goals.
  3. Confidentiality: Reassure the counselee about the confidentiality of the session, explaining any limitations (e.g., mandated reporting).
  4. Background Information: Gather basic demographic information (name, age, contact information).
  5. Presenting Problem(s): Explore the primary reason(s) they are seeking coaching. Encourage them to describe their challenges in their own words.
  6. History of the Problem(s): When did the problem(s) begin? What has been tried to address them? What were the results?
  7. Relevant History: Explore family history, significant relationships, past experiences, and spiritual background.
  8. Goals for Coaching: What do they hope to achieve through coaching? What does success look like?
  9. Questions and Clarification: Provide an opportunity for the counselee to ask questions.
  10. Next Steps: Explain what to expect next (e.g., further assessment, scheduling follow-up sessions).

Remember, this is a flexible guideline. Adjust the structure based on the individual's needs and the flow of the conversation.

Developing Initial Questions

Crafting thoughtful initial questions is key to gathering comprehensive information. These questions should address various aspects of the counselee's life, providing a holistic understanding of their situation. Here are some key areas to explore:

  • Family History: "Tell me about your family growing up. What was your relationship like with your parents and siblings?"
  • Relationships: "How would you describe your current relationships with your spouse, children, friends, and colleagues?"
  • Spiritual Beliefs: "What is your spiritual background? How does your faith influence your life and decisions?"
  • Past Experiences: "What are some significant past experiences that have shaped who you are today?"
  • Emotional Well-being: "How would you describe your overall emotional state? What are some common emotions you experience?"
  • Physical Health: "How would you describe your current physical health? Are there any health conditions or concerns that impact yourlife?"
  • Work/Career: "What is your current work situation? What are your career goals and aspirations?"
  • Finances: "How would you describe your current financial situation? Are there any financial stressors or concerns?"
  • Habits and Lifestyle: "Tell me about your daily routine. What are some of your habits and lifestyle choices?"
  • Strengths and Resources: "What are some of your strengths and resources that you can draw upon during this process?"

Remember to use open-ended questions that encourage the counselee to elaborate and share their experiences in detail. Avoid leading questions that might influence their responses.

Using Standardized Questionnaires

Standardized questionnaires can be valuable tools for gathering structured data and identifying potential areas of concern. However, it's crucial to use them judiciously and understand their limitations. Here's a comparison of some commonly used questionnaires:

Questionnaire Purpose Administration Limitations
Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) Assesses the severity of depressive symptoms. Self-administered; takes approximately 10 minutes. May not be suitable for individuals with cognitive impairments; relies on self-report.
Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7) Screens for generalized anxiety disorder and assesses symptom severity. Self-administered; takes approximately 5 minutes. May not capture all aspects of anxiety; relies on self-report.
Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) Screens for depression and assesses symptom severity. Self-administered; takes approximately 5 minutes. May not be suitable for individuals with cognitive impairments; relies on self-report.
Spiritual Well-Being Scale (SWBS) Measures an individual's sense of well-being in relation to their spiritual beliefs. Self-administered; takes approximately 15-20 minutes. Subjective; may be influenced by social desirability bias.

When using questionnaires, consider the following:

  • Purpose: Ensure the questionnaire aligns with your assessment goals.
  • Validity and Reliability: Choose questionnaires with established validity and reliability.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Select questionnaires that are appropriate for the counselee's cultural background.
  • Interpretation: Interpret the results in conjunction with other assessment data and clinical judgment.

Remember, questionnaires are just one piece of the puzzle. They should not be used in isolation to make diagnoses or treatment decisions.

Addressing Resistance

It's common for counselees to experience resistance to sharing sensitive information. This resistance can stem from various factors, such as fear of judgment, shame, or distrust. Here are some strategies for addressing resistance:

  • Empathy: Acknowledge and validate the counselee's feelings. "I understand that this may be difficult to talk about."
  • Normalization: Reassure the counselee that their experiences are not uncommon. "Many people struggle with similar issues."
  • Gentle Persistence: Don't push too hard, but gently encourage the counselee to share what they feel comfortable sharing.
  • Reframing: Help the counselee see the potential benefits of sharing. "Sharing this information can help us better understand your situation and develop effective strategies."
  • Building Trust: Focus on building a strong, trusting relationship. Be reliable, consistent, and non-judgmental.

If resistance persists, respect the counselee's boundaries and avoid pushing them beyond their comfort level. You can revisit the topic later when they feel more comfortable.

Documenting Findings

Accurate and ethical documentation is essential for maintaining a clear record of the assessment process. Your documentation should include:

  • Demographic Information: Name, age, contact information, etc.
  • Presenting Problem(s): A clear description of the reason(s) the counselee is seeking coaching.
  • History of the Problem(s): Onset, duration, previous attempts to address the problem(s).
  • Relevant History: Family history, relationship history, past experiences, spiritual background.
  • Assessment Methods: A list of the assessment methods used (e.g., intake interview, questionnaires).
  • Findings: A summary of the key findings from the assessment process.
  • Goals for Coaching: The counselee's goals for coaching.
  • Recommendations: Your recommendations for next steps.

Ensure your documentation is clear, concise, and objective. Avoid using jargon or making subjective judgments. Maintain confidentiality and adhere to all ethical and legal guidelines.

Case Study: Practical Application

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old woman, presents with feelings of anxiety and dissatisfaction in her current job.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports experiencing frequent anxiety, difficulty sleeping, and a lack of motivation at work. She feels overwhelmed and unsure of her career path.

Intervention: During the intake interview, the coach used open-ended questions to explore Sarah's family history, past experiences, and career aspirations. The coach also administered the GAD-7 questionnaire to assess Sarah's anxiety level. Sarah scored a 15, indicating moderate to severe anxiety. The coach explored Sarah's spiritual background and discovered that Sarah had a strong faith, but felt disconnected from her church community. They discussed ways to reconnect with her faith and find support.

Outcomes: Through the intake interview, the coach gained a comprehensive understanding of Sarah's challenges and goals. Sarah reported feeling heard and understood. The coach and Sarah collaborated to develop a coaching plan focused on addressing her anxiety, exploring career options, and reconnecting with her faith community. Over the next few weeks, Sarah began attending a small group at her church and reported feeling more connected and supported. She also started exploring different career paths and identified several options that aligned with her interests and skills. Her GAD-7 score decreased to an 8, indicating mild anxiety.

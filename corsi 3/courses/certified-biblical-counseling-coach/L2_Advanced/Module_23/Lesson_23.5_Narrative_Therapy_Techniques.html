Externalizing the Problem

One of the foundational techniques of Narrative Therapy is externalizing the problem. This involves separating the problem from the person, reframing it as an external entity that influences the client's life. Instead of saying "I am anxious," you might explore, "How does anxiety try to control you?" This simple shift creates distance and allows the client to gain perspective and agency over the issue.

This technique directly aligns with the Truthful Assessment (T) phase of the T.R.U.S.T. Frameworkâ„¢. By externalizing the problem, you help the client gain a clearer, more objective understanding of the challenges they face, rather than internalizing them as part of their identity. This can be incredibly liberating!

Practical Application: Ask questions like:

  • "What tactics does [the problem] use to influence your decisions?"
  • "When did you first notice [the problem] entering your life?"
  • "What does [the problem] want you to believe about yourself?"

Deconstruction

Deconstruction involves examining the dominant cultural narratives that contribute to the client's problems. These narratives are often deeply ingrained and taken for granted, shaping our beliefs and behaviors in subtle but powerful ways. By deconstructing these narratives, we can expose their limitations and create space for alternative, more empowering stories.

For example, a client struggling with body image might be internalizing the dominant cultural narrative that thinness equals beauty and worth. As a coach, you can help them deconstruct this narrative by exploring its origins, its impact on their self-esteem, and the alternative perspectives that exist. This process is essential in the Unveiling Beliefs (U) phase of the T.R.U.S.T. Frameworkâ„¢.

Practical Application:

  • Identify the dominant narratives related to the client's problem.
  • Explore the origins and consequences of these narratives.
  • Challenge the assumptions and limitations of these narratives.

Re-authoring

Re-authoring is the collaborative process of constructing new narratives that empower the client and reflect their strengths, values, and hopes. This involves identifyingunique outcomes (moments when the problem did not have control) and using them as building blocks for a new, more positive story.

Re-authoring is tightly linked with the Strengths Identification (S) phase of the T.R.U.S.T. Frameworkâ„¢. By focusing on the client's inherent strengths and past successes, you help them write a new narrative that is grounded in their true potential and aligned with God's purpose for their life.

Practical Application:

  • Identify unique outcomes and exceptions to the problem.
  • Explore the client's strengths, values, and hopes.
  • Collaboratively construct a new narrative that reflects these elements.

Unique Outcomes

Unique outcomes, also known as "sparkling moments" or "exceptions," are instances when the client was able to resist the influence of the problem or act in accordance with their preferred self. These moments, however small, provide evidence that the problem is not all-powerful and that the client has agency to create change.

A 2019 study in the *Journal of Systemic Therapies* found that identifying and amplifying unique outcomes significantly improved clients' sense of self-efficacy and hope (Morgan, 2019). The study involved 67 participants undergoing narrative therapy for depression. The results showed a 32% increase in reported self-efficacy among participants who actively focused on identifying unique outcomes compared to the control group.

Practical Application: Ask questions like:

  • "Can you recall a time when [the problem] didn't have as much influence over you?"
  • "What was different about that situation?"
  • "What strengths or resources did you draw upon in that moment?"

Case Study

Case Study: Emily and the "Critic"

Client: Emily, 35, Marketing Manager

Presenting Symptoms: Emily reported feeling overwhelmed by self-doubt and negative self-talk, which she described as a constant "critic" in her head. This "critic" was affecting her work performance, her relationships, and her overall sense of well-being.

Intervention: Using Narrative Therapy techniques, the coach helped Emily externalize the "critic," giving it a name and exploring its tactics. They then deconstructed the dominant cultural narrative that equates success with perfection, revealing its unrealistic and harmful expectations. Together, they identified unique outcomes â€“ moments when Emily was able to silence the "critic" and act with confidence. Finally, they re-authored Emily's story, focusing on her strengths, values, and accomplishments.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Emily reported a significant decrease in self-doubt and negative self-talk. She was able to recognize the "critic's" voice as separate from her own and develop strategies for managing its influence. Her work performance improved, and she experienced a greater sense of peace and self-acceptance.

Check Your Understanding

  1. How does externalizing the problem create distance and agency for the client?
    Externalizing allows the client to see the problem as something separate from themselves, rather than an inherent part of their identity. This creates psychological distance and empowers them to take action against it.
  2. Explain the role of deconstruction in challenging dominant narratives.
    Deconstruction exposes the limitations and assumptions of dominant narratives, creating space for alternative, more empowering stories to emerge. It helps clients recognize that these narratives are not necessarily true or universally applicable.

  • Re-authoring is the collaborative process of constructing new, empowering narratives.

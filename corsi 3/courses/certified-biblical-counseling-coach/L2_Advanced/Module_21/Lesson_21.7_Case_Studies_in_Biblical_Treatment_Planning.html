Case Study: Sarah, Age 45
S

Sarah M.

45 years old, experiencing anxiety and fear

Presenting Problem: Sarah, a 45-year-old stay-at-home mom transitioning back to the workforce, presents with overwhelming anxiety and fear about her ability to succeed. She experiences panic attacks and has difficulty sleeping.

  • T (Truthful Assessment): Identified Sarah's core fears (failure, inadequacy) and related physical symptoms (panic attacks, insomnia).
  • R (Relational Understanding): Explored Sarah's past experiences and relational patterns contributing to her anxiety.
  • U (Unveiling Beliefs): Uncovered cognitive distortions such as catastrophizing ("I'll definitely fail") and perfectionism ("I have to be perfect").
  • S (Scriptural Guidance): Introduced Sarah to verses addressing fear and God's provision (e.g., Philippians 4:6-7, Matthew 6:34).
  • T (Transformative Action): Developed a plan including cognitive restructuring, prayer, mindfulness, and gradual exposure to work-related tasks.

Outcomes: After 12 weeks, Sarah reported a 60% reduction in panic attack frequency and improved sleep. She successfully secured a part-time job and expressed increased confidence in her abilities. This demonstrates how applying the TRUST Frameworkâ„¢ can lead to tangible, positive change.

Case Study: John and Mary, Ages 48 & 46
J&M

John & Mary S.

48 & 46 years old, experiencing marital conflict

Presenting Problem: John (48) and Mary (46) have been married for 20 years and report increasing conflict and communication breakdown. They struggle with resentment, criticism, and difficulty resolving disagreements.

  • T (Truthful Assessment): Identified patterns of communication (criticism, defensiveness, stonewalling) and underlying unmet needs (e.g., affection, appreciation).
  • R (Relational Understanding): Explored family-of-origin influences, past relational wounds, and individual attachment styles.
  • U (Unveiling Beliefs): Uncovered negative beliefs about each other ("He never listens," "She's always nagging").
  • S (Scriptural Guidance): Focused on verses about communication (Ephesians 4:29, James 1:19), forgiveness (Colossians 3:13), and mutual respect (Ephesians 5:21).
  • T (Transformative Action): Implemented communication exercises (active listening, "I" statements), conflict resolution strategies, and activities to rebuild intimacy and connection.

Outcomes: After six months, John and Mary reported a 40% decrease in the frequency of arguments and improved emotional connection. They demonstrated better communication skills and a willingness to forgive past hurts. Mary reported feeling more understood, and John expressed a greater sense of responsibility in meeting her needs. This case illustrates the power of integrating biblical principles with practical therapeutic techniques to heal and strengthen marriages.

Analyzing Treatment Plan Effectiveness

It's crucial to continuously evaluate the effectiveness of your treatment plans. This involves tracking progress, soliciting feedback, and making adjustments as needed. Let's consider some key metrics and methods.

Key metrics to consider:

  1. Symptom Reduction: Track changes in symptom severity using standardized scales or client self-report.
  2. Goal Attainment: Monitor progress toward specific, measurable goals.
  3. Improved Functioning: Assess improvements in daily life functioning (e.g., work, relationships, self-care).
  4. Client Satisfaction: Obtain feedback from clients about their experience and perceived benefits of therapy.

Methods for analysis:

  1. Regular Check-ins: Schedule regular check-ins with clients to review progress and address any concerns.
  2. Formal Assessments: Use standardized assessments to track changes in symptoms and functioning over time.
  3. Feedback Forms: Provide clients with feedback forms to gather their perspectives on the therapy process.
  4. Case Reviews: Periodically review cases with colleagues or supervisors to gain insights and identify areas for improvement.

Ethical Considerations in Treatment Planning

Treatment planning must always be conducted ethically, respecting client autonomy, confidentiality, and well-being. Here are some critical ethical considerations:

  1. Informed Consent: Ensure clients fully understand the treatment plan, potential risks and benefits, and their right to refuse or modify treatment.
  2. Confidentiality: Maintain strict confidentiality of client information, except in cases where disclosure is legally or ethically required (e.g., imminent risk of harm).
  3. Competence: Only provide services within your scope of competence, seeking supervision or referral when necessary.
  4. Dual Relationships: Avoid dual relationships that could compromise objectivity or exploit the client (e.g., business partnerships, romantic relationships).
  5. Cultural Sensitivity: Consider the client's cultural background and values when developing and implementing the treatment plan.

Ethical dilemmas can arise in various situations. For example, what if a client's goals conflict with your own values? Or what if you suspect a client is not being truthful about their progress? It's crucial to have a framework for ethical decision-making, which includes consulting with colleagues, seeking supervision, and adhering to relevant ethical codes. Remember, your integrity and commitment to ethical practice are essential for building trust and promoting client well-being.

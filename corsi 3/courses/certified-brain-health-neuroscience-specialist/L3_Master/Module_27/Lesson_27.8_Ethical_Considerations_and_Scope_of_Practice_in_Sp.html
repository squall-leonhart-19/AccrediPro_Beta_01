The Compliance Conundrum

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with Emily, 52, struggling with brain fog and anxiety. I suggested a Mediterranean diet, regular exercise, and mindfulness. She started strong, but after two weeks, she's back to her old habits. I feel like I failed her."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Sarah explore Emily's barriers to compliance (time, cost, taste preferences)?
  • Was the plan too ambitious? Did she start small?
  • Did she incorporate Emily's values and motivations?
  • Is Sarah taking it too personally?

Your Mentoring Strategy

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Reframe "Compliance"

Explain that compliance isn't about obedience; it's about collaboration. It's about finding solutions that align with the client's life.

2

Emphasize Small Wins

Discuss the power of behavioral activation. Suggest starting with one small, achievable change (e.g., drinking one extra glass of water daily) to build momentum.

3

Teach Values-Based Motivation

Help Sarah connect Emily's brain health goals to her deeper values. Does she want to be more present with her grandchildren? Excel at work? Knowing the "why" fuels motivation.

4

Address Practitioner Boundaries

Remind Sarah that client choices are ultimately the client's. Her role is to educate, empower, and support, not to control.

Crafting Your Feedback

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Effort
"Sarah, I can tell you care deeply about Emily. It's wonderful that you're so invested in her success."
Explore Underlying Beliefs
"What does it mean to you when a client doesn't follow the plan? Does it feel like a personal failure?"
Offer a New Perspective
"Remember, we're not aiming for perfection, but progress. Every small step counts. And sometimes, 'falling off the wagon' is a learning opportunity."
Suggest a Collaborative Approach
"What if you and Emily brainstormed 2-3 tiny, easy changes she could make this week? Let her choose what feels most doable."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important element of values-based motivation?

Connecting client goals to their core values to create a deeper sense of purpose.

2. Why is it important to address practitioner boundaries in supervision?

To prevent burnout and ensure practitioners understand their role is to support, not control, client choices.

Key Takeaways

  • Reframing "compliance" fosters a collaborative client relationship.
  • Small wins build momentum and increase client confidence.

  • Addressing practitioner boundaries prevents burnout and promotes ethical practice.

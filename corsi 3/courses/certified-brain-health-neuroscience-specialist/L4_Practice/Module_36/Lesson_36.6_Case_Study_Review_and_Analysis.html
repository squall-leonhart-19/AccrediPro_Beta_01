Maria Rodriguez

52 years old, Accountant

Maria, a 52-year-old accountant, reports increasing difficulty with memory and focus, especially at work. She forgets names, misplaces items, and struggles to concentrate during meetings. She's concerned about early-onset cognitive decline, as her mother was diagnosed with Alzheimer's disease at 78. Maria exercises regularly, but her diet is inconsistent, and she often feels stressed due to work deadlines.

Presenting Symptoms:

  • Memory lapses (forgetting names, misplacing items)
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Increased stress levels
  • Family history of Alzheimer's disease

Managing Challenging Client Situations

In brain health coaching, you may encounter challenging situations. Here's how to approach them:

  • Client Resistance: Use motivational interviewing techniques to explore ambivalence and build intrinsic motivation. A meta-analysis published in Health Psychology Review showed that motivational interviewing has a moderate to large effect on health behavior change (OR = 1.45, 95% CI: 1.21-1.74) (DiClemente et al., 2017).
  • Emotional Distress: Provide a safe and supportive environment. If necessary, refer the client to a mental health professional.
  • Unrealistic Expectations: Educate clients about realistic timelines and progress. Emphasize the importance of consistency and patience.

Ethical Considerations

As a Certified Brain Health & Neuroscience Specialistâ„¢, you must adhere to ethical guidelines:

  • Confidentiality: Protect client information.
  • Scope of Practice: Only provide services within your area of expertise. Refer clients to other professionals when necessary.
  • Informed Consent: Ensure clients understand the nature of coaching, potential risks, and benefits.
  • Avoiding Conflicts of Interest: Disclose any potential conflicts of interest.

Remember, ethical practice builds trust and ensures the well-being of your clients. Adherence to these principles is paramount in maintaining your professional integrity and the reputation of the certification.

Navigating Ethical Dilemmas

Scenario: A client discloses that they are taking a medication that may interact negatively with a supplement they are considering. You are not a medical doctor.

Ethical Response: You should advise the client to consult with their physician or a qualified healthcare professional before starting any new supplements. Explain that you are not qualified to provide medical advice and that it is crucial to ensure there are no contraindications with their existing medications. This upholds your ethical responsibility to stay within your scope of practice and prioritize the client's safety.

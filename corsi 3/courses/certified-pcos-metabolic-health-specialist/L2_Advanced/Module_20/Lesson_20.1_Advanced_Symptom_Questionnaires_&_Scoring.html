Rotterdam Criteria-Based Symptom Checklist

The Rotterdam criteria are widely used for diagnosing PCOS. This checklist expands on those criteria, providing a detailed list of symptoms related to ovulatory dysfunction, hyperandrogenism, and polycystic ovaries. It allows for a nuanced understanding of which criteria are most prominent in each individual.

Scoring & Interpretation:

  • Each symptom is rated for frequency and severity.
  • Scores are tallied for each category (ovulatory dysfunction, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries).
  • Higher scores indicate a greater likelihood of that particular diagnostic criterion being met.

By analyzing the scores, you can identify the dominant PCOS phenotype, guiding your initial intervention strategies. For instance, a client with high hyperandrogenism scores may benefit from targeted interventions to lower androgens, while someone with prominent ovulatory dysfunction may require support for cycle regulation.

Modified Ferriman-Gallwey Score

The modified Ferriman-Gallwey (mFG) score is the gold standard for assessing hirsutism, a key indicator of hyperandrogenism in PCOS. This involves evaluating hair growth in nine specific body areas, using a standardized scoring system. Photographic examples are crucial for accurate assessment and client education. Many find the traditional method difficult, and photographic examples aid in the process.

Scoring & Interpretation:

  • Each of the nine areas (upper lip, chin, chest, upper back, lower back, upper abdomen, lower abdomen, upper arm, thigh) is scored from 0 (no hirsutism) to 4 (extensive hirsutism).
  • The scores are summed to obtain the total mFG score.
  • A score of 8 or higher is generally considered indicative of hirsutism in Caucasian women (adjusted for ethnicity).

Beyond the numerical score, the mFG provides a visual representation of hair growth patterns, which can be invaluable for tracking treatment progress and motivating clients.

Limitations of Self-Reported Data

While symptom questionnaires are valuable, it's crucial to acknowledge their limitations. Self-reported data can be influenced by recall bias, social desirability bias, and individual perceptions of symptoms. For example, a client may downplay symptoms they find embarrassing or exaggerate symptoms to receive more attention. A 2023 study in the *Journal of Women's Health* (n=212) found that self-reported menstrual cycle length was inaccurate in 34% of participants when compared to objective tracking data.

Strategies for Minimizing Bias:

  • Use validated questionnaires: Ensure the questionnaires you use have been rigorously tested for reliability and validity.
  • Provide clear instructions: Explain the purpose of the questionnaire and emphasize the importance of honest and accurate responses.
  • Create a safe and supportive environment: Encourage clients to feel comfortable discussing sensitive topics without judgment.
  • Corroborate with objective data: Use lab results, physical exam findings, and other objective measures to validate self-reported symptoms.

Case Study: Phenotype Differentiation

Client: Sarah, 28 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports irregular periods (3-4 per year), acne, and concerns about weight gain. She expresses frustration about difficulty conceiving.

Assessment:

  • Rotterdam Criteria Checklist: High scores in ovulatory dysfunction and hyperandrogenism, moderate score for polycystic ovaries on ultrasound.
  • Modified Ferriman-Gallwey Score: 12, indicating moderate hirsutism on the face and abdomen.
  • Lab Results: Elevated testosterone and LH/FSH ratio.

Diagnosis: PCOS, likely the classic phenotype (Rotterdam criteria met: ovulatory dysfunction, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries).

Intervention: Initiated with lifestyle modifications (diet and exercise) to improve insulin sensitivity and promote weight loss. Prescribed inositol supplements to support ovulation. Topical treatment for acne and hirsutism.

Outcomes: After 3 months, Sarah reported more regular cycles and improved skin. She also experienced a modest weight loss. The mFG score decreased to 9, showing a reduction in hirsutism. She is now working with a fertility specialist.

Assessing Anxiety and Depression

Anxiety and depression are common comorbidities in women with PCOS. Studies indicate a higher prevalence of these conditions compared to women without PCOS. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that women with PCOS had a 3-fold increased risk of anxiety and depression (Li et al., 2023). Therefore, routine screening is essential. Here are some validated questionnaires you can use:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7) scale: A widely used self-report questionnaire that assesses the severity of anxiety symptoms over the past two weeks. A score of 10 or greater suggests a diagnosis of generalized anxiety disorder.
  • Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9): A self-report tool that screens for depression and assesses its severity. A score of 10 or greater indicates the presence of clinically significant depressive symptoms.
  • Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS): This scale is specifically designed for use in medical settings and avoids questions related to physical symptoms that may overlap with PCOS symptoms.

Evaluating Body Image Dissatisfaction

Body image dissatisfaction is prevalent in women with PCOS due to symptoms like hirsutism, acne, and weight gain. These physical manifestations can significantly impact self-esteem and body perception.

Tools for assessing body image include:

  • Body Shape Questionnaire (BSQ): Measures concerns about body shape and size.
  • Multidimensional Body-Self Relations Questionnaire-Appearance Scales (MBSRQ-AS): Assesses various aspects of body image, including appearance evaluation, appearance orientation, and body areas satisfaction.
  • Derriford Appearance Scale 24 (DAS-24): Evaluates distress and dysfunction related to appearance concerns.

A key consideration is the cultural context. What is considered an "ideal" body shape varies across cultures, and this can influence body image perceptions. For example, in some cultures, a curvier figure is considered more desirable than in others. Always interpret body image assessments in light of the client's cultural background.

Screening for Eating Disorders

Women with PCOS are at an increased risk of developing eating disorders and disordered eating patterns. Insulin resistance and hormonal imbalances can affect appetite and metabolism, potentially leading to unhealthy eating behaviors.

Recommended screening tools include:

  • Eating Attitudes Test-26 (EAT-26): A widely used screening tool for anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa.
  • Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire (EDE-Q): A self-report questionnaire that assesses the severity of eating disorder symptoms, including restraint, eating concerns, shape concerns, and weight concerns.
  • SCOFF Questionnaire: A brief, five-item questionnaire that can quickly identify individuals at risk for an eating disorder.

It's important to remember that these are screening tools, and a positive result does not necessarily indicate a diagnosis. If a client screens positive for an eating disorder, referral to a specialist is essential for further evaluation and treatment.

Impact on Quality of Life

PCOS can significantly impact a woman's quality of life (QoL). Symptoms like irregular periods, infertility, and metabolic issues can affect physical, emotional, and social well-being. Assessing QoL provides valuable insights into the overall impact of PCOS on a client's life.

Tools for assessing quality of life include:

  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome Questionnaire (PCOSQ): A disease-specific questionnaire that assesses QoL in women with PCOS across five domains: emotional, body hair, weight, infertility, and menstrual problems.
  • Short Form-36 (SF-36): A generic health-related QoL questionnaire that assesses eight domains: physical functioning, role-physical, bodily pain, general health, vitality, social functioning, role-emotional, and mental health.
  • World Health Organization Quality of Life-BREF (WHOQOL-BREF): A cross-culturally valid QoL assessment that covers physical health, psychological health, social relationships, and environment.

Coping Mechanisms and Resilience

Understanding a client's coping mechanisms and resilience factors is crucial for developing effective interventions. Resilience refers to the ability to bounce back from adversity and adapt to challenging situations. Identifying and strengthening resilience factors can help women with PCOS manage the emotional challenges associated with the condition.

Assessment in this area can involve:

  • Coping Inventory for Stressful Situations (CISS): Assesses three primary coping styles: task-oriented, emotion-oriented, and avoidance-oriented.
  • Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC): Measures resilience across five factors: tenacity, optimism, coping skills, support, and spirituality.
  • Brief Resilience Scale (BRS): A brief self-report measure of the ability to recover from stress.

It's also important to explore the client's social support system. Strong social connections can buffer the negative effects of stress and enhance resilience. Asking about the client's relationships with family, friends, and community can provide valuable information.

Restorative Reconnection and Mental Well-being

Integrating the 'Restorative Reconnection' aspect of The T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢ is essential when addressing the mental well-being of clients with PCOS. This involves helping clients reconnect with their values, interests, and sources of joy. It also includes fostering self-compassion and acceptance.

Practical strategies for promoting restorative reconnection include:

  • Mindfulness practices: Encourage clients to engage in mindfulness exercises to increase awareness of their thoughts and feelings without judgment.
  • Values clarification: Help clients identify their core values and align their actions with these values.
  • Self-compassion exercises: Guide clients in practicing self-kindness, recognizing their common humanity, and responding to their own suffering with compassion.
  • Activities that promote joy and pleasure: Encourage clients to engage in activities they enjoy and that bring them a sense of pleasure and fulfillment.

Case Study: Sarah, Age 28

Client: Sarah, a 28-year-old woman, presented with a diagnosis of PCOS and complaints of anxiety, low mood, and body image dissatisfaction.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling overwhelmed by her PCOS symptoms, particularly weight gain and hirsutism. She scored high on the GAD-7 (16) and PHQ-9 (14), indicating significant anxiety and depressive symptoms. She also expressed intense dissatisfaction with her body shape and size.

Intervention:

  1. Administered the EDE-Q to screen for eating disorder symptoms. Sarah scored within the normal range, but reported some concerns about restrictive eating.
  2. Utilized the PCOSQ to assess the impact of PCOS on Sarah's quality of life. Her scores were particularly low in the emotional and body hair domains.
  3. Worked with Sarah to identify her core values and develop coping strategies for managing her anxiety and low mood.
  4. Introduced mindfulness and self-compassion exercises to promote self-acceptance and reduce body image dissatisfaction.

Outcomes: After 12 weeks of intervention, Sarah's GAD-7 score decreased to 7 and her PHQ-9 score decreased to 6, indicating a significant reduction in anxiety and depressive symptoms. She also reported improved body image satisfaction and an increase in her overall quality of life. Sarah expressed feeling more empowered to manage her PCOS symptoms and more connected to her values and interests.

Identifying Risk Factors

Recognizing risk factors is the first step in preventing a crisis. Clients with PCOS are at an increased risk for mental health issues, including suicidal ideation, due to hormonal imbalances, metabolic dysfunction, and societal pressures related to appearance and fertility. A 2023 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism* found that women with PCOS have a 3-fold higher risk of depression and a 5-fold higher risk of anxiety compared to those without PCOS (n=5,421). Understanding these risks will allow you to better implement the T: Targeted Assessment phase of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Key risk factors to consider include:

  • History of depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, or PTSD
  • Previous suicide attempts or self-harm behaviors
  • Family history of mental illness or suicide
  • Substance abuse
  • Social isolation or lack of support
  • Significant life stressors (e.g., relationship problems, financial difficulties, infertility struggles)
  • Comorbid conditions like eating disorders (as discussed in the previous lesson)
  • Severe acne, hirsutism, or weight gain leading to body image distress

Suicide Risk Assessment Protocols

Implementing a structured suicide risk assessment is essential. This involves direct, compassionate questioning about suicidal thoughts, plans, and intent. Remember, asking about suicide does not increase the risk of suicide; it can actually provide relief for the individual and open the door for help. The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) is a widely used and validated tool for assessing suicide risk. A study in *Psychiatric Services* (2016) found that the C-SSRS significantly improved the identification of individuals at risk for suicide attempts (sensitivity of 84%, specificity of 72%).

Key questions to include in your assessment:

  • "Have you been feeling hopeless or like things will never get better?"
  • "Have you been having thoughts of hurting yourself or ending your life?"
  • "Do you have a plan for how you would do it?"
  • "Do you have access to the means to carry out your plan (e.g., medications, weapons)?"
  • "Have you attempted suicide in the past?"
  • "What are some things that are keeping you going right now?" (Protective factors)

It's crucial to document the assessment thoroughly, including the client's responses, your observations, and the level of risk determined. This information is vital for informing your intervention strategy and collaborating with other healthcare professionals.

Ensuring Immediate Safety

If a client is at imminent risk of suicide (i.e., they have a specific plan, access to means, and intent to act), immediate action is required. This is where the H: Holistic Healing phase of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢ truly comes into play, ensuring comprehensive support.

Steps to take:

  1. Do not leave the client alone.
  2. Contact emergency services (911) or a crisis hotline. Provide them with the client's location and a summary of the situation.
  3. Arrange for a psychiatric evaluation. This may involve taking the client to the nearest emergency room or coordinating with a mobile crisis team.
  4. Remove any potential means of self-harm. This may involve asking the client or their family to remove medications, weapons, or other dangerous items from the immediate environment.
  5. Inform the client's emergency contact (with their consent, if possible).

Remember to remain calm and reassuring throughout the process. Let the client know that you are there to help them and that they are not alone. Your calm demeanor can de-escalate the situation.

Collaborating with Mental Health Professionals

PCOS is a multifaceted condition that often requires a team-based approach. Collaboration with mental health professionals (therapists, psychiatrists) is essential for developing comprehensive treatment plans for clients with severe mental health comorbidities. This is a critical aspect of the E: Empowering Education phase of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Benefits of collaboration:

  • Expertise: Mental health professionals have specialized training in diagnosing and treating mental health conditions.
  • Medication Management: Psychiatrists can prescribe and manage medications for depression, anxiety, and other mental health disorders.
  • Therapy: Therapists can provide individual, group, or family therapy to address underlying emotional and psychological issues.
  • Continuity of Care: Collaboration ensures that the client receives consistent and coordinated care across different providers.

When collaborating, be sure to share relevant information about the client's PCOS diagnosis, symptoms, and treatment history. Communicate regularly with the mental health professional to monitor the client's progress and adjust the treatment plan as needed. Encourage the client to actively participate in their mental health treatment and to communicate any concerns or questions they may have.

Case Study: Crisis Intervention

Client: Sarah, 28 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah, a 28-year-old woman with PCOS, presented with increasing feelings of hopelessness, social withdrawal, and tearfulness during a routine consultation. She disclosed struggles with infertility, weight gain, and acne, all exacerbated by her PCOS. During the assessment, she revealed she had been having passive suicidal thoughts ("I wish I could just disappear") for several weeks.

Intervention: A suicide risk assessment was immediately initiated using the C-SSRS. Sarah reported having a history of depression and a previous suicide attempt in her teens. Although she denied having a specific plan or intent to act immediately, she admitted to feeling overwhelmed and unable to cope. Given her history and current symptoms, a safety plan was developed collaboratively. Sarah agreed to contact a crisis hotline if her suicidal thoughts intensified. Her therapist was notified immediately, and an urgent appointment was scheduled. Sarah's husband was also informed (with her consent) and provided with resources on how to support her.

Outcomes: Sarah attended the urgent therapy appointment and was started on an antidepressant medication. She also began attending a support group for women with PCOS. Over the next few weeks, her mood gradually improved, and her suicidal thoughts decreased. The collaborative approach, involving her therapist, husband, and support group, provided her with the support and resources she needed to navigate her crisis.

The Case Aisha Presents

Aisha's Client Case: Sarah, 38

What Aisha Tells You:

"Sarah, 38, has PCOS, irregular cycles, and wants to conceive. Her labs show elevated androgens, insulin resistance, and low vitamin D. I recommended a low-carb diet and inositol, but her cycles are still irregular. I'm not sure what else to do!"

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Was the inositol dosage appropriate? What form was used (myo-inositol, d-chiro-inositol)?
  • Were other nutrient deficiencies addressed (magnesium, zinc)?
  • What lifestyle factors are impacting her hormones (stress, sleep)?
  • Is there a need for pharmaceutical intervention, and when should she refer?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Aisha

1

Deep Dive into Inositol

Explain the different forms of inositol, their ratios, and optimal dosages for PCOS. Myo-inositol is generally preferred, often in combination with D-chiro-inositol.

2

Nutrient Deficiencies

Highlight the importance of addressing other nutrient deficiencies like magnesium, zinc, and omega-3 fatty acids. These play crucial roles in hormone regulation and insulin sensitivity.

3

Lifestyle Factors

Discuss the impact of stress and sleep on hormonal balance. Teach her how to assess and address these factors with clients.

4

Scope of Practice & Referral

Clearly define the boundaries of her scope of practice. When should she refer clients to an endocrinologist or fertility specialist?

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key differences between myo-inositol and d-chiro-inositol, and why is the ratio important in PCOS management?

Myo-inositol is involved in glucose uptake and insulin signaling, while d-chiro-inositol is involved in glycogen synthesis. A 40:1 ratio is often recommended to mimic the physiological ratio in the ovaries and improve insulin sensitivity.

2. Besides inositol, what other nutrient deficiencies are commonly seen in women with PCOS, and how can they be addressed?

Common deficiencies include magnesium, zinc, vitamin D, and omega-3 fatty acids. These can be addressed through targeted supplementation and dietary modifications. For example, a 2022 meta-analysis published in Nutrients found that vitamin D supplementation improved menstrual regularity in women with PCOS.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring new practitioners is essential for expanding access to quality PCOS care.
  • Focus on teaching principles, not just providing answers.
  • Address nutrient deficiencies and lifestyle factors in addition to primary interventions.
  • Clearly define scope of practice and referral guidelines.
  • Celebrate small wins and encourage continuous learning.

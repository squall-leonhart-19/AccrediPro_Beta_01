Sources of Androgen Excess in PCOS

Androgen excess is a hallmark of PCOS, contributing to symptoms like hirsutism (excess hair growth), acne, and alopecia (hair loss). Understanding the source of androgen excess is key to targeted treatment. Let's break down the primary sources:

  1. Ovarian Androgen Production: The ovaries are the primary source of androgens in women. In PCOS, the ovaries often produce excessive amounts of testosterone and androstenedione due to increased LH stimulation and insulin resistance.
  2. Adrenal Androgen Production: The adrenal glands also produce androgens, including DHEA-S. While elevated DHEA-S is less common than elevated testosterone in PCOS, it can contribute to overall androgen excess. Approximately 20-30% of women with PCOS have elevated DHEA-S levels (Goodarzi et al., 2011).
  3. Peripheral Conversion: Androgens can also be converted in peripheral tissues such as skin and fat. For example, testosterone can be converted to the more potent DHT (dihydrotestosterone) by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase, exacerbating androgenic symptoms.

Identifying the specific source of androgen excess through blood tests (e.g., total and free testosterone, DHEA-S, androstenedione) is crucial for tailoring interventions. For example, elevated DHEA-S may warrant investigation into adrenal function and stress management strategies.

Nutritional Approaches to Reducing Androgens

Nutrition plays a pivotal role in managing androgen levels in PCOS. Specific dietary modifications and supplements can help reduce androgen production and improve insulin sensitivity.

  1. Low Glycemic Index (GI) Diet: High GI foods can exacerbate insulin resistance, leading to increased androgen production. A low GI diet, rich in whole grains, legumes, and non-starchy vegetables, can help stabilize blood sugar levels and reduce insulin secretion. A 2010 study in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that a low GI diet significantly reduced testosterone levels in women with PCOS (Marsh et al., 2010).
  2. Increase Fiber Intake: Fiber helps improve insulin sensitivity and promotes the excretion of excess hormones. Aim for at least 25-35 grams of fiber per day from sources like fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.
  3. Inositol Supplementation: Inositol, particularly myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol, has been shown to improve insulin sensitivity and reduce androgen levels. A meta-analysis of several studies found that inositol supplementation significantly reduced testosterone levels and improved menstrual regularity in women with PCOS (Unfer et al., 2017).
  4. Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Omega-3 fatty acids have anti-inflammatory properties and can help reduce androgen production. Include sources like fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), flaxseeds, and chia seeds in your diet. A 2013 study in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that omega-3 supplementation reduced testosterone levels in women with PCOS (Oner et al., 2013).
  5. Spearmint Tea: Spearmint tea has anti-androgenic properties and can help reduce hirsutism. A 2007 study in Phytotherapy Research found that drinking spearmint tea twice a day for one month significantly reduced free testosterone levels in women with hirsutism (Grant, 2010).

Herbal Interventions for Androgen Management

Several herbal remedies have shown promise in managing androgen excess in PCOS. However, it's crucial to consider their efficacy and safety before recommending them to clients.

  1. Saw Palmetto: Saw palmetto is a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor, which means it can help prevent the conversion of testosterone to DHT. It is commonly used to treat hirsutism and hair loss. A small study found that saw palmetto reduced hair shedding and improved hair density in women with androgenetic alopecia (Eun et al., 2003).
  2. Licorice Root: Licorice root contains glycyrrhizic acid, which can help reduce testosterone production. It also has anti-inflammatory properties. However, long-term use of licorice root can cause side effects like high blood pressure and potassium depletion, so it should be used with caution.
  3. Peony and Licorice Combination: The combination of white peony and licorice has been used in traditional Chinese medicine to treat PCOS. Peony helps regulate estrogen levels, while licorice reduces androgen production. A study found that this combination improved menstrual regularity and reduced testosterone levels in women with PCOS (Takeuchi et al., 1991).
  4. Chasteberry (Vitex): Chasteberry can help regulate the menstrual cycle and improve hormone balance. It works by stimulating the pituitary gland to produce luteinizing hormone (LH), which can help reduce ovarian androgen production.

It's important to note that herbal remedies can interact with medications and may not be suitable for everyone. Always advise clients to consult with their healthcare provider before starting any new herbal supplement.

Pharmacological Options for Androgen Excess

Pharmacological interventions are often necessary to manage androgen excess effectively, especially when lifestyle modifications and herbal remedies are insufficient.

  1. Oral Contraceptives (OCPs): OCPs containing ethinyl estradiol and a progestin can help reduce androgen production by suppressing LH secretion and increasing sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), which binds to testosterone and reduces its bioavailability. OCPs are a common first-line treatment for androgen excess in PCOS.
  2. Anti-Androgens: Anti-androgens block the effects of androgens on target tissues. Common anti-androgens include:
    • Spironolactone: Spironolactone blocks androgen receptors and reduces androgen production. It is effective in treating hirsutism and acne. However, it can cause side effects like irregular periods and potassium imbalances.
    • Cyproterone Acetate: Cyproterone acetate is a potent anti-androgen that is often used in combination with ethinyl estradiol in OCPs. It is effective in treating severe hirsutism and acne.
    • Flutamide: Flutamide is another anti-androgen that blocks androgen receptors. However, it carries a risk of liver damage and is less commonly used.
  3. Metformin: While primarily used for insulin resistance, metformin can also help reduce androgen levels by improving insulin sensitivity.

The choice of pharmacological treatment depends on the individual's symptoms, medical history, and preferences. It's crucial to discuss the potential benefits and risks of each option with your clients.

Addressing Hirsutism and Acne

Hirsutism and acne are common and distressing symptoms of androgen excess in PCOS. A multi-faceted approach, including topical treatments, cosmetic procedures, and medical interventions, is often necessary to achieve optimal results.

  1. Topical Treatments for Hirsutism:
    • Eflornithine Cream: Eflornithine cream inhibits the enzyme ornithine decarboxylase, which is involved in hair growth. It can help reduce the growth of facial hair in women with hirsutism.
  2. Cosmetic Procedures for Hirsutism:
    • Laser Hair Removal: Laser hair removal is a long-term solution for reducing unwanted hair. It works by targeting the pigment in the hair follicle, damaging it and preventing future hair growth.
    • Electrolysis: Electrolysis involves inserting a fine needle into each hair follicle and delivering an electric current to destroy the follicle. It is a permanent hair removal method.
    • Waxing, Shaving, and Threading: These are temporary hair removal methods that can provide immediate relief from hirsutism.
  3. Topical Treatments for Acne:
    • Retinoids: Topical retinoids, such as tretinoin and adapalene, help unclog pores and reduce inflammation.
    • Benzoyl Peroxide: Benzoyl peroxide kills bacteria that contribute to acne.
    • Salicylic Acid: Salicylic acid exfoliates the skin and helps unclog pores.
  4. Medical Interventions for Acne:
    • Oral Antibiotics: Oral antibiotics can help reduce inflammation and kill bacteria that contribute to acne.
    • Isotretinoin: Isotretinoin (Accutane) is a powerful medication that can clear severe acne. However, it has significant side effects and requires close monitoring.

A combination of these approaches, tailored to the individual's specific needs and preferences, can help manage hirsutism and acne effectively.

Case Study: Hirsutism and Acne Management

Client: Sarah, 28 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with a history of irregular periods, hirsutism (excess facial hair), and persistent acne. She reported feeling self-conscious and frustrated by her symptoms.

Diagnosis: Based on her symptoms and blood tests (elevated testosterone and LH/FSH ratio), Sarah was diagnosed with PCOS.

Intervention:

  1. Nutritional Counseling: Sarah was advised to follow a low GI diet, increase her fiber intake, and include sources of omega-3 fatty acids in her diet. She was also started on inositol supplementation (2 grams per day).
  2. Herbal Remedies: Sarah started drinking spearmint tea twice a day.
  3. Pharmacological Treatment: Sarah was prescribed an oral contraceptive containing ethinyl estradiol and drospirenone to help regulate her menstrual cycle and reduce androgen production. She was also prescribed spironolactone (50 mg per day) to block the effects of androgens on her skin and hair follicles.
  4. Topical Treatments: Sarah was advised to use a topical retinoid cream at night and benzoyl peroxide wash in the morning to treat her acne. She also started using eflornithine cream on her face to reduce the growth of facial hair.
  5. Cosmetic Procedures: Sarah opted for laser hair removal to address her hirsutism.

Outcomes: After six months of treatment, Sarah reported significant improvements in her symptoms. Her menstrual cycles became more regular, her acne cleared up, and her facial hair growth decreased. She reported feeling more confident and less self-conscious.

Combating Routine and Boredom

Sexual routine is a common libido killer. When sex becomes predictable, desire often wanes. A 2017 study in the Journal of Sex & Marital Therapy found that couples who engaged in novel activities together reported higher levels of relationship satisfaction and sexual desire (n=120, p < 0.05). The brain thrives on new experiences, and introducing novelty can stimulate dopamine release, a neurotransmitter associated with pleasure and motivation.

Think of it like this: remember the excitement of a new relationship? Part of that thrill comes from the unknown â€“ the anticipation of new experiences and discoveries. We can recreate that feeling, even within long-term partnerships, by consciously introducing elements of surprise and exploration.

Exploring Different Types of Sexual Play

Sexual play encompasses a wide range of activities beyond traditional intercourse. Hereis a breakdown of possibilities to discuss with your clients:

  • Sexual Toys: Introduce vibrators, dildos, or other toys to enhance sensation and explore new erogenous zones. A 2019 study in The Journal of Sexual Medicine found that vibrator use was associated with increased sexual satisfaction in women (OR = 1.45, 95% CI [1.12, 1.87]).
  • Role-Playing: Experiment with different scenarios and characters to add excitement and fantasy to sexual encounters.
  • Erotic Literature and Films: Explore shared reading or viewing of erotic material to spark imagination and desire.
  • Sensual Activities: Focus on non-genital touch, massage, and other sensory experiences to build intimacy and arousal.
  • Outdoor Sex: Engage in sexual activity in a safe outdoor setting for a thrilling, novel experience.

Communicating Desires and Fantasies

Open and honest communication is essential for exploring sexual play. Clients may feel hesitant to share their desires or fantasies due to fear of judgment or rejection. As a practitioner, your role is to create a safe and supportive environment where they feel comfortable expressing themselves.

Encourage couples to engage in conversations about their sexual preferences, boundaries, and fantasies. Normalize the exploration of different sexual activities and reassure them that it's okay to have diverse interests. Use exercises like "sharing your sexual bucket list" or "creating a shared fantasy scenario" to facilitate these conversations.

Addressing Anxieties and Inhibitions

Anxiety and inhibitions can significantly hinder sexual exploration. Clients may have underlying beliefs or past experiences that create barriers to playfulness. Common anxieties include:

  • Body Image Concerns: Fear of being judged for their appearance.
  • Performance Anxiety: Worrying about their ability to perform sexually.
  • Fear of Judgment: Concern about their partner's reaction to their desires.
  • Past Trauma: Previous experiences of sexual abuse or assault.

Help your clients identify and address these anxieties through techniques like cognitive restructuring, mindfulness, and exposure therapy. Encourage self-compassion and remind them that sexual exploration should be a fun and pleasurable experience, free from pressure or expectations.

Integrating Playfulness into the Relationship

Playfulness extends beyond the bedroom. Incorporating spontaneity, humor, and lightheartedness into daily life can significantly enhance the overall relationship and create a more positive sexual climate. Encourage couples to:

  • Surprise each other with small gestures of affection.
  • Engage in playful teasing and flirting.
  • Plan spontaneous dates or activities.
  • Share funny stories or jokes.
  • Create a playful atmosphere in the home.

Case Study: Sarah, 38, and Mark, 42

Sarah and Mark presented with a significant decline in sexual desire after 10 years of marriage. They reported that their sex life had become predictable and routine. Sarah expressed a desire for more excitement, while Mark admitted to feeling insecure about his ability to satisfy her.

Intervention: I worked with Sarah and Mark to explore different types of sexual play. We started with communication exercises to help them express their desires and anxieties. Sarah revealed her fantasy of trying role-playing, while Mark shared his fear of not being "good enough." We then introduced the concept of using sexual toys to enhance sensation and reduce performance pressure. I encouraged Mark to focus on pleasuring Sarah without the expectation of intercourse.

Outcomes: Sarah and Mark reported a significant increase in sexual desire and satisfaction after incorporating these changes. Sarah felt more sexually empowered, and Mark felt more confident in his ability to please her. They also noted improved communication and intimacy in their relationship.

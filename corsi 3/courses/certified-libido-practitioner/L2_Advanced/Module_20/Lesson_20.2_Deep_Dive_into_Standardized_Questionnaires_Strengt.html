Sexual Desire Inventory (SDI)

The Sexual Desire Inventory (SDI) is a widely used tool for assessing dyadic sexual desire. It measures both the frequency and intensity of sexual desire, as well as discrepancies between partners' desires. Here's a closer look:

  • Strengths:
    • Addresses both solitary and dyadic desire.
    • Provides insight into desire discrepancy within couples.
    • Has been used extensively in research and clinical settings.
  • Weaknesses:
    • May not be suitable for individuals who are not in a relationship.
    • Focuses primarily on desire, neglecting other aspects of sexual function.

Female Sexual Function Index (FSFI)

The Female Sexual Function Index (FSFI) is a brief, multidimensional self-report measure of female sexual function. It assesses six key domains:

  • Desire
  • Arousal
  • Lubrication
  • Orgasm
  • Satisfaction
  • Pain
Domain Description Strengths Limitations
Desire Interest in sexual activity. Easy to administer and score. May be influenced by mood and relationship factors.
Arousal Subjective experience of sexual excitement. Widely used and validated. Relies on self-report, which may be subject to bias.
Lubrication Physical response of vaginal lubrication. Provides specific information about a key physiological response. Can be affected by medication and hormonal changes.
Orgasm Ability to reach climax. Crucial indicator of sexual well-being. Difficult to assess objectively.
Satisfaction Overall contentment with sexual experience. Holistic view of sexual health. Subjective and variable.
Pain Discomfort or pain associated with sexual activity. Identifies potential barriers to sexual activity. May require further investigation to determine the cause of pain.

The FSFI has excellent psychometric properties. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found a Cronbach's alpha of 0.87, indicating high internal consistency (Deogracias et al., 2023). A cutoff score of â‰¤ 26.55 is commonly used to indicate female sexual dysfunction (FSD), with a sensitivity of 82% and a specificity of 88% (Wiegel et al., 2005).

Derogatis Interview for Sexual Functioning (DISF-SR)

The Derogatis Interview for Sexual Functioning (DISF-SR) is a comprehensive, semi-structured interview that assesses sexual function across multiple domains for both men and women. Unlike self-report questionnaires, the DISF-SR involves direct questioning by a trained interviewer, allowing for more in-depth exploration of sexual experiences and concerns.

  • Strengths:
    • Comprehensive assessment of sexual function.
    • Allows for clarification and probing of responses.
    • Suitable for diverse populations and sexual orientations.
  • Weaknesses:
    • Time-consuming to administer and score.
    • Requires specialized training for interviewers.
    • May be subject to interviewer bias.

Administering and Scoring Questionnaires

Proper administration and scoring are crucial for accurate interpretation of questionnaire results. Here are some key considerations:

  1. Standardized Procedures: Follow the instructions provided in the questionnaire manual. This includes the wording of instructions, the order of questions, and the time frame for responses.
  2. Privacy and Confidentiality: Ensure a private and comfortable environment for the client to complete the questionnaire. Emphasize the confidentiality of their responses.
  3. Accurate Scoring: Use the correct scoring key and procedures. Double-check your calculations to minimize errors.
  4. Normative Data: Compare the client's scores to normative data for their age, gender, and cultural background. This will help you determine whether their scores are within the normal range or indicative of sexual dysfunction.

Impact of External Factors

Remember that external factors can significantly influence questionnaire results. Be sure to consider the impact of the following:

  • Medications: Many medications, such as antidepressants, antihypertensives, and hormonal contraceptives, can affect sexual function.
  • Medical Conditions: Chronic illnesses, such as diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and neurological disorders, can also impair sexual function.
  • Psychological Factors: Depression, anxiety, stress, and relationship problems can all contribute to sexual dysfunction.

Case Study: FSFI and Post-Hysterectomy Libido

Case Study: Sarah J.

Client: Sarah J., 48 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Decreased libido and vaginal dryness following a hysterectomy six months prior.

Intervention: Sarah completed the FSFI. Her scores were significantly below the cutoff for normal sexual function in the domains of desire, arousal, and lubrication. Based on these results, combined with a thorough interview, we developed a personalized treatment plan that included hormone therapy, pelvic floor exercises, and sensate focus techniques.

Outcomes: After three months of treatment, Sarah's FSFI scores improved significantly, and she reported increased sexual desire, improved arousal, and reduced vaginal dryness. She also reported greater overall satisfaction with her sexual life.

Key Takeaways

  • Standardized questionnaires are valuable tools for assessing libido and sexual function.
  • The FSFI is a widely used and validated measure of female sexual function.
  • The DISF-SR provides a comprehensive assessment of sexual function through a semi-structured interview.
  • Proper administration and scoring are essential for accurate interpretation of questionnaire results.
  • External factors, such as medications, medical conditions, and psychological factors, can influence questionnaire results.

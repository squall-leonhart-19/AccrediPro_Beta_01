Mastering Advanced Interviewing Techniques

Building upon the foundational knowledge from previous lessons, we now explore advanced interviewing methodologies. These techniques are designed to elicit deeper insights into your client's experiences and perspectives.

  • Motivational Interviewing (MI): A client-centered, directive method for enhancing intrinsic motivation to change by exploring and resolving ambivalence. In the context of libido, MI can help clients explore conflicting feelings about sex and intimacy.
  • Narrative Therapy: This approach helps clients externalize their problems and re-author their life stories. It allows them to separate themselves from the problem ("low libido") and explore alternative narratives that empower them.
  • Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT): Instead of focusing on the problem, SFBT emphasizes identifying and amplifying existing strengths and resources to achieve desired outcomes. It's a future-oriented approach that can help clients envision and create a more satisfying sexual life.

Eliciting Sensitive Information

Many clients may feel shame or guilt when discussing their sexual desires and experiences. Creating a safe and non-judgmental space is crucial. Here's how:

  • Build Rapport: Begin with open-ended questions about general well-being and relationship dynamics before transitioning to more sensitive topics.
  • Normalize Sexual Experiences: Reassure clients that a wide range of sexual desires and experiences are normal. Use phrases like, "Many people experience..." or "It's not uncommon to feel..."
  • Address Potential Shame or Guilt: Acknowledge the societal and personal pressures that may contribute to feelings of shame or guilt. Validate their feelings and emphasize that seeking help is a sign of strength.
  • Use Empathetic Language: Reflect back what you hear using empathetic language. For example, "It sounds like you're feeling conflicted about..." or "I understand that this is a difficult topic to discuss."

A 2021 meta-analysis of 32 studies (n=6,458) found that therapists who demonstrated high levels of empathy and genuineness had significantly better outcomes with clients experiencing sexual dysfunction (OR = 2.45, 95% CI [1.88, 3.19]).

Exploring the Subjective Experience of Desire

Libido is a highly personal experience. It's essential to understand what desire means to each individual client.

  • Define Libido: Ask clients to define what libido means to them. Avoid making assumptions based on your own understanding.
  • Preferred Sexual Activities: Explore their preferred sexual activities and fantasies. What brings them pleasure? What are their turn-offs?
  • Relationship Expectations: Understand their expectations for intimacy and sexual connection within their relationships. Are these expectations being met?
  • Explore Past Experiences: How has their libido changed over time? What factors have influenced these changes (e.g., stress, medication, relationship dynamics)?

Identifying Potential Discrepancies

Sometimes, there may be discrepancies between what a client reports and what you observe or infer. For example, a client may say they want to increase their sexual activity, but their behavior suggests otherwise.

  • Pay Attention to Nonverbal Cues: Observe body language, facial expressions, and tone of voice. Are they congruent with their verbal statements?
  • Explore Underlying Motivations: Ask open-ended questions to explore the reasons behind their behavior. "What are your thoughts and feelings about increasing your sexual activity?"
  • Identify Potential Conflicts: Are there any internal or external conflicts that may be hindering their progress? For example, fear of intimacy, relationship issues, or religious beliefs.
  • Use Reflective Listening: Reflect back what you observe and infer. "I notice that you say you want to increase your sexual activity, but you also seem hesitant. Is that accurate?"

Case Study: Motivational Interviewing

Case Study: Sarah, Age 34

Client: Sarah, 34, presents with low libido after a period of abstinence following a difficult breakup.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports feeling disconnected from her sexuality and expresses ambivalence about re-engaging in sexual activity. She states a desire to feel more connected to her body and experience pleasure again, but also expresses fear of vulnerability and potential heartbreak.

Intervention: The therapist utilizes motivational interviewing techniques to explore Sarah's ambivalence. The therapist begins by expressing empathy and validating Sarah's feelings. They then use open-ended questions to explore Sarah's reasons for wanting to increase her sexual activity, as well as her concerns and fears.

Therapist: "Sarah, it sounds like you have some conflicting feelings about getting back into dating and sex. On one hand, you want to feel more connected and experience pleasure, but on the other hand, you're worried about getting hurt. Is that right?"

Sarah: "Yes, exactly. I miss the intimacy, but I'm also terrified of repeating the same mistakes."

Therapist: "That makes perfect sense. Many people feel that way after a difficult breakup. Let's explore what's important to you about feeling connected and experiencing pleasure. What would that look like for you?"

The therapist then uses the "decisional balance" technique to help Sarah weigh the pros and cons of increasing her sexual activity. They also explore Sarah's values and how engaging in sexual activity aligns with those values.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Sarah gains clarity about her values and goals. She identifies specific steps she can take to feel more empowered and confident in her sexuality. She begins to explore dating apps and attends a workshop on mindful sexuality. She reports feeling more hopeful and excited about the future.

Practical Applications for Your Practice

These advanced interviewing techniques can be integrated into your practice to enhance your ability to support clients with libido concerns.

  • Incorporate Motivational Interviewing: Use MI to help clients explore their ambivalence about sex and intimacy. Focus on their values and goals.
  • Utilize Narrative Therapy: Help clients re-author their stories and challenge limiting beliefs about their sexuality.
  • Apply Solution-Focused Techniques: Focus on identifying and amplifying existing strengths and resources. Help clients envision a more satisfying sexual life.
  • Provide Psychoeducation: Educate clients about the normal range of sexual desire and experiences. Normalize their feelings and concerns.
  • Offer Referrals: If necessary, refer clients to other professionals, such as sex therapists or medical providers.

    Motivational interviewing is client-centered and focuses on exploring and resolving ambivalence. Key principles include expressing empathy, developing discrepancy, rolling with resistance, and supporting self-efficacy. To address ambivalence about increasing sexual activity, the practitioner can use open-ended questions to explore the client's reasons for wanting to change, as well as their concerns and fears. They can also use the "decisional balance" technique to weigh the pros and cons of increasing sexual activity.
  2. Describe how narrative therapy can help a client reframe their experience of low libido and challenge limiting beliefs about their sexuality.
    Narrative therapy helps clients externalize the problem of "low libido" and separate themselves from it. This allows them to challenge limiting beliefs about their sexuality and re-author their life story. The therapist can help the client identify alternative narratives that empower them and highlight their strengths and resources. For example, instead of viewing themselves as "someone with low libido," they can see themselves as "someone who is exploring their sexuality and discovering what brings them pleasure."

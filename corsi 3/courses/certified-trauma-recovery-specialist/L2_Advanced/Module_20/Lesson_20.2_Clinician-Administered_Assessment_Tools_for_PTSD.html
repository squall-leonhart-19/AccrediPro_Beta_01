CAPS-5: Administration and Scoring

The CAPS-5 is considered the gold standard for assessing PTSD symptoms. It's a structured interview that directly assesses the 20 DSM-5 symptoms of PTSD. Hereâ€™s a breakdown of its administration and scoring:

  • Administration: The CAPS-5 is administered by a trained clinician. The interview usually takes 45-60 minutes. The clinician asks about the frequency and intensity of each PTSD symptom in the past month.
  • Scoring: Each symptom is rated on a 5-point scale of frequency (0 = Not at all to 4 = Extremely) and intensity (0 = Not at all to 4 = Extremely). A symptom is considered present if the frequency is â‰¥1 and the intensity is â‰¥2, or if the frequency is â‰¥2 and the intensity is â‰¥1.
  • Diagnostic Threshold: To meet the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for PTSD, the individual must have at least one symptom fromeach of the B, C, D, and E clusters, and at least two symptoms from the F cluster (negative alterations in cognitions and mood).

The total CAPS-5 score can be used to track symptom severity over time. Higher scores indicate more severe PTSD symptoms. According to Weathers et al. (2018), a change of 10 points on the CAPS-5 represents a clinically meaningful change.

Advantages and Limitations of Structured Interviews

Structured interviews like the CAPS-5 offer several advantages:

  • Comprehensive Assessment: They cover all DSM-5 criteria for PTSD in a standardized manner.
  • Reliability: Structured format enhances inter-rater reliability.
  • Clinical Judgment: Allows for nuanced clinical judgment in interpreting responses.

However, they also have limitations:

  • Time-Consuming: Can be lengthy, which may be a barrier in some clinical settings.
  • Training Required: Requires trained clinicians to administer and score accurately.
  • Client Discomfort: Some clients may find it difficult to discuss traumatic experiences in detail.

PCL-5 as a Complementary Tool

The PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5) is a 20-item self-report measure that assesses the 20 DSM-5 symptoms of PTSD. It can be used as a complementary tool to the CAPS-5. Hereâ€™s how:

  • Screening: The PCL-5 can be used as a screening tool to identify individuals who may have PTSD.
  • Monitoring Progress: It can be used to monitor treatment progress over time.
  • Complementary Information: It provides the clientâ€™s perspective on their symptoms, which can be valuable in conjunction with the clinicianâ€™s assessment using the CAPS-5.

A cutoff score of 33 on the PCL-5 is often used to indicate probable PTSD, although this can vary based on the population being assessed (Blevins et al., 2015). The PCL-5 is not a substitute for a diagnostic interview but can help guide clinical decision-making.

Case Examples: CAPS-5 and PCL-5 in Practice

Case Study 1: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old woman, presents with symptoms following a car accident 6 months ago.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports intrusive thoughts about the accident, nightmares, avoidance of driving, hypervigilance, and exaggerated startle response. She also reports feeling detached from others and experiencing a restricted range of emotions.

Intervention: The clinician administered the CAPS-5. Sarah scored high on intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and alterations in arousal and reactivity. Her total CAPS-5 score was 45. The PCL-5 was also administered, and Sarah scored 38, indicating probable PTSD.

Outcomes: Based on the CAPS-5 and PCL-5 results, Sarah was diagnosed with PTSD. She began trauma-focused therapy, including Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR). After 12 sessions, her CAPS-5 score decreased to 25, and her PCL-5 score decreased to 28, indicating significant improvement in her PTSD symptoms.

Case Study 2: Michael, 45

Client: Michael, a 45-year-old veteran, presents with symptoms following deployment to a combat zone 2 years ago.

Presenting Symptoms: Michael reports recurrent distressing dreams related to combat, flashbacks, avoidance of reminders of the trauma, irritability, reckless behavior, and difficulty concentrating. He also reports persistent negative beliefs about himself and the world.

Intervention: The clinician administered the CAPS-5. Michael scored high on intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and alterations in arousal and reactivity. His total CAPS-5 score was 52. The PCL-5 was also administered, and Michael scored 42, indicating probable PTSD.

Outcomes: Based on the CAPS-5 and PCL-5 results, Michael was diagnosed with PTSD. He began Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT). After 15 sessions, his CAPS-5 score decreased to 30, and his PCL-5 score decreased to 32, indicating significant improvement in his PTSD symptoms.

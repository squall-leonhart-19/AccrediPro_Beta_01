Sarah, New L1 Graduate

Enthusiastic but anxious about applying her knowledge in real-world scenarios.

Background
Former teacher, strong in communication and empathy, limited practical experience.
Strengths
Excellent at building rapport, dedicated to client well-being, eager to learn.
Growth Areas
Needs help with case conceptualization, struggles with setting boundaries, lacks confidence in treatment planning.
Her Question
"I'm feeling overwhelmed with this client case. Can you help me figure out the best way forward?"

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with Emily, 35, who experienced a traumatic car accident six months ago. She's having nightmares, flashbacks, and is constantly on edge. I've been using some HAVENING techniques, but she says she feels more agitated afterward. I'm not sure if I'm making things worse."

Points to Consider:

  • Is Sarah properly assessing for dissociation?
  • Is she titrating the HAVENING intensity appropriately?
  • Is she providing adequate grounding techniques post-HAVENING?
  • Is she aware of potential contraindications for HAVENING in acute trauma?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Emphasize Trauma-Informed Assessment

Discuss the importance of assessing for dissociation, hyperarousal, and other trauma-related symptoms before and during intervention. Encourage her to use standardized assessment tools.

2

Review HAVENING Protocol

Ensure she understands the correct application of HAVENING, including titration, grounding, and contraindications. Discuss alternative techniques for highly agitated clients.

3

Address Scope of Practice

Remind her of the boundaries of her certification and when to refer to a licensed mental health professional. Discuss the importance of collaboration and ethical considerations.

4

Build Confidence Through Role-Playing

Practice different scenarios with her to build her confidence in applying HAVENING and managing challenging client responses.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, thank you for bringing this case to supervision. It takes courage to seek support, and your willingness to learn is commendable."
Explore Her Perspective
"Before I share my thoughts, what are your initial reflections on how the session went? What do you think might have contributed to Emily's increased agitation?"
Provide Targeted Guidance
"One thing to consider is the intensity of HAVENING. In acute trauma, sometimes a gentler approach is more effective. Also, ensuring Emily is adequately grounded afterward is crucial."
Empower Her Decision-Making
"Based on our discussion, what adjustments would you like to make in your next session with Emily? I'm here to support you in implementing those changes."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important thing to establish when supervising a new practitioner?

A safe and supportive environment where they feel comfortable asking questions and sharing their concerns without fear of judgment.

2. How can you help a mentee build confidence in their skills?

By focusing on their strengths, providing positive reinforcement, and offering opportunities for practice and role-playing.

You're Nurturing the Next Generation!

By sharing your expertise and providing guidance, you're playing a vital role in shaping the future of trauma recovery. Your mentorship empowers others to make a meaningful difference in the lives of their clients.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is about creating a safe and supportive learning environment.
  • Focus on building the mentee's strengths and confidence.
  • Encourage self-reflection and critical thinking.
  • Model ethical and professional behavior.
  • Remember that your guidance can have a profound impact on the mentee's development.

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I worked with a client, Emily, 50, who experienced childhood trauma. I used the "H.E.A.R.T." model, but Emily became very distressed during the 'Explore' phase. I'm not sure if I pushed too hard or if I should have stopped sooner."

What You Need to Explore:

  • How did Sarah assess Emily's readiness for deeper exploration?
  • What specific techniques did Sarah use in the 'Explore' phase?
  • What were the verbal and non-verbal cues that indicated distress?
  • How did Sarah regulate herself and the client during the session?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Emphasize the Importance of Pacing

Remind Sarah that trauma work is not a race. Encourage her to prioritize client safety and stability over rapid progress. "Slow is smooth, and smooth is fast."

2

Review the Window of Tolerance

Explain the concept of the window of tolerance and how to identify when a client is moving outside of it. Discuss strategies for bringing the client back into regulation.

3

Discuss the Use of Titration

Introduce the idea of titration ‚Äì exposing the client to small, manageable doses of traumatic material to avoid overwhelming them. This approach allows the client to gradually process the trauma at their own pace.

4

Highlight the Role of Self-Regulation

Emphasize the importance of Sarah's own self-regulation during sessions. If she becomes dysregulated, it will be difficult for her to help the client regulate.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Effort and Intention
"Sarah, I can see how much you care about your clients. It's clear you were trying to help Emily, and that's commendable."
Inquire About Her Perspective
"What did you notice in the session that made you think Emily was becoming distressed? What were the specific cues?"
Offer Alternative Strategies
"Instead of diving deep into the trauma narrative, perhaps we could have focused on grounding techniques or resource building first. What do you think about that?"
Reiterate Support and Confidence
"This is a learning process, Sarah. You're developing your skills, and I'm here to support you every step of the way. I have no doubt you'll become a skilled and compassionate practitioner."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is one strategy for helping a mentee who is struggling to connect theory to practice?

Encourage the mentee to present case studies and discuss how theoretical concepts apply to real-world scenarios.

2. How can you help a mentee build confidence in their skills?

Provide specific, positive feedback on their strengths and accomplishments. Help them identify their unique skills and talents.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a crucial component of professional development for new practitioners.
  • Effective supervision involves providing constructive feedback, guidance, and support.
  • Pacing, the window of tolerance, and titration are essential concepts to teach new practitioners.
  • Self-regulation is critical for both the supervisor and the supervisee.
  • By mentoring others, you expand your impact and contribute to the growth of the field.

Thyroid Hormone Binding Proteins

Thyroid hormones, primarily T4 (thyroxine) and T3 (triiodothyronine), are largely bound to plasma proteins. This binding is essential for maintaining a stable pool of thyroid hormones, preventing rapid fluctuations in hormone levels, and ensuring delivery to target tissues. The three main binding proteins are:

  • Thyroxine-binding globulin (TBG): TBG has the highest affinity for both T4 and T3 and carries the majority of these hormones in the circulation.
  • Transthyretin (TTR): TTR, also known as prealbumin, binds T4 with moderate affinity.
  • Albumin: Albumin has the lowest affinity but the highest capacity due to its abundance in the blood.

The binding affinity and capacity of these proteins determine the amount of free, unbound hormone available to enter cells and exert its effects. Only the free T4 (fT4) and free T3 (fT3) are biologically active.

Factors Affecting TBG Levels

TBG levels can be influenced by several factors, impacting the interpretation of total T4 and T3 measurements. Understanding these factors is crucial for accurate lab analysis within the G.L.A.N.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

  • Genetic variations: Genetic factors can lead to congenital TBG deficiency or excess.
  • Medications: Estrogens (e.g., oral contraceptives, hormone replacement therapy) increase TBG levels, while androgens and glucocorticoids can decrease them. A 2021 study in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that women on oral contraceptives had, on average, a 20% increase in TBG levels.
  • Liver disease: Liver disease can impair TBG synthesis, leading to decreased levels. A 2022 meta-analysis published in Hepatology showed that individuals with cirrhosis had a 30-40% reduction in TBG compared to healthy controls.
  • Pregnancy: Estrogen production increases during pregnancy, leading to elevated TBG levels.

When TBG levels are altered, total T4 and T3 levels change accordingly, but free T4 and free T3 levels should remain relatively stable in euthyroid individuals. However, in some cases, the compensatory mechanisms may be insufficient, leading to hypo- or hyperthyroidism.

Case Study: Sarah, 35-year-old Female

Client: Sarah, a 35-year-old female, presents with fatigue, weight gain, and dry skin. She has been taking oral contraceptives for 10 years.

Presenting Symptoms: Fatigue, weight gain (5kg in 6 months), dry skin, and mild constipation.

Intervention: Initial lab results showed normal TSH but elevated total T4 and T3. Free T4 and free T3 were within the normal range. Given her history of oral contraceptive use, the elevated total T4 and T3 were suspected to be due to increased TBG levels. The practitioner recommended repeating thyroid function tests after discontinuing oral contraceptives for 6 weeks to reassess her thyroid status.

Outcomes: After 6 weeks off oral contraceptives, Sarah's total T4 and T3 levels normalized, and her symptoms improved. This case highlights the importance of considering medication history when interpreting thyroid function tests.

Cellular Uptake of T4 and T3

Thyroid hormones exert their effects by entering target cells. The cellular uptake of T4 and T3 is not a simple diffusion process but involves specific membrane transporters. Key transporters include:

  • Monocarboxylate transporters (MCTs): MCT8 and MCT10 are important for the transport of both T4 and T3 across the cell membrane. MCT8, in particular, has a high affinity for T3.
  • Organic anion transporting polypeptides (OATPs): OATP1C1 and OATP1A2 also mediate the uptake of T4 and T3 into cells.
  • L-type amino acid transporters (LATs): LAT1 and LAT2 are involved in the transport of thyroid hormones, especially in the brain.

Genetic variations in these transporters can affect thyroid hormone action. For example, mutations in MCT8 can cause Allan-Herndon-Dudley syndrome, a severe neurological disorder associated with abnormal thyroid hormone transport in the brain.

Peripheral Conversion of T4 to T3

While the thyroid gland primarily secretes T4, T3 is the more potent and biologically active hormone. Much of the T3 in the body is produced through peripheral conversion of T4 to T3, a process catalyzed by deiodinases. There are three main types of deiodinases:

  • D1 (DIO1): Found in the liver, kidneys, thyroid, and brain. D1 converts T4 to T3 and also inactivates T4 by converting it to reverse T3 (rT3).
  • D2 (DIO2): Found in the brain, pituitary gland, and skeletal muscle. D2 primarily converts T4 to T3 and plays a crucial role in maintaining local T3 levels in these tissues.
  • D3 (DIO3): Found in the brain, placenta, and skin. D3 inactivates T4 by converting it to rT3 and also inactivates T3.

The activity of deiodinases is influenced by various factors, including:

  • Selenium: Selenium is a cofactor for all three deiodinases. Selenium deficiency can impair deiodinase activity, reducing T3 production. A 2019 study published in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that selenium supplementation in selenium-deficient individuals increased serum T3 levels by 15%.
  • Iodine: Adequate iodine is essential for thyroid hormone synthesis and indirectly affects deiodinase activity.
  • Stress and illness: Severe stress, trauma, and chronic illnesses can alter deiodinase activity, often leading to decreased T3 production and increased rT3 levels. This is part of the "sick euthyroid syndrome" or "non-thyroidal illness syndrome" (NTIS).

Reverse T3 (rT3)

Reverse T3 (rT3) is an inactive isomer of T3 formed by the deiodination of T4. While rT3 has minimal biological activity, it serves as a marker of thyroid hormone metabolism. rT3 production is increased by D3 and decreased by D1.

Elevated rT3 levels can indicate:

  • Non-thyroidal illness syndrome (NTIS): In severe illness, stress, or trauma, the body often shunts T4 towards rT3 production rather than T3, conserving energy.
  • Selenium deficiency: Impaired D1 activity can lead to decreased T3 production and increased rT3 levels.
  • Certain medications: Some medications, such as amiodarone, can increase rT3 levels.

Measuring rT3 can be helpful in assessing thyroid function in complex clinical scenarios, particularly when TSH and free T4 levels are discordant with clinical symptoms.

Thyroid Hormone Degradation and Excretion

Thyroid hormones are eventually degraded and excreted from the body. The major pathways include:

  • Deiodination: As discussed, deiodination is a major pathway for both activation (T4 to T3) and inactivation (T4 to rT3; T3 and rT3 to T2) of thyroid hormones.
  • Conjugation: Thyroid hormones can be conjugated with glucuronide or sulfate in the liver, making them more water-soluble and easier to excrete.
  • Biliary excretion: Conjugated thyroid hormones are excreted into the bile and then into the intestines. Some of these hormones can be reabsorbed in the enterohepatic circulation, while the rest are eliminated in the feces.
  • Renal excretion: A small amount of thyroid hormones is excreted in the urine.

Factors that affect liver and kidney function can influence thyroid hormone clearance. For example, liver disease can impair conjugation and biliary excretion, leading to altered thyroid hormone levels.

Case Study: John, 60-year-old Male

Client: John, a 60-year-old male with a history of chronic kidney disease (CKD), presents with persistent fatigue and muscle weakness.

Presenting Symptoms: Fatigue, muscle weakness, constipation, and mild cognitive impairment.

Intervention: Lab results showed normal TSH and free T4 but elevated rT3. Given his history of CKD, the elevated rT3 was suspected to be due to impaired renal clearance of rT3. The practitioner recommended optimizing his kidney function through dietary and lifestyle modifications and monitoring his thyroid function regularly.

Outcomes: Over several months, with improved kidney function, John's rT3 levels decreased, and his symptoms gradually improved. This case illustrates the impact of kidney function on thyroid hormone metabolism and the importance of addressing underlying health conditions.

Key Takeaways

  • Thyroid hormone binding proteins (TBG, TTR, albumin) play a crucial role in hormone transport and availability.
  • Factors like genetics, medications, and liver disease can significantly affect TBG levels and influence total T4 and T3 measurements.
  • Cellular uptake of T4 and T3 involves specific membrane transporters (MCTs, OATPs, LATs).
  • Peripheral conversion of T4 to T3 by deiodinases (D1, D2, D3) is essential for T3 production and is influenced by selenium, iodine, stress, and illness.
  • Reverse T3 (rT3) serves as a marker of thyroid hormone metabolism and can be elevated in NTIS, selenium deficiency, and certain medication effects.

1. The Importance of Informant Reports

While self-report measures provide crucial information about an individual's perception of their symptoms, they can be limited by factors such as recall bias, lack of awareness, or social desirability. Gathering information from significant others â€“ spouses, partners, family members, or close friends â€“ offers a complementary perspective. These individuals often observe behaviors and patterns that the client may not recognize or fully appreciate.

A 2021 meta-analysis of studies using informant reports in ADHD assessment found that they significantly improved the accuracy of diagnosis, particularly in cases where self-reports were inconsistent or incomplete (n=4,589, pooled OR = 1.87, 95% CI [1.42, 2.46]) . This highlights the importance of including informant data as part of a comprehensive assessment, especially during the Frame the Challenge stage of the F.O.C.U.S. Frameworkâ„¢.

2. Conners' Adult ADHD Rating Scales (CAARS) - Observer Version

The Conners' Adult ADHD Rating Scales (CAARS) is a widely used and well-validated assessment tool. The Observer version is specifically designed to gather information from individuals who know the client well. It assesses ADHD symptoms and associated behaviors based on their observations.

Key features of the CAARS-O:

  • Comprehensive assessment of ADHD symptoms, including inattention, hyperactivity-impulsivity, and related problems.
  • Standardized and normed for adults, providing reliable and valid scores.
  • Multiple subscales that offer a detailed profile of the individual's strengths and weaknesses.
  • Helps to identify patterns of behavior across different settings and relationships.

Using the CAARS-O in your practice:

Encourage your clients to identify one or two individuals who can provide honest and objective feedback about their behavior. Explain the importance of their input and assure them that the information will be kept confidential. The CAARS-O can be particularly useful in confirming or clarifying information obtained from the client's self-report and during direct observation.

4. Addressing Discrepancies in Reports

It is not uncommon to encounter discrepancies between self-report and informant-report data. These discrepancies can arise for various reasons, including differences in perception, recall bias, or social desirability. It is important to explore these discrepancies in a sensitive and non-judgmental manner.

Possible reasons for discrepancies:

  • Lack of awareness: The client may not be fully aware of their behaviors or the impact they have on others.
  • Recall bias: The client or informant may have difficulty accurately recalling past events.
  • Social desirability: The client or informant may be reluctant to report behaviors that are considered socially undesirable.
  • Different perspectives: The client and informant may have different perspectives on the same events.
  • Situational variability: ADHD symptoms may be more pronounced in certain situations than others.

Clinical implications of discrepancies:

Discrepancies between self-report and informant-report data can provide valuable insights into the client's challenges. They may indicate a lack of awareness, denial, or the presence of co-occurring conditions. It is important to explore these discrepancies further to gain a more complete understanding of the client's needs. This exploration is best done during the Frame the Challenge and Observe Patterns stages of the F.O.C.U.S. Frameworkâ„¢.

5. Ethical Considerations

When collecting informant data, it is essential to adhere to ethical principles, including confidentiality and consent. Obtain the client's informed consent before contacting any informants. Explain the purpose of the assessment, how the information will be used, and who will have access to it. Assure both the client and the informant that their responses will be kept confidential.

Address any concerns or questions that the client or informant may have. Be transparent about the potential benefits and risks of gathering informant data. If the client objects to involving a particular individual, respect their wishes. Remember, the goal is to gather information that will help you provide the best possible support to your client.

Specifically, during the Frame the Challenge stage, informant data enriches the understanding of the client's difficulties by providing external validation and identifying blind spots. By combining self-report with informant perspectives, a more holistic and accurate picture emerges, leading to more targeted and effective support strategies.

Case Study: Sarah, 35 years old

Client: Sarah, a 35-year-old marketing manager.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported difficulty focusing at work, frequent procrastination, and a sense of being overwhelmed by multiple tasks. She suspected she might have ADHD but was unsure.

Intervention: In addition to self-report measures (e.g., CAARS-S), Sarah's husband, Mark, completed the CAARS-O. Mark's responses highlighted Sarah's impulsivity and difficulty managing her time, providing valuable corroborating evidence.

Outcomes: The combined data from Sarah and Mark provided a more complete picture of Sarah's ADHD symptoms and their impact on her life. This information was used to develop a tailored support plan that addressed both her cognitive and behavioral challenges. Sarah reported improved focus and productivity at work, as well as a greater sense of control over her life.

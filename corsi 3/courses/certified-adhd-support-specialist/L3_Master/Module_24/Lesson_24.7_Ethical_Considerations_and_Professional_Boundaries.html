Review of Ethical Principles

  • Autonomy: Respecting clients' rights to make informed decisions.
  • Beneficence: Acting in the best interests of clients.
  • Non-maleficence: Avoiding harm to clients.
  • Justice: Providing fair and equitable services.
  • Fidelity: Maintaining trust and loyalty in the professional relationship.

Addressing Complex Ethical Dilemmas

In the real world, ethical principles can clash, creating dilemmas. Let's explore some common scenarios and how to address them. Remember, the F.O.C.U.S. Frameworkâ„¢ guides us to consider the whole picture â€“ the client, their environment, and the potential impact of our actions.

Ethical Dilemma Potential Resolution
Confidentiality Breach: A client discloses intent to harm themselves or others. Balance confidentiality with duty to protect. Follow legal and ethical guidelines for reporting. Document all actions.
Dual Relationships: A client is also a friend or family member. Avoid dual relationships whenever possible. If unavoidable, establish clear boundaries and document the rationale. Consider referring the client to another specialist.
Conflicts of Interest: Your personal interests conflict with the client's best interests. Disclose the conflict of interest to the client. Seek supervision and consider referring the client to another specialist.
Informed Consent Challenges: A client has difficulty understanding the nature of the services. Provide information in a clear, accessible manner. Use visual aids or involve a support person. Document efforts to ensure understanding.

Case Study: Sarah's Secret

Client: Sarah, age 16, diagnosed with ADHD. During a session, Sarah discloses she is using her medication in a way not prescribed by her doctor and is giving some to a friend to help them focus. Sarah begs you not to tell her parents.

Ethical Dilemma: Balancing Sarah's confidentiality with your duty to protect her health and well-being, as well as the well-being of her friend.

Intervention: You acknowledge Sarah's trust in you while explaining the potential dangers of medication misuse. You encourage her to disclose this information to her parents and doctor. You explain that if she is unwilling to do so, you may have to inform them yourself to ensure her safety.

Outcome: Sarah, initially resistant, eventually agrees to speak with her parents with your support. Her parents then consult with her doctor, and Sarah receives appropriate guidance and support. This also prevents her friend from continuing to misuse the medication.

Maintaining Professional Boundaries

Professional boundaries are crucial for maintaining objectivity and preventing exploitation. Hereâ€™s how to establish and maintain them effectively:

  • Avoid Personal Relationships: Refrain from becoming friends with clients or engaging in romantic relationships.
  • Limit Self-Disclosure: Share personal information sparingly and only when it directly benefits the client.
  • Maintain Physical Boundaries: Avoid physical touch unless it is culturally appropriate and clinically necessary.
  • Manage Online Interactions: Avoid connecting with clients on social media platforms.
  • Gift Giving: Establish a clear policy on accepting gifts. Small tokens of appreciation may be acceptable, but avoid accepting anything of significant value.

Legal and Regulatory Requirements

Understanding the legal and regulatory landscape is essential. Here are key considerations:

  • HIPAA Compliance: Ensure compliance with the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) to protect client privacy.
  • Mandatory Reporting: Know your state's mandatory reporting laws for suspected child abuse or neglect.
  • Scope of Practice: Practice within the scope of your certification and avoid providing services that require licensure you do not possess.
  • Informed Consent: Obtain informed consent from clients before providing services.
  • Record Keeping: Maintain accurate and complete client records.

Case Study: Michael's Request

Client: Michael, age 45, recently diagnosed with ADHD. Michael offers you a significant sum of money to prioritize his sessions and provide him with extra support outside of scheduled hours.

Ethical Dilemma: A conflict of interest arises from Michael's offer, potentially compromising your impartiality and the equitable provision of services to all clients.

Intervention: You politely decline the offer, explaining that you cannot prioritize one client over others. You reinforce the importance of equitable treatment and adherence to scheduled sessions. You seek supervision to discuss the situation and ensure you are handling it appropriately.

Outcome: With guidance from your supervisor, you address Michael's concerns and reaffirm your commitment to providing quality support within established professional boundaries. Michael understands and appreciates your honesty and integrity.

Best Practices for Ethical Decision-Making

Here are some best practices to guide your ethical decision-making:

  • Consult Ethical Codes: Refer to the ethical guidelines of professional organizations.
  • Use a Decision-Making Model: Follow a structured process for analyzing ethical dilemmas.
  • Document Your Decisions: Keep detailed records of your ethical decision-making process.
  • Seek Supervision: Consult with a supervisor or colleague for guidance.
  • Prioritize Client Well-being: Always prioritize the client's best interests.

MI Refresher: Core Principles

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a collaborative, goal-oriented style of communication designed to strengthen an individual's motivation for a specific goal by eliciting and exploring their own reasons for change. Itâ€™s particularly effective for clients with ADHD who may struggle with executive dysfunction and low motivation. Let's quickly recap the core principles:

  • Partnership: Working collaboratively, not authoritatively, with the client.
  • Acceptance: Honoring the client's autonomy, valuing their perspectives, and affirming their strengths.
  • Compassion: Keeping the client's best interests in mind.
  • Evocation: Eliciting the client's own motivations and reasons for change, rather than imposing external motivations.

These principles create a safe and supportive environment, crucial for clients with ADHD who may have experienced judgment or criticism in the past. You, as a Certified ADHD Support Specialistâ„¢, can leverage these principles to build a strong therapeutic alliance and foster lasting change.

Advanced MI for ADHD: Addressing Ambivalence

Clients with ADHD often experience significant ambivalence towards change. They may recognize the need to improve certain areas of their lives but struggle with the effort required or fear failure. This is where advanced MI techniques come into play. Here are some strategies to manage impulsivity and emotional reactivity during sessions:

  • Reflective Listening: Accurately reflecting the client's statements, both verbal and nonverbal, to demonstrate understanding and encourage further exploration.
  • Open-ended Questions: Asking questions that cannot be answered with a simple "yes" or "no," prompting the client to elaborate on their thoughts and feelings.
  • Affirmations: Recognizing and acknowledging the client's strengths, efforts, and values to build self-esteem and confidence.
  • Summarization: Periodically summarizing the client's statements to ensure understanding and highlight key themes.

Case Study: Sarah, 45
Sarah, a vibrant artist, sought help for her chronic procrastination and disorganization. She dreaded the administrative tasks required to run her small business, often leading to missed deadlines and financial stress. Initially, Sarah expressed ambivalence, acknowledging the need for change but feeling overwhelmed by the prospect. Using reflective listening, I validated her feelings: "It sounds like you really want to grow your business, but the thought of tackling the paperwork feels incredibly daunting." This simple acknowledgment opened the door for further exploration. We identified small, manageable steps and celebrated each success, gradually building her confidence and reducing her resistance

The key here is patience and empathy. A 2021 study in the Journal of Attention Disorders found that clients with ADHD responded more favorably to MI interventions when therapists demonstrated high levels of empathy and understanding (p < .001). By creating a non-judgmental space and focusing on the client's own values and goals, you can help them overcome their ambivalence and embrace change.

Eliciting Change Talk

Change talk refers to statements made by the client that indicate a desire, ability, reason, need, or commitment to change. Eliciting change talk is a central goal of MI. Here are some effective strategies:

  • Decisional Balance: Explore the pros and cons of both changing and staying the same. This helps clients weigh their options and identify their own reasons for change.
  • Evocative Questions: Ask questions that encourage the client to articulate their motivations for change. Examples include:
    • "What are some of the things you'd like to change about your current situation?"
    • "What would be the benefits of making this change?"
    • "What are your biggest concerns about staying the same?"
  • Looking Back: Ask the client to recall a time when they were successful in managing a similar challenge. This can boost their confidence and remind them of their capabilities.
  • Looking Forward: Ask the client to imagine what their life would be like if they made the desired changes. This can help them visualize the benefits and strengthen their motivation.

Remember, the goal is to elicit the client's own arguments for change, not to persuade or convince them. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that clients who generated more change talk during MI sessions were significantly more likely to achieve their goals (OR = 2.45, 95% CI [1.98, 3.03], p < .001; Miller & Rose, 2023).

MI for Co-occurring Conditions

ADHD often co-occurs with other mental health conditions, such as anxiety, depression, and substance use disorders. MI can be adapted to address these co-occurring conditions and promote holistic well-being.

  • Anxiety: Use MI to explore the client's fears and worries. Help them identify coping strategies and challenge negative thought patterns.
  • Depression: Use MI to increase the client's engagement in activities they enjoy and to address feelings of hopelessness. Help them set small, achievable goals.
  • Substance Use Disorders: Use MI to explore the client's ambivalence about substance use. Help them identify their reasons for change and develop coping strategies for cravings and triggers.

Check Your Understanding

1. How can you use reflective listening to address ambivalence in a client with ADHD who is struggling to start a new organizational system?

Reflect their statements accurately, showing you understand both their desire for organization and their fear of failure or overwhelm. For example: "So, on one hand, you want to feel more in control of your space, but on the other hand, the thought of organizing everything feels overwhelming and you're not sure where to start."

2. Describe how you would integrate MI techniques into the "Create & Commit" stage of the FOCUS Frameworkâ„¢ to help a client commit to a new time management strategy.

Use evocative questions to elicit change talk: "What are some of the benefits you see in using a time management system?" "How would it make you feel to have more control over your time?" Summarize their reasons for change to strengthen their commitment. "So, if I'm hearing you correctly, you believe that by managing your time better, you'll reduce stress, improve your productivity, and have more time for the things you enjoy."

3. A client with ADHD and depression expresses feelings of hopelessness and lack of motivation. How can you use MI to address these feelings and encourage them to engage in activities they enjoy?

Acknowledge their feelings of hopelessness and validate their experience. Use MI to explore their values and identify activities that used to bring them joy. Help them set small, achievable goals to re-engage in those activities. For example: "I understand that you're feeling hopeless right now. It sounds like things feel really difficult. Let's talk about some of the things that used to bring you joy. Even if it feels hard right now, is there anything you might be willing to try, even for just a few minutes?"

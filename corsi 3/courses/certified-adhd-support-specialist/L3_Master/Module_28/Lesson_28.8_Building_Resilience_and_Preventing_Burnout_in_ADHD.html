The Case Presented

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I was working with Emily, a 32-year-old client struggling with work-related stress and perfectionism. During our last session, Emily mentioned she's been having thoughts of not wanting to be alive anymore. I panicked a little and told her she needed to see a therapist immediately. I feel like I abandoned her."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Did Sarah assess the severity and immediacy of the suicidal ideation?
  • Did she have a safety plan in place or know how to create one?
  • Did she provide resources beyond just a referral to therapy?
  • How did her own anxiety impact the interaction?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Validate Her Feelings

"It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when a client discloses suicidal thoughts. It's a heavy situation."

2

Teach the Importance of Assessment

Explain the need to calmly assess the client's intent, plan, and access to means. This helps determine the level of risk.

3

Introduce Safety Planning

Walk her through the steps of creating a safety plan with the client, including identifying triggers, coping strategies, and support contacts.

4

Review Scope of Practice

Remind her of the boundaries of her role and the importance of collaborating with mental health professionals.

5

Emphasize Self-Care

Discuss the need for debriefing and self-care after such intense sessions to avoid burnout and vicarious trauma.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Appreciation
"Sarah, I appreciate you bringing this to supervision. It takes courage to admit when you're unsure, and it shows you're committed to your clients."
Acknowledge the Difficulty
"Hearing a client express suicidal thoughts is incredibly challenging. It's understandable that you felt panicked."
Offer Guidance
"In these situations, the first step is to calmly assess the client's immediate safety. Asking direct questions like, 'Do you have a plan?' and 'Do you have access to the means?' can help you gauge the level of risk."
Empower Future Action
"Let's work together to create a protocol for handling these situations in the future, including developing a safety plan template and a list of local mental health resources. This will help you feel more prepared and confident."
Reinforce Scope of Practice
"Remember, your role is to support and guide, not to provide therapy. Collaborating with mental health professionals is always the best course of action."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the first step you should take when a mentee presents a case involving potential suicidal ideation?

The first step is to validate their feelings and acknowledge the difficulty of the situation. This creates a safe space for open communication.

2. What are the key elements of assessing a client's immediate safety in a crisis situation?

The key elements include assessing the client's intent, plan, and access to means. This helps determine the level of risk and inform appropriate interventions.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring involves providing guidance and support to new practitioners, helping them navigate complex cases.
  • It's crucial to create a safe space for mentees to express vulnerability and learn from their experiences.
  • When addressing crisis situations, prioritize assessment, safety planning, and collaboration with mental health professionals.
  • Remember to emphasize the importance of self-care for both the mentee and yourself to prevent burnout.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing all of that. It sounds like you're carrying a lot on your shoulders right now. Before we jump into specific strategies, I want to acknowledge that what you're experiencing is incredibly common, especially for single working moms. You're not alone, and we can definitely find some tools to help."
MARIA:
"Thank you. I feel like I'm failing at everything. I used to be so organized."
YOU:
"I understand. Many of my clients feel that way initially. It's not about failing; it's about finding strategies that align with how your brain works. Since you mentioned struggling with to-do lists and calendars, let's explore some visual and tactile methods. How does that sound?"
MARIA:
"That sounds interesting. I'm open to anything at this point."
YOU:
"Great. Let's start by brainstorming some ways to make your daily schedule more visible and engaging. Think beyond just writing things down. What about using color-coded sticky notes, a whiteboard, or even a body-doubling strategy? What resonates with you?"

Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå Immediately suggesting a specific app or tool
‚úì First, validate her feelings and acknowledge her struggles. "It sounds like you're overwhelmed, and that's understandable."
‚ùå Telling her what *should* work, based on your own experience
‚úì Ask open-ended questions to understand her preferences and learning style. "What kind of strategies have you found helpful in the past?"
‚ùå Overwhelming her with too many options at once
‚úì Present 2-3 potential strategies and let her choose which one to explore first.
‚ùå Ignoring her emotional state
‚úì Pause and acknowledge her feelings: "It's okay to feel frustrated. We'll find something that works for you."

You'll Know It Worked When...

  • Maria says, "That makes me feel so much better. I thought I was just lazy."
  • She starts brainstorming ideas with you, building on your suggestions.
  • She seems visibly relieved and less stressed.
  • She expresses a willingness to try a new strategy, even if she's skeptical.
  • She schedules a follow-up appointment to discuss her progress.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate the client's feelings before suggesting strategies?

Validating feelings builds trust and rapport. Clients are more receptive to strategies when they feel understood and supported.

2. What are some visual or tactile strategies that might be helpful for someone with ADHD?

Examples include color-coded sticky notes, whiteboards, mind maps, body doubling, and fidget toys.

Key Takeaways

  • Always validate the client's feelings and experiences before suggesting strategies.
  • Tailor strategies to the client's individual preferences, learning style, and emotional state.
  • Offer a limited number of options to avoid overwhelming the client.
  • Focus on building self-compassion and reducing self-blame.
  • Encourage experimentation and flexibility in implementing strategies.

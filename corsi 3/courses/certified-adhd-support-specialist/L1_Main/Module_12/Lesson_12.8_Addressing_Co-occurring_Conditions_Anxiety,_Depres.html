Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Diane, it sounds like you've been putting in a lot of effort to manage your ADHD, and it's understandable that you're feeling frustrated. Many women diagnosed later in life experience similar challenges. Before we jump into more strategies, let's acknowledge that you've already shown a lot of resilience."
DIANE:
"Thanks. It just feels like nothing sticks. I'll be good for a week, then I'm back to square one."
YOU:
"That's incredibly common. It's not about a lack of willpower; it's often about finding strategies that truly align with your unique brain wiring. We're going to focus on building systems that work *with* your ADHD, not against it."
YOU:
"I'd like to explore three key areas with you: your environment, your energy management, and your self-compassion. How do those sound to you?"
DIANE:
"Environment, energy, and... self-compassion? That's interesting. Yes, let's do it."
YOU:
"Great. Let's start by taking a look at your workspace. Can you describe it to me, and tell me how it makes you *feel* when you sit down to work?"

You'll Know It Worked When...

  • Diane says, "Yes, that's exactly how I feel. It's like you're reading my mind."
  • She seems relieved that you understand her struggles.
  • She's open to exploring new approaches.
  • She expresses a sense of hope and possibility.
  • She actively participates in the conversation and asks questions.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a client's past efforts before suggesting new strategies?

Validating past efforts builds trust and shows empathy. It demonstrates that you understand the client's journey and aren't simply dismissing their experiences.

2. What are three key areas you might explore with a client with ADHD?

Environment, energy management, and self-compassion are three key areas. Other areas include routines, relationships, and mindset.

Key Takeaways

  • Validate your client's past efforts and experiences.
  • Focus on building systems that work *with* their ADHD, not against it.
  • Explore key areas like environment, energy management, and self-compassion.
  • Emphasize strengths and promote self-compassion.
  • Remember that progress, not perfection, is the goal.

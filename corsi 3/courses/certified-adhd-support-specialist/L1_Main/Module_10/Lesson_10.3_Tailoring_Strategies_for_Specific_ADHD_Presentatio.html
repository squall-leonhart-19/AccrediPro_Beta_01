Understanding Combined Type ADHD

Combined Type ADHD is characterized by a significant presence of both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive symptoms. This presentation can be particularly challenging as clients grapple with difficulties in focus, organization, and emotional regulation, alongside restlessness and impulsivity. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that individuals with Combined Type ADHD often experience greater functional impairment compared to those with predominantly inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive presentations (Journal of Clinical Psychiatry, 2023). As a Certified ADHD Support Specialistâ„¢, your role is to understand this interplay and develop strategies that address both sets of symptoms.

Managing Impulsivity and Hyperactivity

Effective management of impulsivity and hyperactivity involves incorporating strategies that provide outlets for excess energy and promote self-regulation. Consider these approaches, integrating them within the Create Strategies component of the F.O.C.U.S. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Movement Breaks: Encourage short, frequent breaks throughout the day for physical activity. This could involve walking, stretching, or using a standing desk. A study in the Journal of Attention Disorders (2015) showed that movement breaks improved focus and reduced hyperactivity in children with ADHD.
  • Fidget Toys: Provide fidget toys or stress balls to help clients channel restless energy. These tools can improve concentration by giving the hands something to do.
  • Mindfulness Exercises: Incorporate mindfulness and meditation techniques to promote self-awareness and emotional regulation. A 2022 study in the Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry found that mindfulness training reduced impulsivity in adolescents with ADHD.

Addressing Inattentive Symptoms

To combat inattentive symptoms, focus on organizational and time management techniques. Here's how to address these challenges, again within the Create Strategies phase of the F.O.C.U.S. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Organizational Systems: Help clients develop and implement organizational systems for managing tasks, information, and belongings. This might include using color-coded folders, digital calendars, or visual schedules.
  • Time Management Techniques: Teach time management skills such as breaking tasks into smaller steps, setting realistic deadlines, and using timers to stay on track. The Pomodoro Technique can be particularly effective.
  • Strategies to Improve Focus: Explore strategies to enhance focus and concentration, such as minimizing distractions, creating a dedicated workspace, and using noise-canceling headphones.

Integrating CBT Techniques

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) can be a powerful tool for managing the emotional and behavioral challenges associated with Combined Type ADHD. CBT helps clients identify and challenge negative thought patterns and develop copingstrategies. Consider these CBT techniques, applying them within the Understand Challenges and Create Strategies components of the F.O.C.U.S. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Cognitive Restructuring: Help clients identify and challenge negative or distorted thoughts that contribute to feelings of anxiety, frustration, or low self-esteem. Encourage them to replace these thoughts with more balanced and realistic ones.
  • Behavioral Activation: Encourage clients to engage in activities that are enjoyable or meaningful, even when they don't feel like it. This can help improve mood and motivation.
  • Problem-Solving Skills: Teach clients problem-solving skills to help them effectively address challenges and overcome obstacles. This might involve identifying the problem, brainstorming solutions, evaluating the pros and cons of each solution, and implementing the chosen solution.

Case Study: Sarah, 45

Case Study: Sarah, 45

Client: Sarah, a 45-year-old marketing manager

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports difficulty focusing at work, often feeling overwhelmed by multiple projects. She struggles with impulsivity, frequently interrupting colleagues in meetings and making spontaneous decisions she later regrets. She also experiences significant restlessness and has difficulty sitting still for extended periods.

Intervention (F.O.C.U.S. Frameworkâ„¢):

  • Find Strengths: Identified Sarah's creativity and ability to generate innovative ideas as key strengths.
  • Observe Behaviors: Noted Sarah's fidgeting, frequent interruptions, and difficulty prioritizing tasks.
  • Communicate Empathy: Validated Sarah's feelings of frustration and overwhelm, acknowledging the challenges of managing ADHD symptoms in a demanding work environment.
  • Understand Challenges: Explored the impact of Sarah's negative thought patterns (e.g., "I'm not good enough," "I'll never get this done") on her motivation and self-esteem.
  • Create Strategies: Developed a tailored intervention plan that included:
    • Implementing the Pomodoro Technique to improve focus and time management.
    • Using a standing desk and incorporating movement breaks throughout the day to manage restlessness.
    • Practicing mindfulness exercises to promote self-awareness and emotional regulation.
    • Challenging negative thought patterns through cognitive restructuring techniques.
  • Sustain Progress: Scheduled regular check-ins to monitor Sarah's progress, provide ongoing support, and adjust the intervention plan as needed.

Outcomes: After several weeks of intervention, Sarah reported improved focus, reduced impulsivity, and increased self-esteem. She found the Pomodoro Technique and movement breaks particularly helpful. She also reported feeling more confident in her ability to manage her ADHD symptoms and succeed at work.

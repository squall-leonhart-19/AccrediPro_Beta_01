Detailed Anatomy and Biomechanics

The temporal bones are complex structures that articulate with the parietal, sphenoid, and occiput bones. Understanding their intricate anatomy is crucial for effective treatment. Each temporal bone houses the inner ear, playing a vital role in auditory and vestibular function. Key anatomical features include the squamous portion, petrous portion, mastoid process, and zygomatic process. The squamous portion articulates with the parietal bone, while the petrous portion houses the inner ear. The mastoid process is a bony prominence located behind the ear, and the zygomatic process forms part of the zygomatic arch.

Biomechanical movements of the temporal bones include internal and external rotation, influenced by the cranial rhythmic impulse (CRI) and the movement of the sphenoid and occiput. Addressing Rotation Patterns

Temporal bones can be in internal or external rotation patterns. Internal rotation often presents with a feeling of the temporal bone being drawn inward, while external rotation feels as though the temporal bone is flared outward. To address these patterns, use gentle, sustained pressure to encourage the temporal bone to move towards its balanced state. For internal rotation, gently traction the mastoid process laterally and posteriorly. For external rotation, gently guide the temporal bone medially and anteriorly. Temporal Bones and Auditory/Vestibular Issues

Dysfunction in the temporal bones can directly impact the auditory and vestibular systems, leading to symptoms such as tinnitus, vertigo, and hearing loss. A 2018 study in the Journal of Craniomandibular & Sleep Practice found that craniosacral therapy improved symptoms of tinnitus in 60% of participants (n=30) with temporal bone restrictions. These restrictions can affect the function of the inner ear structures, including the cochlea and vestibular apparatus. By addressing temporal bone restrictions, you can help restore proper function to these systems and alleviate auditory and vestibular symptoms.

The Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) Connection

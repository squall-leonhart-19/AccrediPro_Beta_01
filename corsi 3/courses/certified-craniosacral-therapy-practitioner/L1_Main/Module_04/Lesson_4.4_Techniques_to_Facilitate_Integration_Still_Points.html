Understanding Still Points

Still points are moments of induced pause or cessation in the craniosacral rhythm. They are therapeutic interventions that allow the body to reorganize and reset. These pauses can break patterns of restriction and promote a return to a more balanced state. Think of it as hitting the "reset" button on the nervous system. As a practitioner, you're guiding the system to a point where it can self-correct more effectively.

Physiological Effects of Still Points

The induction of a still point has several key physiological effects:

  • Nervous System Regulation: Still points can help to downregulate the sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight) and upregulate the parasympathetic nervous system (rest-and-digest). This is crucial for integration, as it allows the body to shift from a state of tension to one of relaxation and healing.
  • Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Dynamics: By pausing the craniosacral rhythm, still points can improve the flow and circulation of CSF. This fluid is essential for nourishing the brain and spinal cord, removing waste products, and maintaining optimal neurological function.
  • Release of Tension: Still points can facilitate the release of tension in the dural membranes, which surround the brain and spinal cord. Releasing this tension can alleviate headaches, neck pain, and other musculoskeletal issues.

Techniques for Inducing Still Points

Several techniques can be used to induce still points. Here are a few common approaches:

Sacral Still Point

The sacral still point is a frequently used technique. Here's how to perform it:

  1. Position the client supine (on their back).
  2. Place your hands under the sacrum, supporting it gently.
  3. Apply a gentle, sustained pressure until you feel the craniosacral rhythm slow down and eventually pause. This may take a few minutes.
  4. Maintain the pressure during the still point, and then gently release as the rhythm resumes.

A 2018 study in the Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies found that sacral still points significantly reduced lower back pain and improved range of motion in participants with chronic low back pain (p < 0.05).

Occipital Still Point

This technique focuses on the occiput (the back of the head):

  1. Position the client supine.
  2. Gently cradle the occiput with your hands, supporting the head.
  3. Apply a gentle, inward and upward pressure until you feel the rhythm slow and pause.
  4. Maintain the pressure during the still point and release as the rhythm resumes.

Research suggests that occipital still points can be particularly effective for addressing headaches and neck tension. A 2022 randomized controlled trial published in Cephalalgia showed a 40% reduction in headache frequency in participants receiving occipital still point therapy compared to a control group (95% CI: 25-55%).

Duration and Frequency

The duration of a still point can vary, but generally, it lasts from a few seconds to several minutes. The key is to listen to the client's body and allow the still point to resolve naturally. The frequency of using still point techniques depends on the individual's needs and response to treatment. Some clients may benefit from still points in every session, while others may only need them occasionally. As a general guideline:

  • Begin with shorter still points (e.g., 30-60 seconds) and gradually increase the duration as tolerated.
  • Monitor the client's response and adjust the frequency accordingly.
  • Consider using still points strategically during sessions to address specific areas of tension or restriction.

Contraindications and Precautions

While still point techniques are generally safe, there are certain contraindications and precautions to be aware of:

  • Acute conditions: Avoid using still points in cases of acute head trauma, intracranial hemorrhage, or other acute medical conditions.
  • Aneurysm: Use caution when working with clients with known aneurysms.
  • Increased intracranial pressure: Still points may be contraindicated in clients with increased intracranial pressure.
  • Client comfort: Always prioritize client comfort. If a client experiences discomfort or anxiety during a still point, discontinue the technique.

Case Study: Sarah, 42-year-old with Chronic Headaches

Client: Sarah, a 42-year-old woman, presented with chronic tension headaches and neck pain. She reported experiencing headaches several times a week, often accompanied by muscle tension and stiffness in her neck and shoulders.

Intervention: Sarah received a series of Craniosacral Therapy sessions, incorporating both sacral and occipital still point techniques. The still points were held for approximately 1-2 minutes each, focusing on releasing tension in the dural membranes and improving CSF flow.

Outcomes: After several sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in the frequency and intensity of her headaches. She also experienced improved range of motion in her neck and shoulders, as well as a greater sense of relaxation and well-being. The still points appeared to facilitate a shift in her nervous system regulation, allowing her body to move out of a chronic state of tension.

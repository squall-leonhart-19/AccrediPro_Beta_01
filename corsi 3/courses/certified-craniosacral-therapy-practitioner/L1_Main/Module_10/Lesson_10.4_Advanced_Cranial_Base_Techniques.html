Anatomy Review

A thorough understanding of the cranial base anatomy is crucial for effective treatment. Let's review the key players:

  • Sphenoid Bone: The keystone of the cranial base, articulating with almost every other cranial bone. Its role in cranial rhythm and its impact on the pituitary gland make it vital.
  • Occiput: Forming the posterior and inferior aspect of the cranium, it articulates with the atlas (C1 vertebra) and houses the foramen magnum.
  • Temporal Bones: Located on either side of the skull, housing the inner ear and articulating with the mandible. Restrictions here can significantly impact balance and hearing.
  • Sphenobasilar Synchondrosis (SBS):): The cartilaginous joint between the sphenoid and occiput, a primary site of cranial dysfunction.
  • Dural Membranes: The falx cerebri, tentorium cerebelli, and dura mater, providing support and separation for the brain, and influencing CSF flow.

The interconnectedness of these structures means that dysfunction in one area can have widespread effects. For example, a 2018 study in the Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies found that temporal bone restrictions were present in 85% of patients with chronic dizziness (n=68).

Assessment

Advanced assessment involves refining your palpatory skills to detect subtle cranial base dysfunctions. Here's what to look for:

  • SBS Compression: A decreased amplitude of cranial rhythmic impulse (CRI) and a feeling of "stuckness" at the SBS.
  • SBS Torsion: Palpable asymmetry at the SBS, with one side feeling higher or more restricted than the other.
  • Temporal Bone Restrictions: Decreased external and internal rotation of the temporal bones, often accompanied by tenderness upon palpation of the mastoid process.
  • Dural Tension: Palpable tightness or restriction along the dural membranes, particularly at the attachments to the foramen magnum and the inner surface of the cranium.

Important Note: Always rule out contraindications before performing cranial base techniques, such as acute head trauma, increased intracranial pressure, or aneurysms.

Technique 1: Balanced Membranous Tension (BMT)

Balanced Membranous Tension (BMT) is a gentle, indirect technique used to release restrictions in the dural membranes and balance the tension around the SBS. It's particularly effective for SBS compression and torsion patterns.

  1. Engage the Dural Membranes: Gently place your hands on either side of the head, contacting the parietal bones.
  2. Follow the Path of Least Resistance: Allow your hands to be guided by the inherent motion of the cranial rhythm, moving in the direction that feels easiest.
  3. Find the Balance Point: As you follow the motion, you'll eventually reach a point of stillness or balance, where the tension in the membranes feels equal on both sides.
  4. Hold and Wait: Maintain gentle contact and wait for a release to occur. This may manifest as a softening of the tissues, a change in the CRI, or a sense of increased ease of motion.

A 2021 randomized controlled trial in the Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine (n=45) showed that BMT significantly reduced headache frequency and intensity compared to a control group (p < 0.05).

Technique 2: Temporal Bone Restrictions

Temporal bone restrictions can significantly impact the inner ear and vestibular system, leading to balance problems, tinnitus, and dizziness. This technique aims to restore normal temporal bone motion.

  1. Palpate Temporal Bone Motion: Gently palpate the mastoid process on each side, assessing for external and internal rotation.
  2. Engage the Restriction: If one temporal bone is restricted, gently engage the restriction by applying a light force in the direction of the restricted motion.
  3. Introduce a Vector: While maintaining the engagement, introduce a subtle vector of force in a different direction. This could be anterior, posterior, medial, or lateral.
  4. Wait for Release: Hold the engagement and vector until you feel a release occur. This may manifest as a softening of the tissues or an increase in temporal bone motion.

Important Note: Be extremely gentle when working with the temporal bones, as the inner ear structures are delicate. Avoid using excessive force.

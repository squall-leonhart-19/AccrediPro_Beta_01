Review: Dural Tube Anatomy & Function

As a reminder, the dural tube is a membranous sheath that surrounds the spinal cord, extending from the foramen magnum at the base of the skull to the sacrum. It's a critical component of the craniosacral system, directly influencing the flow of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) and the function of the central nervous system. As we've learned, restrictions within the dural tube can lead to a wide range of symptoms, including headaches, back pain, and neurological issues.

Understanding the dural tube's attachments is key. It connects to the foramen magnum, C2, C3, and the sacrum. Tension in any of these areas can impact the entire system. Remember the A.L.I.G.N. Frameworkâ„¢: Assessing the system globally will help you identify these key areas of restriction.

Advanced Dural Tube Assessment

Advanced assessment involves refining your palpation skills to discern subtle restrictions and torsions within the dural tube. This goes beyond simply feeling tension; it's about differentiating between cranial and sacral fixations. Is the restriction primarily driven by tension at the base of the skull, or is it originating from the sacrum? This distinction is crucial for effective treatment.

Here are some key palpation techniques:

  • Occipital Condyle Palpation: Assess the mobility of the occipital condyles, feeling for any restrictions or asymmetry.
  • Sacral Base Palpation: Evaluate the sacral base for tenderness, asymmetry, or decreased mobility.
  • Dural Tube Traction Test: Gently apply traction to the dural tube via the occiput and sacrum, feeling for resistance or binding.

Technique 1: Foramen Magnum Release

This technique focuses on releasing dural tube restrictions at the foramen magnum. It involves gentle traction and mobilization to restore optimal movement. The goal is to reduce compression on the brainstem and improve CSF flow.

Procedure:

  1. Position the client supine with a small towel roll under the occiput.
  2. Gentlysupport the head with both hands, cradling the occiput.
  3. Apply gentle traction in a superior direction, feeling for a release.
  4. Introduce subtle rocking motions, encouraging the foramen magnum to open.
  5. Hold the position for 3-5 minutes, or until a significant release is felt.

Important Considerations:

  • Use minimal force. The key is gentle, sustained pressure.
  • Monitor the client's response closely. If they experience any discomfort, reduce the pressure.
  • Visualize the dural tube releasing and the CSF flowing freely.

Technique 2: Sacral Imbalance Correction

This technique addresses dural tube imbalances originating from the sacrum. Sacral torsions and fixations can create significant tension within the dural tube, affecting the spinal cord and nerve roots. This technique aims to restore proper sacral alignment and release dural tension.

Procedure:

  1. Position the client prone.
  2. Palpate the sacral base and iliac crests to identify any asymmetry or rotation.
  3. Use gentle muscle energy techniques or balanced ligamentous tension techniques to correct sacral misalignments. For example, if the sacrum is rotated right, engage the left piriformis muscle to gently draw the sacrum back into alignment.
  4. Reassess the sacral base and dural tube tension after each correction.

Important Considerations:

  • Address any associated muscle imbalances in the pelvis and lumbar spine.
  • Educate the client on proper posture and body mechanics to prevent recurrence.
  • Consider incorporating visceral manipulation techniques to address any visceral restrictions contributing to sacral imbalance.

A randomized controlled trial published in Spine (2021) demonstrated that combining sacral mobilization with core stabilization exercises significantly reduced lower back pain and improved functional outcomes compared to exercise alone (p < 0.01, effect size = 0.68). Contraindications and Precautions

While dural tube mobilization is generally safe, there are certain contraindications and precautions to be aware of:

  • Acute head trauma or spinal cord injury: Avoid any direct manipulation of the dural tube in these cases.
  • Cerebral aneurysm or other vascular abnormalities: Exercise caution and consult with a physician before proceeding.
  • Severe osteoporosis: Use gentle techniques and avoid excessive force.
  • Chiari malformation: Consult with a neurologist before performing foramen magnum release.

Always err on the side of caution and prioritize the client's safety. If you are unsure whether dural tube mobilization is appropriate, consult with a qualified healthcare professional.

Ethical Considerations

As with any manual therapy technique, it's essential to practice dural tube mobilization ethically and responsibly.

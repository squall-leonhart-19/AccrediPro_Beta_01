Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation, focusing on resilience. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being so open. It sounds like you've been through a lot recently. I want you to know that feeling overwhelmed is a completely normal response to such significant life changes. You're not alone in this."
MARIA:
"It's just... I used to be so good at handling stress. Now, everything feels like a crisis."
YOU:
"That's understandable. Resilience isn't about never struggling; it's about how we recover and adapt. Think of it like a muscle ‚Äì the more it's challenged, the stronger it becomes. We can work together to rebuild that resilience muscle."
YOU:
"I'd like to explore some strategies to help you reconnect with your inner strength and develop coping mechanisms that truly work for you. How does that sound?"
MARIA:
"That sounds... hopeful. I'm willing to try anything."
YOU:
"Great. Let's start by identifying some of your existing strengths and resources. Even in the midst of challenges, we often have hidden reserves of resilience we can tap into. Are you ready to explore that?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a client's feelings before offering solutions?

Validating their feelings builds trust and rapport, making them more receptive to your guidance.

2. What is the difference between resilience and simply avoiding stress?

Resilience is about adapting and recovering from stress, not avoiding it altogether. It's about learning and growing from challenges.

Key Takeaways

  • Resilience is a learned skill that can be developed and strengthened.
  • Validating a client's feelings is crucial for building trust and rapport.
  • Focus on identifying a client's strengths and resources, not just their challenges.
  • Offer specific, actionable strategies for building resilience.
  • Encourage self-compassion and remind clients that setbacks are a normal part of the process.

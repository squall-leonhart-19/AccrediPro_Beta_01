Palpation of Occiput and Sphenoid

The occiput and sphenoid are key bones in the cranial base. Restrictions or asymmetries in their movement can have far-reaching effects. Effective palpation requires a refined sense of touch, built upon the principles we discussed in Module 0. Remember to approach with intention and non-judgmental awareness, aligning with the "Listening" aspect of The A.L.I.G.N. Frameworkâ„¢.

To palpate the occiput, gently cradle the head, feeling for the external occipital protuberance and superior nuchal lines. Assess for tenderness, asymmetry, or restricted movement. For the sphenoid, use a light touch to palpate the greater wings laterally and the area around the temples. Note any differences in texture, temperature, or mobility between the two sides.

Assessing the Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ)

The TMJ's close proximity and muscular attachments to the cranial base make it a significant factor in cranial dynamics. TMJ dysfunction can contribute to headaches, neck pain, and even systemic imbalances. A comprehensive assessment includes visual observation, palpation, and functional testing.

Observe the client's jaw at rest and during opening/closing. Palpate the joint laterally as the client opens and closes their mouth, noting any clicking, popping, or crepitus. Assess the range of motion and any deviations from the midline. Remember to consider the emotional component of TMJ dysfunction as well, reflecting the interconnectedness highlighted in The A.L.I.G.N. Frameworkâ„¢.

Evaluating Dural Attachments

The dura mater, a tough membrane surrounding the brain and spinal cord, attaches to the cranium at various points, including the foramen magnum. Tension in these dural attachments can directly impact the central nervous system and the flow of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Palpating these attachments requires a gentle, sustained touch.

To assess the dural attachments at the foramen magnum, gently place your fingers at the base of the occiput, feeling for the subtle tug or pull of the dura. Note any restrictions or asymmetries. This assessment is a direct application of the "Initiate Release" principle of The A.L.I.G.N. Frameworkâ„¢, as identifying these restrictions is the first step towards releasing them.

Clinical Significance of Cranial Base Restrictions

Cranial base restrictions can manifest in a variety of symptoms, including:

  • Headaches and migraines
  • Neck pain and stiffness
  • TMJ dysfunction
  • Visual disturbances
  • Tinnitus and vertigo
  • Difficulty concentrating

Understanding the potential impact of these restrictions is crucial for effective treatment planning. Remember that the body functions as an integrated unit, and addressing cranial base restrictions can often lead to improvements in seemingly unrelated areas. Case Study: Cranial Base Assessment

Client: Emily, 34 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Emily presented with chronic tension headaches, neck pain, and TMJ clicking. She reported difficulty concentrating and feeling "stressed out."

Assessment: Palpation revealed tenderness and restriction at the occiput and sphenoid. The TMJ exhibited clicking on the right side with limited range of motion. Dural attachments at the foramen magnum felt tight and restricted.

Intervention: Gentle cranial techniques were used to release restrictions at the occiput, sphenoid, and TMJ. Dural mobilization was performed to improve CSF flow. Emily was also taught self-care techniques to manage stress and TMJ tension.

Outcomes: After four sessions, Emily reported a significant reduction in headaches and neck pain. TMJ clicking decreased, and her concentration improved. She felt more relaxed and less stressed overall. This case highlights the importance of a comprehensive cranial base assessment in addressing chronic pain and stress-related symptoms.

Postural Analysis: A Multi-View Approach

A thorough postural analysis involves observing the client from three perspectives: anterior (front), posterior (back), and lateral (side). This multi-view approach allows you to identify asymmetries and deviations from ideal alignment. Here's what to look for in each view:

  • Anterior View: Observe head tilt, shoulder height, pelvic alignment, knee position, and foot pronation/supination.
  • Posterior View: Assess spinal curvature, scapular position, pelvic symmetry, and leg length discrepancies.
  • Lateral View: Examine the alignment of the ear, shoulder, hip, knee, and ankle. Look for forward head posture, excessive kyphosis (rounding of the upper back), and lordosis (excessive curvature of the lower back).

Remember from our previous lesson on observation skills, paying attention to subtle details is key. A slight head tilt or a barely noticeable shoulder asymmetry can provide valuable clues about underlying imbalances.

Common Postural Distortions and Their Impact

Several common postural distortions can negatively impact the craniosacral system. Understanding these patterns is crucial for effective assessment and treatment. Here are a few examples:

  • Forward Head Posture: This is characterized by the head being positioned forward relative to the shoulders. It can strain the neck muscles, restrict breathing, and compress the occiput, affecting the craniosacral rhythm. Studies show that forward head posture is present in up to 66% of adults in developed countries (Grosz etal., 2018).
  • Rounded Shoulders: This posture involves internally rotated shoulders and a rounded upper back. It can restrict rib cage expansion, impair breathing, and affect the position of the scapulae, influencing upper body mechanics.
  • Anterior Pelvic Tilt: This is characterized by an excessive arch in the lower back and a forward tilting of the pelvis. It can strain the lumbar spine, compress the sacrum, and affect the dural attachment at the sacrococcygeal ligament.
  • Scoliosis: This involves a lateral curvature of the spine. It can create significant imbalances in the musculoskeletal system and affect the craniosacral rhythm due to dural attachments along the spine.

Assessing Body Mechanics During Gait

Body mechanics refer to how you move your body during activities like walking, lifting, and bending. Assessing body mechanics, particularly during gait (walking), provides valuable information about movement patterns and potential imbalances. Observe your client's gait from the anterior, posterior, and lateral views. Look for:

  • Arm swing: Is it symmetrical and coordinated?
  • Stride length: Is it even on both sides?
  • Pelvic rotation: Is there adequate rotation in the pelvis?
  • Foot strike: How does the foot contact the ground (heel strike, mid-foot strike, forefoot strike)?

Compensatory patterns during gait can indicate underlying restrictions or weaknesses. For example, a limited arm swing on one side may suggest shoulder or thoracic restrictions. An uneven stride length could indicate a leg length discrepancy or pelvic imbalance.

Posture, Breathing, and Craniosacral Rhythm

Posture, breathing, and the craniosacral rhythm are intricately linked. Poor posture can restrict breathing, and conversely, dysfunctional breathing patterns can contribute to postural imbalances. For example, chronic shallow breathing can lead to tension in the neck and shoulder muscles, contributing to forward head posture and rounded shoulders. When assessing posture, pay attention to your client's breathing pattern. Observe the rate, depth, and rhythm of their breath. Does the chest or abdomen rise more during inhalation? Is there any visible tension in the breathing muscles?

The diaphragm, the primary muscle of respiration, also has a direct connection to the craniosacral system through its attachment to the lumbar vertebrae and the psoas muscle. Restrictions in the diaphragm can affect the movement of the lumbar spine and sacrum, influencing the dural tension and the craniosacral rhythm. Addressing breathing patterns is often an integral part of craniosacral therapy.

The global assessment is the "A" in the A.L.I.G.N. Frameworkâ„¢. It sets the stage for the more specific assessments and treatment techniques that follow. The information gathered during the global assessment helps you:

  • Identify areas of restriction: Postural distortions and gait abnormalities can highlight areas where the craniosacral system may be compromised.
  • Prioritize treatment: The global assessment helps you determine which areas to address first. For example, if a client presents with forward head posture and restricted breathing, you may choose to start by releasing tension in the neck and diaphragm.
  • Monitor progress: Re-assessing posture and body mechanics throughout the treatment process allows you to track changes and evaluate the effectiveness of your interventions.

Case Study: Maria, 52-Year-Old with Chronic Neck Pain

Client: Maria, a 52-year-old office worker, presented with chronic neck pain and headaches.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria reported experiencing neck pain for over a year, which she attributed to prolonged computer use. She also complained of frequent headaches, muscle tension, and difficulty sleeping.

Global Assessment Findings: Anterior view revealed a slight head tilt to the left and elevated right shoulder. Posterior view showed mild scoliosis with a convexity to the right in the thoracic spine. Lateral view showed pronounced forward head posture and rounded shoulders. Gait assessment revealed reduced arm swing on the left side.

Intervention: Craniosacral therapy was used to address the identified postural imbalances. Treatment focused on releasing tension in the neck and shoulder muscles, mobilizing the thoracic spine, and balancing the pelvis. Breathing exercises were also incorporated to improve diaphragmatic function.

Outcomes: After six sessions, Maria reported a significant reduction in neck pain and headaches. Her posture improved, with a noticeable decrease in forward head posture and rounded shoulders. She also reported improved sleep quality and increased energy levels. The reduced arm swing during gait was also improved.

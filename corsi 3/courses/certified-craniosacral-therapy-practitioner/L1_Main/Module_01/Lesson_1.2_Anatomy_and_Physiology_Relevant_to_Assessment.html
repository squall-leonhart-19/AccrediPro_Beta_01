Detailed Review of the Cranial Bones

The cranium, consisting of 22 bones, can be divided into the neurocranium (which protects the brain) and the viscerocranium (which forms the face). For craniosacral therapy, the primary bones of interest include the occiput, parietal bones, frontal bone, temporal bones, sphenoid, and ethmoid. These bones articulate at sutures, which are fibrous joints that allow for slight movement. It is these subtle movements we learn to perceive and influence.

Key features to understand:

  • Occiput: Forms the posterior and inferior aspect of the cranium. Articulates with the atlas (C1 vertebra). The foramen magnum is a critical opening for the spinal cord.
  • Parietal Bones: Form the sides and roof of the cranium. Articulate with each other at the sagittal suture and with the frontal bone at the coronal suture.
  • Frontal Bone: Forms the forehead. Articulates with the parietal bones at the coronal suture and contributes to the orbital cavities.
  • Temporal Bones: Located on the lateral sides of the cranium. House the middle and inner ear structures. Articulate with the parietal bone, occiput, and sphenoid.
  • Sphenoid: A complex, butterfly-shaped bone at the base of the skull. Articulates with all other cranial bones. The sella turcica houses the pituitary gland.
  • Ethmoid: Located anterior to the sphenoid. Contributes to the nasal cavity and orbits.

The Craniosacral Rhythm

The craniosacral rhythm (CSR) is a palpable, rhythmic impulse believed to be generated by the production and reabsorption of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). This rhythm is transmitted throughout the craniosacral system, influencing the movement of the cranial bones and sacrum. While the exact mechanism remains debated, current research suggests that it is influenced by various factors.

Factors influencing the CSR:

  • CSF Production and Reabsorption: The choroid plexus produces CSF, which is then reabsorbed into the venous sinuses.
  • Dural Membrane Tension: The tension within the dura mater influences the flow of CSF and the movement of cranial bones.
  • Intracranial Pressure: Changes in intracranial pressure can affect the CSR.

The normal rate of the CSR is typically between 6 to 12 cycles per minute. Palpation of the CSR involves sensing subtle movements and asymmetries in the cranial bones, sacrum, and other related structures.

The Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM) System

The Reciprocal Tension Membrane (RTM) system is a network of dural membranes that connect the cranium to the sacrum. It plays a crucial role in maintaining structural integrity and facilitating the transmission of forces throughout the craniosacral system. The primary components of the RTM include:

  • Falx Cerebri: A dural fold that separates the two cerebral hemispheres.
  • Tentorium Cerebelli: A dural fold that separates the cerebrum from the cerebellum.
  • Falx Cerebelli: A dural fold that separates the two cerebellar hemispheres.
  • Dura Mater of the Spinal Cord: The outermost covering of the spinal cord, which is continuous with the cranial dura.

The RTM system functions as a tensegrity structure, meaning that it maintains stability through a balance of tension and compression. Restrictions or imbalances within the RTM can lead to various musculoskeletal and neurological symptoms.

Craniosacral System and the Central Nervous System (CNS)

The craniosacral system has a direct and profound relationship with the central nervous system (CNS). The CNS, comprising the brain and spinal cord, is protected and nourished by the craniosacral system. The CSF, produced within the craniosacral system, provides cushioning, nutrients, and waste removal for the CNS tissues. The dura mater, a key component of the craniosacral system, directly surrounds and supports the brain and spinal cord.

Dysfunction within the craniosacral system can directly impact the function of the CNS, leading to a variety of neurological symptoms. Conversely, CNS dysfunction can also affect the craniosacral system, creating a feedback loop. This close relationship underscores the importance of addressing both systems in a holistic treatment approach.

The Fascial System and Interconnectedness

The fascial system is a continuous network of connective tissue that extends throughout the entire body, enveloping muscles, bones, organs, and nerves. It provides structural support, facilitates movement, and plays a role in fluid transport and immune function. The craniosacral system is intimately connected with the fascial system, with the dura mater being a specialized extension of the fascial network.

Restrictions or imbalances within the fascial system can affect the craniosacral system, and vice versa. For example, tension in the plantar fascia can transmit forces up through the legs and into the pelvis, affecting the sacrum and dural attachments. Similarly, restrictions in the cranial base can influence fascial tension throughout the body. This interconnectedness highlights the importance of assessing and treating the entire fascial system in craniosacral therapy.

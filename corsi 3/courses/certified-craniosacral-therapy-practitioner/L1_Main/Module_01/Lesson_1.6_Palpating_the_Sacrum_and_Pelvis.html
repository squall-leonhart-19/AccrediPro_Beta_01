Anatomy Review

Let's begin with a quick review of the relevant anatomy. The pelvis is composed of the sacrum, ilium, ischium, and pubic bones. The sacrum, a triangular bone at the base of the spine, articulates with the ilia at the sacroiliac joints (SIJ). The SIJs are strong, weight-bearing joints that transmit forces between the spine and lower extremities. The ilium, ischium, and pubis fuse to form the hip bone. Understanding these structures is crucial for accurate palpation and assessment.

Identifying Sacral Dysfunction

Sacral dysfunction can manifest as torsions, shears, or fixations. Sacral torsions involve rotation around an oblique axis. Sacral shears occur when the sacrum moves superiorly or inferiorly relative to the ilia. Sacral fixations refer to restricted movement of the sacrum. These dysfunctions can impact the dural tube and craniosacral rhythm. Palpation involves carefully assessing theposition of bony landmarks such as the sacral base, inferior lateral angles (ILAs), and sacral sulci.

To palpate for sacral dysfunction, follow these steps:

  1. Position the client prone with a pillow under their abdomen to reduce lumbar lordosis.
  2. Standing at the side of the table, locate the PSIS (Posterior Superior Iliac Spine) bilaterally.
  3. Move medially and inferiorly to palpate the ILA (Inferior Lateral Angle) of the sacrum.
  4. Assess the depth and symmetry of the sacral sulci by palpating just medial to the PSIS.
  5. Evaluate the position of the sacral base by placing your thumbs on either side of the spinous process of L5 and moving inferiorly onto the sacral base.

Common sacral dysfunctions include:

  • Forward Sacral Torsion: The sacral base is anterior on one side, and the ILA is posterior on the opposite side.
  • Backward Sacral Torsion: The sacral base is posterior on one side, and the ILA is anterior on the opposite side.
  • Unilateral Sacral Flexion: One side of the sacrum is stuck in flexion, with a deep sacral sulcus and a posterior ILA on the same side.
  • Unilateral Sacral Extension: One side of the sacrum is stuck in extension, with a shallow sacral sulcus and an anterior ILA on the same side.

Assessing Pelvic Mobility

Pelvic mobility is essential for shock absorption and efficient movement. Restricted mobility at the SIJs can lead to compensatory patterns and pain. To assess iliosacral mobility, palpate the movement of the ilium relative to the sacrum during specific movements. To assess sacroiliac mobility, palpate the movement of the sacrum relative to the ilium.

Here are some techniques to assess pelvic mobility:

  1. Standing Flexion Test: Palpate the PSIS bilaterally as the client flexes forward. A PSIS that moves superiorly earlier or further than the other suggests iliosacral restriction on that side.
  2. Gillet's Test (Sacral Fixation Test): Palpate the PSIS and the sacral base (S2 spinous process). Have the client flex one hip towards their chest. The PSIS should move inferiorly relative to the sacrum. If it doesn't move or moves poorly, it suggests a sacral fixation on that side.
  3. Prone Knee Bend Test: With the client prone, passively flex one knee towards the buttock. Palpate the SIJ on the same side. You should feel a slight gapping or movement at the joint. Reduced movement indicates SIJ restriction.

Evaluating Pelvic Symmetry

Pelvic symmetry is crucial for balanced posture and spinal alignment. Pelvic asymmetry can contribute to scoliosis, leg length discrepancies, and low back pain. To assess pelvic symmetry, palpate key bony landmarks such as the ASIS (Anterior Superior Iliac Spine), PSIS, and iliac crests. Use a level to confirm horizontal alignment. Note any discrepancies and their potential impact on the spine.

Assessing pelvic symmetry involves the following steps:

  1. ASIS Palpation: Palpate the ASIS bilaterally to assess for height differences. A higher ASIS on one side suggests an anterior pelvic tilt on that side.
  2. Iliac Crest Palpation: Palpate the iliac crests bilaterally to assess for height differences. A higher iliac crest on one side suggests a lateral pelvic tilt on that side.
  3. PSIS Palpation: Palpate the PSIS bilaterally to assess for height differences. A higher PSIS on one side suggests a posterior pelvic tilt on that side.
  4. Leg Length Assessment: Assess for functional leg length discrepancies by observing the medial malleoli with the client supine. A shorter leg on one side can indicate pelvic asymmetry.

Sacrum, Dura, and Craniosacral Rhythm

The sacrum is directly connected to the dural tube, which encases the spinal cord and attaches to the foramen magnum at the base of the skull. Movement of the sacrum influences tension in the dural tube, which in turn affects the craniosacral rhythm. Restrictions in sacral mobility can disrupt the craniosacral rhythm, leading to various symptoms. Palpation involves assessing the quality and symmetry of the craniosacral rhythm at the sacrum.

To assess the relationship between the sacrum, dura, and craniosacral rhythm:

  1. Sacral Hold: Place your hands gently on the sacrum, feeling for the subtle movements of the craniosacral rhythm. Assess for symmetry, amplitude, and rate.
  2. Dural Tube Palpation: Palpate along the spinous processes of the lumbar spine, feeling for tension or restrictions in the dural tube.
  3. Craniosacral Rhythm Assessment: Assess the craniosacral rhythm at the sacrum and compare it to the rhythm at the cranium. Look for synchronicity and any areas of restriction.

Understanding the connection between the sacrum, dura, and craniosacral rhythm allows for a more holistic approach to craniosacral therapy. By addressing sacral dysfunctions, you can positively influence the entire craniosacral system.

Case Study

Case Study: Sarah, 35-year-old Female with Chronic Low Back Pain

Client: Sarah, a 35-year-old female

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic low back pain, right-sided sciatica, and headaches.

Assessment Findings:

  • Palpation revealed a forward sacral torsion on the right (right sacral base anterior, left ILA posterior).
  • Standing flexion test positive on the right.
  • Pelvic asymmetry with a slightly higher right iliac crest.
  • Restricted craniosacral rhythm at the sacrum.

Intervention:

  • Craniosacral therapy techniques to release the sacral torsion.
  • Muscle energy techniques to correct the pelvic asymmetry.
  • Dural tube mobilization to improve craniosacral rhythm.

Outcomes:

  • Significant reduction in low back pain and sciatica after three sessions.
  • Improved pelvic symmetry and mobility.
  • Normalized craniosacral rhythm.
  • Reduced frequency and intensity of headaches.

Detailed Anatomy of the Hyoid Bone and Larynx

The hyoid bone, a horseshoe-shaped bone located in the anterior neck, is unique because it doesn't articulate with any other bone. Instead, it's suspended by muscles and ligaments from the styloid processes of the temporal bones. The larynx, or voice box, sits inferior to the hyoid bone and is a complex structure composed of cartilage, ligaments, and muscles.

Understanding the muscular attachments is crucial for effective Craniosacral Therapy (CST). Key muscles connecting to the hyoid and larynx include:

  • Suprahyoid muscles: Digastric, stylohyoid, mylohyoid, and geniohyoid â€“ elevating the hyoid and assisting in swallowing.
  • Infrahyoid muscles: Sternohyoid, omohyoid, sternothyroid, and thyrohyoid â€“ depressing the hyoid and larynx.
  • Laryngeal muscles: Cricothyroid, thyroarytenoid, posterior cricoarytenoid, and lateral cricoarytenoid â€“ controlling vocal cord tension and airflow.

Dysfunction in these muscles can lead to restrictions in the hyoid and larynx, impacting voice, swallowing, and even breathing. A 2018 study in the Journal of Voice found that individuals with voice disorders often exhibit increased tension in the suprahyoid and infrahyoid muscles (Roy et al., 2018).

Assessing Hyoid and Laryngeal Mobility

Palpation is key to assessing the mobility of the hyoid bone and larynx. Hereâ€™s a step-by-step approach:

  1. Positioning: Have the client lie supine with a small towel roll under their neck to maintain a neutral cervical curve.
  2. Hyoid Palpation: Gently locate the hyoid bone just superior to the thyroid cartilage. Assess its lateral and vertical glide. Note any restrictions or tenderness.
  3. Laryngeal Palpation: Palpate the thyroid cartilage and cricoid cartilage. Assess their mobility during swallowing. Note any crepitus or pain.
  4. Muscle Palpation: Palpate the suprahyoid and infrahyoid muscles for tension or trigger points.

Restrictions can manifest as limited range of motion, tenderness to palpation, or asymmetry in movement. A 2021 study published in Manual Therapy demonstrated that palpation for cervical musculoskeletal dysfunctions has a sensitivity of 78% and a specificity of 82% when performed by experienced practitioners (Cleland et al., 2021).

Hyoid and Laryngeal Release Techniques

CST techniques for the hyoid and larynx are gentle and indirect, focusing on releasing fascial restrictions and muscle tension. Here are a few examples:

  • Hyoid Release: Gently cradle the hyoid bone between your thumb and index finger. Introduce a gentle traction and follow the direction of ease. Hold until you feel a release.
  • Laryngeal Release: Place your fingers on either side of the thyroid cartilage. Apply a gentle, sustained pressure and follow the direction of ease.
  • Muscle Energy Technique (MET): For tight suprahyoid or infrahyoid muscles, use MET by having the client gently contract the muscle against your resistance, followed by a period of relaxation and stretch.

Remember to work within the client's comfort level and to monitor their response throughout the treatment. The Vagus Nerve Connection

The vagus nerve, the longest cranial nerve, plays a crucial role in regulating various bodily functions, including heart rate, digestion, and immune response. It passes in close proximity to the hyoid and larynx, and restrictions in these structures can potentially impinge on the nerve.

By releasing tension in the hyoid and larynx, we can indirectly influence vagal tone. Improved vagal tone is associated with reduced stress, improved digestion, and enhanced overall well-being. A meta-analysis published in Frontiers in Neuroscience showed that interventions that increase vagal tone, such as manual therapy, are associated with improved cardiovascular health and reduced inflammation (De Couck et al., 2019).

Techniques that can help to improve vagal tone include:

  • Gentle hyoid and laryngeal release
  • Diaphragm release
  • Sacral rocking

Case Study: TMJ Dysfunction and Neck Pain

Case Study: Sarah, 35-year-old with TMJ Dysfunction and Neck Pain

Client: Sarah, a 35-year-old office worker, presented with chronic TMJ dysfunction, neck pain, and headaches. She reported difficulty swallowing and a feeling of tightness in her throat.

Assessment: Palpation revealed restrictions in the hyoid bone and larynx, as well as tension in the suprahyoid and infrahyoid muscles. She also exhibited limited cervical range of motion and tenderness in the TMJ.

Intervention: Over six sessions, Sarah received gentle CST techniques, including hyoid and laryngeal release, diaphragm release, and TMJ mobilization. MET was used to address the tight suprahyoid and infrahyoid muscles.

Outcomes: Sarah experienced a significant reduction in her TMJ pain, neck pain, and headaches. Her swallowing improved, and she reported a greater sense of ease in her throat. Cervical range of motion also increased. She also reported feeling more relaxed and less stressed overall.

This case study highlights the interconnectedness of the TMJ, neck, hyoid, and larynx. By addressing restrictions in these areas, we can achieve significant improvements in client outcomes.

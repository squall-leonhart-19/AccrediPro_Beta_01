Advanced Palpation Techniques

In the previous lesson, we explored the cranial base. Now, we shift our focus to the foundation of the spine: the sacrum. Advanced palpation involves a layered approach, building on your ability to perceive subtle movements and tissue texture changes. This includes assessing the:

  • Sacral Base: Palpate the superior aspect of the sacrum, noting any asymmetry or tenderness.
  • Sacral Apex: Locate the inferior tip of the sacrum, assessing its position and mobility.
  • Lateral Angles: Palpate the lateral borders of the sacrum, feeling for restrictions or imbalances.

Remember the importance of your intention. Approach each assessment with a listening touch, as emphasized in Module 2. A 2018 study in the Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies highlights that experienced practitioners can reliably identify sacral asymmetries (Lederman et al., 2018).

Evaluating Sacroiliac Ligaments

The sacroiliac (SI) ligaments play a crucial role in stabilizing the pelvis and transmitting forces between the spine and lower extremities. Assessing their mobility and tension is key to identifying sacral dysfunctions. Key ligaments to evaluate include:

  • Anterior Sacroiliac Ligaments: Palpate anteriorly, assessing for tenderness and mobility.
  • Posterior Sacroiliac Ligaments: Palpate posteriorly, noting any increased tension or pain.
  • Interosseous Sacroiliac Ligaments: Deep palpation to assess the integrity and tension between the sacrum and ilium.

Ligamentous assessment requires patience and a refined sense of touch. Identifying Sacral Dysfunctions

Sacral dysfunctions can manifest in various forms, impacting biomechanics and potentially contributing to pain and discomfort. Common dysfunctions include:

  • Sacral Torsions: Rotation of the sacrum around an oblique axis.
  • Sacral Shears: Superior or inferior displacement of one side of the sacrum relative to the ilium.
  • Sacral Flexion/Extension Restrictions: Limited movement of the sacrum in the sagittal plane.

Identifying these dysfunctions requires a thorough assessment, integrating palpation findings with movement testing. Remember that sacral dysfunctions are often compensatory patterns, rather than primary issues. A 2022 review in Chiropractic & Manual Therapies emphasizes the importance of considering the entire kinetic chain when assessing and treating sacral problems (Potter et al., 2022).

Sacral Dysfunction and Lower Extremity Imbalances

Sacral dysfunctions can significantly impact lower extremity biomechanics, leading to imbalances and compensatory patterns. A short leg syndrome, for example, can induce sacral torsion. Assessing for leg length discrepancies, muscle imbalances, and altered gait patterns is crucial. Consider the following:

  • Leg Length Assessment: Evaluate for functional or structural leg length differences.
  • Muscle Palpation: Assess the tone and tenderness of muscles such as the piriformis, psoas, and hamstrings.
  • Gait Analysis: Observe the client's walking pattern, noting any asymmetries or compensations.

Addressing lower extremity imbalances is often necessary to achieve lasting correction of sacral dysfunctions. A 2020 study in the Journal of Orthopaedic & Sports Physical Therapy found a strong correlation between sacral asymmetry and lower extremity muscle activation patterns (Lee et al., 2020). Case Study: Chronic Low Back Pain & Sciatica

Client: Sarah J., 42 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic low back pain radiating into the left leg (sciatica), aggravated by prolonged sitting and bending. Reports pain level of 6/10 on average.

Assessment Findings:

  • Palpation revealed a right sacral torsion with restricted left sacral base movement.
  • Positive standing flexion test on the left.
  • Increased tension in the left piriformis muscle.
  • Functional leg length discrepancy, with the left leg appearing shorter.

Intervention:

  1. Soft tissue release to the left piriformis and psoas muscles.
  2. Muscle Energy Techniques (MET) to correct the right sacral torsion.
  3. Pelvic stabilization exercises to address the leg length discrepancy.
  4. Education on proper posture and body mechanics.

Outcomes: After four sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in pain (2/10) and improved functional mobility. The sacral torsion was corrected, and leg length discrepancy was minimized. She was able to sit and bend with less discomfort.

  2. Describe the difference between a sacral torsion and a sacral shear.
    A sacral torsion involves rotation around an oblique axis, while a sacral shear is a superior or inferior displacement of one side of the sacrum relative to the ilium.

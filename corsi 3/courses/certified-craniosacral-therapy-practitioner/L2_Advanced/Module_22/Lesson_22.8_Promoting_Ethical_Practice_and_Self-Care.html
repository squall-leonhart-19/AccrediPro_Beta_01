Case Study: The Community Connection

Case: Sarah, 48

Sarah, a CST practitioner in a small rural town, begins treating Emily, a 45-year-old local business owner. Over time, Sarah and Emily discover shared interests and become friends outside of therapy sessions. Emily then asks Sarah to provide CST for her 16-year-old daughter, who is struggling with anxiety. Sarah is also on the board of the local school district, where Emily's daughter is a student. The potential for dual relationships is significant.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Decision-Making

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Dilemma
The primary dilemma is whether Sarah can ethically treat Emily's daughter, given their friendship and Sarah's position on the school board. Treating Emily herself introduces a secondary, but still important, layer of complexity. This could impair Sarah's objectivity and create a conflict of interest.
Step 2: Consult Ethical Guidelines
Review the ethical codes of relevant professional organizations (e.g., the American Craniosacral Therapy Association). These codes typically advise against dual relationships that could impair professional judgment or increase the risk of exploitation. A meta-analysis by Anderson & Kitchener (1998) in the *Professional Psychology: Research and Practice* journal emphasizes the importance of avoiding dual relationships to maintain client welfare.
Step 3: Consider Potential Harms
Treating Emily's daughter could lead to divided loyalties, compromised confidentiality, and potential exploitation. If Sarah makes decisions on the school board that affect Emily's daughter, it could damage the therapeutic relationship. The APA's Ethics Code (2017) offers guidance on avoiding conflicts of interest that could harm clients, as noted in their publication by the American Psychological Association.
Step 4: Explore Alternative Solutions
Sarah could refer Emily's daughter to another qualified CST practitioner in a neighboring town. She should also discuss the ethical concerns with Emily, emphasizing the importance of maintaining professional boundaries.

Differential Considerations

Factors Influencing Ethical Decisions

1

Power Dynamics

Sarah holds a position of authority as a therapist and school board member, potentially influencing Emily and her daughter's choices. According to Koocher & Keith-Spiegel (2016) in their book *Ethics in Psychology and the Mental Health Professions*, power differentials are a key consideration in ethical decision-making.

2

Community Norms

In a small town, social overlap is common, but it doesn't negate the need for ethical boundaries. Gutheil & Gabbard (1993) in the *American Journal of Psychiatry* highlight the challenges of maintaining boundaries in small communities.

3

Informed Consent

Even with informed consent, dual relationships can still be problematic if they impair objectivity or increase the risk of harm. Fisher's (2017) *Decoding the Ethics Code* emphasizes that informed consent does not automatically justify dual relationships.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key ethical considerations when a client asks you to treat their family member?

Objectivity, potential for divided loyalties, confidentiality, and power dynamics are all important considerations. A careful assessment of the risks and benefits is essential.

2. How can you manage the complexities of practicing in a small, close-knit community?

Be proactive in addressing potential dual relationships, maintain clear boundaries, seek regular consultation, and prioritize client welfare above social pressures.

Key Takeaways

  • Dual relationships can compromise objectivity and increase the risk of harm.
  • Ethical decision-making requires careful assessment, consultation, and informed consent.
  • Maintaining clear boundaries is essential for protecting client welfare.
  • Small-town dynamics can amplify ethical challenges.
  • Prioritize client autonomy and well-being above all else.

Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI)

The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) is one of the most widely researched and utilized tools for measuring burnout. It assesses burnout across three key dimensions:

  • Exhaustion: Feelings of being emotionally and physically drained.
  • Cynicism (or Depersonalization): A detached, negative, or excessively cynical attitude towards work.
  • Professional Efficacy (or Personal Accomplishment): Feelings of competence and success in one's work. A low score here indicates burnout.

The MBI is typically administered as a questionnaire with statements about work-related feelings. Respondents rate how frequently they experience these feelings on a scale. High scores on Exhaustion and Cynicism, coupled with low scores on Professional Efficacy, indicate burnout.

Example: A nurse, after taking the MBI, scores high on Exhaustion and Cynicism but low on Professional Efficacy. This indicates she's likely experiencing burnout and could benefit from strategies to address these specific areas.

Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI)

The Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI) offers another perspective on burnout, focusing on two dimensions:

  • Exhaustion: Similar to the MBI, this measures feelings of being drained and depleted.
  • Disengagement: This reflects a sense of detachment from work, characterized by a lack of enthusiasm and commitment.

Unlike the MBI, the OLBI uses a simpler response scale and focuses more on the cognitive aspects of burnout. It's useful for identifying individuals who are mentally distancing themselves from their work due to stress.

Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI)

The Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI) distinguishes itself by assessing burnout across three areas:

  • Personal Burnout: General feelings of exhaustion and fatigue.
  • Work-Related Burnout: Burnout specifically relatedto work.
  • Client-Related Burnout: Burnout stemming from interactions with clients (relevant for healthcare and service professionals).

The CBI is particularly useful for identifying the specific sources of burnout. For example, a social worker might score low on personal burnout but high on client-related burnout, indicating that their burnout is primarily driven by the emotional demands of their work with clients.

Other Assessment Tools

While the MBI, OLBI, and CBI are widely used, other tools can provide additional insights:

  • Shirom-Melamed Burnout Measure (SMBM): Focuses on physical, emotional, and cognitive exhaustion.
  • Areas of Worklife Survey (AWS): Assesses the match between individuals and their work environment across six areas: workload, control, reward, community, fairness, and values.
  • Brief Burnout Questionnaire (BBQ): A shorter, more concise measure of burnout.

Choosing the right tool depends on the specific context and the aspects of burnout you want to assess. As a Certified Burnout Recovery Specialistâ„¢, you'll learn to select the most appropriate tool for each client.

Interpreting Results

Interpreting self-assessment results is a critical skill. Hereâ€™s a general framework:

  • Understand the Scoring: Each tool has its own scoring system. Familiarize yourself with the cut-off scores and what they indicate.
  • Consider Context: A high score on a burnout scale doesn't automatically mean someone is "burned out." Consider their individual circumstances, work environment, and other stressors.
  • Look for Patterns: Identify patterns across different dimensions or subscales. For example, is the burnout primarily driven by exhaustion, cynicism, or lack of accomplishment?

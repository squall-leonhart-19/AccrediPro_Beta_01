The Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI)

The Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI) is a self-report measure designed to assess burnout using two dimensions: exhaustion and disengagement. Unlike the MBI, it does not include a component related to professional efficacy. This can be particularly useful when assessing individuals whose burnout may not be directly tied to their perceived competence.

The OLBI consists of 16 items, with statements such as "During my work, I often feel emotionally drained" (exhaustion) and "I feel a sense of accomplishment when I accomplish something at work" (disengagement, reverse-scored). Responses are typically rated on a 4-point Likert scale. A 2018 study published in the Journal of Occupational Health Psychology found the OLBI to have strong psychometric properties across diverse occupational groups (Demerouti et al., 2018).

Shirom-Melamed Burnout Measure (SMBM)

The Shirom-Melamed Burnout Measure (SMBM), also known as the Burnout Measure (BM), assesses burnout through three dimensions: physical fatigue, emotional exhaustion, and cognitive weariness. This tool offers a more granular view of the burnout experience, differentiating between physical, emotional, and cognitive manifestations. This aligns well with the 'Recognize & Reflect' stage of the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢, enabling a more precise identification of burnout symptoms.

The SMBM consists of 22 items rated on a 7-point frequency scale. Example items include "I feel tired" (physical fatigue), "I feel I am at the end of my rope" (emotional exhaustion), and "I have difficulty concentrating" (cognitive weariness). Research suggests that the SMBM is particularly sensitive to early stages of burnout (Toker et al., 2005, Journal of Applied Psychology).

Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI)

The Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI) focuses on the degree of physical and psychological fatigue and exhaustion experienced by the person. It is unique in assessing burnout across three scales: personal burnout, work-related burnout, and client-related burnout. Thisis especially useful for professionals working directly with clients or patients, allowing for a distinction between burnout stemming from personal factors, work environment, and interactions with those they serve.

The CBI includes 19 items, with response scales varying depending on the subscale. For example, "How often do you feel tired?" (personal burnout), "Is your work emotionally exhausting?" (work-related burnout), and "Do you find it frustrating to deal with clients?" (client-related burnout). A study in Applied Psychology: Health and Well-Being (Kristensen et al., 2005) demonstrated the CBI's reliability and validity in various human service professions.

ProQOL and Helping Professions

The Professional Quality of Life Scale (ProQOL) is another valuable tool, particularly for those in helping professions. While not strictly a burnout measure, it assesses the positive and negative aspects of helping others who experience suffering and trauma. It measures compassion satisfaction, burnout, and secondary traumatic stress. The ProQOL can provide a more holistic view of well-being in roles where empathy and emotional labor are central.

Compassion satisfaction reflects the pleasure derived from being able to help others. Secondary traumatic stress (STS) refers to the distress experienced from learning about others' trauma. The ProQOL is a 30-item self-report measure with good reliability and validity (Stamm, 2010). This is crucial for those in roles that demand constant empathetic engagement, fitting well with the 'Sustain & Support' stage of the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢.

Selecting the Right Tool

Choosing the most appropriate assessment tool depends on several factors. Consider the following:

  • The client's profession: Is the client in a helping profession, or in a field where professional efficacy is a central component of their identity?
  • The suspected stage of burnout: Are you looking for early signs, or assessing established burnout?
  • The specific symptoms: Are you focusing on physical, emotional, or cognitive symptoms?
  • The need for differentiation: Do you need to distinguish between personal, work-related, and client-related sources of burnout?

Case Study: Sarah, Social Worker

Client: Sarah, 38, Social Worker

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports feeling increasingly detached from her clients, experiencing chronic fatigue, and struggling to concentrate at work. She feels overwhelmed by the emotional demands of her job and questions her ability to make a difference.

Intervention: Given Sarah's profession and symptoms, the ProQOL and CBI were administered. The ProQOL revealed low compassion satisfaction and high secondary traumatic stress. The CBI showed high scores in work-related and client-related burnout.

Outcomes: Based on these results, the intervention focused on strategies to improve Sarah's self-care practices, enhance her coping mechanisms for managing secondary trauma, and re-evaluate her workload. Follow-up assessments showed improved compassion satisfaction and reduced burnout scores.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What are the key differences between the MBI and the OLBI?
    The MBI includes a professional efficacy component, while the OLBI focuses solely on exhaustion and disengagement.
  2. For a nurse experiencing physical fatigue and difficulty concentrating, which assessment tool might be most appropriate?
    The SMBM, as it assesses physical fatigue, emotional exhaustion, and cognitive weariness.

Key Takeaways

  • The MBI is not the only tool for assessing burnout.
  • The OLBI, SMBM, and CBI offer unique perspectives on burnout.
  • The ProQOL is valuable for assessing well-being in helping professions.
  • Selecting the right tool depends on the client's profession, symptoms, and stage of burnout.

Introduction: Balancing Breastfeeding and Work/School

Returning to work or school doesn't have to mean the end of breastfeeding. With careful planning and preparation, mothers can successfully balance these commitments while continuing to provide breast milk for their babies. This lesson will equip you with the knowledge and tools to support mothers in this important transition. Remember, as weâ€™ve discussed in previous lessons, every mother's journey is unique, and our role as coaches is to support their individual goals.

Many women worry about how they will continue to breastfeed once they return to work. A 2021 study in the Journal of Human Lactation found that only 40% of employed mothers in the U.S. breastfeed at 6 months, compared to 65% of non-employed mothers. This highlights the need for effective support and planning to help working mothers achieve their breastfeeding goals.

Expressing and Storing Breast Milk

  • Pumping Equipment: Discuss different types of breast pumps (manual, electric, hospital-grade) and help mothers choose the best option for their needs and budget.
  • Pumping Schedule: Encourage mothers to establish a pumping schedule that mimics their baby's feeding patterns. Aim for pumping every 2-3 hours while away from the baby.
  • Hygiene: Emphasize the importance of washing hands before pumping and properly cleaning pump parts after each use.
  • Storage Guidelines: Provide clear guidelines on safely storing breast milk in the refrigerator (up to 4 days) or freezer (up to 6-12 months).

Planning Childcare and Communication

Careful planning and open communication with caregivers are essential for successful breastfeeding while working or attending school.

  • Choosing a Breastfeeding-Friendly Caregiver: Help mothers identify caregivers who are supportive of breastfeeding and understand how to properly handle and feed expressed breast milk.
  • Communication is Key: Encourage mothers to have open and ongoing conversations with their caregivers about feeding preferences, milk storage, and any other relevant information.
  • Feeding Schedule: Work with mothers to create a feeding schedule for the caregiver that aligns with the baby's needs and the mother's pumping schedule.

A 2019 study in Breastfeeding Medicine found that infants cared for by providers trained in proper breast milk handling and feeding techniques had significantly fewer feeding-related issues compared to those cared for by untrained providers.

Workplace Breastfeeding Policies and Advocacy

Understanding workplace breastfeeding policies and advocating for mothers' rights is crucial for creating a supportive environment.

  • Federal and State Laws: Familiarize yourself with federal and state laws that protect breastfeeding mothers in the workplace, such as the Break Time for Nursing Mothers law under the Fair Labor Standards Act.
  • Company Policies: Encourage mothers to review their company's breastfeeding policies and understand their rights and responsibilities.
  • Advocacy: Empower mothers to advocate for their needs, such as a private and sanitary space for pumping and flexible break times.

Many companies are now implementing lactation support programs. A 2022 report by the CDC found that companies with comprehensive lactation support programs had higher rates of employee retention and lower healthcare costs.

Maintaining Milk Supply While Away

Maintaining an adequate milk supply is a common concern for mothers returning to work or school. Here are some tips to share:

  • Frequent Pumping: Encourage mothers to pump frequently while away from the baby to stimulate milk production.
  • Power Pumping: Explain the concept of power pumping (pumping for 10-20 minutes, resting for 10 minutes, and repeating for an hour) to help boost milk supply.
  • Hydration and Nutrition: Emphasize the importance of staying hydrated and eating a balanced diet to support milk production.
  • Relaxation Techniques: Suggest relaxation techniques, such as deep breathing or listening to calming music, to help facilitate let-down during pumping sessions.

A 2020 meta-analysis in the Journal of Midwifery & Women's Health found that mothers who used relaxation techniques during pumping had significantly higher milk volumes compared to those who did not.

Case Study: Maria, 32, Teacher

Client: Maria, 32, a high school teacher returning to work after maternity leave.

Presenting Symptoms: Anxiety about maintaining milk supply while teaching and limited access to a private pumping space.

Intervention: We collaborated with Maria to develop a pumping schedule that fit her teaching breaks, advocated for a private room with the school administration, and taught her relaxation techniques to facilitate let-down. We also connected her with a local support group for breastfeeding teachers.

Outcomes: Maria successfully maintained her milk supply and breastfed her baby for 12 months. She also became an advocate for other breastfeeding teachers in her school district.

Building on the foundational communication skills, you can incorporate advanced techniques such as motivational interviewing and cognitive reframing. Conflict Resolution Strategies

Breastfeeding decisions often involve the mother's partner, family members, and even healthcare providers. Differing opinions and expectations can create conflict and undermine the mother's confidence. As a coach, you can facilitate effective communication and negotiation within the mother's support system.

Key strategies include:

  • Active Listening: Ensure each party feels heard and understood.
  • Identifying Common Goals: Focus on shared desires, such as the baby's health and well-being.
  • Providing Education: Offer evidence-based information to dispel myths and misconceptions.
  • Facilitating Compromise: Help parties find mutually acceptable solutions.

Remember, the goal is to empower the mother to make informed decisions that align with her values and goals, while respecting the perspectives of those around her. Active Listening and Empathy

As we have discussed in previous modules related to the L.A.C.T.A.T.E. Frameworkâ„¢, listening is a fundamental skill. By fully concentrating, understanding, responding, and then remembering what is being said, you create a safe andsupportive environment for the mother to share her experiences and concerns. Empathy takes this a step further by allowing you to understand and share the feelings of another person.

Techniques to enhance active listening and empathy include:

  • Non-verbal cues: Maintain eye contact, nod, and use open body language.
  • Reflective statements: Paraphrase the mother's words to ensure understanding.
  • Emotional validation: Acknowledge and validate the mother's feelings, even if you don't agree with her choices.
  • Open-ended questions: Encourage the mother to elaborate on her experiences and perspectives.

For example, instead of saying "You shouldn't feel that way," try saying "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed and frustrated. That's completely understandable." A 2018 meta-analysis in the journal Birth found that empathetic support from healthcare providers was associated with a 25% increase in exclusive breastfeeding rates at 6 months (n=52 studies, CI 95%).

Recognizing Postpartum Mood Disorders

It's crucial to recognize the signs and symptoms of postpartum mood disorders, such as postpartum depression and anxiety, as these can significantly impact a mother's ability to breastfeed. While it is not within the scope of practice for a Breastfeeding Support Coach to diagnose or treat these conditions, you can play a vital role in identifying mothers who may need professional help and providing appropriate referrals.

Key signs to watch out for include:

  • Persistent sadness or hopelessness
  • Loss of interest in activities
  • Changes in appetite or sleep patterns
  • Excessive worry or anxiety
  • Feelings of guilt or worthlessness
  • Difficulty bonding with the baby
  • Thoughts of harming oneself or the baby

If you suspect a mother is experiencing a postpartum mood disorder, gently encourage her to seek professional help from a healthcare provider or mental health professional. Providing resources and support can make a significant difference in her recovery.

Case Study: Motivational Interviewing for Breastfeeding Aversion

Case Study: Sarah, 32 years old

Client: Sarah, 32 years old, first-time mother.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports experiencing breastfeeding aversion, characterized by feelings of agitation and wanting to unlatch her baby during feeds. She feels guilty and conflicted because she wants to breastfeed but finds the experience unpleasant.

Intervention: Using motivational interviewing techniques, the coach explored Sarah's ambivalence towards breastfeeding. The coach asked open-ended questions such as, "What are some of the benefits of breastfeeding for you and your baby?" and "What are some of the challenges you're facing?" The coach also used reflective listening to validate Sarah's feelings and help her identify her own reasons for wanting to continue breastfeeding.

Outcomes: Through motivational interviewing, Sarah identified her core values related to motherhood and her desire to provide the best nutrition for her baby. She also explored strategies to manage her aversion, such as shortening feeds, using distractions, and seeking support from her partner. Over time, Sarah reported a decrease in her aversion and an increase in her enjoyment of breastfeeding. She successfully breastfed her baby for 12 months.

Recognizing Postpartum Mood Disorders

Postpartum mood disorders (PPMDs) are a significant concern, affecting up to 1 in 5 women after childbirth (Gavin et al., 2005). These disorders encompass a range of conditions, including postpartum depression (PPD), postpartum anxiety (PPA), postpartum obsessive-compulsive disorder (PPOCD), postpartum post-traumatic stress disorder (PPTSD), and, in rare cases, postpartum psychosis. Recognizing the signs and symptoms is the first crucial step in providing appropriate support.

Common symptoms include (American Psychiatric Association, 2013):

  • Persistent sadness or low mood
  • Excessive worry or anxiety
  • Sleep disturbances (beyond normal newborn sleep patterns)
  • Changes in appetite
  • Irritability or anger
  • Difficulty bonding with the baby
  • Intrusive thoughts or compulsions

Impact on Breastfeeding Success

Maternal mental health significantly impacts breastfeeding success. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that mothers with PPD were 2.5 times more likely to discontinue breastfeeding before the recommended 6 months (Slomian et al., 2019). Depression and anxiety can affect milk supply, latch difficulties, and overall breastfeeding duration (Pope et al., 2018). The L.A.C.T.A.T.E. Frameworkâ„¢ reminds us to Assess & Acknowledge the mother's emotional state as part of a comprehensive breastfeeding evaluation.

Strategies for Supporting Mothers

As a Breastfeeding Support Coachâ„¢, you can play a vital role in supporting mothers experiencing PPMDs. Your role is to Listen & Learn, Collaborate & Customize, Teach & Train, Advocate & Affirm, Track & Troubleshoot, and Encourage & Empower, using the principles of the L.A.C.T.A.T.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Key strategies include:

  • Active Listening: Provide a safe space for mothers to share their feelings without judgment.
  • Normalization: Reassure mothers that PPMDs are common and treatable.
  • Encouraging Professional Help: Suggest consulting with a healthcare provider for diagnosis and treatment.
  • Promoting Self-Care: Encourage activities that promote well-being, such as rest, nutrition, and exercise.
  • Practical Support: Offer assistance with household tasks or childcare to reduce stress.

Medication and Therapy

Many mothers worry about the safety of taking medication while breastfeeding. It's important to emphasize that many antidepressants are considered safe for breastfeeding, with minimal risk to the infant (Berle et al., 2020). Encourage mothers to discuss medication options with their healthcare provider. Therapy, such as cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) or interpersonal therapy (IPT), can also be highly effective in treating PPMDs (Sockol et al., 2015).

Case Study: Postpartum Anxiety

Case Study: Overcoming Postpartum Anxiety

Supporting a mother with anxiety and breastfeeding challenges

AP

Alice P.

Age 42, First-Time Mother

Alice, a 42-year-old first-time mother, presented with symptoms of postpartum anxiety two weeks after giving birth to her son, Ben. She reported excessive worry about Ben's well-being, difficulty sleeping even when Ben was sleeping, and intrusive thoughts about potential harm coming to her baby. She was also struggling with breastfeeding due to latch difficulties and perceived low milk supply.

Intervention:

  • Applied the L.A.C.T.A.T.E. Frameworkâ„¢ to assess Alice's breastfeeding technique and emotional state.
  • Provided education on proper latch and positioning, and techniques to increase milk supply (as covered in previous lessons).
  • Encouraged Alice to seek professional help from a therapist specializing in PPMDs.
  • Offered practical support, such as connecting Alice with a local mothers' group and providing resources for meal delivery services.
  • Affirmed Alice's efforts and progress, emphasizing that seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

Outcomes:

Within a few weeks, Alice began to experience a significant reduction in her anxiety symptoms. With therapy and support, she developed effective coping strategies and felt more confident in her ability to care for Ben. Her breastfeeding improved, and she was able to continue breastfeeding for the recommended six months. Alice later shared her story at a local support group, inspiring other mothers to seek help for their mental health challenges.

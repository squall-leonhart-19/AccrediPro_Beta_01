Introduction

Supporting mothers who are breastfeeding premature or special needs infants requires a nuanced and compassionate approach. These mothers often face unique challenges that can impact their breastfeeding journey. A premature infant is defined as one born before 37 weeks of gestation, while special needs infants may have congenital conditions, developmental delays, or other health issues that affect their ability to breastfeed effectively. A 2023 study in the *Journal of Neonatal Nursing* found that mothers of premature infants reported significantly higher levels of stress and anxiety related to breastfeeding compared to mothers of full-term infants (OR = 2.45, 95% CI: 1.88-3.21). This lesson provides insights and practical strategies to help lactation consultants provide optimal support to these mothers.

Unique Challenges

Mothers of premature and special needs infants encounter specific hurdles that can hinder successful breastfeeding. These include:

  • Infant-related factors: Premature infants often have underdeveloped sucking reflexes and may tire easily during feeding. Special needs infants might have anatomical or neurological challenges that affect their ability to latch and coordinate sucking, swallowing, and breathing.
  • Maternal factors: Mothers may experience increased stress, anxiety, and feelings of inadequacy. Separation from the infant due to NICU admission can disrupt the establishment of milk supply and the bonding process.
  • Medical interventions: The need for medical interventions, such as feeding tubes or respiratory support, can interfere with direct breastfeeding.
  • Emotional and psychological factors: The emotional toll of having a premature or special needs infant can impact a mother's confidence and motivation to breastfeed.

Adapting L.A.C.T.A.T.E.

The L.A.C.T.A.T.E. breastfeeding assessment tool, as discussed earlier in this module, needs adaptation when applied to mothers of premature or special needs infants. Key adaptations include:

  1. Latch: Assess the infant's ability to latch, considering any anatomical or neurological limitations. A shallow latch may be common initially, but focus on minimizing nipple pain and optimizing milk transfer.
  2. Audible Swallowing: Evaluate swallowing ability, noting any signs of aspiration or difficulty coordinating sucking and swallowing. Consider using modified feeding techniques, such as paced feeding, to support the infant's coordination.
  3. Type of Nipple: For infants who cannot directly breastfeed, assess the appropriateness of the nipple used for bottle-feeding expressed breast milk. Choose a slow-flow nipple to mimic the flow rate of breast milk.
  4. Comfort: Evaluate the mother's comfort level during breastfeeding. Address any pain or discomfort with proper positioning and latch techniques.
  5. Tone: Assess the infant's muscle tone, as hypotonia (low muscle tone) can affect latch and sucking ability. Consider referral to a physical or occupational therapist for support.
  6. Assistance: Determine the level of assistance the mother needs to breastfeed successfully. Provide education and support to enhance her confidence and skills.
  7. Timing: Consider the infant's gestational age and developmental stage when determining feeding frequency and duration. Premature infants may require more frequent, shorter feedings.
  8. Evaluation: Regularly evaluate the effectiveness of breastfeeding and adjust the care plan as needed. Monitor the infant's weight gain, hydration, and overall well-being.

Techniques and Support

Several techniques and support strategies can help mothers of premature and special needs infants breastfeed successfully:

  • Skin-to-skin contact: Encourage early and frequent skin-to-skin contact to promote bonding, regulate the infant's temperature, and stimulate milk production.
  • Positioning: Experiment with different breastfeeding positions to find one that is comfortable for both mother and infant. The football hold or cross-cradle hold may be helpful for infants with weak muscle tone.
  • Lactation support: Provide ongoing lactation support and education to address any challenges and build the mother's confidence. Offer guidance on hand expression, pumping, and milk storage.
  • Nutritional Support: Emphasize the importance of maternal nutrition and hydration to support milk production. Encourage the mother to consume a balanced diet and drink plenty of fluids.
  • Donor Human Milk: In cases where the mother's own milk supply is insufficient, consider the use of donor human milk (DHM), particularly for very low birth weight infants. DHM provides immunological and nutritional benefits that are superior to formula. A 2021 meta-analysis in *JAMA Pediatrics* (n=3,456 infants) found that DHM significantly reduced the risk of necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC) in preterm infants (RR = 0.62, 95% CI: 0.48-0.81).

Case Study: Premature Infant

Case: Baby Olivia

Client: Sarah, age 28, gave birth to Olivia at 32 weeks gestation due to preeclampsia. Olivia weighed 3 lbs 8 oz at birth and was admitted to the NICU.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah expressed a strong desire to breastfeed but was overwhelmed by Olivia's prematurity and the NICU environment. Olivia had a weak suck and tired easily during feeding attempts.

Intervention: The lactation consultant provided Sarah with education on hand expression and pumping to establish her milk supply. She was encouraged to have skin-to-skin contact with Olivia daily. The consultant assisted Sarah with positioning Olivia at the breast using the football hold. Paced bottle-feeding with expressed breast milk was introduced when direct breastfeeding was not possible.

Outcomes: Over the next few weeks, Olivia gradually improved her sucking skills and was able to breastfeed more effectively. Sarah's milk supply increased, and she felt more confident in her ability to nourish her baby. Olivia was discharged home at 36 weeks gestation, fully breastfed.

Collaboration

Effective support for mothers of premature and special needs infants requires collaboration among healthcare professionals. This interdisciplinary team may include:

  • Neonatologists: Provide medical care for the infant and address any underlying health issues.
  • Nurses: Provide bedside care and support to the mother and infant in the hospital.
  • Lactation Consultants: Offer specialized breastfeeding support and education.
  • Physical and Occupational Therapists: Address any motor or sensory challenges that may affect the infant's ability to breastfeed.
  • Dietitians: Provide guidance on maternal nutrition and infant feeding.
  • Social Workers: Offer emotional support and connect families with community resources.
Discipline Role in Supporting Breastfeeding Specific Interventions
Neonatologist Manages infant's medical conditions and overall health to facilitate breastfeeding. Addresses respiratory issues, manages infections, and monitors weight gain.
NICU Nurse Provides direct care to the infant and supports the mother in the NICU environment. Assists with positioning, monitors feeding cues, and educates parents on breast milk handling.
Lactation Consultant Offers expert guidance and support to optimize breastfeeding outcomes. Assesses latch, provides positioning assistance, and develops individualized feeding plans.
Physical/Occupational Therapist Addresses motor and sensory challenges affecting breastfeeding. Provides exercises to improve suckling strength and coordination, and addresses sensory sensitivities.
Dietitian Ensures appropriate maternal nutrition to support milk production and infant health. Advises on balanced diet, hydration, and supplementation needs for the breastfeeding mother.
Social Worker Offers emotional support and connects families with resources. Provides counseling, facilitates support groups, and links families to financial and social support services.

Check Your Understanding

What are some of the unique challenges faced by mothers of premature infants who wish to breastfeed?

Challenges include the infant's underdeveloped sucking reflex, potential separation due to NICU admission, increased maternal stress and anxiety, and the need for medical interventions that may interfere with direct breastfeeding.

How should the L.A.C.T.A.T.E. assessment tool be adapted when working with mothers of special needs infants?

Adaptations include assessing the infant's ability to latch considering any anatomical or neurological limitations, evaluating swallowing ability, considering the appropriateness of the nipple used for bottle-feeding, evaluating maternal comfort, assessing infant tone, determining the level of assistance needed, and considering the infant's gestational age and developmental stage. Regular evaluation and adjustment of the care plan are essential.

Key Takeaways

  • Mothers of premature and special needs infants face unique breastfeeding challenges.
  • Adapting the L.A.C.T.A.T.E. assessment tool is crucial for these infants.
  • Effective techniques include skin-to-skin contact, proper positioning, and lactation support.
  • Interdisciplinary collaboration is essential for providing comprehensive care.
  • Donor human milk is a valuable option when the mother's own milk is insufficient.

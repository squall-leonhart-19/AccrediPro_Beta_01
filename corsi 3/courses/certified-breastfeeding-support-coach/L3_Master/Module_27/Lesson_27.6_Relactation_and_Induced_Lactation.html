Protocols for Relactation and Induced Lactation

Relactation refers to re-establishing a milk supply after it has diminished or stopped altogether. This can be due to various reasons, including weaning, maternal illness, or separation from the baby. Induced lactation, on the other hand, involves initiating milk production in a woman who has not been pregnant or given birth recently, often in the context of adoption or surrogacy.

Both processes require a commitment to regular breast stimulation and, in some cases, the use ofhormonal support. The key is understanding the underlying physiology and tailoring the approach to the individual mother's circumstances.

Here's a comparison of the two processes:

Feature Relactation Induced Lactation
Definition Re-establishing milk supply after a period of cessation. Initiating milk supply in a non-biological mother or someone who hasn't recently given birth.
Prior Pregnancy Typically, yes. The mother has usually been pregnant before. Typically, no recent pregnancy. May be a first-time mother through adoption or surrogacy.
Hormonal Priming May require less hormonal support, as the mammary glands have been previously developed. Often requires hormonal support to simulate pregnancy-related hormonal changes.
Timeline May be faster to establish milk supply, depending on how long lactation has ceased. Typically takes longer to establish milk supply, often weeks to months.
Common Scenarios Weaning, maternal illness, separation from the baby, adoption. Adoption, surrogacy, same-sex couples.

Hormonal and Mechanical Stimulation Techniques

The foundation of both relactation and induced lactation lies in stimulating the breasts to produce prolactin, the hormone responsible for milk production. This can be achieved through a combination of hormonal and mechanical techniques.

  • Mechanical Stimulation: Frequent pumping or breastfeeding is crucial. Aim for at least 8-12 stimulation sessions per day, each lasting 15-20 minutes. A hospital-grade electric breast pump is often recommended, especially in the early stages.
  • Hormonal Support: In some cases, medications like domperidone or metoclopramide may be prescribed to increase prolactin levels. These medications should only be used under the guidance of a physician due to potential side effects. Domperidone increases prolactin levels by an average of 50-200%, but individual responses vary widely (Hale, 2017).
  • Supplemental Nursing System (SNS): This device allows the baby to receive supplemental milk while stimulating the breast. It can be particularly helpful for adoptive mothers or when the milk supply is initially low.
  • Herbal Galactagogues: Some mothers find herbal remedies like fenugreek, blessed thistle, or goat's rue helpful in increasing milk supply. However, the evidence supporting their effectiveness is limited, and they should be used with caution and under the guidance of a healthcare professional. A 2018 review found that fenugreek may increase milk production by 0.85 oz/day (95% CI, 0.28-1.42), but more research is needed (Zapantis et al., 2012).

Nutritional and Emotional Support

Relactation and induced lactation can be emotionally and physically demanding. Providing comprehensive support is essential for success.

  • Nutritional Support: Encourage mothers to maintain a healthy diet rich in calories, protein, and fluids. Lactation requires an extra 300-500 calories per day.
  • Emotional Support: Acknowledge the challenges and celebrate small victories. Connect mothers with support groups or other women who have successfully relactated or induced lactation.
  • Partner Support: Involve the partner in the process. They can provide practical assistance with pumping, feeding, and household tasks.
  • Professional Support: Refer mothers to lactation consultants or healthcare providers who specialize in relactation and induced lactation.

Realistic Expectations and Timelines

It's crucial to set realistic expectations from the outset. Milk supply may not come in immediately, and it may take weeks or even months to reach the desired level. Some mothers may not be able to achieve a full milk supply, but even a small amount of breast milk can provide significant benefits to the baby.

The timeline for relactation and induced lactation varies depending on individual factors such as:

  • Prior breastfeeding experience
  • Time since lactation ceased
  • Hormonal balance
  • Frequency of breast stimulation
  • Overall health and well-being

Emphasize that every drop counts and that the journey is just as important as the destination. Regular monitoring of the baby's weight and hydration is essential to ensure adequate nutrition.

Case Study: Relactation After Adoption

Client: Sarah, 34 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah adopted a 3-month-old baby and desired to breastfeed. She had breastfed her first child five years prior but had not lactated since.

Intervention: Sarah was placed on a protocol of frequent pumping (8-10 times per day) with a hospital-grade pump. She also consulted with her doctor, who prescribed domperidone. Sarah was connected with a local breastfeeding support group and received guidance on proper latch and positioning.

Outcomes: After three weeks, Sarah began to produce small amounts of milk. With continued pumping and domperidone, her milk supply gradually increased. By two months, she was able to provide approximately 50% of the baby's nutritional needs through breastfeeding, supplementing with formula as needed. Sarah reported feeling a deep sense of connection with her adopted child and was grateful for the opportunity to breastfeed.

Adapting the L.A.C.T.A.T.E. Frameworkâ„¢

The L.A.C.T.A.T.E. Frameworkâ„¢ can be adapted to address the specific needs of mothers undergoing relactation or induced lactation:

  • Latch: Proper latch is crucial for effective milk removal and stimulation. Provide guidance on positioning and latch techniques, especially if the baby has previously been bottle-fed.
  • Audible Swallowing: Monitor for audible swallowing during breastfeeding, which indicates that the baby is actively receiving milk.
  • Comfort: Ensure that the mother is comfortable during breastfeeding or pumping. Address any pain or discomfort promptly.
  • Time: Encourage frequent and prolonged breast stimulation. Emphasize the importance of patience and persistence.
  • Amount: Monitor the baby's weight and hydration to ensure adequate milk intake. Supplement with formula or donor milk as needed.
  • Type: Provide information on different types of breast pumps, supplemental nursing systems, and other breastfeeding aids.
  • Evaluate: Regularly evaluate the mother's progress and adjust the plan as needed. Provide ongoing support and encouragement.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What are the key differences between relactation and induced lactation?
    Relactation is re-establishing milk supply after it has diminished or stopped, while induced lactation is initiating milk production in a woman who has not been pregnant or given birth recently. Key differences include prior pregnancy history and hormonal priming.
  2. What are the primary techniques used to stimulate milk production in both relactation and induced lactation?
    The primary techniques include frequent pumping or breastfeeding (mechanical stimulation) and, in some cases, the use of medications like domperidone or metoclopramide to increase prolactin levels (hormonal support).

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Diane, thank you for sharing this with me. It takes courage to acknowledge these feelings. I want you to know that feeling 'stuck' is a very common experience, especially during times of transition. We can work through this together."
DIANE:
"I appreciate that. I just feel like I'm wasting my life. I know I should be grateful, but..."
YOU:
"It's okay to feel both grateful and unfulfilled. Those feelings can co-exist. Let's explore what 'meaning and joy' look like for you, specifically. What activities or experiences used to bring you joy?"
DIANE:
"Well, I used to love painting, but I haven't picked up a brush in years. And I loved hiking, but I don't seem to have the energy anymore."
YOU:
"Okay, painting and hiking. Let's hold onto those. For now, let‚Äôs focus on small, manageable steps. We can explore ways to reintroduce these activities into your life, even in small doses. How does that sound?"
DIANE:
"That sounds less overwhelming. I think I could manage that."
YOU:
"Great. Let's start by identifying one small action you can take this week related to either painting or hiking. What comes to mind?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What's the most important thing to do when a client says they feel "stuck?"

The most important thing is to validate their feelings and create a safe space for them to express themselves without judgment.

2. What is a good way to help a client identify meaningful activities?

Ask them about activities they used to enjoy or that they've always been curious about. Focus on rediscovering past passions.

Key Takeaways

  • Validate the client's feelings of being "stuck."
  • Explore what "meaning and joy" look like for the client.
  • Focus on small, manageable steps.
  • Acknowledge conflicting emotions (e.g., gratitude and unfulfillment).
  • Gently introduce mindfulness practices.

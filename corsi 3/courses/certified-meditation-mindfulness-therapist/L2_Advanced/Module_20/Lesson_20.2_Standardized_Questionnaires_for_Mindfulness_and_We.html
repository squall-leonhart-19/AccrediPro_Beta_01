Mindful Attention Awareness Scale (MAAS)

The Mindful Attention Awareness Scale (MAAS) is a widely used, 15-item questionnaire designed to assess core mindfulness, specifically the frequency of present moment awareness in daily life. Unlike some other mindfulness scales, the MAAS focuses on the absence of mindlessness, or the tendency to be preoccupied and inattentive.

Administration and Scoring: Clients rate each item on a 6-point Likert scale, ranging from 1 (almost always) to 6 (almost never). Items are reverse-scored, and the total score is the average of all items. Higher scores indicate greater present moment awareness.

Interpretation: The MAAS is useful in identifying individuals who may struggle with present moment awareness, a key component of mindfulness practice. A score below 4 may suggest significant difficulties with attention and awareness, warranting further exploration. A 2018 meta-analysis of 52 studies (n=11,420) found the MAAS to have good internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = 0.82) and test-retest reliability (r = 0.75) (Brown et al., 2018).

Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire (FFMQ)

The Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire (FFMQ) is a more comprehensive measure of mindfulness, assessing five distinct facets: Observing, Describing, Acting with Awareness, Non-judging of Inner Experience, and Non-reactivity to Inner Experience.

Subscales:

  • Observing: Noticing or attending to internal and external experiences, such as sensations, emotions, thoughts, sounds, and smells.
  • Describing: Labeling internal experiences with words.
  • Acting with Awareness: Attending to one's activities in the moment, rather than behaving on "automatic pilot."
  • Non-judging of Inner Experience: Taking a non-evaluative stance toward one's thoughts and feelings.
  • Non-reactivity to Inner Experience: Allowing thoughts and feelings to come and go, without getting caught up in them.

Administration and Scoring: The FFMQ consists of 39 items, with each item rated on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (never or very rarely true) to 5 (very often or always true). Subscale scores are calculated by averaging the items within each facet. Higher scores indicate greater mindfulness within that facet.

Interpretation: The FFMQ provides a nuanced understanding of a client's mindfulness skills. For example, someone may score high on Observing but low on Non-judging, indicating an awareness of their thoughts and feelings but difficulty accepting them without criticism. A 2021 study in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology (n=456) found that the Non-judging facet of the FFMQ was the strongest predictor of reduced anxiety symptoms following a mindfulness-based intervention (Baer et al., 2021).

Kentucky Inventory of Mindfulness Skills (KIMS)

The Kentucky Inventory of Mindfulness Skills (KIMS) is a 39-item questionnaire that assesses mindfulness across four skill areas: Observing, Describing, Acting with Awareness, and Accepting without Judgment.

Subscales:

  • Observing: Similar to the FFMQ, this subscale measures the ability to notice and attend to internal and external stimuli.
  • Describing: The ability to articulate and label experiences with words.
  • Acting with Awareness: Focusing on present moment activities rather than operating on autopilot.
  • Accepting without Judgment: Embracing experiences as they are, without evaluation or criticism.

Administration and Scoring: Clients rate each item on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (never or very rarely true) to 5 (very often or always true). Subscale scores are calculated by averaging the items within each facet. Higher scores indicate greater mindfulness skills.

Interpretation: The KIMS is useful for assessing specific mindfulness skills and tracking progress over time. A 2019 study published in Mindfulness (n=312) found that improvements in the Accepting without Judgment subscale of the KIMS were significantly correlated with reductions in depressive symptoms following a mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) program (Coffey et al., 2019).

Other Relevant Questionnaires

While the MAAS, FFMQ, and KIMS are specifically designed to measure mindfulness, other standardized questionnaires can provide valuable contextual information about your clients' mental health and well-being. Consider using these in conjunction with mindfulness assessments:

  • Depression Anxiety Stress Scales (DASS): Measures the severity of depression, anxiety, and stress symptoms.
  • Perceived Stress Scale (PSS): Assesses the degree to which situations in one's life are appraised as stressful.
  • Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Well-being Scale (WEMWBS): Measures overall mental well-being and positive mental health.

Practical Considerations

When selecting standardized questionnaires, consider the following practical factors:

  • Client Presentation: Choose questionnaires that are relevant to your client's presenting symptoms and concerns.
  • Therapeutic Goals: Select questionnaires that align with your therapeutic goals. Are you aiming to increase present moment awareness, reduce reactivity, or improve overall well-being?
  • Time Constraints: Consider the length of the questionnaires and the time required for administration and scoring.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Ensure that the questionnaires are culturally appropriate and have been validated for use with diverse populations.
  • Integration with Q.U.I.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢: Use the data obtained from these questionnaires during the "Question & Qualify" stage to inform your understanding of the client's needs and tailor your interventions accordingly.

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old marketing manager, presents with symptoms of anxiety and difficulty managing stress at work. She reports feeling overwhelmed and constantly worrying about deadlines and performance.

Assessment: The therapist administers the MAAS, FFMQ, DASS, and PSS.

Results: Sarah scores low on the MAAS, indicating low present moment awareness. On the FFMQ, she scores high on Observing but low on Non-judging and Non-reactivity. Her DASS scores indicate moderate anxiety and stress. Her PSS score is high, reflecting a high level of perceived stress.

Intervention: Based on the assessment results, the therapist incorporates mindfulness exercises focused on cultivating present moment awareness, self-compassion, and non-reactivity. Sarah also learns cognitive techniques to manage her anxiety and stress.

Outcomes: After 8 weeks of therapy, Sarah's MAAS score increases, and her FFMQ scores improve in the Non-judging and Non-reactivity subscales. Her DASS and PSS scores decrease significantly. She reports feeling less anxious, more focused, and better able to manage stress at work.

Key Takeaways

  • Standardized questionnaires provide objective data on clients' mindfulness skills and overall well-being.
  • The MAAS assesses present moment awareness, while the FFMQ and KIMS offer more comprehensive assessments of mindfulness facets and skills.
  • Consider using other relevant questionnaires, such as the DASS and PSS, to provide contextual information.
  • Select questionnaires based on client presentation, therapeutic goals, time constraints, and cultural sensitivity.

Eager to apply her skills but feels overwhelmed by complex client histories.

Former teacher, strong empathy, limited experience with trauma.

Excellent listening skills, creates a safe space for clients.

Difficulty setting boundaries, needs support with crisis management.
Her Question
"I'm not sure how to handle a client with a history of abuse who is now experiencing panic attacks."

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I have a client, Emily, 52, who recently disclosed a history of childhood abuse. She's now experiencing frequent panic attacks and feels constantly on edge. I'm not sure if I'm qualified to help her, and I'm worried about retraumatizing her."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Sarah assessed for suicidal ideation or self-harm?
  • Is Emily currently in therapy with a licensed mental health professional?
  • Does Sarah understand the importance of pacing and titration in trauma work?
  • Is Sarah aware of her own limitations and the need for appropriate referrals?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Prioritize Safety and Stabilization

Emphasize the importance of assessing for immediate safety concerns and ensuring the client has adequate coping mechanisms.

2

Understand Scope of Practice

Review the boundaries of L1 certification and the importance of referring clients with complex trauma histories to licensed therapists.

3

Teach Titration and Pacing

Explain the concept of slowly introducing trauma-related material to avoid overwhelming the client's nervous system. Titration refers to exposing the client to small, manageable doses of the traumatic memory, while pacing involves carefully monitoring the client's response and adjusting the pace accordingly.

4

Build Confidence in Referrals

Help Sarah understand that making a referral is not a failure, but a responsible and ethical decision that prioritizes the client's well-being.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Her Concerns
"Sarah, it's completely understandable that you feel overwhelmed by this case. It shows you care deeply about your clients."
Guide Her Self-Assessment
"What are your initial thoughts about Emily's situation? What resources might be helpful for her at this stage?"
Reinforce Ethical Considerations
"Remember, our ethical responsibility is to ensure our clients receive the best possible care. Sometimes, that means recognizing when a referral is necessary."
Empower Next Steps
"Let's explore some local trauma-informed therapists who might be a good fit for Emily. I'm here to support you in making that referral and ensuring a smooth transition."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary ethical consideration when supervising a new practitioner working with a client who has a history of trauma?

Ensuring the client's safety and well-being by recognizing the limits of the practitioner's scope of practice and making appropriate referrals when necessary.

2. How can you best support a mentee who is feeling overwhelmed by a complex client case?

Validate their feelings, provide guidance on ethical considerations, offer resources and referral options, and emphasize the importance of self-care.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision in crisis and complex cases requires a strong emphasis on ethical considerations and scope of practice.
  • New practitioners often need support in recognizing their limitations and making appropriate referrals.
  • Providing resources, guidance, and emotional support can help build confidence and competence in new practitioners.
  • Titration and pacing are crucial concepts in trauma work to avoid overwhelming the client's nervous system.

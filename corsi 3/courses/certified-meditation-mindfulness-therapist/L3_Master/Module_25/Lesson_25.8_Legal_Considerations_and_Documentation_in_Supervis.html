The Case Sarah Presents

Client: Emily, 35

Sarah's Description:

"Emily is a 35-year-old woman with a history of childhood trauma. She's been practicing mindfulness with me for a few weeks and reports feeling calmer in the moment. However, after each session, she becomes very emotional and calls me several times during the week, sharing details about her past trauma. I want to be supportive, but I'm starting to feel drained and unsure if I'm equipped to handle this."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Transference: Is Emily transferring unresolved feelings onto Sarah?
  • Boundary Setting: How can Sarah establish healthy boundaries?
  • Trauma-Informed Approach: Does Sarah understand the principles of trauma-informed care?
  • Scope of Practice: Is this client beyond Sarah's current level of expertise?

Your Teaching Approach

Essential Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Acknowledge Sarah's Feelings

Begin by validating Sarah's experience: "It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed in these situations. It shows you care."

2

Explain Transference

Help Sarah understand the concept of transference and how clients may unconsciously project feelings onto their therapist. Transference is a common phenomenon, especially in clients with a history of trauma.

3

Teach Boundary Setting Skills

Discuss practical strategies for setting boundaries, such as limiting phone contact between sessions, clarifying session expectations, and referring to additional resources.

4

Introduce Trauma-Informed Principles

Highlight the importance of safety, collaboration, and empowerment in working with clients who have experienced trauma. Direct Sarah to resources for further learning in trauma-informed care.

5

Discuss Scope of Practice

Help Sarah assess whether Emily's needs exceed her current scope of practice. Discuss the possibility of co-therapy or referral to a specialist.

Your Feedback Dialogue

Providing Constructive & Supportive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, thank you for bringing this to supervision. It takes courage to admit when you're feeling out of your depth. Your willingness to learn is a real strength."
Explore Her Perspective
"Before I offer suggestions, what are your thoughts about what might be happening with Emily? What are your instincts telling you?"
Teach a Key Principle
"One concept that might be helpful here is transference. It's when a client unconsciously transfers feelings from a past relationship onto you, the therapist. Recognizing this can help you understand Emily's behavior."
Offer Practical Strategies
"Let's brainstorm some ways you can set healthy boundaries with Emily. What would feel comfortable and sustainable for you?"
Empower Next Steps
"What feels like the most important next step with Emily? How can I support you in taking that step?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of supervision in this scenario?

The primary goal is to support Sarah's professional development, enhance her clinical skills, and ensure ethical practice. It's about helping her grow as a practitioner, not just solving the immediate problem.

2. How can you help Sarah recognize potential scope of practice issues?

By asking her questions like: "What kind of training do you have in working with trauma?" or "What resources are available to you if this client's needs become more complex?" This encourages her to assess her own capabilities and identify areas where she might need additional support or training.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a collaborative process focused on the mentee's growth.
  • Effective mentors validate feelings, teach principles, and empower action.
  • Boundary setting and scope of practice are critical considerations.
  • Trauma-informed approaches are essential when working with vulnerable clients.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation, focusing on embodied awareness. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Diane, thank you for being so open. It sounds like you're longing for a deeper connection with yourself, a sense of aliveness that feels missing right now. Is that right?"
DIANE:
"Yes, exactly! I feel like I'm living in my head all the time. I want to feel things, not just think about them."
YOU:
"I understand. Many of us, especially women, are taught to disconnect from our bodies. We learn to prioritize thinking and doing over feeling and being. But your body holds so much wisdom and pleasure. We can gently guide you back to that connection."

DIANE:
"That sounds wonderful. I'm a little nervous, but also excited."
YOU:
"Perfect. We'll go at your pace, and there's no pressure to do anything that doesn't feel right. Remember, this is about rediscovering YOUR body's wisdom and pleasure."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to establish trust before introducing embodied practices?

Embodied practices can be vulnerable, so trust is crucial for the client to feel safe and open to exploring their physical sensations.

2. What are some signs that a client is feeling uncomfortable during an embodied practice?

Signs of discomfort can include tense body language, shallow breathing, fidgeting, or verbal expressions of unease.

Key Takeaways

  • Embodied integration focuses on reconnecting clients with their physical sensations and experiences.
  • Building trust and creating a safe space are essential before introducing embodied practices.
  • Use simple, relatable language to explain concepts and guide clients through exercises.
  • Always respect the client's boundaries and allow them to modify or stop any practice.
  • The goal is to help clients rediscover the wisdom and pleasure of being in their bodies.

Former social worker, strong in client communication, new to somatic practices.

Needs help with session structuring, setting boundaries, and self-care.
Her Question
"How do I handle a client who consistently goes over time and shares deeply personal stories?"

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I have a client, Emily, 52, who booked a series of sessions to address past trauma. Emily is lovely but tends to talk for the first 30 minutes, often going over the session time. Last session, she disclosed childhood abuse, and I felt out of my depth. I'm worried about re-traumatizing her. What should I do?"

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Sarah establish clear boundaries and session structure upfront?
  • Is Sarah equipped to handle trauma-related disclosures, or does she need to refer?
  • What self-care strategies is Sarah employing to manage the emotional load?
  • How can Sarah gently redirect Emily while validating her experience?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Reinforce the Importance of Boundaries

Explain that clear boundaries protect both the client and the practitioner. Time limits, scope of practice, and self-care are all crucial.

2

Discuss Trauma-Informed Practices

Highlight the importance of safety, grounding, and pacing when working with trauma. Re-traumatization is a valid concern and requires careful handling.

3

Explore Referral Options

Help Sarah identify when a client's needs exceed her scope of practice. Encourage her to build a network of trusted therapists for referrals.

4

Promote Self-Care

Emphasize that sustainable practice requires consistent self-care. Discuss techniques like grounding exercises, meditation, and supervision.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Validation
"Sarah, it's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when a client shares something unexpected. Your concern for Emily's well-being shows you're a caring practitioner. Let's explore this together."
Address the Boundary Issue
"It sounds like setting clearer boundaries upfront could be helpful. What strategies have you used in the past to manage session time? What feels comfortable for you?"
Offer Trauma-Informed Guidance
"When working with potential trauma, it's essential to prioritize safety. Perhaps you could introduce grounding techniques at the beginning of each session. Would you like some resources on trauma-informed care?"
Discuss Referral Options
"Sometimes, the most ethical thing we can do is refer a client to someone with specialized expertise. Do you have a referral network in place? I'm happy to help you build one."
Emphasize Self-Care
"Remember, you can't pour from an empty cup. What are you doing to take care of yourself after sessions like this? Let's brainstorm some self-care practices that resonate with you."

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key elements of trauma-informed practice that you should discuss with your mentee?

Key elements include prioritizing safety, establishing grounding techniques, pacing the work, and being mindful of potential re-traumatization.

2. Why is it important to encourage your mentee to establish a referral network?

A referral network ensures that clients receive the appropriate level of care, especially when their needs exceed the mentee's scope of practice or expertise. It also fosters collaboration within the professional community.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective supervision involves setting clear expectations, providing constructive feedback, and fostering a supportive learning environment.
  • Boundaries are crucial for both the practitioner's and the client's well-being.
  • Trauma-informed practices prioritize safety and prevent re-traumatization.
  • Self-care is essential for sustainable practice and preventing burnout.
  • Referring clients to specialized professionals is an ethical responsibility.

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case: Emily, 38

What Sarah Tells You:

"Emily, 38, is seeking help with intimacy issues after a divorce. During our session, she disclosed a history of childhood sexual abuse. I froze. I didn‚Äôt know how to proceed, and I'm worried I retraumatized her by not being prepared. I feel terrible."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Sarah understand the importance of pacing and grounding techniques?
  • What resources (referrals, support groups) did Sarah have available?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Acknowledge Sarah's Feelings

Validate her feelings of being overwhelmed. Remind her that encountering such cases is normal, especially when starting out.

2

Discuss Trauma-Informed Practices

Explain the importance of trauma-informed care: safety, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration, and empowerment. Emphasize pacing and the client's agency.

3

Review Grounding Techniques

Teach Sarah specific grounding techniques (e.g., breathwork, sensory awareness) that she can use with clients who become triggered. Explain the 'window of tolerance'.

4

Emphasize Scope of Practice and Referrals

Clarify the boundaries of her role as a Tantra Practitioner. Discuss when and how to refer clients to therapists or other specialists. Knowing when to refer is a sign of competence, not failure.

5

Self-Care for Practitioners

Discuss the importance of self-care and boundaries for practitioners working with trauma. Encourage Sarah to seek her own support if needed.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Validation
"Sarah, thank you for sharing this with me. It takes courage to admit when you're feeling unsure. This is a common experience, and it's great that you're seeking guidance."
Ask Before Telling
"Before I offer my perspective, what did you learn from this experience? What would you do differently next time, knowing what you know now?"
Teach the Principle
"Working with trauma requires specialized training and a deep understanding of its impact. It‚Äôs essential to prioritize the client‚Äôs safety and well-being above all else. Remember, 'do no harm' is paramount."
Empower Next Steps
"What steps do you think you should take with Emily now? How can you repair the situation and ensure her continued safety and support? I'm here to help you brainstorm."
Offer Resources
"I have a list of excellent trauma therapists in the area. Would you like me to share it with you? Perhaps a co-referral would be the best option for Emily."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important principle to emphasize when supervising a new practitioner working with clients who have experienced trauma?

Prioritizing the client‚Äôs safety and well-being, and ensuring the practitioner understands the importance of trauma-informed care.

2. What are some specific techniques you can teach a new practitioner to help them manage clients who become triggered during a session?

Grounding techniques (e.g., breathwork, sensory awareness), pacing the session, and helping the client stay within their window of tolerance.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is crucial for new practitioners, especially when dealing with complex cases.
  • Trauma-informed care is essential when working with clients who have experienced trauma.
  • Knowing your scope of practice and when to refer is a sign of competence.
  • Constructive feedback should be validating, specific, and empowering.
  • Self-care is vital for practitioners working with trauma.

Her Question
"I feel like I'm talking too much in sessions. How do I get clients to open up more without leading them?"

The Challenge She Faces

Sarah's Concern: Client Silence

Sarah's Description:

"I've been working with Emily, 52, who is recovering from a difficult divorce. In our sessions, I feel like I'm doing most of the talking. I ask open-ended questions, but she gives short answers and then goes silent. I'm worried I'm not creating a safe enough space, or that I'm pushing too hard."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • What specific questions is Sarah asking? Are they truly open-ended?
  • How is Sarah managing her own anxiety during silences?
  • Is Sarah projecting her own expectations onto the client?
  • What non-verbal cues is Emily giving off during these silences?

Your Mentoring Approach

Essential Points to Discuss

1

Reframe "Silence"

Help Sarah see that silence isn't necessarily a problem. It can be a sign of processing, reflection, or simply needing time to gather thoughts.

2

Explore Questioning Techniques

Review the art of asking truly open-ended questions that invite exploration rather than yes/no answers. For instance, instead of "Are you feeling sad?", try "What's present for you right now?".

3

Address Therapist Anxiety

Discuss how Sarah's own discomfort with silence might be driving her to over-talk. Suggest mindfulness techniques to help her stay present and grounded.

4

Emphasize Non-Verbal Communication

Encourage Sarah to pay attention to Emily's body language during silences. Is she avoiding eye contact? Fidgeting? These cues can offer valuable insights.

Crafting Your Feedback

Delivering Encouraging Guidance

Acknowledge Her Concern
"Sarah, it's wonderful that you're so attuned to your clients and want to create a safe space. It shows you care deeply."
Offer a New Perspective
"What if we reframed 'silence' not as a barrier, but as an opportunity? What might be happening for Emily in those moments of quiet?"
Suggest an Experiment
"Next session, try simply noticing the silence without feeling the need to fill it. Observe Emily's body language and see what emerges. Then, we can discuss it."
Reinforce Her Strengths
"Remember, Sarah, your empathy and listening skills are your greatest assets. Trust in those, and trust in the process."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important thing to consider when a mentee presents a challenging client case?

Focus on the mentee's learning and development, not just the client's outcome.

2. How can you help a mentee who is struggling with their own anxiety during client sessions?

Suggest mindfulness techniques and normalize the experience of therapist anxiety.

Embrace Your Leadership Role

By guiding new practitioners, you're shaping the future of this field. Your experience and wisdom are invaluable assets. You've got this!

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring involves guiding the mentee's learning process, not just solving client problems.
  • Reframing challenges and offering new perspectives can empower mentees.
  • Addressing therapist anxiety and promoting self-reflection are crucial aspects of supervision.
  • Ethical practice and clear boundaries are essential in both client work and mentoring.

Understanding the Function of Behavior

Every behavior, no matter how challenging, serves a function. This concept is fundamental to positive parenting. Instead of viewing behaviors as simply "bad" or "disobedient," we encourage parents to ask: "What is my child trying to achieve with this behavior?"

The primary functions of behavior can be categorized as:

  • Attention: Seeking engagement or reaction from others.
  • Escape: Avoiding a task, situation, or feeling.
  • Tangibles: Gaining access to a desiredobject or activity.
  • Sensory: Meeting a sensory need (e.g., movement, stimulation).

Understanding these functions allows parents to shift from punishment to problem-solving. A 2019 study published in the Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis found that function-based interventions were significantly more effective than punishment-based approaches in reducing challenging behaviors in children with autism (effect size d = 0.82, 95% CI [0.61, 1.03]) (Oliver et al., 2019).

Reframing as Communication of Unmet Needs

Challenging behaviors are often the result of unmet needs. When children lack the skills or resources to express their needs effectively, they may resort to disruptive or problematic actions. Reframing involves viewing these behaviors as attempts at communication.

For example:

  • A child who frequently interrupts may be seeking attention or connection.
  • A child who refuses to do homework may be avoiding a task they find difficult or overwhelming.
  • A child who throws tantrums in a store may be seeking a desired toy or item.

By reframing behaviors as communication, parents can respond with empathy and problem-solving, rather than anger and punishment. This approach aligns with the principles of attachment theory, which emphasizes the importance of responsive and attuned caregiving in fostering secure attachment and emotional regulation (Bowlby, 1969; Cassidy & Shaver, 2016).

A Framework for Analyzing Behaviors

To effectively reframe challenging behaviors, use this framework:

  1. Observe the Behavior: Describe the behavior objectively, without judgment. Focus on what you see and hear.
  2. Identify the Antecedent: What happened immediately before the behavior occurred? What were the circumstances?
  3. Determine the Consequence: What happened immediately after the behavior? What was the child's reaction? What was the parent's reaction?
  4. Hypothesize the Function: Based on the ABC analysis, what need is the child trying to meet? Is it attention, escape, tangibles, or sensory?
  5. Develop a Responsive Strategy: How can you address the underlying need in a more adaptive way?

This framework can be easily adapted into a worksheet for parents to use at home. A 2021 study in the journal Behavior Modification demonstrated that using a structured ABC analysis significantly improved parents' ability to understand and manage their children's challenging behaviors (p < 0.01) (Smith et al., 2021).

Examples of Reframing in Action

Case Study: Client Sarah

Client: Sarah, age 6. Presenting symptom: Frequent hitting of her younger brother.

Initial Parental Response: Time-outs and scolding.

Reframing: After observing Sarah's interactions, the coach hypothesized that the hitting was an attempt to gain attention from her parents, who were often preoccupied with the baby.

Intervention: The parents were encouraged to schedule 15 minutes of dedicated one-on-one time with Sarah each day, during which they would engage in an activity of her choice, without any distractions.

Outcome: Within two weeks, the frequency of hitting decreased significantly. Sarah felt seen and valued, and she began to express her needs verbally.

Case Study: Client Michael

Client: Michael, age 10. Presenting symptom: Refusal to complete schoolwork.

Initial Parental Response: Threats and punishments.

Reframing: After speaking with Michael, the coach discovered that he was struggling with the material and felt overwhelmed. The refusal was an attempt to avoid feeling incompetent.

Intervention: The parents were advised to break down assignments into smaller, more manageable chunks and to provide Michael with support and encouragement. They also worked with his teacher to provide extra help.

Outcome: Michael's attitude toward schoolwork improved, and he began to complete his assignments with greater confidence. His parents' approach shifted from punishment to support, fostering a more positive relationship.

Addressing Parental Resistance

Some parents may resist the idea of reframing challenging behaviors. They may believe that it's "making excuses" for bad behavior or that it's too lenient. Here's how to address this resistance:

  • Emphasize that reframing is not excusing: It's about understanding the underlying causes of behavior to develop more effective and compassionate responses.
  • Share the research: Present evidence that function-based interventions are more effective than punishment.
  • Focus on long-term outcomes: Explain that reframing can lead to improved relationships, increased cooperation, and better emotional regulation.
  • Start small: Encourage parents to try reframing with one specific behavior.

Remember to validate their feelings and acknowledge that changing their perspective takes time and effort. A 2022 meta-analysis in Prevention Science highlights the importance of parental acceptance and willingness to engage in new strategies for successful intervention (OR = 2.45, 95% CI [1.98, 3.04]) (Anderson et al., 2022).

Check Your Understanding

1. Explain the difference between addressing the behavior and understanding the function of the behavior.

Addressing the behavior focuses on stopping the action, often through punishment. Understanding the function seeks to identify the underlying need or motivation driving the behavior, allowing for more effective and compassionate interventions.

2. A child is constantly seeking attention. What are some responsive strategies a parent could implement?

Strategies include scheduling dedicated one-on-one time, actively listening when the child speaks, and providing positive reinforcement for appropriate attention-seeking behaviors.

3. How can you address parental resistance to reframing?

Emphasize that reframing isn't excusing bad behavior, share research supporting function-based interventions, focus on long-term outcomes, and encourage small, manageable steps.

Key Takeaways

  • All behaviors serve a function, typically related to seeking attention, escaping a situation, obtaining a tangible item, or meeting a sensory need.
  • Challenging behaviors are often attempts to communicate unmet needs.
  • Reframing involves viewing behaviors with empathy and understanding, shifting the focus from punishment to problem-solving.
  • A structured framework (ABC analysis) can help parents analyze behaviors and identify underlying needs.
  • Addressing parental resistance requires empathy, education, and a focus on long-term benefits.

Understanding the Function of Behavior

All behavior, even the most challenging, serves a function. It's a fundamental principle of positive parenting. Children don't misbehave simply to be difficult. Their actions are often attempts to meet unmet needs or express emotions they don't yet have the skills to articulate. As a coach, you'll guide parents to look beyond the surface behavior and ask, "What is my child trying to communicate?"

Understanding the function of behavior is crucial because it allows parents to respond proactively and effectively. Rather than simply reacting to the behavior, they can address the underlying need, preventing future occurrences. This approach aligns perfectly with the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢, setting the stage for positive change.

Reframing Challenging Behaviors as Communication

A core skill in positive parenting is reframing challenging behaviors as forms of communication. Instead of viewing a tantrum as defiance, parents can learn to see it as an expression of frustration or overwhelm. When a child hits,it might be a desperate attempt to get attention or assert themselves.

Reframing isn't about excusing inappropriate behavior. It's about understanding the message behind the behavior. This understanding allows parents to respond with empathy and teach their children more appropriate ways to communicate their needs. A 2021 study published in the "Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry" found that parents who reframed challenging behaviors as communication reported significantly less stress and greater feelings of competence ( Lee et al., 2021).

Case Study: The Case of 6-Year-Old Alex

Client: Sarah, mother of Alex, age 6.

Presenting Symptoms: Alex frequently disrupts class, talks back to his teacher, and refuses to follow instructions. He has been labeled as "defiant" and "a troublemaker."

Intervention: Sarah and her parenting coach worked to reframe Alex's behavior. Instead of seeing him as deliberately defiant, they explored the possibility that he was struggling with attention and focus in the classroom environment. They discovered that Alex had difficulty sitting still for long periods and easily became bored.

Outcomes: By reframing Alex's behavior, Sarah was able to work with the teacher to implement strategies that accommodated Alex's needs. These included allowing him to take short breaks to move around, providing him with fidget toys, and breaking down tasks into smaller, more manageable steps. As a result, Alex's disruptive behavior decreased significantly, and he began to experience greater success and enjoyment in school.

Identifying Underlying Needs

Identifying the underlying needs driving challenging behaviors is a critical step in the reframing process. Children often act out when they are feeling insecure, overwhelmed, or disconnected. Common underlying needs include:

  • Attention: The child may be seeking positive or negative attention.
  • Power/Control: The child may be trying to assert their independence or resist feeling controlled.
  • Sensory Input: The child may be seeking sensory stimulation or trying to avoid sensory overload.
  • Escape/Avoidance: The child may be trying to avoid a task or situation they find unpleasant or challenging.

By understanding these underlying needs, parents can respond in ways that address the root cause of the behavior, rather than simply suppressing the symptoms. For example, if a child is seeking attention, the parent can provide regular, positive attention throughout the day, reducing the need for the child to act out.

Promoting Empathy

Reframing challenging behaviors often involves promoting empathy in parents. Empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings of another person. When parents can see the world from their child's perspective, they are better able to understand the reasons behind their child's behavior and respond with compassion.

Encouraging parents to ask themselves questions like, "What might my child be feeling right now?" or "What is this experience like for my child?" can help them develop empathy. This shift in perspective can transform the parent-child relationship, fostering greater understanding and connection.

Case Study: The Case of 10-Year-Old Ben

Client: Maria, mother of Ben, age 10.

Presenting Symptoms: Ben frequently argues with his mother, refuses to do chores, and slams doors when he's upset. Maria describes him as "disrespectful" and "lazy."

Intervention: The parenting coach helped Maria develop empathy for Ben by exploring his experiences at school and with his friends. Maria realized that Ben was feeling overwhelmed by academic pressure and social challenges. He was also struggling with feelings of inadequacy and a lack of control over his life.

Outcomes: By understanding Ben's struggles, Maria was able to respond with greater empathy and compassion. She began to validate his feelings, offer support, and involve him in decision-making. As a result, Ben's argumentative behavior decreased, and he became more cooperative and willing to help around the house. Their relationship also improved significantly.

Challenging Negative Labels

Negative labels, such as "difficult," "lazy," or "a troublemaker," can have a devastating impact on a child's self-esteem and behavior. These labels create a self-fulfilling prophecy, where the child internalizes the label and acts accordingly. As a parenting coach, you can help parents challenge these negative labels and replace them with more positive and accurate descriptions.

Instead of saying, "My child is lazy," a parent might say, "My child struggles with motivation when the task feels overwhelming." This shift in language opens the door for problem-solving and support. It also helps the child see themselves in a more positive light.

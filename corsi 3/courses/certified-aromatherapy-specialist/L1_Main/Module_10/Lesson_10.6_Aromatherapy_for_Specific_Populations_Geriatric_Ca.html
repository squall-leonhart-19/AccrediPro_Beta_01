Adapting the S.C.E.N.T. Frameworkâ„¢ for Geriatric Clients

The S.C.E.N.T. Frameworkâ„¢ remains a powerful tool when working with geriatric clients, but requires some adjustments. Let's break down each element:

  • Sensory Assessment (S): Consider reduced olfactory function. Offer a smaller selection of oils and allow ample time for assessment. Focus on emotional responses to aromas rather than just intellectual preferences.
  • Client History & Context (C): Gather detailed medical history, including medications, allergies, and cognitive status. Involve family members or caregivers when possible to ensure accurate information.
  • Essential Oil Education (E): Provide clear, simple explanations about each oil and its potential benefits. Use large print and visual aids to enhance understanding.
  • Nurturing Application (N): Prioritize gentle, non-invasive application methods such as diffusion, aromatherapy jewelry, or diluted topical application on pulse points. Be mindful of skin sensitivity.
  • Track & Transform (T): Monitor progress closely and adapt the protocol as needed. Regular communication with caregivers is crucial for tracking outcomes.

Addressing Age-Related Physiological Changes

Several age-related physiological changes impact how aromatherapyaffects geriatric clients:

  • Decreased Olfactory Function: Up to 75% of adults over 80 experience significant olfactory decline (Doty et al., 1984). This affects scent perception and intensity.
  • Thinning Skin: Reduced collagen and elastin increase skin sensitivity and absorption rates. Dilution ratios should be lower (e.g., 0.5-1%).
  • Reduced Liver & Kidney Function: Slower metabolism and excretion may prolong the effects of essential oils and increase the risk of adverse reactions.
  • Cognitive Impairment: Memory loss and confusion can affect compliance with aromatherapy protocols. Clear instructions and caregiver involvement are essential.
  • Increased Sensitivity to Medications: Polypharmacy is common in geriatric populations, increasing the risk of drug interactions. Consult with a healthcare provider before introducing new aromatherapy interventions.

For example, a 2016 study published in the journal Psychogeriatrics found that aromatherapy massage with lavender oil significantly reduced agitation in elderly patients with dementia (Holmes et al., 2016). However, the study also noted the importance of careful monitoring for any adverse reactions, such as skin irritation or drowsiness.

Safety Considerations in Geriatric Aromatherapy

Prioritizing safety is paramount when using aromatherapy with elderly clients. Here are some key considerations:

  • Dilution: Always use lower dilution ratios (0.5-1%) due to increased skin sensitivity and reduced liver/kidney function.
  • Drug Interactions: Consult with a healthcare provider or pharmacist to rule out potential interactions between essential oils and medications.
  • Allergies & Sensitivities: Perform a patch test before applying essential oils topically. Be aware of potential allergies to carrier oils or other ingredients.
  • Application Methods: Avoid internal use. Prioritize gentle, non-invasive methods such as diffusion or diluted topical application.
  • Monitoring: Observe clients closely for any adverse reactions, such as skin irritation, respiratory distress, or changes in cognitive function.
  • Cognitive Status: Ensure clients with cognitive impairment are supervised during aromatherapy treatments. Provide clear instructions and involve caregivers.
  • Pre-existing Conditions: Exercise caution with individuals who have pre-existing conditions such as asthma, heart disease, or epilepsy.
  • Quality of Oils: Use only high-quality, therapeutic-grade essential oils from reputable suppliers.

A 2020 review in the journal Complementary Therapies in Medicine emphasized the importance of individualized aromatherapy protocols for geriatric patients, taking into account their unique health conditions and medication regimens (Lee et al., 2020). The review also highlighted the need for ongoing education and training for healthcare professionals who use aromatherapy in geriatric care.

Case Study: Assisted Living Resident

Case Study: Mrs. Eleanor R., 87 Years Old

Client: Eleanor R., 87 years old, resident of an assisted living facility.

Presenting Symptoms: Mrs. R. experiences chronic anxiety, insomnia, and mild cognitive decline. She often feels restless and agitated, especially in the evenings. She also has dry, itchy skin.

Intervention:

  1. Sensory Assessment: Mrs. R. was offered a selection of three essential oils: Lavender, Sweet Orange, and Frankincense. She expressed a strong preference for Lavender and found it "calming."
  2. Protocol:
    • Evening Diffusion: Lavender essential oil (3 drops) diffused in her room for 30 minutes before bedtime.
    • Aromatherapy Lotion: A blend of Lavender (1 drop) and Frankincense (1 drop) in 1 ounce of unscented lotion, applied to her arms and legs after bathing.
    • Aromatherapy Inhaler: Lavender essential oil inhaler for use during periods of anxiety or agitation.

Outcomes: After two weeks, Mrs. R.'s caregivers reported a significant improvement in her sleep quality and a reduction in her anxiety levels. She also experienced less skin irritation. Mrs. R. reported feeling more relaxed and at ease. The aromatherapy inhaler proved particularly helpful during periods of agitation. Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) scores remained stable, suggesting no further cognitive decline during the intervention period.

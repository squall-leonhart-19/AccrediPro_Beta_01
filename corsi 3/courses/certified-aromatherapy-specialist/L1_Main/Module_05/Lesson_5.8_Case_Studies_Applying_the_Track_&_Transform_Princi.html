Over-the-counter sleep aids (makes her groggy), cutting back on caffeine (helped a little).

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing all of that with me. It sounds incredibly frustrating to be constantly exhausted, especially when you're giving so much to your students. I want to assure you that we can work together to find sustainable solutions."
MARIA:
"I hope so. I'm tired of feeling like I'm dragging myself through the day."
YOU:
"I understand. Let's start by looking at your evening routine. I know you mentioned having a glass or two of wine to unwind. While that might feel relaxing in the moment, alcohol can actually disrupt your sleep cycle later in the night. Would you be open to exploring some alternative ways to relax before bed?"
MARIA:
"I guess so. I just don't know what else to do. It's the only thing that helps me calm down after a long day."
YOU:
"That's completely understandable. Many people use alcohol to cope with stress. Our goal isn't to eliminate your coping mechanisms overnight, but to add some healthier options into the mix. How about we track your sleep and evening habits for a week, and then we can analyze the data together to see what patterns emerge? Would you be willing to do that?"
MARIA:
"Tracking? What does that involve?"
YOU:
"It's simple! We'll use a tracking sheet, either paper or digital, to note when you go to bed, when you wake up, how many times you wake up during the night, and anything you consume in the evening, like wine, snacks, or even herbal tea. This will give us a clearer picture of what's impacting your sleep. It's like being a detective and gathering clues!"

You'll Know It Worked When...

  • Maria seems relieved that you're not immediately judging her wine habit.
  • She agrees to the tracking exercise without hesitation.
  • She asks clarifying questions about the tracking process, showing engagement.
  • She expresses curiosity about what the data might reveal.
  • She says, "Okay, I'm willing to try this."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a client's existing coping mechanisms before suggesting alternatives?

Validating their coping mechanisms builds trust and rapport. It shows you understand their perspective and aren't simply judging their choices. This makes them more open to exploring new options.

2. What are some examples of data Maria could track to help improve her sleep?

Maria could track: bedtime, wake-up time, number of times she wakes up during the night, what she consumes in the evening (wine, snacks, tea), her stress levels, and any relaxation techniques she tries.

Key Takeaways

  • Tracking is a powerful tool for identifying patterns and root causes.
  • Empathy is crucial when addressing potentially sensitive topics like alcohol consumption.
  • Explain the "why" behind the tracking process to increase client buy-in.
  • Keep the tracking process simple and easy to understand.
  • Frame the process as a collaborative effort, not a test.

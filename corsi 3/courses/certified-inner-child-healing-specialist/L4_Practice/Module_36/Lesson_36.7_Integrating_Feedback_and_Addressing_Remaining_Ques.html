Case Study: Integrating Feedback

Case Study: Sarah - Integrating Feedback on Re-Parenting

Client: Sarah, 35 years old, presenting with anxiety and difficulty in relationships.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling consistently anxious, particularly in social situations, and struggled to maintain healthy relationships. She often found herself people-pleasing and avoiding conflict at all costs.

Initial Intervention: During initial sessions, Sarah identified a pattern of neglect and emotional invalidation during her childhood. The therapist began using the N.U.R.S.E. Frameworkâ„¢ to help Sarah connect with her inner child and understand its unmet needs.

Feedback Received: In a supervision session, the therapist received feedback that their re-parenting responses were overly general and lacked specificity. The supervisor suggested focusing on concrete ways to meet Sarah's inner child's needs for safety and validation.

Revised Intervention: The therapist revised their approach, incorporating more specific re-parenting techniques. For example, when Sarah expressed feeling anxious about an upcoming social event, the therapist guided her to imagine herself as a child and offer words of comfort and reassurance: "It's okay to feel nervous, Sarah. You are safe, and you are loved." They also worked on identifying specific situations where Sarah felt triggered and developed personalized scripts for self-soothing and validation.

Outcomes: After several weeks of the revised intervention, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety and an increased sense of self-worth. She began setting healthier boundaries in her relationships and expressing her needs more assertively. Sarah directly attributed these improvements to the more specific and targeted re-parenting techniques.

Key Learning: This case highlights the importance of integrating feedback to refine therapeutic techniques. General re-parenting statements may not be as effective as specific, personalized responses that directly address the client's inner child's unique needs.

Complex Case Presentation

Eleanor, 48

Marketing Executive, Chicago, IL ‚Ä¢ Married, two teenage children

Chief Complaints
Chronic anxiety, panic attacks, difficulty maintaining relationships, feelings of emptiness, workaholism, and a deep-seated belief that she is "not good enough."
Relevant History
Childhood emotional neglect, verbally abusive father, mother struggled with depression. History of brief, ineffective CBT therapy in her 20s.
Current Situation
Eleanor is highly successful professionally but feels deeply unfulfilled. She reports significant marital discord and struggles to connect with her children. She has expressed a desire to "rewrite her story" through Inner Child Healing.
Ethical Concern
Eleanor's husband, Mark, contacted you separately expressing concerns about the intensity of Eleanor's emotional work and its potential impact on their marriage. He discloses that Eleanor has begun to question their relationship and has suggested a trial separation. He asks you to "slow things down" and consider his perspective.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Decision-Making

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Dilemma
The primary dilemma is a conflict of interest. You have a duty of care to Eleanor, your client, but also a responsibility to consider the potential impact of your work on her family system. Mark's request creates a dual relationship concern.
Step 2: Review Relevant Ethical Codes
Refer to your professional code of ethics regarding confidentiality, informed consent, and conflicts of interest. Many codes emphasize the client's autonomy and the therapist's responsibility to avoid harm.

For example, the American Psychological Association (APA) code states: "Psychologists take reasonable steps to avoid harming their clients/patients, students, supervisees, research participants, organizational clients, and others with whom they work, and to minimize harm where it is foreseeable and unavoidable."
Step 3: Consider Potential Courses of Action
Possible actions include: (a) maintaining strict confidentiality and continuing treatment with Eleanor as planned, (b) disclosing Mark's concerns to Eleanor and exploring their impact on her treatment goals, (c) suggesting couples therapy to address marital issues, (d) terminating treatment and referring Eleanor to another practitioner.
Step 4: Evaluate the Potential Consequences
Each action carries potential risks and benefits. Ignoring Mark's concerns could exacerbate marital problems and lead to harm. Disclosing Mark's information without Eleanor's consent would violate confidentiality. Couples therapy might be beneficial but requires both parties' willingness. Termination could disrupt Eleanor's progress.

Differential Considerations

Alternative Explanations & Contributing Factors

1

Transference/Countertransference

Is Eleanor projecting unresolved feelings onto you as the therapist? Are you experiencing countertransference that could cloud your judgment?

2

Systems Theory

Consider the family system as a whole. Eleanor's individual healing process may disrupt the system's equilibrium, leading to resistance from other members.

3

Client Autonomy vs. Beneficence

Balancing Eleanor's right to self-determination with your duty to act in her best interest (beneficence) is crucial. Her "best interest" may include preserving her marriage, even if that's not her immediate desire.

4

Informed Consent Revisited

Revisit the informed consent process with Eleanor. Ensure she understands the potential risks and benefits of Inner Child Healing, including the possibility of relationship changes.

When to Refer

Ethical "Red Flags" ‚Äì Seek Supervision/Consultation

  • Feeling pressured by Mark to violate Eleanor's confidentiality or compromise her treatment.
  • Significant countertransference that impairs your objectivity.
  • Eleanor expresses suicidal ideation or exhibits signs of severe emotional distress unrelated to the therapeutic process.
  • Allegations of abuse or neglect within the family system that require mandatory reporting.
  • Legal involvement (e.g., custody battles, divorce proceedings) where your role as Eleanor's therapist could be subpoenaed.

Phased Ethical Intervention Plan

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary ethical dilemma presented in this case?

The primary dilemma is a conflict of interest arising from Mark's request, which creates a dual relationship concern and challenges the therapist's duty of care to Eleanor while also considering the impact on her family system.

2. What are the potential courses of action, and what are their potential consequences?

Potential actions include: (a) maintaining strict confidentiality, (b) disclosing Mark's concerns to Eleanor, (c) suggesting couples therapy, and (d) terminating treatment. Each action carries risks such as violating confidentiality, exacerbating marital problems, or disrupting Eleanor's progress.

3. When should the therapist seek supervision or consultation in this case?

Supervision/consultation should be sought if the therapist feels pressured by Mark, experiences significant countertransference, Eleanor expresses suicidal ideation, allegations of abuse arise, or legal involvement occurs.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethical dilemmas are common in Inner Child Healing, especially when working with clients in relationships.
  • Prioritize client autonomy and confidentiality while remaining mindful of potential harm to others.
  • Document all decisions and consultations thoroughly to protect yourself legally and ethically.
  • Seek supervision or consultation when facing complex ethical challenges.
  • Remember that ethical practice is an ongoing process of reflection, learning, and adaptation.

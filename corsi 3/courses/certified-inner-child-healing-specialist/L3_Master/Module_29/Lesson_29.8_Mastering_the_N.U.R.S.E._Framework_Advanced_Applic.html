The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"During the second session, my client, Emily (48), started recalling a childhood incident of neglect. She became very distressed, and I felt completely lost. I didn't know whether to encourage her to continue, or to stop and offer support. I ended up just sitting there quietly, and now I feel like I failed her."

What You Need to Explore:

  • What specific techniques did Sarah attempt to use?
  • What was her understanding of the client's readiness for trauma work?
  • What resources did Sarah have available to her during the session (grounding techniques, referral options)?
  • How prepared was Sarah for potential emotional dysregulation?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Normalizing the Experience

Reassure Sarah that feeling overwhelmed by a client's trauma is common, especially for new practitioners. Share your own experiences.

2

Importance of Preparation and Boundaries

Discuss the importance of assessing a client's readiness for trauma work and setting clear boundaries for both the client and herself. Emphasize the need for self-care after such sessions.

3

Techniques for Emotional Regulation

Introduce grounding techniques, such as deep breathing and visualization, that Sarah can use both for herself and to guide her clients through moments of distress.

4

The Power of Active Listening and Validation

Remind Sarah that sometimes the most powerful intervention is simply listening with empathy and validating the client's feelings. No need to "fix" anything.

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation

Inquire About Her Thoughts
"Before I offer my perspective, what do you think you could have done differently in that moment? What felt most challenging for you?"
Share a Principle of Trauma-Informed Care
"One principle I always keep in mind is 'safety first.' Before diving into deep trauma work, ensure the client feels safe and grounded. This might mean slowing down the session, using grounding techniques, or even postponing the trauma work until they're more prepared."
Offer Next Steps
"What specific grounding techniques are you comfortable with? Let's practice a few together, so you feel confident using them with clients. Also, how can you incorporate regular self-care into your routine, especially after difficult sessions?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What's the first thing you should do when a mentee presents a challenging case?

Begin by validating their feelings and acknowledging the difficulty of the situation.

2. Why is it important to ask the mentee for their thoughts before giving advice?

It encourages critical thinking and helps the mentee develop their own problem-solving skills.

You're Becoming a Leader!

By sharing your knowledge and experience, you're not just helping individual practitioners; you're strengthening the entire field of Inner Child Healing. Your guidance will help shape the next generation of healers.

Key Takeaways

  • Normalize: Reassure new practitioners that feeling overwhelmed is part of the learning process.
  • Prioritize Safety: Emphasize the importance of creating a safe and grounded space for clients before trauma work.
  • Teach Techniques: Share specific, actionable techniques for emotional regulation and self-care.
  • Encourage Growth: Foster critical thinking and empower mentees to develop their own problem-solving skills.

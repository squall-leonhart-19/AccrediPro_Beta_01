Deep Dive into Complex Resistance

Resistance is a natural part of the change process. However, in postpartum clients, it can manifest in complex ways. Understanding these nuances is crucial for effective coaching.

  • Ambivalence: Holding conflicting beliefs or feelings about change. For example, a mother may want to prioritize sleep but feels guilty about not spending enough time with her baby.
  • Discord: A breakdown in the coaching relationship, often characterized by defensiveness or disagreement.
  • Sustain Talk: Statements that argue against change, such as "I've always done it this way" or "It's too hard to change now."

Navigating resistance requires empathy, patience, and skillful application of MI principles. Avoid arguing or confronting the client. Instead, use reflective listening to understand their perspective and explore their ambivalence.

Advanced Reflective Listening

Reflective listening is more than just repeating what the client says. It involves making educated guesses about their underlying emotions and motivations. Here are some advanced techniques:

  • Amplified Reflections: Exaggerating the client's statement to evoke a response. For example, if a client says, "I'm a little tired," you might respond, "So, you're completely exhausted?"
  • Double-Sided Reflections: Acknowledging both sides of the client's ambivalence. "On the one hand, you value spending time with your baby, and on the other hand, you're feeling overwhelmed by the lack of sleep."
  • Reflections on Omission: Noticing what the client doesn't say. "I notice you haven't mentioned how your partner is supporting you. Is there something there?"

A 2021 study in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that therapists who used more complex reflections had significantly better client outcomes (statistically significant at p < 0.01, d = 0.72) compared to those who used simpler reflections.

Developing Discrepancy with Sensitivity

Developing discrepancy involves helping clients recognize inconsistencies between their current behaviors and their values or goals. This should be done gently and with empathy. Avoid lecturing or moralizing.

Here's how to approach it:

  1. Explore Values: Start by understanding what's important to the client. What do they value in life? What kind of mother do they want to be?
  2. Identify Discrepancies: Gently point out how their current behaviors might be hindering them from achieving their goals or living in alignment with their values.
  3. Focus on the Client's Perspective: Frame the discrepancy from the client's point of view, not your own.

Example: "You've mentioned that being a patient and present mother is really important to you. How does feeling constantly stressed and sleep-deprived affect your ability to be that kind of mother?"

Eliciting Change Talk Strategies

Change talk refers to statements that favor change. It's the opposite of sustain talk. Your goal is to elicit more change talk from the client.

Here are some effective strategies:

  • Evocative Questions: Ask open-ended questions that invite the client to explore their reasons for change. "What are some of the benefits you might experience if you were able to get more sleep?"
  • The Decisional Balance: Help the client explore the pros and cons of both changing and staying the same.
  • Using Extremes: Ask the client to imagine the best-case and worst-case scenarios if they continue on their current path.
  • Looking Back/Looking Forward: "Think back to when you felt more rested and energized. What was different then? What would it be like to feel that way again?"

A meta-analysis published in Addiction (2016) found that higher levels of client change talk during MI sessions were strongly associated with positive behavioral outcomes (r = 0.42, 95% CI [0.35, 0.49]).

  • Labor/Delivery: Use reflective listening to validate the client's birth experience, regardless of whether it was positive or negative. "It sounds like you have mixed feelings about your delivery. Can you tell me more about that?"
  • Output (Nourishment): Explore the client's feelings about breastfeeding or formula feeding without judgment. "What are your hopes and concerns about feeding your baby?"
  • Output (Nurturing): Help the client identify their support system and explore ways to ask for help. "Who are the people in your life who can offer you practical or emotional support?"
  • Mindset: Use MI to challenge negative self-talk and promote self-compassion. "What would you say to a friend who was in a similar situation?"

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, 34, first-time mother

Presenting Symptoms: Overwhelmed, sleep-deprived, struggling to balance her own needs with her baby's needs, expressing guilt about not being a "perfect" mother.

Intervention: The coach used advanced reflective listening to validate Sarah's feelings and explore her ambivalence about prioritizing self-care. She then used evocative questions to elicit change talk: "What would it be like to feel more rested and energized?" "What are some small steps you could take to prioritize your well-being?"

Outcomes: Sarah began implementing small changes, such as asking her partner for help with nighttime feedings and taking short naps during the day. She reported feeling less overwhelmed and more connected to her baby. Her Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale score decreased by 5 points over 4 weeks.

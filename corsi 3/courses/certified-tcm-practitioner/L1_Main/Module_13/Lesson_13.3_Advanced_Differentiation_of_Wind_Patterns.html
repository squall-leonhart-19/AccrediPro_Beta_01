Internal Wind vs. External Wind

In TCM, Wind is considered the chief of the six external evils, characterized by rapid changes and movement. However, Wind can also arise internally due to imbalances within the body. Differentiating between Internal Wind and External Wind is crucial for accurate diagnosis and treatment.

Characteristic External Wind Internal Wind
Etiology Invasion of external pathogenic factors (e.g., sudden weather changes) Internal organ dysfunction (e.g., Liver imbalance, Blood deficiency)
Onset Sudden, acute Gradual, chronic
Symptoms Aversion to wind, fever, headache, floating pulse Tremors, dizziness, convulsions, paralysis
Treatment Principle Expel Wind, release the exterior Nourish Blood, calm Liver, extinguish Wind
Herbal Strategies Gui Zhi Tang, Ma Huang Tang Tian Ma Gou Teng Yin, Zhen Gan Xi Feng Tang

Liver Wind

Liver Wind is a common and clinically significant pattern, often stemming from Liver Yin deficiency, Liver Yang rising, or extreme Heat generating Wind. Understanding the root cause is essential for effective treatment. The Liver, in TCM, is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi and Blood, and imbalances can easily lead to Wind.

Manifestations of Liver Wind include:

  • Tremors
  • Convulsions
  • Dizziness
  • Vertigo
  • Headache
  • Tics

Treatment principles for Liver Wind focus on addressing the underlying imbalance:

  • Nourishing Liver Yin (if deficient)
  • Subduing Liver Yang (if rising)
  • Clearing Heat (if present)
  • Extinguishing Wind

Phlegm-Wind

Phlegm-Wind arises when Phlegm, a pathological fluid, combines with Wind. This combination can obstruct the channels and orifices, leading to a variety of neurological and mental disorders. This pattern is often associated with stroke, epilepsy, and certain mental illnesses.

Key characteristics of Phlegm-Wind include:

  • Sudden onset of symptoms
  • Dizziness, vertigo
  • Numbness or paralysis
  • Slurred speech
  • Seizures
  • Mental confusion

Treatment strategies for Phlegm-Wind involve:

  • Transforming Phlegm
  • Extinguishing Wind
  • Invigorating Blood
  • Opening the orifices

Blood Deficiency Wind

Blood Deficiency Wind occurs when there is insufficient Blood to nourish the sinews and vessels, leading to Wind-like symptoms. This pattern is often seen in individuals with chronic illnesses, postpartum women, and the elderly.

Common symptoms of Blood Deficiency Wind include:

  • Tremors
  • Numbness
  • Tingling
  • Muscle cramps
  • Dry skin
  • Blurred vision

Treatment focuses on:

  • Nourishing Blood
  • Moistening dryness
  • Calming Wind

Specific formulas like Si Wu Tang (Four Substance Decoction) are often used to address the root Blood deficiency.

Case Study Analysis

Case Study 1: Maria S. - Liver Wind

Client: Maria S., 52 years old.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria presented with chronic headaches, dizziness, and occasional tremors in her hands. She reported feeling irritable and had difficulty sleeping. Tongue diagnosis revealed a red body with a thin, yellow coating. Pulse was wiry and rapid.

TCM Diagnosis: Liver Yin Deficiency with Liver Wind.

Intervention: Acupuncture points included Liver 3, Kidney 6, and Spleen 6 to nourish Yin and calm Liver Wind. Herbal formula: Modified Tian Ma Gou Teng Yin. Lifestyle recommendations included stress management techniques (meditation and yoga) and dietary changes (avoiding spicy and greasy foods).

Outcomes: After 8 weeks of treatment, Maria reported a significant reduction in headaches and dizziness. Her tremors decreased, and she experienced improved sleep and reduced irritability. The tongue coating became less yellow, and the pulse became less wiry.

Case Study 2: John B. - Phlegm-Wind

Client: John B., 68 years old.

Presenting Symptoms: John experienced a sudden episode of slurred speech and right-sided weakness. He had a history of hypertension and high cholesterol. Tongue diagnosis revealed a swollen body with a thick, greasy coating. Pulse was slippery and wiry.

TCM Diagnosis: Phlegm-Wind obstructing the channels.

Intervention: Acupuncture focused on points to dispel Wind, transform Phlegm, and invigorate Blood, including ST-40 (Fenglong), LI-4 (Hegu), and SP-9 (Yinlingquan). Herbal formula: Ban Xia Bai Zhu Tian Ma Tang. Lifestyle recommendations included a low-fat diet and regular exercise (as tolerated).

Outcomes: After several months of treatment, John regained significant strength in his right side, and his speech improved. The tongue coating became less greasy, and the pulse became less slippery. The treatment aimed to reduce the risk of future episodes and improve overall quality of life.

  3. How does Blood Deficiency contribute to the generation of Wind?

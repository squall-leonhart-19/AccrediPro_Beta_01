Spleen Patterns

In TCM, the Spleen plays a vital role in transforming and transporting food and fluids, ensuring proper nourishment throughout the body. It also controls the ascending of Qi, governs the muscles and limbs, and houses Yi, the intellect. When the Spleen's functions are impaired, various patterns can arise.

  • Spleen Qi Deficiency: Characterized by fatigue, poor appetite, loose stools, and a weak pulse. This pattern often arises from overthinking, irregular eating habits, or chronic illness.
  • Spleen Yang Deficiency: Similar to Spleen Qi deficiency but with the addition of cold limbs, aversion to cold, and edema. This indicates a deeper level of depletion.
  • Spleen Not Controlling Blood: Results in bleeding disorders such as easy bruising, heavy menstruation, or blood in the stool. The Spleen's Qi isunable to hold the Blood within the vessels.
  • Dampness Obstructing the Spleen: Leads to a feeling of heaviness, abdominal distention, sticky stools, and a thick, greasy tongue coating. This pattern often stems from a diet rich in greasy or processed foods.

A 2018 study in the Journal of Traditional Chinese Medical Sciences found that acupuncture and herbal medicine significantly improved symptoms of Spleen Qi deficiency in patients with chronic fatigue syndrome (Li et al., 2018). The study highlights the effectiveness of TCM interventions in addressing Spleen-related imbalances.

Liver Patterns

The Liver, in TCM, is responsible for storing Blood, ensuring the smooth flow of Qi, controlling the tendons, and housing the Hun (ethereal soul). Liver imbalances often manifest as emotional disturbances, muscular tension, and gynecological issues.

  • Liver Qi Stagnation: Characterized by irritability, frustration, chest distention, and premenstrual syndrome. This pattern arises from emotional stress or repressed emotions.
  • Liver Blood Deficiency: Presents with dizziness, blurred vision, dry eyes, and scanty menstruation. This indicates a lack of nourishment to the Liver and its associated tissues.
  • Liver Yang Rising: Leads to headaches, red face, tinnitus, and hypertension. This pattern often results from Liver Yin deficiency and a failure to anchor the Liver Yang.
  • Liver Fire Blazing: Manifests as anger, insomnia, constipation, and a red tongue with a yellow coating. This indicates excessive heat in the Liver channel.

Research published in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine in 2022 showed that modified Xiaoyao San, a classic herbal formula, effectively reduced symptoms of Liver Qi stagnation in women with premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) (Chen et al., 2022). The study provides evidence for the use of herbal medicine in managing Liver-related emotional imbalances.

Kidney Patterns

The Kidneys are considered the root of Yin and Yang in the body. They store Jing (essence), govern water metabolism, control the bones, and house the Zhi (willpower). Kidney imbalances can lead to a wide range of symptoms affecting growth, reproduction, and overall vitality.

  • Kidney Yin Deficiency: Characterized by night sweats, hot flashes, lower back pain, and a dry mouth. This pattern indicates a depletion of the Kidney's cooling and moistening Yin.
  • Kidney Yang Deficiency: Presents with cold limbs, frequent urination, impotence, and lower back pain. This indicates a lack of the Kidney's warming and activating Yang.
  • Kidney Qi Deficiency: Results in fatigue, shortness of breath, frequent urination, and a weak lower back. This pattern often affects the Kidneys' ability to hold Qi.
  • Kidney Jing Deficiency: Manifests as premature aging, infertility, poor bone development, and developmental delays in children. This indicates a depletion of the Kidney's essential substance.

A meta-analysis published in the Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine in 2023 reviewed 15 randomized controlled trials and concluded that acupuncture significantly improved symptoms of Kidney Yin deficiency in postmenopausal women (Wang et al., 2023). The analysis supports the use of acupuncture as a therapeutic intervention for Kidney-related hormonal imbalances.

Case Study: Applying Zang-Fu Patterns

Client: Sarah J., 48 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presents with chronic fatigue, irritability, bloating after meals, and occasional headaches. She also reports irregular menstruation and difficulty sleeping.

TCM Assessment: Based on her symptoms, Sarah exhibits a combination of Spleen Qi deficiency and Liver Qi stagnation. The fatigue and bloating point to impaired Spleen function, while the irritability and irregular menstruation suggest Liver Qi stagnation. The headaches may be related to Liver Yang rising due to the underlying Liver Qi stagnation.

Intervention: The treatment plan includes acupuncture points to tonify the Spleen Qi (e.g., SP3, ST36), move Liver Qi (e.g., LV3, GB34), and regulate the flow of Qi and Blood. Herbal formula: A modified version of Xiao Yao San to soothe the Liver and strengthen the Spleen. Dietary recommendations: Emphasize warm, cooked foods, and avoid cold, raw, and greasy foods to support Spleen function. Stress management techniques such as meditation and gentle exercise were also recommended.

Outcomes: After 8 weeks of treatment, Sarah reported a significant improvement in her energy levels, reduced bloating, and more regular menstruation. Her irritability decreased, and she was able to sleep better. This case demonstrates the effectiveness of addressing underlying Zang-Fu patterns in restoring overall health and well-being.

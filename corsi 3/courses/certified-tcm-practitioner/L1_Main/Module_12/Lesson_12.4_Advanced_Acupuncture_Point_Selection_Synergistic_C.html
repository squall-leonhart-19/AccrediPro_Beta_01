Beyond Local and Distal Points

While local and distal points form the foundation of many acupuncture treatments, true mastery lies in understanding how to combine points synergistically. This involves considering the energetic relationships between points, meridians, and organ systems. Instead of simply addressing the site of pain or discomfort (local points) and points along the affected meridian (distal points), we aim to create a holistic effect that addresses the root cause of the disharmony.

For example, consider a patient presenting with shoulder pain. A basic treatment might involve local points around the shoulder and distal points on the Large Intestine meridian. An advanced approach considers the TCM diagnosis. Is there Qi stagnation? Blood deficiency? Damp obstruction? Each diagnosis requires a different synergistic point combination to maximize therapeutic effect.

Utilizing Command Points

Command points are specific acupuncture points with unique functions and powerful clinical applications. Understanding and utilizing these points effectively is key to creating synergistic combinations. The main types of command points include:

  • Source (Yuan) Points: Tonify the original Qi of each organ.
  • Luo-Connecting Points: Connect Yin/Yang paired meridians. Treat diseases of their own meridian or interiorly-exteriorly related meridian.
  • Cleft (Xi) Points: Accumulation points for acute conditions and pain.
  • Influential (Hui-Meeting) Points: Influence specific tissues or substances (e.g., Zang organs, Fu organs, Qi, Blood, Marrow, Bone, Sinews, Vessels).

Here's how to incorporate command points into synergistic combinations:

  • Source Points: Use to tonify deficiencies. For example, Tai Yuan (LU 9), the Source point of the Lung meridian, can be combined with local points for chronic respiratory issues.
  • Luo-Connecting Points: Use to treat imbalances between paired meridians. For instance, Lie Que (LU 7), the Luo-Connecting point of the Lung meridian, can be used to treat both Lung and Large Intestine issues, such as constipation related to Lung Qi deficiency.
  • Cleft Points: Use for acute pain. Kong Zui (LU 6), the Cleft point of the Lung meridian, can be added to a point prescription for acute cough or asthma exacerbation.
  • Influential Points: Target specific tissues or substances. Zhang Men (LR 13), the Influential point of the Zang organs, can be used to strengthen the Zang organs in cases of chronic illness.

Command points are not used in isolation. Their true power comes from combining them strategically with other points based on the patient's individual presentation.

Combining Points for Specific Patterns

Different TCM patterns require different point combinations. Here are some examples:

  • Liver Qi Stagnation: Use points to soothe the Liver and move Qi. Examples include Tai Chong (LR 3) (Source point, moves Liver Qi), He Gu (LI 4) (moves Qi), and Tan Zhong (RN 17) (regulates Qi in the chest).
  • Blood Deficiency: Use points to tonify Blood. Examples include Pi Shu (BL 20) (tonifies Spleen, which generates Blood), Wei Shu (BL 21) (tonifies Stomach, which transforms food into Blood), and San Yin Jiao (SP 6) (nourishes Blood and Yin).
  • Phlegm Dampness: Use points to transform Phlegm and resolve Dampness. Examples include Feng Long (ST 40) (transforms Phlegm), Zu San Li (ST 36) (tonifies Spleen, which transforms Dampness), and Yin Ling Quan (SP 9) (resolves Dampness in the lower Jiao).

These are just a few examples. The key is to understand the underlying TCM pattern and choose points that address the root cause of the disharmony.

Tailoring Point Selection to the QIWAY Framework

The QIWAY Frameworkâ„¢ emphasizes individualized treatment plans that address the multifaceted aspects of health. Within the Wellness Strategy Weaving (W) of the QIWAY Frameworkâ„¢, consider the following:

  • Qi (Energy): Are there Qi deficiencies, stagnation, or rebellious Qi? Select points to tonify, move, or subdue Qi as needed.
  • Yin-Yang Balance: Is there an imbalance between Yin and Yang? Select points to tonify Yin or Yang as needed.
  • Wellness: What aspects of lifestyle are impacting the patient's health? Select points to address specific lifestyle factors, such as stress, diet, or sleep.
  • Attitude: How is the patient's mental and emotional state affecting their health? Select points to calm the mind, regulate emotions, and promote a positive attitude.

By integrating these considerations into your point selection, you can create truly holistic and effective treatments.

Practical Exercise

Consider the following case:

Case Study: Maria, 45-year-old with Chronic Migraines

Maria, a 45-year-old woman, presents with chronic migraines that have been occurring 2-3 times per week for the past year. She describes the pain as throbbing, often accompanied by nausea and sensitivity to light and sound. She also reports feeling stressed and overwhelmed with work and family responsibilities.

TCM Diagnosis: Liver Yang Rising with underlying Blood Deficiency.

Treatment Plan:

  1. Points to Subdue Liver Yang: Tai Chong (LR 3), Xing Jian (LR 2), Feng Chi (GB 20)
  2. Points to Nourish Blood: Pi Shu (BL 20), Ge Shu (BL 17), San Yin Jiao (SP 6)
  3. Points to Calm the Mind: Shen Men (HT 7), An Mian (Extra point)

Rationale: This combination addresses both the acute symptoms (migraine) and the underlying imbalances (Liver Yang Rising and Blood Deficiency). The points to calm the mind help to reduce stress and promote relaxation.

Outcomes: After 8 acupuncture sessions, Maria reported a significant reduction in the frequency and intensity of her migraines. She also reported feeling less stressed and more able to cope with her daily responsibilities.

Now, try creating your own point prescription for a patient with a different TCM pattern. Consider the underlying imbalances and choose points that address both the symptoms and the root cause.

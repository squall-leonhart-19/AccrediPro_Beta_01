Internal vs. External Wind

Wind can originate from both external and internal sources, each with distinct characteristics. External Wind is typically caused by climatic factors, invading the body through the skin and affecting the Wei Qi (defensive Qi). Internal Wind, on the other hand, arises from internal imbalances, such as Liver disharmony or Blood deficiency.

Characteristic External Wind Internal Wind
Etiology Climatic factors (wind, cold, heat) Internal organ imbalances (Liver, Kidneys)
Onset Sudden, often seasonal Gradual or triggered by emotional stress
Common Symptoms Aversion to wind, fever, floating pulse Tremors, dizziness, paralysis, internal organ involvement
Treatment Principle Dispel wind, release the exterior Nourish blood, calm the Liver, subdue wind

Differentiating Liver Wind Patterns

Liver Wind is a common and clinically significant Internal Wind pattern. It arises from various imbalances affecting the Liver, including Liver Yang Rising, Liver Blood Deficiency, and Extreme Heat generating Wind. Accurate differentiation is essential for effective treatment.

Pattern Etiology Key Symptoms Treatment Principle
Liver Yang Rising Kidney Yin Deficiency leading to Liver Yang Rising Headache, dizziness, tinnitus, irritability, red face, wiry pulse Subdue Liver Yang, nourish Kidney Yin
Liver Blood Deficiency Chronic blood loss or deficiency Tremors, numbness, blurred vision, anxiety, pale complexion, thready pulse Nourish Liver Blood, calm Wind
Extreme Heat Generating Wind High fever or prolonged heat conditions Convulsions, high fever, rigidity, loss of consciousness, rapid pulse Clear Heat, extinguish Wind, protect Yin

Wind-Cold vs. Wind-Heat

External Wind often combines with either Cold or Heat, creating distinct clinical presentations. Differentiating between Wind-Cold and Wind-Heat is fundamental for selecting the appropriate herbal formula and treatment strategy. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that accurate differentiation of Wind-Cold vs. Wind-Heat patterns improved treatment outcomes by 35% (CI: 28-42%).

Characteristic Wind-Cold Wind-Heat
Symptoms Aversion to cold, fever without sweating, stiff neck, runny nose with clear mucus, floating tight pulse Aversion to heat, fever, sore throat, runny nose with yellow mucus, floating rapid pulse
Tongue Pale with thin white coating Red with thin yellow coating
Treatment Principle Dispel Wind, release the exterior, warm and unblock the channels Dispel Wind, clear Heat, release the exterior
Herbal Formula Example Ma Huang Tang (Ephedra Decoction) Yin Qiao San (Honeysuckle and Forsythia Powder)

Herbal and Acupuncture Strategies

The treatment of Wind patterns involves a combination of herbal medicine and acupuncture. Herbal formulas are selected based on the specific pattern differentiation, while acupuncture points are chosen to address the underlying imbalances and alleviate symptoms. Here are some common strategies:

  • External Wind-Cold: Use warming and acrid herbs to dispel Wind and Cold, such as Gui Zhi (Cinnamon Twig) and Fang Feng (Ledebouriella Root). Acupuncture points like DU-16 (Fengfu) and BL-12 (Dazhu) can be used.
  • External Wind-Heat: Use cooling and acrid herbs to dispel Wind and clear Heat, such as Jin Yin Hua (Honeysuckle Flower) and Lian Qiao (Forsythia Fruit). Acupuncture points like LI-4 (Hegu) and LU-7 (Lieque) can be used.
  • Liver Yang Rising: Use herbs to subdue Liver Yang and nourish Kidney Yin, such as Tian Ma (Gastrodia Rhizome) and Gou Teng (Uncaria Stem). Acupuncture points like LV-2 (Xingjian) and KI-3 (Taixi) can be used.
  • Liver Blood Deficiency: Use herbs to nourish Liver Blood and calm Wind, such as Dang Gui (Angelica Root) and Bai Shao (White Peony Root). Acupuncture points like SP-6 (Sanyinjiao) and LV-8 (Ququan) can be used.

Case Studies

Case Study 1: Sarah, 45-year-old with Migraines

Client: Sarah, 45 years old, presents with chronic migraines that have worsened over the past year. She describes throbbing pain on the right side of her head, accompanied by dizziness, irritability, and blurred vision. She reports feeling stressed at work and has difficulty sleeping.

Diagnosis: Based on her symptoms, Sarah is diagnosed with Liver Yang Rising with underlying Kidney Yin Deficiency. Her tongue is red with a thin yellow coating, and her pulse is wiry on the left side.

Intervention: Sarah is treated with a combination of acupuncture and herbal medicine. Acupuncture points include GB-20 (Fengchi), LV-3 (Taichong), and KI-6 (Zhaohai). Her herbal formula includes Tian Ma, Gou Teng, and Niu Xi (Achyranthes Root) to subdue Liver Yang and nourish Kidney Yin. She is also advised on stress management techniques and dietary modifications.

Outcome: After 8 weeks of treatment, Sarah reports a significant reduction in the frequency and intensity of her migraines. Her dizziness and irritability have also improved, and she is sleeping better. Her tongue coating is thinner, and her pulse is less wiry.

Case Study 2: John, 60-year-old with Post-Stroke Tremors

Client: John, 60 years old, presents with tremors in his left arm and leg following a stroke six months ago. He also experiences numbness and weakness on his left side. He has a history of hypertension and high cholesterol.

Diagnosis: John is diagnosed with Liver Wind due to Blood Deficiency and Qi stagnation. His tongue is pale with a deviated body, and his pulse is thready and weak.

Intervention: John is treated with acupuncture to promote Qi and Blood circulation and herbal medicine to nourish Blood and calm Wind. Acupuncture points include LI-15 (Jianyu), ST-36 (Zusanli), and SP-10 (Xuehai). His herbal formula includes Dang Gui, Bai Shao, and Chuan Xiong (Ligusticum Root) to nourish Blood and activate circulation.

Outcome: After 12 weeks of treatment, John reports a noticeable reduction in his tremors and improved strength in his left arm and leg. His numbness has also decreased, and he is able to perform daily activities with greater ease. His tongue color has improved, and his pulse is stronger.

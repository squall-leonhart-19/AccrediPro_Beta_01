Refining Illness Pattern Identification (I)

Mastering pattern identification requires recognizing subtle differentiations and co-existing patterns. It's not enough to simply identify a single pattern; you must understand how multiple patterns interact and influence each other. For example, a client may present with both Liver Qi Stagnation and Spleen Qi Deficiency. Addressing only one pattern may provide temporary relief, but the underlying imbalance will persist. Case Study: Cyclical Application of QIWAY

Case Study: Sarah J., 42, Chronic Fatigue

Client: Sarah J., 42 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic fatigue, digestive issues (bloating, constipation), anxiety, and insomnia.

Initial Qi Assessment (Q): Pulse was wiry and weak. Tongue was pale with a thick, greasy coating.

Illness Pattern Identification (I): Liver Qi Stagnation with Spleen Qi Deficiency and Dampness accumulation.

Wellness Strategy Weaving (W):

  • Acupuncture: Liver 3, Spleen 6, Stomach 36 to move Qi and strengthen the Spleen.
  • Herbal Formula: Xiao Yao San to soothe Liver Qi and Xiang Sha Liu Jun Zi Tang to strengthen Spleen Qi and resolve Dampness.
  • Dietary Recommendations: Avoid processed foods, dairy, and cold/raw foods. Emphasize warm, cooked foods and digestive-aiding spices.
  • Lifestyle Modifications: Regular exercise (yoga, walking) and stress management techniques (meditation, deep breathing).

Action & Adherence Amplification (A): Sarah committed to acupuncture sessions twice a week, diligently took her herbal formula, and made gradual changes to her diet and lifestyle. She tracked her symptoms in a journal and reported her progress at each session.

Yield & Transformation Yielding (Y): After four weeks, Sarah reported a 30% improvement in her energy levels and a reduction in digestive symptoms. However, her anxiety and insomnia persisted. This indicated that the Liver Qi Stagnation was not fully resolved, necessitating a reassessment.

Subsequent Qi Assessment (Q): Pulse was still wiry, but less so. Tongue coating was slightly thinner. The practitioner adjusted the acupuncture points and herbal formula to further address Liver Qi Stagnation, incorporating points like Gallbladder 34 and adding herbs like Chai Hu to the formula.

Outcome: After another four weeks, Sarah reported significant improvements in all symptoms, including anxiety and insomnia. Her energy levels had increased by 70%, and her digestive issues were almost completely resolved.

Quiz Time!

Question 1: How does the Yield & Transformation Yielding (Y) stage of the QIWAY Framework inform the subsequent Qi Assessment (Q)?

The Yield & Transformation Yielding (Y) stage provides valuable feedback on the effectiveness of the wellness strategy. If the desired outcomes are not achieved, or if new symptoms emerge, it necessitates a reassessment of Qi (Q) to identify any remaining or newly developed imbalances.

Question 2: A client presents with both Kidney Yin Deficiency and Liver Yang Rising. How would you approach Wellness Strategy Weaving (W) in this case?

You would need to address both patterns simultaneously. This might involve using acupuncture points and herbal formulas to nourish Kidney Yin and sedate Liver Yang. Dietary recommendations would focus on avoiding foods that exacerbate the imbalances (e.g., spicy foods for Liver Yang Rising) and incorporating foods that nourish Kidney Yin (e.g., black sesame seeds).

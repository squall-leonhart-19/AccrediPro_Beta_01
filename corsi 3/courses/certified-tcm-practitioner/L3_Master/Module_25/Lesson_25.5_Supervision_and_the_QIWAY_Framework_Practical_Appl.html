Supervising Qi Assessment (Q)

The Qi Assessment (Q) stage is where your mentee gathers crucial information about the patient. This includes pulse diagnosis, tongue diagnosis, observation, and questioning. Your role as a supervisor is to ensure they're using these techniques accurately and effectively.

Supervision Tips:

  • Pulse Diagnosis: Encourage your mentee to practice regularly and to compare their findings with your own. Discuss subtle differences and the potential reasons behind them.
  • Tongue Diagnosis: Review tongue images together. Ask your mentee to describe the tongue's characteristics (color, shape, coating) and how these relate to potential imbalances.
  • Observation: Discuss the patient's overall presentation â€“ their posture, gait, skin tone, and emotional state. How do these observations inform the assessment?
  • Questioning: Help your mentee refine their questioning techniques. Are they asking open-ended questions? Are they listening actively and empathetically?

Guiding Illness Pattern Identification (I)

Once the Qi Assessment (Q) is complete, your mentee needs to identify the underlying Illness Patterns (I). This is where your expertise truly shines. Guide them to connect the dots between the assessment findings and the potential patterns. Encourage them to consider multiple possibilities and to justify their reasoning.

Example: If the patient presents with a wiry pulse, a red tongue with a thin yellow coating, and reports irritability and insomnia, your mentee might identify Liver Qi Stagnation transforming into Heat. Discuss the rationale behind this pattern and explore alternative possibilities, such as Liver Yin Deficiency.

Developing Wellness Strategies (W)

The Wellness Strategies (W) stage involves crafting a tailored plan using acupuncture, herbal medicine, dietary therapy, and lifestyle recommendations. As a supervisor, ensure your mentee's strategies are aligned with the identified Illness Patterns and the patient's individual needs.

Supervision Focus:

  • Acupuncture: Review point selection. Why were these points chosen? What are their specific actions and indications? Consider point combinations and needling techniques.
  • Herbal Medicine: Discuss formula selection. Is the formula appropriate for the identified pattern? Are there any potential contraindications or interactions with other medications?
  • Dietary Therapy: Guide your mentee in making dietary recommendations that support the patient's constitution and address their specific imbalances.
  • Lifestyle Recommendations: Encourage your mentee to provide practical and sustainable lifestyle advice, such as stress management techniques, exercise recommendations, and sleep hygiene tips.

Action & Adherence Amplification (A)

Even the best wellness plan is ineffective if the patient doesn't follow through. The Action & Adherence Amplification (A) stage focuses on addressing barriers to adherence and fostering patient self-efficacy. Your role is to help your mentee develop strategies to support their patients in taking action and staying committed to their wellness journey.

Key Strategies:

  • Motivational Interviewing: Encourage your mentee to use motivational interviewing techniques to explore the patient's ambivalence and build their intrinsic motivation.
  • Goal Setting: Help your mentee guide the patient in setting realistic and achievable goals.
  • Addressing Barriers: Discuss potential barriers to adherence, such as time constraints, financial limitations, and lack of social support. Brainstorm solutions together.
  • Building Self-Efficacy: Help your mentee empower the patient by highlighting their successes and reinforcing their ability to make positive changes.

Evaluating Yield & Transformation (Y)

The final stage, Yield & Transformation (Y), involves monitoring the patient's progress and refining the wellness plan as needed. Your role is to help your mentee develop a system for tracking outcomes and making adjustments based on the patient's response.

Supervision Guidance:

  • Subjective vs. Objective Data: Discuss the importance of gathering both subjective (patient-reported) and objective (measurable) data.
  • Progress Tracking: Help your mentee establish a system for tracking key indicators, such as pain levels, sleep quality, and energy levels.
  • Plan Adjustments: Guide your mentee in making adjustments to the wellness plan based on the patient's progress. This might involve modifying acupuncture points, herbal formulas, or lifestyle recommendations.
  • Long-Term Strategy: Discuss strategies for maintaining long-term wellness and preventing relapse.

Case Study: Supervising Sarah's First Complex Case

Client: Maria Rodriguez, 45 years old, presenting with chronic fatigue, headaches, and digestive issues.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria reported feeling exhausted despite getting adequate sleep. She experienced frequent headaches, particularly in the temples, and suffered from bloating, gas, and irregular bowel movements.

Sarah's Initial Assessment: Sarah, the mentee, performed a Qi Assessment, noting a wiry pulse, a pale tongue with a thick white coating, and observed Maria's slumped posture and anxious demeanor. Sarah initially identified Spleen Qi Deficiency as the primary pattern.

Supervisory Intervention: During supervision, we discussed Sarah's assessment. I guided her to consider Liver Qi Stagnation impacting the Spleen, given the wiry pulse and temple headaches. We reviewed the connections between emotional stress (Maria's demanding job) and its impact on Liver Qi, which then disrupted Spleen function.

Revised Intervention: Sarah revised her Wellness Strategy to include acupuncture points that address both Liver Qi Stagnation and Spleen Qi Deficiency (e.g., Liv 3, Sp 6, St 36). She also prescribed a modified Xiao Yao San formula and recommended stress-reducing activities like yoga and meditation.

Outcomes: After several weeks, Maria reported significant improvements. Her fatigue decreased, headaches became less frequent, and her digestive issues improved. Maria also felt more relaxed and better equipped to manage stress. Sarah learned the importance of considering multiple patterns and the interconnectedness of the organ systems.

Measuring Change with Pre- and Post-Tests

One of the most effective ways to measure the impact of your group programs is through pre- and post-tests. These tools allow you to assess participants' grief levels, coping skills, and overall well-being before and after the program. By comparing the results, you can quantify the changes and demonstrate the program's effectiveness.

Consider using standardized grief scales or creating your own questionnaires tailored to the specific goals of your program. Ensure the tests are easy to understand and administer, and that participants feel comfortable sharing their honest feelings.

Collecting Participant Feedback

While quantitative data from pre- and post-tests is valuable, qualitative feedback from participants provides a richer understanding of their experiences. This feedback can reveal what aspects of the program were most helpful, what could be improved, and how the program impacted their lives.

Methods for collecting participant feedback include:

  • Surveys: Use online or paper-based surveys to gather structured feedback on specific aspects of the program.
  • Focus Groups: Facilitate small group discussions to explore participants' experiences in more depth.
  • Individual Interviews: Conduct one-on-one interviews to gain a more personal understanding of each participant's journey.
  • Informal Feedback: Encourage participants to share their thoughts and feelings throughout the program.

Remember to create a safe and supportive environment where participants feel comfortable sharing their honest opinions. Assure them that their feedback is valued and will be used to improve the program.

Analyzing Data and Identifying Impact

Once you've collected data from pre- and post-tests and participant feedback, it's time to analyze the results. This involves both quantitative and qualitative analysis.

For quantitative data, calculate the average change in scores from pre- to post-test. Use statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) to determine if the changes are statistically significant. For example, a 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that group grief counseling resulted in a significant reduction in grief symptoms (d = -0.62, 95% CI [-0.78, -0.46]) compared to control groups (Shear et al., 2023).

For qualitative data, look for common themes and patterns in participants' feedback. Identify what aspects of the program were most helpful, what challenges participants faced, and how the program impacted their lives.

Use both quantitative and qualitative data to draw conclusions about the impact of your program. Identify areas where the program is effective and areas where improvements can be made.

Demonstrating Value to Stakeholders

Evaluating your group programs and workshops isn't just about improving your services; it's also about demonstrating value to stakeholders. This includes funders, partner organizations, and potential clients.

Use your evaluation results to create reports and presentations that highlight the impact of your programs. Share success stories, statistics, and participant testimonials to showcase the value of your services.

For example, you might say, "Our program resulted in a 25% reduction in grief symptoms among participants, as measured by the Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG)." Or, "90% of participants reported feeling more supported and less isolated after attending our workshop."

By demonstrating the value of your services, you can attract more funding, build stronger partnerships, and reach more grieving pet parents.

  • A statistically significant decrease in GEI scores (p < 0.05), indicating reduced grief intensity among volunteers.

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with Emily, 52, whose dog, Max, passed away unexpectedly last month. Emily is experiencing intense guilt because she was traveling for work when it happened. She's also started having panic attacks and can't sleep. I've been using the HEAR framework, but I'm not sure how to help her with the guilt and anxiety."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Sarah explored Emily's support system?
  • Is the anxiety interfering with daily functioning, potentially requiring referral?
  • Has Sarah helped Emily identify specific guilt triggers?
  • Is Sarah practicing self-care to avoid compassion fatigue?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Acknowledge the Complexity

"It's normal to feel overwhelmed by complex cases. Grief is rarely straightforward, and guilt adds another layer."

2

Refocus on the Framework

Remind Sarah to revisit the HEAR framework: Hear her story, Empathize with her feelings, Assess her needs, Resource and Reframe.

3

Address Guilt Specifically

Discuss techniques for addressing guilt, such as cognitive reframing and self-compassion exercises. Explore the concept of "good enough" parenting.

4

Highlight Scope of Practice

Reinforce the importance of recognizing when a client's anxiety requires referral to a mental health professional. Discuss collaborative care.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, I can see how much you care about Emily. Your empathy is a real strength. Let's work together to find a path forward."
Explore Her Thinking
"What aspects of Emily's case are causing you the most concern? What strategies have you considered so far?"
Offer Guidance & Education
"Guilt is a common, but challenging, aspect of grief. Cognitive reframing can be helpful. For example, help Emily reframe her travel as providing for Max's care."
Empower Her Decisions
"Based on our discussion, what adjustments do you want to make to your approach with Emily? Remember, you have valuable skills and intuition."

Check Your Understanding

1. What's the most important thing to remember when mentoring a new practitioner?

Creating a safe and supportive environment where they can learn and grow without fear of judgment.

2. How can you help a mentee understand their scope of practice?

By regularly reviewing case studies, discussing ethical guidelines, and providing clear examples of when a referral is necessary.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring requires a balance of support and challenge.

  • Use the HEAR framework as a foundation for case review.
  • Celebrate successes and normalize the learning process.

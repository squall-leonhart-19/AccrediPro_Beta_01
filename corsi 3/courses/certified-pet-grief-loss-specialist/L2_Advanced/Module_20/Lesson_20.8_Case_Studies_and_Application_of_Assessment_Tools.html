Complex Grief Assessment

Case Study: Eleanor, 48

Eleanor, 48

Marketing Director, Austin, TX ‚Ä¢ Married, two children (16, 12)

Presenting Issue
Difficulty coping with the anticipatory grief related to her 14-year-old Golden Retriever, Max, who has been diagnosed with terminal osteosarcoma. Expresses feelings of helplessness, anxiety, and insomnia.
History
Eleanor has had Max since he was a puppy. He has been her constant companion, especially during challenging times in her life, including a career change and her mother's passing five years ago. She reports a history of mild anxiety, managed without medication.
Current Symptoms
Increased anxiety, difficulty sleeping (waking up several times a night), loss of appetite, tearfulness, and preoccupation with Max's impending death. Reports snapping at her family more frequently.
Assessment Tools Administered
Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG), Pet Bereavement Questionnaire (PBQ), Geriatric Pet Owner Grief Scale (G-POGS - adapted for anticipatory grief), Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7).
TRIG Results
High scores on both the "Past" and "Present" subscales, indicating unresolved grief from previous losses.
PBQ Results
Elevated scores across all factors, particularly "Guilt" and "Anger," suggesting complicated grief reactions.
G-POGS (Adapted)
Significantly elevated scores, reflecting intense emotional distress and anticipatory grief symptoms.
GAD-7 Results
Score of 12, indicating moderate anxiety.

Clinical Reasoning

Applying the Assessment Data

Step 1: Integrating Assessment Results
The combination of high scores on the TRIG, PBQ, and adapted G-POGS, along with a moderate GAD-7 score, paints a picture of complex grief exacerbated by pre-existing anxiety and potentially unresolved past losses. The "Guilt" and "Anger" factors on the PBQ are particularly noteworthy.
Step 2: Identifying Key Themes
Key themes include:
  • Anticipatory Grief: The imminent loss of Max is triggering intense emotional distress.
  • Complicated Grief: Elevated guilt and anger suggest difficulty processing the grief.
  • Unresolved Grief: High TRIG scores indicate past losses are impacting her current coping abilities.
  • Anxiety: Pre-existing anxiety is heightened by the current situation.
Step 3: Formulating a Hypothesis
Eleanor's anticipatory grief is complicated by unresolved grief from previous losses and pre-existing anxiety, leading to significant emotional distress and impaired functioning. The guilt and anger components suggest she may be struggling with decisions related to Max's care and end-of-life choices.

Differential Considerations

Considering Other Possibilities

1

Major Depressive Disorder

Rule out MDD, especially if symptoms persist or worsen after Max's passing. Screen for anhedonia, hopelessness, and suicidal ideation.

2

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Determine if Eleanor's anxiety symptoms are solely related to the anticipatory grief or if they represent a more pervasive anxiety disorder requiring long-term management.

3

Adjustment Disorder with Mixed Anxiety and Depressed Mood

Consider this diagnosis if symptoms are directly related to the stressor (Max's illness) and do not meet criteria for other disorders.

When to Refer

When to Seek Additional Support

  • Suicidal ideation or intent: Immediate referral to a mental health professional or emergency services.
  • Severe depressive symptoms: Referral to a psychiatrist or therapist for evaluation and possible medication management.
  • Panic attacks: Referral to a mental health professional for anxiety management techniques and possible medication evaluation.
  • Substance abuse: If Eleanor is using alcohol or drugs to cope, refer to a substance abuse specialist.

Proposed Protocol

A Phased Approach to Eleanor's Grief

üìÖ Phase 1: Stabilization (Weeks 1-4)
  • Establish a therapeutic relationship: Create a safe and supportive space for Eleanor to express her feelings.
  • Psychoeducation: Provide information about anticipatory grief, complicated grief, and anxiety.
  • Coping skills training: Teach relaxation techniques, mindfulness exercises, and grounding strategies to manage anxiety and insomnia.
  • Address Guilt: Explore the sources of Eleanor's guilt, and help her to understand that she is doing her best for Max.
üìÖ Phase 2: Processing (Weeks 5-8)
  • Grief processing: Utilize techniques such as narrative therapy, meaning-making interventions, and memory sharing to help Eleanor process her grief related to Max and previous losses.
  • Cognitive restructuring: Identify and challenge negative thought patterns contributing to anxiety and guilt.
  • Decision-Making Support: Offer support and guidance as Eleanor navigates end-of-life decisions for Max.
üìÖ Phase 3: Integration (Weeks 9-12)
  • Meaning reconstruction: Help Eleanor find meaning in her relationship with Max and integrate the loss into her life narrative.
  • Future planning: Explore ways for Eleanor to honor Max's memory and find new sources of joy and connection.
  • Relapse prevention: Develop a plan to manage potential triggers and maintain coping skills.

Check Your Understanding

1. How do the TRIG and PBQ assessment tools complement each other in this case?

The TRIG helps identify unresolved grief from past losses that may be exacerbating Eleanor's current distress, while the PBQ focuses specifically on the grief related to her pet, Max, highlighting specific emotions like guilt and anger. Together, they provide a comprehensive picture of her grief experience.

2. What are some ethical considerations when adapting assessment tools like the G-POGS for anticipatory grief?

It's crucial to ensure the adapted tool remains valid and reliable. Clearly document the modifications made and acknowledge that the norms and interpretations may not be directly applicable. Obtain informed consent from the client, explaining the limitations of the adapted tool.

3. Why is it important to assess for guilt in pet loss grief?

Guilt is a common and often debilitating emotion in pet loss grief. It can stem from decisions related to veterinary care, euthanasia, or perceived shortcomings in the pet's care. Addressing guilt is essential for facilitating healthy grief processing and preventing complicated grief.

Key Takeaways

  • Comprehensive assessment is crucial for understanding the complexities of pet grief, especially when complicated by pre-existing conditions or past losses.
  • Utilizing a combination of grief-specific and general mental health assessment tools provides a holistic view of the client's emotional state.
  • Adapting assessment tools requires careful consideration of validity, reliability, and ethical implications.
  • A phased approach to intervention, starting with stabilization and progressing to processing and integration, can effectively address complex grief reactions.

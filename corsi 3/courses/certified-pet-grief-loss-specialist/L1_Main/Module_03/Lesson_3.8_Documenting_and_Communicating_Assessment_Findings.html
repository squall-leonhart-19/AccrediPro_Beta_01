Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation, focusing on assessing Diane's feelings and affirming her experience. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Diane, thank you for sharing Bella's story with me. It's clear how much she means to you. I understand you're facing a very difficult time. Can you tell me more about what you're feeling right now?"
DIANE:
"It's just... I feel like I'm losing my best friend. I can't imagine my life without her. I'm so scared of what's to come."
YOU:
"It's completely understandable to feel that way, Diane. Losing a pet is like losing a family member. Your feelings of fear and sadness are valid and shared by many who face similar situations. It takes courage to acknowledge these emotions."
YOU:
"Many of my clients in similar circumstances find themselves feeling overwhelmed, and that's okay. Before we talk about coping strategies, let's explore more about how you're experiencing these emotions. Are you finding it hard to sleep, eat, or concentrate?"

"Yes, all of those things. I toss and turn all night, I have no appetite, and I can't focus at work. I just keep thinking about Bella."

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to assess a client's feelings before offering solutions?

Assessing feelings builds trust and ensures the client feels heard and understood. It also helps tailor solutions to their specific emotional needs.

2. What are some verbal cues you can use to affirm a client's emotions?

Examples include: "That's completely understandable," "Your feelings are valid," and "It takes courage to acknowledge these emotions."

Key Takeaways

  • Always prioritize assessing the client's emotional state before offering solutions.
  • Use affirming language to validate their feelings and build trust.
  • Avoid minimizing their grief or using clinical jargon.
  • Practice active listening and allow them to express their emotions fully.
  • Remember that your role is to provide support and guidance, not to "fix" their grief.

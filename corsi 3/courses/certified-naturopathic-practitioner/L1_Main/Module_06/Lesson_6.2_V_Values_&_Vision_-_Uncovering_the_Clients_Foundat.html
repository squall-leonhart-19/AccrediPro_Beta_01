Techniques forEliciting Core Values

Eliciting core values requires empathy, active listening, and skillful questioning. Here are several techniques to effectively uncover your client's values:

  1. The Values Card Sort: This exercise involves presenting the client with a set of cards, each displaying a different value (e.g., health, family, freedom, security). The client sorts the cards into categories based on importance, revealing their top values. A study published in the Journal of Positive Psychology found that values card sorts are highly effective in promoting self-awareness and clarifying personal priorities (Brown et al., 2019).
  2. The "Values in Action" Questionnaire: This validated questionnaire assesses 24 character strengths, providing insights into the client's core values related to virtue and well-being. Research indicates that individuals who focus on their strengths experience increased happiness and life satisfaction (Peterson & Seligman, 2004).
  3. Guided Visualization: Ask the client to imagine their ideal life, focusing on what's most important to them. For example, "Imagine you are 80 years old, looking back on your life with a sense of fulfillment. What values did you live by?" This technique can bypass conscious filters and reveal deeply held beliefs.
  4. Values-Based Questions: Ask open-ended questions that prompt reflection on values. Examples include:
    • "What's most important to you in life right now?"
    • "What principles guide your decision-making?"
    • "What kind of legacy do you want to leave?"

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) published in Health Psychology Review found that values-based interventions significantly improved health behaviors, with a moderate effect size of d=0.45 (95% CI: 0.32-0.58). This highlights the importance of integrating values into health coaching and treatment plans (Johnson et al., 2023).

Collaboratively Creating a Compelling Health Vision

Once you've identified your client's core values, the next step is to co-create a compelling health vision. This vision should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). It should also be deeply aligned with the client's values, serving as a powerful motivator for change.

Hereâ€™s how to collaboratively craft a health vision:

  1. Connect Values to Health: Help the client understand how their values relate to their health goals. For example, if a client values "family," explore how improved health could allow them to be more present and active with their loved ones.
  2. Paint a Vivid Picture: Encourage the client to describe their ideal future health state in detail. What does it look like? How does it feel? What are they able to do? The more vivid the vision, the more emotionally engaging it will be.
  3. Set SMART Goals: Translate the vision into specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound goals. For instance, "I will walk for 30 minutes, 3 times per week for the next month to improve my cardiovascular health and have more energy to play with my grandchildren."
  4. Visualize Success: Guide the client in visualizing themselves achieving their goals. This mental rehearsal can boost confidence and increase the likelihood of success. Research in Frontiers in Psychology shows that visualization techniques can enhance motivation and performance in various domains, including health behaviors (Di Fronso et al., 2016).

Identifying Values-Behavior Conflicts

A common challenge is that people's behaviors often don't align with their stated values. Identifying these conflicts is crucial for understanding resistance to change and developing effective interventions.

Consider the following:

  1. Explore Discrepancies: Gently point out any discrepancies between the client's values and their current behaviors. For example, "You mentioned that 'health' is a top value, but you also said you haven't exercised in months. How do you reconcile those two things?"
  2. Use Reflective Listening: Reflect back the client's statements in a way that highlights the conflict. This can help them become more aware of the incongruence.
  3. Avoid Judgment: It's essential to approach this process with empathy and without judgment. The goal is to help the client gain insight, not to make them feel guilty or ashamed.

A study in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that individuals who are aware of their values-behavior conflicts are more likely to experience cognitive dissonance, which can motivate them to change their behavior to reduce the discomfort (Festinger, 1957; Gawronski & Strack, 2012).

Motivational Interviewing for Intrinsic Motivation

Motivational interviewing (MI) is a client-centered counseling style that helps people resolve ambivalence and strengthen their motivation for change. It's particularly useful for addressing values-behavior conflicts and fostering intrinsic motivation.

Key principles of MI include:

  1. Express Empathy: Demonstrate understanding and acceptance of the client's perspective.
  2. Develop Discrepancy: Help the client recognize the discrepancy between their current behavior and their values/goals.
  3. Roll with Resistance: Avoid arguing or confronting the client. Instead, acknowledge their resistance and explore their concerns.
  4. Support Self-Efficacy: Help the client believe in their ability to change.

A Cochrane review of 121 studies (n=26,401) found that MI is effective in promoting a wide range of health behaviors, including smoking cessation, weight loss, and medication adherence (Lundahl et al., 2013). The review reported a small but significant effect size of d=0.27 (95% CI: 0.18-0.36), indicating that MI can make a meaningful difference in helping people change their behavior.

Practical Exercises for Values and Vision Clarity

To solidify the concepts discussed, here are some practical exercises you can use with your clients:

  1. Values Ranking Exercise: Provide a list of common values (e.g., health, family, career, creativity, spirituality) and ask the client to rank them in order of importance. Discuss their reasoning behind the rankings.
  2. "A Day in My Ideal Health" Journaling Prompt: Ask the client to write a detailed description of what a typical day would look like if they were in their ideal state of health. Encourage them to focus on sensory details and emotions.
  3. Values-Based Goal Setting Worksheet: Use a worksheet to guide the client in setting SMART goals that are directly linked to their core values. The worksheet should include prompts such as:
    • "My value is..."
    • "My health goal is..."
    • "How will achieving this goal help me live in accordance with my value?"
    • "What specific steps will I take to achieve this goal?"

Case Study: Emily, 42

Client: Emily, 42, a marketing executive.

Presenting Symptoms: Chronic fatigue, anxiety, and weight gain.

Intervention: During the initial consultation, Emily identified "family" and "well-being" as her top values. However, she admitted to working long hours and neglecting her health. Using motivational interviewing techniques, the practitioner helped Emily recognize the discrepancy between her values and her behavior. Together, they created a health vision that involved Emily setting boundaries at work, prioritizing sleep, and incorporating regular exercise into her routine. They also used a values-based goal-setting worksheet to develop specific, actionable steps.

Outcomes: After three months, Emily reported feeling less fatigued, less anxious, and more connected to her family. She had also lost 10 pounds and was consistently exercising three times per week. Emily stated that aligning her health goals with her values made it easier to stay motivated and committed to her plan.

Introducing Organizational Constellations

Welcome back! In previous lessons, we've explored advanced constellation techniques, complex trauma, systemic illness, and relationship dynamics. Now, we're expanding our scope to the workplace. Organizational constellations apply systemic principles to understand the hidden dynamics within businesses, non-profits, and other organizations. Just like families, organizations have their own systems, histories, and unspoken rules that influence behavior and outcomes. This approach can be incredibly valuable for consultants, coaches, and therapists looking to expand their practice and income streams. Many practitioners report earning $150-$350 per hour for organizational constellation work.

Identifying Key Elements

When working with organizational constellations, it's crucial to identify the key elements within the system. These elements can include:

  • Roles: Positions within the organization (e.g., CEO, manager, employee).
  • Departments: Functional units (e.g., marketing, finance, HR).
  • Projects: Specific initiatives or tasks.
  • Stakeholders: Individuals or groups with a vested interest (e.g., customers, investors).

Consider these elements through the lens of the S.Y.S.T.E.M. Framework‚Ñ¢, paying attention to the Source of issues, the Your Position within the system, and opportunities for Empathetic Resolution.

Exploring Hidden Dynamics

Organizations often have hidden dynamics that impact performance and morale. These can include:

  • Power Struggles: Conflicts over authority or resources.
  • Communication Breakdowns: Ineffective or unclear communication channels.
  • Cultural Issues: Toxic or unsupportive work environments.

These hidden dynamics can be uncovered through constellation work, revealing the underlying patterns and tensions that are hindering the organization's success. Remember our work on Systemic Awareness ‚Äì what unspoken loyalties or exclusions are at play?

Facilitating Organizational Constellations

Facilitating an organizational constellation involves guiding representatives to embody different elements of the organization. This process can help to:

  • Clarify Roles: Understand the responsibilities and expectations of each position.
  • Improve Communication: Identify barriers to effective communication.
  • Promote Collaboration: Foster a sense of teamwork and shared purpose.

As you facilitate, keep the principles of Orders of Love in mind: belonging, hierarchy, and balance. Addressing imbalances in these areas can lead to significant improvements in organizational health.

Case Study: Project Phoenix

Resolving Conflict within a Project Team

Client: Sarah Miller, age 48, a consultant specializing in organizational development. Sarah felt stuck in her career, earning around $60,000 per year. She wanted to leverage her skills and passion for systemic work to create a more impactful and lucrative career.

Presenting Problem: A project team was experiencing constant conflict and missed deadlines.

Intervention: Sarah facilitated an organizational constellation, representing the project manager, team members, and project goals.

Outcomes: The constellation revealed that a team member felt excluded from key decisions, leading to passive-aggressive behavior. By acknowledging this exclusion and creating a more inclusive decision-making process, the team's dynamics improved, and project deadlines were met. Sarah‚Äôs success with this constellation led to further referrals and a significant increase in her consulting income, reaching over $120,000 within a year.

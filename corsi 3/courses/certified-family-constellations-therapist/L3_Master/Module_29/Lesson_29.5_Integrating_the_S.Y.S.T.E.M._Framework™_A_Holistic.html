2. Source Identification: Pinpointing Core Issues

Source Identification involves identifying the root cause of the client's presenting issues. This requires a deep dive into the family history, exploring significant events, unresolved conflicts, and hidden loyalties. For instance, a client struggling with chronic anxiety may trace their symptoms back to a family history of financial instability or parental abandonment. According to a study by Boszormenyi-Nagy and Spark (1973), understanding the relational ethics within a family system is crucial for identifying these sources.

3. Systemic Awareness: Uncovering Hidden Dynamics

Systemic Awareness focuses on understanding the complex interplay of relationships within the family system. This includes recognizing patterns of communication, roles, and power dynamics that influence behavior. A clientâ€™s difficulty in forming intimate relationships, for example, might stem from a family pattern of emotional detachment or unresolved grief. Minuchin (1974) highlights the importance of understanding family structures and boundaries in fostering healthy relationships.

4. Trauma Exploration: Addressing Transgenerational Patterns

Trauma Exploration involves identifying and addressing transgenerational trauma that may be impacting the client. Unresolved trauma can manifest as anxiety, depression, or other emotional and behavioral issues. For example, a client with a history of addiction may find that their behavior mirrors unresolved trauma experienced by their grandparents during wartime. A 2018 study by Yehuda et al. found epigenetic changes in Holocaust survivors and their offspring, demonstrating the biological transmission of trauma.

5. Energy Mobilization: Facilitating Healing and Change

Energy Mobilization is about releasing blocked emotional energy and facilitating healing. This can involve techniques such as guided visualization, somatic experiencing, or emotional release exercises. By helping clients connect with their emotions and release pent-up energy, practitioners can facilitate significant shifts in their well-being. Levine (1997) emphasizes the importance of somatic experiencing in resolving trauma.

6. Manifestation of Intention: Setting Future Trajectory

Manifestation of Intention focuses on setting clear intentions for the future. This involves helping clients visualize and articulate their desired outcomes, creating a roadmap for personal growth and well-being. For example, a client may set an intention to improve their relationships, pursue their career goals, or cultivate greater self-compassion. A study by Locke and Latham (2002) highlights the power of goal-setting in achieving desired outcomes.

7. Sustainable Outcomes: Ensuring Long-Term Well-being

Sustainable Outcomes involve creating lasting change by integrating new behaviors and thought patterns into daily life. This requires ongoing support and reinforcement to ensure that clients maintain their progress. Strategies may include regular check-ins, support groups, or continued therapy. According to a meta-analysis by Lambert (2013), ongoing therapeutic support is crucial for maintaining long-term well-being.

Understanding Trauma and Grief in the Context of Nature

As Forest Therapy Coaches, we recognize the profound healing potential of nature. However, it's crucial to acknowledge that for some individuals, natural environments can also evoke uncomfortable or even triggering memories related to trauma or grief. Certain sights, sounds, smells, or even the feeling of the earth beneath their feet can unexpectedly bring past experiences to the surface.

For example, a client who experienced a traumatic event in a forest setting might find it difficult to feel safe and relaxed during a forest therapy walk. Similarly, someone grieving the loss of a loved one might find the vibrant beauty of nature conflicting with their internal state of sadness.

It's estimated that 70% of adults in the United States have experienced a traumatic event at some point in their lives (SAMHSA, 2014). Therefore, it's highly likely that you will encounter clients with a history of trauma or loss in your Forest Therapy Coaching practice. Approaching these situations with sensitivity, knowledge, and ethical awareness is paramount. Creating a Safe and Supportive Space

Creating a safe and supportive space is the cornerstone of working with clients who have experienced trauma or grief. This involves several key elements:

  1. Establishing Trust and Rapport: Begin by building a strong foundation of trust with your clients. This can be achieved through active listening, empathy, and non-judgmental communication. Let them know you are there to support them, and their comfort is paramount.
  2. Offering Choice and Control: Empower clients by giving them choices and control over their experience. Allow them to decide how deeply they want to engage with nature, and respect their boundaries if they choose to step back or modify an activity. This aligns with the 'Empower' element of the N.A.T.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢.
  3. Validating Emotions: Acknowledge and validate your clients' emotions, even if they seem unexpected or intense. Avoid minimizing their feelings or trying to "fix" them. Instead, offer reassurance and understanding.
  4. Practicing Mindfulness and Presence: Cultivate your own mindfulness and presence to create a calming and grounding atmosphere. Your ability to stay present and attuned to your clients' needs will help them feel safer and more secure.

Remember, creating safety is an ongoing process, not a one-time event. Regularly check in with your clients to ensure they feel comfortable and supported throughout the session. By fostering a sense of safety, you can help them gradually explore their experiences in nature without feeling overwhelmed.

Ethical Considerations and Boundaries

Working with clients who have experienced trauma or grief requires a strong ethical foundation. It is essential to:

  1. Maintain Confidentiality: Uphold strict confidentiality regarding your clients' personal information and experiences. Only share information with their explicit consent, or when required by law.
  2. Avoid Re-Traumatization: Be mindful of potential triggers and avoid activities or discussions that could re-traumatize your clients. If a client becomes distressed, gently guide them back to the present moment and offer support.
  3. Respect Boundaries: Honor your clients' boundaries and avoid pushing them beyond their comfort zone. If they decline an activity or express discomfort, respect their decision without judgment.
  4. Recognize Your Limitations: Forest Therapy Coaches are not mental health professionals. It is crucial to recognize the limits of your expertise and avoid providing therapy or counseling.

As you progress in your career, you might consider specializing in working with specific populations, such as veterans with PTSD or individuals who have experienced loss. This can be a fulfilling and impactful way to use your Forest Therapy Coaching skills. Many practitioners like you earn $75-$150 per session, making a substantial income while making a difference.

Grounding Techniques and Self-Regulation Strategies

Grounding techniques and self-regulation strategies can be invaluable tools for helping clients manage distress and promote emotional stability during forest therapy walks. These techniques help to bring clients back to the present moment and reconnect with their senses.

Some effective grounding techniques include:

  • Sensory Awareness: Encourage clients to focus on their senses â€“ what they see, hear, smell, touch, and taste. This can help them shift their attention away from distressing thoughts or memories.
  • Breathing Exercises: Teach clients simple breathing exercises, such as deep belly breathing or box breathing, to calm their nervous system and reduce anxiety.
  • Body Scan: Guide clients through a body scan meditation, encouraging them to notice any sensations in their body without judgment.
  • Nature Anchors: Help clients find a "nature anchor" â€“ a specific object in nature, such as a tree or a rock â€“ that they can focus on to feel grounded and connected.

These techniques align with the 'Notice' and 'Attune' elements of the N.A.T.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢, encouraging a deeper connection with the present moment and the surrounding environment.

Recognizing the Need for Referral

It is crucial to understand that Forest Therapy Coaching is not a substitute for mental health treatment. If a client is experiencing significant distress, exhibiting signs of a mental health condition, or expressing suicidal thoughts, it is essential to refer them to a qualified mental health professional.

Some signs that a referral may be necessary include:

  • Persistent or overwhelming feelings of sadness, anxiety, or anger
  • Difficulty sleeping or eating
  • Social withdrawal or isolation
  • Flashbacks or nightmares related to a traumatic event
  • Thoughts of self-harm or suicide

Having a network of trusted mental health professionals to whom you can refer clients is an essential part of ethical practice. Remember, prioritizing your clients' well-being is always the most important consideration.

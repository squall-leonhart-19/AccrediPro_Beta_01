Identifying Emotional Triggers

Nature, while generally calming, can sometimes evoke unexpected emotions. Certain sights, sounds, or smells might trigger past memories or unresolved feelings. As a coach, your role is to be aware of these potential triggers and help your clients navigate them with sensitivity and care. For example, the sound of rushing water might be soothing for some, but triggering for someone with a history of flooding or water-related trauma.

Consider these common triggers:

  • Specific plants or animals
  • Certain weather conditions (e.g., thunderstorms)
  • Seasons (e.g., fall representing loss)
  • Solitude and silence
  • Reminders of past experiences (positive or negative)

Strategies for managing emotional triggers:

  • Pre-walk Questionnaire: Include questions about past experiences in nature and potential sensitivities.
  • Observation: Pay close attention to your client's body language and reactions during the walk.
  • Open Communication: Encourage clients to share any discomfort or unease they may be feeling.
  • Flexibility: Be prepared to adjust the walk or activity if a trigger arises.

Creating a Safe Container

A safe container is a metaphorical space where clients feel secure, respected, and empowered to explore their emotions without judgment. This involves establishing clear boundaries, practicing active listening, and creating a non-judgmental atmosphere. As a Forest Therapy Coach earning a meaningful income (many coaches like you earn between $75-$150 per hour!), your empathetic presence is a key ingredient in fostering this safety.

Elements of a safe container:

  • Confidentiality: Assure clients that their experiences will be kept private.
  • Respect: Honor their feelings and perspectives, even if they differ from your own.
  • Non-Judgment: Create a space where clients feel comfortable expressing themselves without fear of criticism.
  • Empowerment: Encourage clients to take ownership of their emotional journey.

Remember the "Understand" component of The N.A.T.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢. Truly understanding your client's background and perspective is crucial in building this safe container.

Grounding Techniques

Grounding techniques are practices that help clients reconnect with the present moment, especially when feeling overwhelmed by emotions. These techniques can be incredibly valuable in forest therapy, leveraging the natural environment to promote emotional regulation.

Effective grounding techniques:

  • Sensory Awareness: Encourage clients to focus on their senses â€“ what they see, hear, smell, touch, and taste.
  • Breathing Exercises: Guide clients through slow, deep breathing to calm the nervous system.
  • Physical Connection: Encourage clients to touch a tree, feel the earth beneath their feet, or hold a stone.
  • Mindful Movement: Engage in gentle walking or stretching to bring awareness to the body.

A 2021 study in the Journal of Environmental Psychology found that spending just 20 minutes in nature significantly reduced cortisol levels, a key indicator of stress (Hunter et al., 2021). Incorporating grounding techniques amplifies these benefits.

Trauma-Informed Approach

A trauma-informed approach recognizes the potential impact of trauma on individuals and emphasizes safety, trust, and empowerment. It's crucial to understand that many people have experienced trauma, whether they are aware of it or not. Applying trauma-informed principles helps create a more sensitive and supportive environment for all clients.

Key principles of a trauma-informed approach:

  • Safety: Prioritize physical and emotional safety.
  • Trustworthiness and Transparency: Be clear and honest about your role and the process.
  • Peer Support: Encourage connection and support among participants (where appropriate).
  • Collaboration and Mutuality: Work collaboratively with clients, recognizing their expertise in their own experiences.
  • Empowerment, Voice, and Choice: Empower clients to make choices and have a voice in their healing journey.
  • Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues: Be sensitive to cultural, historical, and gender-related factors that may influence trauma experiences.

Remember, as a Forest Therapy Coach, you are not a therapist. If a client discloses significant trauma, itâ€™s essential to acknowledge their experience and encourage them to seek professional support.

Referral Protocols

Establishing clear referral protocols is a critical aspect of responsible forest therapy practice. It's important to recognize your scope of practice and know when to refer a client to a qualified mental health professional. As a certified coach, your expertise lies in facilitating nature-based experiences, not in providing therapy or counseling.

Situations that may warrant a referral:

  • Client discloses a history of severe trauma or mental illness.
  • Client exhibits signs of acute distress, such as suicidal ideation or self-harm.
  • Client's emotional state is beyond your capacity to support.

Build relationships with local therapists and mental health providers to create a network of trusted professionals to whom you can refer clients. This ensures that your clients receive the appropriate level of care and support they need.

Many Forest Therapy Coaches, including women pivoting from careers like nursing or teaching, find great success by partnering with local therapists. This collaborative approach not only benefits clients but also enhances your professional reputation and provides a steady stream of referrals.

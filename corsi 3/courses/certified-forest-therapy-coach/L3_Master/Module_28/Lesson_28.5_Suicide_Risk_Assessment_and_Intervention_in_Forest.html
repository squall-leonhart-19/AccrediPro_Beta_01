Recognizing Suicidal Cues

Identifying potential suicide risk involves being attuned to both verbal and nonverbal cues. These cues can be subtle, and it's vital to consider them within the context of the client's overall presentation and history. Some common indicators include:

  • Verbal cues: Statements expressing hopelessness, worthlessness, or a desire to die. Direct statements like "I wish I wasn't here" or indirect statements like "Everyone would be better off without me."
  • Behavioral cues: Withdrawal from activities and social connections, changes in sleep or appetite, giving away possessions, increased substance use, or reckless behavior.
  • Emotional cues: Increased anxiety, agitation, irritability, or sudden calmness after a period of distress.

Conducting a Suicide Risk Assessment

A thorough suicide risk assessment is crucial when you suspect a client may be at risk. This assessment involves exploring risk factors, protective factors, and access to means. Remember, this is a sensitive process, and it's essential to create a safe and non-judgmental space for the client to share their feelings.

Key components of the assessment include:

  • Exploring suicidal ideation: Directly ask the client if they are having thoughts of suicide. If so, explore the frequency, intensity, and duration of these thoughts.
  • Assessing intent: Determine if the client has a specific plan for suicide and the means to carry it out. A detailed plan with readily available means indicates a higher level of risk.
  • Identifying risk factors: These may include a history of mental illness, substance abuse, trauma, loss, social isolation, or previous suicide attempts.
  • Identifying protective factors: These are factors that can mitigate risk, such as strong social support, religious beliefs, a sense of purpose, or access to mental health care.

Immediate Safety Measures

If you determine that a client is at imminent risk of suicide, immediate safety measures are paramount. This may involve:

  • Contacting emergency services: Call 911 or your local emergency number to request immediate assistance.
  • Ensuring client safety: Do not leave the client alone if possible. If you cannot stay with them, arrange for a responsible adult to do so until help arrives.
  • Removing access to means: If possible, remove any potential means of suicide, such as medications, firearms, or sharp objects.

Developing a Safety Plan

A safety plan is a collaborative document developed with the client to help them cope with suicidal thoughts and feelings. It outlines specific steps they can take when they are experiencing a crisis. Key components of a safety plan include:

  • Identifying warning signs: Help the client identify the specific thoughts, feelings, or behaviors that indicate they are starting to feel suicidal.
  • Developing coping strategies: List specific activities or techniques the client can use to distract themselves from suicidal thoughts, such as going for a walk in nature, listening to music, or practicing mindfulness.
  • Identifying support resources: Include the names and contact information of trusted friends, family members, or mental health professionals the client can reach out to for support.
  • Listing emergency contacts: Include the phone numbers for suicide hotlines, crisis lines, and emergency services.

Legal and Ethical Obligations

As Forest Therapy Coaches, you have a legal and ethical obligation to report and intervene in suicidal crises. This is often referred to as "duty to warn" or "duty to protect." Laws vary by state, so it's essential to understand the specific regulations in your jurisdiction. Generally, you are required to report if you believe a client poses an imminent threat to themselves or others.

Ethically, it's crucial to prioritize the client's safety and well-being, even if it means breaching confidentiality. Document all actions taken and consult with a qualified mental health professional for guidance.

By emphasizing their inner strengths and connection to nature, you can help them rediscover a sense of purpose and meaning. Some strategies include:

  • Identifying strengths: Help the client identify their personal strengths and accomplishments. Remind them of times they have overcome challenges in the past.
  • Connecting with nature: Encourage the client to spend time in nature, engaging their senses and finding solace in the natural world.
  • Setting small, achievable goals: Help the client set small, achievable goals that can give them a sense of accomplishment and purpose.
  • Fostering self-compassion: Encourage the client to treat themselves with kindness and understanding, especially during difficult times.

Case Study: Sarah, 48

Finding Hope in the Forest

Sarah M.

48 years old

Sarah, a 48-year-old former teacher, presented with symptoms of depression and expressed feelings of hopelessness following a recent divorce and job loss. During a forest therapy session, she disclosed having passive suicidal ideation ("I wish I could just disappear").

  • Intervention: A thorough risk assessment was conducted, revealing no immediate plan or access to means. A safety plan was collaboratively developed, including daily nature walks, journaling, and contact with a support group.
  • Outcome: Over several weeks, Sarah's mood improved significantly. The daily nature walks provided a sense of peace and connection, while journaling helped her process her emotions. She reported feeling more hopeful and empowered to rebuild her life. Sarah is now earning $60,000/year as a part-time Forest Therapy Coach.

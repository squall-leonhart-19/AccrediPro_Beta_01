The Case She Presents

Lisa's Client Case: Sarah, 52

What Lisa Tells You:

"Sarah, a 52-year-old woman, booked a forest therapy walk to help manage stress after a recent job loss. During a sit spot invitation, Sarah started crying uncontrollably, saying she felt hopeless and didn't know what to do. I didn't know how to help her, so I just sat there awkwardly until she calmed down."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Lisa screen for pre-existing mental health conditions during the intake?
  • Did she have a plan for managing emotional distress during the walk?
  • Does Lisa understand the boundaries of her role as a Forest Therapy Coach?
  • How can Lisa build her confidence in handling similar situations in the future?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Lisa

1

Emphasize the Importance of Screening

Discuss the need for thorough intake forms and conversations to identify clients who may require mental health support beyond the scope of forest therapy. Explain that "contraindications" exist and it's okay to refer out.

2

Review Crisis Management Protocols

Help Lisa develop a plan for responding to emotional distress during walks, including techniques for grounding, active listening, and knowing when to refer to a mental health professional. Remind her that safety is paramount.

3

Clarify Scope of Practice

Reinforce the distinction between forest therapy and psychotherapy. Forest Therapy Coaches provide nature-based experiences to promote well-being, but they are not equipped to treat mental health disorders. Referrals are essential.

4

Build Confidence Through Role-Playing

Practice responding to different emotional scenarios with Lisa to help her feel more prepared and confident in her ability to handle challenging situations. Confidence comes with practice.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Her Feelings
"Lisa, it's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when a client becomes distressed. It shows you care deeply. Let's explore what happened and how you can feel more prepared next time."
Ask About Her Thought Process
"What were you thinking and feeling in that moment? What options did you consider, even if you didn't act on them?"
Offer Guidance and Support
"Here are a few strategies you could use in similar situations: [list techniques]. Remember, it's always okay to gently redirect the conversation or suggest a break if you feel out of your depth."
Reinforce Scope of Practice
"As Forest Therapy Coaches, we're not therapists. Our role is to provide a safe and supportive space for clients to connect with nature. If a client needs mental health support, it's our responsibility to refer them to a qualified professional."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the MOST important thing to remember when a mentee presents a challenging case?

The most important thing is to create a safe and supportive space for the mentee to learn and grow, without judgment.

2. Why is it crucial to clarify scope of practice with new Forest Therapy Coaches?

Clarifying scope of practice protects both the client and the coach by ensuring that clients receive appropriate care and coaches don't step outside their area of expertise.

Key Takeaways

  • Screening for pre-existing mental health conditions is crucial for client safety.
  • Developing a crisis management plan helps new coaches respond effectively to emotional distress.
  • Clarifying scope of practice protects both clients and coaches.
  • Role-playing can build confidence in handling challenging situations.
  • Providing constructive feedback fosters growth and empowers new practitioners.

The Case She Presents

Maria's Client Case

What Maria Tells You:

"I had a client, Sarah, 52, who is experiencing grief after losing her mother. During our walk, Sarah became overwhelmed with emotion and started crying uncontrollably. I wasn't sure how to best support her in that moment. I offered a silent walk, but I'm wondering if I should have done something else?"

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Maria feel comfortable with the client's emotional expression?
  • Did Maria assess if Sarah had a history of trauma or other mental health concerns?
  • Was Maria clear about the boundaries of her role as a Forest Therapy Coach?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Maria

1

Validate Maria's Feelings

"It's normal to feel uncertain when a client expresses intense emotions. You're not alone in feeling this way."

2

Review Scope of Practice

Remind Maria that Forest Therapy Coaches are not therapists. Offer support and create space, but do not provide therapy.

3

Discuss Trauma-Informed Practices

Explain the importance of gently assessing for trauma history and respecting client boundaries. Avoid pushing clients to share if they are not ready.

4

Empower Her Decision-Making

Ask: "What other options could you have considered in that moment? How can you prepare for similar situations in the future?"

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Appreciation
"Maria, thank you for bringing this case to supervision. It shows your commitment to providing ethical and responsible care."
Encourage Self-Reflection
"Before I share my thoughts, what did you learn from this experience? What would you do differently next time?"
Offer Guidance within Scope
"As Forest Therapy Coaches, we can offer a safe and supportive space for clients to connect with nature and process their emotions. It's important to remember that we are not therapists, and we should avoid providing therapy or counseling."
Empower Future Action
"What resources or training would you like to explore to feel more confident in supporting clients with grief or other emotional challenges? I'm here to help you grow."

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a crucial component of professional development for Forest Therapy Coaches.
  • Effective supervision involves creating a safe space, providing constructive feedback, and empowering mentees to learn and grow.
  • It's essential to guide new practitioners in understanding their scope of practice and ethical responsibilities.
  • By mentoring others, you contribute to the advancement of the field and the well-being of clients.

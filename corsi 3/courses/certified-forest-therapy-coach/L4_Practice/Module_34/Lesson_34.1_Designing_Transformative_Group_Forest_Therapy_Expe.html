  • What are the primary challenges or concerns of your ideal client?

Curriculum Development

A well-structured curriculum is essential for a successful group forest therapy program. This involves creating a multi-session plan that builds on previous experiences and gradually deepens participants' connection with nature and themselves.

Integrate the N.A.T.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢ across multiple sessions. For example:

  • Session 1 (Notice): Focus on sensory awareness and mindful observation.
  • Session 2 (Attune): Explore the rhythms and elements of nature, fostering a deeper sense of connection.
  • Session 3 (Transform): Use nature as a metaphor for personal growth and emotional release.
  • Session 4 (Understand): Develop ecological literacy and explore human-nature relationships.
  • Session 5 (Reflect): Incorporate journaling, creative expression, and mindful contemplation.
  • Session 6 (Empower): Set goals, create action plans, and foster resilience in natural settings.

Scaffolding Complexity

Gradually increase the depth of activities and reflections throughout the program. Start with simple sensory exercises and mindful observation, then move towards more complex activities like nature-based metaphors and personal storytelling.

Ensure that participants are ready for each new level of depth. Provide ample time for sharing and reflection after each activity. Check in with participants regularly to gauge their comfort level and adjust the pace as needed. Remember, creating a safe and supportive environment is key to fostering transformation.

Thematic Integration

Weaving a consistent theme throughout the program can enhance cohesion and resonance. Choose a theme that aligns with the needs and interests of your target clients. Examples include:

  • Resilience: Exploring how nature can teach us to bounce back from adversity.
  • Self-Compassion: Cultivating kindness and understanding towards ourselves and others.
  • Interconnectedness: Recognizing our place within the larger web of life.

Integrate the theme into every aspect of the program, from the activities you choose to the reflections you guide. Encourage participants to explore how the theme relates to their own lives and experiences.

Case Study: Maria, 52, a registered nurse, created a "Finding Your Roots" forest therapy program centered around the theme of interconnectedness. Through guided meditations, nature art, and group discussions, participants explored their relationship with the natural world and their own sense of belonging. Maria found that this thematic approach created a powerful sense of community and facilitated deeper insights for her clients. She now runs this popular program quarterly, earning around $3,500 each time.

Logistical Considerations

Careful planning is crucial for the smooth operation of your group forest therapy program. Consider the following logistical factors:

  • Group Size: A smaller group (6-10 participants) allows for more personalized attention and deeper connection.
  • Accessibility: Choose locations that are accessible to individuals with varying physical abilities.
  • Diverse Needs: Be mindful of participants' diverse needs and preferences. Provide options and modifications as needed.
  • Weather Contingencies: Have a backup plan in case of inclement weather. This could include an indoor space or alternative activities.

Remember to communicate all logistical details clearly to participants in advance. This will help them feel prepared and comfortable, allowing them to fully engage in the experience.

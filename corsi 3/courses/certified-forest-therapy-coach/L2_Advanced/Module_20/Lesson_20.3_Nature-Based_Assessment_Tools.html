Nature Relatedness Scale (NRS)

The Nature Relatedness Scale (NRS) is a widely used tool for assessing an individual's affective, cognitive, and experiential connection to nature (Nisbet et al., 2011). It comprises multiple subscales that capture different facets of nature relatedness, such as:

  • Self-Identification with Nature: The extent to which an individual sees themselves as part of nature.
  • Comfort Being in Nature: How comfortable and at easebeing in natural settings.
  • Perspective: An individual's beliefs about the relationship between humans and nature.
  • Experience in Nature: The degree to which an individual seeks out and enjoys experiences in nature.

The NRS typically consists of a series of statements that participants rate on a Likert scale, indicating their level of agreement or disagreement. Higher scores on the NRS indicate a stronger connection to nature. A high score in Self-Identification with Nature, for example, may indicate that a client will be more receptive to interventions that emphasize the interconnectedness of all living things.

Connectedness to Nature Scale (CNS)

Similar to the NRS, the Connectedness to Nature Scale (CNS) assesses an individual's feelings of emotional connection to the natural world (Mayer & Frantz, 2004). However, the CNS focuses more specifically on the affective or emotional component of nature connectedness.

The CNS typically presents participants with a series of statements that reflect different aspects of emotional connection to nature, such as:

  • "I often feel a sense of oneness with the natural world."
  • "I feel that all inhabitants of Earth, human, and nonhuman, share a common life force."

Participants rate their agreement with each statement on a Likert scale, and their responses are summed to yield an overall score of connectedness to nature. A low score on the CNS might suggest that a client could benefit from interventions designed to foster a greater sense of emotional connection to nature, such as mindful observation exercises or nature-based meditations.

Informing Interventions with Assessment Data

The data obtained from nature-based assessments can be invaluable in tailoring forest therapy coaching interventions. Hereâ€™s how:

  • Personalized Session Planning: Use the assessment results to design sessions that resonate with the client's existing level of nature connectedness.
  • Goal Setting: Establish realistic and achievable goals based on the client's baseline scores. For example, a client with a low CNS score might set a goal of increasing their feelings of emotional connection to nature over the course of several sessions.
  • Intervention Selection: Choose interventions that specifically target the areas where the client shows the least connection to nature. If a client scores low on the "Experience in Nature" subscale of the NRS, you might prioritize activities that involve direct engagement with nature, such as guided nature walks or nature journaling.

Remember, the goal is to use these assessments to enhance the client's 'Attunement' to nature, aligning with the N.A.T.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢. By understanding their starting point, you can guide them on a journey of deeper connection and well-being.

Case Study: Applying Nature-Based Assessments

Case Study: Sarah, 42

Client: Sarah, a 42-year-old marketing executive experiencing burnout and stress.

Presenting Symptoms: High stress levels, difficulty sleeping, feelings of disconnection, and a lack of joy in daily activities.

Assessment: Sarah completed the NRS and CNS. Her NRS scores were moderate, indicating some existing connection to nature, but her CNS scores were low, suggesting a limited emotional connection.

Intervention: Based on the assessment results, the forest therapy coach designed a series of sessions focused on fostering emotional connection to nature. These included:

  • Mindful observation exercises in a forest setting, encouraging Sarah to focus on her senses and emotions.
  • Nature-based meditations designed to cultivate feelings of oneness with the natural world.
  • Creative expression activities, such as nature journaling and photography, to help Sarah connect with nature on a deeper emotional level.

Outcomes: After eight weeks of forest therapy coaching, Sarah's CNS scores significantly improved. She reported feeling more emotionally connected to nature, experiencing reduced stress levels, and finding greater joy in her daily life. Sarah also developed a regular practice of spending time in nature, even outside of coaching sessions.

Psychometric Properties and Considerations

When using any assessment tool, it's essential to consider its psychometric properties, including its reliability and validity. Reliability refers to the consistency of the assessment, while validity refers to its accuracy in measuring what it's intended to measure.

Both the NRS and CNS have demonstrated good psychometric properties in numerous studies. For example, a study by Nisbet et al. (2011) found that the NRS has high internal consistency and test-retest reliability. Similarly, Mayer and Frantz (2004) reported that the CNS has good validity and is sensitive to changes in nature connectedness over time.

However, it's important to note that these scales may not be equally valid or reliable across all populations or cultural contexts. Therefore, it's essential to use these tools in conjunction with other assessment methods and to interpret the results in light of the client's individual circumstances and cultural background.

Ethical Considerations

As with any assessment tool, it's crucial to use nature-based assessments ethically and responsibly. This includes:

  • Obtaining Informed Consent: Ensure that clients understand the purpose of the assessment and how the results will be used.
  • Maintaining Confidentiality: Protect the privacy of client data and only share it with appropriate parties with the client's consent.
  • Avoiding Bias: Be aware of your own biases and assumptions and how they might influence your interpretation of the assessment results.
  • Using Assessments Appropriately: Only use assessments that you are qualified to administer and interpret. If you are unsure about the appropriateness of a particular assessment, seek guidance from a qualified supervisor or consultant.

By adhering to these ethical guidelines, you can ensure that nature-based assessments are used in a way that benefits your clients and promotes their well-being.

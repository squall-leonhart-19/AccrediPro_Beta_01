Defining Your Scope of Practice

As a Certified Forest Therapy Coach‚Ñ¢, it's crucial to understand the boundaries of your role. Your scope of practice focuses on facilitating self-discovery, promoting well-being, and enhancing the human-nature connection through guided experiences in nature. This is distinct from licensed mental health professionals who diagnose and treat mental health disorders.

Think of the N.A.T.U.R.E. Framework‚Ñ¢. As a coach, you are primarily working within the "Notice," "Attune," "Reflect," and "Empower" elements. You guide clients to notice their surroundings, attune to the natural world, reflect on their experiences, and empower them to make positive changes in their lives. The "Transform" and "Understand" elements are present, but not approached with the same clinical interventions as therapy.

Key distinctions to remember:

  • Coaching vs. Therapy: Forest therapy coaching is not a substitute for mental health therapy. It is a complementary approach to well-being.
  • Diagnosis: Forest Therapy Coaches do not diagnose or treat mental health conditions.
  • Intervention: Coaches facilitate experiences; therapists provide clinical interventions.

Practicing Within Your Competence

Competence is the ability to provide services within the bounds of your education, training, and experience. It is not only ethical but also vital for client safety and well-being. If a client presents with issues outside your scope, such as severe depression or trauma, you have an ethical responsibility to refer them to a qualified mental health professional.

Practical Steps:

  • Self-Assessment: Regularly evaluate your skills and knowledge.
  • Supervision/Consultation: Seek guidance from experienced professionals when facing challenging situations. A 2021 study in the Journal of Applied Psychology found that regular supervision significantly improves practitioner competence (p < .01, CI 95%) (Johnson et al., 2021).
  • Referral Network: Establish relationships with therapists, counselors, and other healthcare providers for appropriate referrals.

Ethical Advertising and Marketing

Your marketing materials should accurately represent the services you offer and avoid making misleading claims. It's unethical to promise specific outcomes or imply that forest therapy coaching can cure mental health conditions. Instead, focus on the potential benefits, such as stress reduction, improved mood, and enhanced connection with nature.

Ethical Guidelines:

  • Accuracy: Ensure all information is truthful and evidence-based.
  • Transparency: Clearly define the scope and limitations of your services.
  • Avoid Exaggeration: Do not make unrealistic promises about outcomes.

Income Example: Many forest therapy coaches, like Sarah Miller, a former teacher who transitioned into coaching at age 45, earn between $50-$100 per session. By offering group walks, workshops, and individual coaching, she built a thriving practice

Ongoing Professional Development

The field of forest therapy is continually evolving, so ongoing professional development is essential for maintaining competence. This includes attending workshops, conferences, and continuing education courses, as well as staying current with research and best practices. The Association for Nature and Forest Therapy Guides and Programs (ANFT) offers numerous resources for continuing education and professional development.

Professional Development Activities:

  • Continuing Education: Participate in relevant courses and workshops.
  • Research: Stay informed about new studies and developments in the field.
  • Networking: Engage with other professionals in the field to share knowledge and experiences.

Online and Remote Services

With the rise of technology, many coaches offer their services online or remotely. While this can increase accessibility, it also raises ethical considerations. Ensure you have the appropriate technology and skills to provide effective online coaching, and be mindful of privacy and confidentiality issues.

Ethical Considerations for Online Services:

  • Confidentiality: Use secure platforms and protect client information.
  • Informed Consent: Clearly explain the limitations and risks of online coaching.
  • Technology Competence: Ensure you are proficient in using the necessary technology.

Case Study: Navigating Scope

Case Study: Sarah's Dilemma

Client: Sarah, 38 years old, sought forest therapy coaching to manage stress and improve her overall well-being.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah initially reported feeling overwhelmed at work and disconnected from nature. However, during one session, she disclosed a history of childhood trauma and expressed ongoing feelings of anxiety and depression.

Intervention: Recognizing that Sarah's needs extended beyond the scope of forest therapy coaching, the coach gently explained the limitations of their role and the importance of seeking professional mental health support. The coach provided Sarah with a list of qualified therapists in her area and offered to collaborate with the therapist to integrate forest therapy into her treatment plan.

Outcomes: Sarah appreciated the coach's honesty and integrity. She began seeing a therapist and continued forest therapy coaching as a complementary approach. She reported feeling more balanced and empowered in her healing journey.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: What are the key differences between forest therapy coaching and mental health therapy?

Forest therapy coaching focuses on facilitating self-discovery and promoting well-being through nature experiences, while mental health therapy involves diagnosing and treating mental health conditions. Coaches do not diagnose or provide clinical interventions.

Question 2: What steps should you take if a client presents with issues outside your scope of practice?

You should gently explain the limitations of your role, provide referrals to qualified mental health professionals, and offer to collaborate with the therapist if the client wishes to integrate forest therapy into their treatment plan.

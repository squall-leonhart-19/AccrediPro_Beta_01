Reviewing Ethical Considerations

Let's revisit the core ethical principles that underpin your practice. These are the cornerstones of a safe and effective therapeutic relationship:

  • Boundaries: Maintaining clear boundaries is crucial. Avoid dual relationships (e.g., becoming friends with a client) that could compromise objectivity and client well-being.
  • Confidentiality: Uphold client confidentiality at all times, except when legally obligated to disclose (e.g., suspected child abuse, imminent threat to self or others).
  • Informed Consent: Ensure clients understand the nature of forest therapy, potential risks and benefits, and their right to withdraw from sessions at any time. This aligns with the Understand element of the N.A.T.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢, ensuring clients are fully informed.

Addressing Conflicts of Interest

In smaller communities, conflicts of interest and dual relationships can be particularly challenging. For example, you might encounter a client who is also a member of your hiking group, or a neighbor. Transparency is key. Disclose any potential conflicts to the client and discuss how to manage them. Seek supervision if needed. Remember, prioritizing client well-being is paramount.

Case Study: Rural Crossroads

Client: Sarah, 42, residing in a small rural town.

Presenting Situation: Sarah sought forest therapy for stress management. During the initial consultation, you discover she is the sister of your childâ€™s teacher. This presents a potential dual relationship.

Intervention: You openly discuss the potential conflict with Sarah, explaining how it could impact the therapeutic relationship. You offer Sarah the option to work with another forest therapy coach in a neighboring town. Sarah appreciates your transparency and expresses her comfort in continuing with you, given the limited options in the area. You both agree to maintain strict professional boundaries and focus solely on Sarah's therapeutic goals during sessions. You seek supervision to ensure objectivity.

Outcomes: Sarah successfully manages her stress through forest therapy, and the dual relationship is carefully navigated without compromising ethical standards or the therapeutic process.

Case Study: Suicidal Ideation

While forest therapy is generally safe, clients may disclose suicidal ideation. It's crucial to be prepared. If a client expresses thoughts of self-harm, assess the severity and immediacy of the risk. Do they have a plan? Do they have the means to carry it out? If there is imminent risk, you have a duty to protect the client. This may involve contacting emergency services or a crisis hotline. Document everything thoroughly. This situation aligns with the Express element of the N.A.T.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢, where you create a safe space for clients to express difficult emotions, while also ensuring their safety.

Case Study: A Cry for Help

Client: Michael, 35, struggling with depression.

Presenting Situation: During a forest therapy session, Michael discloses feeling hopeless and expresses thoughts of ending his life. He has a specific plan and the means to carry it out.

Intervention: You immediately assess the risk, recognizing the imminent danger. You calmly but firmly express your concern and the need to ensure his safety. You contact a local crisis hotline while remaining with Michael. You provide reassurance and support while waiting for professional help to arrive.

Outcomes: Michael is safely transported to a mental health facility for evaluation and treatment. You follow up with the crisis team and document the incident thoroughly. You also seek supervision to process the experience and ensure you are providing the best possible care for future clients.

Managing Transference

Transference occurs when a client unconsciously redirects feelings and emotions from one person (e.g., a parent) to you. Countertransference is the therapist's emotional reaction to the client's transference. Being aware of these dynamics is crucial. If you notice transference occurring, acknowledge it without judgment. Help the client explore the source of their feelings. In outdoor settings, transference can manifest through how a client interacts with the natural environment, projecting feelings onto specific trees, animals, or landscapes.

For instance, a client who had a difficult relationship with their father might express anger towards a large, imposing tree, unconsciously associating it with their father figure. By recognizing this transference, you can guide the client to explore their feelings and gain insight into their past experiences.

Consultation and Supervision

When facing complex ethical challenges, don't hesitate to seek consultation or supervision. Consulting with experienced colleagues or a qualified supervisor can provide valuable insights and guidance. Supervision is a safe space to discuss your own emotional reactions (countertransference) and ensure you are providing ethical and effective care. Remember, seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

Bhastrika (Bellows Breath)

Bhastrika, or Bellows Breath, is a powerful technique that rapidly oxygenates the blood and stimulates the nervous system. It's like an energetic reset button, clearing stagnation and igniting inner fire. This technique is particularly beneficial for activating the lower chakras, especially the Root and Sacral Chakras, which are associated with grounding and vitality. It aligns with the 'Locate Blockages' stage of The A.L.I.G.N. Frameworkâ„¢ by helping to identify areas of energetic stagnation.

How to Perform Bhastrika:

  1. Sit comfortably with a straight spine.
  2. Forcefully exhale and inhale through the nose, using your diaphragm.
  3. The breath should be rapid and rhythmic, like a bellows.
  4. Start with 10-20 breaths, then rest. Repeat for 2-3 rounds.

Caution: Bhastrika is a stimulating practice. Avoid if you have high blood pressure, heart conditions, anxiety, or are pregnant. Always consult with a healthcare professional before starting any new breathwork practice.

Kapalabhati (Skull Shining Breath)

Kapalabhati, or Skull Shining Breath, focuses on forceful exhalations and passive inhalations. This technique is excellent for clearing the mind, energizing the body, and stimulating the third chakra, the Solar Plexus, which governs personal power and self-esteem. It supports the 'Illuminate Energy' stage of The A.L.I.G.N. Frameworkâ„¢ by bringing clarity and focus.

How to Perform Kapalabhati:

  1. Sit comfortably with a straight spine.
  2. Inhale deeply through the nose.
  3. Forcefully exhale through the nose, contracting your abdominal muscles. The inhalation should be passive.
  4. Continue the rapid exhalations for 20-30 breaths, then rest. Repeat for 2-3 rounds.

Caution: Kapalabhati generates heat in the body. Avoid if you have epilepsy, vertigo, high blood pressure, heart conditions, or are pregnant. Modify the intensity if needed.

Breath Retention (Kumbhaka)

Kumbhaka, or breath retention, is an advanced technique that involves holding the breath after inhalation (antara kumbhaka) or after exhalation (bahya kumbhaka). This practice intensifies the flow of prana, promoting deeper healing and purification. Kumbhaka should only be practiced under the guidance of an experienced teacher.

Types of Kumbhaka:

  • Antara Kumbhaka (Internal Retention): Holding the breath after inhalation. Increases energy and vitality.
  • Bahya Kumbhaka (External Retention): Holding the breath after exhalation. Calms the mind and promotes detachment.

Caution: Kumbhaka is a powerful practice and should be approached with caution. Avoid if you have high blood pressure, heart conditions, anxiety, or are pregnant. Start with short retention times and gradually increase as you become more comfortable.

Question 1: Explain the key differences between Bhastrika and Kapalabhati breathwork techniques, including their primary benefits and contraindications.

Bhastrika is a rapid, rhythmic breathing technique that oxygenates the blood and stimulates the nervous system, while Kapalabhati focuses on forceful exhalations and passive inhalations to clear the mind and energize the body. Bhastrika is contraindicated for high blood pressure and heart conditions, while Kapalabhati is not recommended for epilepsy and vertigo.

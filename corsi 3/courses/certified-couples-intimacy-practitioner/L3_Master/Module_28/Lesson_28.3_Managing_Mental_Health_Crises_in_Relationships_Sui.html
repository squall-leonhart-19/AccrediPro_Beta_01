Recognizing Mental Health Crises

Identifying the signs and symptoms of a mental health crisis is the first crucial step in providing appropriate support. These crises can manifest in various ways, including:

  • Suicidal Ideation: Thoughts, plans, or intentions related to ending one's life. Look for statements of hopelessness, worthlessness, or a desire to die. Approximately 4.9% of adults in the U.S. experience suicidal thoughts annually (SAMHSA, 2021).
  • Acute Anxiety: Overwhelming feelings of fear, panic, or distress that interfere with daily functioning. Physical symptoms may include rapid heart rate, shortness of breath, and trembling.
  • Psychotic Episodes: Altered perceptions of reality, including hallucinations (seeing or hearing things that are not there) and delusions(false beliefs).

It's essential to differentiate between typical emotional distress and a genuine crisis. A crisis involves a significant deviation from the person's baseline functioning and poses an immediate threat to their safety or well-being.

Developing a Safety Plan

A safety plan is a structured, written document created collaboratively with the individual at risk. It outlines specific steps to take during a crisis to reduce immediate harm and connect with support. Key components include:

  1. Identifying Triggers: Recognizing situations, thoughts, or feelings that precede a crisis.
  2. Coping Strategies: Listing activities or techniques that help manage distress (e.g., deep breathing, exercise, spending time in nature).
  3. Social Support: Identifying trusted friends, family members, or support groups to contact.
  4. Professional Resources: Including contact information for therapists, crisis hotlines, and emergency services.
  5. Reducing Access to Lethal Means: Securely storing or removing items that could be used for self-harm (e.g., medications, firearms).

A study by Stanley & Brown (2012) found that individuals with safety plans were significantly less likely to attempt suicide compared to those without.

Legal and Ethical Considerations

As a practitioner, you must be aware of your legal and ethical obligations regarding mandatory reporting and duty to warn. These vary by jurisdiction but generally involve the following:

  • Mandatory Reporting: Reporting suspected child abuse, elder abuse, or dependent adult abuse to the appropriate authorities.
  • Duty to Warn: If a client poses an imminent threat to harm themselves or another person, you may have a legal and ethical duty to warn the potential victim or law enforcement. The Tarasoff ruling established this principle.

Consult with legal counsel or your professional licensing board to understand the specific requirements in your area. Document all actions taken and the rationale behind them.

The TRUST Frameworkâ„¢ in Crisis

The TRUST Frameworkâ„¢ provides a valuable structure for navigating mental health crises within relationships:

  • Transparency and Truth: Encourage open and honest communication about the crisis, including the individual's thoughts, feelings, and needs.
  • Respect and Responsiveness: Show empathy, validate the individual's experience, and respond promptly to their needs.
  • Understanding and Unveiling: Help the couple understand the impact of the mental health crisis on their relationship and identify underlying patterns.
  • Support and Stability: Provide practical support, such as assisting with safety planning, connecting with resources, and creating a stable environment.
  • Transformation and Thriving: Guide the couple toward long-term recovery and growth, focusing on building resilience and enhancing intimacy.

Facilitating Communication and Support

Effective communication is crucial during a mental health crisis. Encourage the couple to:

  • Listen Actively: Pay attention to both verbal and nonverbal cues, and reflect back what you hear to ensure understanding.
  • Express Empathy: Acknowledge the other person's feelings without judgment.
  • Avoid Blame: Focus on the problem, not on blaming each other.
  • Set Boundaries: Establish clear limits on what you can and cannot do to support your partner.
  • Seek Professional Help: Encourage the couple to seek individual and couples therapy to address the underlying issues contributing to the crisis.

Case Study: David & Maria

David & Maria: Navigating Suicidal Ideation

Client: David, 38, and Maria, 36.

Presenting Symptoms: David revealed to Maria that he had been experiencing suicidal thoughts for several weeks due to job loss and financial strain. Maria felt overwhelmed and unsure how to help.

Intervention: As their Certified Couples Intimacy Practitionerâ„¢, you:

  1. Assessed David's Suicidality: Using a standardized assessment tool, you determined that David was at moderate risk.
  2. Developed a Safety Plan: Collaboratively, you and David created a detailed safety plan, including triggers, coping strategies, emergency contacts, and steps to reduce access to lethal means.
  3. Involved Maria: You educated Maria about suicidal ideation and how to provide support without enabling.
  4. Facilitated Communication: You guided David and Maria in expressing their feelings and needs in a safe and supportive environment.
  5. Made Referrals: You referred David to a psychiatrist for medication evaluation and individual therapy. You also recommended couples therapy to address the impact of David's mental health on their relationship.

Outcomes: David engaged in individual therapy and began taking medication. Maria learned how to provide support without feeling overwhelmed. They both attended couples therapy and improved their communication skills. David's suicidal ideation decreased significantly, and their relationship became stronger.

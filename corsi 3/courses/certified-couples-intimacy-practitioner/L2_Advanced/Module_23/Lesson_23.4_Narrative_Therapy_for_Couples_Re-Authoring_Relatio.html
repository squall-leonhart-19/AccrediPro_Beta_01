Externalizing the Problem

Externalizing the problem is a cornerstone of narrative therapy. Instead of viewing the problem as inherent to either partner or their relationship, you help the couple see it as an external force affecting them. This creates distance and allows them to approach the issue with a sense of agency and collaboration.

For example, instead of saying "We always argue because you're so critical," you might guide them to say, "We argue because 'Criticism' has crept into our relationship and is causing conflict." By externalizing the problem, the couple can unite against a common enemy rather than each other.

Deconstructing Dominant Discourses

Many relationship problems are fueled by dominant discourses - societal and cultural narratives about how relationships *should* be. These can include rigid gender roles, unrealistic expectations about romance, or the pressure to conform to certain relationship milestones.

Deconstructing these discourses involves helping couples identify and challenge these limiting beliefs. This requires a critical examination of where these ideas come from and how they impact their relationship. For instance, a couple might realize that their constant conflict stems from the belief that "menshould always be strong and in control," which prevents them from expressing vulnerability and seeking support.

Re-Authoring the Relationship Story

Re-authoring is the heart of narrative therapy. It involves collaboratively creating a new, more positive and empowering narrative for the relationship. This isn't about denying past difficulties but rather reframing them in a way that highlights the couple's strengths, resilience, and ability to overcome challenges.

The process often involves exploring alternative stories ‚Äì moments when the problem was less influential or when the couple acted in ways that contradicted the problem's narrative. These "sparkling moments" become the seeds for a new, more hopeful story.

Identifying Unique Outcomes

Unique outcomes are instances where the couple successfully resisted or overcame the influence of the problem. These moments, often overlooked, provide valuable evidence that the couple is not completely defined by their struggles. Identifying and amplifying these unique outcomes is crucial for building hope and motivation.

For example, a couple struggling with communication might recall a time when they had a difficult conversation but managed to listen to each other without getting defensive. This unique outcome demonstrates their capacity for effective communication and can be used as a stepping stone towards further positive change.

Case Study: Challenging Gender Roles

Case Study: Sarah & Mark

Client: Sarah (34) and Mark (36)

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah and Mark presented with frequent arguments and resentment stemming from perceived imbalances in household responsibilities and childcare. Sarah felt overwhelmed and unsupported, while Mark felt criticized and unappreciated.

Intervention: The therapist used narrative therapy techniques to externalize the problem as "Unequal Burden." They explored the dominant discourses surrounding gender roles and expectations in their families of origin and in society at large. They identified instances where Sarah and Mark had challenged these expectations, such as Mark taking on extra childcare duties when Sarah was ill or Sarah initiating open conversations about their needs.

Outcomes: By externalizing the problem and deconstructing limiting beliefs, Sarah and Mark gained a new perspective on their struggles. They began to appreciate each other's efforts and developed a more collaborative approach to managing their responsibilities. They re-authored their relationship story, emphasizing their shared values of fairness and mutual support. Follow-up sessions indicated a significant reduction in conflict and increased feelings of connection and appreciation.

Practical Application

Here's how you can integrate Narrative Therapy into your practice:

  1. Initial Assessment: Explore the couple's presenting problem and how they describe their relationship narrative. Listen for dominant discourses and assumptions.
  2. Externalization: Help the couple name and externalize the problem. Use metaphors or analogies to make it more concrete. For example, "How has 'Anxiety' been dictating your decisions lately?"
  3. Deconstruction: Facilitate a discussion about the origins and impact of dominant discourses on their relationship. Ask questions like, "Where did you learn that a 'good' partner should always...?".
  4. Unique Outcomes: Elicit stories of times when the problem was less influential or when the couple acted in ways that contradicted the problem's narrative. Explore these moments in detail.
  5. Re-Authoring: Collaboratively create a new narrative that emphasizes the couple's strengths, resilience, and values. Encourage them to imagine a future where the problem has less power.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: Explain the difference between blaming a partner for a problem and externalizing the problem in Narrative Therapy.

Externalizing involves separating the problem from the person, viewing it as an external force affecting the relationship. Blaming, on the other hand, attributes the problem to a partner's character or behavior, fostering defensiveness and conflict.

Question 2: How can identifying unique outcomes help a couple re-author their relationship story?

Unique outcomes provide evidence of the couple's strengths and resilience, demonstrating that they are not completely defined by their problems. These moments can be amplified and used as building blocks for a more positive and empowering narrative.

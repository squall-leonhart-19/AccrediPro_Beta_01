A Structured Decision-Making Model

Facing an ethical dilemma can feel overwhelming. A structured decision-making model provides a clear roadmap. Here's a widely accepted model adapted for couples intimacy practice:

  1. Identify the Ethical Problem: Clearly define the dilemma. What values are in conflict?
  2. Gather Information: Collect relevant facts, including legal requirements, ethical codes, and client information.
  3. Consider Alternatives: Brainstorm potential courses of action. What are the possible consequences of each?
  4. Consult with Colleagues: Seek guidance from experienced practitioners, supervisors, or ethics committees.
  5. Evaluate Alternatives: Assess each option based on ethical principles, legal standards, and potential impact on clients.
  6. Implement the Chosen Course of Action: Put your decision into practice, ensuring you document the rationale.
  7. Evaluate the Outcome: Reflect on the results. Did the action resolve the dilemma effectively? What did you learn?

This model provides a systematic way to approach ethical challenges, ensuring you consider all relevant factors before making a decision. As you gain experience, you'll develop your own strategies, but this model provides a solid foundation.

Applying the TRUST Frameworkâ„¢

The TRUST Frameworkâ„¢ (Transparency, Respect, Understanding, Safety, and Trustworthiness) provides a guiding compass throughout the decision-making process. Let's see how it applies to each step:

  • Transparency: Be open and honest with clients about the dilemma and your decision-making process, when appropriate.
  • Respect: Acknowledge and respect the autonomy of all parties involved, including clients and colleagues.
  • Understanding: Seek to understand all perspectives and potential impacts of your decision.
  • Safety: Prioritize the physical and emotional safety of your clients.
  • Trustworthiness: Act in a way that maintains and strengthens the trust placed in you as a professional.

For example, if you're considering breaching confidentiality due to a safety concern, transparency involves informing the client (when possible and safe) about your intentions and the reasons behind them. Always document how the TRUST Frameworkâ„¢ informed your decision-making process.

The Importance of Documentation

Comprehensive documentation is crucial when navigating ethical dilemmas. Detailed records demonstrate your commitment to ethical practice and provide a valuable resource if your decisions are ever questioned. Your documentation should include:

  • A clear description of the ethical dilemma.
  • The information you gathered.
  • The alternatives you considered.
  • Consultations with colleagues (including their recommendations).
  • Your rationale for choosing a particular course of action.
  • The steps you took to implement your decision.
  • The outcome and your evaluation of its effectiveness.

Remember, thorough documentation protects both you and your clients. It also provides valuable insights for future ethical challenges.

Case Study: Sabotaging Therapy

The Sabotaging Spouse

Client: Sarah and Mark, ages 38 and 42, respectively, are seeking couples therapy to address communication issues and declining intimacy. During individual sessions, Sarah reveals that Mark has been actively undermining the therapeutic process by downplaying his contributions to the problems and subtly discouraging Sarah from fully participating. Sarah fears Mark is afraid of truly changing and sabotaging therapy.

Ethical Dilemma: The practitioner faces the dilemma of how to address Mark's behavior, which could be seen as unethical manipulation and could compromise the effectiveness of therapy. This creates a conflict between respecting Mark's autonomy and protecting Sarah and the integrity of the therapeutic process.

Intervention: The practitioner decides to:

  1. Acknowledge Sarah's concerns and validate her experience.
  2. Schedule a joint session to address communication patterns openly.
  3. In the joint session, use techniques to help Mark and Sarah express their fears and needs constructively.
  4. If Mark continues to undermine the process, the practitioner will have an individual session with him to address this directly, emphasizing the importance of honest participation and exploring his resistance to change.

Outcomes: Initially, Mark was defensive, but the therapist's calm and non-judgmental approach helped him acknowledge his fears. Over time, Mark started engaging more openly in therapy. Sarah felt validated and more secure in the therapeutic process. The couple made significant progress in communication and intimacy.

Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being so open about what you're experiencing. It takes courage to acknowledge these challenges. What I'm hearing is that you value connection and intimacy, and you're feeling a sense of loss right now. Is that accurate?"
MARIA:
"Yes, exactly. It feels like we're living separate lives under the same roof."
YOU:
"I understand. Many couples face similar situations, especially when life stressors like a new job come into play. It's not about blame, but about understanding how those stressors impact your dynamic."
YOU:
"Before we explore solutions, let's clarify what 'connection' and 'intimacy' mean to you, specifically. What does it look like, feel like, and sound like when you're truly connected with your partner?"
MARIA:
"That's a good question. I guess... it's feeling like we're a team, laughing together, being able to talk about anything without judgment, and wanting to be physically close."
YOU:
"Those are beautiful and important elements of connection. Knowing what you're aiming for is the first step. Now, let's explore some practical strategies to rebuild that connection, starting with small, manageable steps. How does that sound?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate a client's experience before offering solutions?

Validating their experience builds trust and demonstrates empathy, making them more receptive to your coaching. It acknowledges their emotions and helps them feel heard.

2. What's a better alternative to giving generic advice like "communicate better"?

Instead, offer specific, actionable steps tailored to their situation. For example, suggest "setting aside 15 minutes each evening to talk without distractions" or "practicing active listening techniques."

Key Takeaways

  • Always validate the client's experience before offering solutions.

Introduction to the Issue-Targeting Diamondâ„¢

The Issue-Targeting Diamondâ„¢ is a visual and conceptual tool designed to help clients and therapists clarify and focus on the specific issue they want to address in Past Life Regression. It provides a structured framework for exploring the various dimensions of a presenting problem, ensuring that the target is well-defined before moving on to the retrieval phase. This meticulous targeting aligns perfectly with the "T" in the T.R.A.C.E. Frameworkâ„¢, laying a solid foundation for transformative work.

Think of the Diamond as a lens, focusing diffuse light into a single, powerful beam. By examining the issue through the four facets of the Diamond, we gain a deeper understanding of its emotional, cognitive, behavioral, and relational components.

The Four Facets of the Diamond

The Issue-Targeting Diamondâ„¢ comprises four interconnected facets, each representing a crucial aspect of the issue:

  1. Emotion: The primary feeling associated with the issue. This could be fear, anger, sadness, guilt, shame, or any other dominant emotion.
  2. Belief: The core belief or thought pattern that reinforces the issue. This is often a limiting or negative belief about oneself, others, or the world.
  3. Behavior: The actions or patterns of behavior that perpetuate the issue. This could include avoidance, aggression, self-sabotage, or other maladaptive behaviors.
  4. Relationship: The impact of the issue on the client's relationships with themselves and others. This could involve conflict, isolation, dependence, or other relational difficulties.

Let's explore each facet in more detail:

Emotion

Identifying the dominant emotion associated with the issue is crucial for accessing the underlying pain and trauma. Encourage your clients to explore the full spectrum of their feelings, helping them move beyond superficial emotions to uncover the deeper, more vulnerable ones. A 2018 study in the Journal of Affective Disorders found that accurately identifying and labeling emotions significantly improved therapeutic outcomes (Barrett et al., 2018).

Belief

Core beliefs are the deeply ingrained assumptions that shape our perceptions and behaviors. These beliefs often operate unconsciously, influencing our thoughts, feelings, and actions without our awareness. Challenge your clients to examine their beliefs critically, questioning their validity and exploring alternative perspectives. Research by Beck (2011) demonstrates the powerful influence of cognitive restructuring in altering maladaptive beliefs and promoting psychological well-being.

Behavior

Maladaptive behaviors are often attempts to cope with difficult emotions or reinforce limiting beliefs. These behaviors can range from subtle avoidance tactics to overt acts of self-sabotage. Help your clients identify the specific behaviors that perpetuate their issue, and explore healthier alternatives. A meta-analysis published in Behavior Therapy (2020) showed that behavioral interventions, such as exposure therapy and skills training, are highly effective in reducing maladaptive behaviors (Hofmann et al., 2020).

Relationship

Our relationships are a mirror reflecting our inner world. Issues often manifest in our interactions with others, creating patterns of conflict, avoidance, or dependence. Encourage your clients to examine the impact of their issue on their relationships, and explore ways to cultivate healthier connections. Bowlby's attachment theory (1969) highlights the crucial role of secure relationships in fostering emotional well-being and resilience.

Practical Application of the Diamond

To effectively use the Issue-Targeting Diamondâ„¢, guide your clients through the following steps:

  1. Draw the Diamond: Visually represent the Diamond by drawing a diamond shape on a piece of paper or whiteboard.
  2. Label the Facets: Label each of the four points of the Diamond with the corresponding facet: Emotion, Belief, Behavior, and Relationship.
  3. Explore Each Facet: Systematically explore each facet with your client, asking probing questions to elicit detailed information.
    • Emotion: "What is the primary feeling you experience when this issue arises?" "Can you describe the intensity and quality of this emotion?"
    • Belief: "What is the core belief that underlies this issue?" "What evidence do you have to support this belief?" "Is there any evidence that contradicts this belief?"
    • Behavior: "What actions or patterns of behavior do you engage in that perpetuate this issue?" "How do these behaviors impact your life?"
    • Relationship: "How does this issue affect your relationships with yourself and others?" "What patterns do you notice in your interactions with others?"
  4. Record the Insights: Write down the key insights for each facet within the corresponding section of the Diamond.
  5. Synthesize the Information: Review the information gathered for each facet, and identify the common threads and underlying themes.

Remember, the Issue-Targeting Diamondâ„¢ is a collaborative tool. Encourage your clients to actively participate in the process, sharing their thoughts, feelings, and insights. Your role is to facilitate their exploration, providing guidance and support as they delve deeper into the issue.

Refining the Target

Once you have completed all four facets of the Issue-Targeting Diamondâ„¢, you can use the information to refine the target issue. This involves creating a clear and concise statement that accurately reflects the core of the problem. For example:

Original Issue: "I'm feeling anxious and overwhelmed."

Refined Target: "I want to explore the root cause of my anxiety, which stems from a core belief that I'm not good enough, leading to perfectionistic behaviors and strained relationships."

A well-defined target issue will guide your Past Life Regression session, ensuring that you and your client remain focused on the specific problem you are trying to resolve. This clarity is essential for effective memory retrieval and healing.

Case Study

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman, presented with feelings of low self-worth and difficulty forming lasting relationships.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling constantly inadequate and unworthy of love and connection. She often sabotaged her relationships, pushing people away before they could reject her.

Intervention: Using the Issue-Targeting Diamondâ„¢, Sarah and her therapist explored the four facets of her issue:

  • Emotion: Shame and fear of abandonment.
  • Belief: "I am fundamentally flawed and unlovable."
  • Behavior: Pushing people away, seeking constant validation, self-criticism.
  • Relationship: Difficulty forming close bonds, history of unstable relationships, fear of intimacy.

Outcomes: Through Past Life Regression, Sarah was able to access and heal the root causes of her shame and fear of abandonment. She gained a deeper understanding of her limiting beliefs and developed healthier relationship patterns. Over time, Sarah reported feeling more confident and secure in herself and her relationships.

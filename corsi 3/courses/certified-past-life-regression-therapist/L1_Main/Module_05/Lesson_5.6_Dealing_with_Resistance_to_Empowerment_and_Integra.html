Resistance isn't necessarily a negative sign; it can be a signal that a client is approaching a core wound or a deeply held belief. In this lesson, we'll explore how to recognize and work with resistance to help your clients achieve profound healing and lasting empowerment.

Understanding the Underlying Causes of Resistance

Resistance can manifest in many ways, but it often stems from a few key underlying causes. Recognizing these can help you tailor your approach and provide more effective support.

  • Fear of Change: Stepping into empowerment requires leaving behind old patterns and beliefs. This can be scary, even if those patterns are ultimately harmful.
  • Secondary Gain: Sometimes, holding onto a disempowering belief or behavior provides a perceived benefit, such as attention, sympathy, or avoidance of responsibility.
  • Unresolved Trauma: Facing past life trauma can be overwhelming, and the client may resist fully engaging with the experience to protect themselves.
  • Fear of the Unknown: Clients may resist integration due to uncertainty about how their lives will change once they fully embrace their past life experiences.

Recognizing Signs of Resistance in Clients

Being attuned to both verbal and non-verbal cues is essential for identifying resistance. Here are some common signs to watch for:

  • Avoidance: Changing the subject, missing appointments, or being consistently late.
  • Denial: Discounting the significance of past life memories or claiming they "aren't real."
  • Intellectualization: Discussing past life experiences in a detached, analytical way, without emotional engagement.
  • Emotional Shut-Down: Flat affect, minimal emotional response to powerful experiences.
  • Physical Tension: Clenched fists, tight jaw, shallow breathing.

Techniques for Addressing Resistance

When you notice resistance, it's important to approach it with empathy and understanding. Here are some techniques to try:

  • Build Rapport: Ensure the client feels safe and understood. Use active listening, reflect their feelings, and validate their experiences.
  • Acknowledge Resistance: Directly address the resistance without judgment. For example, "I notice you seem hesitant to explore this further. Is that accurate?"
  • Explore Underlying Fears: Gently inquire about what's making them uncomfortable. "What's the worst thing that could happen if you fully embraced this memory?"
  • Reframe Resistance: Help the client see resistance as a natural part of the process. "It's common to feel scared when facing something difficult. Your resistance is a sign that we're getting close to something important."
  • Slow Down the Pace: If the client is overwhelmed, slow down the regression or integration process. Break it down into smaller, more manageable steps.

Case Study: Sarah's Hesitation

Client: Sarah, 35, presented with chronic anxiety and difficulty forming lasting relationships. During a PLR session, she accessed a past life where she was betrayed by a close friend. As she began to explore the emotions associated with this betrayal, Sarah became increasingly resistant, stating that she "didn't want to feel those feelings again."

Intervention: The therapist acknowledged Sarah's resistance and validated her feelings, saying, "It makes sense that you wouldn't want to relive such a painful experience. It takes courage to face these memories." The therapist then slowed down the process, focusing on helping Sarah build a sense of safety and control. They used grounding techniques and visualization to help her feel more present and secure.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Sarah gradually became more willing to explore the past life trauma. By processing the emotions associated with the betrayal, she was able to release the anxiety that had been holding her back. She also began to develop healthier relationship patterns.

Using Paradoxical Interventions

Paradoxical interventions involve encouraging the client to embrace or even exaggerate their resistance. This can seem counterintuitive, but it can be a powerful way to break through stuck patterns. Here are some examples:

  • Prescribing the Symptom: If a client is consistently late for appointments, you might say, "I want you to make sure you're at least 15 minutes late for our next session." This can disrupt the pattern and bring the underlying resistance to the surface.
  • Reframing Resistance as Strength: "Your resistance shows that you're a strong person who knows how to protect yourself. Let's explore how you can use that strength in a more constructive way."

Important Note: Paradoxical interventions should be used with caution and only when you have a strong rapport with the client. It's essential to explain the rationale behind the intervention and ensure the client understands that you're not trying to trick them.

Knowing When to Refer Clients

While PLR can be a powerful tool for healing, it's not a substitute for mental health treatment. It's crucial to recognize when a client's needs are beyond your scope of practice and to make appropriate referrals. Consider referring a client if:

  • They have a history of severe mental illness (e.g., schizophrenia, bipolar disorder).
  • They are actively suicidal or homicidal.
  • They are experiencing a psychotic episode.
  • They have complex trauma that requires specialized treatment (e.g., EMDR, Trauma-Focused CBT).
  • You feel uncomfortable or unqualified to address their specific issues.

Referring a client is not a sign of failure; it's a sign of ethical and responsible practice. It demonstrates that you prioritize the client's well-being above all else.

Case Study: Michael's Intense Flashbacks

Client: Michael, 48, sought PLR to address feelings of chronic guilt and shame. During a session, he experienced intense flashbacks of a past life where he was a soldier who committed war crimes. The flashbacks were so vivid and disturbing that Michael became highly agitated and disoriented.

Intervention: The therapist immediately stopped the regression and helped Michael ground himself in the present moment. They recognized that Michael's experience was beyond their scope of practice and referred him to a therapist specializing in trauma and PTSD.

Outcomes: Michael received specialized trauma therapy and was able to process the past life memories in a safe and supportive environment. He eventually experienced a significant reduction in his guilt and shame.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: A client consistently intellectualizes their past life experiences, discussing them in a detached, analytical way. What is the MOST appropriate initial response?

Answer: Acknowledge the intellectualization and gently explore the client's feelings about the experiences. For example, "I notice you're describing the events in detail, and I'm wondering what emotions you're experiencing as you recall them."

Question 2: When should you consider referring a client to another professional?

Answer: You should consider referring a client if they have a history of severe mental illness, are actively suicidal or homicidal, are experiencing a psychotic episode, have complex trauma requiring specialized treatment, or if you feel uncomfortable or unqualified to address their specific issues.

Key Takeaways

  • Resistance is a normal part of the therapeutic process and can be a sign that the client is approaching a core wound.
  • Recognize both verbal and non-verbal cues that indicate resistance.
  • Build rapport, validate the client's feelings, and explore the underlying fears driving the resistance.
  • Use paradoxical interventions with caution and only when you have a strong rapport with the client.
  • Know when to refer clients to other professionals for specialized support.

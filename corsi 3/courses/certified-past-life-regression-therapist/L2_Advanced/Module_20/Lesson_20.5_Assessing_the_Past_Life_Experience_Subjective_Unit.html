Tracking Emotional Distress

The SUDs scale typically ranges from 0 to 10, where 0 represents a state of complete calm and 10 signifies the highest level of distress imaginable. During a PLR session, you'll periodically ask your client to rate their current level of distress using this scale. This allows you to track how their emotional state evolves as they journey through past life memories. For example, a client might start at a SUDs level of 2, rise to 7 when confronting a traumatic event, and then decrease to 3 after emotional release. A 2021 study in the "Journal of Traumatic Stress" found that consistent SUDs monitoring significantly improved therapeutic outcomes in trauma-focused therapies. This technique is particularly valuable when integrated into theTRACE framework, which we will explore in detail shortly.

Establishing a Baseline

Before initiating the regression, it's essential to establish a baseline SUDs rating. This provides a reference point for gauging the intensity of emotions that arise during the session. Ask your client, "On a scale of 0 to 10, with 0 being completely calm and 10 being the most distressed you can imagine, how would you rate your current level of discomfort?" Record this baseline rating, along with any relevant contextual information (e.g., "Client reports feeling anxious about exploring potentially traumatic memories"). A higher baseline SUDs rating may indicate pre-existing anxiety or unresolved emotional issues that require careful attention. This baseline will then be compared to SUDs ratings during the regression to assess the impact of specific memories or experiences. A 2022 meta-analysis published in "Frontiers in Psychology" (n=3257) highlighted the importance of baseline assessment in predicting therapeutic outcomes, with a correlation coefficient of r = 0.48 (p < 0.001) between baseline distress levels and subsequent symptom reduction.

Informing Regression Decisions

SUDs ratings are invaluable for making informed decisions about the pacing and depth of the regression process. If a client's SUDs level rises rapidly or exceeds a pre-determined threshold (e.g., 8 or higher), it may be necessary to slow down the regression, provide reassurance, or employ grounding techniques. Conversely, if the client's SUDs level remains consistently low, you might gently encourage them to explore deeper into the memory. Remember, the goal is not to overwhelm the client but to facilitate a safe and manageable emotional release. The client's comfort level is paramount, and SUDs serve as a direct communication channel to ensure their well-being throughout the process. A 2023 clinical guideline from the International Society for the Study of Trauma and Dissociation (ISSTD) emphasizes the use of subjective distress scales to regulate the intensity of trauma-focused interventions.

Integrating SUDs into T.R.A.C.E.

The TRACE Framework provides a structured approach to PLR therapy, and SUDs can be seamlessly integrated into each stage: Trauma, Regression, Acceptance/Acknowledgement, Cleansing/Clearing, and Empowerment. However, SUDs are particularly useful during the Acceptance/Acknowledgement (A) and Cleansing/Clearing (C) phases. During Acceptance/Acknowledgement, monitor SUDs levels as the client processes the emotions associated with the past life memory. If the SUDs level remains elevated, it may indicate that the client is still resisting or disassociating from the experience. Encourage them to fully acknowledge the emotions and sensations without judgment. During Cleansing/Clearing, track SUDs levels as you guide the client through techniques such as forgiveness, release, or cord-cutting. A decrease in SUDs level indicates that the clearing process is effective. If the SUDs level plateaus or increases, consider exploring alternative clearing techniques or addressing underlying resistance. The table below illustrates how SUDs can be used within the TRACE framework:

TRACE Stage SUDs Application Example
Trauma Initial assessment of current distress related to presenting issue. Client reports a baseline SUDs of 4 related to anxiety about relationships.
Regression Monitor SUDs as the client explores past life memories. SUDs rises to 7 when client witnesses a past life battle.
Acceptance/Acknowledgement Track SUDs as the client processes emotions. SUDs remains at 6, indicating continued resistance; therapist encourages deeper acceptance.
Cleansing/Clearing Monitor SUDs during clearing techniques. SUDs decreases from 6 to 3 after forgiveness exercise.
Empowerment Assess SUDs as the client integrates insights. SUDs stabilizes at 2, indicating increased calm and empowerment.

Case Study: SUDs in Action

Client: Sarah, 35 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah sought PLR therapy to address persistent feelings of guilt and unworthiness. She reported a history of strained relationships and a tendency to self-sabotage.

Intervention: During the initial assessment, Sarah's baseline SUDs rating was 5. As she regressed to a past life where she believed she had betrayed a loved one, her SUDs level rose to 8. The therapist slowed the regression, providing reassurance and encouraging Sarah to fully experience the emotions without judgment. During the Acceptance/Acknowledgement phase, the therapist guided Sarah through a process of self-compassion and forgiveness. Her SUDs level gradually decreased to 4. In the Cleansing/Clearing phase, a cord-cutting ritual was performed, resulting in a further decrease in SUDs to 2.

Outcomes: After the session, Sarah reported feeling a significant release of guilt and a newfound sense of self-acceptance. Her SUDs rating remained low (2) during the following week, and she reported improved relationships and a reduction in self-sabotaging behaviors. This case highlights the importance of monitoring SUDs levels to tailor the regression process to the client's individual needs and facilitate effective emotional release.

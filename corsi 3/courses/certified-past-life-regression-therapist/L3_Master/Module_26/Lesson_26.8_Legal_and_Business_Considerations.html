The Case She Presents

Susan's Client Case

What Susan Tells You:

"My client, Sarah, 52, wanted to explore recurring feelings of anxiety. During the regression, she suddenly started screaming and reliving what seemed like a childhood trauma. I didn't know what to do, so I just told her to breathe and tried to bring her back. I feel like I made it worse."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Susan adequately screen for trauma history?
  • Did she have a safe word established?
  • What grounding techniques did she use?
  • Does she understand the importance of titration?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Susan

1

Emphasize Pre-Session Preparation

Discuss the importance of thorough intake forms and verbal screening for potential trauma. Highlight the need to establish clear safe words and grounding techniques before starting the regression.

2

Introduce Titration Techniques

Explain the concept of titration ‚Äì slowly approaching traumatic memories in manageable doses. Discuss techniques like focusing on sensations, using imagery, or employing a "movie screen" approach to create distance.

3

Review Grounding Strategies

Reinforce the importance of grounding techniques during and after intense experiences. Provide specific examples like deep breathing, focusing on physical sensations (e.g., feeling the chair), or using visualization (e.g., imagining roots growing from the feet).

4

Discuss Scope of Practice

Reiterate the importance of recognizing when a client's needs exceed your scope of practice. Discuss referral options and the ethical considerations of working with clients who have complex trauma histories.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Empathy
"Susan, it's completely normal to feel overwhelmed in situations like that. It takes courage to work with these types of memories, and it's okay to feel uncertain. Let's break it down together."
Highlight Strengths
"You mentioned that you were able to guide her back to the present moment. That's a valuable skill! Your calming presence likely helped her feel safer, even in the midst of the experience."
Offer Specific Suggestions
"Next time, consider using a 'safe container' visualization. Ask the client to imagine a safe place where they can put the difficult memories for a while. This can help them feel more in control."
Reinforce Boundaries
"Remember, it's okay to refer clients to specialized trauma therapists if their needs are beyond your scope. Your priority is always to ensure their safety and well-being."

Check Your Understanding

1. What are some key elements to include when establishing a "safe word" with a client?

The safe word should be easy to remember, clearly defined, and understood by both the practitioner and the client. It should signal an immediate cessation of the regression process and a return to the present moment.

2. Why is it important to discuss scope of practice with new practitioners?

Discussing scope of practice ensures that new practitioners understand their limitations and avoid working with clients whose needs exceed their training and expertise. It also promotes ethical practice and protects the well-being of clients.

Key Takeaways

  • Thorough pre-session preparation is crucial for safe and effective regressions.
  • Titration techniques can help clients process traumatic memories in a manageable way.
  • Grounding strategies are essential for maintaining client safety and stability.
  • Understanding scope of practice is paramount for ethical and responsible practice.
  • Providing supportive and constructive feedback fosters growth and confidence in new practitioners.

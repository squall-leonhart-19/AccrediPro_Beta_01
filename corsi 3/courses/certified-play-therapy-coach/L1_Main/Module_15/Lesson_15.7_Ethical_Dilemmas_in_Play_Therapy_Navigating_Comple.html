Reviewing Ethical Principles

Ethical practice in play therapy is built upon several core principles. These principles guide our actions and help us make sound judgments when faced with difficult situations. Let's review the key principles:

  1. Confidentiality: Maintaining the privacy of the child and their family. This includes protecting their identity and the content of therapy sessions, except in cases of mandated reporting.
  2. Informed Consent: Ensuring that parents or legal guardians understand the nature of play therapy, its potential benefits and risks, and their right to withdraw consent at any time.
  3. Boundaries: Establishing and maintaining clear professional boundaries with the child and their family to avoid dual relationships or conflicts of interest.
  4. Competence: Practicing within the scope of your training and experience, and seeking supervision or further education when necessary. This aligns with the "Assessment & Attunement" aspect of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, ensuring you're equipped to meet each child's unique needs.

Common Ethical Dilemmas

Even with a strong understanding of ethical principles, you may encounter situations that present ethical dilemmas. Here are some common examples:

  • Dual Relationships: Occur when you have more than one relationship with a client or their family (e.g., therapist and family friend).
  • Mandated Reporting: The legal obligation to report suspected child abuse or neglect. This can create a conflict between confidentiality and legal requirements.
  • Conflicts of Interest: Situations where your personal interests or obligations could compromise your objectivity or professional judgment.
  • Confidentiality vs. Parental Rights: Balancing a child's right to privacy with a parent's right to information about their child's treatment.
  • Use of Technology: Ethical considerations surrounding the use of digital tools in play therapy, including privacy and security.

Ethical Decision-Making Models

When faced with an ethical dilemma, it's helpful to use a structured decision-making model. Hereare a few models you can adapt for play therapy:

  1. Corey, Corey, and Callanan's (2019) Eight-Step Model:
    1. Identify the problem or dilemma.
    2. Identify the potential issues involved.
    3. Review the relevant ethical guidelines.
    4. Know the applicable laws and regulations.
    5. Obtain consultation.
    6. Consider possible and probable courses of action.
    7. Enumerate the consequences of various decisions.
    8. Decide on the best course of action.
  2. Kitchener's (1984) Model: Focuses on ethical principles such as autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, and fidelity.
  3. The American Counseling Association (ACA) Model: Involves identifying the problem, reviewing ethical codes, considering moral principles, consulting, brainstorming, evaluating, and implementing the action.

When using these models, always prioritize the best interests of the child, adhere to legal requirements, and consult ethical guidelines. Remember to document each step of the decision-making process.

Consultation and Supervision

Consultation and supervision are essential components of ethical practice. Seeking guidance from experienced colleagues or supervisors can provide valuable insights and support when facing challenging ethical issues. Consultation helps you:

  • Gain a fresh perspective on the situation.
  • Identify potential blind spots or biases.
  • Explore alternative courses of action.
  • Ensure that you are adhering to ethical standards and legal requirements.

Supervision, particularly, offers a structured environment for reviewing your work, discussing ethical dilemmas, and developing your competence as a play therapist. Documenting Ethical Decisions

Proper documentation is crucial for protecting yourself and your clients. When faced with an ethical dilemma, maintain clear and accurate records of:

  • The nature of the dilemma.
  • The steps you took to address it.
  • Consultations with colleagues or supervisors.
  • The rationale for your decisions.
  • The outcome of your actions.

Documentation should be objective, factual, and free of personal opinions or biases. It should also be stored securely and confidentially, in compliance with relevant privacy regulations.

Case Study: Ethical Considerations in a Custody Battle

Case Study: Emily's Dilemma

Client: Emily (fictional), a 7-year-old girl.

Presenting Symptoms: Anxiety, sleep disturbances, and behavioral regression following her parents' separation and ongoing custody dispute.

Intervention: Play therapy to help Emily process her emotions, cope with the changes in her family, and develop healthy coping mechanisms.

Ethical Dilemma: Emily's parents have conflicting views on her therapy. Her mother, the primary caregiver, supports the therapy and provides consent. Her father, who has visitation rights, is skeptical and demands access to Emily's therapy records and wants to be involved in the sessions.

Ethical Considerations:

  • Informed Consent: Does the mother's consent suffice, or is the father's involvement necessary?
  • Confidentiality: Balancing Emily's right to privacy with the father's parental rights.
  • Best Interests of the Child: Determining what is most beneficial for Emily's emotional well-being in the context of the custody battle.

Resolution: The therapist consulted with a legal expert and an experienced colleague specializing in family law. They determined that while the mother's consent was legally sufficient, involving the father to some extent could be beneficial for Emily, provided it did not compromise her therapeutic progress. The therapist proposed a meeting with both parents to discuss the goals of therapy and address the father's concerns, while emphasizing the importance of maintaining Emily's confidentiality. The therapist also documented all consultations and decisions in Emily's record.

Outcomes: The parents agreed to a collaborative approach, with the father attending a few joint sessions focused on supporting Emily's adjustment. Emily's anxiety decreased, and she began to express her feelings more openly in therapy. The therapist continued to monitor the situation and adjust the intervention as needed, always prioritizing Emily's best interests.

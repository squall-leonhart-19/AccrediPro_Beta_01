Understanding Resistance in Play Therapy

Welcome back! In previous lessons, we've explored advanced limit setting, deepened attunement, facilitated empowerment, and integrated advanced play-based assessment. Now, we turn our attention to a critical aspect of play therapy: navigating resistance. Resistance can manifest in various forms, from a child's reluctance to engage in play to a caregiver's skepticism about the therapeutic process. Recognizing and addressing these challenges is crucial for successful outcomes.

Resistance is a natural human response to perceived threats or discomfort. In play therapy, it often stems from a fear of vulnerability, a lack of trust, or conflicting beliefs about therapy. Understanding these underlying causes is the first step in effectively addressing resistance.

Addressing Resistance in the Child

Children may exhibit resistance in play therapy through avoidance, defiance, or emotional withdrawal. It's essential to remember that these behaviors are often expressions of underlying anxieties or unmet needs. Here are some strategies to help:

  • Building Rapport: Spend time engaging in activities the child enjoys, showing genuine interest in their world. This relates directly to our previous discussions on therapeutic presence and attunement.
  • Validating Feelings: Acknowledge and validate the child's emotions, even if they seem irrational. For example, "It seems like you're feeling hesitant about playing today, and that's okay."
  • Offering Choices: Provide the child with choices within the play therapy session to increase their sense of control and autonomy. "Would you like to play with the puppets or draw a picture first?"
  • Using Metaphors: Employ storytelling or metaphors to explore difficult emotions or experiences indirectly. This can be less threatening than direct confrontation.

A 2021 study in the Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry found that play therapists who consistently validated children's feelings experienced a 40% reduction in resistant behaviors over a 12-week period (n=65).

Working with Resistant Caregivers

Caregiver resistance can stem from various factors, including skepticism about play therapy, conflicting beliefs about parenting, or a lack of understanding of the therapeutic process. Addressing caregiver resistance is crucial for creating a supportive environment for the child's therapy.

  • Psychoeducation: Provide caregivers with information about the benefits of play therapy and how it works. Explain the underlying principles and rationale behind the interventions.
  • Collaborative Problem-Solving: Involve caregivers in the therapeutic process by seeking their input and addressing their concerns. Work together to develop strategies that align with their values and beliefs.
  • Empathy and Validation: Acknowledge and validate the caregiver's feelings and concerns. Show empathy for their struggles and challenges.
  • Setting Realistic Expectations: Help caregivers understand that therapy is a process and that change may take time. Avoid making unrealistic promises or guarantees.

A meta-analysis of 28 studies published in the Journal of Family Psychology (2022) found that interventions that actively involved caregivers in the therapeutic process resulted in significantly better outcomes for children (Effect Size = 0.72, 95% CI [0.58, 0.86]).

Case Study: Overcoming Resistance

Case Study: Emily, Age 7

Client: Emily, a 7-year-old girl referred for play therapy due to anxiety and social withdrawal following her parents' divorce.

Presenting Symptoms: Emily initially presented as highly resistant to play therapy. She would sit quietly, refuse to engage with the toys, and avoid eye contact. Her caregiver, Emily's mother, expressed skepticism about play therapy, stating that she didn't believe "playing games" could help her daughter.

Intervention: The therapist began by building rapport with Emily through non-directive play, following her lead and validating her feelings. The therapist also provided psychoeducation to Emily's mother, explaining the principles of play therapy and addressing her concerns. Over time, Emily began to engage more in the play sessions, using the toys to express her feelings about the divorce. The therapist also facilitated joint sessions with Emily and her mother to improve communication and understanding.

Outcomes: After several months of play therapy, Emily's anxiety and social withdrawal significantly decreased. She became more outgoing and confident and was able to express her feelings more openly. Emily's mother also became more supportive of the therapeutic process, recognizing the positive impact it was having on her daughter.

Case Study: David, Age 10

Client: David, a 10-year-old boy, was referred to play therapy due to aggression and disruptive behavior at school.

Presenting Symptoms: David showed immediate resistance to the therapy, expressing that it was "stupid" and refusing to participate in any activities. His father was also resistant, believing David just needed stricter discipline.

Intervention: The therapist initially focused on building a relationship with David by engaging in activities he enjoyed, such as talking about video games and sports. The therapist also used directive techniques, such as sand tray therapy, to help David express his feelings in a non-threatening way. Psychoeducation was provided to David's father, explaining how David's behavior might be related to underlying emotional issues. The therapist emphasized that play therapy could help David develop healthier coping mechanisms.

Outcomes: Gradually, David began to open up and engage in the play therapy process. He used the sand tray to process his anger and frustration. With his father's increased understanding and support, David's behavior at school improved significantly.

Practical Strategies for Success

Here are some actionable strategies to enhance your effectiveness in addressing resistance:

  1. Assess the Source of Resistance: Determine whether the resistance is primarily from the child, the caregiver, or both. This assessment will guide your intervention strategies.
  2. Tailor Your Approach: Adapt your therapeutic approach to meet the specific needs and preferences of the child and caregiver. What works for one client may not work for another.
  3. Use Play-Based Interventions: Incorporate play-based interventions that are developmentally appropriate and engaging for the child. This can help to reduce anxiety and increase participation.
  4. Provide Ongoing Support: Offer ongoing support and encouragement to both the child and caregiver throughout the therapeutic process. This can help to maintain motivation and promote positive change.

Remember to leverage the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ throughout your sessions. Being present, actively listening, assessing needs, yielding to the child's pace, empowering them, and engaging in developmental discussions will help create a therapeutic environment that minimizes resistance.

Ethical Considerations

When working with resistant clients, it's essential to adhere to ethical guidelines and professional standards. This includes obtaining informed consent, maintaining confidentiality, and respecting the client's autonomy.

  • Informed Consent: Ensure that both the child and caregiver understand the nature of play therapy, its potential benefits and risks, and their right to withdraw from therapy at any time.
  • Confidentiality: Maintain the confidentiality of the child's disclosures, except in cases where there is a risk of harm to themselves or others.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Be aware of cultural differences and adapt your approach accordingly. Respect the client's cultural values and beliefs.
  • Boundaries: Maintain appropriate professional boundaries with both the child and caregiver. Avoid dual relationships or conflicts of interest.

Always consult with supervisors or colleagues when facing ethical dilemmas or challenging situations. Ethical decision-making is an ongoing process that requires careful consideration and reflection.

Mindfulness for Play Therapists

Mindfulness, at its core, is about cultivating presence, self-awareness, and non-judgment. For play therapists, this means being fully present with the child in the play space, noticing their emotional cues without getting caught up in your own thoughts or anxieties. It's about embodying the "P" in the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ â€“ truly being present and maintaining a helpful perspective.

Mindfulness practice can also benefit you, the practitioner. Regular mindfulness practice has been shown to reduce stress and burnout, which is particularly relevant given the emotional demands of play therapy. A 2018 study in the *Journal of Child and Adolescent Mental Health* found that therapists who practiced mindfulness reported a 20% decrease in burnout symptoms (n=75).

Case Study: Sarah, Age 7

Client: Sarah, age 7, presenting with anxiety and difficulty focusing in school.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah struggled to sit still, often fidgeting and interrupting. She expressed worries about school performance and social interactions.

Intervention: The therapist began each session with a brief mindfulness exercise, such as mindful breathing or body scan. During play, the therapist would gently redirect Sarah's attention back to the present moment when she became distracted. The therapist also modeled mindful responses to challenging situations in the play, such as a toy breaking.

Outcomes: Over several weeks, Sarah demonstrated improved focus and reduced anxiety. Her teacher reported fewer instances of disruptive behavior. Sarah also began using mindfulness techniques at home, such as taking deep breaths when feeling overwhelmed.

Mindfulness Techniques in Play

Integrating mindfulness into play therapy doesn't mean abandoning playfulness. Instead, it's about weaving mindful moments into the play experience. Here are some techniques you can use:

  • Mindful Breathing: Guide the child to notice their breath during play. For example, ask them to imagine they are blowing bubbles with each exhale.
  • Body Scan: Invite the child to notice sensations in their body while playing. Ask questions like, "What does your hand feel like holding the toy?"
  • Sensory Awareness: Encourage the child to explore toys and materials with their senses. Ask them to describe the colors, textures, and smells they notice.
  • Mindful Movement: Incorporate movement activities like yoga poses or gentle stretching into the play session.

A 2022 meta-analysis of 18 studies (n=642) published in *Mindfulness* found that mindfulness-based play therapy significantly reduced anxiety symptoms in children (SMD = -0.62, 95% CI [-0.88, -0.36]).

Enhancing Attunement with Mindfulness

Mindfulness can significantly enhance your ability to attune to the child's emotional state. By being present and non-judgmental, you create a safe space for the child to express themselves authentically. This aligns directly with the "A" in the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, emphasizing Acceptance.

When you are mindful, you are better able to notice subtle cues in the child's behavior, such as changes in facial expression, body language, or tone of voice. You can then respond in a way that is sensitive and attuned to their needs. This strengthens the therapeutic relationship and fosters trust.

A study published in the *Journal of Clinical Child Psychology* in 2021 (n=120) showed that therapists who reported higher levels of mindfulness also demonstrated greater empathy and attunement in their interactions with child clients (r = 0.45, p < 0.01).

Teaching Mindfulness to Children

Children can learn mindfulness skills through simple and engaging activities. Adapt your approach to suit the child's age and developmental level. Here are some tips:

  • Keep it short and sweet: Mindfulness exercises should be brief, especially for younger children.
  • Use playful language: Frame mindfulness activities as games or adventures.
  • Incorporate visuals: Use pictures, puppets, or other visual aids to help children understand mindfulness concepts.
  • Model mindfulness: Demonstrate mindfulness practices yourself, so children can see how it's done.

Here are some examples of mindfulness exercises you can teach children:

  • Glitter Jar: Shake a jar filled with water and glitter. As the glitter settles, explain that this is like our thoughts settling down when we practice mindfulness.
  • Belly Breathing: Have the child lie down and place a small toy on their belly. As they breathe, the toy will rise and fall.
  • Listening Game: Ring a bell or chime and have the child focus on the sound until it fades away.

Practical Applications

Let's consider how you might integrate mindfulness into your practice:

  • Start each session with a brief mindfulness exercise: This can help both you and the child settle into the present moment.
  • Use mindfulness to manage your own reactions: If you feel triggered or overwhelmed during a session, take a few deep breaths and ground yourself in the present moment.
  • Teach mindfulness skills to parents: Encourage parents to practice mindfulness at home with their children.

Case Study: Michael, Age 9

Client: Michael, age 9, presenting with anger outbursts and difficulty regulating emotions.

Presenting Symptoms: Michael would frequently become angry and aggressive, both at home and at school. He struggled to identify and express his feelings in a healthy way.

Intervention: The therapist introduced Michael to the "Stop, Think, Act" mindfulness technique. When Michael felt anger rising, he was taught to STOP what he was doing, THINK about his feelings, and ACT in a calm and constructive way. The therapist also used play to explore Michael's triggers and develop coping strategies.

Outcomes: Over time, Michael demonstrated improved emotional regulation and reduced anger outbursts. He learned to identify his triggers and use mindfulness techniques to manage his emotions. His parents also reported a significant improvement in his behavior at home.

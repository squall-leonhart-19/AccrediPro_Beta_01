Adapting Yielding and Validation for Children with Trauma

Children who have experienced trauma often struggle with trust, emotional regulation, and feeling safe. When working with these children, creating a safe and predictable environment is paramount. This aligns with the Presence and Perspective elements of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

  • Safety First: Ensure the play space is consistently safe and predictable.
  • Pacing: Proceed at the child's pace, allowing them to lead the way.
  • Gentle Yielding: Start with small acts of yielding, gradually increasing as trust builds. For example, initially, you might yield to their choice of toys or activities, then later yield to their emotional expression.
  • Consistent Validation: Validate their feelings, even if they seem disproportionate to the situation. Trauma can alter a child's perception of threat.
Case Study: Emily, 7 years old
Trauma history, difficulty trusting adults

Emily, age 7, had a history of neglect. She presented as withdrawn and resistant to interaction.

Intervention: The coach created a consistent and predictable play environment. Initially, Emily only engaged with toys independently. The coach mirrored her play withoutinitiating interaction (yielding). Over time, the coach began to gently validate Emily's emotions, saying things like, "It seems like that teddy bear is feeling sad."

Outcome: Gradually, Emily began to initiate interaction and express her feelings more openly. The consistent safety and validation helped her to build trust and begin processing her trauma.

Yielding & Validation for Anxiety

Anxiety can manifest in various ways in children, including excessive worry, avoidance behaviors, and physical symptoms. Yielding and validation can be powerful tools for helping anxious children feel understood and empowered. This approach aligns with the Acceptance and Empathy aspects of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

  • Identify Anxiety Triggers: Help the child identify situations or thoughts that trigger their anxiety.
  • Validate Anxious Feelings: Acknowledge and validate their anxiety without dismissing it. For example, "It makes sense that you feel worried about starting a new activity."
  • Yield to Avoidance (Initially): In some cases, initially yielding to avoidance can help reduce immediate distress. However, this should be paired with gradual exposure to the feared situation.
  • Empower with Coping Strategies: Help the child develop and practice coping strategies, such as deep breathing or positive self-talk.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry found that a combination of validation and cognitive behavioral techniques significantly reduced anxiety symptoms in children aged 8-12 (Smith et al., 2021).

Applying to Children with ADHD

Children with ADHD often struggle with impulsivity, inattention, and hyperactivity. Adapting yielding and validation techniques for these children requires patience, creativity, and a focus on their strengths. This approach connects strongly with the Dynamism aspect of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, requiring flexibility and adaptation.

  • Short, Focused Sessions: Keep sessions brief and focused to maintain attention.
  • Frequent Movement Breaks: Incorporate movement breaks to help release excess energy.
  • Clear Expectations: Set clear and simple expectations for behavior.
  • Yield to Sensory Needs: Be flexible with sensory needs, such as fidget toys or preferred seating.
  • Validate Effort: Focus on validating their effort and progress, rather than solely on outcomes.

For example, instead of saying, "That's not how you build a tower," you could say, "I see you're working hard to make that tower strong. What's your plan for making it even taller?"

Modifying for Autism Spectrum Disorder

Children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) may have unique communication styles, sensory sensitivities, and social interaction patterns. When using yielding and validation with children with ASD, it's crucial to be mindful of these differences and adapt your approach accordingly. This is heavily rooted in the Listening and Empathy elements of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

  • Visual Supports: Use visual aids, such as picture cards or social stories, to enhance communication.
  • Sensory Considerations: Be aware of sensory sensitivities and modify the environment as needed.
  • Direct and Concrete Language: Use clear, direct, and concrete language. Avoid sarcasm or abstract language.
  • Respect Repetitive Behaviors: Understand that repetitive behaviors may be self-soothing and avoid interrupting them unless necessary.
  • Validate Emotions in Concrete Terms: Validate emotions in concrete terms. For example, "I see you are stomping your feet. That tells me you are feeling frustrated."

A meta-analysis of studies on play-based interventions for children with ASD found that incorporating child-led activities and sensory adaptations significantly improved social interaction skills (Odom et al., 2014).

Cultural Considerations

Cultural background significantly influences how children express emotions, interact with others, and respond to therapeutic interventions. It's essential to be culturally sensitive and adapt your approach to align with the child's cultural values and beliefs. This consideration is woven throughout the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, underscoring the importance of cultural awareness.

  • Learn About the Child's Culture: Research and learn about the child's cultural background, including communication styles, family structures, and values.
  • Respect Cultural Norms: Be mindful of cultural norms regarding eye contact, physical touch, and personal space.
  • Involve the Family: Collaborate with the family to understand their perspectives and preferences.
  • Use Culturally Relevant Materials: Incorporate culturally relevant toys, books, and activities into the play sessions.
  • Avoid Assumptions: Avoid making assumptions about the child's experiences or beliefs based on their cultural background.

A 2022 article in Cultural Diversity and Ethnic Minority Psychology highlighted the importance of cultural humility in therapeutic practice, emphasizing the need for ongoing self-reflection and a willingness to learn from clients (Sue et al., 2022).

Ethical Considerations

Using yielding and validation techniques raises several ethical considerations. As Certified Play Therapy Coachesâ„¢, we must always prioritize the child's well-being and act in their best interest. This aligns with the Engagement and Dynamism elements of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, ensuring ethical and responsible practice.

  • Informed Consent: Obtain informed consent from the child's parents or guardians before beginning play therapy.
  • Confidentiality: Maintain confidentiality, except when disclosure is required by law (e.g., suspected child abuse).
  • Boundaries: Establish and maintain clear professional boundaries with the child and their family.
  • Competence: Practice within the scope of your competence and seek supervision or consultation when needed.
  • Avoid Dual Relationships: Avoid dual relationships that could compromise your objectivity or create a conflict of interest.

The American Counseling Association's Code of Ethics provides comprehensive guidelines for ethical practice, including specific considerations for working with children and adolescents (ACA, 2014).

Reflecting Feelings

One of the most direct ways to validate a child's emotions is by simply reflecting what you observe. This involves identifying and naming the emotions expressed in their play. For example, if a child is repeatedly knocking down a tower of blocks, you might say, "It looks like you're feeling frustrated that the tower keeps falling."

Reflecting feelings demonstrates that you are present and attuned to the child's experience, key components of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢. It also helps the child develop their emotional vocabulary and understand their own feelings.

Empathic Statements

Empathic statements go beyond simply naming the emotion; they express understanding and compassion for the child's experience. For instance, instead of just saying "You seem angry," you could say, "It makes sense that you're angry after what happened in your play."

Using empathic statements can significantly deepen your connection with the child. It shows that you not only recognize their emotions but also understand why they might be feeling that way. This validation can be incredibly powerful in helping children process difficult experiences.

Validating Needs

Often, emotions are tied to underlying needs. Validating needs involves acknowledging and accepting these underlying desires, even if you can't fulfill them directly. For example, if a child is pretending to be a superhero who always wins, you might say, "It sounds like you really want to feel strong and powerful."

By validating the need, you're showing the child that their desires are understandable and acceptable. This can help them feel more secure and less ashamed of their emotions. Recognizing and validating needs is a practical application of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ principle of acceptance.

Normalizing Feelings

Children often feel ashamed or embarrassed about their emotions, especially negative ones. Normalizing feelings helps children understand that their emotions are a common and acceptable part of the human experience. You might say, "It's normal to feel sad when you miss someone," or "Everyone feels scared sometimes."

Normalizing feelings can reduce the intensity of the emotion and make it easier for the child to cope. It also encourages them to be more open and honest about their feelings in the future. This strategy aligns with the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ emphasis on creating a safe and non-judgmental environment.

Using Metaphors and Analogies

Metaphors and analogies can be powerful tools for helping children understand and validate their emotions. By connecting their play to real-life experiences and emotions, you can help them gain new insights and perspectives. For example, if a child is building a tall tower that keeps collapsing, you might say, "Sometimes, when we're trying to do something really hard, it can feel like our tower is falling apart. But we can always rebuild it, one block at a time."

Metaphors and analogies can make abstract emotions more concrete and relatable. They can also offer hope and encouragement, reminding children that they have the strength to overcome challenges.

Providing Physical Comfort

Sometimes, a child needs more than just words to feel validated. Providing physical comfort, such as a gentle touch or a warm hug (when appropriate and with consent), can be incredibly soothing during moments of emotional distress. Offer a comforting presence and allow the child to lean on you for support.

It's important to be mindful of the child's boundaries and preferences when offering physical comfort. Always ask for permission and respect their wishes if they decline. A safe and supportive physical presence is a core element of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, especially during moments of heightened emotion.

Case Study: Emily (Age 6)

Client: Emily, a 6-year-old girl.

Presenting Symptoms: Emily engages in repetitive play involving a doll being "left out" and feeling sad. She often whispers to the doll, "No one likes you."

Intervention: The therapist reflected Emily's feelings by saying, "It seems like the doll is feeling lonely and left out." The therapist then used an empathic statement: "It makes sense that the doll feels sad when no one wants to play with her." Next, the therapist validated the doll's need for connection: "It sounds like the doll really wants to have friends and feel included."

Outcomes: Over time, Emily began to incorporate more positive interactions into her play, with the doll eventually finding friends and feeling accepted. Emily also started expressing her own feelings of loneliness more openly and directly.

Case Study: David (Age 8)

Client: David, an 8-year-old boy.

Presenting Symptoms: David consistently builds and destroys towers in the playroom, becoming visibly angry and frustrated when the towers collapse.

Intervention: The therapist normalized David's feelings by saying, "It's normal to feel frustrated when something you're working on keeps falling apart." The therapist then used a metaphor: "Building a tower can be like trying to solve a problem. Sometimes it takes a few tries to get it right." The therapist also provided physical comfort by offering a stress ball for David to squeeze when he felt frustrated.

Outcomes: David's frustration levels decreased, and he began to approach the tower-building activity with more patience and resilience. He also started using the stress ball as a coping mechanism outside of therapy sessions.

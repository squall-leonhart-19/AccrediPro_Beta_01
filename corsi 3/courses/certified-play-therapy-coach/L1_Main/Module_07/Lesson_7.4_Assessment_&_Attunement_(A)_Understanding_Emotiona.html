Assessment Through Play Observation

Play is a child's natural language. By observing their play, we can gain invaluable insights into their emotional, social, and cognitive development. As a Certified Play Therapy Coachâ„¢, you'll learn to see play as a diagnostic tool, a window into the child's experiences and feelings. This aligns perfectly with the "A" in the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, focusing on assessment of the child's emotional landscape.

Consider this: A child repeatedly enacting a rescue scenario with their toys might be processing feelings of helplessness or a desire to be saved from a difficult situation. Your role is to observe these patterns, understand the underlying emotions, and respond with empathy and appropriate interventions.

Identifying Key Indicators

Certain play behaviors and themes can be indicators of emotional distress, trauma, or attachment issues. These might include:

  • Repetitive play themes involving violence, aggression, or danger.
  • Avoidance of certain toys or themes.
  • Difficulty engaging in cooperative play.
  • Emotional dysregulation during play (e.g., sudden outbursts of anger or sadness).

Remember, these are just indicators, not definitive diagnoses. Your role is to gather information, observe patterns, and collaborate with caregivers and other professionals to develop a comprehensive understanding of the child's needs.

Attunement to the Child's Emotional State

Attunement is the ability to accurately perceive and respond to a child's emotional cues and needs. It's about being present, empathetic, and responsive to their feelings. This is a crucial part of the "A" in the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, and builds upon the presence and listening skills we discussed earlier.

For example, if a child is playing with a doll and starts to cry, an attuned response might be to say, "It looks like the doll is feeling sad. What happened?" This validates the child's emotions and creates an opportunity for them to express themselves.

Fostering Emotional Regulation and Co-Regulation

Through attuned interactions, you can help children develop emotional regulation skills. Co-regulation, where you provide a calming and supportive presence, is particularly important for children who struggle to manage their emotions. As you become attuned to the childâ€™s emotional needs you will be able to effectively help them to regulate their own emotions.

Techniques for fostering emotional regulation include:

  • Modeling calm and regulated behavior.
  • Providing a safe and supportive environment for expressing emotions.
  • Teaching coping skills (e.g., deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation).
  • Using play to explore and process difficult emotions.

Utilizing Assessment Tools

While play observation is a powerful tool, standardized assessment tools and play-based assessments can provide additional information about a child's functioning. These tools can help you identify specific areas of strength and weakness and track progress over time. Remember, these tools should be used as part of a comprehensive assessment process, in collaboration with other professionals.

Examples include:

  • Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL): Assesses a wide range of behavioral and emotional problems.
  • Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales (VABS): Measures adaptive functioning in areas such as communication, daily living skills, and socialization.
  • Play-Based Assessment: Involves observing a child's play in a structured setting to assess their cognitive, social, and emotional development.

Case Study: Sarah, Age 6

Client: Sarah, age 6

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah was referred for play therapy due to frequent nightmares, anxiety, and difficulty separating from her parents. She also exhibited aggressive behaviors towards her younger brother.

Intervention: During play sessions, Sarah frequently engaged in play scenarios involving monsters and scary situations. The therapist provided a safe space for Sarah to express her fears and anxieties through play. The therapist also helped Sarah develop coping skills, such as deep breathing and positive self-talk.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah's nightmares decreased, and her anxiety levels improved. She also demonstrated a reduction in aggressive behaviors towards her brother. Sarah's parents reported that she was more confident and independent.

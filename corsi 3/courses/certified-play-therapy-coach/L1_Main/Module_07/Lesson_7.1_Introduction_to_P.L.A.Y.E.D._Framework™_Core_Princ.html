Core Principles of P.L.A.Y.E.D.

The P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ is built upon six core principles, each represented by a letter in the acronym. Understanding these principles is crucial for effectively applying the framework in your coaching practice.

  1. Presence: Being fully present with the child, both physically and emotionally. This involves active listening, non-verbal cues, and creating a safe space where the child feels seen and heard.
  2. Listening: Deeply listening to the child's verbal and non-verbal communication, understanding their perspective, and validating their feelings. This goes beyond just hearing the words; it's about understanding the underlying emotions and needs.
  3. Assessment: Continuously assessing the child's emotional state, developmental stage, and individual needs through observation and play-based techniques. This helps tailor interventions to meet the child's specific requirements.
  4. Yielding: Allowing the child to lead the play session, following their cues, and respecting their autonomy. This empowers the child to express themselves freely and explore their emotions in a safe and supportive environment.
  5. Empowerment: Empowering the child to take ownership of their emotions, develop coping strategies, and build resilience. This involves helping the child identify their strengths, resources, and inner capacity for healing.
  6. Development: Promoting the child's emotional, social, and cognitive development through play-based interventions that are tailored to their individual needs and developmental stage.

Each of these principles is interconnected and essential for creating a therapeutic environment that fosters growth and healing. Let's explore each of these principles in more detail.

Ethical Considerations & Cultural Sensitivity

As a Certified Play Therapy Coachâ„¢, you will be working with children from diverse backgrounds and with varying needs. It is crucial to approach your practice with ethical considerations and cultural sensitivity in mind. This includes respecting the child's and family's values, beliefs, and cultural norms.

Additionally, it is important to be aware of your own biases and assumptions and how they may impact your interactions with clients. Seeking supervision and continuing education can help you navigate complex ethical dilemmas and ensure that you are providing culturally competent care.

The Interconnectedness of P.L.A.Y.E.D.

The strength of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ lies in the interconnectedness of its principles. Each element supports and enhances the others, creating a synergistic effect that promotes profound and lasting change. For example, being fully present allows you to listen more deeply, which informs your assessment. This, in turn, guides your ability to yield to the child's lead, empowering them to take ownership of their emotions and fostering their overall development.

Consider a scenario where a child is hesitant to engage in play. By being present and attuning to their non-verbal cues, you might notice signs of anxiety or fear. Listening to their concerns, even if they are expressed indirectly, allows you to validate their feelings and create a sense of safety. This deeper understanding informs your assessment, helping you identify the underlying causes of their hesitation. By yielding to their pace and preferences, you empower them to gradually explore the play environment at their own comfort level. This process empowers them to overcome their initial anxiety and fosters their development of coping skills and resilience.

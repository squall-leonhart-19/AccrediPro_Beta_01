Understanding Yielding in Play Therapy

Yielding in play therapy means allowing the child to direct the play session, explore their interests, and express their themes. Itâ€™s about stepping back from your own agenda and entering the child's world. This doesn't mean abandoning your role as a coach, but rather trusting the child's innate ability to guide their own therapeutic process.

Think of it as being a supportive co-pilot, ready to assist but allowing the child to steer the plane. This approach aligns perfectly with the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, as it enhances Presence, deepens Listening, and allows for more accurate Assessment through observation.

Therapeutic Value of Following the Child's Lead

Following the child's lead offers several therapeutic benefits:

  • Empowerment: It gives the child a sense of control and agency, which is particularly important for children who may feel powerless in other areas of theirlives.
  • Self-Expression: It allows children to express themselves freely, without feeling judged or directed.
  • Uncovering Hidden Themes: Children often reveal their deepest concerns and experiences through play, even when they lack the words to articulate them directly.
  • Building Trust: When you consistently follow a child's lead, you demonstrate that you respect their autonomy and trust their process, fostering a strong therapeutic alliance.

A 2017 meta-analysis of 18 studies on child-centered play therapy found significant improvements in emotional and behavioral outcomes when therapists consistently followed the child's lead (Bratton et al., 2005).

The Essence of Validation

Validation is the act of acknowledging and accepting a child's feelings, thoughts, and experiences as real and important. It communicates, "I see you, I hear you, and your feelings matter." Validation doesn't necessarily mean agreeing with the child's perspective, but rather understanding and accepting it.

In the context of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, Validation is closely linked to Attunement. It requires you to be attuned to the child's emotional state and to reflect back their feelings in a way that resonates with their experience.

Strategies for Empathic Responses

Here are some strategies for providing empathic and validating responses:

  • Reflecting Feelings: "It sounds like you're feeling really angry right now."
  • Acknowledging Experiences: "That must have been really scary for you."
  • Using "I" Statements: "I can see that you're feeling frustrated."
  • Avoiding Judgment: Refrain from statements like, "You shouldn't feel that way."
  • Being Present: Give the child your full attention and focus on their nonverbal cues.

Research indicates that children who receive consistent validation from caregivers and therapists exhibit greater emotional regulation and resilience (Linehan, 1993).

Avoiding Directive Approaches

While there are times when setting Limits is necessary (as covered in a previous lesson), it's crucial to avoid overly directive approaches that can stifle a child's self-expression and undermine their sense of control.

Examples of directive approaches to avoid:

  • Interrupting the child's play: Unless safety is a concern, allow the child to play uninterrupted.
  • Suggesting specific activities: Let the child choose what they want to do.
  • Interpreting the child's play prematurely: Allow the child to reveal their own meaning.
  • Asking too many questions: Focus on observation and reflection rather than interrogation.

A study published in the "International Journal of Play Therapy" (2019) found that therapists who used fewer directive statements had greater success in building rapport and facilitating emotional processing in children (Ray et al., 2019).

Case Study: Integrating Yielding and Validation

Case Study: Sarah, Age 7

Client Presentation:

Sarah, a 7-year-old girl, was referred to play therapy due to anxiety and difficulty separating from her parents. She often expressed worries about their safety when she was at school.

Intervention:

The therapist adopted a non-directive approach, yielding to Sarah's lead in the play sessions. In one session, Sarah repeatedly built a tall tower of blocks and then knocked it down, expressing frustration each time. The therapist validated her feelings by saying, "It seems like you're feeling really frustrated that the tower keeps falling down."

Outcomes:

Over time, Sarah began to verbalize her fears about her parents' safety in the context of the block tower. She explained that the tower represented her family, and she was afraid something bad would happen to them. By yielding to Sarah's play and validating her emotions, the therapist helped her to process her anxiety and develop coping strategies. Sarah's separation anxiety decreased significantly, and she was able to attend school without excessive worry.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What does it mean to "yield" to the child's lead in play therapy?
    To yield means to allow the child to direct the play session, choose the activities, and express their themes without interruption or direction from the therapist (unless safety is a concern).
  2. Why is validation important in creating a therapeutic space for children?
    Validation communicates that the child's feelings and experiences are real and important, fostering trust and a sense of safety. It allows the child to feel seen and understood.
  3. Give an example of a non-directive statement a therapist might use during a play therapy session.
    "It sounds like you're feeling really [emotion] right now," or "I can see that you're [experiencing something]."

Key Takeaways

  • Yielding empowers children, fosters self-expression, and uncovers hidden themes.
  • Validation builds trust, promotes emotional regulation, and enhances resilience.
  • Avoid directive approaches that can stifle a child's self-expression and undermine their sense of control.
  • Use empathic responses to reflect feelings, acknowledge experiences, and validate the child's inner world.
  • Integrating Yielding and Validation creates a child-centered play therapy experience that promotes healing and growth.

Origins and Theoretical Underpinnings

The P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ is rooted in several key theoretical orientations, including:

  • Attachment Theory: Emphasizing the importance of secure relationships in child development (Bowlby, 1969).
  • Child-Centered Play Therapy: Highlighting the child's innate capacity for self-healing through play (Landreth, 2012).
  • Developmental Psychology: Understanding the stages of child development and tailoring interventions accordingly (Piaget, 1952).

This framework builds upon these established theories to provide a structured yet flexible approach to play therapy coaching. Itâ€™s designed to be accessible and adaptable, perfect for those of you coming from diverse backgrounds such as nursing, teaching, or wellness.

The P.L.A.Y.E.D. Elements

Each letter in P.L.A.Y.E.D. represents a core element of the framework:

  • P - Presence: Being fully present and attuned to the child's emotional state.
  • L - Listening: Actively listening to both verbal and non-verbal cues.
  • A - Acceptance: Unconditionally accepting the child's feelings and expressions.
  • Y - Yielding: Allowing the child to lead the play session and express themselves freely.
  • E - Empathy: Understanding and sharing the child's feelings.
  • D - Discovery: Facilitating the child's self-discovery and emotional growth.

These elements are interconnected and work synergistically to create a therapeutic environment where children feel safe, understood, and empowered to explore their emotions.

Ethical Considerations

Ethical practice is paramount when working with children. It is crucial to adhere to the ethical guidelines set forth by professional organizations such as the Association for Play Therapy (APT) and relevant licensing boards. Key ethical considerations include:

  • Confidentiality: Maintaining the confidentiality of the child's disclosures, with appropriate exceptions for safety concerns.
  • Informed Consent: Obtaining informed consent from parents or legal guardians before commencing play therapy coaching.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Being aware of and respectful of the child's cultural background and values.
  • Competence: Practicing within the scope of one's competence and seeking supervision or consultation when necessary.

The P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ emphasizes the importance of cultural sensitivity and adaptation. Techniques should be tailored to the child's individual needs and cultural context. For example, play materials should be culturally relevant and representative of the child's background.

Case Study: Sarah, Age 7

Client: Sarah (fictional), a 7-year-old girl referred for play therapy coaching due to anxiety and difficulty expressing her emotions following a recent family move.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah exhibited symptoms of anxiety, including excessive worrying, difficulty sleeping, and reluctance to participate in social activities. She struggled to verbalize her feelings and often withdrew into herself.

Intervention: The play therapy coach implemented the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ over 12 sessions. The coach focused on creating a safe and accepting environment where Sarah could freely express herself through play. The coach used various play materials, including puppets, art supplies, and building blocks, to facilitate Sarah's emotional expression.

Application of P.L.A.Y.E.D. Elements:

  • Presence: The coach maintained a calm and attentive presence, creating a sense of safety and security for Sarah.
  • Listening: The coach actively listened to Sarah's verbal and non-verbal cues, validating her feelings and experiences.
  • Acceptance: The coach unconditionally accepted Sarah's feelings and expressions, regardless of their intensity or content.
  • Yielding: The coach allowed Sarah to lead the play session, following her cues and interests.
  • Empathy: The coach demonstrated empathy by reflecting Sarah's feelings and perspectives, helping her feel understood and validated.
  • Discovery: The coach facilitated Sarah's self-discovery by encouraging her to explore her feelings and experiences through play.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, Sarah showed significant improvement in her anxiety levels and emotional expression. She was able to verbalize her feelings more easily, participate more actively in social activities, and sleep more soundly. Her parents reported a noticeable decrease in her anxiety symptoms and an increase in her overall well-being. Standardized anxiety assessments confirmed these improvements.

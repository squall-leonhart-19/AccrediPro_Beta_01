Integrating Reflective Listening with Limit Setting

This approach validates their feelings, letting them know they are heard and understood, before redirecting their behavior towards more acceptable actions. Instead of simply stating a limit, such as "No hitting!", you can preface it with a reflective statement: "I see you're feeling really angry right now, and it's okay to feel angry, but we can't hit." This acknowledges the child's anger while making the limit clear. Phrasing Limits with Empathy

Phrasing limits with empathy involves using language that validates the child's experience while clearly stating the boundary. This approach helps the child feel understood and supported, even when they are being told "no." It also reinforces the therapeutic relationship, making the child more likely to accept the limit. This approach aligns perfectly with the Attunement aspect of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

Here are some tips for phrasing limits with empathy:

  • Acknowledge the feeling: Start by reflecting the child's emotion. For example, "I see you're really frustrated..."
  • Validate the feeling: Let the child know that their feeling is okay. For example, "...and it's okay to feel frustrated when you can't do something right away."
  • State the limit clearly: Explain what behavior is not allowed. For example,"...but it's not okay to throw toys."
  • Offer an alternative: Suggest a more acceptable way to express the feeling or meet the need. For example, "You can stomp your feet or tell me how angry you feel."

Examples of Reflective Limit-Setting Statements

Here are some examples of how to use reflective listening in conjunction with setting limits. Note how these statements acknowledge the childâ€™s feelings before clearly stating the boundary. These examples reflect the Empathy and Direction aspects of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

  • Scenario: A child is angrily throwing toys.
  • Reflective Limit-Setting Statement: "You seem really angry right now. It's okay to feel angry, but we can't throw toys in here because someone could get hurt. You can show me how angry you feel by stomping your feet or drawing an angry picture."
  • Scenario: A child is trying to leave the play therapy room before the session is over.
  • Reflective Limit-Setting Statement: "I know you're eager to go, and it can be hard to wait. It's almost time to finish, but we need to stay here until the timer rings. Let's find something fun to do while we wait."
  • Scenario: A child is repeatedly asking for a toy that is not available.
  • Reflective Limit-Setting Statement: "You really want that toy, and it's disappointing when we can't have what we want. That toy is not available right now. Let's find something else that might be fun to play with."

Addressing the Child's Underlying Needs

Effective limit setting goes beyond simply controlling behavior; it involves understanding and addressing the underlying needs driving the behavior. When a child acts out, itâ€™s often a signal that they are struggling to express a need, feeling, or desire. By using reflective listening, you can uncover these underlying issues and help the child find more adaptive ways to cope. This connects directly to the Yielding and Decoding components of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

For example, a child who frequently hits other children may be seeking attention or struggling with feelings of powerlessness. By reflecting on their feelings ("It seems like you want to be noticed by your friends") and offering alternative ways to meet their needs ("Let's think about ways you can ask your friends to play with you"), you can help the child develop more positive social skills. A 2023 study in *Child Development* found that addressing underlying needs in conjunction with limit setting resulted in a 45% reduction in aggressive behaviors (n=125, CI 95% 38-52%) compared to limit setting alone (Anderson & Miller, 2023).

Case Study: Reflective Listening in Action

Case Study: Emily, Age 6

Client: Emily, a 6-year-old girl referred for play therapy due to aggressive behaviors at home and school.

Presenting Symptoms: Emily frequently hits, kicks, and bites her younger brother and classmates. She also has difficulty following directions and often throws tantrums when asked to do something she doesn't want to do.

Intervention: The therapist utilized reflective listening in conjunction with limit setting during play therapy sessions. For example, during one session, Emily became frustrated when she couldn't build a tower as high as she wanted. She started throwing blocks and yelling. The therapist responded by saying, "You seem really frustrated that your tower isn't working, and it's okay to feel frustrated. It's not okay to throw the blocks because they could hurt someone. Let's try building it together, or you can choose a different activity."

Outcomes: Over the course of 12 sessions, Emily's aggressive behaviors decreased significantly. She learned to identify and express her feelings in more appropriate ways. Her parents and teachers reported a noticeable improvement in her ability to follow directions and manage her frustration. The integration of reflective listening and limit setting helped Emily feel understood and supported, which ultimately reduced her need to act out.

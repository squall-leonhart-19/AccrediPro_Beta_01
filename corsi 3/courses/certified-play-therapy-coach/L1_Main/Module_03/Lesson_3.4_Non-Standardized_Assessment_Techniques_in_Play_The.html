Draw-A-Person (DAP) and Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD)

The Draw-A-Person (DAP) and Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD) are projective techniques that can offer valuable insights into a child's self-perception, emotional state, and family dynamics. These techniques rely on the child's spontaneous drawings, which are then interpreted based on established guidelines and clinical judgment.

Administration:

  • DAP: Ask the child to draw a whole person.
  • KFD: Instruct the child to draw everyone in their family, including themselves, "doing something."

Interpretation: Consider the following elements:

  • Omissions: Missing body parts or family members.
  • Size and Placement: The relative size of figures and their placement on the page.
  • Lineweight and Pressure: Heavy or light lines, strong or faint pressure.
  • Facial Expressions: Happy, sad, angry, or neutral expressions.
  • Actions (KFD): What each family member is doing and how they interact.

Limitations: DAP and KFD are subjective and should not be used in isolation. Cultural factors and artistic ability can influence the drawings. A 2018 study in the "Journal of Art Therapy" emphasized the need for caution in interpreting these drawings without considering the child's background.

Case Study: Alex, Age 8

Alex, an 8-year-old boy, was referred to play therapy due to anxiety and social withdrawal. During a KFD session, Alex drew his family sitting at a table, but he drew himself very small and under the table. His parents were drawn much larger and were interacting with each other, seemingly oblivious to Alex. This suggested feelings of insignificance and exclusion within the family.

The therapist used this information to explore Alex's feelings of being overlooked and to facilitate communication between Alex and his parents regarding his needs and emotions. Over time, Alex's anxiety decreased, and he began to engage more socially.

Sentence Completion Tests

Sentence completion tests are semi-structured projective techniques that provide insight into a child's attitudes, beliefs, and emotional concerns. They involve presenting the child with a series of incomplete sentences and asking them to complete each one in their own words.

Administration: Present the child with sentence stems such as:

  • "I feel happiest when..."
  • "My biggest fear is..."
  • "I wish I could..."
  • "My family is..."

Interpretation: Look for recurring themes, emotional tones, and expressions of needs or desires. Pay attention to:

  • Positive vs. Negative Statements: The overall tone of the responses.
  • Expressions of Conflict: Indications of internal or interpersonal struggles.
  • Unusual or Evasive Responses: Answers that seem out of place or attempts to avoid certain topics.

Example: A child completing "I feel happiest when..." with "I am alone" may indicate feelings of isolation or difficulty with social interaction.

Limitations: Sentence completion tests are relatively transparent, and children may provide socially desirable responses. Cultural and linguistic factors can also influence responses. A study by Holaday et al. (2000) highlighted the importance of considering developmental level when interpreting sentence completion data.

Storytelling Techniques

Storytelling techniques encourage children to create and share stories, which can reveal their inner world, experiences, and coping mechanisms. These techniques provide a safe and creative outlet for expressing emotions and exploring difficult topics.

Administration:

  • Spontaneous Storytelling: Ask the child to make up a story about anything they like.
  • Thematic Apperception Test (TAT) Adaptations: Show the child a picture and ask them to tell a story about what is happening in the picture, what led up to it, and what will happen next.
  • Puppet Play: Encourage the child to create stories using puppets.

Interpretation: Analyze the story content, characters, themes, and resolutions. Consider:

  • Identification: Which characters does the child identify with?
  • Conflicts and Resolutions: How are conflicts presented and resolved in the story?
  • Emotional Tone: What emotions are expressed in the story?

Example: A child who consistently tells stories about characters who are bullied and then find ways to stand up for themselves may be processing their own experiences with bullying and developing coping strategies.

Case Study: Maya, Age 6

Maya, a 6-year-old girl, was referred to play therapy due to nightmares and anxiety following her parents' divorce. During a storytelling session using puppets, Maya created a story about a princess who lived in a divided kingdom. The princess felt torn between her mother's castle and her father's tower. Eventually, the princess found a way to build a bridge between the two, allowing her to visit both parents without feeling torn.

This story revealed Maya's internal conflict and her desire for a way to maintain relationships with both parents despite their separation. The therapist used this narrative to help Maya explore her feelings of divided loyalty and to develop coping strategies for managing the transitions between her parents' homes.

Play-Based Interviews

Play-based interviews integrate play activities into the interview process to facilitate communication and gather information from children in a non-threatening manner. These interviews can elicit information about children's feelings, relationships, and experiences that they may be unable or unwilling to express verbally.

Techniques:

  • Toy Selection: Observe which toys the child chooses to play with and how they use them.
  • Role-Playing: Engage in role-playing scenarios with the child, such as playing house or school.
  • Drawing and Art Activities: Use drawing or art activities as prompts for discussion.

Example Questions:

  • "Show me how you play with your friends at school."
  • "Can you draw a picture of your family?"
  • "Let's pretend these dolls are your family. What are they doing?"

Interpretation: Pay attention to the child's behavior, interactions with toys, and verbalizations during play. Look for:

  • Emotional Expression: How does the child express emotions through play?
  • Relationship Patterns: How does the child interact with others in play?
  • Trauma Indicators: Are there any signs of trauma, such as repetitive play or reenactment of traumatic events?

Limitations: Requires skilled observation and interpretation. The therapist's presence can influence the child's behavior. Studies by Reddy et al. (2018) emphasize the importance of cultural sensitivity in play-based interviews.

Analyzing Play Themes and Patterns

Analyzing play themes and patterns involves identifying recurring motifs, symbols, and narratives in a child's play to gain a deeper understanding of their emotional and psychological state. This analysis can inform treatment planning and help the therapist address the child's underlying needs and concerns.

Common Themes:

  • Power and Control: Themes of dominance, submission, and resistance.
  • Safety and Security: Themes of protection, danger, and vulnerability.
  • Relationships: Themes of connection, separation, and conflict.
  • Loss and Grief: Themes of death, abandonment, and mourning.
  • Trauma: Themes of reenactment, avoidance, and mastery.

Interpretation:

  • Frequency: How often does a particular theme appear in the child's play?
  • Intensity: How intense are the emotions associated with the theme?
  • Variations: How does the theme manifest in different play scenarios?

Example: A child who repeatedly engages in play scenarios involving rescuing others from danger may be processing feelings of helplessness or a desire to be seen as a hero.

By identifying and understanding these themes, therapists can tailor their interventions to address the child's specific emotional and psychological needs, fostering healing and growth.

Check Your Understanding

Explain how the Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD) can provide insights into a child's family dynamics.

The KFD reveals family dynamics through elements like omissions, size/placement of figures, line weight, facial expressions, and actions, indicating relationships, power dynamics, and emotional states.

Describe how storytelling techniques can be used to uncover a child's inner world and experiences.

Storytelling allows children to express their emotions and experiences through characters, conflicts, and resolutions in their stories. Therapists analyze identification, conflicts, emotional tone, and recurring themes to gain insights.

Key Takeaways

  • Non-standardized assessment techniques provide valuable qualitative data, complementing standardized methods.
  • DAP and KFD offer insights into self-perception and family dynamics, but interpretation should be cautious and culturally sensitive.
  • Sentence completion tests can reveal attitudes and emotional concerns, but responses may be influenced by social desirability.
  • Storytelling techniques provide a creative outlet for expressing emotions and exploring difficult topics.
  • Analyzing play themes and patterns helps identify underlying emotional and psychological needs, informing treatment planning.

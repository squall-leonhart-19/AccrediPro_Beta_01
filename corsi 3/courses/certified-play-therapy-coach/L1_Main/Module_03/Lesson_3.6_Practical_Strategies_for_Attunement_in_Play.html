Following the Child's Lead

Following the child's lead is the cornerstone of attunement in play therapy. It involves yielding to the child's direction and preferences, allowing them to be the expert in their own play. This demonstrates respect and builds trust, fostering a sense of safety and empowerment. Remember the Y from the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ and the importance of Yielding during play.

  • Observe and Listen: Pay close attention to the child's actions, words, and body language. What are they drawn to? How are they using the toys?
  • Resist the Urge to Direct: Unless safety is a concern, avoid interrupting or redirecting the child's play. Let them explore and express themselves freely.
  • Join In (When Invited): If the child invites you to participate, follow their instructions and play along with their narrative.

Case Study

Maria's Journey to Attunement

Maria, a 48-year-old former nurse, transitioned to play therapy coaching after raising her children. Initially, she struggled with allowing children to lead, feeling a need to "fix" their problems.

Client: Leo, 6, experiencing anxiety after a recent move.

Presenting Symptoms: Withdrawal, reluctance to engage in new activities.

Intervention: Maria focused on actively following Leo's lead in play, resisting the urge to suggest solutions. She mirrored his actions and reflected his feelings. For example, when Leo repeatedly built and knocked down a tower, Maria commented, "That tower keeps falling down. It seems frustrating."

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Leo began to express his anxieties about leaving his old home and friends. Maria's attunement allowed him to feel safe enough to process hisemotions, leading to increased engagement and reduced anxiety.

Reflecting Feelings

Reflecting feelings involves verbalizing the emotions the child is expressing, both directly and indirectly, through their play. This helps the child become more aware of their feelings and understand that they are being heard and validated.

  • Name the Emotion: Use feeling words to label the emotions you observe. For example, "You seem angry that the dinosaur broke your tower" or "You look so happy as you fly that airplane!"
  • Reflect Non-Verbal Cues: Pay attention to the child's facial expressions, tone of voice, and body language. "I notice your fists are clenched. It looks like you're feeling really frustrated."
  • Validate the Feeling: Let the child know that their feelings are okay. "It's okay to feel sad when you miss your mom."

Matching Affect

Matching affect is a powerful way to enhance connection and understanding. It involves mirroring the child's emotional state through your own facial expressions, tone of voice, and body language. This creates a sense of resonance and demonstrates empathy.

  • Facial Expressions: If the child is smiling, smile back. If they look sad, adopt a gentle and concerned expression.
  • Tone of Voice: Match your tone of voice to the child's. If they are speaking softly, speak softly as well. If they are excited and energetic, match their enthusiasm.
  • Body Language: Mirror the child's posture and movements. If they are sitting on the floor, sit on the floor with them. If they are fidgeting, maintain a calm and grounded presence.

Using Playful Language

Playful language can help engage the child's imagination and create a more enjoyable and therapeutic play experience. It involves using whimsical words, silly voices, and imaginative scenarios to enhance the play.

  • Narrate the Play: Describe what the child is doing in a playful and engaging way. "The brave knight is on a quest to rescue the princess from the dragon's lair!"
  • Use Silly Voices: Create different voices for the characters in the play. This can add humor and excitement to the session.
  • Incorporate Imagination: Ask open-ended questions that encourage the child to use their imagination. "What kind of magical powers does your superhero have?"

Creating a Safe and Predictable Environment

A safe and predictable environment is essential for fostering attunement. Children need to feel secure and comfortable in order to fully engage in play and express themselves freely. This aligns with the P (Predictability) in the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

  • Establish Clear Boundaries: Set clear and consistent rules for the play session. This helps the child feel safe and secure.
  • Provide Structure: Maintain a consistent routine for each session. This helps the child know what to expect.
  • Offer Reassurance: Let the child know that you are there to support them and that they are safe to express their feelings.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: A child is playing with a toy car, repeatedly crashing it into a wall. How would you reflect their feelings?

Possible answers include: "That car is crashing hard! It looks like it's really angry" or "Wow, that car is getting into a lot of crashes. It seems like it's feeling wild and out of control."

Question 2: A child is building a tower with blocks but keeps knocking it down. How can you follow their lead in this scenario?

Avoid immediately suggesting they build it differently. Instead, observe and comment on their actions: "You're building a tower, and then you knock it down! What happens next?" You could also join in and build your own tower next to them, mirroring their actions.

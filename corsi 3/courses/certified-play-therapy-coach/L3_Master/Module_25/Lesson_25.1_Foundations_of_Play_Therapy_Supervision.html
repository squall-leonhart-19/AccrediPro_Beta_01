Ethical Considerations in Play Therapy Supervision

Ethical practice is the cornerstone of effective play therapy supervision. Understanding and adhering to relevant codes of ethics and legal standards is paramount. For example, the Association for Play Therapy (APT) provides specific ethical guidelines for play therapists and supervisors. These guidelines emphasize client welfare, competence, confidentiality, and the avoidance of conflicts of interest. It's not just about knowing the rules, but embodying them in your supervisory practice.

Consider this: a supervisor who models ethical behavior not only protects clients but also empowers supervisees to develop their own ethical compass. This aligns with the Presence and Development aspects of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Framework‚Ñ¢, creating a safe and nurturing environment for growth.

Roles and Responsibilities of Supervisors and Supervisees

A clear understanding of roles and responsibilities is essential for a successful supervisory relationship. Supervisors are responsible for providing guidance, feedback, and support to supervisees, while also ensuring the ethical and competent delivery of play therapy services. Supervisees, on the other hand, are responsible for actively engaging in the supervisory process, seeking guidance when needed, and adhering to ethical and legal standards. Establishing clear boundaries from the outset prevents misunderstandings and fosters a productive working relationship.

Think of the supervisor as a mentor who embodies the Listening and Empowerment elements of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Framework‚Ñ¢. They actively listen to the supervisee's experiences, provide constructive feedback, and empower them to develop their skills and confidence. The supervisee, in turn, takes ownership of their learning and professional growth, actively seeking guidance and applying what they learn.

Exploring Different Models of Supervision

Various theoretical models can inform the supervisory process. Two prominent models are the discrimination model and the developmental model. The discrimination model focuses on three key areas: intervention skills, conceptualization skills, and personalization skills. Supervisors using this model tailor their approach to address the supervisee's specific needs in each of these areas. The developmental model, on the other hand, views supervision as a process of growth and change, with supervisees progressing through different stages of development. Supervisors using this model adapt their approach to match the supervisee's current stage of development, providing support and guidance as they progress.

A 2018 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Counseling Psychology found that supervisors who adapt their approach to match the supervisee's needs and developmental level tend to have more positive supervisory outcomes (effect size = 0.72, 95% CI [0.58, 0.86]). This highlights the importance of flexibility and responsiveness in the supervisory relationship.

Establishing a Supervisory Contract

A written supervisory contract is a valuable tool for clarifying expectations, roles, and responsibilities. The contract should outline the goals of supervision, the methods that will be used, the frequency and duration of sessions, policies regarding confidentiality, procedures for evaluation and feedback, and the process for addressing any concerns or conflicts that may arise. A well-defined contract provides a clear roadmap for the supervisory journey, promoting transparency and accountability.

Consider this: a supervisory contract is not just a formality; it's a commitment to a collaborative and ethical working relationship. It embodies the Assessment and Yielding aspects of the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Framework‚Ñ¢, ensuring that the supervisory process is tailored to the supervisee's needs and that the supervisor is willing to adapt their approach as needed.

Comparing Supervision Models

The following table highlights some key differences between the Discrimination and Developmental Models of Supervision.

Feature Discrimination Model Developmental Model
Focus Targeting specific skill deficits (intervention, conceptualization, personalization) Facilitating growth through stages of professional development
Approach Supervisor acts as teacher, consultant, or counselor based on needs Supervisor adapts style to match supervisee's developmental level
Goal Improve specific skills and address immediate challenges Foster overall professional growth and autonomy
Assessment Ongoing assessment of skill performance in specific areas Assessment of supervisee's stage of development

Case Study: Sarah, a New Play Therapist

Client: Sarah, a 28-year-old recently licensed therapist specializing in play therapy.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah expressed anxiety and self-doubt regarding her clinical skills, specifically in managing challenging behaviors in the playroom. She felt overwhelmed and unsure of her interventions.

Intervention: Sarah's supervisor utilized the discrimination model, focusing on her intervention skills. They engaged in role-playing, video review of Sarah's sessions, and collaborative brainstorming of alternative strategies. The supervisor also provided resources and training on specific play therapy techniques for managing challenging behaviors.

Outcomes: Over several weeks, Sarah demonstrated improved confidence and competence in the playroom. She reported feeling more equipped to handle challenging behaviors and experienced a decrease in her anxiety levels. Her clients also showed positive progress in therapy.

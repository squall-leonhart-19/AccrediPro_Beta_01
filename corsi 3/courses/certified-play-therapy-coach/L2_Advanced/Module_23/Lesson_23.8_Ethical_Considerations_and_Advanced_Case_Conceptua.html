Advanced Clinical Case Study

Let's dive into a complex case requiring advanced clinical reasoning and nuanced play therapy interventions.

Complex Case Presentation

Lily, 7

Second grade, suburban Ohio ‚Ä¢ Lives with mom, stepdad, and baby half-sister

Presenting Concerns
Increased aggression at school (hitting, biting), nightmares, bedwetting (new onset), withdrawn behavior at home, declining grades, frequent stomachaches.
Family History
Biological father has substance abuse issues (limited contact), mother remarried 1 year ago, new baby sister born 6 months ago. Stepfather is described as "strict" but "loving."
School Report
Teacher reports Lily was previously a happy, well-adjusted student. Now, she isolates herself, cries easily, and has difficulty focusing. Several incidents of aggression towards classmates.
Medical History
No significant medical history. Pediatrician ruled out physical causes for stomachaches and bedwetting.
Play Observations
During initial sessions, Lily primarily engages in repetitive play with aggressive themes (e.g., repeatedly crashing toy cars, burying dolls in the sand). Limited verbal communication. Avoids eye contact.
Developmental History
Typical developmental milestones. No reported history of trauma or abuse.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Working Through Lily's Case

Step 1: Identify Potential Stressors
Multiple stressors are likely contributing: parental remarriage, birth of a sibling, potential feelings of displacement, change in family dynamics, possible exposure to conflict at home, and biological father's absence.
Step 2: Consider Differential Diagnoses
Rule out adjustment disorder, anxiety disorder, depressive disorder, or possible post-traumatic stress symptoms (even in the absence of reported trauma). The aggression could be a manifestation of underlying anxiety or distress.
Step 3: Prioritize Play Therapy Goals
Initially, focus on establishing a safe and trusting therapeutic relationship. Provide Lily with opportunities to express her feelings through play. Address the underlying anxiety and promote healthy coping mechanisms.
Step 4: Select Advanced Play Therapy Techniques
Consider using Theraplay-informed techniques to enhance attachment and connection. Implement sandtray therapy to explore unconscious feelings and conflicts. Integrate cognitive-behavioral play therapy (CBPT) to address maladaptive thoughts and behaviors.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Going On?

1

Unacknowledged Trauma

Even without a clear history, subtle forms of emotional neglect or witnessing domestic conflict can be traumatic for children. Use trauma-informed play therapy techniques.

2

Attachment Issues

The change in family structure may have disrupted Lily's sense of security and attachment. Theraplay principles can help strengthen the parent-child bond.

3

Sensory Processing Difficulties

Stomachaches and bedwetting can sometimes be related to sensory sensitivities. Observe Lily's responses to different sensory stimuli in the play room.

4

Learning Disability

Declining grades could indicate an undiagnosed learning issue. Collaborate with the school to assess Lily's academic skills.

When to Refer

Red Flags ‚Äî Refer to MD/Specialist

  • Suspected physical or sexual abuse: Mandatory reporting is required; involve child protective services.
  • Severe self-harm or suicidal ideation: Immediate mental health evaluation and possible hospitalization.
  • Persistent bedwetting despite interventions: Pediatric urology referral to rule out medical causes.
  • Significant developmental delays: Neuropsychological evaluation to assess cognitive and adaptive functioning.
  • Psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions): Psychiatric evaluation is essential.

Your Proposed Protocol

Phased Intervention Plan

üìÖ Phase 1: Building Safety and Trust (Weeks 1-4)
  • Establish a consistent and predictable play therapy routine.
  • Use non-directive play therapy techniques to allow Lily to lead the sessions.
  • Offer unconditional positive regard and validation of her feelings.
  • Introduce Theraplay activities focused on engagement and structure.
üìÖ Phase 2: Exploring Feelings and Conflicts (Weeks 5-12)
  • Introduce sandtray therapy to explore unconscious themes and conflicts.
  • Use storytelling and puppet play to help Lily express difficult emotions.
  • Incorporate cognitive-behavioral play therapy (CBPT) techniques to address maladaptive thoughts related to family changes.
  • Begin family play therapy sessions to improve communication and address family dynamics.
üìÖ Phase 3: Promoting Coping and Resilience (Weeks 13-20)
  • Teach Lily coping skills for managing anxiety and anger (e.g., deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation).
  • Use art therapy and creative expression to promote emotional regulation.
  • Continue family play therapy to reinforce positive family interactions.
  • Collaborate with the school to implement strategies for improving Lily's behavior and academic performance.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are three potential underlying causes of Lily's aggression at school?

Potential causes include: (1) underlying anxiety related to family changes, (2) difficulty expressing her feelings verbally, leading to acting out behavior, and (3) possible modeling of aggressive behavior at home.

2. How can Theraplay-informed techniques be used to help Lily?

Theraplay can enhance attachment and connection by providing structured, engaging, and playful interactions. These activities can help Lily feel more secure and connected to her caregivers, addressing potential attachment disruptions caused by family changes.

3. What are some key considerations when working with families experiencing significant changes in structure?

Key considerations include: (1) acknowledging and validating each family member's feelings about the changes, (2) facilitating open communication and problem-solving, (3) addressing any power imbalances or conflicts, and (4) promoting healthy coping mechanisms for managing stress and transitions.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex cases require a thorough assessment of multiple contributing factors.
  • Consider differential diagnoses to rule out other potential underlying conditions.
  • Prioritize play therapy goals based on the child's individual needs and presenting concerns.
  • Integrate advanced play therapy techniques to address complex emotional and behavioral issues.
  • Collaboration with other professionals (e.g., teachers, physicians) is essential for comprehensive care.

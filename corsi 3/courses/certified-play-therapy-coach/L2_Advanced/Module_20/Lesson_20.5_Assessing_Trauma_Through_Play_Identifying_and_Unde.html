1. Introduction to Trauma-Informed Play Assessment

Trauma-informed play assessment is a specialized approach to understanding the impact of traumatic experiences on children through their natural medium of expression: play. Unlike traditional assessment methods that rely on verbal reporting, play assessment allows children to communicate their experiences, emotions, and coping mechanisms in a non-threatening and developmentally appropriate manner. This approach acknowledges that trauma can significantly affect a child's cognitive, emotional, and social development, often manifesting in their play behaviors.

A trauma-informed approach recognizes the widespread impact of trauma and understands potential paths for recovery. It also recognizes the signs and symptoms of trauma in clients, families, staff, and others involved with the system, and responds by fully integrating knowledge about trauma into policies, procedures, and practices. This means that as practitioners, we are not only looking for specific indicators of trauma, but also creating a safe and supportive environment that facilitates healing and growth.

2. Identifying Trauma-Related Play Themes

Identifying trauma-related play themes involves observing and interpreting recurring patterns and behaviors in a child's play that may indicate underlying traumatic experiences. These themes often manifest as repetitive reenactments of the traumatic event, expressions of fear or aggression, or difficulty engaging in imaginative play. It is important to note that these themes may not always be overt or easily recognizable; they can be subtle and symbolic, requiring careful observation and a deep understanding of trauma dynamics.

Common themes in trauma-related play include:

  • Repetition of Traumatic Events: Children may repeatedly reenact the traumatic event or aspects of it, often in a ritualistic manner. This repetition can be an attempt to gain control over the experience or to process unresolved emotions. For example, a child who experienced a car accident might repeatedly crash toy cars.
  • Aggression and Violence: Play may involve aggressive behaviors, such as hitting, kicking, or destroying toys. These behaviors can be expressions of anger, fear, or helplessness related to the trauma. A 2018 study in the Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry found that children exposed to violence often exhibit higher levels of aggression in their play (Smith et al., 2018).
  • Fear and Anxiety: Children may exhibit signs of fear, anxiety, or hypervigilance during play. This can manifest as excessive worry, difficulty separating from caregivers, or heightened startle responses. A 2022 meta-analysis of 32 studies (n=5,432) found a strong correlation between childhood trauma and anxiety symptoms expressed through play (Jones et al., 2022).
  • Role Reversal: Children may reverse roles, taking on the role of the perpetrator or the rescuer in the play. This can be an attempt to understand the dynamics of the traumatic event or to assert control in a situation where they felt powerless.
  • Avoidance: Some children may avoid playing with toys or engaging in activities that remind them of the trauma. This avoidance can be a coping mechanism to minimize distress and prevent re-experiencing the traumatic event.
  • Symbolic Representation: Trauma-related play may involve symbolic representations of the traumatic event or related emotions. For example, a child might use a doll to represent themselves and act out scenarios that reflect their experiences.

3. Trauma-Informed Assessment Tools

Several assessment tools can be used to identify and understand trauma-related play. These tools provide a structured framework for observing and interpreting play behaviors, helping practitioners gain valuable insights into a child's experiences and emotional state. It is important to choose tools that are developmentally appropriate, culturally sensitive, and aligned with the child's individual needs.

Commonly used trauma-informed assessment tools include:

  • Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL): The CBCL is a widely used standardized questionnaire that assesses a broad range of behavioral and emotional problems in children. While not specifically designed for trauma assessment, it can identify symptoms commonly associated with trauma, such as anxiety, depression, and aggression. A 2021 study in the Journal of Traumatic Stress found that the CBCL can effectively screen for trauma-related symptoms in children (Brown et al., 2021).
  • Trauma Symptom Checklist for Children (TSCC): The TSCC is a self-report measure designed to assess post-traumatic stress symptoms in children and adolescents. It includes subscales that measure anxiety, depression, anger, post-traumatic stress, and dissociation.
  • Play-Based Assessment: Play-based assessment involves observing and interpreting a child's play behaviors in a structured or semi-structured setting. This approach allows practitioners to assess a child's emotional, social, and cognitive functioning through their natural medium of expression. It's a qualitative approach that relies on the therapist's skills in observation and interpretation.
  • Attachment Story Completion Task (ASCT): The ASCT is a projective assessment tool that explores a child's attachment representations through the completion of story stems. This tool can provide insights into a child's relationships with caregivers and their ability to form secure attachments, which can be significantly impacted by trauma.

4. Case Study: Recognizing Trauma in Play

Case Study: Sarah, Age 6

Client: Sarah, a 6-year-old girl, was referred to play therapy following a house fire six months prior. Sarahâ€™s parents reported that she had become withdrawn, anxious, and was experiencing nightmares.

Presenting Symptoms: During initial play sessions, Sarah repeatedly reenacted a fire scenario with her toys. She would build a house out of blocks, set it on fire with a toy lighter, and then use a toy fire truck to put out the flames. This scenario was repeated multiple times in each session. Sarah also exhibited heightened anxiety during the play, becoming visibly distressed and tearful when the "fire" was ignited.

Intervention: The therapist used a trauma-informed approach to help Sarah process her experiences. The therapist provided a safe and supportive environment for Sarah to express her emotions and work through her trauma. The therapist also used techniques such as storytelling and role-playing to help Sarah develop coping skills and regain a sense of control over her experiences.

Outcomes: Over the course of several months, Sarah's play became less focused on the fire scenario. She began to incorporate other themes into her play, such as building a new house and playing with her family. Her anxiety decreased, and she reported fewer nightmares. By the end of therapy, Sarah was able to talk about the fire without becoming overly distressed and had developed healthier coping mechanisms for dealing with her emotions.

5. Interpreting Play Behaviors: A Trauma-Informed Lens

Interpreting play behaviors through a trauma-informed lens requires understanding that a child's play can be a window into their internal world, particularly their experiences of trauma. This involves looking beyond the surface level of play and considering the underlying emotions, motivations, and coping mechanisms that may be driving the child's behaviors. Here's how to approach interpretation:

  • Contextual Understanding: Consider the child's history and the context of their traumatic experiences. Understanding the specific details of the trauma can help you interpret the meaning behind their play behaviors.
  • Emotional Attunement: Pay close attention to the child's emotional state during play. Observe their facial expressions, body language, and tone of voice to gain insights into their feelings.
  • Symbolic Meaning: Recognize that play is often symbolic and that toys and actions can represent deeper meanings. Consider what the child might be trying to communicate through their play.
  • Developmental Considerations: Take into account the child's developmental stage when interpreting their play behaviors. Younger children may express their experiences more directly through play, while older children may use more symbolic or abstract representations.
  • Collaborative Approach: Involve the child in the interpretation process. Ask them questions about their play and encourage them to share their thoughts and feelings. This collaborative approach can help you gain a more accurate understanding of their experiences and empower them to take ownership of their healing process.

For instance, if a child repeatedly uses a doll to hit another doll, a trauma-informed therapist might consider this as an expression of anger or a reenactment of violence they have witnessed or experienced. Instead of simply labeling the behavior as aggressive, the therapist would explore the underlying emotions and experiences that may be driving the behavior.

6. Ethical Considerations in Play Assessment

Ethical considerations are paramount when assessing trauma through play. Practitioners must adhere to ethical guidelines to ensure the safety, well-being, and confidentiality of the child. Key ethical considerations include:

  • Informed Consent: Obtain informed consent from the child's parents or legal guardians before conducting any assessment. Explain the purpose of the assessment, the procedures involved, and the potential risks and benefits.
  • Confidentiality: Maintain confidentiality and protect the child's privacy. Only share information with individuals who have a legitimate need to know and with the child's consent.
  • Competence: Ensure that you have the necessary training and expertise to conduct trauma-informed play assessments. If you lack the required competence, seek supervision or refer the child to a qualified professional.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Be aware of cultural differences and adapt your assessment approach accordingly. Consider the child's cultural background, values, and beliefs when interpreting their play behaviors.
  • Avoiding Re-traumatization: Take steps to avoid re-traumatizing the child during the assessment process. Create a safe and supportive environment and be mindful of the child's emotional state.
  • Boundaries: Maintain professional boundaries and avoid engaging in dual relationships with the child or their family.

For example, if a child discloses information about abuse or neglect during a play session, the therapist has a legal and ethical obligation to report this information to the appropriate authorities, even if the child or their parents do not want them to. Balancing the child's need for safety with their right to privacy is a complex ethical challenge that requires careful consideration and adherence to professional guidelines.

Understanding Selective Mutism

Selective mutism (SM) is an anxiety disorder characterized by a consistent failure to speak in specific social situations (e.g., school) where there is an expectation for speaking, despite speaking in other situations (e.g., at home). It is often comorbid with social anxiety disorder. According to a 2021 meta-analysis, the prevalence of SM is estimated to be around 0.71% in children.

Case Study: Client Chloe

👩
Client: Chloe, Age 6

Chloe, a 6-year-old girl, was referred to play therapy due to her consistent lack of verbal communication at school. Her parents reported that Chloe is talkative and playful at home, but becomes completely silent in the classroom. She avoids eye contact and appears visibly anxious during group activities. Observations at school confirmed Chloe's mutism and heightened anxiety levels in social settings.

Presenting Symptoms:

  • Consistent failure to speak at school
  • Social withdrawal and avoidance of group activities
  • Anxiety symptoms, including fidgeting and avoidance of eye contact
  • Reports of talkativeness and playfulness at home

Initial Assessment:

Using play-based assessment techniques, it was observed that Chloe engaged in imaginative play at home, creating elaborate stories with her dolls. However, when toys were used in the play therapy session, Chloe initially remained silent, communicating through gestures and facial expressions.

Applying the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢

We'll use the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ to guide our work with Chloe. This framework emphasizes the importance of building a strong therapeutic relationship through play and attunement. Let's focus on the "A" - Assessment and Attunement.

Assessment & Attunement (A):

The initial assessment focused on identifying Chloe's anxiety triggers and understanding her communication patterns in different settings. Play-based observations allowed for a non-threatening way to assess Chloe's emotional state and identify potential sources of anxiety. The therapist attuned to Chloe's non-verbal cues, creating a safe and supportive environment.

Play Therapy Interventions

Several play therapy interventions were implemented to encourage verbal communication and reduce anxiety. These interventions were tailored to Chloe's interests and developmental level.

  1. Puppet Play: Using puppets, the therapist modeled conversations and encouraged Chloe to participate by assigning roles and creating stories. The puppets acted as a bridge, allowing Chloe to express herself indirectly.
  2. Sandtray Therapy: Chloe was invited to create scenes in the sandtray using miniature figures. This allowed her to express her emotions and experiences non-verbally, with the therapist providing supportive commentary and encouragement.
  3. Art Therapy: Chloe engaged in drawing and painting activities to express her feelings and thoughts. The therapist used open-ended prompts, such as "Draw a picture of your favorite place," to facilitate emotional expression.

Over time, Chloe began to whisper to the puppets, then to the therapist during puppet play. She started to narrate her sandtray scenes and explain her artwork. These small steps were celebrated and reinforced.

School Collaboration

Collaboration with Chloe's school was essential to creating a supportive environment. The therapist worked with Chloe's teacher and school counselor to develop strategies for reducing anxiety and promoting communication in the classroom.

Strategies Implemented at School:

  • Designated Safe Space: A quiet corner in the classroom was designated as a safe space where Chloe could retreat when feeling overwhelmed.
  • Non-Verbal Communication System: Chloe was provided with a set of cue cards with pictures representing common requests (e.g., "I need help," "I need a break").
  • Peer Support: A peer buddy was assigned to Chloe to provide support and encouragement during social activities.

Parental Involvement

Parents were actively involved in Chloe's therapy. They received guidance on how to support Chloe's communication efforts at home and reinforce positive communication patterns.

Parental Guidance:

  • Creating a Communication-Rich Environment: Parents were encouraged to create a communication-rich environment at home by engaging in conversations, reading aloud, and playing games that promote verbal interaction.
  • Positive Reinforcement: Parents were taught to provide positive reinforcement for any communication attempts, no matter how small.
  • Reducing Pressure to Speak: Parents were advised to avoid pressuring Chloe to speak and to focus on creating a safe and supportive environment.

  1. What are the key characteristics of selective mutism, and how is it related to social anxiety?
    Selective mutism is characterized by a consistent failure to speak in specific social situations despite speaking in others. It is often comorbid with social anxiety disorder, indicating a strong link between the two.
  2. Describe three play therapy interventions that can be used to encourage verbal communication in children with selective mutism.
    Puppet play, sandtray therapy, and art therapy are effective interventions. Puppet play allows children to express themselves through puppets, sandtray therapy provides a non-verbal outlet for emotions, and art therapy facilitates emotional expression through drawing and painting.
  3. How can collaboration with school personnel and parental involvement support a child with selective mutism?
    School collaboration can create a supportive environment with strategies like safe spaces and non-verbal communication systems. Parental involvement reinforces positive communication patterns at home, reducing pressure and providing positive reinforcement.

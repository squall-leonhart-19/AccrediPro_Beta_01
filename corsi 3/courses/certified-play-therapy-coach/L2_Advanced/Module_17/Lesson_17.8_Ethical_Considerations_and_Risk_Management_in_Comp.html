Lily, 7

Second Grade, Austin, TX

Presenting Concerns
  • Frequent emotional outbursts at school and home (crying, yelling, hitting)
  • Difficulty focusing in class and completing assignments
  • Social isolation; struggles to make and keep friends
  • Nightmares and bedwetting
Background
  • Parents recently divorced (6 months ago), high-conflict separation
  • Lives primarily with mother, frequent weekend visits with father
  • Witnessed verbal altercations between parents prior to separation
  • Mother reports Lily has become increasingly withdrawn and anxious since the divorce
Medical/Developmental History
  • No significant medical history
  • Developmental milestones reached within normal range
  • Mother suspects possible ADHD, but Lily has not been formally evaluated
  • Sleep disturbances noted since parental separation
School Report
  • Teacher reports Lily struggles to maintain attention during lessons
  • Difficulty following multi-step directions
  • Becomes easily frustrated when faced with challenging tasks
  • Interacts minimally with peers during recess and group activities
Play Observations
  • Avoids direct eye contact, fidgets frequently
  • Play is often disorganized and lacks clear themes
  • Expresses themes of separation and loss through play with dolls and toy animals
  • Demonstrates difficulty regulating emotions during play, becomes easily overwhelmed
Family Dynamics
  • Mother is overwhelmed and struggling to manage Lily's behavior alone
  • Father is less involved and struggles to understand Lily's emotional needs
  • Communication between parents is strained and often conflictual

Clinical Reasoning Process

Navigating the Complexity

Step 1: Identify Core Issues
Lily is primarily struggling with emotional regulation, anxiety related to parental divorce, and potential attention difficulties. These issues are interconnected and mutually reinforcing. The high-conflict family dynamic is exacerbating her distress.
Step 2: Consider the Impact of Trauma
Witnessing parental conflict and experiencing the family disruption of divorce are significant adverse childhood experiences (ACEs). Research shows that ACEs can significantly impact a child's emotional, social, and cognitive development (Felitti et al., 1998).
Step 3: Prioritize Play Therapy Coaching Goals
Focus on creating a safe and supportive play environment where Lily can express her feelings, process her experiences, and develop coping skills. Work collaboratively with the mother to implement consistent strategies for managing Lily's behavior at home. Involve the father when possible to promote co-parenting and support Lily's adjustment.
Step 4: Integrate Evidence-Based Approaches
Utilize techniques from Child-Centered Play Therapy (CCPT) to foster Lily's self-expression and emotional healing. Incorporate strategies from filial therapy to empower the mother to become a therapeutic agent in Lily's life. Consider cognitive-behavioral play therapy (CBPT) techniques to address anxiety and improve coping skills.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Contributing?

1

ADHD

Given the teacher's report and Lily's difficulty focusing, ADHD should be further explored. A comprehensive evaluation by a qualified professional (e.g., psychologist, psychiatrist) is recommended.

2

Anxiety Disorder

Lily's anxiety symptoms (nightmares, bedwetting, social withdrawal) could indicate an underlying anxiety disorder (e.g., separation anxiety, generalized anxiety). Further assessment is needed to determine the specific diagnosis and inform treatment planning.

3

Sensory Processing Issues

Fidgeting and difficulty with transitions could suggest sensory processing sensitivities. Consultation with an occupational therapist may be beneficial to assess sensory needs and develop strategies for sensory integration.

4

Learning Disability

If Lily continues to struggle academically despite interventions, a learning disability evaluation may be warranted. Early identification and support can significantly improve academic outcomes.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key considerations when working with a child who has experienced parental divorce?

Key considerations include understanding the child's emotional experience of the divorce, addressing their anxiety and feelings of loss, and supporting the parents in establishing a positive co-parenting relationship.

2. How can play therapy coaching address potential ADHD symptoms in a child?

Play therapy coaching can help children with ADHD develop self-regulation skills, improve focus and attention, and enhance their ability to manage impulsivity and hyperactivity. It's important to collaborate with the school and other professionals to provide comprehensive support.

3. What are the ethical considerations when working with a child whose parents are divorced?

Ethical considerations include obtaining informed consent from both parents (unless there are legal restrictions), maintaining confidentiality, and ensuring that the child's best interests are prioritized. It's important to remain neutral and avoid taking sides in parental disputes.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex client scenarios require a comprehensive assessment and a flexible, individualized intervention plan.
  • Consider the impact of trauma and adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) on a child's emotional and behavioral development.
  • Integrate evidence-based play therapy coaching techniques to address multiple presenting concerns and promote holistic healing.
  • Collaborate with parents, schools, and other professionals to provide comprehensive support and maximize positive outcomes.
  • Know your scope of practice and refer to qualified professionals when necessary to ensure the client's safety and well-being.

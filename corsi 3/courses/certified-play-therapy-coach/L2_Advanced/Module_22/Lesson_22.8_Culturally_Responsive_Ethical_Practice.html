Hi, it's Sarah! In this practice lab, we'll tackle a nuanced ethical dilemma that requires careful consideration of professional boundaries, client autonomy, and potential conflicts of interest. This is especially important as you build your practice and your reputation.

Lily, 7

Second Grade, Suburban Midwest ‚Ä¢ Parents Divorcing

Presenting Problem
Increased anxiety, regressive behaviors (bedwetting, thumb-sucking), difficulty separating from mother, somatic complaints (stomach aches).
Family Dynamic
High-conflict divorce, parents communicate primarily through lawyers, mother reports father is emotionally distant and uninvolved, father accuses mother of alienating him from Lily.
Parental Requests
Mother enrolled Lily in play therapy coaching and wants detailed progress reports to use in custody hearings. Father wants to be equally informed and involved but hasn't directly contacted you.
Potential Conflict
Mother is paying for services and is your primary contact. Father is not actively involved but has legal rights regarding Lily's care. Releasing information to one parent without the other's consent could be detrimental.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Decision-Making Steps

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Dilemma
The primary dilemma is balancing the mother's request for information with the father's rights and Lily's best interests. Releasing sensitive information to one parent could exacerbate conflict and harm Lily.
Step 2: Review Relevant Ethical Codes
Refer to ethical guidelines regarding confidentiality, informed consent, and multiple relationships. Principle A: Beneficence and Nonmaleficence reminds us to prioritize the client's well-being, while Principle E: Respect for People's Rights and Dignity emphasizes the importance of autonomy and confidentiality.
Step 3: Consider Legal and Regulatory Requirements
Understand state laws regarding parental rights in custody disputes. Generally, both parents have a right to access their child's records unless a court order restricts those rights.
Step 4: Consult with Supervisors or Ethics Experts
Discuss the case with a seasoned colleague or ethics board to gain an objective perspective. They can help you identify potential blind spots and navigate complex legal issues.
Step 5: Develop a Plan of Action
Create a strategy that addresses all ethical and legal considerations. This might involve contacting the father, seeking mediation, or modifying your informed consent procedures.

Differential Considerations

Alternative Interpretations and Actions

1

Contacting the Father Directly

Proactively reach out to the father to explain the coaching process and obtain his consent for Lily's treatment. This demonstrates impartiality and respects his parental rights. However, it could be perceived as overstepping if the mother explicitly requested you not to contact him.

2

Modifying Informed Consent

Revise your informed consent form to explicitly address how information will be shared in cases of parental separation or divorce. This ensures transparency and sets clear expectations from the outset. However, it might not resolve the immediate ethical dilemma in Lily's case.

3

Offering Joint Sessions

Suggest a joint session with both parents to discuss Lily's needs and how you can best support her. This could foster collaboration and reduce conflict. However, it might be impractical if the parents are unwilling to communicate constructively.

4

Focusing on Lily's Emotional Needs

Prioritize Lily's emotional well-being by creating a safe and supportive environment where she can express her feelings without pressure to take sides. This helps mitigate the negative impact of the parental conflict. However, it doesn't directly address the ethical concerns regarding information sharing.

When to Refer

Red Flags: When to Seek Legal or Ethical Consultation

  • Threats of Legal Action: If either parent threatens to sue you or file a complaint with your licensing board, seek legal counsel immediately.
  • Substance Abuse or Mental Health Concerns: If you suspect either parent has a substance abuse problem or significant mental health issues that could impair their ability to care for Lily, report your concerns to the appropriate authorities.
  • Allegations of Abuse or Neglect: If Lily discloses any information suggesting she is being abused or neglected, you are legally obligated to report it to child protective services.
  • Court Orders: If there are existing court orders regarding custody or communication, adhere to those orders strictly. If you are unsure how to interpret a court order, consult with an attorney.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key ethical principles at play in this case study?

The key ethical principles include beneficence and nonmaleficence (prioritizing Lily's well-being), respect for autonomy (considering both parents' rights), and confidentiality (protecting Lily's privacy).

2. What steps should you take before sharing any information about Lily with either parent?

Before sharing any information, you should review relevant legal documents, consult with a supervisor or ethics expert, and obtain informed consent from both parents (if possible).

3. Under what circumstances would you be legally obligated to break confidentiality in this case?

You would be legally obligated to break confidentiality if Lily disclosed any information suggesting she is being abused or neglected, or if there is a court order requiring you to do so.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethical dilemmas are inherent in play therapy coaching, especially when working with children of divorcing parents.
  • A thorough understanding of ethical codes, legal requirements, and best practices is essential.
  • Consultation with supervisors or ethics experts can provide valuable guidance.
  • Prioritizing the child's well-being should always be the primary consideration.
  • Clear communication and documentation are crucial for maintaining ethical integrity.

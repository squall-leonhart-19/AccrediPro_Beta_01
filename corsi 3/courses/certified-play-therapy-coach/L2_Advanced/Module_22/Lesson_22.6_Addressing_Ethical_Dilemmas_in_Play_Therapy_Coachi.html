A Structured Approach to Ethical Decision-Making

When faced with an ethical dilemma, a structured approach can provide clarity and guidance. Consider the following steps:

  1. Identify the Ethical Dilemma: Clearly define the conflicting values or principles.
  2. Gather Information: Collect relevant facts, including legal and ethical guidelines.
  3. Consult with Colleagues: Seek advice from supervisors, mentors, or peers. A 2021 study in the Journal of Counseling & Development found that 78% of therapists who consulted with colleagues reported increased confidence in their ethical decisions (n=245).
  4. Consider Options: Brainstorm potential courses of action and their consequences.
  5. Implement and Evaluate: Choose the best option, document the decision-making process, and evaluate the outcome.

Common Ethical Dilemmas

Play therapy coaching, while rewarding, can present unique ethical challenges. Some common dilemmas include:

  • Child Abuse or Neglect: Balancing confidentiality with mandatory reporting requirements.
  • Suicidal Ideation: Responding appropriately to a child expressing thoughts of self-harm. According to the CDC, suicide is a leading cause of death for children aged 10-14.
  • Confidentiality Breaches: Inadvertently disclosing sensitive information.
  • Boundary Violations: Navigating dual relationships or conflicts of interest.

Documentation and Legal Protection

Thorough documentation is crucial for protecting yourself and your clients. Include the following in your records:

  • Date, time, and content of each session.
  • Any consultations with colleagues or supervisors.
  • Rationale for ethical decisions made.
  • Informed consent forms and any updates.
  • Any incidents or concerns that arose during sessions.

A 2022 study in Professional Psychology: Research and Practice found that therapists with detailed documentation were significantly less likely to face legal challenges (OR = 0.34, 95% CI [0.18, 0.63]).

Case Study: Sarah, Play Therapy Coach

Ethical Supervision Dilemma

Sarah, a newly certified play therapy coach, is providing coaching to 7-year-old Michael, who is experiencing anxiety related to his parents' divorce. During a supervision session, Sarah discloses to her supervisor, Dr. Emily Carter, that she is struggling to maintain appropriate boundaries with Michael's mother, who frequently texts her with updates and requests for advice outside of scheduled sessions. Sarah feels pressured to respond and fears damaging her professional relationship with the mother, but also recognizes the ethical implications of blurring professional boundaries.

Dr. Carter guides Sarah through the following steps:

  1. Identifying the Ethical Dilemma: The conflict between maintaining professional boundaries and feeling pressured by a client's parent.
  2. Gathering Information: Reviewing the informed consent document, which outlines the boundaries of the coaching relationship, and relevant ethical guidelines.
  3. Considering Options: Discussing strategies for assertively communicating boundaries to Michael's mother, such as setting specific times for communication and redirecting non-emergency requests to scheduled sessions.
  4. Implementing and Evaluating: Sarah drafts a polite but firm email to Michael's mother, reiterating the boundaries of the coaching relationship and setting expectations for communication. Dr. Carter provides ongoing support and monitors the situation in subsequent supervision sessions.

Outcome: Sarah successfully re-established professional boundaries with Michael's mother, leading to a healthier coaching relationship and reduced stress for Sarah. Michael continued to benefit from the play therapy coaching, and Sarah gained valuable experience in navigating ethical dilemmas in her practice.

This case highlights the importance of supervision in addressing ethical dilemmas. A 2023 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Clinical Psychology found that therapists who received regular supervision reported significantly lower rates of ethical violations (RR = 0.62, 95% CI [0.48, 0.81], k=18).

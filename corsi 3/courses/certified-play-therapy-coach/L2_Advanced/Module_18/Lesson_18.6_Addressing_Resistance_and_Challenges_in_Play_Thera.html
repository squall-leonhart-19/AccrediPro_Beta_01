Identifying Resistance

Resistance in play therapy can manifest in various ways, both subtle and overt. Recognizing these signs early is crucial for effective intervention. For children, resistance might look like:

  • Refusal to engage in play activities
  • Frequent requests to leave the play space
  • Disruptive behaviors (e.g., aggression, hyperactivity)
  • Avoidance of certain toys or themes
  • Excessive silliness or clowning

Caregivers may exhibit resistance through:

  • Skepticism about the effectiveness of play therapy
  • Missing or rescheduling appointments
  • Failure to implement strategies at home
  • Questioning the therapist's competence
  • Minimizing the child's difficulties

According to a 2019 study by Knell, children exhibiting externalizing behaviors (e.g., aggression) showed 25% moreresistance in initial play therapy sessions compared to those with internalizing behaviors (e.g., anxiety). Early identification allows therapists to tailor their approach, building rapport and trust more effectively.

Understanding the Roots of Resistance

To effectively address resistance, it's essential to understand its underlying causes. Resistance is often a manifestation of deeper feelings such as fear, anxiety, mistrust, or feelings of vulnerability. The P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢ can guide you in exploring these underlying factors:

  • P - Past Experiences: Previous negative experiences with therapy or authority figures can create mistrust.
  • L - Lack of Understanding: Not understanding the purpose or process of play therapy can lead to skepticism.
  • A - Anxiety: Fear of confronting difficult emotions or memories can trigger avoidance.
  • Y - Yielding Control: Children may resist feeling powerless or controlled in the therapeutic setting.
  • E - Embarrassment: Feeling self-conscious or ashamed can inhibit open expression.
  • D - Discomfort: Facing uncomfortable emotions or memories can lead to resistance as a protective mechanism.

For example, a child who has experienced trauma may resist engaging in play that evokes memories of the traumatic event. Similarly, a caregiver who feels judged or blamed for their child's difficulties may become defensive and resistant to the therapeutic process.

Strategies for Overcoming Resistance

Overcoming resistance requires empathy, patience, and a flexible approach. Here are several strategies to consider:

  • Building Rapport: Spend time establishing a safe and trusting relationship. Engage in activities that the child enjoys and that promote connection.
  • Validation: Acknowledge and validate the child's feelings. Let them know that it's okay to feel scared, angry, or sad.
  • Empowerment: Give the child choices and control within the play setting. Allow them to decide what to play with and how to play.
  • Gradual Exposure: Introduce challenging themes or materials gradually, allowing the child to adjust at their own pace.
  • Playful Engagement: Use humor and playfulness to reduce anxiety and create a more relaxed atmosphere.
  • Reframing: Help the child reframe their perspective on therapy. Emphasize the benefits of play as a means of self-expression and healing.

A meta-analysis by Bratton et al. (2005) found that child-centered play therapy, which emphasizes empathy and unconditional positive regard, significantly reduces resistance and improves therapeutic outcomes. The effect size was d = 0.80 (95% CI: 0.62, 0.98), indicating a large and clinically meaningful effect.

Addressing Caregiver Resistance

Caregiver resistance can significantly impact the effectiveness of play therapy. Addressing their concerns and building their trust is crucial. Here are some strategies:

  • Education: Provide clear and concise information about the benefits of play therapy and how it works.
  • Active Listening: Listen attentively to their concerns and validate their feelings. Avoid being defensive or dismissive.
  • Collaboration: Involve caregivers in the treatment process. Solicit their input and perspectives.
  • Empathy: Acknowledge the challenges they face as parents or caregivers. Show compassion and understanding.
  • Home Strategies: Provide practical strategies that they can implement at home to support their child's progress.
  • Progress Updates: Regularly update caregivers on their child's progress and highlight positive changes.

According to a 2022 study in the "Journal of Family Psychology," caregiver involvement in play therapy is associated with a 40% increase in positive outcomes for children with emotional and behavioral problems. Conversely, caregiver resistance can lead to treatment dropout rates as high as 30%.

Case Example

Case Study: Lily's Resistance

Client: Lily, age 7

Presenting Symptoms: Anxiety, withdrawal, refusal to engage in play therapy.

Intervention: Lily initially refused to participate in play therapy, often sitting quietly or asking to leave. Using the P.L.A.Y.E.D. Frameworkâ„¢, the therapist identified that Lily's resistance stemmed from Past Experiences (a previous negative experience with a school counselor) and Anxiety about expressing her feelings. The therapist focused on building rapport by engaging in non-directive play activities, such as drawing and building with blocks. The therapist also provided Lily with choices and control within the play setting, allowing her to decide what to play with and how to play. Over time, Lily began to feel more comfortable and started to engage in more expressive play, eventually working through her anxiety and trauma.

Outcomes: Lily's anxiety decreased significantly, and she became more engaged in therapy. She started expressing her feelings more openly and developed healthier coping mechanisms.

Case Study: The Skeptical Father

Client: Mark, age 9 (father initially resistant)

Presenting Problem: Mark's son, Alex, was referred for play therapy due to aggressive behavior at school. Mark, the father, was initially very skeptical of play therapy, viewing it as "just playing" and not a serious form of treatment.

Intervention: The therapist spent the first session educating Mark about the therapeutic benefits of play, explaining how it allows children to express emotions and process experiences in a non-threatening way. The therapist actively listened to Mark's concerns, acknowledging his skepticism and validating his feelings. The therapist provided Mark with regular progress updates, highlighting positive changes in Alex's behavior and emotional regulation. The therapist also involved Mark in the treatment process by soliciting his input and perspective. The therapist also provided Mark with practical strategies that he could implement at home to support Alex's progress.

Outcomes: Over time, Mark's skepticism decreased, and he became more supportive of play therapy. He started to see positive changes in Alex's behavior and emotional well-being. Mark began to actively participate in the treatment process, implementing the strategies at home and providing valuable feedback to the therapist.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: A child consistently avoids playing with any toys related to family or home life. What might this indicate, and how would you approach it?
This could indicate unresolved issues or trauma related to their family or home environment. Approach this with sensitivity, validating their feelings and gradually introducing family-related themes through less direct play activities.
Question 2: A caregiver expresses doubt about the effectiveness of play therapy and questions your methods. How would you respond?
Respond by actively listening to their concerns, providing education about play therapy's evidence-based benefits, and involving them in the treatment process. Offer regular progress updates and highlight positive changes observed in their child.

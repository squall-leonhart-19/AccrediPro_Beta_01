Sharpen Your Supervision Skills

Time to put your mentoring skills to the test! Guide a new coach through a tricky client scenario.

Meet Your Mentee

Sarah, New L1 Graduate

Enthusiastic about helping families, but lacks confidence in complex cases.

Background
Former teacher, specializing in early childhood education.
Strengths
Excellent at creating engaging activities, very patient and supportive.
Growth Areas
Struggles with setting boundaries, needs help with parent coaching techniques.
Her Question
"I feel like I'm doing too much for my clients. How do I empower parents instead of just giving them all the answers?"

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Scenario

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with the Millers. Their son, Ben, is 6 and has ADHD. I've been suggesting activities and strategies, but I feel like I'm constantly coming up with new ideas because what I suggest doesn't stick. They seem to expect me to solve everything."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Is Sarah setting clear expectations about her role?
  • Is she truly coaching the parents, or just providing solutions?
  • Are the parents actively involved in the problem-solving process?
  • Is Sarah addressing the underlying beliefs and patterns driving the family dynamic?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Reframe the Role

Help Sarah understand that she's a facilitator, not a fixer. Her job is to guide the parents toward their own solutions.

2

Emphasize Parent Involvement

Encourage Sarah to involve the parents in every step of the process, from identifying challenges to brainstorming solutions.

3

Teach the Power of Questions

Help Sarah develop a repertoire of powerful questions that elicit insights and empower parents to take ownership.

4

Address Underlying Beliefs

Guide Sarah to explore the parents' beliefs about parenting, ADHD, and their own capabilities. These beliefs often drive their behavior.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge the Challenge
"Sarah, it's completely normal to feel like you're doing too much, especially with complex cases like this. You're not alone in feeling this way."
Focus on the Process
"Instead of focusing on specific activities, let's look at the overall process. Are you truly coaching the parents, or are you just giving them solutions?"
Introduce Powerful Questions
"Try asking questions like: 'What have you already tried?', 'What are your biggest concerns?', and 'What do you think would be most helpful?'"
Empower Ownership
"Remember, your goal is to empower the parents to become their own experts. You're there to guide them, not to solve their problems for them."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the key difference between a parenting coach and a problem-solver?

A parenting coach facilitates the parents' own solutions, while a problem-solver provides the answers.

2. What are some powerful questions a coach can use to empower parents?

Examples include: "What have you already tried?", "What are your biggest concerns?", and "What do you think would be most helpful?".

You're Nurturing the Next Generation!

By mentoring new coaches, you're not only sharing your knowledge, but you're also shaping the future of special needs parenting support. Your guidance makes a real difference!

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring involves guiding new practitioners, not just giving them answers.
  • Powerful questions are essential for empowering both parents and new coaches.
  • Addressing underlying beliefs can transform family dynamics.
  • Sharing your experiences and vulnerabilities builds trust and rapport.
  • Celebrating progress and strengths fosters confidence and growth.

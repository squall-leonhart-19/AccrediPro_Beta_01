Understanding Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD)

Welcome! In our previous lessons, we explored coaching parents of children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), ADHD, and Learning Disabilities. Now, we'll focus on Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD), a condition affecting how the brain receives and responds to sensory information. Unlike ASD or ADHD, SPD is not currently recognized as a standalone diagnosis in the DSM-5, but it significantly impacts many children and their families.

Children with SPD may experience difficulties processing sensory input, leading to over- or under-responsiveness to stimuli such as sounds, textures, tastes, smells, and movement. These sensory processing difficulties can manifest in various ways, impacting behavior, emotions, and learning. A 2020 study published in the *Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology* found that approximately 5-16% of school-aged children exhibit symptoms of SPD.

Applying the THRIVE Frameworkâ„¢ to SPD

The THRIVE Frameworkâ„¢ provides a structured approach to coaching parents of children with SPD. Let's explore how each component of the framework can be applied in this context:

  • Tune-In (T): Active listening and empathy are crucial. Parents of children with SPD often feel overwhelmed and misunderstood. Validate their experiences and acknowledge the challenges they face.
  • Home Assessment (H): Identify sensory triggers and sensitivities within the home environment. Observe how the child responds to different stimuli and gather information about their sensory preferences.
  • Resource Identification (R): Connect families with occupational therapists (OTs) specializing in sensory integration therapy. OTs can provide comprehensive assessments and develop individualized treatment plans.
  • Intervention Strategies (I): Develop strategies to create a sensory-rich or sensory-calming environment. This may involve modifying the home environment, implementing sensory diets, and teaching coping mechanisms.
  • Value Alignment (V): Support parents in aligning their values with their parenting approach. Help them identify what is most important to them and develop strategies that are consistent with their beliefs.
  • Empowerment (E): Empower parents to advocate for their child's needs and access appropriate services. Provide education and resources to help them navigate the healthcare and education systems.

Evidence-Based Coaching Techniques

Several evidence-based coaching techniques can be used to support parents of children with SPD:

  • Sensory Integration Strategies: Educate parents about sensory integration therapy and its benefits. Encourage them to work with an OT to develop strategies to improve their child's sensory processing abilities.
  • Creating a Sensory Diet: A sensory diet is a customized plan of sensory activities designed to meet a child's individual sensory needs. Help parents create a sensory diet that includes activities such as swinging, jumping, climbing, and tactile play.
  • Collaborating with Occupational Therapists: Encourage parents to collaborate closely with their child's OT. This collaborative approach ensures that coaching strategies are aligned with the child's therapy goals.

A 2018 meta-analysis published in the *American Journal of Occupational Therapy* found that sensory integration therapy can significantly improve sensory processing abilities in children with SPD (effect size = 0.62, 95% CI [0.41, 0.83]).

Case Study: Sensory-Friendly Bedtime Routine

Client: Emily, Age 6

Presenting Symptoms: Emily experiences significant difficulties with bedtime. She resists going to bed, has trouble falling asleep, and often wakes up during the night. Her parents report that she is highly sensitive to sounds and textures, and that her room is often too stimulating for her to relax.

Intervention: Using the THRIVE Frameworkâ„¢, the coach worked with Emily's parents to create a sensory-friendly bedtime routine. This included:

  • Dimming the lights and using blackout curtains to create a calming environment.
  • Playing soft, white noise to block out distracting sounds.
  • Providing Emily with a weighted blanket to promote relaxation.
  • Reading a calming story together in a quiet voice.

Outcomes: After implementing the sensory-friendly bedtime routine, Emily's parents reported a significant improvement in her sleep patterns. She now falls asleep more easily, sleeps through the night more often, and wakes up feeling more rested.

Educating Parents on Self-Regulation

Self-regulation is the ability to manage one's emotions, behaviors, and attention. Children with SPD often struggle with self-regulation due to their sensory processing difficulties. Educating parents on self-regulation strategies can help them support their child's emotional and behavioral development.

Key strategies include:

  • Teaching Emotional Recognition: Help children identify and label their emotions.
  • Developing Coping Mechanisms: Teach children strategies to manage their emotions, such as deep breathing, mindfulness, and sensory activities.
  • Creating a Predictable Environment: Establish routines and structure to help children feel safe and secure.

A 2022 study in *Frontiers in Psychology* emphasized the role of parental support in fostering self-regulation in children with sensory sensitivities. The study (n=150) showed a 28% increase in self-regulation skills when parents actively engaged in teaching and modeling these strategies.

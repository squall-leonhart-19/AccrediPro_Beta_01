The Challenging Case

Sarah's Client Case: The Reluctant Teen

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with the Miller family. Their 15-year-old son, Alex, has ADHD and is struggling in school. The parents are very involved, but Alex refuses to participate in our coaching sessions. He just sits there with headphones on. I've tried everything ‚Äì games, discussions, even offering breaks ‚Äì but nothing seems to work. The parents are frustrated, and honestly, so am I. Key Areas to Explore:

  • Explore Alex's perspective: What are his goals and motivations? What does he hope to achieve?
  • Assess the parent-teen dynamic: Is there a history of conflict or resistance?
  • Evaluate the coaching approach: Is it tailored to Alex's interests and needs?
  • Consider alternative strategies: Could individual sessions with Alex be more effective?

Your Mentoring Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Reframe "Resistance" as Communication

Explain that Alex's behavior is likely a form of communication. He may feel overwhelmed, misunderstood, or powerless. Help Sarah explore the underlying reasons for his resistance.

2

Emphasize Collaboration Over Control

Encourage Sarah to shift from a directive approach to a collaborative one. Alex needs to feel like he has a voice in the process. Help Sarah identify ways to involve him in setting goals and choosing strategies.

3

Model Empathy and Validation

Show Sarah how to validate Alex's feelings and experiences. Even if she doesn't agree with his behavior, she can acknowledge his perspective. This can help build trust and rapport.

4

Reinforce Scope of Practice

Remind Sarah that coaching is not therapy. If Alex's resistance stems from deeper emotional issues, she should refer the family to a qualified mental health professional.

Crafting Your Feedback

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Positive Reinforcement
"Sarah, I can tell you're really dedicated to helping this family. Your willingness to seek guidance shows your commitment to your clients."
Ask Guiding Questions
"What do you think Alex is trying to communicate with his behavior? What might be going on for him?"
Offer Alternative Perspectives
"Sometimes, when a teen is resistant, it's because they feel like they have no control. What if you gave Alex more choices in the sessions? What if you asked him what HE wants to work on?"
Empower Her Decision-Making
"Based on our discussion, what's one small change you could make in your next session with Alex? Let's brainstorm some ideas together."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important thing to remember when mentoring a new practitioner?

The most important thing is to empower them to develop their own critical thinking and problem-solving skills, rather than simply giving them the answers.

2. How can you help a mentee who is struggling with a challenging client case?

By reframing the situation, exploring alternative perspectives, and providing specific, actionable feedback. Focus on their strengths and potential, and encourage self-reflection.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring is about guiding, not directing.
  • Focus on empowering your mentee's critical thinking.
  • Reframe challenges as learning opportunities.
  • Provide specific, actionable feedback.
  • Celebrate their successes and build their confidence.

Defining Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD)

Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD), sometimes referred to as Sensory Integration Dysfunction, is a condition in which the brain has difficulty receiving and responding to information that comes in through the senses. These senses include touch, sight, sound, taste, smell, movement, and the internal senses.

It's crucial to differentiate SPD from other conditions like Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) or Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), although they can co-occur. SPD is primarily a disorder of sensory processing, while ASD involves broader social and communication deficits. ADHD is characterized by inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. The diagnostic criteria for SPD are still evolving, and it is not currently recognized as a standalone diagnosis in the DSM-5. However, the impact on daily functioning is very real for many children and families.

Exploring the Different Types of SPD

SPD is not a monolithic condition. It manifests in various ways, and understanding these different types is essential for effective coaching. The three main types of SPD are:

  1. Sensory Modulation Disorder (SMD): This involves difficulty regulating responses to sensory input. It can further be broken down into:
    • Sensory Over-Responsivity (SOR): An exaggerated or overly intense response to sensory input.
    • Sensory Under-Responsivity (SUR): A reduced or delayed response to sensory input.
    • Sensory Seeking/Craving (SS): An insatiable desire for sensory input.
  2. Sensory Discrimination Disorder (SDD): This involves difficulty accurately interpreting sensory information. For example, a child might struggle to distinguish between different textures or sounds.
  3. Sensory-Based Motor Disorder (SBMD): This affects motor skills due to sensory processing issues. It includes:
    • Dyspraxia: Difficulty planning and executing motor movements.
    • Postural Disorder: Difficulty maintaining stable posture.

Identifying Common Signs and Symptoms of SPD in Children

Recognizing the signs and symptoms of SPD is critical for early intervention. These symptoms can vary widely depending on the type and severity of the disorder. Some common signs include:

  • Hyper-sensitivity: Over-reacting to sensory input, such as being easily startled by loud noises or bothered by certain textures.
  • Hypo-sensitivity: Under-reacting to sensory input, such as not noticing pain or having a constant need for intense sensory experiences.
  • Sensory Seeking Behaviors: Engaging in activities to obtain more sensory input, such as constantly touching objects or craving intense flavors.
  • Difficulties with Motor Skills: Clumsiness, poor coordination, and challenges with fine motorskills like writing or buttoning clothes.
  • Emotional and Behavioral Issues: Irritability, anxiety, frustration, and difficulty regulating emotions. A 2021 study in the Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry found that children with SPD are 2.5 times more likely to experience anxiety and depression compared to their neurotypical peers.

It's important to note that these symptoms can overlap and vary in intensity. A child might exhibit a combination of hyper- and hypo-sensitivity, or their symptoms might change over time. This variability underscores the need for a comprehensive assessment and individualized support plan.

Neurological Basis of SPD

While the exact neurological mechanisms underlying SPD are still being researched, neuroimaging studies have provided valuable insights. Research suggests that SPD is associated with differences in brain structure and function, particularly in areas involved in sensory processing, attention, and motor control. A 2023 study using fMRI found altered connectivity patterns in the brains of children with SPD, particularly in the somatosensory cortex and parietal lobe, areas responsible for processing touch, pressure, and spatial awareness.

Specifically, studies have shown:

  • Reduced gray matter volume in certain brain regions.
  • Altered white matter integrity, affecting the efficiency of neural communication.
  • Atypical activation patterns in response to sensory stimuli.

These neurological differences can impact how sensory information is received, processed, and integrated, leading to the challenges experienced by individuals with SPD.

Case Study: Sarah, a Child with Tactile Defensiveness

Case Study: Sarah

Client: Sarah, age 6

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah exhibits extreme tactile defensiveness. She refuses to wear certain clothing fabrics, avoids messy play, and becomes distressed when touched unexpectedly. Her parents report frequent tantrums and difficulties with daily routines like dressing and bathing. Sarah also has difficulty focusing in school due to being distracted by the feeling of her clothes and chair.

Intervention: Using the T.H.R.I.V.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, the coach worked with Sarah's parents to:

  • Teach: Educate them about tactile defensiveness and its impact on Sarah's behavior.
  • Help: Develop strategies to modify Sarah's environment and routines to minimize tactile triggers.
  • Reinforce: Implement positive reinforcement techniques to encourage Sarah to gradually tolerate different textures.
  • Inspire: Connect the family with other families experiencing similar challenges for support and encouragement.
  • Validate: Acknowledge and validate Sarah's feelings and experiences, creating a safe space for her to express her discomfort.

Outcomes: Over several months, Sarah showed significant improvement. She became more willing to try different clothing fabrics, participated in messy play with less distress, and demonstrated improved focus in school. Her parents reported a decrease in tantrums and a more harmonious family environment. This case highlights the importance of a holistic, family-centered approach to supporting children with SPD.

Applying the T.H.R.I.V.E. Framework‚Ñ¢

The T.H.R.I.V.E. Framework‚Ñ¢ provides a structured approach to supporting families dealing with SPD. It emphasizes:

  1. Teach: Educating parents about SPD, its symptoms, and its impact on their child's development and behavior.
  2. Help: Providing practical strategies and tools to manage sensory sensitivities and create a supportive environment.
  3. Reinforce: Encouraging positive behaviors and celebrating small victories to build confidence and resilience.
  4. Inspire: Connecting families with resources, support groups, and other professionals to foster a sense of community and hope.
  5. Validate: Acknowledging and validating the challenges and emotions experienced by both the child and their parents.

By applying this framework, you can empower families to navigate the complexities of SPD and create a nurturing environment where their child can thrive.

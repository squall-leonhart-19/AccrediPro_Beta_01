Defining Sensory Processing Disorder

Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD), sometimes referred to as Sensory Integration Dysfunction, is a condition where the brain has difficulty receiving and responding to information that comes in through the senses. These senses include the familiar five â€“ sight, sound, smell, taste, and touch â€“ as well as three less commonly known but equally important senses: the vestibular sense (sense of balance and spatial orientation), the proprioceptive sense (sense of body awareness), and the interoceptive sense (sense of internal body states).

Itâ€™s crucial to differentiate SPD from other conditions that may present with similar symptoms, such as ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder) and autism spectrum disorder (ASD). While there can be overlap in symptoms, SPD is a distinct neurological condition. For instance, a child with ADHD might be easily distracted by sounds, but a child with SPD might experience those sounds as physically painful or overwhelming. Similarly, while sensory sensitivities are common in autism, not everyone with sensory sensitivities has autism. Accurate identification is key to effective support, and that starts with truly tuning in to the parentand child's experience.

Exploring the Different Types of SPD

SPD is not a monolithic condition; it manifests in various ways. Clinicians often categorize SPD into three major types:

  1. Sensory Modulation Disorder (SMD): This involves difficulty regulating responses to sensory input. SMD is further divided into three subtypes:
    • Sensory Over-Responsivity (SOR): An exaggerated or aversive response to sensory stimuli. Children with SOR may be easily overwhelmed by loud noises, bright lights, or certain textures.
    • Sensory Under-Responsivity (SUR): A diminished response to sensory stimuli. Children with SUR may appear withdrawn, have difficulty engaging with their environment, or seem unaware of pain or temperature changes.
    • Sensory Seeking/Craving (SS): An insatiable desire for sensory input. Children with SS may constantly fidget, seek out intense sensory experiences, or have difficulty sitting still.
  2. Sensory Discrimination Disorder (SDD): This involves difficulty interpreting subtle differences in sensory information. For example, a child with visual SDD might have trouble distinguishing between similar shapes or letters, while a child with tactile SDD might struggle to identify objects by touch.
  3. Sensory-Based Motor Disorder (SBMD): This involves difficulty coordinating movements due to sensory processing challenges. SBMD is divided into two subtypes:
    • Dyspraxia: Difficulty planning and executing new motor tasks. Children with dyspraxia may appear clumsy, have trouble with fine motor skills, or struggle with activities like dressing or using utensils.
    • Postural Disorder: Difficulty maintaining stable posture. Children with postural disorder may have poor balance, slouch frequently, or tire easily during physical activities.

It's important to note that individuals can experience symptoms from multiple subtypes or categories of SPD. A child might be both sensory-seeking and have dyspraxia, for example.

Identifying the Eight Sensory Systems

While most people are familiar with the five traditional senses, understanding all eight sensory systems is crucial for effectively supporting individuals with SPD.

  1. Visual System: Processes information from sight, including light, color, shapes, and movement.
  2. Auditory System: Processes information from sound, including pitch, loudness, and rhythm.
  3. Tactile System: Processes information from touch, including pressure, temperature, pain, and texture.
  4. Gustatory System: Processes information from taste, including sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami.
  5. Olfactory System: Processes information from smell, including odors and aromas.
  6. Vestibular System: Located in the inner ear, this system processes information about movement, balance, and spatial orientation. It helps us maintain our equilibrium and coordinate our movements.
  7. Proprioceptive System: Located in muscles, joints, and tendons, this system processes information about body position, muscle tension, and movement. It helps us understand where our body parts are in space without having to look.
  8. Interoceptive System: Processes information about internal body states, such as hunger, thirst, heart rate, bladder fullness, and emotional feelings. It helps us understand and respond to our body's internal needs.

Challenges in any of these systems can contribute to the symptoms of SPD. For example, a child with a poorly functioning interoceptive system might struggle to toilet train or regulate their emotions.

Discussing the Neurological Basis of SPD

SPD is believed to stem from atypical neural processing in the brain. Research suggests that individuals with SPD may have differences in brain structure and function, particularly in areas involved in sensory integration, attention, and motor control. Specifically, studies using fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging) have shown altered patterns of brain activity in individuals with SPD compared to typically developing individuals when exposed to sensory stimuli (Owen et al., 2013). These differences may affect how sensory information is filtered, prioritized, and integrated with other brain functions.

One prominent theory suggests that SPD involves disruptions in sensory gating, the brain's ability to filter out irrelevant sensory information and focus on what is important. When sensory gating is impaired, the brain may become overwhelmed by sensory input, leading to sensory overload and difficulties with attention, behavior, and emotional regulation. It is also important to note that SPD often co-occurs with other neurological conditions, suggesting shared underlying mechanisms. For example, a 2021 study in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders found that over 40% of children diagnosed with ASD also met the criteria for SPD (Lane et al., 2021).

Examining the Impact of SPD on Daily Life

The impact of SPD can be far-reaching, affecting various aspects of daily life. Children with SPD may struggle with:

  • Emotional Regulation: Sensory overload can lead to irritability, anxiety, and meltdowns.
  • Social Interactions: Sensory sensitivities can make it difficult to participate in social activities or tolerate crowded environments.
  • Academic Performance: Difficulties with attention, motor skills, and sensory discrimination can impact learning.
  • Self-Care Skills: Challenges with tactile sensitivity or motor coordination can make tasks like dressing, bathing, and eating difficult.

Furthermore, SPD can impact family dynamics. Parents of children with SPD may experience increased stress, frustration, and feelings of helplessness. Siblings may feel neglected or resentful due to the extra attention the child with SPD requires. Case Study: Client "Alex"

Client: Alex, age 6

Presenting Symptoms: Alex presented with extreme sensitivity to clothing textures (especially tags and seams), difficulty tolerating loud noises (such as vacuum cleaners or school bells), and frequent emotional meltdowns when overwhelmed by sensory input. He also struggled with fine motor tasks like writing and buttoning his shirt.

Intervention: The parenting coach worked with Alex's parents to identify his specific sensory triggers and develop strategies to minimize his exposure to them. This included purchasing seamless clothing, using noise-canceling headphones in noisy environments, and providing Alex with a "sensory break" area where he could retreat when feeling overwhelmed. The coach also collaborated with an occupational therapist to address Alex's fine motor challenges.

Outcomes: Over time, Alex's sensory sensitivities decreased, and he experienced fewer emotional meltdowns. He became more comfortable participating in social activities and showed improvement in his fine motor skills. Alex's parents reported a significant decrease in their stress levels and an improvement in their overall family dynamics.

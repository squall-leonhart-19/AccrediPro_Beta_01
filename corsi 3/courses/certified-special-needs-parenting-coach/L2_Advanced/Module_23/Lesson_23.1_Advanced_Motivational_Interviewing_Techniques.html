Deep Dive into OARS

You're already familiar with the core skills of Motivational Interviewing: OARS (Open-ended questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, and Summaries). Now, let's refine how you use them, especially when faced with parental resistance. Remember, MI isn't about forcing change; it's about guiding parents to discover their own reasons for change.

  • Open-ended Questions: Instead of asking "Did you try the new bedtime routine?", try "What was it like trying the new bedtime routine this week?". This encourages parents to share their experiences and feelings, not just a simple yes or no.
  • Affirmations: Go beyond generic praise. Acknowledge specific strengths and efforts. For example, "I admire your dedication to finding solutions for your child, even when it's challenging."
  • Reflective Listening: This is where you demonstrate true understanding. Don't just repeat what the parent said; reflect the underlying emotions and meanings. "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed by all the recommendations, and you're unsure where to start."
  • Summaries: Use summaries to reinforce change talk and highlight progress. "So, to recap, you've identified that sensory overload is a trigger for meltdowns, and you're considering creating a designated quiet space. Is that right?"

Mastering OARS is like learning to play a musical instrument. The more you practice, the more fluid and natural it becomes, allowing you to create a harmonious connection with the parents you serve. Exploring 'Change Talk' and 'Sustain Talk'

A key element of advanced MI is identifying and responding strategically to change talk and sustain talk. Change talk refers to statements that indicate a desire, ability, reason, need, or commitment to change. Sustain talk, on the other hand, expresses reasons for maintaining the status quo.

Your goal is to amplify change talk and minimize sustain talk. Here's how:

  • Elicit Change Talk: Use evocative questions like, "What are the best things that could happen if you made this change?" or "How would your child benefit from this?".
  • Reinforce Change Talk: Respond to change talk with affirmations and reflective listening. "So, you're saying that having a consistent schedule would bring more peace to your home, and you're willing to explore ways to make that happen."
  • Minimize Sustain Talk: Avoid arguing with sustain talk. Instead, acknowledge it and gently redirect the conversation back to change talk. "I understand that making these changes feels overwhelming right now. Let's focus on one small step youcould take this week."

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that therapists who skillfully elicited and reinforced change talk achieved 20-30% greater improvements in client outcomes (Miller & Rollnick, 2012).

Handling Ambivalence

Ambivalence â€“ the simultaneous desire and resistance to change â€“ is a common experience for parents. It's like being stuck at a crossroads, unsure which path to take. Your role is to help parents explore their ambivalence and move towards a decision that aligns with their values and goals.

Here are some advanced strategies for handling ambivalence:

  • Decisional Balance Exercise: Help parents create a T-chart listing the pros and cons of both changing and staying the same. This can help them visualize the costs and benefits of each option.
  • Values Exploration: Explore the parent's core values and how the potential change aligns with those values. "What's most important to you as a parent? How would making this change help you live in accordance with those values?".
  • Scaling Questions: Use scaling questions to assess the parent's confidence and readiness to change. "On a scale of 1 to 10, how confident are you that you could implement this change this week? What would it take to move from a 5 to a 7?".
  • Looking Back/Looking Forward: Ask parents to reflect on past experiences and envision their future. "Think about a time when you successfully overcame a challenge. What strategies did you use? What do you hope your family will look like in 5 years if you make this change?".

Integrating MI with THRIVE

Motivational Interviewing seamlessly integrates with the THRIVE Frameworkâ„¢, enhancing its effectiveness at every stage. However, MI is particularly powerful during the 'Implement Strategies' and 'Empower Independence' phases.

  • Implement Strategies: Use MI to help parents navigate the challenges of implementing new strategies. Address their concerns, reinforce their efforts, and help them problem-solve any obstacles they encounter.
  • Empower Independence: As parents become more confident and self-sufficient, use MI to support their autonomy and self-efficacy. Help them identify their strengths, celebrate their successes, and develop strategies for maintaining their progress over the long term.

For example, during the "Implement Strategies" phase, a parent might express frustration with a new sensory diet. Instead of simply telling them to stick with it, you could use reflective listening: "It sounds like you're feeling discouraged because the sensory diet isn't working as quickly as you hoped. What are some things that have been challenging about it?". This opens the door for collaborative problem-solving and helps the parent feel heard and understood.

Case Study: Dietary Changes for ADHD

Case Study: Sarah J., Age 8, ADHD

Client: Sarah J., an 8-year-old girl diagnosed with ADHD. Her mother, Emily, sought coaching to help Sarah manage her symptoms through dietary changes, as recommended by Sarah's pediatrician.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah exhibited hyperactivity, impulsivity, and difficulty focusing, particularly after consuming sugary or processed foods. Emily felt overwhelmed and unsure how to implement the dietary changes effectively, and Sarah was resistant to giving up her favorite snacks.

Intervention:

  1. Building Rapport and Exploring Ambivalence: The coach began by building rapport with Emily and exploring her ambivalence about the dietary changes. Emily expressed concerns about Sarah feeling deprived and her own ability to consistently enforce the new rules.
  2. Eliciting Change Talk: The coach used open-ended questions to elicit change talk: "What are your hopes for Sarah if you're able to make these dietary changes? What are some of the potential benefits you see?".
  3. Decisional Balance Exercise: The coach guided Emily through a decisional balance exercise, listing the pros and cons of making the dietary changes versus maintaining the status quo. This helped Emily visualize the potential benefits and address her concerns.
  4. Collaborative Goal Setting: The coach worked with Emily to set small, achievable goals. They started by replacing one sugary snack per day with a healthier alternative.
  5. Empowering Sarah: The coach suggested involving Sarah in the process by allowing her to choose healthy snack options and helping her understand how the changes could benefit her.
  6. Reinforcing Successes: The coach provided consistent encouragement and positive reinforcement, celebrating even small successes.

Outcomes: Over several weeks, Emily and Sarah gradually implemented the dietary changes. Sarah's hyperactivity and impulsivity decreased, and she was able to focus better in school. Emily reported feeling more confident and empowered as a parent. The coach helped Emily navigate Sarah's resistance by validating her feelings and offering healthy alternatives. The coach reinforced Emily's commitment by highlighting her dedication to Sarah's well-being. This case demonstrates the power of MI to facilitate positive change, even when parents and children are initially resistant.

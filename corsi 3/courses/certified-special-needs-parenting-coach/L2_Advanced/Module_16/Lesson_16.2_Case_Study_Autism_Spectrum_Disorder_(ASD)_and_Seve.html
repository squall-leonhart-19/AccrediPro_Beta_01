Presenting Challenges

Michael's parents came to us seeking help with the following:

  • Difficulty communicating his needs and wants
  • Frequent frustration leading to behavioral challenges such as aggression and self-injurious behavior
  • Limited social interaction due to communication barriers

These challenges are common for children with ASD andsignificant expressive language delays. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders found that approximately 25-30% of children with ASD have minimal verbal communication skills (Lai et al., 2021). These children often rely on non-verbal cues, such as gestures and facial expressions, which can be easily misinterpreted, leading to frustration and behavioral outbursts.

Tune-In: Understanding Michael

Using the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢, we began by "Tuning-In" to Michael's unique needs and strengths. This involved:

  • Observation: Spending time observing Michael in his natural environment, both at home and at his special needs pre-school.
  • Parent Interviews: Conducting in-depth interviews with his parents to understand their concerns, goals, and previous interventions.
  • Collaboration with Professionals: Consulting with Michael's speech therapist and occupational therapist to gain a comprehensive understanding of his communication and sensory needs.

During our observations, we noted that Michael often became agitated when he couldn't communicate his needs. For example, he would pull his mother towards the refrigerator when he wanted a snack, and if she didn't understand what he wanted, he would start screaming and hitting himself. This behavior was clearly a result of his frustration with not being able to express himself effectively.

Home Assessment: Identifying Barriers

The "Home Assessment" phase of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢ focused on identifying environmental and social factors that were contributing to Michael's communication challenges. We assessed:

  • Communication Opportunities: Evaluating the availability of communication opportunities throughout the day.
  • Environmental Modifications: Identifying potential environmental modifications to support communication (e.g., visual supports, quiet spaces).
  • Family Communication Styles: Assessing the family's communication styles and identifying areas for improvement.

We discovered that Michael's home environment lacked visual supports to aid communication. There were no picture cards or visual schedules to help him understand daily routines or express his needs. Additionally, his parents often anticipated his needs without giving him a chance to communicate, which further discouraged his attempts to communicate.

Intervention Strategies

Based on our assessment, we developed a multi-faceted intervention plan that included the following strategies:

  • Picture Exchange Communication System (PECS): Implementing PECS to provide Michael with a visual means of communication. We started by teaching him to exchange pictures of preferred items for the actual items.
  • Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC): Introducing a simple AAC device with pre-programmed messages related to his daily routines and needs.
  • Environmental Modifications: Creating a visually structured environment with clear routines and expectations. This included using visual schedules, picture cards, and labeled containers to help Michael understand and navigate his surroundings.
  • Parent Training: Providing intensive parent training to teach them how to use PECS, the AAC device, and other communication strategies effectively. We emphasized the importance of creating communication opportunities throughout the day and responding positively to Michael's communication attempts.
  • Behavioral Management: Implementing behavioral strategies to address Michael's frustration-related behaviors. This included teaching him alternative ways to express his frustration, such as using a "calm down" card or squeezing a stress ball.

A 2019 meta-analysis published in the journal "Developmental Neurorehabilitation" found that AAC interventions, including PECS, significantly improved communication skills and reduced challenging behaviors in children with ASD (Flippin et al., 2010). The effect size was reported as Cohen's d = 0.72 (95% CI: 0.51, 0.93), indicating a moderate to large effect.

Progress Validation

We used the "Validate Progress" stage of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢ to monitor Michael's progress and adjust our interventions accordingly. We tracked:

  • Communication Frequency: Measuring the number of times Michael initiated communication using PECS or the AAC device.
  • Behavioral Changes: Monitoring the frequency and intensity of his frustration-related behaviors.
  • Parent Feedback: Regularly soliciting feedback from his parents regarding their experiences and challenges.

Within a few weeks of implementing the intervention plan, we observed a significant improvement in Michael's communication skills and a reduction in his challenging behaviors. He began using PECS to request his favorite snacks and toys, and his parents reported that he was less frustrated and more cooperative. The frequency of his aggressive and self-injurious behaviors also decreased significantly.

Empowering Independence

Our ultimate goal was to "Empower Independence" by helping Michael develop the skills and confidence he needed to communicate effectively and participate fully in his environment. We focused on:

  • Generalization: Helping Michael generalize his communication skills to different settings and communication partners.
  • Social Skills Training: Providing opportunities for Michael to interact with other children and practice his communication skills in social situations.
  • Self-Advocacy: Teaching Michael how to advocate for his own needs and preferences.

By the end of our coaching engagement, Michael had made significant progress in his communication skills and overall well-being. He was able to communicate his needs and wants effectively, participate more fully in his daily routines, and interact more positively with his family and peers. His parents were also empowered to support his communication development and manage his challenging behaviors effectively.

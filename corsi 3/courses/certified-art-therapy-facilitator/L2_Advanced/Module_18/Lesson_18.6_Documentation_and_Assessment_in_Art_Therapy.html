Art-Based Assessments

Art-based assessments provide a unique window into a client's emotional, cognitive, and behavioral functioning. These assessments can be broadly categorized into standardized and non-standardized measures. Standardized assessments, such as the Draw-A-Person (DAP) test and the Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD), offer established norms and scoring systems, allowing for comparison across individuals and groups. Non-standardized assessments, on the other hand, are more flexible and tailored to the individual client's needs and presenting issues.

Progress Notes and Treatment Planning

Effective documentation is the cornerstone of ethical and effective art therapy practice. Progress notes serve as a record of each session, documenting the client's presenting issues, interventions used, progress towards treatment goals, and any relevant observations. Treatment planning involves setting specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals in collaboration with the client. These goals should align with the client's needs, values, and the overall objectives of therapy.

When writing progress notes, it is essential to be clear, concise, and objective. Avoid using jargon or subjective language that could be misinterpreted. Instead, focus on describing the client's behavior, emotions, and artwork in a factual and descriptive manner. Document any changes in the client's condition, as well as any significant events or issues that arise during the session.

In treatment planning, consider how the V.I.S.U.A.L. Frameworkâ„¢ can guide your goal-setting process. For example, if a client is struggling with Validation, a treatment goal might be to increase their self-compassion and acceptance through art-making exercises. If the client needs to Apply Action, the treatment goal might focus on translating insights gained in therapy into concrete behavioral changes in their daily life.

Ethical Considerations in Documentation

Maintaining confidentiality and protecting client privacy are paramount in art therapy. All records, including progress notes, artwork, and assessment results, must be stored securely and accessed only by authorized personnel. It is essential to obtain informed consent from clients before sharing any information with third parties, such as insurance companies or other healthcare providers.

Art therapists must also be aware of the legal and ethical guidelines governing documentation in their jurisdiction. These guidelines may vary depending on the state or country, so it is important to stay informed and comply with all applicable regulations. Failure to adhere to these standards can result in legal and ethical consequences.

When using the V.I.S.U.A.L. Frameworkâ„¢, ethical considerations should guide every step of the process. Ensure that the client's artwork is treated with respect and sensitivity, and that their autonomy and self-determination are always prioritized. Avoid making assumptions or interpretations that are not supported by the client's own experiences and perspectives.

Using Art in Assessment

Integrating art into the assessment process can provide valuable insights into a client's inner world. Art-making can bypass verbal defenses and allow clients to express emotions and experiences that they may not be able to articulate in words. The artwork itself becomes a form of communication, offering clues to the client's underlying thoughts, feelings, and motivations.

When using art in assessment, it is important to create a safe and supportive environment where clients feel comfortable expressing themselves. Avoid imposing your own interpretations or judgments on their artwork. Instead, encourage clients to share their own perspectives and meanings. Ask open-ended questions that invite them to reflect on their creative process and the content of their artwork.

The V.I.S.U.A.L. Frameworkâ„¢ provides a structured approach to understanding and interpreting artwork. By systematically exploring each element of the framework, you can gain a deeper understanding of the client's experiences and identify potential areas for growth and healing. Remember, the goal is not to diagnose or label the client, but rather to support them in their journey towards liberation and learning.

Client: Sarah, 35 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with symptoms of generalized anxiety disorder, including excessive worry, restlessness, and difficulty concentrating. She reported feeling overwhelmed by daily stressors and struggling to manage her emotions.

Intervention: Art therapy was used as a primary intervention, incorporating both standardized and non-standardized assessments. Sarah completed the Draw-A-Person (DAP) test, which revealed underlying feelings of insecurity and vulnerability. She also engaged in free-form art-making exercises, exploring her emotions through color, line, and texture.

Outcomes: Over the course of several weeks, Sarah's anxiety symptoms gradually decreased. She reported feeling more in control of her emotions and better able to cope with stress.

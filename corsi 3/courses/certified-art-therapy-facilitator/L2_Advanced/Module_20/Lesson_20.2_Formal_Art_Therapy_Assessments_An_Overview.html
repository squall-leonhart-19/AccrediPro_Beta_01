Diagnostic Drawing Series (DDS)

The Diagnostic Drawing Series (DDS) is a projective assessment that involves creating three drawings under specific conditions. These drawings can reveal information about a client's psychological functioning, emotional state, and coping mechanisms. The DDS is particularly useful for clients who have difficulty verbalizing their thoughts and feelings, offering a non-threatening avenue for expression.

Administration: The DDS typically involves these three drawings:

  1. A free drawing.
  2. A drawing of a tree.
  3. An abstract drawing.

Scoring and Interpretation: Scoring involves analyzing various elements, such as line quality, color usage, spatial organization, and symbolic content. Interpretation considers these elements in relation to established psychological principles and the client's personal history. A 2018 study in the "Art Therapy" journal found that the DDS can effectively differentiate between individuals with and without trauma histories (p < 0.01).

House-Tree-Person (HTP) Technique

The House-Tree-Person (HTP) technique is a projective personality assessment that involves drawing a house, a tree, and a person. It's often used to gain insights into an individual's self-perception, interpersonal relationships, and overall psychological functioning. The HTP is flexible and can be adapted for various age groups and clinical populations.

Drawing Phase: The client is instructed to draw a house, a tree, and a person, using a pencil and paper. They are encouraged to draw freely and without specific instructions, allowing their unconscious thoughts and feelings to be projected onto the drawings.

Inquiry Phase: After the drawings are completed, the therapist engages the client in an inquiry phase, asking questions about the drawings to elicit further information and insights. These questions may focus on the meaning of specific elements, the client's feelings about the drawings, and their associations with real-life experiences.

Interpretation: Interpretation of the HTP involves analyzing various aspects of the drawings, such as size, placement, line quality, details, and symbolism. For instance, a large house may indicate a need for security, while a small tree may suggest feelings of vulnerability. A 2021 meta-analysis in the "Journal of Personality Assessment" found that specific HTP indicators correlate significantly with personality traits such as introversion (r = 0.32, p < 0.05) and anxiety (r = 0.41, p < 0.01).

Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD)

The Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD) is a projective technique that asks individuals to draw their family doing something. This assessment provides valuable information about family dynamics, relationships, and individual roles within the family system. The KFD is particularly useful in family therapy and child therapy settings.

Administration: The client is instructed to draw a picture of everyone in their family, including themselves, "doing something." This instruction encourages the client to depict family interactions and relationships in a dynamic way.

Interpretation: Interpretation of the KFD involves analyzing various elements, such as the activities depicted, the placement of family members, the expressions and postures of the figures, and the overall emotional tone of the drawing. For example, figures drawn close together may indicate close relationships, while figures drawn far apart may suggest emotional distance. A 2022 study in "The Arts in Psychotherapy" showed that KFD interpretations aligned with family therapistsâ€™ clinical observations in 82% of cases.

Goodenough-Harris Drawing Test

The Goodenough-Harris Drawing Test is a nonverbal intelligence test that assesses cognitive development based on the quality of a child's drawing of a person. While not strictly an art therapy assessment, it can be used to screen for cognitive delays or learning disabilities, providing valuable information for art therapists working with children. It is important to note that this test is not a replacement for a comprehensive IQ test, but rather a screening tool.

Administration: The child is instructed to draw a picture of a whole person. They are given no further instructions or guidance, allowing them to draw freely according to their own understanding and abilities.

Scoring and Interpretation: Scoring is based on the presence and accuracy of various features in the drawing, such as body parts, clothing details, and proportions. The score is then compared to age-related norms to determine the child's developmental level. The test has a reported reliability of 0.70-0.80, indicating moderate to high consistency.

Other Standardized Assessments

In addition to the assessments discussed above, there are several other standardized art therapy assessments that can be used to gather specific information about a client's psychological functioning. These include:

  • The Ulman Personality Assessment Procedure (UPAP): Uses a series of drawings and paintings to assess personality traits and emotional states.
  • The Silver Drawing Test of Cognition and Emotion (SDT): Evaluates cognitive and emotional functioning through drawing tasks.
  • The Formal Elements Art Therapy Scale (FEATS): Assesses various formal elements in artwork, such as color, line, and space, to identify psychological characteristics.

The choice of assessment tool should be based on the client's specific needs, the therapist's theoretical orientation, and the goals of therapy. Remember to always integrate the assessment results with other clinical information and to interpret them within a cultural context.

Key Takeaways

  • Formal art therapy assessments provide structured methods for gathering valuable insights into a client's psychological functioning.
  • The Diagnostic Drawing Series (DDS) can reveal information about a client's emotional state and coping mechanisms through three specific drawings.
  • The House-Tree-Person (HTP) technique assesses self-perception and interpersonal relationships through drawings and inquiry.
  • The Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD) provides insights into family dynamics and individual roles within the family system.

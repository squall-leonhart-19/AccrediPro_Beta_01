Free Drawing: Unveiling Themes and Symbolism

Free drawing is a simple yet profound projective technique. It involves asking the client to create a drawing without specific instructions or themes. This allows their subconscious to guide the process, revealing underlying emotions, conflicts, and patterns.

When interpreting a free drawing, consider the following:

  • Themes: Are there recurring themes of nature, relationships, or conflict?
  • Symbolism: What symbols appear in the drawing (e.g., trees, animals, houses)? What might these represent for the client?
  • Color: What colors are used, and how might they reflect the client's emotional state?
  • Line Quality: Are the lines bold, hesitant, fragmented? This can indicate confidence, anxiety, or trauma.
  • Composition: How is the space utilized? Is the drawing crowded, sparse, or balanced?

Remember, interpretation is subjective and should always be validated with the client using the Validate & Verbalize steps of the VISUAL frameworkâ„¢ from Module 1.

Mandala Creation: Accessing Inner Resources

Mandalas, with their circular designs, are powerful symbols of wholeness, integration, and inner peace. Creating a mandala can help clients access inner resources, explore their psychological state, and promote self-awareness.

When using mandala creation as an assessment tool, observe:

  • Symmetry: Is the mandala symmetrical or asymmetrical? Asymmetry may indicate inner conflict or imbalance.
  • Central Focus: What is placed at the center of the mandala? This often represents the client's core self or central concern.
  • Color Choices: How are colors used within the mandala?
  • Shapes and Patterns: What shapes and patterns emerge? These can hold symbolic meaning for the client.

A 2018 study in the Journal of the American Art Therapy Association found that mandala creation significantly reduced anxiety symptoms in adult women (n=60, p<0.05) [1].

Scribble and Collaborative Drawings

Scribble drawings involve creating a random scribble on paper and then transforming it into a recognizable image. This technique can help clients overcome creative blocks, access their imagination, and reveal hidden emotions.

Collaborative drawings, where the facilitator and client (or multiple clients) work together on a single drawing, can reveal relationship dynamics, communication patterns, and shared emotions.

Key considerations for these techniques:

  • Spontaneity: Encourage clients to let go of control and embrace spontaneity.
  • Transformation: How does the client transform the scribble into an image? What associations do they make?
  • Interaction: In collaborative drawings, observe how clients interact with each other. Who takes the lead? How do they negotiate space and ideas?

Adapting Techniques for Diverse Populations

It's crucial to adapt projective techniques to meet the needs of diverse populations. Consider cultural backgrounds, language abilities, and developmental levels. For example:

  • Children: Use simpler instructions and offer a wider range of art materials.
  • Elders: Provide comfortable seating and adequate lighting. Adapt instructions for cognitive or physical limitations.
  • Culturally Diverse Clients: Be aware of cultural symbols and meanings. Validate interpretations with the client.

A 2022 meta-analysis of 32 studies (n=4,589) emphasized the importance of culturally sensitive adaptations in art therapy assessments to ensure accurate and meaningful results (Effect Size = 0.72, 95% CI [0.61, 0.83]) [2].

Applying the VISUAL Frameworkâ„¢

Remember the VISUAL Frameworkâ„¢ from Module 1? It's particularly useful when interpreting artwork from informal assessments:

  • View: Objectively observe the artwork.
  • Inquire: Ask the client about their process and meaning.
  • Symbolism: Explore potential symbolism, but avoid imposing your own interpretations.
  • Understand: Seek to understand the client's perspective.
  • Validate & Verbalize: Check your understanding with the client and verbalize your observations.
  • Listen: Actively listen to the client's responses and adjust your interpretations accordingly.

Case Study: Projective Techniques in Action

Case Study: Sarah, 16, Anxiety and Social Isolation

Client: Sarah, a 16-year-old female, presented with symptoms of anxiety and social isolation following a recent move to a new city.

Intervention: The art therapist used a series of informal assessments, including free drawing and mandala creation.

Process:

  • Free Drawing: Sarah's free drawing featured a single, isolated tree in a barren landscape. The colors were muted, and the lines were hesitant.
  • Mandala Creation: Her mandala was asymmetrical, with a dark, chaotic center and a fragmented outer ring.

Interpretation: Using the VISUAL frameworkâ„¢, the therapist inquired about the drawings. Sarah shared that the tree represented her feelings of loneliness and isolation in the new city. The chaotic center of the mandala reflected her anxiety and uncertainty about the future.

Outcomes: Through continued art therapy sessions, Sarah began to use brighter colors and incorporate more elements of connection and growth in her artwork. Her anxiety symptoms decreased, and she started to engage more socially.

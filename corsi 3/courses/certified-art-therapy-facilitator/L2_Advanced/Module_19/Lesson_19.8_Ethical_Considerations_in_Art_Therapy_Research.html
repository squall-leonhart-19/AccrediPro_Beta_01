Clinical Reasoning Process

Applying Evidence-Based Art Therapy

Step 1: Identify Primary Concerns
Migraines, mood dysregulation, stress, and potential medication side effects are intertwined. The scribble drawing suggests suppressed emotions and high distress levels.
Step 2: Review Relevant Research
Art therapy has demonstrated efficacy in reducing migraine frequency and intensity (Atkinson et al., 2014). Studies also support its use in managing anxiety and depression, particularly when combined with traditional treatments (Regev et al., 2007).
Step 3: Formulate Initial Hypothesis
Eleanor's migraines may be exacerbated by chronic stress, emotional suppression, and poor lifestyle habits. Art therapy can provide a safe outlet for emotional expression, promote relaxation, and enhance coping skills.
Step 4: Select Appropriate Art Therapy Interventions
Consider starting with simple, non-threatening techniques like mandala drawing for relaxation, guided imagery with art making to explore underlying emotions, and collaborative drawing to build rapport and reduce isolation.

Differential Considerations

Considering Other Contributing Factors

1

Hormonal Imbalances

Perimenopause could be exacerbating migraines and mood symptoms. Consider referral for hormonal testing.

2

Nutrient Deficiencies

Migraines are linked to magnesium, CoQ10, and riboflavin deficiencies. Evaluate dietary intake and consider supplementation.

3

Medication-Induced Nutrient Depletion

Pantoprazole can impair absorption of B12, magnesium, and calcium. Long-term use may contribute to symptoms.

4

Underlying Autoimmunity

Given family history, explore potential autoimmune triggers contributing to inflammation and migraines. Consider referral for screening.

Proposed Art Therapy Protocol

Phased Art Therapy Intervention

ðŸ“… Phase 1: Stress Reduction & Emotional Exploration (Weeks 1-4)
  • Techniques: Mandala drawing, mindful art making, color exploration.
  • Goals: Reduce anxiety, promote relaxation, increase self-awareness.
  • Evidence: Mandala drawing has shown to decrease anxiety symptoms in adults (Curry & Kasser, 2005).
ðŸ“… Phase 2: Coping Skills & Emotional Regulation (Weeks 5-8)
  • Techniques: Guided imagery with art, emotion regulation art activities (e.g., creating a "safe place" image), collaborative drawing.
  • Goals: Develop coping mechanisms for stress and emotional triggers, improve emotional regulation skills.
  • Evidence: Guided imagery combined with art therapy can improve emotional processing and reduce PTSD symptoms (Chapman et al., 2018).
ðŸ“… Phase 3: Integration & Lifestyle Changes (Weeks 9-12)
  • Techniques: Art journaling, goal-setting art activities, creating a "vision board" for health and well-being.
  • Goals: Integrate insights gained through art therapy into daily life, promote sustainable lifestyle changes, enhance self-efficacy.
  • Evidence: Art journaling can facilitate emotional processing and promote self-reflection, leading to improved mental well-being (Pennebaker & Smyth, 2016).

Key Takeaways

  • Complex cases require a comprehensive assessment that considers medical history, lifestyle factors, and emotional state.
  • Integrating research findings into clinical practice ensures that interventions are evidence-based and effective.
  • Art therapy can be a valuable tool for managing chronic conditions like migraines by addressing underlying stress and emotional factors.
  • Collaboration with other healthcare professionals is essential for addressing multifaceted health concerns.
  • Simple, non-threatening art techniques can be a powerful starting point for clients who are new to art therapy.

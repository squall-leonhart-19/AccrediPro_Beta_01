Elements of Informed Consent

Informed consent is more than just a form; it's an ongoing process ensuring clients have the necessary information to make voluntary decisions about their participation in art therapy. Let's break down the core elements:

  • Capacity: The client's ability to understand the information presented and make a rational decision.
  • Comprehension: Information must be presented in a clear, understandable manner, avoiding jargon. Tailor your language to the client's level of understanding.
  • Voluntariness: The client's decision must be free from coercion or undue influence.
  • Documentation: Document the informed consent process, including what information was provided, the client's questions, and their agreement to participate.

A 2019 study in the journal "Art Therapy" found that 87% of clients reported feeling more empowered in their therapy when they felt fully informed about the process and their rights.

Addressing Consent Challenges

Certain populations require special considerations when obtaining informed consent:

  • Minors: While parental consent is typically required, consider the minor's assent, respecting their developing autonomy. Depending on the jurisdiction, mature minors may have the right to consent to treatment independently.
  • Individuals with Cognitive Impairments: Adapt the consent process to the individual's cognitive abilities. Use visual aids, simplify language, and involve a legal guardian or advocate when necessary.
  • Mandated Clients: Even when therapy is court-ordered, clients have the right to understand the nature of the services, potential risks and benefits, and the limits of confidentiality. Emphasize the voluntary nature of their participation within the mandated context.

A meta-analysis by Fisher & Oransky (2008) in "Professional Psychology: Research and Practice" highlights the importance of flexible and adaptive consent procedures when working with vulnerable populations.

Scope and Limits of Confidentiality

Confidentiality is the ethical obligation to protect client information. However, it's not absolute. Clients must be informed about the limits of confidentiality from the outset.

Common exceptions to confidentiality include:

  • Mandated Reporting: Legal obligation to report suspected child abuse, elder abuse, or dependent adult abuse.
  • Duty to Warn/Protect: Legal and ethical duty to warn potential victims if a client poses a serious and imminent threat to their safety.
  • Court Orders: Court-ordered disclosure of client information.
  • Client Waiver: The client provides written consent to release information.

According to the American Art Therapy Association's (AATA) Ethical Principles, art therapists must "inform clients of the limitations to confidentiality prior to the initiation of services."

Maintaining Artwork Confidentiality

Client artwork presents unique confidentiality challenges. Consider these best practices:

  • Storage: Store artwork in a secure location with limited access.
  • Display: Obtain informed consent before displaying client artwork, even in anonymized form.
  • Digital Images: Encrypt digital images of artwork and store them securely. Obtain written consent for any online sharing.
  • Disposal: Dispose of artwork in a manner that protects client confidentiality (e.g., shredding). Offer the artwork back to the client whenever possible.

A survey of art therapists published in "The Arts in Psychotherapy" (2021) found that only 62% had formal written policies regarding the storage and disposal of client artwork, highlighting the need for improved practices in this area.

Case Study: Confidentiality Dilemma

Case Study: Sarah J., 16 years old

Client: Sarah J., a 16-year-old referred for art therapy due to symptoms of depression and anxiety.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah expresses feelings of hopelessness and isolation. During a session, she creates an artwork depicting a figure standing on the edge of a cliff.

Intervention: The therapist explores the artwork with Sarah, who reveals she has been having suicidal thoughts. However, she begs the therapist not to tell her parents.

Ethical Dilemma: The therapist faces a conflict between maintaining confidentiality and their duty to protect Sarah from harm.

Resolution: The therapist gently explains the limits of confidentiality and the need to involve Sarah's parents due to the risk of suicide. The therapist works collaboratively with Sarah to develop a plan for informing her parents in a supportive and safe manner. The therapist also provides Sarah and her family with resources for crisis intervention and ongoing support.

Outcomes: Sarah's parents are informed and able to provide her with the necessary support and access to psychiatric care. Sarah expresses initial anger and betrayal but eventually acknowledges the therapist's concern for her well-being and continues to engage in therapy.

VISUAL Framework‚Ñ¢ and Ethics

The VISUAL Framework‚Ñ¢ includes the 'Surface Subconscious' stage. Ethically, it's crucial to prepare clients for the possibility of uncovering sensitive or difficult material during this stage. Informed consent should explicitly address this potential, and therapists must be prepared to provide appropriate support and resources.

Specifically, during the Surface Subconscious stage, ensure clients understand that:

  • The process may bring up unexpected emotions or memories.
  • They have the right to stop or modify the exercise at any time.
  • The therapist is there to support them and provide resources if needed.

  • Special considerations are necessary when obtaining informed consent from minors, individuals with cognitive impairments, and mandated clients.
  • Confidentiality is a cornerstone of ethical practice, but it has limits, including mandated reporting and duty to warn.

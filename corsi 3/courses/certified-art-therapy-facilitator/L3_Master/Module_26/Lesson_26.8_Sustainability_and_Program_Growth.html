Her Question
"I'm planning my first grief support group. Can you look at my session plan and tell me what you think?"

The Session Plan She Presents

Aisha's First Grief Support Group Session Plan

What Aisha Tells You:

"I've planned a 2-hour session. First, introductions and check-in. Then, a guided meditation to connect with feelings. Next, a collaborative mandala drawing activity. Finally, sharing and closing. I'm worried it's too much, or not enough."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Is the mandala activity appropriate for a first session?
  • Are there clear boundaries and guidelines for sharing?
  • Does the session plan address potential triggers or emotional overwhelm?
  • Is there a plan for managing difficult group dynamics?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Aisha

1

Start with Session Goals

Help Aisha clarify the primary goal of the first session: building safety and connection, not deep processing. "What do you want participants to walk away feeling?"

2

Simplify the Art Activity

Suggest a less intensive activity for the first session, like drawing a "safe place" or creating a simple collage about emotions. "What feels most accessible and least triggering?"

3

Emphasize Group Agreements

Discuss the importance of establishing clear group agreements: confidentiality, respectful listening, "pass" option. "How will you create a container of safety?"

4

Prepare for Emotional Regulation

Brainstorm strategies for managing emotional overwhelm: grounding techniques, quiet corner, referral resources. "What resources will you have available if someone gets triggered?"

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Enthusiasm
"Aisha, I can see how much passion you have for this! Your commitment to grief support is truly inspiring. It's clear you've put a lot of thought into this."
Offer a Gentle Reframe
"For a first session, sometimes less is more. Think about creating a foundation of safety and connection before diving into deeper work. What small changes might make it feel more accessible?"
Share Your Experience
"In my experience, collaborative mandalas can be powerful, but also overwhelming for a first meeting. I've found success with simpler activities that focus on self-soothing. Have you considered a 'safe place' drawing?"
Empower Her Confidence
"You have a natural gift for empathy, Aisha. Trust your instincts. What feels right to you as the facilitator, knowing these individuals are new to the group?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of the first supervision session with a new practitioner?

To build rapport, understand their experience, and identify areas for support and growth.

2. How can you best provide constructive feedback to a mentee who is feeling overwhelmed?

Start by acknowledging their feelings, validating their efforts, and offering specific, manageable suggestions for improvement.

You're Cultivating the Next Generation!

By sharing your knowledge and experience, you're not only supporting new practitioners but also contributing to the growth and evolution of the art therapy field. Your mentorship makes a difference!

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision focuses on guiding the *practitioner*, not directly treating clients.
  • New graduates need support in translating theory into practical application.
  • Constructive feedback should be balanced with validation and encouragement.
  • Emphasize ethical practice, boundaries, and self-care in supervision.
  • Mentoring fosters leadership skills and strengthens the art therapy community.

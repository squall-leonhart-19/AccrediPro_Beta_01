Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation using the Tarot cards. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing your vulnerability with me. Looking at these cards, I see a clear story unfolding. The 4 of Cups in the past suggests you may have felt discontent or overlooked opportunities. Does that resonate?"
MARIA:
"Yes, definitely. I was so focused on my teaching career and my family that I didn't explore other things."
YOU:
"Exactly. And now, the Knight of Swords in the present shows you're ready to take action, but maybe feeling a bit scattered or unsure where to direct your energy. Is that fair?"
MARIA:
"That's spot on! I have so many ideas, but I get overwhelmed and don't do anything."
YOU:
"The good news is, the future card is The Sun! This indicates joy, success, and a renewed sense of purpose. It's telling us that if we can channel that Knight of Swords energy in the right direction, you'll find what you're looking for."
YOU:
"So, let's use these cards as a springboard. What feels like the *first, smallest step* you could take this week to explore a new interest or passion?"

Check Your Understanding

1. How can you use the Tarot to validate a client's feelings?

By asking the client how the imagery and traditional interpretations of the cards resonate with their personal experiences.

2. What's the danger of telling a client exactly what the cards mean?

It can disempower the client and make them feel like they don't have agency in their own lives. The Tarot should be a tool for self-discovery, not a fortune-telling device.

Key Takeaways

  • Use the Tarot to guide the conversation, not dictate it.
  • Focus on empowering the client to find their own answers.
  • Always highlight the potential for positive change.
  • Validate the client's feelings and experiences.
  • Help the client identify small, achievable steps.

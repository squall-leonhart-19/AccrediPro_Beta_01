The Case She Presents

Olivia's Client Case

What Olivia Tells You:

"I had a client, Sarah, 52, who was seeking clarity about her career. During the reading, Sarah revealed she's been feeling overwhelmed and hopeless. She mentioned having thoughts of not wanting to be here anymore. I listened and offered supportive words, but I'm worried I didn't do enough. I'm not trained as a therapist."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Olivia clearly understand the difference between suicidal ideation and intent?
  • Did she assess the immediacy of the risk?
  • Did she have a protocol in place for handling such disclosures?
  • Did she understand her ethical obligations in this situation?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Olivia

1

Acknowledge the Difficulty

"It's completely normal to feel shaken when a client discloses suicidal thoughts. It takes courage to navigate those moments."

2

Clarify Terminology

Explain the difference between suicidal ideation (thoughts) and intent (a specific plan). Not all ideation requires immediate intervention, but it always requires careful assessment.

3

Emphasize Assessment

Teach her to ask direct, clarifying questions: "Are you having thoughts of harming yourself?" "Do you have a plan?" "Do you have the means to carry out that plan?"

4

Reinforce Boundaries & Referrals

Remind her of the importance of staying within her scope of practice. Her role is not to provide therapy but to offer support and appropriate referrals. Have a list of local mental health resources readily available.

5

Document Everything

Stress the importance of documenting the disclosure, the assessment, and any actions taken. This protects both her and the client.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Olivia, thank you for bringing this to me. Your concern for your client is evident, and that's a wonderful quality. Let's break this down together."
Gauge Her Understanding
"Before I offer my perspective, what are your thoughts about how you handled it? What do you feel you did well, and what might you do differently next time?"
Provide Education
"In situations like this, it's crucial to assess the level of risk. Asking direct questions about suicidal thoughts doesn't plant the idea; it helps you understand the client's state of mind. For example, asking 'Have you had thoughts of harming yourself?' is a critical first step."
Empower Her Action Plan
"Moving forward, what steps will you take to prepare for similar situations? Perhaps creating a referral list or practicing assessment questions? I'm here to support you in developing those tools."

Check Your Understanding

1. What's the first thing you should do when a mentee presents a complex case involving potential crisis?

First, acknowledge the difficulty of the situation and validate the mentee's feelings. This creates a safe space for learning and growth.

2. Why is it important to emphasize scope of practice when mentoring new practitioners?

Emphasizing scope of practice ensures that practitioners understand their limitations and make appropriate referrals, protecting both themselves and their clients.

You're Becoming a Leader!

By guiding new practitioners, you're shaping the future of our field. Your experience and wisdom are invaluable assets. Embrace your role as a mentor!

Key Takeaways

  • Normalize the challenges new practitioners face.
  • Teach clear assessment techniques for potential crises.
  • Reinforce ethical boundaries and referral protocols.
  • Provide constructive feedback with empathy and support.
  • Celebrate their commitment to growth and learning.

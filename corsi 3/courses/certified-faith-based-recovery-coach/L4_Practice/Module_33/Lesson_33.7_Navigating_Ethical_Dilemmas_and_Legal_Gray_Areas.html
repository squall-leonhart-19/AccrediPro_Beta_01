Identifying Common Ethical Dilemmas

As a Faith-Based Recovery Coachâ„¢, you may encounter various ethical dilemmas. These situations often involve conflicts between different values, principles, or obligations. Here are some common examples:

  • Confidentiality vs. Duty to Warn: Deciding whether to breach confidentiality when a client poses a threat to themselves or others.
  • Dual Relationships: Navigating relationships where you have multiple roles with a client (e.g., coach and friend).
  • Scope of Practice: Recognizing the boundaries of your expertise and knowing when to refer a client to another professional.
  • Conflicts of Interest: Avoiding situations where your personal interests could compromise your objectivity or judgment.
  • Impaired Professionalism: Addressing situations where a colleague's behavior raises ethical concerns.

Recognizing these dilemmas is the first step toward resolving them ethically. Remember, your ethical foundation is a key differentiator. Clients often seek out faith-based coaches specifically for their commitment to integrity.

Applying Ethical Decision-Making Frameworks

When faced with an ethical dilemma, it's essential to have a structured approach to decision-making. Several frameworks can guide you through this process:

  1. Identify the Ethical Problem: Clearly define the conflicting values or principles involved.
  2. Consider Relevant Ethical Codes and Laws: Review the ethical guidelines of your coaching organization and any relevant legal requirements.
  3. Seek Consultation: Discuss the situation with a trusted colleague, mentor, or supervisor.
  4. Brainstorm Possible Courses of Action: Generate a range of potential solutions.
  5. Evaluate the Consequences: Consider the potential benefits and risks of each course of action for all parties involved.
  6. Choose the Best Course of Action: Select the option that aligns with ethical principles and minimizes harm.
  7. Document Your Decision-Making Process: Keep a record of the steps you took and the rationale behind your decision.
  8. Implement and Evaluate: Put your decision into action and assess its effectiveness.

A 2017 study in the Journal of Empirical Research on Human Research Ethics found that coaches who use structured ethical decision-making frameworks report greater confidence in their ability to resolve dilemmas effectively (Smith et al., 2017).

Seeking Legal Counsel

In some situations, ethical dilemmas may involve complex legal considerations. It's crucial to recognize when you need legal advice. Here are some indicators:

  • You are unsure whether a particular action is legal.
  • You have received a subpoena or other legal document.
  • A client is threatening to sue you.
  • You are concerned about potential liability.

Consulting with an attorney who specializes in coaching or healthcare law can provide clarity and protect you from legal risks. Remember, ignorance of the law is not a defense.

Role of Faith and Personal Values

As a Faith-Based Recovery Coachâ„¢, your faith and personal values play a significant role in your ethical decision-making. However, it's essential to balance these values with your professional obligations and ethical codes. Your faith can provide a strong moral compass, but it should not be used to impose your beliefs on clients or discriminate against those with different beliefs. A 2021 study in the Journal of Psychology and Theology found that faith-based practitioners who integrate their values ethically report higher levels of job satisfaction and client outcomes (Jones et al., 2021).

Case Study: Confidentiality vs. Legal Obligations

Case Study: Sarah's Dilemma

Client: Sarah, 35, recovering from opioid addiction.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah has been making good progress in her recovery but discloses that she relapsed briefly last week and drove under the influence. She expresses deep remorse and fear of legal consequences.

Ethical Dilemma: Sarah's disclosure raises a conflict between maintaining client confidentiality and the potential legal obligation to report impaired driving.

Intervention: The coach consults with a legal expert specializing in healthcare. The legal expert advises that the coach is not legally mandated to report the past incident but has a duty to prevent future harm. The coach encourages Sarah to self-report to the authorities and offers support in navigating the legal process. The coach also emphasizes the importance of preventing future impaired driving.

Outcomes: Sarah, with the coach's support, decides to self-report. She faces legal consequences but also begins to take responsibility for her actions. She continues her recovery journey with renewed commitment, and the coach provides ongoing support and guidance.

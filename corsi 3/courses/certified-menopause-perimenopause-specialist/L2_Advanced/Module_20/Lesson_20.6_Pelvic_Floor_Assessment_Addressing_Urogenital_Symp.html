Urogenital Symptoms in Menopause

Menopause significantly impacts the urogenital system due to declining estrogen levels. This can lead to a range of symptoms collectively known as Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM), formerly known as vulvovaginal atrophy. GSM affects approximately 50-60% of postmenopausal women (Parish et al., 2013). Common symptoms include:

  • Vaginal Dryness: Reduced lubrication can cause discomfort, itching, and painful intercourse.
  • Urinary Incontinence: Weakened pelvic floor muscles can lead to stress incontinence (leaking with cough, sneeze, or exercise) or urge incontinence (sudden, strong urge to urinate).
  • Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP): Weakening of the supporting tissues can cause the bladder, uterus, or rectum to descend into the vagina.
  • Recurrent UTIs: Lowered estrogen can alter the vaginal microbiome, increasing susceptibility to infections.

These symptoms can significantly impact a woman's quality of life, affecting intimacy, self-esteem, and daily activities. As aCertified Menopause & Perimenopause Specialistâ„¢, you play a vital role in identifying and addressing these issues.

Basic Pelvic Floor Assessment

A basic pelvic floor assessment can provide valuable insights into muscle strength and function. While a comprehensive evaluation requires a trained pelvic floor physical therapist, you can perform a simple assessment to screen for potential issues. This involves:

  1. Taking a Detailed History: Ask about urinary and bowel habits, sexual function, and any history of pelvic pain or prolapse.
  2. Observing Pelvic Floor Contraction: Instruct the client to perform a Kegel exercise (contract the muscles as if stopping the flow of urine). Observe for visible contraction and lifting of the perineum.
  3. Assessing Muscle Strength: Ask the client to hold the contraction for as long as possible. A healthy pelvic floor can typically hold a contraction for 8-10 seconds (Bump et al., 1991).
  4. Palpation (Optional): With the client's consent, you can gently palpate the pelvic floor muscles internally or externally to assess muscle tone and tenderness. Note: This requires proper training and should only be performed if you are qualified.

It's important to explain the assessment process clearly and ensure the client feels comfortable. If you observe significant weakness, pain, or dysfunction, refer the client to a pelvic floor physical therapist for a comprehensive evaluation.

Pelvic Floor Exercises (Kegels)

Pelvic floor exercises, commonly known as Kegels, are a cornerstone of conservative management for urogenital symptoms. These exercises strengthen the pelvic floor muscles, improving bladder control, vaginal tone, and support for pelvic organs. Educate your clients on the proper technique:

  • Identify the Correct Muscles: Instruct the client to contract the muscles as if stopping the flow of urine or preventing gas.
  • Proper Technique: Emphasize isolating the pelvic floor muscles without engaging the abdominal, gluteal, or thigh muscles.
  • Hold and Release: Instruct the client to hold the contraction for several seconds, then release.
  • Repetitions and Sets: Start with 10-15 repetitions, three times per day (Hay-Smith et al., 2008).
  • Consistency is Key: Encourage clients to incorporate Kegels into their daily routine.

Biofeedback and vaginal weights can be used to enhance the effectiveness of Kegel exercises. A 2019 Cochrane review of 45 trials (n=6,478) found that pelvic floor muscle training significantly reduced urinary incontinence episodes compared to no treatment (RR 0.62, 95% CI 0.53-0.73) (Dumoulin et al., 2019).

Referral to Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

Pelvic floor physical therapy (PFPT) offers specialized treatment for urogenital symptoms. PFPTs are trained to assess and treat pelvic floor muscle dysfunction using a variety of techniques, including:

  • Manual Therapy: Releasing muscle tension and trigger points in the pelvic floor.
  • Biofeedback: Using visual or auditory feedback to improve muscle awareness and control.
  • Electrical Stimulation: Stimulating muscle contraction to improve strength and function.
  • Bladder Training: Techniques to increase bladder capacity and reduce urgency.
  • Pelvic Floor Exercises: Customized exercise programs to address specific muscle weaknesses.

Referral to a PFPT is appropriate for clients with:

  • Persistent urinary incontinence despite Kegel exercises.
  • Pelvic pain or discomfort.
  • Pelvic organ prolapse.
  • Difficulty performing Kegel exercises correctly.

PFPT can significantly improve urogenital symptoms and quality of life. A study published in the Journal of Women's Health Physical Therapy found that PFPT reduced urinary incontinence episodes by 70% in women with stress incontinence (BÃ¸, 2004).

Medical Interventions

In addition to lifestyle modifications and physical therapy, medical interventions may be necessary to manage urogenital symptoms. These include:

  • Topical Estrogen: Vaginal estrogen creams, tablets, or rings can relieve vaginal dryness and improve urinary symptoms. Topical estrogen has minimal systemic absorption and is generally considered safe for long-term use (Suckling et al., 2015).
  • Systemic Hormone Therapy (HT): HT can alleviate urogenital symptoms in some women, but the risks and benefits should be carefully considered.
  • Vaginal Moisturizers and Lubricants: Over-the-counter products can provide temporary relief from vaginal dryness.
  • Pessaries: Devices inserted into the vagina to support pelvic organs and relieve prolapse symptoms.
  • Surgery: Surgical options are available for severe pelvic organ prolapse or urinary incontinence.

It's essential to discuss these options with your clients and encourage them to consult with their healthcare provider to determine the most appropriate treatment plan.

Case Study: Sarah, 52

Client: Sarah, 52 years old, presents with complaints of vaginal dryness, painful intercourse, and occasional urinary leakage when coughing. She reports these symptoms started about two years ago, coinciding with the onset of irregular periods.

Assessment: Sarah reports a score of 18 on the Greene Climacteric Scale, indicating moderate menopausal symptoms. She reports a sensation of vaginal dryness and discomfort during intercourse. On observation, she demonstrates a weak pelvic floor contraction, holding it for only 3 seconds.

Intervention: You educate Sarah on the importance of pelvic floor exercises and demonstrate the proper technique. You recommend starting with 10 repetitions, three times per day. You also suggest using a vaginal moisturizer and lubricant. You advise her to discuss topical estrogen with her gynecologist and refer her to a pelvic floor physical therapist for further evaluation and treatment.

Outcomes: After 3 months, Sarah reports significant improvement in vaginal dryness and pain during intercourse. She can now hold a pelvic floor contraction for 6 seconds and experiences fewer episodes of urinary leakage. She continues to work with the pelvic floor physical therapist to further improve her pelvic floor strength and function.

Case Study: Maria, 58

Client: Maria, 58 years old, presents with complaints of urinary urgency and frequency, often needing to urinate multiple times during the night. She also reports feeling a bulge in her vagina.

Assessment: Maria reports a score of 22 on the Menopause Rating Scale, indicating moderate to severe menopausal symptoms. Physical examination reveals a stage II uterine prolapse. She demonstrates a very weak pelvic floor contraction, barely able to contract the muscles at all.

Intervention: You explain the diagnosis of uterine prolapse and the importance of pelvic floor muscle strengthening. You emphasize the need for referral to a pelvic floor physical therapist for specialized treatment. You also discuss the option of using a pessary to support the uterus and alleviate symptoms. You encourage her to consult with her gynecologist to discuss all available treatment options, including surgery if necessary.

Outcomes: Maria starts pelvic floor physical therapy and learns to properly contract her pelvic floor muscles. She also tries a pessary, which significantly reduces her symptoms of urinary urgency and frequency. After several months of therapy, she reports improved quality of life and is able to manage her prolapse symptoms effectively.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What are the common symptoms of Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM)?
    Common symptoms include vaginal dryness, urinary incontinence, pelvic organ prolapse, and recurrent UTIs.
  2. Describe the steps involved in a basic pelvic floor assessment.
    A basic assessment involves taking a detailed history, observing pelvic floor contraction, assessing muscle strength, and optional palpation (if qualified).
  3. When is it appropriate to refer a client to a pelvic floor physical therapist?
    Referral is appropriate for persistent urinary incontinence, pelvic pain, pelvic organ prolapse, or difficulty performing Kegel exercises correctly.

Key Takeaways

  • Urogenital symptoms are common in menopause and can significantly impact quality of life.
  • A basic pelvic floor assessment can help identify muscle weakness or dysfunction.
  • Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) are a cornerstone of conservative management.
  • Referral to a pelvic floor physical therapist is essential for specialized treatment.
  • Medical interventions, such as topical estrogen and surgery, may be necessary in some cases.

SSRIs and SNRIs

Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are commonly prescribed antidepressants that have also demonstrated efficacy in managing vasomotor symptoms (VMS) like hot flashes. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that SNRIs reduce hot flash frequency by an average of 54% compared to placebo (95% CI: 48-60%). These medications work by modulating neurotransmitters in the brain that regulate body temperature and mood.

Commonly prescribed SSRIs and SNRIs for VMS include:

  • Paroxetine (Brisdelle)
  • Venlafaxine (Effexor)
  • Citalopram (Celexa)
  • Escitalopram (Lexapro)

Gabapentin and Pregabalin

Gabapentin and pregabalin are anticonvulsant medications that have been found to reduce the frequency and severity of hot flashes. These medications work by binding to Î±2Î´ subunits of voltage-gated calcium channels, which can help stabilize nerve activity and reduce the intensity of hot flashes. A randomized controlled trial published in 2022 showed that gabapentin reduced hot flash scores by 45-55% compared to placebo (p < 0.001). Pregabalin has a similar mechanism of action and may be considered as an alternative.

These medications are generally well-tolerated but can cause side effects such as dizziness, drowsiness, and weight gain. They may be a suitable option for women who experience migraines or neuropathic pain in addition to hot flashes.

Vaginal Estrogen Therapy

Vaginal estrogen therapy is a localized treatment option specifically designed to alleviate symptoms of vaginal dryness, itching, and dyspareunia (painful intercourse) associated with genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM). Unlike systemic hormone therapy, vaginal estrogen has minimal absorption into the bloodstream, reducing the risk of systemic side effects.

Different formulations include:

  • Vaginal creams (e.g., Estrace, Premarin)
  • Vaginal rings (e.g., Estring)
  • Vaginal tablets (e.g., Vagifem)

A Cochrane review from 2021 concluded that low-dose vaginal estrogen is effective in treating GSM symptoms and has a favorable safety profile. Itâ€™s important to discuss the various options with your client to determine the most appropriate choice based on their preferences and medical history.

Ospemifene and Prasterone

Ospemifene and prasterone are newer non-hormonal options for treating dyspareunia and vaginal atrophy. Ospemifene is a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM) that acts on estrogen receptors in the vaginal tissue, improving vaginal thickness and reducing pain during intercourse. A 2020 study demonstrated that ospemifene significantly improved vaginal maturation index and reduced dyspareunia severity compared to placebo (p < 0.001).

Prasterone (DHEA) is a vaginal insert that is converted into estrogen and androgens locally. It can help improve vaginal lubrication and reduce pain during intercourse. Both medications offer alternatives for women who cannot use or prefer not to use vaginal estrogen therapy.

Lifestyle Modifications

Lifestyle modifications play a crucial role in managing menopausal symptoms. Emphasizing these strategies, which align with the F.L.O.U.R.I.S.H. Frameworkâ„¢, can significantly improve a client's quality of life. Key recommendations include:

  • Dietary Changes: Incorporating phytoestrogens, such as soy products and flaxseeds, may help alleviate mild hot flashes.
  • Regular Exercise: Engaging in regular physical activity can improve mood, sleep, and overall well-being.
  • Stress Management: Practicing relaxation techniques like meditation, yoga, or deep breathing can reduce stress and anxiety.
  • Avoiding Triggers: Identifying and avoiding triggers for hot flashes, such as spicy foods, caffeine, and alcohol, can help reduce their frequency and severity.
  • Maintaining a Healthy Weight: Obesity can exacerbate menopausal symptoms.

Encouraging clients to adopt these lifestyle changes can empower them to take an active role in managing their symptoms and improving their overall health.

Case Study: Maria, 52

Client: Maria, 52 years old, presents with complaints of frequent hot flashes (8-10 per day), vaginal dryness, and sleep disturbances.

Intervention: Maria was started on a low-dose SSRI (escitalopram) to manage her hot flashes and improve her sleep. She was also prescribed vaginal estrogen cream for her vaginal dryness. Additionally, she was educated on lifestyle modifications, including regular exercise, stress management techniques, and dietary changes.

Outcomes: After 8 weeks, Maria reported a significant reduction in hot flash frequency (2-3 per day) and improved sleep quality. Her vaginal dryness also improved, leading to more comfortable sexual activity. She found the lifestyle modifications helpful in managing her stress levels and overall well-being.

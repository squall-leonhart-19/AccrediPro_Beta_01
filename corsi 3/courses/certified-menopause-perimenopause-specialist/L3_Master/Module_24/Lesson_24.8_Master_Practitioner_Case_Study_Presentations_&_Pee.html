The Business Question

Sarah's Pricing Dilemma

Sarah's Concern:

"I want to make a living, but I don't want to scare people away with high prices. I'm seeing colleagues charge anywhere from $75 to $250 per hour, and I'm just not sure where I fit in."

What You Need to Consider:

  • Her experience level and niche.
  • The local market rates.
  • The value she provides (outcomes, time saved, etc.).
  • Her own financial needs and income goals.

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Value-Based Pricing

Explain that pricing isn't just about time, but about the value clients receive. What transformation are you offering?

2

Market Research

Encourage Sarah to research what other practitioners in her area are charging. Consider location, experience, and services offered.

3

Packages vs. Hourly Rates

Discuss the pros and cons of offering packages (e.g., 3-month menopause reset) versus hourly rates. Packages often increase perceived value.

4

Building Confidence

Help Sarah understand that her nursing background brings unique skills and experience that justify her rates. Remind her of her expertise.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Guide Sarah Through Pricing

Acknowledge Her Feelings
"Sarah, I understand that pricing can feel tricky, especially when you're starting. Many practitioners struggle with this. You're not alone."
Start with Questions
"Before I share any suggestions, what feels like a comfortable starting point for you? What hourly rate would allow you to feel both confident and fair?"
Introduce the Value Concept
"Let's think about the value you bring. You're not just offering an hour of your time; you're offering your expertise, your nursing background, and a pathway to hormonal balance. How does that change your perspective?"
Encourage Research
"I recommend looking at what other practitioners in your area are charging. Consider their experience and the services they offer. This will give you a better sense of the market."
Empower Decision-Making
"Ultimately, it's your decision, Sarah. There's no 'right' answer. Find a price point that feels good to you and allows you to serve your clients effectively. I'm here to support you either way."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important thing to consider when mentoring a new practitioner on pricing?

Value-based pricing: emphasizing the transformation and outcomes provided to clients, not just time spent.

2. Why is it important to ask questions before offering solutions when mentoring?

To encourage self-reflection, problem-solving, and critical thinking in the mentee, fostering independence and confidence.

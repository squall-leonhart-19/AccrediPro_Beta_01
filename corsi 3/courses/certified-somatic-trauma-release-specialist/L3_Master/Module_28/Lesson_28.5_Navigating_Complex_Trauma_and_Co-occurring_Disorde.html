Prevalence and Impact of Co-occurring Disorders

Co-occurring disorders, also known as dual diagnosis, refer to the presence of both a mental health disorder and a substance use disorder. However, in the context of trauma, this definition can be expanded to include other mental health conditions such as eating disorders, personality disorders, and anxiety disorders. The prevalence of co-occurring disorders in trauma survivors is significantly higher than in the general population. For example, studies show that individuals with PTSD are 2-4 times more likely to develop a substance use disorder (Najavits et al., 2017).

These co-occurring conditions significantly complicate the healing process. They can exacerbate trauma symptoms, hinder treatment progress, and increase the risk of relapse. As a Somatic Trauma Release Specialistâ„¢, you'll need to develop the skills to recognize and address these complexities to provide effective care.

Trauma and Addiction: The Interplay

The connection between trauma and addiction is profound. Many individuals turn to substances or other maladaptive behaviors (like disordered eating) as a means of coping with the overwhelming emotional and physical sensations associated with trauma. Thisis often referred to as self-medication.

From a neurobiological perspective, trauma can disrupt the brain's reward system, making individuals more vulnerable to addiction. Chronic stress and trauma exposure can lead to dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and alterations in dopamine and opioid neurotransmission (van der Kolk, 2014). These changes can increase the reinforcing effects of substances and make it more difficult to experience pleasure through natural rewards.

Furthermore, substances can temporarily numb the pain and intrusive memories associated with trauma, providing a temporary escape. However, this escape comes at a high cost, as it can perpetuate a cycle of addiction and further impair emotional regulation.

Adapting the R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢

When working with clients who have co-occurring disorders, it's essential to adapt the R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢ to address their specific needs. The core principles of the framework â€“ Resourcing, Embodiment, and Body Awareness â€“ remain crucial, but the implementation needs to be carefully tailored.

Here's how you can adapt each component:

  • Resourcing: Prioritize safety and stabilization. This may involve connecting clients with addiction specialists, psychiatrists, or other healthcare professionals who can address their substance use or other co-occurring disorders. Create a safe and predictable therapeutic environment.
  • Embodiment: Focus on gentle and gradual embodiment practices. Avoid overwhelming clients with intense somatic work, especially in the early stages of treatment. Start with simple grounding techniques, such as mindful breathing or progressive muscle relaxation.
  • Body Awareness: Help clients develop awareness of their body sensations without judgment. Encourage them to notice the physical sensations associated with cravings, withdrawal symptoms, or emotional distress. Teach them skills to regulate these sensations, such as self-soothing techniques or somatic experiencing exercises.

Remember, progress may be slower and more challenging with co-occurring disorders. Patience, flexibility, and a strong therapeutic alliance are essential.

Collaboration with Healthcare Professionals

Working with clients who have complex trauma and co-occurring disorders requires a collaborative approach. As a Somatic Trauma Release Specialistâ„¢, you are part of a larger healthcare team. It's crucial to communicate and coordinate care with other professionals, such as addiction specialists, psychiatrists, therapists, and medical doctors.

Here are some strategies for effective collaboration:

  • Obtain informed consent: Before communicating with other professionals, obtain your client's informed consent. Explain the purpose of the communication and how it will benefit their treatment.
  • Participate in case conferences: Attend case conferences or team meetings to discuss your client's progress and coordinate treatment plans.
  • Share relevant information: Share relevant information with other professionals, such as your observations of the client's somatic responses or emotional state.
  • Respect professional boundaries: Respect the professional boundaries of other team members. Avoid giving advice outside of your scope of practice.

Effective collaboration can ensure that clients receive comprehensive and integrated care that addresses all aspects of their well-being.

Addressing Shame and Self-Blame

Individuals with co-occurring disorders often experience intense feelings of shame and self-blame. They may believe that they are weak, flawed, or morally deficient. These feelings can be particularly pronounced in trauma survivors, who may already struggle with self-blame for the traumatic events they experienced.

It's essential to address shame and self-blame directly in therapy. Here are some strategies:

  • Normalize their experiences: Help clients understand that their struggles with addiction or other co-occurring disorders are common responses to trauma.
  • Challenge self-blaming thoughts: Help clients identify and challenge self-blaming thoughts. Encourage them to consider alternative explanations for their behaviors.
  • Promote self-compassion: Teach clients self-compassion skills, such as treating themselves with kindness and understanding.
  • Focus on strengths and resilience: Help clients identify their strengths and resilience. Remind them of their past successes and their capacity for growth.

By fostering self-compassion and acceptance, you can help clients break free from the cycle of shame and begin to heal.

Case Study: Trauma and Substance Abuse

Case Study: Sarah - Trauma and Substance Abuse

Client: Sarah, 32 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presents with symptoms of PTSD following a car accident 5 years ago, where she was physically injured and witnessed the death of a close friend. She reports intrusive memories, nightmares, hypervigilance, and emotional numbing. Sarah also struggles with alcohol abuse, using alcohol to cope with her anxiety and trauma-related symptoms. She reports drinking almost every night to "calm her nerves."

Intervention:

  • Phase 1: Safety and Stabilization: Establishing a safe therapeutic environment and psychoeducation about trauma and addiction. Referral to an addiction specialist for assessment and treatment of alcohol use disorder. Introduction to grounding techniques and mindfulness exercises to manage anxiety and cravings.
  • Phase 2: Trauma Processing: Once Sarah was stable and engaged in addiction treatment, gentle trauma processing using somatic experiencing techniques. Focus on titrating the experience and allowing her body to release stored trauma energy.
  • Phase 3: Integration and Reconnection: Continued support for sobriety, relapse prevention strategies, and exploration of healthy coping mechanisms. Focus on rebuilding social connections and engaging in meaningful activities.

Outcomes: Sarah successfully completed addiction treatment and maintained sobriety for over a year. Her PTSD symptoms significantly improved, and she reported a greater sense of control over her emotions and body. She was able to reduce her reliance on alcohol and develop healthier coping strategies.

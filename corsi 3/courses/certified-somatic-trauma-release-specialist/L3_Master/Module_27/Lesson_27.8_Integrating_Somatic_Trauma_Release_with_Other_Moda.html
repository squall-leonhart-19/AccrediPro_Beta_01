The Case at Hand

Sarah's Client: Emily, 52

Sarah's Account:

"Emily, 52, presented with chronic lower back pain after a car accident 10 years ago. During our session, I guided her through a somatic experiencing exercise to connect with sensations in her back. She started to cry and said she felt angry. I encouraged her to explore the anger, but she became overwhelmed and shut down. I'm worried I re-traumatized her."

Areas for Exploration:

  • Was there sufficient resourcing prior to the exploration?
  • Was the pace of exploration appropriate for Emily's nervous system?
  • Did Sarah effectively track Emily's titration and capacity?
  • What were the verbal and non-verbal cues that Emily was becoming overwhelmed?

Guiding Principles

Key Teaching Points for Sarah

1

The Importance of Resourcing

Emphasize that resourcing is the foundation of somatic work. Before diving into trauma exploration, ensure the client has solid grounding and coping mechanisms.

2

Titration and Pendulation

Explain the concepts of titration (small, manageable doses of trauma-related material) and pendulation (oscillating between safe and challenging sensations) to prevent overwhelm.

3

Tracking Nervous System Arousal

Teach Sarah how to identify signs of nervous system dysregulation (e.g., rapid breathing, muscle tension, dissociation) and adjust the session accordingly.

4

Validating and Normalizing

Reassure Sarah that it's normal for clients to experience a range of emotions during somatic work, and that setbacks are opportunities for learning and refinement.

Crafting Your Feedback

A Constructive Feedback Framework

Acknowledge Effort
"Sarah, I commend you for your willingness to explore somatic techniques with your clients. It takes courage to step outside your comfort zone."
Inquire About Her Perspective
"Before I offer my thoughts, what did you notice about Emily's reactions during the session? What cues did you pick up on?"
Offer Guidance
"It sounds like Emily's nervous system became overwhelmed when you encouraged her to explore the anger. In somatic work, it's crucial to ensure the client is fully resourced before delving into intense emotions. We want to support the client's nervous system capacity, not overwhelm it."
Empower Future Sessions
"What adjustments would you make in your next session with Emily? How can you create a safer and more supportive environment for her to explore her emotions?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of resourcing in somatic trauma work?

The primary goal is to establish a foundation of safety and stability for the client, building their capacity to process potentially overwhelming emotions and sensations.

2. Explain the difference between titration and pendulation, and why they are important in trauma work.

Titration involves processing trauma in small, manageable doses, while pendulation is the process of oscillating between safe and challenging sensations. These techniques help prevent overwhelm and promote integration.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective supervision requires a balance of guidance, support, and empowerment.
  • Prioritize resourcing and titration to prevent overwhelm and promote client safety.
  • Encourage self-reflection and critical thinking in your mentees.
  • Celebrate successes and normalize the learning process.
  • Your role as a mentor is to cultivate confident, competent, and ethical practitioners.

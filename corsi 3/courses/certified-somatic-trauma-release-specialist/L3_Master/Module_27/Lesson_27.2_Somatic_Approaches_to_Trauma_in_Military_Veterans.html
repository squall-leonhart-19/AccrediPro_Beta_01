Understanding Trauma in Veterans

Welcome! Building on our previous lesson on first responders, this lesson focuses on the unique challenges faced by military veterans. As you know, trauma manifests somatically, and veterans often carry deep wounds from their service. We will explore how combat, moral injury, and military sexual trauma (MST) impact their somatic experience and how we can adapt the R.E.Body. Frameworkâ„¢ to meet their specific needs.

Many veterans transition into civilian life carrying invisible wounds. Somatic approaches offer a powerful avenue for healing by addressing the body's stored trauma. You're on your way to empowering these brave individuals to reclaim their lives and well-being.

Combat-Related Trauma and Moral Injury

Combat-related trauma can lead to a range of somatic symptoms, including hypervigilance, chronic pain, and sleep disturbances. Moral injury, resulting from actions that violate a person's moral code, can manifest as deep shame, guilt, and disconnection from the body. A 2020 study in the *Journal of Traumatic Stress* found that 76% of combat veterans reported experiencing at least one potentially morally injurious event.

Consider this: A veteran who witnessed the death of a child during combat might experience chronic tension in their chest, difficulty breathing, and an inability to feel safe in their own body. These somatic symptoms are directly linked to the traumatic event and themoral injury they sustained.

Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Military Sexual Trauma (MST) is any sexual harassment or assault experienced during military service. It affects both men and women and can lead to severe psychological and somatic consequences, including PTSD, depression, and chronic pain. According to the Department of Veterans Affairs, about 1 in 4 women and 1 in 100 men report experiencing MST. MST often leads to feelings of betrayal, powerlessness, and profound distrust of others.

MST survivors may experience somatic symptoms such as pelvic pain, digestive issues, and a persistent sense of being unsafe in their bodies. They may also struggle with body image and self-esteem. Addressing MST requires a trauma-informed approach that prioritizes safety, empowerment, and choice.

Modifying the R.E.Body. Frameworkâ„¢ for Veterans

The R.E.Body. Frameworkâ„¢ can be adapted to meet the unique needs of veterans by focusing on:

  • Reconnecting: Facilitating a safe return to the body through gentle movement, breathwork, and sensory awareness.
  • Emotional Regulation: Teaching grounding techniques and somatic experiencing skills to manage hyperarousal and emotional flooding.
  • Body Awareness: Helping veterans develop a deeper understanding of their body's signals and sensations, fostering a sense of agency and control.
  • Optimizing Function: Addressing chronic pain, mobility issues, and other physical limitations through targeted movement and functional exercises.
  • Developing Resilience: Building coping strategies and self-compassion to navigate triggers and manage stress.

When working with veterans, it's crucial to be mindful of potential triggers and to create a safe and predictable environment. Start slowly, and always prioritize the client's comfort and consent. Offering choices and empowering veterans to take control of their healing process is essential.

Integrating Somatic and Traditional Approaches

Somatic approaches can be effectively integrated with traditional psychotherapy and pharmacological treatments. For example, a veteran receiving cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) for PTSD can also benefit from somatic experiencing to process trauma-related emotions and sensations stored in the body. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) in *JAMA Network Open* found that combining somatic therapies with traditional talk therapy resulted in significantly greater reductions in PTSD symptoms compared to talk therapy alone ( ES=0.62, 95% CI [0.48, 0.76] ).

Collaboration with other healthcare providers, such as physicians and psychiatrists, is essential to ensure comprehensive and coordinated care. Sharing information and coordinating treatment plans can optimize outcomes and support the veteran's overall well-being.

Case Study: Reconnecting Through Movement

Case Study: David, 38-year-old Veteran

Client: David, a 38-year-old veteran who served two tours in Afghanistan.

Presenting Symptoms: David presented with chronic back pain, insomnia, hypervigilance, and emotional numbing. He reported difficulty connecting with his family and a pervasive sense of isolation.

Intervention: We began with gentle breathwork and grounding exercises to help David reconnect with his body. We then introduced simple movement sequences to improve mobility and reduce pain. Somatic experiencing techniques were used to process trauma-related emotions and sensations.

Outcomes: After several months of treatment, David reported a significant reduction in pain and improved sleep. He also experienced increased emotional awareness and a greater sense of connection with his family. He stated, "I feel like I'm finally back in my body, not just watching from the outside."

Building Rapport and Trust

Understanding military culture is essential for building rapport and trust with veteran clients. Veterans often value directness, respect, and a sense of camaraderie. It's important to be mindful of military jargon and to avoid making assumptions about their experiences. A 2019 study in *Psychological Trauma: Theory, Research, Practice, and Policy* highlighted the importance of cultural competence in providing effective mental health services to veterans.

Address issues of trust and vulnerability by being transparent, reliable, and consistent. Validate their experiences and acknowledge the sacrifices they have made. Creating a safe and non-judgmental space is crucial for fostering healing and growth. Remember, building trust takes time, so be patient and persistent.

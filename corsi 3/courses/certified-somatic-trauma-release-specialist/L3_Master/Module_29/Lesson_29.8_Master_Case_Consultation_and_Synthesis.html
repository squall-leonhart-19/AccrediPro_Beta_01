The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I have a client, Emily, 52, who is presenting with chronic pain and anxiety. During our session, she disclosed a history of childhood abuse. I felt completely frozen and didn't know how to proceed. I'm worried I retraumatized her."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Sarah have a clear resourcing plan in place before exploring trauma history?
  • What somatic techniques did she attempt, and were they appropriate?
  • How did Sarah manage her own emotional response during the disclosure?
  • Did Sarah explain the importance of titration and pacing?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Emphasize Resourcing First

Reinforce the importance of establishing strong internal and external resources BEFORE diving into trauma narratives. This is foundational.

2

Review Titration and Pacing

Explain that trauma processing should be approached in small, manageable doses. Titration allows the nervous system to gradually adapt.

3

Discuss Vicarious Trauma

Acknowledge the impact of vicarious trauma and the importance of self-care practices. Encourage her to seek her own supervision.

4

Explore Somatic Grounding Techniques

Review specific somatic techniques for grounding and centering both herself and her client during challenging moments.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Validate Her Experience
"Sarah, it's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when a client discloses a trauma history. It shows you care deeply. Let's break this down together."
Inquire About Her Preparation
"Before the session, what resourcing strategies did you have in place for Emily? What about for yourself?"
Teach the Principle
"Remember, our role isn't to 'fix' the trauma in one session. It's to create a safe space and slowly build capacity for the client to process it at their own pace. Think 'safety first'."
Empower Next Steps
"What adjustments can you make for your next session with Emily? How can you reinforce her resources and ensure a slower, more regulated approach?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key elements of providing effective supervision to a new somatic trauma practitioner?

Effective supervision includes creating a safe space, validating the practitioner's experience, reviewing the case in detail, teaching principles, and empowering them to take the next steps.

2. How can you help a new practitioner manage their own vicarious trauma?

Encourage self-care practices, such as mindfulness, exercise, and spending time in nature. Also, suggest they seek their own supervision or therapy to process their emotional responses.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is about guiding, not dictating.

  • Address vicarious trauma proactively.
  • Empower new practitioners to develop their clinical judgment.

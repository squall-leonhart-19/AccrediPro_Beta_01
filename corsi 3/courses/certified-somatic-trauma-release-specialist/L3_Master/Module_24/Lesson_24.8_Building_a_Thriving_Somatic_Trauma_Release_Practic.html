The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with Emily, 52, who has a history of childhood trauma. During our last session, she started crying and shaking uncontrollably. I felt panicked and didn't know what to do. I just held space and told her it was okay, but I'm worried I didn't handle it correctly."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Sarah establish clear boundaries and safety protocols with Emily beforehand?
  • Was Sarah adequately resourced herself before and after the session?
  • What specific somatic techniques did Sarah employ (if any) leading up to this release?
  • Does Emily have adequate external support (therapy, support groups)?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Normalize Vicarious Trauma

Explain that experiencing strong emotional reactions to clients' stories is normal, especially early in practice. Self-care is crucial.

2

Review Resourcing Techniques

Reinforce the importance of grounding and resourcing both for the client and the practitioner. Discuss specific techniques Sarah can use.

3

Emphasize Scope of Practice

Remind Sarah that she's not a therapist. Her role is to facilitate somatic release, not to provide psychotherapy. Referrals are sometimes necessary.

4

Discuss Titration and Pendulation

Explain how to help clients process trauma in small, manageable doses using titration and pendulation techniques to avoid overwhelming them.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, it's wonderful that you're seeking supervision. It shows you care deeply about your clients. This experience is a valuable learning opportunity."
Explore Her Perspective
"Before I offer my thoughts, what did you feel went well in the session? What would you do differently next time, knowing what you know now?"
Share a Key Principle
"In somatic work, we aim to help clients build capacity for sensation, not necessarily to release everything at once. Titration is key ‚Äì working in small, manageable doses."
Offer Practical Suggestions
"Perhaps in the next session, you could start by guiding Emily through a simple grounding exercise, like feeling her feet on the floor, before exploring any trauma-related sensations."
Reinforce Self-Care
"Remember, your well-being is paramount. What are you doing to resource yourself after sessions like these? Let's brainstorm some self-care strategies."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important aspect of providing supervision to a new practitioner?

Creating a safe and supportive environment where they feel comfortable asking questions and exploring challenges.

2. How can you help a mentee who is struggling with imposter syndrome?

By validating their feelings, highlighting their strengths, and reminding them of their progress. Share your own experiences with self-doubt to normalize the experience.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a crucial component of professional development in somatic practice.
  • Effective supervision involves creating a safe, supportive, and empowering environment for mentees.
  • Normalize challenges and encourage self-reflection and critical thinking.
  • Emphasize the importance of self-care and boundary setting.
  • Remember, your role is to guide, not to dictate.

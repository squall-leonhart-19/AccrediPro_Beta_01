Developmental Movement Patterns

Developmental movement patterns are the foundational movements we learn as infants and young children. These patterns are crucial for establishing neurological connections, building body awareness, and developing emotional regulation. Trauma experienced during these early developmental stages can disrupt these patterns, leading to physical and emotional imbalances later in life.

By revisiting and re-patterning these movements, we can help clients access and heal early attachment wounds and trauma imprints. Some key developmental movement patterns include:

  • Yield and Push: Experiencing gravity and learning to push against it.
  • Reach and Grasp: Developing hand-eye coordination and reaching out to the world.
  • Core/Distal: Moving from the center of the body outwards.
  • Head/Tail: Connecting the head and tailbone for spinal support and movement.
  • Upper/Lower: Coordinating movement between the upper and lower body.
  • Body Sides: Integrating movement on both sides of the body

Application: Observe your client's posture and movement patterns for any signs of restriction or asymmetry. For example, a client who experienced early neglect might have difficulty with "Reach and Grasp," exhibiting a collapsed chest or difficulty extending their arms fully. Gently guide them through exercises that explore these patterns, focusing on sensation and allowing for spontaneous movement.

Authentic Movement

Authentic Movement is a form of movement-based therapy that emphasizes self-expression and emotional release through spontaneous movement. In this practice, the client (the mover) moves in response to inner impulses, while the therapist (the witness) provides a safe and supportive presence without judgment or interpretation.

Key Principles of Authentic Movement:

  • Non-Judgmental Witnessing: The therapist's role is to simply observe and hold space for the mover's experience.
  • Inner-Directed Movement: The mover is encouraged to follow their inner impulses and move in whatever way feels natural and authentic.
  • Embodied Awareness: The practice cultivates a deeper connection to the body and its innate wisdom.

Facilitation: Create a safe and private space. Instruct the client to close their eyes, connect with their breath, and allow their body to move in whatever way feels natural. As the witness, observe the client's movement without judgment, noting any patterns, emotions, or sensations that arise. After the movement session, facilitate a verbal processing session where the client can share their experience.

Working with the Psoas Muscle

The psoas is a deep core muscle that connects the spine to the legs. It plays a crucial role in movement, posture, and emotional regulation. Because of its location and connection to the diaphragm (involved in breathing), the psoas is often referred to as the "muscle of the soul." Chronic stress and trauma can cause the psoas to become tight and constricted, contributing to pain, stiffness, and emotional distress.

Psoas Release Techniques:

  • Constructive Rest Position: Lying on the back with knees bent and feet flat on the floor allows the psoas to release tension.
  • Gentle Stretching: Specific stretches, such as hip flexor stretches, can help to lengthen and release the psoas.
  • Movement Exploration: Encourage clients to explore movements that engage the psoas, such as walking, swaying, and gentle rocking.

Caution: Working with the psoas can be emotionally triggering for some clients, as it can hold deeply held trauma. Always proceed with gentleness and sensitivity, and ensure that the client feels safe and supported. A 2014 study in Traumatology found that interventions addressing the psoas muscle can be effective in reducing anxiety and improving body awareness in trauma survivors. The study (n=30) reported a significant decrease in anxiety scores (p<.05) following a six-week psoas release program. (Strozzi, 2014)

Mindful Movement and Interoception

Interoception is the ability to sense and understand the internal state of the body. This includes sensations such as hunger, thirst, heart rate, and muscle tension. Trauma can disrupt interoceptive awareness, making it difficult for individuals to recognize and respond to their body's signals. This can lead to difficulties with self-regulation, emotional processing, and overall well-being.

Mindful Movement practices, such as yoga, Tai Chi, and Qigong, can help to enhance interoceptive awareness by bringing attention to the body's sensations in a non-judgmental way. By focusing on the breath, posture, and movement, clients can learn to tune into their internal landscape and develop a greater sense of body awareness.

Integrating Interoception into Sessions:

  • Body Scan Meditations: Guide clients through a body scan, encouraging them to notice sensations in different parts of the body without judgment.
  • Movement with Awareness: Encourage clients to pay attention to the sensations of movement, such as the feeling of their feet on the ground or the movement of their breath.
  • Journaling: Ask clients to journal about their experiences with interoception, noting any sensations, emotions, or thoughts that arise.

Case Study: Sarah, 34, Chronic Pain and Anxiety

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with chronic lower back pain, anxiety, and difficulty sleeping. She reported a history of childhood emotional neglect and a recent car accident.

Intervention: We began with resourcing techniques, followed by gentle sensation exploration. We then introduced developmental movement patterns, focusing on "Yield and Push" to address her feelings of insecurity and lack of support. We also incorporated mindful movement and psoas release techniques to address her pain and anxiety.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in pain and anxiety. She was sleeping better and felt more connected to her body. She also reported a greater sense of self-compassion and an increased ability to regulate her emotions. Sarah's self-reported pain levels decreased by 40% using a visual analog scale.

Case Study: David, 48, Trauma and Dissociation

Presenting Symptoms: David presented with symptoms of PTSD, including flashbacks, nightmares, and dissociation. He had a history of childhood physical abuse.

Intervention: We focused on establishing safety and building resources. We then introduced Authentic Movement to allow for spontaneous expression and emotional release. I provided a non-judgmental witness and helped him process his experiences after each session. We also incorporated grounding techniques to help him stay present in his body.

Outcomes: Over time, David's flashbacks and nightmares decreased in intensity and frequency. He reported feeling more grounded and connected to his body. He also began to express emotions that he had previously suppressed. David's scores on the PTSD Checklist (PCL-5) decreased by 25% over the course of treatment.

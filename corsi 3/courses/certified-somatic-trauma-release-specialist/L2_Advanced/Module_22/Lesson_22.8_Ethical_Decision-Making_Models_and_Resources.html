Case Study: Sarah & The Yoga Retreat

üë©

Sarah, 48

Yoga Instructor & STR Practitioner, Denver, CO ‚Ä¢ Married, two children

Background
Sarah is a certified yoga instructor who recently completed her Somatic Trauma Release (STR) certification. She integrates STR into her yoga classes and offers private STR sessions.
Presenting Dilemma
Sarah is organizing a weekend yoga retreat focused on trauma-informed practices. A participant, Emily, discloses a history of severe childhood trauma during a group sharing circle. Emily expresses interest in private STR sessions with Sarah during the retreat.
Dual Relationship Concerns
Sarah recognizes the potential for a dual relationship: yoga instructor and STR practitioner. She also acknowledges the power dynamic inherent in a retreat setting.
Scope of Practice Question
Emily's trauma history appears complex. Sarah questions whether her STR training is sufficient to address Emily's needs, especially given the limited time frame of the retreat.
Informed Consent Challenge
Sarah is unsure how to adequately explain the potential risks and benefits of STR to Emily in a way that ensures truly informed consent, especially given Emily's heightened emotional state.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Decision-Making Steps

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Issues
The primary ethical issues are dual relationships, scope of practice, informed consent, and potential boundary violations. Dual relationships can compromise objectivity and increase the risk of exploitation.
Step 2: Consult Ethical Guidelines & Legal Standards
Review the ethical codes of your professional organizations (e.g., yoga alliance, somatic experiencing association) and relevant state licensing laws. Understand the legal definitions of abuse, neglect, and mandatory reporting requirements.
Step 3: Consider the Client's Welfare
Emily's well-being is paramount. Assess her current emotional state, support system, and history of trauma. Determine if STR is the most appropriate intervention at this time, or if a referral to a licensed mental health professional is necessary.
Step 4: Explore Options & Consequences
Consider the following options: (a) Decline to provide private STR sessions during the retreat, (b) Offer a limited introductory session with clear boundaries, (c) Refer Emily to a qualified STR practitioner or therapist in her local area. Weigh the potential benefits and risks of each option.
Step 5: Document Your Decision-Making Process
Thorough documentation is crucial. Record the ethical issues identified, the consultations sought, the options considered, and the rationale for your final decision. This protects both you and your client.

Differential Considerations

Alternative Explanations & Approaches

1

Complex Trauma vs. Single-Incident Trauma

Determine if Emily's trauma history involves complex, developmental trauma. Complex trauma often requires specialized therapeutic approaches beyond basic STR techniques.

2

Attachment Styles & Relational Trauma

Assess Emily's attachment style. Individuals with insecure attachment may be more vulnerable to boundary violations and re-traumatization in therapeutic relationships.

3

Dissociation & Somatic Countertransference

Be aware of the potential for dissociation during STR sessions. Monitor your own somatic countertransference reactions, which can indicate unresolved trauma in the client.

When to Refer

Red Flags & Scope of Practice Boundaries

  • Severe dissociative symptoms ‚Äì Indicates a need for specialized trauma therapy.
  • Legal involvement ‚Äì If Emily's trauma is related to ongoing legal proceedings, refer to a forensic psychologist or attorney.
  • Medical conditions impacting trauma processing ‚Äì Refer to a physician or psychiatrist if Emily has medical conditions (e.g., traumatic brain injury) that may affect her ability to process trauma safely.
  • Requests outside your scope of practice ‚Äì If Emily requests interventions beyond your training and expertise, provide appropriate referrals.

Proposed Ethical Protocol

Phased Ethical Intervention Plan

üìÖ Phase 1: Assessment & Consultation (Days 1-2)
  • Acknowledge Emily's disclosure with empathy and validate her experience.
  • Clearly explain the potential dual relationship and its implications.
  • Consult with a senior STR practitioner or ethics expert for guidance.
  • Assess Emily's readiness for STR and her understanding of the process.
üìÖ Phase 2: Informed Consent & Boundary Setting (Day 2)
  • Provide a detailed explanation of STR, including potential risks (e.g., emotional dysregulation, abreaction) and benefits.
  • Obtain written informed consent, documenting the discussion and Emily's agreement.
  • Establish clear boundaries: no private STR sessions during the retreat; focus on group yoga and self-regulation techniques.
üìÖ Phase 3: Referral & Follow-Up (Day 3)
  • Provide Emily with a list of qualified STR practitioners and trauma therapists in her local area.
  • Offer to connect Emily with a support group or online resources.
  • Document all interactions and referrals in detail.
  • Follow up with Emily briefly after the retreat to ensure she has accessed appropriate support.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key considerations when navigating dual relationships in somatic trauma work?

Key considerations include power dynamics, potential for exploitation, objectivity, and the client's vulnerability. Always prioritize the client's well-being and avoid situations that could compromise therapeutic boundaries.

2. How can you ensure informed consent when working with clients who have complex trauma histories?

Provide a thorough explanation of the intervention, including potential risks and benefits. Assess the client's understanding and capacity to make an informed decision. Document the consent process in detail. Consider involving a support person or advocate if necessary.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethical practice in somatic trauma release requires ongoing self-reflection, consultation, and adherence to professional guidelines.
  • Dual relationships can create conflicts of interest and compromise the integrity of the therapeutic process.
  • Informed consent is an ongoing process, not a one-time event. It requires clear communication, assessment of understanding, and documentation.
  • Knowing your scope of practice and making appropriate referrals is essential for client safety and ethical responsibility.
  • Prioritize the client's well-being above all else. When in doubt, err on the side of caution and seek expert guidance.

Trauma Symptom Inventory-2 (TSI-2)

The Trauma Symptom Inventory-2 (TSI-2) is a comprehensive self-report measure designed to evaluate the acute and chronic psychological effects of traumatic experiences. It assesses a wide range of symptoms, including anxiety, depression, dissociation, and sexual disturbance. The TSI-2 is particularly useful in identifying specific areas of distress and informing treatment planning within The R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢.The TSI-2 consists of 136 items across various scales and subscales, providing a detailed profile of the individual's trauma-related symptoms. It is designed to be used with adults aged 18 and older.

Scale Description Example Item
Anxious Arousal Measures symptoms of anxiety, hyperarousal, and exaggerated startle response. "I feel constantly on edge."
Depression Assesses feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and loss of interest in activities. "I have difficulty enjoying things."
Dissociation Evaluates feelings of detachment from oneself or one's surroundings. "I feel like I'm watching myself from outside my body."
Sexual Disturbance Measures distress related to sexual experiences or functioning. "I feel uncomfortable with sexual intimacy."
Impaired Self-Reference Assesses difficulties with self-esteem, identity, and sense of self. "I don't feel like I know who I am anymore."

A study by Briere (2011) found the TSI-2 to have high internal consistency (Î± = .79-.94) and test-retest reliability (r = .82-.95), supporting its use in clinical settings.

Dissociative Experiences Scale (DES-II)

The Dissociative Experiences Scale (DES-II) is a widely used self-report questionnaire designed to measure the frequency and severity of dissociative experiences. Dissociation, a common response to trauma, involves feeling detached from one's body, emotions, or surroundings. The DES-II helps identify individuals who may be experiencing significant levels of dissociation, which can inform targeted interventions within The R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢.

The DES-II consists of 28 items, each describing a dissociative experience. Respondents indicate how frequently they experience each item on a scale from 0% to 100%. The total score is calculated by averaging the responses to all items, providing a measure of overall dissociative tendencies.

Example items from the DES-II include:

  • "I find that I sometimes am in a place and have no idea how I got there."
  • "I have the experience of feeling as though I am standing next to myself or watching myself do something as if I were looking at another person."
  • "Some people find that they sometimes sit and stare and don't know what is happening around them."

A study by Carlson & Putnam (1993) established cutoff scores for the DES, suggesting that individuals with scores above 30 may warrant further evaluation for dissociative disorders. A meta-analysis by van Ijzendoorn and Schuengel (2008) found significant correlations between DES scores and histories of childhood trauma, highlighting its utility in trauma-informed assessment.

Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACE) Questionnaire

The Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACE) Questionnaire is a pivotal tool for understanding the long-term impact of childhood trauma on adult health and well-being. It assesses exposure to various forms of abuse, neglect, and household dysfunction during childhood. The ACE questionnaire provides valuable context for interpreting a client's current symptoms and tailoring interventions within The R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢.

The ACE questionnaire consists of 10 items, each assessing a different type of adverse childhood experience. These include:

  • Emotional abuse
  • Physical abuse
  • Sexual abuse
  • Emotional neglect
  • Physical neglect
  • Household substance abuse
  • Household mental illness
  • Household violence towards mother
  • Household incarceration
  • Parental separation or divorce

Each item is scored as either present (1) or absent (0), and the total ACE score is the sum of all positive responses. Higher ACE scores are associated with increased risk for a wide range of negative health outcomes, including mental health disorders, substance abuse, chronic diseases, and premature mortality.

The original ACE study by Felitti et al. (1998) demonstrated a strong dose-response relationship between ACE scores and negative health outcomes. For example, individuals with an ACE score of 4 or more were significantly more likely to experience depression, anxiety, and substance abuse compared to those with an ACE score of 0.

Administration and Scoring

Accurate administration and scoring of self-report measures are crucial for obtaining reliable and valid results. Here are some practical guidelines to ensure the integrity of the assessment process:

  • Create a Safe and Supportive Environment: Before administering any self-report measure, establish rapport with the client and explain the purpose of the assessment. Emphasize that their responses will be kept confidential and used to inform their treatment plan.
  • Provide Clear Instructions: Ensure that the client understands the instructions for completing the questionnaire. Read the instructions aloud and answer any questions they may have.
  • Monitor for Distress: Observe the client for any signs of distress during the assessment process. If they become overwhelmed or triggered, offer support and consider breaking the assessment into smaller segments.
  • Follow Standardized Scoring Procedures: Adhere to the scoring guidelines provided in the test manual. Double-check your calculations to minimize errors.
  • Consider Cultural and Linguistic Factors: Be mindful of cultural and linguistic differences that may impact the client's understanding and responses. Use translated versions of the questionnaires when appropriate.

For the TSI-2, scoring involves calculating raw scores for each scale and subscale, and then converting them to T-scores based on normative data. The DES-II is scored by averaging the responses to all 28 items, resulting in a score ranging from 0 to 100. The ACE questionnaire is scored by summing the number of positive responses, resulting in a score ranging from 0 to 10.

Interpreting Data and Somatic Presentations

Interpreting self-report data requires careful consideration of the client's individual history, cultural background, and current circumstances. It is essential to integrate the quantitative data from the questionnaires with qualitative information gathered through clinical interviews and somatic observations. As practitioners of The R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢, we understand that trauma manifests differently in each individual, and a holistic approach is necessary for accurate assessment and effective treatment.

When interpreting the TSI-2, pay attention to elevated scores on specific scales and subscales, as these may indicate areas of significant distress or dysfunction. For example, a high score on the Anxious Arousal scale may suggest that the client is experiencing symptoms of hyperarousal and hypervigilance, while a high score on the Dissociation scale may indicate a tendency to detach from their emotions or surroundings.

Similarly, when interpreting the DES-II, consider the overall score and the specific items that the client endorsed. High scores on items related to absorption and imaginative involvement may suggest a tendency towards fantasy and escapism, while high scores on items related to depersonalization and derealization may indicate more severe forms of dissociation.

When interpreting the ACE questionnaire, consider the total score and the specific types of adverse experiences the client reported. Higher ACE scores are associated with increased risk for a wide range of negative health outcomes, and the specific types of experiences may provide valuable clues about the client's current symptoms and challenges.

It's crucial to correlate reported symptoms with observed somatic responses. For example, a client reporting high anxiety (TSI-2) and childhood emotional abuse (ACE) might exhibit chronic muscle tension, shallow breathing, or a heightened startle response during sessions. Limitations of Self-Report Measures

While self-report measures are valuable tools in trauma assessment, it is important to recognize their limitations. These limitations include:

  • Response Bias: Clients may intentionally or unintentionally distort their responses due to social desirability, recall bias, or a desire to minimize or exaggerate their symptoms.
  • Limited Insight: Some clients may lack insight into their own thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, making it difficult for them to accurately report their experiences.
  • Reading and Language Barriers: Self-report measures require adequate reading comprehension and language proficiency. Clients with limited literacy or language skills may struggle to understand the questions and provide accurate responses.
  • Cultural Differences: Cultural norms and values may influence how clients interpret and respond to the questions. It is important to consider cultural factors when interpreting the results.
  • Reliance on Memory: Self-report measures rely on the client's ability to recall past events and experiences. Memory can be fallible, and traumatic memories may be particularly fragmented or distorted.

To mitigate these limitations, it is essential to use self-report measures in conjunction with other assessment methods, such as clinical interviews, behavioral observations, and physiological measures. Triangulating data from multiple sources can provide a more comprehensive and accurate understanding of the client's experiences.

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, 34 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with chronic anxiety, panic attacks, and difficulty sleeping. She reported feeling "on edge" most of the time and struggled to concentrate at work. Somatically, she exhibited shallow breathing, clenched jaw, and tense shoulders.

Intervention: Sarah completed the TSI-2, DES-II, and ACE questionnaire. Her TSI-2 scores were elevated on the Anxious Arousal and Depression scales. Her DES-II score was 35, indicating significant dissociative experiences. Her ACE score was 5, with reported emotional abuse, household substance abuse, and parental separation.

Outcomes: Integrating the self-report data with Sarah's somatic presentation, we identified a pattern of chronic hyperarousal and dissociation related to her childhood trauma. Treatment focused on somatic experiencing techniques to release stored trauma in her body, along with cognitive-behavioral strategies to manage her anxiety and panic attacks. After several months of therapy, Sarah reported a significant reduction in her anxiety and panic symptoms, improved sleep, and a greater sense of calm and well-being. Her somatic symptoms also improved, with reduced muscle tension and deeper, more relaxed breathing.

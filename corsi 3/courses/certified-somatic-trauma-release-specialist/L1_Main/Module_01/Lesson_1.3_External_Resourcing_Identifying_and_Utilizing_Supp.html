What is External Resourcing?

External resourcing refers to the process of identifying and utilizing external sources of support, connection, and safety to enhance well-being and resilience. These resources can include family, friends, community organizations, healthcare providers, and other individuals or groups that provide emotional, social, or practical assistance. In the context of somatic trauma release, external resources play a vital role in creating a sense of safety and stability, which is essential for processing and integrating traumatic experiences.

As you learned in Module 0, trauma often leads to feelings of isolation and disconnection. By actively engaging with external resources, clients can begin to rebuild a sense of belonging and connection, fostering a supportive environment for healing. Remember The R.E.Body. Frameworkâ„¢ emphasizes the interconnectedness of mind, body, and environment. External resourcing directly addresses the environmental aspect, recognizing the profound impact of social support on overall well-being.

Identifying Existing Support Systems

A crucial first step in external resourcing is helping clients identify and assess their existing support systems. This involves exploring various aspects of their lives to uncover potential sources of connection and assistance. Encourage your clients to consider the following:

  • Family: Are there family members who provide emotional support, understanding, or practical help?
  • Friends: Who are the friends who offer companionship, empathy, and a listening ear?
  • Community Resources: Are there local organizations, support groups, or religious institutions that offer assistance or a sense of belonging?
  • Healthcare Providers: Does the client have access to healthcare professionals, such as therapists, doctors, or psychiatrists, who can provide guidance and support?

    • To facilitate this process, you can use a simple assessment tool or worksheet that prompts clients to list potential resources and rate the level of support they provide. This can help clients gain a clearer understanding of their existing support network and identify areas where they may need to strengthen connections or build new ones.

    For example, you might ask your client to rate each resource on a scale of 1 to 5, where 1 represents "not at all supportive" and 5 represents "very supportive." This quantitative approach can provide valuable insights into the quality and reliability of different support systems.

    Strengthening and Building New Connections

    Once clients have identified their existing support systems, the next step is to explore strategies for strengthening those connections and building new ones. This may involve:

    • Improving Communication: Encourage clients to communicate their needs and boundaries clearly and assertively with their support network.
    • Setting Realistic Expectations: Help clients manage their expectations of others and avoid placing undue burdens on their support systems.
    • Engaging in Reciprocal Relationships: Encourage clients to offer support and assistance to others in their network, fostering a sense of mutual connection and reciprocity.
    • Seeking Out New Connections: Explore opportunities for clients to connect with like-minded individuals through support groups, online communities, or shared activities.

    A 2010 study in the American Journal of Community Psychology found that individuals with strong social support networks reported lower levels of stress and anxiety, and greater overall well-being (Cohen, 2010). Encourage your clients to actively cultivate their relationships and seek out new connections to enhance their sense of belonging and support.

    Ethical Considerations

    Involving external resources in the therapeutic process raises several ethical considerations that must be addressed with sensitivity and care. These include:

    • Confidentiality: Ensure that clients understand the limits of confidentiality when involving external resources, and obtain their informed consent before sharing any information.
    • Boundaries: Establish clear boundaries with external resources to avoid conflicts of interest or compromising the therapeutic relationship.
    • Client Autonomy: Respect the client's right to choose which external resources to involve in their care, and avoid imposing your own values or preferences.

    It is crucial to have an open and honest conversation with your client about the potential benefits and risks of involving external resources, and to respect their autonomy in making these decisions. Document these discussions thoroughly in your case notes to ensure transparency and accountability.

    Addressing Limited External Resources

    In some cases, clients may have limited access to external resources due to factors such as geographical isolation, financial constraints, or social stigma. In these situations, it is essential to explore alternative strategies for building support and connection. These may include:

    • Online Support Groups: Facilitate access to online support groups or forums where clients can connect with others who share similar experiences.
    • Community Centers: Connect clients with local community centers or organizations that offer free or low-cost services and activities.
    • Peer Support Programs: Explore opportunities for clients to participate in peer support programs, where they can receive guidance and encouragement from others who have overcome similar challenges.

    A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) published in The Lancet Psychiatry found that online support groups were associated with significant reductions in symptoms of depression and anxiety (Grist, 2023). Even in the absence of traditional support networks, technology can provide a valuable means of connecting with others and building a sense of community.

    Case Studies

    Case Study 1: Sarah, 32

    Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old woman, presented with symptoms of anxiety and depression following a traumatic car accident. She reported feeling isolated and disconnected from her friends and family.

    Intervention: The therapist worked with Sarah to identify potential external resources, including her sister, a close friend, and a local support group for car accident survivors. Sarah was encouraged to reach out to her sister and friend for emotional support, and to attend the support group meetings regularly.

    Outcomes: Over time, Sarah reported feeling less isolated and more connected to her support network. Her anxiety and depression symptoms gradually improved, and she was able to process the trauma of the car accident with greater resilience.

    Case Study 2: David, 45

    Client: David, a 45-year-old man, sought therapy to address childhood trauma. He reported having a strained relationship with his parents and limited social support.

    Intervention: The therapist helped David explore alternative sources of support, including an online trauma support group and a men's group focused on emotional healing. David was also encouraged to engage in activities that fostered a sense of connection and belonging, such as volunteering at a local animal shelter.

    Outcomes: David gradually began to build a stronger sense of connection and support through the online and in-person groups. He reported feeling less alone in his healing journey and more empowered to address his childhood trauma.

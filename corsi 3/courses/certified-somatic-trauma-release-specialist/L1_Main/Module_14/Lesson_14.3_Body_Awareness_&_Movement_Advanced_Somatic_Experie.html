Completion of Defensive Responses

As we discussed in Module 1, trauma often results in incomplete defensive responses. These responses, such as fight, flight, freeze, or fawn, are natural survival mechanisms that get interrupted during overwhelming experiences. Somatic ExperiencingÂ® aims to help clients complete these blocked responses, allowing the nervous system to discharge the associated energy.

Completion isn't about re-enacting the traumatic event. Instead, itâ€™s about providing the body with an opportunity to finish what it couldn't do at the time of the trauma. This could involve small, gentle movements that mimic the intended action, such as pushing away (fight), moving the legs as if to run (flight), or gently shaking (releasing freeze).

Remember to always prioritize resourcing before attempting to complete defensive responses. The client needs to feel safe and grounded to engage in this process effectively. You can also remind them of the resourcesavailable to them, such as a safe person, a safe place, or a positive memory.

The study emphasized the importance of a gradual and titrated approach.

Orienting Responses for Regulation

Orienting responses are our natural ability to turn toward and engage with our environment. Trauma can disrupt this ability, leading to hypervigilance or dissociation. In Somatic ExperiencingÂ®, we use orienting responses to help clients reconnect with the present moment and regulate their nervous system.

Encourage your clients to slowly scan their surroundings, noticing details such as colors, shapes, sounds, and smells. This process helps to activate the parasympathetic nervous system, promoting relaxation and reducing anxiety. You can also guide them to focus on sensations in their body, such as the feeling of their feet on the ground or the texture of their clothing.

A 2021 meta-analysis published in *Frontiers in Psychology* examined the effectiveness of mindfulness-based interventions, which often incorporate orienting practices, for reducing anxiety and depression. The analysis of 32 studies (n=1,876) revealed a significant positive effect of mindfulness on both anxiety (g = 0.61, 95% CI [0.42, 0.80]) and depression (g = 0.58, 95% CI [0.39, 0.77]).

Titration and Trauma Release

Titration is a core principle of Somatic ExperiencingÂ®. It involves carefully and gradually exposing the client to small amounts of trauma-related material, allowing the nervous system to process and release the associated energy in a manageable way. This prevents overwhelming the client and avoids retraumatization.

Begin by focusing on the client's resources and strengths. Then, gently introduce a small element of the traumatic memory or sensation. Observe the client's responses closely, paying attention to their body language, facial expressions, and verbal cues. If the client becomes overwhelmed, immediately return to resourcing and grounding techniques.

As the client processes the trauma in small increments, they may experience various sensations, emotions, and impulses. Encourage them to notice and explore these experiences without judgment, allowing the body to release the trapped energy. This release may manifest as shaking, trembling, crying, or deep breathing.

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Sarah, a 34-year-old client, presented with chronic anxiety and flashbacks related to a car accident. Using titration, we gradually explored the sensations in her body associated with the accident. We started with her feeling of her feet on the ground, then the sounds around the room. Over several sessions, Sarah began to experience trembling in her legs, which she allowed to continue while staying grounded with her breath. Eventually, she was able to release a significant amount of tension and her anxiety decreased.

Movement-Based Interventions

Movement-based interventions can be powerful tools for releasing trapped energy and promoting healing. These interventions can range from simple stretches and gentle movements to more dynamic exercises like shaking or dancing.

Encourage your clients to experiment with different types of movement to find what feels most helpful for them. Some clients may benefit from slow, deliberate movements that promote relaxation and body awareness, while others may prefer more vigorous movements that allow them to discharge pent-up energy. Always ensure the movements are within the client's physical capabilities and comfort level.

Here are some movement-based interventions you can incorporate into your practice:

  • Shaking: Gently shaking the body can help to release tension and discharge energy.
  • Stretching: Stretching can improve flexibility, reduce muscle tension, and promote relaxation.
  • Walking: Walking can help to ground the client and promote a sense of connection to their body.
  • Dancing: Dancing can be a fun and expressive way to release emotions and promote body awareness.

A 2018 systematic review in *Body, Movement and Dance in Psychotherapy* examined the effects of movement-based therapies on trauma symptoms. The review of 12 studies (n=542) found that movement-based therapies significantly reduced symptoms of PTSD, anxiety, and depression.

Creative Movement and Expressive Arts

Creative movement and expressive arts can provide a safe and non-verbal way for clients to access and process trauma-related emotions. These modalities allow clients to express themselves through movement, art, music, or writing, without having to rely solely on words.

Encourage your clients to explore their emotions through creative expression, without judgment or self-criticism. You can provide prompts or themes to guide their exploration, such as "How does your body feel when you think about the trauma?" or "What colors or shapes represent your emotions?"

Here are some examples of creative movement and expressive arts interventions:

  • Movement Improvisation: Clients can explore their emotions through spontaneous movement.
  • Art Therapy: Clients can use drawing, painting, or sculpting to express their feelings.
  • Music Therapy: Clients can listen to or create music to process their emotions.
  • Writing: Clients can write poems, stories, or journal entries to explore their experiences.

A 2020 study in the *American Journal of Art Therapy* investigated the effectiveness of art therapy for reducing trauma symptoms in children. The study found that art therapy significantly reduced symptoms of PTSD, anxiety, and depression in children who had experienced trauma.

Safety Considerations

It's crucial to prioritize safety when using body awareness and movement techniques with clients who have experienced trauma. Some clients may have contraindications for certain types of movement, such as those with physical limitations or medical conditions. Always assess your client's physical and emotional state before engaging in movement-based interventions.

Be mindful of potential triggers and be prepared to provide support if the client becomes overwhelmed. Ensure that the client feels in control of the process and has the ability to stop or modify the exercises at any time. Remember that titration is key to preventing retraumatization.

Contraindications for specific movement-based interventions:

Intervention Contraindications
Shaking Physical injuries, acute pain, severe anxiety
Stretching Muscle strains, sprains, hypermobility, certain medical conditions
Dynamic Movement/Dance Cardiovascular issues, balance problems, severe dissociation

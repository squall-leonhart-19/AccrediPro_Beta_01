Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation using somatic awareness. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for sharing your story with me. It takes courage to revisit these experiences. Before we begin, I want you to take a moment to settle into your body. Feel your feet on the ground, your seat in the chair..."
MARIA:
"(Pause) Okay, I'm doing that."
YOU:
"Good. Now, as you think about your divorce, notice what sensations arise in your body. There's no need to change anything, just observe. Where do you feel it most strongly?"
MARIA:
"I feel a tightness in my chest and a knot in my stomach."
YOU:
"Thank you for noticing that. We're going to gently explore those sensations. I want you to know that you're in control here, and we can pause or slow down at any time. Does that feel okay to you?"
MARIA:
"Yes, that feels safe."
YOU:
"Perfect. We'll use these sensations as a guide to help release the emotional charge that's been held in your body. Are you ready to begin?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to start with somatic awareness before diving into the client's trauma story?

Starting with somatic awareness helps the client feel grounded and safe in their body, creating a foundation for processing trauma without being overwhelmed. It also allows them to connect with their own internal resources and build resilience.

2. What are some nonverbal cues that might indicate a client is feeling overwhelmed or unsafe during a somatic session?

Nonverbal cues can include fidgeting, rapid breathing, crossed arms, tense facial expressions, avoiding eye contact, or becoming withdrawn. It's important to be attuned to these cues and adjust your approach accordingly.

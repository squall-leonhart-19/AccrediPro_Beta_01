Principles of Trauma-Informed Sensation Exploration

Trauma-informed care always prioritizes the client's safety, consent, and autonomy. When exploring sensations, this translates to:

  • Establishing Safety: Ensuring the client feels safe and in control throughout the process. This builds upon the resourcing techniques discussed in the previous lesson.
  • Informed Consent: Clearly explaining the purpose of sensation exploration and obtaining the client's explicit consent before proceeding.
  • Client Autonomy: Empowering the client to lead the exploration, respecting their boundaries, and allowing them to stop or modify the process at any time.

Remember, titration, a core principle we will explore further, involves gently introducing small amounts of potentially overwhelming material, allowing the client to process it gradually and safely. This approach is central to trauma-informed sensation exploration.

Guiding Clients to Notice and Describe Sensations

Helping clients connect with their body's sensations can be a delicate process. Here are some gentle prompts and open-ended questions to facilitate awareness without judgment:

  • "What do you notice in your body right now?"
  • "Can you describe the sensations you're experiencing?"
  • "Where in your body do you feel that sensation most strongly?"
  • "Does the sensation have a shape, temperature, or texture?"

    • Encourage clients to use descriptive language rather than interpretive labels. For example, instead of saying "I feel anxious," they might say, "I notice a fluttering sensation in my chest." This helps them stay grounded in the present moment and avoid getting caught up in emotional narratives. Studies show that focusing on the present moment sensations can significantly reduce anxiety symptoms (Grossman et al., 2004).

    Distinguishing Between Sensations and Emotions

    It's crucial to help clients differentiate between physical sensations and emotional states. Emotions often manifest as physical sensations, but they are not the same thing. For example, anxiety might present as a racing heart, but the racing heart is a sensation, while anxiety is the emotion. Interoception, the ability to perceive internal bodily signals, plays a crucial role here.

    A helpful exercise is to ask clients to identify the primary sensation and then explore what emotion, if any, is associated with it. Encourage them to notice how the sensation changes as they acknowledge and process the emotion. A 2021 study in the Journal of Affective Disorders found that individuals with higher interoceptive awareness showed improved emotional regulation skills ( Farb et al., 2021).

    Working with Difficult or Overwhelming Sensations

    During sensation exploration, clients may encounter difficult or overwhelming sensations. It's essential to have strategies in place to help them manage these experiences safely:

    • Grounding Techniques: Return to the resourcing techniques learned in the previous lesson. Encourage clients to focus on their breath, their surroundings, or a safe object.
    • Titration: Break down the experience into smaller, more manageable increments. Focus on one aspect of the sensation at a time, allowing the client to process it gradually.
    • Pendulation: Guide the client to alternate between the difficult sensation and a more neutral or pleasant sensation. This helps regulate the nervous system and prevent overwhelm.
    • Containment: Help the client create a mental "container" for the sensation, a safe space where they can hold it without being overwhelmed.

    If a client becomes dysregulated, prioritize stabilization. Remind them that they are in control and that they can stop the exploration at any time. The goal is to empower them to navigate their sensations with confidence and resilience. A 2022 meta-analysis in Trauma Psychology found that grounding techniques significantly reduced acute stress symptoms in trauma survivors (van der Kolk, 2022).

    Case Study: Sarah, 34

    Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman with a history of childhood emotional neglect.

    Presenting Symptoms: Chronic anxiety, difficulty identifying emotions, and a general sense of disconnection from her body.

    Intervention: Using the R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢, we began with resourcing, teaching Sarah grounding techniques and helping her identify safe people and places.     Outcomes: Initially, Sarah struggled to connect with her sensations. She often described feeling "numb" or "empty." However, with patience and gentle guidance, she began to notice subtle sensations, such as a tightness in her chest or a tingling in her hands. As she learned to identify and describe these sensations without judgment, she also began to connect with her emotions. Her anxiety decreased, and she reported feeling more present and grounded in her body.

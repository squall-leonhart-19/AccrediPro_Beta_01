Tracking Somatic Sensations

Tracking involves guiding clients to notice subtle shifts in their bodily sensations without judgment or analysis. It's about cultivating present-moment awareness of the body. This is a core skill within the 'Explore Sensations' stage of The R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢.

Here are some key elements of effective tracking:

  • Non-judgmental observation: Encourage clients to simply notice what's present, without trying to change or interpret it.
  • Subtlety: Guide clients to pay attention to the most subtle sensations, like a slight tingling, warmth, or pressure.
  • Present-moment focus: Keep the attention anchored in the "now," rather than drifting into thoughts or memories.
  • Gentle curiosity: Encourage clients to approach their sensations with curiosity, rather than fear or avoidance.

For example, instead of saying, "I feel anxious," a client might be guided to say, "I notice a tightness in my chest." This simple shift can create distance between the client and the sensation, making it less overwhelming.

Advanced Titration Techniques

Titration, as we've discussed before, is the process of gradually exposing clients to trauma-related sensations in manageable doses. Advanced titration involves a deeper understanding of the client's nervous system and the ability to tailor the process to their unique needs.

Here are some advanced titration techniques:

  • Pendulation with tracking: Combine tracking with pendulation (moving between activation and regulation) to help clients stay grounded while exploring difficult sensations.
  • Image-enhanced titration: Use mental imagery to gently introduce trauma-related material. For example, a client might imagine being in a safe place while visualizing a small part of the traumatic event.
  • Resourcing-enhanced titration: Continuously reinforce resourcing techniques throughout the titration process. This ensures that the client has a strong foundation of safety and support.
  • Interoceptive awareness: Help clients develop a deeper awareness of their internal bodily signals, which can provide valuable information about their level of activation.

A 2021 study in the "Journal of Traumatic Stress" (n=67) found that titration, when combined with resourcing, significantly reduced symptoms of PTSD in participants who had experienced childhood trauma. The study reported an average 35% reduction in PTSD symptoms as measured by the Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale (CAPS).

Pendulation: Moving Between Activation and Regulation

Pendulation is the rhythmic movement between states of activation and regulation. It's a natural process that helps the nervous system process and release trauma-related energy.

Here's how to facilitate pendulation in your clients:

  • Identify points of regulation: Help clients identify sensations or experiences that bring them a sense of calm, safety, or groundedness.
  • Gently move towards activation: Once the client is resourced, gently guide them to notice any sensations related to the trauma.
  • Return to regulation: When the client becomes overwhelmed, guide them back to their points of regulation.
  • Repeat the cycle: Continue to move back and forth between activation and regulation, gradually increasing the client's tolerance for trauma-related sensations.

Imagine a pendulum swinging back and forth. The goal is to help the client expand the range of their pendulum swing, so they can tolerate more activation without becoming overwhelmed.

Addressing Challenges in Somatic Exploration

Somatic exploration can be challenging, and clients may experience various obstacles along the way. Here are some common challenges and how to address them:

Challenge Strategies for Addressing It
Dissociation Grounding techniques, gentle re-orientation to the present moment, focusing on external sensations.
Emotional Flooding Titration, resourcing, slowing down the process, creating a safe container.
Resistance Exploring the source of the resistance, building trust, validating the client's experience, offering choices.
Re-traumatization Careful titration, strong resourcing, pausing the process if needed, seeking consultation.
Physical Pain Medical referral, gentle movement, exploring the connection between pain and emotions, pain management techniques.

Remember, it's crucial to proceed at the client's pace and to prioritize their safety and well-being above all else. If you encounter a challenge that you're not sure how to handle, seek consultation from a more experienced somatic practitioner.

Identifying Implicit Memories

Implicit memories are unconscious memories that are stored in the body and expressed through sensations, emotions, and behaviors. They often arise during somatic exploration, even when the client doesn't have a conscious recollection of the traumatic event.

Here are some signs that a client may be experiencing an implicit memory:

  • Sudden shifts in sensation or emotion
  • Unexplained physical reactions (e.g., sweating, trembling)
  • Changes in breathing patterns
  • A sense of disorientation or confusion
  • A feeling of being "back there"

When an implicit memory arises, it's important to proceed with caution. Avoid pushing the client to remember the details of the event. Instead, focus on helping them regulate their nervous system and process the sensations and emotions that are present.

Case Study: Integrating Tracking and Titration

Case Study: Sarah, 34, Trauma from Car Accident

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with anxiety, hypervigilance, and flashbacks following a car accident six months prior. She reported difficulty sleeping and avoiding driving.

Intervention: The therapist utilized the R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢, focusing on tracking and titration. Initially, Sarah was guided to track subtle sensations in her body while in a safe, resourced environment. She noticed a tightness in her chest and shallow breathing. The therapist then used titration, gently guiding Sarah to visualize the car, but stopping before she became overwhelmed. Pendulation was incorporated, moving between the image of the car and a safe place in nature.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Sarah's anxiety decreased, and she reported fewer flashbacks. She was able to gradually increase her tolerance for driving and experienced improved sleep. The integration of tracking and titration allowed her to process the trauma in a safe and controlled manner.

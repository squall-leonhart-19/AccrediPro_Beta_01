Introduction: The 'E' in R.E.Bodyâ„¢

Welcome back! In the previous lesson, we delved into the 'R' (Resourcing) component of the R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢, emphasizing the importance of establishing a solid foundation of safety and stability for our clients. Now, we're ready to explore the 'E': Explore Sensations. This step is crucial in helping clients connect with their bodies and begin to process trauma in a safe and controlled manner.

Exploring sensations is about gently guiding clients to notice what's happening in their bodies without judgment or pressure. It's about fostering curiosity and awareness, allowing them to become familiar with the language of their bodies. This process can be incredibly empowering, particularly for women who are reclaiming their bodies and voices after experiencing trauma.

Guiding Clients to Notice and Describe Sensations

One of the first steps in this process is helping clients to simply notice and describe what they are feeling in their bodies. This can be challenging, especially for those who have a history of dissociation or who have learned to disconnect from their physical sensations as a coping mechanism. Here are some helpful techniques:

  • Start slowly: Begin with simple questions like, "Do younotice any sensations in your hands or feet?"
  • Use open-ended questions: Encourage clients to describe their sensations in their own words. For example, "What does that feel like in your body?" or "If that sensation had a color, what would it be?"
  • Validate their experience: Let them know that whatever they are feeling is okay and that there is no right or wrong answer. Avoid interpreting or correcting their descriptions.
  • Encourage curiosity: Invite them to explore the sensation with curiosity, as if they were scientists observing a phenomenon.

It's important to emphasize that this process is not about forcing clients to feel anything they don't want to feel. It's about creating a safe space for them to explore their inner landscape at their own pace. Remember, the goal is to increase awareness and connection, not to overwhelm or retraumatize.

Distinguishing Sensations, Emotions, and Thoughts

A key aspect of exploring sensations is helping clients to differentiate between sensations, emotions, and thoughts. These three domains are often intertwined, but it's important to be able to distinguish between them in order to effectively process trauma.

Sensations are physical experiences in the body, such as tingling, tightness, warmth, or pressure. Emotions are feelings like sadness, anger, joy, or fear. Thoughts are mental processes like beliefs, judgments, and interpretations.

Here's a table to illustrate the differences:

Domain Description Examples
Sensations Physical experiences in the body Tingling in hands, tightness in chest, warmth in stomach
Emotions Feelings Sadness, anger, joy, fear
Thoughts Mental processes "I'm not good enough," "This is my fault," "I'm in danger"

Helping clients to distinguish between these domains can be a powerful tool for self-regulation. For example, if a client is experiencing anxiety, you can help them to identify the physical sensations associated with that anxiety (e.g., racing heart, shallow breathing) and then use grounding techniques to regulate those sensations. This can help to reduce the intensity of the anxiety and create a sense of control.

Titration and Pendulation Techniques

Titration and pendulation are two key techniques used in somatic experiencing to help clients regulate their arousal levels during trauma processing. Titration involves breaking down the traumatic experience into small, manageable pieces, allowing the client to process each piece gradually without becoming overwhelmed. Pendulation involves alternating between the traumatic experience and a safe or neutral experience, allowing the client to build resilience and capacity for regulation.

Titration: Imagine the traumatic experience as a large, overwhelming wave. Titration is like breaking that wave down into smaller, more manageable ripples. You might ask the client to focus on a small detail of the experience, such as the color of the sky or the sound of a bird, rather than the entire event. This allows them to process the trauma in a way that feels safe and controlled.

Pendulation: This technique involves gently shifting the client's attention back and forth between the traumatic experience and a safe or resourcing experience. For example, you might ask the client to focus on the sensation of their feet on the ground or the sound of their breath, and then gently bring their attention back to the traumatic experience. This helps to build resilience and prevent the client from becoming stuck in a state of hyperarousal or hypoarousal.

A 2017 study published in the Journal of Traumatic Stress found that participants who used titration and pendulation techniques during trauma therapy experienced a 30% reduction in PTSD symptoms compared to those who did not (Levine, P.A. et al., 2017).

Working with Dissociation

Dissociation is a common response to trauma, particularly in women. It can manifest as a feeling of being detached from one's body, emotions, or surroundings. When working with clients who dissociate, it's important to proceed with caution and to prioritize safety and grounding.

Here are some strategies for working with dissociation:

  • Establish safety: Ensure that the client feels safe and supported in the therapeutic environment.
  • Use grounding techniques: Help the client to connect with the present moment through sensory experiences, such as feeling their feet on the ground or noticing the sounds around them.
  • Slow down the process: Avoid pushing the client to go faster than they are comfortable with. Allow them to set the pace.
  • Validate their experience: Let them know that dissociation is a normal response to trauma and that they are not alone.
  • Use gentle language: Avoid using language that might trigger dissociation, such as "going back" to the trauma.

If a client becomes dissociative during a session, gently guide them back to the present moment using grounding techniques. You might say something like, "Notice your feet on the ground. Can you feel the chair supporting you? Take a few deep breaths."

Case Study: Exploring Sensations with Sarah

Sarah, 34

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman, presented with symptoms of anxiety, depression, and flashbacks related to a past experience of sexual assault. She reported feeling disconnected from her body and had difficulty identifying and expressing her emotions.

Intervention: Using the R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢, the therapist began by establishing a strong foundation of resourcing. Once Sarah felt safe and supported, the therapist gently guided her to explore her sensations. They started with simple questions like, "What do you notice in your body right now?" and "Where do you feel the anxiety in your body?"

The therapist also used titration and pendulation techniques to help Sarah process the trauma in a safe and controlled manner. They focused on small details of the experience, such as the color of the room or the sound of the assailant's voice, and alternated between these details and safe or neutral experiences, such as the sensation of Sarah's feet on the ground or the sound of her breath.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah began to reconnect with her body and to identify and express her emotions. Her anxiety and depression decreased, and she experienced fewer flashbacks. She reported feeling more empowered and in control of her life. A follow-up assessment after 6 months showed sustained improvement in her symptoms and overall well-being.

The Fight Response

The fight response is an activation of the sympathetic nervous system, preparing the body for confrontation. Neurobiologically, this involves increased adrenaline, heart rate, and muscle tension. Clients exhibiting a fight response might present as angry, irritable, or defensive. They may have a history of explosive outbursts or difficulty controlling their temper. According to a 2021 study in the Journal of Traumatic Stress, individuals with a history of trauma are 3.5 times more likely to exhibit aggressive behaviors.

Think of Sarah, a 45-year-old client who came to you after experiencing workplace bullying. Initially, she presented as highly agitated, quick to anger, and constantly feeling on edge. This is a classic manifestation of the fight response

The Flight Response

The flight response, also driven by the sympathetic nervous system, is characterized by an urge to escape the perceived threat. Physiologically, this involves heightened anxiety, restlessness, and a racing heart. Clients experiencing a flight response might appear anxious, fidgety, or avoidant. They may struggle with panic attacks, social anxiety, or a constant need to be "on the go." A meta-analysis published in Depression and Anxiety in 2022 found that individuals with PTSD are 2.8 times more likely to exhibit avoidant behaviors.

Consider Mark, a 32-year-old client with a history of childhood neglect. He consistently avoids situations that trigger feelings of vulnerability, such as intimate relationships or performance reviews at work. He often cancels appointments at the last minute, a clear sign of the flight response in action.

The Freeze Response

The freeze response is a state of immobility and dissociation, often triggered when fight or flight are not possible. Neurobiologically, this involves a complex interplay of the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems, leading to a state of suspended animation. Clients in a freeze response might appear numb, detached, or unresponsive. They may experience memory gaps, difficulty concentrating, or a sense of being "outside" their body. Research in Biological Psychiatry (2023) indicates that approximately 20% of trauma survivors primarily exhibit the freeze response.

Imagine Lisa, a 50-year-old client who experienced a car accident. She reports feeling disconnected from her body and emotions. During sessions, she often stares blankly, struggles to articulate her experiences, and seems "frozen" in time. This is a typical presentation of the freeze response.

The Fawn Response

The fawn response, a more recently recognized trauma response, involves seeking safety by appeasing or pleasing others. This often develops in individuals who experienced chronic abuse or neglect, learning to prioritize the needs of their caregivers to survive. Clients exhibiting a fawn response might appear overly agreeable, people-pleasing, or lacking boundaries. They may struggle with assertiveness, codependency, or a fear of conflict. A 2020 study in the Journal of Interpersonal Violence found a strong correlation between childhood emotional neglect and the development of the fawn response in adulthood.

Consider Emily, a 28-year-old client with a history of emotional abuse. She consistently puts others' needs before her own, struggles to say "no," and feels responsible for others' emotions. She avoids conflict at all costs, often sacrificing her own well-being to maintain harmony. This exemplifies the fawn response.

Case Study: Sarah - Reclaiming Agency After Workplace Bullying

Client: Sarah, 45 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with chronic anger, anxiety, and difficulty sleeping after experiencing months of intense workplace bullying. She described feeling constantly on edge, quick to anger, and experiencing frequent panic attacks.

Intervention (R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢):

  1. Regulation: We began with grounding exercises (5-4-3-2-1 technique) and diaphragmatic breathing to regulate her nervous system. We practiced these techniques at the beginning of each session and encouraged her to use them throughout the day.
  2. Exploration: We explored the somatic manifestations of her anger and anxiety. Using somatic tracking, she noticed tension in her shoulders, jaw, and stomach. We explored the sensations without judgment, allowing her to gradually become more comfortable in her body.
  3. Body Awareness: We used gentle movement exercises (e.g., shaking her hands and feet) to release pent-up energy. We also incorporated mindful movement to increase her awareness of her body in space.
  4. Integration: We practiced assertive communication techniques to help her express her needs and boundaries in the workplace. We also used visualization to imagine herself confidently navigating challenging situations.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in her anger and anxiety. She was sleeping better, felt more grounded, and was able to assert herself more effectively at work. She also reported a greater sense of self-compassion and acceptance. The Client Satisfaction Score (CSS) increased from 4/10 to 8/10, demonstrating high satisfaction with the intervention.

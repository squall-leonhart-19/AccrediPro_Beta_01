Tracking: Observing Internal Sensations

Tracking involves guiding clients to mindfully observe their internal sensations and bodily responses as they recall a traumatic event or experience. This process is not about reliving the trauma, but rather about noticing the subtle shifts and changes occurring within the body. It's like becoming a "body detective", noticing clues and signals that the body is sending.

Key aspects of tracking include:

  • Gentle Guidance: Encouraging the client to notice sensations without judgment.
  • Present Moment Focus: Anchoring the client in the "now" to prevent overwhelm.
  • Descriptive Language: Helping the client articulate sensations using vivid and specific words (e.g., "tingling," "tightness," "heat").

A 2017 study published in the Journal of Trauma & Dissociation found that mindful body awareness practices, similar to tracking, significantly reduced symptoms of PTSD in participants (p < 0.01) (van der Kolk, 2017).

Felt Sense: Accessing Embodied Experience

The felt sense, a term coined by Eugene Gendlin, refers to the holistic, embodied experience of a situation or event. It's not just a physical sensation, but a complex, pre-verbal knowing that arises from deep within the body. Accessing the felt sense allows clients to tap into a profound source of information about their trauma.

Guiding clients to access their felt sense involves:

  • Inviting Attention: Asking the client to turn their attention inward and notice what arises in their body.
  • Waiting and Listening: Encouraging the client to be patient and allow the felt sense to emerge without forcing it.
  • Symbolic Language: Helping the client find words or images that resonate with their felt sense.

For example, a client might describe their felt sense as a "knot in my stomach" or a "weight on my chest." These metaphors can provide valuable insights into the emotional and energetic blockages associated with the trauma.

Research indicates that accessing the felt sense can enhance emotional processing and promote self-regulation. A meta-analysis by Hendricks et al. (2019) in the Journal of Counseling Psychology found a moderate to large effect size (d = 0.68, 95% CI [0.45, 0.91]) for interventions that incorporate felt sense awareness in reducing anxiety symptoms.

Facilitating Trauma Processing

Tracking and accessing the felt sense are powerful tools for facilitating trauma processing. By guiding clients to gently explore their embodied experience of trauma, we can help them release trapped energy, resolve unfinished responses, and restore a sense of wholeness.

Key principles for facilitating trauma processing with these techniques include:

  • Titration: Breaking down the traumatic experience into small, manageable pieces.
  • Pendulation: Alternating between the traumatic material and safe, resourcing experiences.
  • Completion: Supporting the client to complete defensive responses that were interrupted during the trauma.

For instance, if a client was unable to fight back during an assault, we might guide them to imagine completing that defensive action in a safe and controlled way. This can help release the pent-up energy and restore a sense of agency.

Case Study: Applying Tracking and Felt Sense

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, 34 years old, presenting with anxiety and flashbacks related to a car accident six months prior.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported difficulty sleeping, intrusive thoughts of the accident, and panic attacks when driving. She felt disconnected from her body and described feeling "numb."

Intervention: Using tracking and felt sense, the therapist guided Sarah to gently explore her bodily sensations while recalling the accident. Sarah initially reported feeling "frozen" and "empty" in her chest. The therapist encouraged her to stay with these sensations without judgment. Over several sessions, Sarah began to notice subtle shifts in her body. She described a "tightness" in her shoulders and a "fluttering" in her stomach. The therapist helped her articulate these sensations and explore their connection to the accident.

The therapist then guided Sarah to access her felt sense of the accident. Sarah described it as a "whirlwind of chaos and fear." The therapist supported her to find words and images that resonated with this felt sense. Sarah eventually visualized herself slamming on the brakes and bracing for impact. The therapist encouraged her to complete this defensive response in her imagination, allowing her body to shake and tremble. As Sarah completed this response, she reported feeling a release of tension and a sense of calm.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety and flashbacks. She was able to drive without experiencing panic attacks and felt more connected to her body. She described feeling "grounded" and "present" in her life. This case underscores the power of tracking and felt sense in facilitating trauma processing and restoring a sense of wholeness.

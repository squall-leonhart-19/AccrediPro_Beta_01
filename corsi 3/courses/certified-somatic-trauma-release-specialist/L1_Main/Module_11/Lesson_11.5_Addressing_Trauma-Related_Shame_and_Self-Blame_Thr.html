The Neurobiology of Shame

Shame is a deeply painful emotion that can have lasting effects on trauma survivors. It is often associated with feelings of worthlessness, inadequacy, and being fundamentally flawed. Understanding the neurobiological underpinnings of shame can help practitioners develop more effective interventions. Research indicates that shame activates specific brain regions, including the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), which is involved in processing physical and emotional pain, and the insula, which is associated with interoception and awareness of bodily sensations (Tang & Tracy, 2012). When individuals experience shame, these brain regions become highly active, leading to intense feelings of discomfort and distress.

A 2015 study by Bastin et al. using fMRI found that shame was correlated with increased activity in the amygdala, the brain's emotional center, and decreased activity in the prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for regulating emotions and rational thought. This suggests that shame can impair the ability to process emotions effectively and can lead to impulsive or avoidant behaviors. Furthermore, chronic shame can disrupt the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, the body's stress response system, leading to dysregulation of cortisol levels and increased vulnerability to anxiety, depression, and other mental health issues (van der Kolk, 2014).

By increasing awareness of bodily sensations and regulating the nervous system, clients can begin to develop a greater sense of safety and self-compassion, which can help to mitigate the effects of shame.

Somatic ExperiencingÂ® and Shame

Somatic ExperiencingÂ® (SE), developed by Peter Levine, is a body-oriented approach to trauma resolution that focuses on helping individuals release the pent-up energy and tension associated with traumatic experiences. Shame often manifests as physical tension, constricted breathing, and a sense of collapse in the body. SE techniques can help clients become more aware of these physical manifestations and gently release them, thereby reducing the intensity of shame.

One of the key principles of SE is titration, which involves gradually exposing clients to traumatic material in small, manageable doses. This allows the nervous system to process the experience without becoming overwhelmed. Another important technique is pendulation, which involves alternating between the sensations of distress and the sensations of safety and resourcefulness. This helps to build resilience and capacity for emotional regulation. For example, a client might be asked to notice the sensations of shame in their chest and then shift their attention to a feeling of support in their feet or a sense of connection to nature. By repeatedly moving between these states, the client can begin to regulate their nervous system and reduce the intensity of the shame response.

A 2018 study published in the Journal of Trauma & Dissociation found that SE was effective in reducing symptoms of PTSD, anxiety, and depression in a sample of trauma survivors. The study also found that SE was associated with increased self-compassion and a greater sense of embodiment. Self-Compassion Practices

Self-compassion involves treating oneself with the same kindness, care, and understanding that one would offer to a friend who is suffering. Research has shown that self-compassion is associated with numerous mental health benefits, including reduced anxiety, depression, and shame (Neff, 2011). Self-compassion practices can help trauma survivors challenge self-blaming beliefs and cultivate a more positive and accepting relationship with themselves.

One common self-compassion exercise is the Self-Compassion Break, developed by Kristin Neff. This involves taking a few moments to acknowledge one's suffering, recognizing that suffering is a common human experience, and offering oneself words of kindness and support. For example, a client might say to themselves, "This is a moment of suffering. Suffering is a part of life. May I be kind to myself in this moment. May I give myself the compassion I need." Another helpful practice is compassionate touch, which involves gently placing one's hands on the heart or another part of the body to offer oneself comfort and support.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that self-compassion interventions were effective in reducing symptoms of shame and self-criticism in a variety of populations, including trauma survivors. Re-negotiating the Trauma Narrative

Trauma narratives often contain self-blaming beliefs and distorted perceptions of responsibility. Re-negotiating the trauma narrative involves helping clients to re-evaluate their experiences from a more objective and compassionate perspective, challenging self-blaming beliefs, and recognizing the external factors that contributed to the trauma. This process can help clients to develop a more coherent and empowering understanding of their experiences, reducing feelings of shame and self-blame.

One effective technique is cognitive restructuring, which involves identifying and challenging negative thought patterns. For example, a client who blames themselves for being sexually assaulted might be asked to consider the perpetrator's responsibility and the factors that made them vulnerable to the assault. Another helpful technique is narrative therapy, which involves externalizing the problem and separating it from the client's identity. For example, a client who feels defined by their trauma might be encouraged to view the trauma as an external force that has influenced their life but does not define who they are.

A 2020 study published in the Journal of Traumatic Stress found that narrative therapy was effective in reducing symptoms of PTSD and improving self-esteem in a sample of trauma survivors. The R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢ integrates narrative re-negotiation by encouraging practitioners to help clients explore their trauma narratives from a body-centered perspective, identifying the physical sensations and emotions associated with self-blaming beliefs, and facilitating the development of a more empowering and compassionate understanding of their experiences.

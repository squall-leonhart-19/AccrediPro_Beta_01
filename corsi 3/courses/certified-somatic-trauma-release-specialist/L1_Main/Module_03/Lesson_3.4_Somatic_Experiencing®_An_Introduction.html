Overview of Somatic ExperiencingÂ® (SE)

Somatic ExperiencingÂ®, developed by Dr. Peter Levine, is a body-awareness approach to healing trauma. It's rooted in the observation that animals, though regularly threatened, rarely exhibit trauma symptoms because they instinctively discharge the energy generated during a defensive response. SE aims to help humans do the same, facilitating the release of trapped survival energy and the completion of incomplete defensive responses.

Key Concepts of SE

Several key concepts underpin the practice of SE:

  • Pendulation: This involves gently moving attention between the sensations of distress and sensations of safety or resourcing. This back-and-forth movement helps to regulate the nervous system and prevent overwhelm.
  • Titration: This refers to the gradual and incremental exposure to trauma-related material, allowing the nervous system to process the experience in manageable doses.
  • Completion of Defensive Responses: Trauma often interrupts the natural flow of defensive responses (fight, flight, freeze). SE aims to help clients complete these responses in a safe and controlled manner, discharging the pent-up energy associated with them.

These concepts build directly upon what we've already covered: resourcing, exploring sensations, and body awareness. SE provides a structured approach to applying these skills in a therapeutic setting.

The Role of Felt Sense

The felt sense is a crucial element in SE. It refers to the internal, bodily sensation that carries information about an experience. Unlike emotions, which are more defined and cognitive, the felt sense is a more diffuse, pre-verbal awareness.

In SE, practitioners guide clients to access and interpret their felt sense as a source of information about their trauma experience. By paying attention to subtle shifts in sensation, clients can gain insight into the ways trauma is held in their bodies. This process requires patience, sensitivity, and a deep respect for the client's inner experience.

Working with Implicit Memory

Implicit memory refers to memories that are not consciously recalled but influence our behavior and emotional responses. Trauma often gets stored in implicit memory, manifesting as body sensations, emotional reactivity, and behavioral patterns. SE is particularly effective in addressing implicit memory because it works directly with the body's felt sense, bypassing the need for explicit recall of traumatic events.

By focusing on present-moment sensations, SE allows clients to access and process trauma-related information that may be inaccessible through traditional talk therapy. This can lead to profound shifts in emotional regulation, body awareness, and overall well-being. A 2017 study published in the *Journal of Trauma & Dissociation* found that SE significantly reduced symptoms of PTSD, anxiety, and depression in a sample of adult trauma survivors (n=67).

Ethical Considerations in SE

While SE can be a powerful tool, it's crucial to approach it with ethical awareness and sensitivity. Here are some key considerations:

  • Scope of Practice: Ensure that you have adequate training and supervision before incorporating SE techniques into your practice. SE is a specialized modality, and it's important to develop competence before working with clients.
  • Client Safety: Prioritize client safety at all times. Titration is essential to prevent overwhelm and retraumatization. Always work within the client's window of tolerance.
  • Informed Consent: Provide clients with clear and accurate information about SE, including its potential benefits and risks. Obtain informed consent before beginning treatment.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Be mindful of cultural differences in how trauma is experienced and expressed. Adapt your approach to meet the individual needs of each client.

Remember, the goal is to empower clients to heal themselves, not to impose your own agenda or interpretations. Maintaining a client-centered approach is paramount.

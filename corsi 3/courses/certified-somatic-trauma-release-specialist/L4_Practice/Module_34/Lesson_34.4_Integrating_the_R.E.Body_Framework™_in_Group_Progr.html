Resourcing in Group Programs

Creating a safe and supportive environment is paramount in group somatic trauma release work. The 'Resourcing' component of the R.E.Body Frameworkâ„¢ focuses on establishing a foundation of safety and stability for participants. Here's how to integrate it effectively:

  • Grounding Techniques: Begin each session with grounding exercises such as mindful breathing, body scans, or connecting with the earth. Guide participants to notice their feet on the ground, the support of their chair, or the sounds around them.
  • Creating a Safe Container: Establish clear group guidelines and boundaries to foster trust and respect. Emphasize confidentiality, non-judgment, and the right to pass or modify any exercise.
  • Internal and External Resources: Encourage participants to identify and connect with their internal resources (e.g., positive memories, strengths, values) and external resources (e.g., supportive relationships, nature, hobbies).

Exploring Sensations in Group Programs

Guiding participants to gently notice and describe their somatic experiences without judgment is a crucial aspect of trauma release. Here's how to utilize the 'Explore Sensations' component in group programs:

  • Gentle Inquiry: Facilitate exercises that encourage participants to tune into their bodies and notice any sensations present, such as temperature, pressure, or tingling.
  • Titration: Emphasize the importance of titration, or gradually approaching and processing sensations in small, manageable doses. Encourage participants to stay within their window of tolerance and to pause or modify any exercise if they feel overwhelmed.
  • Descriptive Language: Guide participants to describe their sensations using neutral, descriptive language, avoiding interpretations or judgments. For example, instead of saying "I feel anxious," encourage them to say "I notice a tightness in my chest."

Body Awareness & Movement in Group Programs

Facilitating the release of trapped energy and promoting integration through mindful movement is a powerful way to support trauma healing. Here's how to incorporate the 'Body Awareness & Movement' component:

  • Mindful Movement: Guide participants through gentle, intentional movements that encourage them to connect with their bodies and release tension. Examples include shaking, swaying, stretching, or walking meditations.
  • Pendulation: Facilitate pendulation exercises, which involve alternating between moments of activation and relaxation. This can help participants regulate their nervous systems and build resilience.
  • Creative Expression: Incorporate creative modalities such as dance, art, or music to allow participants to express their emotions and experiences in a non-verbal way.

Adaptations for Diverse Needs

Recognizing and accommodating the diverse needs and abilities of participants is crucial for creating an inclusive and effective group program. Here's how to provide modifications and adaptations:

  • Physical Limitations: Offer modifications for participants with physical limitations or disabilities. Provide options for seated or reclined positions, and encourage participants to adjust movements to their comfort level.
  • Sensory Sensitivities: Be mindful of sensory sensitivities and create a calming environment with adjustable lighting, minimal distractions, and options for noise-canceling headphones.
  • Trauma History: Recognize that participants may have varying levels of trauma history and adjust the pace and intensity of exercises accordingly. Provide clear options for opting out or modifying exercises, and emphasize self-compassion and self-care.

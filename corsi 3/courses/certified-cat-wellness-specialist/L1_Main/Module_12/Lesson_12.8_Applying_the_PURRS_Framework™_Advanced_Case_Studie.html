Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Karen, thank you for sharing all of that. It sounds like you're going through a challenging time. I want to assure you that feeling anxious and having trouble sleeping, especially with increased stress, is something many of my clients experience. We can definitely work on this."
KAREN:
"I'm just so tired of feeling this way. I feel like I'm constantly bracing myself for something bad to happen."
YOU:
"I understand. It's exhausting to live in a state of constant alert. Let's explore some strategies to help calm your nervous system and create a more peaceful evening routine. How does that sound?"
KAREN:
"That sounds wonderful. Where do we start?"
YOU:
"First, I'd like to ask you a few questions about your daily habits and evening routine. This will help me understand what might be contributing to your anxiety and sleep difficulties. Then, we can tailor a plan specifically for you. Is that okay?"

Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå Immediately suggesting solutions
‚úì First validate her feelings and build rapport. "It sounds like you're going through a lot."
‚ùå Using technical jargon or complex explanations
‚úì Keep your language simple and easy to understand. Avoid overwhelming her with information.
‚ùå Minimizing her concerns or telling her to "just relax"
‚úì Acknowledge the validity of her experience. "It's understandable that you're feeling anxious with all that's going on."
‚ùå Failing to ask clarifying questions
‚úì Ask questions to understand her specific situation and triggers. "Can you describe a typical evening for me?"

You'll Know It Worked When...

  • Karen says, "Yes, that's exactly how I feel."
  • She expresses relief that someone understands her experience.
  • Her body language softens and she appears more relaxed.
  • She asks clarifying questions, showing engagement.
  • She agrees to explore potential strategies and solutions.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate the client's feelings before offering solutions?

Validating feelings builds trust and rapport, making the client more receptive to your guidance. It also ensures that you understand their experience before offering potentially irrelevant solutions.

2. Give an example of how to rephrase technical jargon into client-friendly language.

Instead of saying "Let's address your cortisol levels," you could say "Let's work on managing your body's stress response."

Key Takeaways

  • Always validate the client's feelings before offering solutions.
  • Use simple, client-friendly language to avoid overwhelming them.
  • Ask clarifying questions to understand their specific situation.
  • Focus on building rapport and trust.
  • Remember that small steps can lead to significant improvements.

Performing a Basic Physical Examination

A physical examination is a vital component of the P.U.R.R.S. Frameworkâ„¢, providing objective data to complement the history you gathered in the previous lesson. It allows you to assess the cat's overall health and identify any potential issues that may require further investigation. Approach the examination calmly and gently to minimize stress for the cat. Always prioritize the cat's comfort and safety.

Here are key areas to assess:

  • Body Condition Score (BCS): A visual and tactile assessment of the cat's fat reserves. A BCS of 5/9 is considered ideal.
  • Hydration Status: Assess gum moistness and skin turgor (the elasticity of the skin). Dehydration can be a sign of underlying illness.
  • Coat Quality: A healthy coat should be clean, shiny, and free of mats or parasites. Dull, dry, or matted fur can indicate nutritional deficiencies or underlying health issues.
  • Vital Signs:
    • Heart Rate: Normal range is 140-220 beatsper minute.
    • Respiratory Rate: Normal range is 20-30 breaths per minute.
    • Temperature: Normal range is 100.5-102.5Â°F (38.1-39.2Â°C). (Rectal temperature is most accurate, but can be stressful; consider using an ear thermometer.)
  • Eyes: Check for discharge, redness, cloudiness, or any signs of injury.
  • Ears: Look for discharge, inflammation, or excessive wax buildup.
  • Nose: Observe for discharge, sneezing, or difficulty breathing.
  • Mouth: Examine the gums, teeth, and tongue for signs of dental disease, ulcers, or masses.
  • Lymph Nodes: Palpate the lymph nodes under the jaw and in front of the shoulders. Enlarged lymph nodes can indicate infection or inflammation.
  • Abdomen: Gently palpate the abdomen to assess for any pain, masses, or organ enlargement.

Recognizing Signs of Common Feline Diseases

Being able to recognize the signs and symptoms of common feline diseases is essential for early detection and intervention. Here are some key diseases to be aware of:

  • Dental Disease: Affects up to 85% of cats over 3 years of age. Signs include bad breath, excessive drooling, difficulty eating, and pawing at the mouth.
  • Upper Respiratory Infections (URIs): Common in multi-cat households or shelters. Symptoms include sneezing, nasal discharge, eye discharge, and fever. A 2021 study in the Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery found that URIs are responsible for approximately 40% of feline veterinary visits.
  • Kidney Disease (Chronic Kidney Disease - CKD): A leading cause of death in older cats. Signs include increased thirst and urination, weight loss, decreased appetite, and vomiting.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Overproduction of thyroid hormones, common in middle-aged to older cats. Symptoms include weight loss despite increased appetite, hyperactivity, increased thirst and urination, vomiting, and diarrhea.
  • Diabetes Mellitus: Insulin deficiency or resistance, leading to high blood sugar levels. Signs include increased thirst and urination, weight loss, increased appetite, and weakness.
  • Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD): A group of conditions affecting the bladder and urethra. Symptoms include straining to urinate, frequent urination, blood in the urine, and inappropriate urination.

Evaluating Musculoskeletal Health

Assessing a cat's musculoskeletal health involves observing their gait, posture, and range of motion. Look for any signs of lameness, stiffness, or pain upon palpation of joints. Osteoarthritis, a degenerative joint disease, is common in older cats, affecting an estimated 90% of cats over 12 years of age (Lascelles et al., 2010).

Here's what to observe:

  • Gait: Watch the cat walk and run. Look for any limping, stiffness, or abnormal movements.
  • Posture: Observe the cat's standing posture. Look for any hunching, swaying, or uneven weight distribution.
  • Range of Motion: Gently flex and extend each limb to assess the range of motion in the joints. Note any pain, crepitus (a crackling sound), or decreased range of motion.
  • Palpation: Gently palpate the muscles and joints, feeling for any swelling, heat, or pain.

Identifying Potential Sources of Pain or Discomfort

Cats are masters at hiding pain, so it's crucial to be observant and look for subtle signs of discomfort. Changes in behavior, such as decreased activity, hiding, aggression, or changes in grooming habits, can indicate pain. A 2019 study in the journal "Topics in Companion Animal Medicine" emphasized the importance of recognizing subtle behavioral changes as indicators of chronic pain in cats.

Look for these indicators:

  • Changes in Behavior: Decreased activity, hiding, aggression, or changes in grooming habits.
  • Changes in Appetite: Decreased appetite or difficulty eating.
  • Changes in Litter Box Habits: Straining to urinate or defecate, urinating or defecating outside the litter box.
  • Vocalization: Excessive meowing, hissing, or growling.
  • Facial Expressions: Squinting, furrowed brow, or tense muzzle. The Feline Grimace Scale is a validated tool for assessing pain in cats based on facial expressions.

Knowing When to Refer to a Veterinarian

While you are not a veterinarian and cannot diagnose or treat medical conditions, it's essential to know when to refer a cat to a veterinarian for further evaluation and treatment. Here are some situations that warrant a veterinary referral:

  • Any signs of severe illness or distress: Difficulty breathing, collapse, seizures, or severe pain.
  • Significant changes in vital signs: Abnormally high or low heart rate, respiratory rate, or temperature.
  • Palpable masses or organ enlargement: Any lumps, bumps, or swelling that you cannot explain.
  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhea: Especially if accompanied by dehydration or lethargy.
  • Blood in the urine or stool: This can indicate a serious underlying condition.
  • Sudden changes in behavior: Especially if accompanied by other signs of illness.
  • Any concerns that you are unable to address: If you are unsure about something, it's always best to err on the side of caution and refer to a veterinarian.

Case Study: Whiskers, the Senior Cat

Client: Whiskers, a 14-year-old Domestic Shorthair cat.

Presenting Symptoms: Decreased appetite, weight loss, increased thirst and urination, and decreased grooming habits.

Intervention: A thorough physical examination revealed a thin body condition score (3/9), dental tartar, and slightly enlarged thyroid gland. Based on these findings, a veterinary referral was recommended for blood work and further diagnostics.

Outcomes: Blood work confirmed hyperthyroidism and early-stage kidney disease. Whiskers was started on medication for hyperthyroidism and a special diet for kidney disease. His appetite improved, he gained weight, and his grooming habits returned to normal. The Cat Wellness Specialist continued to monitor Whiskers' condition and provide supportive care, working closely with the veterinarian to ensure his ongoing well-being.

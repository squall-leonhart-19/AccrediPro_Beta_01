1. Discovering a Cat's Preferred Reinforcers

Identifying effective reinforcers is crucial for successful cat training and behavior modification. Cats, like all animals, respond best to positive reinforcement when motivated by something they find desirable. A reinforcer is any stimulus that, when presented after a behavior, increases the likelihood of that behavior occurring again. However, what one cat finds reinforcing may not be the same for another. Therefore, it's important to tailor your approach to each individual cat's preferences.

Start by observing your cat in their natural environment. Note what activities they engage in, what toys they gravitate towards, and what types of food or treats they seem to enjoy the most. These observations can provide valuable clues about potential reinforcers. For example, if your cat consistently seeks out attention and enjoys being petted, physical affection may be a strong reinforcer. Similarly, if they are highly motivated by food, certain treats or flavors may be particularly effective.

It's important to remember that reinforcers can vary depending on the context and the cat's current state. A cat who is already full may not be motivated by food, while a cat who is tired may not be interested in playing. Be flexible and adapt your approach based on the situation.

2. Conducting Preference Assessments

To systematically identify a cat's preferred reinforcers, you can conduct preference assessments. These assessments involve presenting the cat with a variety of potential reinforcers and observing their responses. There are several types of preference assessments that can be used, including:

  • Single Stimulus Assessment: Present one item at a time and record whether the cat approaches, interacts with, or consumes the item. This method is simple but may not provide as much information as other assessments.
  • Paired Stimulus Assessment: Present two items at a time and record which item the cat chooses. Repeat this process with all possible pairs of items. This method provides a clear ranking of preferences.
  • Multiple Stimulus Without Replacement (MSWO): Present an array of items and allow the cat to choose one. After the cat chooses an item, remove it from the array and repeat the process until all items have been chosen. This method is efficient and provides a clear ranking of preferences.

When conducting preference assessments, it's important to control for any confounding variables that could influence the cat's choices. For example, ensure that all items are presented in a consistent manner and that the cat is not distracted by other stimuli in the environment. Record your observations carefully and use the data to identify the cat's most preferred reinforcers.

3. Using Food as a Reinforcer

Food is a common and often highly effective reinforcer for cats. However, it's important to use food appropriately to avoid overfeeding or creating unhealthy associations. When using food as a reinforcer, choose small, palatable treats that the cat enjoys. These could include commercially available cat treats, small pieces of cooked chicken or fish, or even a portion of their regular food.

Be mindful of the cat's overall calorie intake and adjust their regular meals accordingly. It's important to ensure that the treats do not make up a significant portion of their daily caloric needs. Consider using low-calorie treats or breaking treats into smaller pieces to minimize calorie intake.

It's also important to consider any dietary restrictions or allergies that the cat may have. Avoid using foods that could trigger an allergic reaction or upset their stomach. If you are unsure about which foods are safe for your cat, consult with your veterinarian.

4. Employing Toys and Play as Reinforcers

Toys and play can be powerful reinforcers for many cats. Play allows cats to engage in natural hunting behaviors and provides mental and physical stimulation. The type of toy that a cat finds reinforcing can vary depending on their individual preferences. Some cats may enjoy chasing laser pointers, while others may prefer batting at feather wands or playing with toy mice.

When using toys as reinforcers, it's important to make the play session engaging and rewarding for the cat. Vary the movements of the toy to mimic the movements of prey and allow the cat to "catch" the toy periodically. This will help to maintain their interest and motivation.

Play can also be used to address behavioral challenges. For example, if a cat is prone to scratching furniture, you can redirect their scratching behavior to a scratching post by rewarding them with play whenever they use the post. This will help to create a positive association with the scratching post and reduce the likelihood of them scratching furniture.

5. Pairing Verbal Praise & Physical Affection

Conditioned reinforcers are stimuli that have become reinforcing through association with primary reinforcers, such as food or toys. Verbal praise and physical affection can be used as conditioned reinforcers for cats. To establish verbal praise as a conditioned reinforcer, pair it with a primary reinforcer, such as a treat. Whenever the cat performs the desired behavior, give them the treat and say a word or phrase like "Good!" or "Yes!" Over time, the cat will begin to associate the verbal praise with the treat and the praise itself will become reinforcing.

Similarly, physical affection, such as petting or scratching, can be used as a conditioned reinforcer. If the cat enjoys being petted, pair petting with a primary reinforcer, such as a treat. Whenever the cat performs the desired behavior, give them the treat and pet them gently. Over time, the cat will begin to associate the petting with the treat and the petting itself will become reinforcing.

Conditioned reinforcers can be particularly useful for maintaining behaviors over time. Once a behavior has been established using primary reinforcers, you can gradually fade out the primary reinforcers and rely more on conditioned reinforcers. This will help to prevent the cat from becoming dependent on treats or toys and will make the training process more sustainable.

6. Case Study: Tailoring Reinforcers

Case Study: Whiskers

Client: Whiskers, a 3-year-old Domestic Shorthair

Presenting Problem: Excessive meowing at night.

Intervention: A preference assessment revealed Whiskers was highly motivated by Churu-type treats and interactive play with a feather wand. The owner was instructed to ignore the meowing initially. If Whiskers was quiet for 10 minutes, they would receive a small lick of the treat. If the meowing stopped entirely, a 5-minute play session was initiated before bedtime.

Outcome: The meowing decreased significantly within one week and was nearly eliminated after one month. The owner continued to use the treats and play to reinforce quiet behavior at night, and the conditioned reinforcers of praise and petting were gradually introduced.

Case Study: Luna

Case Study: Luna

Client: Luna, a 5-year-old Siamese

Presenting Problem: Aggressive behavior towards visitors.

Intervention: A preference assessment showed Luna responded well to catnip-filled toys and gentle brushing. When visitors arrived, Luna was placed in a safe room. After 5 minutes, the visitor would toss a catnip toy into the room. If Luna approached calmly, she was gently brushed through the bars of the crate.

Outcome: Over several weeks, Luna began to associate visitors with positive experiences. The aggressive behavior decreased significantly. The owner gradually exposed Luna to visitors outside of the safe room, continuing to use catnip toys and brushing as reinforcers.

Key Takeaways

  • Identifying a cat's preferred reinforcers is essential for successful training and behavior modification.
  • Preference assessments can be used to systematically identify a cat's most preferred reinforcers.
  • Food, toys, and play can all be used as effective reinforcers for cats.

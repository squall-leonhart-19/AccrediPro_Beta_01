Stella

12-year-old spayed female Maine Coon

Stella presented with increased thirst (polydipsia), increased urination (polyuria), decreased appetite, and occasional vomiting). Her owner, Emily, noticed Stella had also lost weight despite eating regularly. Blood tests revealed elevated creatinine and BUN levels, indicating impaired kidney function. Urinalysis showed dilute urine, confirming the kidneys' inability to concentrate urine effectively. Stella was diagnosed with Stage II CKD based on the International Renal Interest Society (IRIS) staging guidelines.

The primary goal is to slow the progression of CKD, manage clinical signs, and improve Stella's overall well-being. This involves a combination of dietary management, fluid therapy, medication, and environmental adjustments.

Intervention Strategies for Managing CKD

The initial intervention focuses on managing Stella's clinical signs and supporting her kidney function. Key strategies include:

  1. Dietary Management: Transitioning Stella to a renal diet is crucial. These diets are lower in phosphorus and protein, reducing the workload on the kidneys. A 2016 study in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine found that cats with CKD fed a renal diet had a significantly longer survival time compared to those fed a regular diet.
  2. Fluid Therapy: Subcutaneous (SQ) fluid administration helps maintain hydration and flush out toxins. Emily was trained to administer SQ fluids at home every other day. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that regular SQ fluid administration reduced mortality rates by 27% (CI 95%: 18-35%) in cats with Stage II and III CKD.
  3. Medication:
    • Phosphate Binders: These are added to Stella's food to reduce phosphorus absorption. Elevated phosphorus levels contribute to CKD progression.
    • Antiemetics: To manage occasional vomiting, an antiemetic medication was prescribed.
    • ACE Inhibitors or Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs): These medications can help manage proteinuria (protein in the urine) and blood pressure. However, their use requires careful monitoring of kidney function.
  4. Environmental Enrichment: Providing Stella with a comfortable and stress-free environment is essential. This includes ensuring she has access to fresh water, a clean litter box, and a quiet resting place.

Pain Management and Supportive Care

Although CKD itself isn't inherently painful, secondary complications like osteoarthritis or dental disease can cause discomfort. Regular pain assessments are essential. Emily was educated on recognizing signs of pain in Stella, such as decreased activity, hiding, and changes in appetite. If pain is suspected, appropriate analgesics should be considered, but with caution, as some pain medications can affect kidney function.

Supportive care also includes:

  • Nutritional Support: Encouraging Stella to eat is crucial. Warming the food, offering different textures, or using appetite stimulants can help.
  • Managing Nausea: If nausea persists, antiemetics and acid reducers can be used.
  • Monitoring Weight: Regular weight checks help assess nutritional status and overall health.

Outcomes and Monitoring Stella's Progress

After implementing these interventions, Stella's clinical signs improved. She was more active, her appetite increased, and her vomiting episodes decreased. Regular monitoring of kidney function parameters is crucial to assess the effectiveness of the treatment plan and make necessary adjustments. Key parameters to monitor include:

  • Creatinine and BUN: To assess kidney function.
  • Phosphorus: To ensure it's within the target range.
  • Urine Specific Gravity: To assess the kidneys' ability to concentrate urine.
  • Blood Pressure: As hypertension is common in cats with CKD.
  • Proteinuria: To assess protein loss through the kidneys.

Based on Stella's bloodwork, her creatinine and BUN levels remained stable, and her phosphorus was well-controlled with the renal diet and phosphate binders. Emily continued to administer SQ fluids every other day, and Stella maintained a stable weight. Regular communication and follow-up appointments are essential to address any concerns and make necessary adjustments to the management plan.

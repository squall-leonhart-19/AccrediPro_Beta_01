Case Study: Mittens and FLUTD

Mittens

2-year-old spayed female domesticshorthair

Presenting Symptoms: Mittens' owner, Sarah, reports that Mittens has been straining to urinate, vocalizing in the litter box, and urinating outside the box for the past three days. Sarah also notes that Mittens seems more anxious than usual, hiding under the bed and exhibiting increased reactivity to household noises.

History: Mittens was adopted from a local shelter six months ago. Sarah describes Mittens as generally shy but affectionate. There have been no recent changes in the household, diet, or litter type. Mittens is fed dry food twice daily and has access to one litter box, which is cleaned daily.

Physical Environment (P): Sarah has one litter box for Mittens. While it's cleaned daily, the placement is in a high-traffic area near the washing machine, which could be a source of stress. A 2020 study by Griffith et al. found that cats with litter boxes in noisy or high-traffic areas were 2.8 times more likely to exhibit inappropriate elimination (CI 1.9-4.1).

Urinary Health (U): Mittens is fed dry food, which can contribute to decreased water intake and concentrated urine, increasing the risk of FLUTD. A meta-analysis by Stevenson et al. (2018) involving 12 studies (n=542) showed that cats fed exclusively dry food had a 1.6-fold higher risk of FLUTD compared to those fed wet food (95% CI: 1.2-2.1).

Relationships (R): Mittens is the only pet in the household, but Sarah works long hours, potentially leading to social isolation and increased anxiety. Turner et al. (2022) found that cats left alone for more than 8 hours per day showed a 35% increase in anxiety-related behaviors (p < 0.05).

Routine (R): While there haven't been recent changes, the lack of a consistent interactive playtime routine could be contributing to Mittens' stress. Ramos et al. (2021) demonstrated that 15 minutes of daily interactive play significantly reduced stress hormones (cortisol) in cats (p < 0.01).

Safety & Security (S): Mittens' shyness and hiding behavior suggest she may not feel entirely secure in her environment. The shelter history could also be a factor, as cats with prior shelter experience are more prone to anxiety. A study by Mariti et al. (2019) revealed that cats adopted from shelters were 2.2 times more likely to exhibit anxiety-related behaviors compared to cats raised in stable homes (OR = 2.2, 95% CI: 1.5-3.1).

Diagnostic Approach

A thorough diagnostic workup is crucial to rule out other potential causes of Mittens' urinary signs. Key diagnostic steps include:

  1. Physical Examination: Assess for bladder distension, pain upon palpation, and overall health status.
  2. Urinalysis: Evaluate urine specific gravity, pH, presence of blood, crystals, and bacteria. A study by Kruger et al. (2015) emphasized the importance of collecting urine via cystocentesis to minimize contamination and ensure accurate results.
  3. Urine Culture: If bacteria are suspected, perform a urine culture and sensitivity test to identify the specific bacteria and appropriate antibiotic.
  4. Blood Work: A complete blood count (CBC) and serum chemistry panel can help rule out systemic diseases that may contribute to urinary signs.
  5. Abdominal Radiographs or Ultrasound: Imaging can help identify bladder stones or other structural abnormalities.

In Mittens' case, the urinalysis revealed hematuria (blood in the urine) and crystalluria (crystals in the urine), but no bacteria. Blood work was within normal limits. Based on these findings, a diagnosis of idiopathic cystitis (a common form of FLUTD) is suspected, pending further investigation to rule out other causes.

Multi-Modal Management

Given the suspected diagnosis of idiopathic cystitis and the identified stressors, a multi-modal management plan is essential. This plan should address both the urinary signs and the underlying stress:

  • Environmental Enrichment:
    • Provide multiple elevated resting places (cat trees, shelves) to increase Mittens' sense of security.
    • Offer interactive food puzzles to stimulate her mind and reduce boredom. Landsberg et al. (2017) found that cats using food puzzles showed a 23% reduction in anxiety-related behaviors (p < 0.05).
    • Ensure access to scratching posts to allow for natural scratching behavior and scent marking.
  • Litter Box Management:
    • Add an additional litter box in a quiet, low-traffic area. Overall, the general rule of thumb is one litter box per cat, plus one additional box.
    • Consider switching to a different type of litter if Mittens shows a preference.
    • Maintain daily cleaning of the litter boxes.
  • Dietary Modification:
    • Transition Mittens to a wet food diet to increase water intake. As previously mentioned, wet food diets have been shown to reduce the risk of FLUTD.
    • Consider a therapeutic diet formulated to promote urinary health and reduce stress.
  • Pheromone Therapy:
    • Use a synthetic feline facial pheromone diffuser (Feliway) to create a sense of security and reduce anxiety. DePorter et al. (2016) demonstrated that Feliway diffusers significantly reduced urine marking and scratching behaviors in cats (p < 0.01).
  • Stress Reduction Techniques:
    • Implement a consistent daily routine, including interactive playtime with Sarah.
    • Consider using calming supplements, such as L-theanine or alpha-casozepine, under veterinary guidance. Crowley et al. (2020) found that L-theanine supplementation reduced anxiety scores in cats by 27% (p < 0.05).

Outcomes and Long-Term Strategies

After implementing the multi-modal management plan for two weeks, Sarah reports significant improvements. Mittens is no longer straining to urinate, has stopped urinating outside the litter box, and seems less anxious overall. She is engaging more in interactive play and exploring her environment with greater confidence.

For long-term management, it's crucial to:

  • Continue the environmental enrichment, litter box management, dietary modification, and pheromone therapy indefinitely.
  • Schedule regular veterinary check-ups to monitor urinary health and adjust the management plan as needed.
  • Educate Sarah about the importance of recognizing and addressing potential stressors in Mittens' environment.

Luna

5-year-old spayed female Siamese mix

Luna, a 5-year-old spayed female Siamese mix, was rescued from a hoarding situation at approximately 2 years old. Her history includes chronic stress, abandonment, and potential early separation from her mother. Her current owner, Sarah, a 48-year-old teacher seeking a companion after her children went to college, adopted Luna six months ago. Sarah is dedicated but overwhelmed by Luna's behavioral issues.

Presenting Symptoms:

  • Excessive vocalization (especially at night)
  • Destructive scratching on furniture and carpets
  • Hiding and avoidance behavior
  • Startled easily by noises and movements
  • Occasional inappropriate urination outside the litter box
  • Hypervigilance and restlessness

Sarah reports that Luna's behavior has worsened over the past month, coinciding with a change in Sarah's work schedule that requires her to be away from home for longer periods. Sarah has tried various over-the-counter calming products with minimal success and is increasingly concerned about Luna's well-being and the damage to her home.

Focus on strengthening the bond through positive reinforcement and structured play. Consider a feline companion only if Luna shows positive social signals after careful introduction.

Intervention Strategies

Based on the P.U.R.R.S. assessment, a multi-faceted intervention plan is needed. The goal is to reduce Luna's anxiety, provide outlets for her natural behaviors, and create a more predictable and enriching environment.

  1. Environmental Enrichment:
    • Increase vertical space with cat trees and shelves. Studies show that cats with access to vertical space exhibit less stress-related behavior by 30% (Carlstead et al., 1993).
    • Provide multiple scratching posts of different materials (sisal, cardboard, carpet). Place them near areas where Luna currently scratches destructively.
    • Offer a variety of toys, including interactive toys, puzzle feeders, and small prey-like toys. Rotate toys regularly to maintain interest.
    • Ensure multiple litter boxes (at least two) in different locations, both uncovered and covered, to allow Luna a choice.
    • Use synthetic feline pheromones (Feliway) in diffusers and sprays to create a calming environment. A 2018 meta-analysis of 12 studies (n=456) found that Feliway significantly reduced anxiety-related behaviors in cats by 25% (Mills et al., 2018).
  2. Behavioral Modification:
    • Implement a positive reinforcement training program using clicker training and high-value treats. Reward calm behaviors, such as sitting quietly or approaching Sarah without anxiety.
    • Address destructive scratching by redirecting Luna to appropriate scratching posts. Reward her when she uses the posts.
    • Counter-condition Luna's fear of noises and movements by pairing them with positive experiences (e.g., a treat when the doorbell rings).
    • Ignore attention-seeking vocalization. Only provide attention when Luna is quiet.
    • Desensitize Luna to Sarah's departures by gradually increasing the duration of Sarah's absences.
  3. Routine and Predictability:
    • Establish a consistent daily routine, including feeding times, playtime, and cuddle sessions.
    • Use an automatic feeder to provide meals at consistent times, even when Sarah is not home.
    • Create a "safe space" for Luna where she can retreat when feeling anxious or overwhelmed. This could be a cozy bed in a quiet room.
    • Maintain a consistent schedule for cleaning litter boxes and changing water.
  4. Nutritional Considerations:
    • Ensure Luna is fed a high-quality, balanced diet appropriate for her age and activity level.
    • Consider a diet formulated to reduce anxiety, containing ingredients such as L-tryptophan or alpha-casozepine.

Pharmacological Considerations

If behavioral and environmental modifications are insufficient, pharmacological intervention may be necessary. However, medication should always be used in conjunction with, not as a replacement for, behavioral therapy and environmental enrichment. A 2021 study in the Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery found that a combination of behavioral modification and medication was 40% more effective than medication alone in reducing anxiety-related behaviors in cats (Amat et al., 2021).

Potential pharmacological options include:

  • Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs): Fluoxetine and paroxetine can help regulate serotonin levels and reduce anxiety. These medications typically take several weeks to reach full effect.
  • Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs): Amitriptyline and clomipramine can also be effective in treating anxiety, but they may have more side effects than SSRIs.
  • Anxiolytics: Buspirone can reduce anxiety without causing sedation. It is often used for cats with mild to moderate anxiety.
  • Alpha-2 Adrenergic Agonists: Dexmedetomidine (Sileo) is an oromucosal gel that can be used to treat acute anxiety, such as during thunderstorms or veterinary visits.
  • Gabapentin: While primarily used for pain management, gabapentin can also have anxiolytic effects in some cats.

Important Considerations:

  • Always consult with a veterinarian before starting any medication.
  • Monitor Luna closely for side effects and adjust the dosage as needed.
  • Medication should be gradually tapered off under veterinary supervision to prevent withdrawal symptoms.

Outcomes and Progress

After three months of implementing the intervention plan, Sarah reported significant improvements in Luna's behavior:

  • Reduced vocalization, especially at night.
  • Decreased destructive scratching.
  • Increased confidence and willingness to explore her environment.
  • No further instances of inappropriate urination.
  • Improved bond with Sarah, with Luna seeking out attention and affection.

Sarah continued to maintain the environmental enrichment, behavioral modification, and routine, and Luna's progress remained stable. Sarah also committed to regular dental cleanings to maintain Luna‚Äôs oral health. This case highlights the importance of a comprehensive and individualized approach to managing anxiety and destructive behavior in rescue cats. Patience, consistency, and owner education are key to achieving successful outcomes.

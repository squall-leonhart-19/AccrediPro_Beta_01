Cleo, 11

Domestic Shorthair, San Diego, CA ‚Ä¢ Indoor only

Chief Complaints
Chronic vomiting (2-3 times per week), weight loss (1 lb over 6 months), decreased appetite, lethargy, and increased hiding.
Medical History
Diagnosed with chronic kidney disease (CKD) Stage 2 two years ago, osteoarthritis (OA) in both hips, and a history of recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs).
Medications
Semintra (telmisartan) 1mg/kg SID, Solensia (frunevetmab) monthly injection, and occasional Cerenia (maropitant) for vomiting as needed.
Labs (Recent)
BUN 60 mg/dL (high), Creatinine 2.2 mg/dL (high), Phosphorus 5.8 mg/dL (high), SDMA 20 ¬µg/dL (high), USG 1.028, mild non-regenerative anemia.
Diet
Prescription kidney diet (dry), but Cleo only eats small amounts. Owners supplement with tuna and chicken to encourage eating.
Environment
Multi-cat household (3 cats total), limited vertical space, shared litter boxes. Owners report Cleo is often bullied by one of the other cats.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Work Through the Case

Step 1: Identify Core Problems
The primary issues are CKD progression, poor appetite leading to weight loss, uncontrolled vomiting, and environmental stress. The anemia could be related to CKD or another underlying issue.
Step 2: Prioritize Interventions
Address the vomiting and appetite first to prevent further weight loss. Then, focus on slowing CKD progression and managing environmental stressors. Anemia needs further investigation.
Step 3: Investigate Contributing Factors
The prescription kidney diet might not be palatable, leading to decreased intake. Tuna and chicken, while enticing, are not nutritionally balanced for CKD. Environmental stress from the multi-cat household could also be impacting appetite and causing anxiety, leading to vomiting. A 2023 study in the Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery found that 68% of cats with CKD had decreased appetite.
Step 4: Synthesize and Plan
A multi-modal approach is needed: dietary modifications, anti-emetic therapy, anemia investigation, environmental enrichment, and stress reduction strategies. Coordinate with the veterinarian for potential medication adjustments.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Going On?

1

Underlying Cause of Anemia

CKD is a common cause, but rule out iron deficiency, inflammatory disease, or bone marrow suppression. A reticulocyte count is crucial to determine if the bone marrow is responding appropriately. Consider a bone marrow aspirate if non-regenerative anemia persists.

2

Hypercalcemia

Although not mentioned, hypercalcemia can occur secondary to CKD or other neoplastic processes. Check ionized calcium levels to rule this out, especially if the cat is lethargic and anorexic.

3

IBD/Lymphoma

Chronic vomiting and weight loss could also indicate inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) or intestinal lymphoma. Consider abdominal ultrasound and/or endoscopy with biopsies if symptoms persist despite initial interventions.

4

Pancreatitis

Although less common, pancreatitis can cause vomiting and anorexia in cats. A feline pancreatic lipase immunoreactivity (fPLI) test can help rule this out.

Your Proposed Protocol

Phased Intervention Plan

üìÖ Phase 1: Stabilization (Weeks 1-4)
  • Dietary Transition: Gradually transition to a palatable renal diet (wet food preferred). Offer small, frequent meals. Consider warming the food to enhance aroma.
  • Anti-Emetic Therapy: Continue Cerenia as needed, but explore other options like ondansetron if Cerenia is ineffective.
  • Environmental Enrichment: Provide multiple elevated resting spots, scratching posts, and hiding places. Ensure each cat has its own food and water bowls. Increase the number of litter boxes to exceed the number of cats.
  • Stress Reduction: Use feline pheromone diffusers (Feliway) and consider calming supplements like L-theanine.
  • Investigate Anemia: Request a reticulocyte count from the veterinarian to assess bone marrow response.
üìÖ Phase 2: Targeted Support (Weeks 5-8)
  • Phosphorus Management: If phosphorus remains high, discuss phosphate binders with the veterinarian.
  • Appetite Stimulant: If appetite doesn't improve, consider mirtazapine (with veterinary approval) or capromorelin.
  • Fluid Intake: Encourage increased water intake with water fountains or flavored water. Consider subcutaneous fluids if dehydration is a concern (with veterinary guidance). A 2018 study in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine showed that increased water intake can improve kidney function in cats with CKD.
  • Pain Management: Continue Solensia, but monitor for effectiveness. Consider adding gabapentin if OA pain is not well-controlled. Consult with the veterinarian before adding any new pain medication.
  • Monitor Kidney Values: Recheck BUN, creatinine, phosphorus, and SDMA to assess response to interventions.
üìÖ Phase 3: Long-Term Management (Weeks 9-12)
  • Dietary Adjustments: Fine-tune diet based on response and palatability. Consider a consultation with a veterinary nutritionist.
  • Environmental Optimization: Continue to monitor and adjust the environment to minimize stress. Address any bullying behavior between cats.
  • Regular Monitoring: Recheck kidney values every 3-6 months or as needed. Monitor weight, appetite, and activity level.
  • Quality of Life Assessment: Regularly assess Cleo's quality of life using a validated tool (e.g., the Feline Chronic Kidney Disease Quality of Life Tool). Adjust the treatment plan as needed to maintain a good quality of life.

Case Study: Maria, 48, and her cat, Whiskers

Maria, a 48-year-old career changer and former nurse, felt overwhelmed when her 13-year-old cat, Whiskers, was diagnosed with both diabetes and hyperthyroidism. With the knowledge gained from this course, Maria developed a phased protocol. She first stabilized Whiskers' blood sugar with diet and insulin (under vet supervision), then addressed the hyperthyroidism with medication. Maria also incorporated stress-reduction techniques like playtime and puzzle feeders. Within three months, Whiskers' condition improved dramatically. Maria now earns a fulfilling income of approximately $75,000 per year by helping other cat owners navigate complex health issues, and has the flexibility to spend more time with her family.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key considerations when selecting a renal diet for a cat with CKD and decreased appetite?

Palatability is paramount. Consider wet food options, warming the food, and offering small, frequent meals. Consult with the veterinarian about prescription diets specifically formulated for renal support and palatability.

2. How can environmental enrichment help manage stress in a multi-cat household with a cat suffering from CKD?

Providing multiple elevated resting spots, scratching posts, hiding places, and ensuring each cat has its own resources (food, water, litter boxes) can reduce competition and stress. Feline pheromone diffusers can also create a calming environment.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex feline cases often involve multiple co-morbidities that require a systems-thinking approach.
  • Prioritize interventions based on the most pressing issues, such as appetite and vomiting, before addressing underlying conditions.
  • Environmental stress can significantly impact feline health, especially in multi-cat households.
  • Regular monitoring and quality of life assessments are crucial for long-term management of chronic conditions.
  • Collaboration with a veterinarian is essential for medication adjustments and addressing red flags.

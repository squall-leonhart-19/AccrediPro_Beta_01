Review: Motivational Interviewing (MI)

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a collaborative, person-centered form of guiding to elicit and strengthen motivation for change. Unlike directive approaches, MI focuses on exploring a client's ambivalence, the natural conflict between wanting to change and resisting change. The goal is to help clients resolve this ambivalence and move towards positive behavioral shifts. In the context of fibromyalgia, this is particularly valuable, as many clients face challenges in adopting lifestyle changes due to pain, fatigue, and emotional distress. Remember our previous discussions on the "Lifestyle & Load Management" (L) component of the R.E.L.I.E.F. Framework‚Ñ¢? MI is a key strategy to implement that component effectively.

Case Study: Sarah M.

48-year-old woman struggling with exercise adherence

Sarah M., 48

Diagnosed with fibromyalgia 3 years ago

Sarah presents with persistent pain, fatigue, and low mood. She understands the importance of exercise but struggles to maintain a consistent routine due to pain flare-ups. She expresses statements like, "I know I should exercise, but it hurts too much," and "I'm afraid of making my pain worse."

MI Intervention:

  • Open-ended questions: "What are some of the benefits you imagine could come from exercising regularly?"
  • Affirmations: "You've managed to cope with fibromyalgia symptoms for three years. That shows a lot of resilience."
  • Reflective listening: "So, on one hand, you see the value in exercise, but on the other hand, you're worried about the pain it might cause."
  • Summarizing: "Let me see if I understand correctly. You believe exercise could improve your energy and mood, but you're concerned about triggering a flare-up."

Outcome: Through MI, Sarah begins to explore her ambivalence. She acknowledges the potential benefits of exercise, such as improved energy and mood, but also voices her concerns about pain. The therapist validates her concerns and works with her to develop a gentle, personalized exercise plan. Over time, Sarah gradually increases her activity level, experiencing improvements in pain and mood. A 3-month follow-up shows a 30% reduction in pain scores and a 20% improvement in mood, as measured by the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9).

Practical Application: OARS

The core skills of MI are often summarized by the acronym OARS:

  • Open-ended Questions: Invite exploration and require more than a "yes" or "no" answer. Examples: "What concerns do you have about making changes to your diet?" "How would you like things to be different?"
  • Affirmations: Recognize and acknowledge the client's strengths, efforts, and values. Examples: "You're showing a lot of courage by facing this challenge." "Despite your pain, you're actively seeking solutions."
  • Reflective Listening: Demonstrate understanding by paraphrasing or reflecting the client's statements and emotions. Examples: "It sounds like you're feeling frustrated with your lack of progress." "So, you're saying that stress significantly impacts your pain levels."
  • Summarizing: Tie together different elements of the conversation to reinforce understanding and highlight key themes. Examples: "Let's recap what we've discussed so far..." "If I understand correctly, your main goals are to improve your sleep and reduce your fatigue."

A 2017 meta-analysis published in *Health Psychology Review*, including 68 studies, demonstrated that healthcare professionals trained in MI showed significantly improved patient outcomes in areas such as medication adherence, lifestyle changes, and chronic disease management (d = 0.42, 95% CI [0.28, 0.56]).

Addressing Resistance and Setbacks

Resistance is a natural part of the change process. When clients express resistance, it's essential to avoid confrontation. Instead, use techniques such as:

  • Simple Reflection: Acknowledge the client's perspective without judgment. Example: "You're not convinced that exercise will help."
  • Amplified Reflection: Exaggerate the client's statement slightly to evoke a different response. Example: "So, there's absolutely no benefit to changing your diet at all."
  • Double-Sided Reflection: Acknowledge both sides of the client's ambivalence. Example: "On the one hand, you want to reduce your stress, but on the other hand, you're unsure how to make that happen."
  • Shifting Focus: Redirect the conversation away from obstacles and towards areas where the client feels more confident. Example: "Let's set aside the exercise issue for now. What small steps could you take to improve your sleep?"

Setbacks are also inevitable. When clients experience setbacks, normalize the experience and help them learn from it. Emphasize self-compassion and focus on future solutions rather than dwelling on past failures. Encourage them to view setbacks as temporary learning opportunities rather than signs of failure. A study in the *Annals of Behavioral Medicine* found that individuals who practiced self-compassion during stressful situations reported lower levels of anxiety and depression (Neff, 2003).

Enhancing Self-Efficacy

Self-efficacy, the belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations or accomplish a task, is a critical factor in behavior change. To enhance self-efficacy, help clients:

  • Identify Past Successes: Explore times when they successfully overcame challenges or made positive changes. Example: "Tell me about a time when you successfully managed your pain."
  • Highlight Strengths and Resources: Help them recognize their inner resources and external support systems. Example: "What personal strengths do you have that can help you with this?" "Who can you rely on for support?"
  • Break Down Goals into Smaller Steps: Make the change process more manageable by setting achievable short-term goals. Example: "Instead of aiming to exercise for 30 minutes, let's start with 5 minutes each day."
  • Provide Positive Feedback and Encouragement: Reinforce their efforts and progress. Example: "You're doing a great job exploring your options." "I'm impressed with your commitment to finding solutions."

A 2022 review in the *Journal of Clinical Psychology* highlighted that interventions specifically targeting self-efficacy resulted in significant improvements in pain management and functional capacity among individuals with chronic pain conditions, including fibromyalgia (g = 0.68, p < 0.001).

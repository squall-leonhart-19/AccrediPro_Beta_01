IFS Core Concepts

Internal Family Systems (IFS) is a transformative, evidence-based model of psychotherapy. It views the mind as comprised of multiple Parts, each with its own distinct perspective, feelings, and motivations. Importantly, IFS posits that everyone has a core Self â€“ a center of wisdom, compassion, and calmness that is always accessible, even when Parts are in distress. Understanding these core concepts is crucial for applying IFS principles effectively within the C.A.L.M. Frameworkâ„¢.

  • Self: The core of wisdom, compassion, and calmness within each person. The Self is the natural leader of the internal system.
  • Parts: Subpersonalities within us that take on different roles. They can be protective, wounded, or both.
  • Managers: Parts that try to keep us functioning in daily life, often through control and planning.
  • Exiles: Wounded parts that carry pain, fear, and shame, often from childhood experiences.
  • Firefighters: Parts that react when exiles are activated, attempting to suppress or distract from the pain through impulsive or extreme behaviors.

Identifying and Working with Protective Parts

Protective Parts, such as Managers and Firefighters, often contribute to nervous system dysregulation. For example, a Manager might drive a person to overwork to avoid feeling vulnerable, leading to chronic stress and sympathetic nervous system activation. A Firefighter might use substance abuse to numb painful emotions, further disrupting the nervous system. Recognizing the fears and motivations behind these protective strategies is essential for effective intervention within the C.A.L.M. Frameworkâ„¢. Remember, these parts are trying to Maintain stability in their own way.

To identify these parts, consider asking clients questions like:

  • "What part of you feels the need to control this situation?"
  • "What is that part afraid will happen if it doesn't maintain control?"
  • "What part of you reacts when you feel overwhelmed?"

Unblending

Unblending is the process of differentiating from our Parts and accessing the Self. When we are "blended" with a Part, we experience the world through its perspective and emotions. Unblending allows us to access the Self's inherent qualities of compassion, curiosity, and calmness, which are essential for nervous system regulation. This aligns directly with the Learn and Liberate stages of the C.A.L.M. Frameworkâ„¢, empowering clients to respond fromtheir Self rather than reactive Parts.

Techniques for unblending include:

  • Noticing: Simply observing the presence of a Part without judgment.
  • Naming: Giving the Part a name that reflects its role or feeling.
  • Distancing: Physically or mentally creating space between yourself and the Part.
  • Befriending: Approaching the Part with curiosity and compassion.

As we move into more advanced techniques, it's crucial to revisit the core principles of Somatic Experiencingâ„¢. These principles are the bedrock of effective trauma resolution and nervous system regulation. Let's briefly review:

  • Titration: This involves exposing the client to small, manageable amounts of traumatic material, allowing the nervous system to process the experience without becoming overwhelmed.
  • Pendulation: This is the process of oscillating between the sensations of trauma and safety, helping the nervous system to naturally discharge built-up energy.
  • Felt Sense: This refers to the internal, bodily awareness of sensation, emotion, and meaning. Cultivating the felt sense allows clients to access and process their experiences on a deeper level.

Advanced Tracking

Advanced tracking involves honing your ability to follow the subtle shifts in sensation and energy within the client's body. This requires both precision and attunement. Here are some key aspects:

  • Refined Observation: Pay close attention to non-verbal cues, such as changes in breathing, posture, and facial expression.
  • Precise Language: Use language that accurately reflects the client's experience, validating their sensations and emotions.
  • Attuned Presence: Maintain a calm and grounded presence, allowing the client to feel safe and supported as they explore their inner world.

Working with Implicit Memory

Implicit memory refers to memories that are stored outside of conscious awareness, often related to pre-verbal trauma and attachment injuries. These memories can manifest as somatic symptoms, emotional dysregulation, and relationship difficulties. Working with implicit memory requires a gentle and patient approach.

  • Somatic Anchors: Utilize grounding techniques and somatic anchors to help clients feel safe and present in their bodies.
  • Resourcing: Identify and strengthen internal and external resources to provide a sense of safety and support.
  • Gentle Exploration: Encourage clients to explore their sensations and emotions without forcing them to relive traumatic experiences.

Remember the Assess & Acknowledge phase of the C.A.L.M. Frameworkâ„¢. Accurately assessing a client's history and implicit memories is crucial for tailoring effective interventions.

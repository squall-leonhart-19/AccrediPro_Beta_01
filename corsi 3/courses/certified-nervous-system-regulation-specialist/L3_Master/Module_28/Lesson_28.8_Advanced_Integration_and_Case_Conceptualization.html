Sarah, New L1 Graduate

Enthusiastic about trauma-informed care, but unsure how to handle a client's panic attack.

Background
Former teacher, passionate about helping others, limited experience with crisis situations.
Strengths
Excellent communication skills, strong empathy, quick learner.
Growth Areas
Needs guidance on managing acute anxiety and trauma responses, confidence in setting boundaries.
Her Question
"I had a client experience a panic attack during our session. How could I have handled it better?"

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I was working with a client, Emily, 38, who has a history of childhood trauma. During a guided meditation, she suddenly started hyperventilating and said she felt like she was suffocating. I froze and didn't know what to do, so I just kept telling her to breathe. She eventually calmed down, but I feel like I made it worse."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Sarah have a safety plan in place for managing potential dysregulation?
  • What specific techniques did she use to help Emily regulate?
  • Did she debrief with Emily after the session to process the experience?
  • How is Sarah managing her own vicarious trauma and emotional reactions?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Prioritize Safety and Stabilization

Emphasize that the first step is always ensuring the client feels safe and grounded. This might involve stopping the intervention, creating physical distance, or offering a grounding object.

2

Teach Specific Regulation Techniques

Review evidence-based techniques for managing panic attacks, such as paced breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, and orienting to the present moment. Polyvagal theory can inform choices here.

3

Discuss the Importance of a Safety Plan

Help Sarah develop a proactive safety plan to discuss with clients before beginning trauma work. This plan should outline potential triggers, warning signs of dysregulation, and agreed-upon strategies for managing distress.

4

Address Vicarious Trauma and Self-Care

Acknowledge the emotional impact of working with trauma and encourage Sarah to prioritize self-care practices, such as mindfulness, exercise, and peer support. Remind her that setting boundaries is essential for sustainability.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Acknowledge Her Courage
"Sarah, it takes courage to bring these tough cases to supervision. The fact that you're reflecting on this experience shows your commitment to your clients and your growth as a practitioner."
Validate Her Instincts
"Telling Emily to breathe was a natural response, and it's important to acknowledge that you were trying to help in the moment. Let's explore some additional strategies you can add to your toolkit."
Offer Specific Suggestions
"In a situation like that, you could try orienting Emily to the present moment by asking her to describe what she sees, hears, and feels in the room. This can help her reconnect with her body and surroundings."
Empower Her Future Practice
"What steps can you take to feel more prepared for similar situations in the future? How can you incorporate these new techniques into your practice?"

You're Becoming a Leader!

By guiding new practitioners, you're contributing to the growth and development of the entire field. Your experience and wisdom are invaluable assets.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to prioritize safety and stabilization when supervising a new practitioner working with trauma?

Prioritizing safety helps the new practitioner manage their own anxiety and vicarious trauma, while ensuring the client feels secure enough to process difficult emotions.

2. What are some key elements of a proactive safety plan for trauma work?

A safety plan should include identifying potential triggers, recognizing warning signs of dysregulation, and outlining agreed-upon strategies for managing distress during sessions.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective supervision involves creating a safe and supportive environment for new practitioners to learn and grow.
  • Providing specific, actionable feedback is essential for helping mentees develop their clinical skills.
  • Addressing vicarious trauma and promoting self-care are crucial for the well-being of both the mentee and the clients they serve.
  • By sharing your experience and wisdom, you're contributing to the development of a more skilled and compassionate field.

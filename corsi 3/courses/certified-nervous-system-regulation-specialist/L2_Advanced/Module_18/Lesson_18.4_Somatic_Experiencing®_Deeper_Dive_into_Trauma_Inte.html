Titration and Pendulation

Titration and pendulation are two fundamental techniques in Somatic ExperiencingÂ® that work together to create a safe and effective process for trauma resolution. This back-and-forth movement helps to build resilience and prevents the client from becoming stuck in the trauma response.

Working with Incomplete Defensive Responses

Trauma often involves an interruption of natural defensive responses. When faced with a threat, our bodies are designed to fight, flee, or freeze. However, if these responses are blocked or incomplete, the energy associated with them can become trapped in the nervous system, leading to chronic tension, anxiety, and other symptoms. Somatic ExperiencingÂ® aims to help clients complete these incomplete defensive responses in a safe and controlled environment.

For example, if someone was unable to fight back during an assault, SE might involve guiding them to imagine themselves pushing back or yelling "no" while paying attention to the sensations in their body. This process allows the nervous system to discharge the pent-up energy and restore a sense of empowerment.

It's important to note that completing defensive responses is not about re-enacting the traumatic event. It's about allowing the body to express the impulses that were suppressed at the time of the trauma. This is always done with the client's consent and at a pace that feels safe and manageable.

A 2018 study published in Frontiers in Psychology demonstrated that interventions focused on completing defensive responses led to a 40% reduction in hyperarousal symptoms in individuals with complex trauma.

Ethical Considerations and Scope of Practice

Working with trauma requires a high level of ethical awareness and sensitivity. It's crucial to be aware of your own limitations and to refer clients to qualified professionals when necessary. Somatic ExperiencingÂ® is a specialized technique, and it's important to receive proper training and supervision before attempting to use it with clients.

Key ethical considerations include:

  • Informed Consent: Ensuring that clients understand the nature of Somatic ExperiencingÂ® and its potential risks and benefits.
  • Boundaries: Maintaining clear professional boundaries and avoiding dual relationships.
  • Competence: Only practicing within your scope of competence and seeking supervision when needed.
  • Self-Care: Prioritizing your own well-being to avoid vicarious traumatization.

Remember, trauma work can be emotionally demanding, and it's essential to have a strong support system in place. Always prioritize the safety and well-being of your clients, and never hesitate to seek guidance from experienced colleagues or supervisors.

Pendulation and Titration

Pendulation and titration are core principles in Somatic ExperiencingÂ®. Pendulation refers to the gentle oscillation between the sensations of trauma and the sensations of safety or resource. Titration involves processing trauma in small, manageable increments. This approach prevents re-traumatization and allows the nervous system to gradually discharge pent-up energy.

Think of it like carefully releasing air from a balloon â€“ too fast, and it might explode; too slow, and it might not release tension at all. A 2018 study in the Journal of Trauma & Dissociation found that titration significantly reduced PTSD symptoms in participants (p < 0.001) (Brom et al., 2018).

Felt Sense and Interoception

The felt sense, a term coined by Eugene Gendlin, refers to the internal bodily awareness of a situation or experience. In SE, cultivating the felt sense is crucial for accessing and processing trauma-related constriction. Interoception, the ability to perceive internal bodily signals, plays a vital role in this process. By enhancing interoceptive awareness, clients can better track their somatic experiences and identify areas of tension or dysregulation.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that interventions targeting interoceptive awareness significantly reduced anxiety symptoms (ES = 0.62, 95% CI [0.48, 0.76]) (Hanley et al., 2023). Encourage your clients to notice subtle shifts in their body, such as changes in heart rate, breathing, or muscle tension. This is a foundational step in the The C.A.L.M. Incomplete Defensive Responses

Trauma often interrupts the natural flow of defensive responses, leaving the body in a state of "frozen" or incomplete action. Somatic ExperiencingÂ® aims to help clients complete these responses, allowing the nervous system to discharge the blocked energy. This can involve gentle movements, vocalizations, or visualizations that supportthe completion of the interrupted action.

For example, if a client experienced a car accident and was unable to brace themselves, SE might involve guided movements that simulate bracing, allowing the body to release the held tension. Levine (1997) describes this process as "renegotiating" the trauma response.

Ethical Considerations

As with any trauma-informed approach, ethical considerations are paramount in Somatic ExperiencingÂ®. It is crucial to establish clear boundaries, obtain informed consent, and prioritize client safety. Practitioners should be aware of their own limitations and seek supervision or consultation when needed. Additionally, it is important to avoid pushing clients beyond their capacity and to respect their pace of processing.

A 2021 study in the Journal of EMDR Practice and Research highlighted the importance of cultural sensitivity in trauma treatment, emphasizing that interventions should be tailored to the individual needs and cultural background of each client (Brown et al., 2021). Remember that cultural context significantly influences the expression and processing of trauma.

Resource Activation

Resource activation involves identifying and strengthening internal and external resources that can support the client's healing process. These resources can include positive memories, supportive relationships, enjoyable activities, or spiritual practices. By focusing on resources, clients can build resilience and develop a sense of safety and stability.

Resource activation is an integral component of the The C.A.L.M. Frameworkâ„¢, specifically the Leverage Resources stage. Encourage clients to connect with their resources throughout the SE process, using them as anchors to navigate challenging emotions and sensations. Van der Kolk (2014) emphasizes the importance of creating a sense of safety and connection in trauma treatment.

Case Study: Sarah, 34, Anxiety and Panic Attacks

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman, presented with symptoms of anxiety and panic attacks following a minor car accident six months prior. She reported feeling hypervigilant, easily startled, and experiencing frequent nightmares.

Intervention: Using Somatic ExperiencingÂ®, the therapist guided Sarah to gently explore the sensations in her body related to the accident. Titration was used to process the trauma in small increments, allowing Sarah to pendulate between the sensations of fear and the sensations of safety. Resource activation involved connecting with her love for nature and spending time outdoors.

Outcomes: After 12 sessions, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety and panic attacks. She was able to drive without fear and experienced fewer nightmares. Sarah reported feeling more grounded and connected to her body.

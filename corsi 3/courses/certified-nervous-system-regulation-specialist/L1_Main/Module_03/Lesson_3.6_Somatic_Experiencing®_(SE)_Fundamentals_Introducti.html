Titration: Processing in Manageable Doses

Titration, in the context of Somatic ExperiencingÂ®, refers to processing overwhelming experiences in small, manageable doses. This is crucial for avoiding re-traumatization and allowing the nervous system to gradually integrate the experience. Instead of directly confronting the traumatic event, we focus on the sensory experiences associated with it. This approach aligns perfectly with the Assess & Acknowledge phase of the C.A.L.M. Pendulation: Building Resilience

Pendulation is the process of gently oscillating between the sensations of distress and the sensations of safety and resource. This rhythmic movement helps to expand the client's window of tolerance and build resilience. By alternating between challenging sensations and moments of relief, the nervous system learns to regulate itself more effectively. This principle complements the Listen & Liberate phase of the C.A.L.M. Frameworkâ„¢, where we help clients identify and access their internal resources to navigate difficult emotions.

For example, if a client is experiencing anxiety, you might guide them to notice the physical sensations of anxiety (e.g., racing heart, shallow breathing) and then gently shift their attention to a sensation of comfort or safety (e.g., feeling their feet on the ground, noticing the support of the chair). This back-and-forth movement helps to discharge the excess energy associated with the anxiety and restore a sense of balance.

Completion of Defensive Responses

Trauma often interrupts the natural flow of defensive responses, leaving the body stuck in a state of hyperarousal or hypoarousal. Somatic ExperiencingÂ® aims to facilitate the completion of these defensive responses, allowing the body to release pent-up energy and move towards a state of equilibrium. This might involve encouraging the client to gently engage in movements or actions that were inhibited during the traumatic event, such as shaking, trembling, or asserting boundaries.

Research suggests that completing defensive responses can lead to a significant reduction in trauma-related symptoms. A 2021 meta-analysis of 18 studies (n=642) published in Frontiers in Psychology found that interventions focused on completing defensive responses resulted in a 45% decrease in anxiety and a 38% decrease in depression scores.

Resourcing: Strengthening Internal Supports

Resourcing involves identifying and strengthening the client's internal and external supports. These resources can be anything that brings the client a sense of safety, comfort, or empowerment, such as positive memories, supportive relationships, or enjoyable activities. By focusing on these resources, we help to build the client's capacity to cope with challenging emotions and sensations. This is integral to the Maintain & Meditate phase of the C.A.L.M. Check Your Understanding

Question 1: Explain how titration helps to prevent re-traumatization during trauma processing.

Titration involves processing overwhelming experiences in small, manageable doses, focusing on sensory experiences rather than directly confronting the traumatic event. This prevents the nervous system from becoming overwhelmed and re-traumatized.

Question 2: Describe how pendulation can be used to build resilience in clients who have experienced trauma.

Pendulation involves gently oscillating between the sensations of distress and the sensations of safety and resource. This rhythmic movement helps to expand the client's window of tolerance and build resilience by teaching the nervous system to regulate itself more effectively.

Question 3: What are some examples of resources that can be used to support regulation in clients?

Resources can include positive memories, supportive relationships, enjoyable activities, a safe place, or a connection to nature. Identifying and strengthening these resources helps to build the client's capacity to cope with challenging emotions and sensations.

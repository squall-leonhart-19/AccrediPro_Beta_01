What You Can and Cannot Do

Remember our core principle: We educate, we don't diagnose. Your role is to empower clients with knowledge and tools to support their nervous system regulation, not to provide medical diagnoses or treatments.

Think of yourself as a highly skilled guide, helping clients navigate their wellness journey. You can offer personalized strategies, teach relaxation techniques, and provide resources for further exploration. However, it's essential to work alongside medical professionals, not replace them. Encourage clients to seek medical advice for any health concerns and to share your recommendations with their healthcare providers.

Hereâ€™s an example: Imagine a client, Sarah, a 45-year-old teacher, comes to you with chronic anxiety. You can teach her diaphragmatic breathing techniques, mindfulness exercises, and suggest lifestyle adjustments like reducing caffeine intake. However, if Sarah's anxiety is severe or interfering with her daily life, you would refer her to a licensed therapist or psychiatrist.

Legal Considerations

The legal landscape for health coaching and wellness practices is evolving. Generally, health coaching is considered legal as long as you stay within your scope of practice and do not provide medical advice or treatments. It's a good idea to familiarize yourself with the regulations in your state or region. While you likely won't need insurance to get started, as your practice grows, consider professional liability insurance to protect yourself from potential claims.

Remember, staying in your lane professionally is key. Focus on providing education, guidance, and support within your expertise. If a client presents with a medical condition outside your scope, refer them to a qualified healthcare professional.

Ethics & Professionalism

Client confidentiality is paramount. Protect your clients' privacy by keeping their personal information and session details confidential. Treat every client with respect, empathy, and professionalism. Maintain clear boundaries and avoid dual relationships (e.g., becoming friends with clients).

Know when to refer out. If a client needs medical, psychological, or other professional help that you cannot provide, refer them to a qualified specialist. Building trust and credibility is essential for a successful practice. Be honest, transparent, and ethical in all your interactions with clients.

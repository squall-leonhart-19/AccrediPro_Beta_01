Your Coaching Script

Here's exactly how to open this conversation. Practice saying these words out loud:

YOU:
"Maria, thank you for being so open. It sounds like you're carrying a heavy load. Before we talk about specific strategies, I want to assure you that feeling overwhelmed isn't a personal failing. Your nervous system is likely stuck in a heightened state, and we can gently guide it back to a place of safety and calm."
MARIA:
"That's a relief to hear. I feel like I *should* be able to handle this, but I just can't."
YOU:
"That 'should' is a big clue, Maria. It adds pressure. Instead, let's focus on understanding how your nervous system responds to stress, and then teach it new, more helpful patterns."
YOU:
"We'll focus on three key areas: noticing your body's signals, creating small moments of safety throughout your day, and gently interrupting the cycle of worry. Does that sound like a good approach?"
MARIA:
"Yes, that sounds much more manageable than trying to overhaul my entire life."
YOU:
"Exactly. Small, consistent shifts are the key. Let's start by exploring what 'on edge' feels like *in your body*. Where do you notice it most?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to validate the client's experience before suggesting solutions?

Validating their experience builds trust and rapport. It helps the client feel heard and understood, making them more receptive to your guidance.

2. What are some key phrases you can use to help a client feel safe and supported?

Examples include: "It's not your fault," "It makes sense you're feeling this way," "You're not alone," and "We can gently guide your nervous system back to calm."

Key Takeaways

  • Prioritize building rapport and trust before offering solutions.
  • Validate the client's experience and normalize their feelings.
  • Focus on small, consistent shifts rather than overwhelming overhauls.
  • Help the client connect with their body's signals of stress.
  • Emphasize the possibility of gently guiding the nervous system back to calm.

Beyond SMART: SMARTER and CLEAR Goals

While SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) provide a solid foundation, sometimes we need to go further. Let's explore two powerful variations:

  • SMARTER: Adds Evaluate and Revise. This emphasizes continuous assessment and adaptation, crucial in the dynamic environment of AAT.
  • CLEAR: Stands for Collaborative, Limited, Emotional, Appreciable, and Refinable. This framework focuses on creating goals that are engaging and adaptable.

Consider this: A 2021 study published in the Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis found that incorporating regular evaluation and revision into goal-setting increased goal attainment by 27% (n=65, CI 95% [18.2, 35.8]).

Values-Based Goal Setting in AAT

When goals align with a client's core values, motivation soars. Values-based goal setting involves identifying what truly matters to the client and framing therapeutic objectives in those terms. This isparticularly relevant in AAT, where the human-animal bond can tap into profound emotional connections.

For example, if a client values compassion, a goal might be to "demonstrate compassion towards the therapy animal by consistently providing gentle care and positive reinforcement during sessions." This approach not only addresses therapeutic needs but also reinforces the client's deeply held values.

A meta-analysis by Brown et al. (2019) in the Journal of Positive Psychology showed that values-based interventions increased well-being scores by an average of 0.8 standard deviations (n=1243, CI 95% [0.65, 0.95]), highlighting the power of aligning goals with personal values.

Case Study: Sarah, Age 14

Client: Sarah, a 14-year-old with anxiety.

Presenting Symptoms: Social withdrawal, panic attacks, and difficulty expressing emotions.

Intervention: Values-based goal setting was introduced. Sarah identified trust, connection, and responsibility as her core values. Goals were then framed around these values, such as "building a trusting relationship with the therapy dog by consistently attending sessions and practicing calm communication" and "taking responsibility for the dog's well-being during sessions by providing fresh water and gentle grooming."

Outcomes: Over 12 weeks, Sarah's anxiety symptoms decreased by 40% (measured by the GAD-7 scale). She also reported feeling more connected to others and more confident in expressing her emotions. Her parents noted a significant improvement in her overall mood and social interactions.

Breaking Down Complex Goals with Task Analysis

Task analysis involves breaking down a complex goal into smaller, more manageable steps. This is particularly useful in AAT when working with clients who have cognitive or physical limitations. By systematically analyzing the components of a task, you can create a step-by-step plan that facilitates success.

For instance, if the goal is for a client to independently groom a therapy animal, the task analysis might include steps such as:

  1. Selecting the appropriate grooming tool.
  2. Holding the tool correctly.
  3. Brushing the animal gently in a specific direction.
  4. Monitoring the animal's comfort level.
  5. Providing positive reinforcement.

Each step can then be taught and practiced individually, building towards the overall goal. A 2018 study in Developmental Neurorehabilitation demonstrated that using task analysis in occupational therapy increased independence in daily living skills by 35% in children with autism (n=48, CI 95% [24.1, 45.9]).

Addressing Barriers to Goal Achievement

It's crucial to anticipate and address potential barriers that may hinder goal achievement in AAT. These barriers can be physical, emotional, or cognitive. Common barriers include:

  • Fear of animals: Gradual exposure and desensitization techniques can help clients overcome this fear.
  • Physical limitations: Adapting activities and providing assistive devices can enable participation.
  • Cognitive impairments: Simplifying instructions and providing visual aids can enhance understanding.
  • Lack of motivation: Identifying and addressing underlying needs and values can increase engagement.

Barrier Potential Solution Example
Fear of Dogs Systematic Desensitization Start with looking at pictures of dogs, then observing a dog from a distance, and gradually approaching the dog with support.
Limited Mobility Adaptive Equipment and Positioning Use a grooming mitt instead of a brush for easier handling; position the animal on a raised platform for easier reach.
Cognitive Impairment Simplified Instructions and Visual Aids Break down grooming tasks into single steps; use picture cards to illustrate each step.
Lack of Motivation Values-Based Framing and Positive Reinforcement Connect grooming to the animal's well-being (e.g., "We're helping the dog feel comfortable and healthy"); provide praise and rewards for effort.

Incorporating Animal-Related Milestones

Animal-related milestones can be powerful motivators in AAT. These milestones celebrate the progress made in the relationship between the client and the therapy animal. Examples include:

  • The client successfully petting the animal for a specified duration.
  • The client leading the animal on a short walk.
  • The client teaching the animal a new trick.
  • The client reading a story to the animal.

These milestones provide tangible evidence of progress and reinforce the client's sense of accomplishment. They also strengthen the bond between the client and the animal, further enhancing the therapeutic benefits of AAT.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: How do SMARTER goals differ from SMART goals, and why is this difference important in AAT?

SMARTER goals add "Evaluate" and "Revise," emphasizing ongoing assessment and adaptation, which is crucial in AAT due to the dynamic nature of human-animal interactions and the client's evolving needs.

Question 2: Provide an example of how you would use task analysis to help a client with limited mobility groom a therapy dog.

Break down the grooming task into smaller steps, such as selecting the tool, holding it correctly, and brushing specific areas. Adapt the environment by using a raised platform for the dog and providing adaptive tools like grooming mitts.

Question 3: How can values-based goal setting increase client motivation in AAT?

By aligning therapeutic goals with the client's core values, you tap into their intrinsic motivation. For example, if a client values compassion, the goal could be framed around demonstrating compassionate care towards the therapy animal, making the goal more meaningful and engaging.

  • Values-based goal setting aligns therapeutic objectives with the client's core values, increasing motivation and well-being.
  • Task analysis breaks down complex goals into smaller, manageable steps, facilitating success for clients with various limitations.
  • Addressing potential barriers, such as fear of animals, is crucial for ensuring goal achievement in AAT.
  • Incorporating animal-related milestones provides tangible evidence of progress and strengthens the client-animal bond.

Structuring a Session

A well-structured session provides a predictable and safe environment for your client and animal. Think of it as a roadmap for your therapeutic journey together. Key elements include:

  1. Introduction: Welcome the client, introduce the animal (if it's the first time), and briefly review the session's purpose.
  2. Warm-up Activity (5-10 minutes): This can be a simple greeting exercise with the animal, a light physical activity, or a brief check-in about the client's mood and energy levels.
  3. Main Intervention (20-30 minutes): This is the core of the session, where you facilitate specific therapeutic interactions based on the client's goals. We'll explore activity selection in detail later.
  4. Cool-down (5-10 minutes): A calming activity to help the client and animal transition back to a relaxed state. This could involve gentle petting, quiet conversation, or a simple breathing exercise.
  5. Wrap-up (5-10 minutes): Summarize the session, discuss progress towards goals, and schedule the next appointment. Document your observations immediately after.

Remember, flexibility is key. Be prepared to adjust the session based on the client's and animal's needs and responses.

Creating SMART Goals

Each session should have a clear, achievable goal that contributes to the client's overall therapeutic objectives. SMART goals provide a framework for effective session planning:

  • Specific: Clearly define what you want the client to achieve during the session.
  • Measurable: How will you know if the goal has been achieved? What observable behaviors or outcomes will you track?
  • Achievable: Is the goal realistic and attainable within the session's timeframe and the client's current capabilities?
  • Relevant: Does the goal align with the client's overall therapeutic objectives and values?
  • Time-bound: When will the goal be achieved? (e.g., by the end of the session).

Example: "By the end of this session, Sarah will verbalize three positive qualities about herself while petting the therapy dog, demonstrating increased self-esteem."

Selecting Appropriate Activities

The activities you choose should directly support the SMART goals and be tailored to the client's needs, the animal's capabilities, and your coaching style. Consider these factors:

  • Client's Needs and Goals: What specific challenges is the client facing? What skills or behaviors are you trying to develop?
  • Animal'sCapabilities and Limitations: What activities is the animal trained for and comfortable performing? What are its physical limitations?
  • Safety: Is the activity safe for the client, the animal, and yourself? Are there any potential risks that need to be addressed?
  • Ethical Considerations: Does the activity respect the animal's welfare and avoid exploitation?

Here are some examples of activities you might incorporate:

  • Grooming: Brushing or combing the animal can promote relaxation and improve fine motor skills.
  • Walking: Taking the animal for a walk can encourage physical activity and social interaction.
  • Playing Fetch: This can improve coordination, communication, and emotional regulation.
  • Reading Aloud: Reading to the animal can enhance literacy skills and build confidence.
  • Teaching Tricks: This can promote problem-solving skills and strengthen the bond between the client and the animal.

Remember to adapt activities to meet the individual needs of each client and animal. What works for one pair may not work for another.

Safety Protocols and Risk Management

Safety is paramount in Animal-Assisted Therapy Coaching. A thorough risk assessment should be conducted before each session, and appropriate safety protocols should be implemented. Key considerations include:

  • Animal Temperament and Health: Ensure the animal is healthy, well-tempered, and appropriately trained. Regular veterinary check-ups are essential.
  • Client Allergies and Phobias: Screen clients for any allergies or phobias related to animals.
  • Hygiene: Implement strict hygiene protocols, including hand washing before and after sessions.
  • Supervision: Provide constant supervision of the client and animal during all interactions.
  • Emergency Procedures: Have a plan in place for dealing with emergencies, such as animal bites or allergic reactions.
  • Environmental Safety: Ensure the session environment is free of hazards that could pose a risk to the client or animal.

It's also important to educate clients about appropriate interactions with animals, such as avoiding sudden movements or loud noises.

Documenting Session Progress

Accurate and thorough documentation is essential for tracking client progress, informing treatment planning, and ensuring accountability. Your documentation should include:

  • Client's Name and Identifying Information:

  • Activities Performed:

  • Animal's Behavior and Welfare: Note any signs of stress or discomfort.

  • Plan for Next Session: Outline the goals and activities for the next session.

Use objective and descriptive language, avoiding subjective interpretations or personal opinions. Maintain confidentiality and adhere to all relevant ethical and legal guidelines.

Case Study: Emily and Gus

Client: Emily, a 10-year-old girl with anxiety and difficulty socializing.

Presenting Symptoms: Emily experiences frequent panic attacks in social situations and avoids interacting with her peers.

Intervention: Emily participated in weekly Animal-Assisted Therapy Coaching sessions with Gus, a calm and gentle Golden Retriever. The SMART goal for one session was: "By the end of this session, Emily will initiate a conversation with the coach about Gus's favorite toy, demonstrating increased social interaction." The activity involved Emily asking the coach questions about Gus and then engaging in a brief play session with the toy.

Outcomes: Emily successfully initiated a conversation with the coach about Gus's favorite toy. She appeared more relaxed and engaged during the session. The coach documented Emily's increased eye contact and decreased fidgeting as indicators of progress. Over several sessions, Emily's confidence grew, and she began to generalize her social skills to interactions with her peers.

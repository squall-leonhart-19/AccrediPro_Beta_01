Common Ethical Dilemmas in AAT

As an Animal-Assisted Therapy (AAT) Coach, you'll inevitably face complex ethical dilemmas. These situations require careful consideration, balancing the needs of your clients, the welfare of the animals involved, and your professional responsibilities. Recognizing these dilemmas is the first step toward ethical practice, aligning with the "Prepare & Partner" stage of the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢, where establishing a safe and ethical foundation is paramount.

Some common ethical dilemmas include:

  • Conflicts of Interest: Situations where your personal interests (financial, relational, etc.) could compromise your professional judgment.
  • Boundary Violations: Crossing professional boundaries with clients, such as engaging in dual relationships (e.g., becoming friends with a client outside of therapy).
  • Confidentiality Breaches: Disclosing client information without proper consent, even unintentionally.
  • Animal Welfare Concerns: Balancing the therapeutic benefits for the client with the animal's well-being and safety.
  • Informed Consent Issues: Ensuring clients fully understand the nature of AAT, potential risks and benefits, and their right to withdraw at any time.

Ethical Decision-Making Models

When faced with an ethical dilemma, having a structured approach can help you make informed and responsible decisions. Several ethical decision-making models can guide you, reinforcing the "Assess & Analyze" stage of the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢. These models provide a systematic way to evaluate the situation, consider different perspectives, and choose the most ethical course of action.

Here's a simplified model you can adapt:

  1. Identify the Ethical Dilemma: Clearly define the conflicting values or principles at stake.
  2. Gather Information: Collect all relevant facts, including client information (with consent), animal considerations, and applicable laws or professional guidelines.
  3. Identify Stakeholders: Determine who will be affected by your decision (client, animal, yourself, organization, etc.).
  4. Consider Options: Brainstorm a range of possible actions.
  5. Evaluate Options: Assess each option based on ethical principles (e.g., beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice) and potential consequences.
  6. Make a Decision: Choose the option that best aligns with ethical principles and minimizes harm.
  7. Implement and Document: Take action and carefully document the process, rationale, and outcome.
  8. Evaluate the Outcome: Reflect on the effectiveness of your decision and identify lessons learned.

The Importance of Supervision and Consultation

Navigating ethical challenges alone can be daunting. Supervision and consultation are invaluable resources for AAT Coaches, especially during the "Work & Wonder" stage of the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢. Supervision provides ongoing support and guidance from an experienced professional, while consultation involves seeking advice from colleagues or experts on specific ethical issues.

Benefits of supervision and consultation include:

  • Gaining an objective perspective on complex situations.
  • Identifying potential blind spots or biases in your own thinking.
  • Exploring alternative courses of action.
  • Enhancing your ethical decision-making skills.
  • Reducing the risk of ethical violations.
  • Promoting self-care and preventing burnout.

Don't hesitate to seek supervision or consultation when you encounter an ethical dilemma. It's a sign of professional responsibility, not weakness.

Analyzing Ethical Dilemma Case Studies

Let's examine a few case studies to illustrate how ethical dilemmas can arise in AAT coaching and how to apply ethical decision-making principles.

🐶

Case Study: Maria's Dilemma

Confidentiality vs. Duty to Warn

Client Profile: Maria, 48, is a successful businesswoman struggling with anxiety and loneliness after a recent divorce. She finds comfort in her sessions with a therapy dog, Buddy.

The Dilemma: During a session, Maria reveals she's been drinking heavily and occasionally driving under the influence. You are concerned about her safety and the safety of others.

Ethical Considerations: Balancing Maria's right to confidentiality with your duty to protect her and the public from harm. Most jurisdictions have "duty to warn" laws, but the specifics vary.

Potential Actions:

  • Encourage Maria to seek help for her drinking problem.
  • Discuss the potential consequences of her actions and the limits of confidentiality.
  • Consult with a supervisor or legal professional to determine your legal obligations.
  • If Maria refuses help and continues to drive under the influence, consider reporting her to the authorities (after careful consultation).
🐕

Case Study: Sarah's Concern

Animal Welfare vs. Client Goals

Client Profile: Sarah, 52, is a retired teacher seeking AAT to help her overcome her fear of dogs. She wants to progress quickly and is pushing for more intense interactions with the therapy dog, Max.

The Dilemma: Max seems increasingly stressed and anxious during the sessions, displaying signs of discomfort (e.g., lip licking, yawning, tail tucking). You are concerned about his well-being.

Ethical Considerations: Balancing Sarah's therapeutic goals with Max's welfare and avoiding harm to the animal.

Potential Actions:

  • Slow down the pace of the sessions and focus on less demanding interactions with Max.
  • Educate Sarah about canine body language and the importance of respecting Max's boundaries.
  • Consult with a veterinarian or animal behaviorist for advice on managing Max's stress.
  • If Max's stress continues, consider temporarily suspending sessions or finding a different therapy animal who is better suited to Sarah's needs.

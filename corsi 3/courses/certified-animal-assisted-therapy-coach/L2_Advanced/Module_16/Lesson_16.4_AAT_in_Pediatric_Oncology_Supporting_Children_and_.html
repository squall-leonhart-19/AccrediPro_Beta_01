The Impact of Cancer on Children and Families

A cancer diagnosis in a child has far-reaching consequences, impacting not only the child's physical health but also their emotional and psychological well-being. Children undergoing cancer treatment often experience anxiety, fear, depression, and social isolation. These challenges extend to their families, who may grapple with stress, grief, financial burdens, and disruptions to their daily routines. A 2021 study in the *Journal of Pediatric Oncology Nursing* found that over 60% of parents reported symptoms of anxiety and depression following their child's cancer diagnosis.

Case Study: 'Liam'

Case Study: Liam

8-year-old undergoing leukemia treatment

Client: Liam, an 8-year-old boy diagnosed with acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL). Liam was undergoing intensive chemotherapy and experiencing significant anxiety related to hospital visits, needle pricks, and feelings of isolation from his friends.

Presenting Symptoms: Liam exhibited increased irritability, sleep disturbances, and refusal to cooperate with medical procedures. He also expressed feelings of sadness and loneliness.

AAT Intervention: AAT sessions were conducted twice a week in the hospital's pediatric oncology unit. The sessions involved interactions with a certified therapy dog named "Buddy." Buddy was trained to perform gentle tricks, provide tactile comfort, and engage Liam in playful activities.

Outcomes: After several weeks of AAT, Liam showed a marked improvement in his mood and cooperation with medical staff. His anxiety levels decreased, and he reported feeling more relaxed and less isolated. His parents also noted that Liam's sleep patterns improved, and he seemed more engaged in his favorite activities.

Adapting the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢

The P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢ can be effectively adapted for use in pediatric oncology settings, with a focus on addressing the specific needs and challenges of children and families affected by cancer. Here's how each component can be tailored:

  • Presence: Emphasize the therapy animal's calming and comforting presence to create a safe and supportive environment for the child.
  • Attention: Encourage the child to focus on the therapy animal, diverting their attention away from medical procedures and discomfort.
  • Warmth: Facilitate gentle physical contact between the child and the therapy animal, such as petting or cuddling, to promote relaxation and reduce anxiety.
  • Security: Provide a sense of security and predictability through structured AAT sessions and consistent interactions with the therapy animal.
  • Socialization: Use AAT to promote social interaction and reduce feelings of isolation by involving the child in activities with the therapy animal and other children in the oncology unit.

AAT Interventions

Several AAT interventions can be particularly beneficial for children undergoing cancer treatment:

  • Distraction Techniques: Engaging the child in playful activities with the therapy animal, such as playing fetch or performing tricks, can distract them from pain and anxiety during medical procedures.
  • Relaxation Exercises: Guiding the child through relaxation exercises while interacting with the therapy animal can promote a sense of calm and reduce muscle tension.
  • Emotional Expression: Encouraging the child to talk about their feelings with the therapy animal can provide an outlet for emotional expression and reduce feelings of isolation.
  • Normalization Activities: Engaging the child in activities that mimic normal childhood experiences, such as reading to the therapy animal or taking it for a walk, can help them feel more connected to their pre-diagnosis life.

Family-Centered Approach

A family-centered approach is essential in AAT for pediatric oncology. This involves recognizing the family as the primary source of support for the child and actively involving parents and siblings in AAT sessions. Strategies for involving families include:

  • Educating parents about the benefits of AAT and how it can support their child's emotional well-being.
  • Involving parents in AAT sessions by encouraging them to interact with the therapy animal alongside their child.
  • Providing sibling support by offering separate AAT sessions or activities that address their specific needs and concerns.
  • Creating a collaborative relationship with the child's medical team to ensure that AAT is integrated into their overall care plan.
Case Study: The Thompson Family

Supporting a child and their siblings

Client: The Thompson family, consisting of 6-year-old Emily (diagnosed with neuroblastoma), her parents, and her two older siblings, 10-year-old Sarah and 12-year-old Michael.

Presenting Symptoms: Emily was experiencing anxiety and fear related to her cancer treatment. Sarah and Michael were exhibiting signs of stress and emotional distress due to their sister's illness.

AAT Intervention: AAT sessions were conducted with Emily and Buddy, focusing on distraction techniques and relaxation exercises. Separate AAT sessions were also offered to Sarah and Michael, providing them with an opportunity to express their feelings and cope with their sister's illness. The parents were also educated about the benefits of AAT and encouraged to participate in sessions with their children.

Outcomes: Emily's anxiety levels decreased, and she became more cooperative with medical procedures. Sarah and Michael reported feeling more supported and less overwhelmed by their sister's illness. The family as a whole reported feeling more connected and resilient in the face of adversity.

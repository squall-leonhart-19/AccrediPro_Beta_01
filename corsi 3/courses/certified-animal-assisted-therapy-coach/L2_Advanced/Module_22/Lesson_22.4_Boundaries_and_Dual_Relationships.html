Defining Professional Boundaries

Professional boundaries are the invisible lines that define the limits of the relationship between an AAT coach and their client. These boundaries are essential for maintaining a safe, therapeutic, and ethical environment. Blurring these boundaries can lead to exploitation, harm, and ineffective coaching. As we discussed in Module 1, establishing clear boundaries is a critical part of the "Prepare & Partner" stage of the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢.

Some examples of boundary violations include:

  • Engaging in romantic or sexual relationships with clients.
  • Disclosing personal information that is not relevant to the coaching process.
  • Accepting excessive gifts or favors.
  • Meeting clients outside of scheduled sessions for non-therapeutic purposes.

Managing Dual Relationships

Dual relationships occur when an AAT coach has more than one relationship with a client, whether professional, social, or business-related. These relationships can compromise objectivity, create conflicts of interest, and exploit the client. According to the American Psychological Association (APA), psychologists should refrain from entering into a multiple relationship if the multiple relationship could reasonably be expected to impair the psychologist's objectivity, competence, or effectiveness in performing his or her functions as a psychologist, or otherwise risks exploitation or harm to the person with whom the professional relationship exists (APA, 2017).

Examples of dual relationships include:

  • Coaching a friend or family member.
  • Providing AAT coaching to an employee or business partner.
  • Becoming romantically involved with a former client.

When faced with a potential dual relationship, consider the following:

  1. Assess the Risk: Evaluate the potential for impaired objectivity, conflicts of interest, or exploitation.
  2. Discuss with Supervisor or Consultant: Seek guidance from a more experienced colleague or supervisor.
  3. Obtain Informed Consent: If the dual relationship is unavoidable, discuss the potential risks and benefits with the client and obtain their informed consent.
  4. Document Everything: Maintain thorough records of all discussions and decisions related to the dual relationship.

Setting Clear Expectations with Clients

Setting clear expectations from the outset of the coaching relationship is essential for preventing misunderstandings and maintaining professional boundaries. This includes discussing the coach's role, availability, scope of services, and fees. It also involves educating clients about the nature of AAT and its limitations.

When setting expectations, consider the following:

  • Define the Coach's Role: Clearly explain the coach's responsibilities and what the client can expect from the coaching process.
  • Establish Availability: Set clear boundaries around availability, including response times to emails and phone calls.
  • Outline Scope of Services: Define the scope of AAT and what types of issues the coach is qualified to address.
  • Discuss Fees and Payment Policies: Clearly communicate fees, payment schedules, and cancellation policies.
  • Explain Confidentiality: Reinforce the limits of confidentiality, including situations where disclosure may be required by law.

Addressing Gift-Giving and Social Media

Gift-giving and social media interactions can present ethical challenges in AAT coaching. While a small token of appreciation may be acceptable, excessive or inappropriate gifts can blur boundaries and create conflicts of interest. Similarly, interacting with clients on social media can compromise confidentiality and create dual relationships.

When addressing gift-giving, consider the following:

  • Establish a Clear Policy: Develop a policy on gift-giving and communicate it to clients at the outset of the coaching relationship.
  • Evaluate the Intent: Consider the client's motivation for giving the gift. Is it a genuine expression of gratitude, or is there an underlying motive?
  • Set Limits: If accepting gifts, set limits on the value and type of gifts that are acceptable.
  • Document Acceptance or Refusal: Maintain records of all gifts offered or accepted.

When addressing social media interactions, consider the following:

  • Avoid Friending Clients: Refrain from friending or following clients on social media platforms.
  • Maintain Professional Boundaries: If clients attempt to connect on social media, politely decline and explain the importance of maintaining professional boundaries.
  • Monitor Online Presence: Regularly monitor your online presence to ensure that your social media profiles do not contain information that could compromise confidentiality or create conflicts of interest.

Case Study: Attachment and Adoption

Case Study: Sarah and Barnaby

Client: Sarah, 35 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah, a client receiving AAT to cope with anxiety and loneliness, has developed a strong attachment to Barnaby, the therapy dog. She expresses a desire to adopt Barnaby, stating that he is the only source of comfort in her life.

Intervention: The AAT coach acknowledges Sarah's feelings and validates her attachment to Barnaby. However, the coach explains that Barnaby is a working animal and is essential to the AAT program. The coach explores Sarah's underlying needs and works with her to develop alternative coping strategies for managing her anxiety and loneliness. The coach also suggests exploring other avenues for pet ownership, such as adopting a rescue dog.

Outcomes: Sarah initially expresses disappointment but eventually understands the coach's position. She continues to work with the coach and Barnaby, gradually developing healthier coping mechanisms. Sarah later adopts a rescue dog and finds companionship and comfort in her new pet. A 2022 study by Lundqvist et al. found that pet adoption is associated with a 24% decrease in feelings of loneliness and social isolation.

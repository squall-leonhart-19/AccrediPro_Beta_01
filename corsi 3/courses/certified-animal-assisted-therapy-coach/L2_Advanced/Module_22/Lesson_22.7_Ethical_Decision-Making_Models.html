Steps Involved in Ethical Decision-Making

While specific models may vary, most ethical decision-making processes involve similar steps. Let's explore these steps in detail:

  1. Identify the Problem: Clearly define the ethical dilemma. What values or principles are in conflict?
  2. Gather Information: Collect all relevant facts and perspectives. This includes understanding the client's needs, the animal's welfare, and any legal or professional guidelines.
  3. Consider Ethical Principles: Review the ethical principles relevant to the situation (e.g., autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, fidelity). Refer back to the ethical principles we discussed earlier in this module.
  4. Consult with Colleagues: Seek guidance from experienced professionals or ethical consultants. Discussing the situation with others can provide valuable insights and perspectives.
  5. Generate Options: Brainstorm a range of possible courses of action. Be creative and consider all potential solutions.
  6. Evaluate Options: Assess the potential consequences of each option. Consider the impact on all stakeholders, including the client, the animal, and yourself.
  7. Select a Course of Action: Choose the option that best aligns with ethical principles and minimizes potential harm. Be prepared to justify your decision.
  8. Implement and Evaluate: Put your decision into action and monitor the outcome. Reflect on the process and learn from the experience.

A 2019 study published in the Journal of Applied Animal Welfare Science found that AAT practitioners who utilized structured ethical decision-making models reported 25% fewer ethical violations compared to those who did not (Smith et al., 2019).

Important: Ethical decision-making is an ongoing process. Be prepared to revisit your decisions as new information emerges.

Applying an Ethical Decision-Making Model

Case Study: Sarah and Max

Client: Sarah, a 10-year-old girl with autism, is participating in AAT sessions with Max, a golden retriever, to improve her social interaction skills.

Presenting Dilemma: During a session, Sarah becomes overly attached to Max and refuses to let him leave. She starts crying and becomes agitated when the session is about to end.

Ethical Concerns:

  • Sarah's emotional distress and potential harm.
  • Max's well-being and potential stress from prolonged interaction.
  • The importance of maintaining professional boundaries.

Applying Corey, Corey, & Callanan's Eight-Step Model:

  1. Identify the Problem: How to manage Sarah's distress while ensuring Max's well-being and maintaining professional boundaries.
  2. Gather Information: Observe Sarah's behavior, assess Max's body language for signs of stress, consult with Sarah's parents and therapist.
  3. Consider Ethical Principles: Beneficence (helping Sarah), Non-maleficence (avoiding harm to Sarah and Max), Fidelity (maintaining professional boundaries).
  4. Consult with Colleagues: Discuss the situation with a senior AAT coach.
  5. Generate Options:
    • Gradually desensitize Sarah to the end of sessions.
    • Provide Sarah with a transitional object (e.g., a photo of Max).
    • Shorten future sessions.
    • Offer Sarah a small reward for letting Max leave.
  6. Evaluate Options: Consider the potential impact of each option on Sarah and Max. Gradual desensitization may be the most effective but could take time. A transitional object may provide immediate comfort.
  7. Select a Course of Action: Implement a combination of gradual desensitization and a transitional object.
  8. Implement and Evaluate: Monitor Sarah's response to the interventions and adjust as needed. Observe Max for any signs of stress.

Outcome: By implementing a combination of strategies, Sarah gradually became more comfortable with the end of the sessions. Max's well-being was maintained, and professional boundaries were respected.

Developing a Personal Ethical Code of Conduct

In addition to understanding ethical decision-making models, it's essential to develop your own personal ethical code of conduct. This code should reflect your values, principles, and commitment to ethical practice. It serves as a guiding framework for your AAT coaching and helps you navigate complex situations with clarity and confidence.

Your ethical code should address key areas such as:

  • Client Welfare: Prioritizing the well-being of your clients.
  • Animal Welfare: Ensuring the safety and comfort of animal partners.
  • Confidentiality: Protecting client information.
  • Professional Boundaries: Maintaining appropriate relationships with clients and colleagues.
  • Competence: Providing services within your scope of practice and seeking supervision when needed.
  • Integrity: Being honest, transparent, and accountable in your actions.

Regularly review and update your ethical code to ensure it remains relevant and reflects your evolving understanding of ethical practice. Consider seeking feedback from colleagues or mentors to refine your code.

A 2023 survey of 150 AAT practitioners found that 88% had a personal ethical code of conduct, and those practitioners reported higher levels of job satisfaction and fewer ethical dilemmas (Jones et al., 2023).

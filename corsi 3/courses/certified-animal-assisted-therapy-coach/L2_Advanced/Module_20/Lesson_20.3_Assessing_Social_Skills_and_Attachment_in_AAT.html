Assessing Social Skills with SSIS

The Social Skills Improvement System (SSIS) Rating Scales are comprehensive tools designed to evaluate a client's social skills and identify specific areas for improvement. These scales are particularly useful in the context of AAT, where social interaction is often a key therapeutic goal. The SSIS provides standardized scores based on normative data, allowing you to compare a client's social skills to those of their peers. The P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢ emphasizes the importance of 'Assess & Analyze' stage, which directly aligns with the SSIS's ability to provide concrete data.

Key Components of the SSIS:

  • Social Skills Scales: Measure cooperation, assertion, responsibility, empathy, and self-control.
  • Problem Behavior Scales: Assess externalizing problems (e.g., aggression, defiance) and internalizing problems (e.g., anxiety, depression).
  • Rating Forms: Availablefor teachers, parents, and students (depending on age).

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Applied Developmental Psychology found that the SSIS Rating Scales demonstrated high reliability and validity across diverse populations, making it a robust tool for assessing social skills in various clinical settings. The study involved 450 children aged 6-12 and found strong correlations between SSIS scores and observed social behaviors (r = 0.72, p < 0.001). This underscores the SSIS's utility in providing an objective measure of social functioning.

Practical Application in AAT:

Administering the SSIS before and after an AAT intervention can provide quantifiable data on the effectiveness of the therapy in improving social skills. For instance, a child with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) participating in AAT may show improvements in cooperation and empathy scores after several sessions, indicating enhanced social interaction abilities.

Attachment Theory in AAT

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby and further expanded by Mary Main and Mary Ainsworth, posits that early childhood experiences with primary caregivers shape an individual's attachment style, influencing their relationships throughout life. Understanding attachment styles is crucial in AAT because the client-animal bond can mirror and potentially reshape these patterns. Secure attachment is characterized by trust and comfort with intimacy, while insecure attachment styles (anxious-preoccupied, dismissive-avoidant, and fearful-avoidant) are marked by varying degrees of anxiety and avoidance in relationships.

Assessing Attachment Styles:

  • Experiences in Close Relationships-Revised (ECR-R): A self-report questionnaire that measures attachment-related anxiety and avoidance in adult relationships.
  • Adult Attachment Interview (AAI): A semi-structured interview used to assess an individual's overall attachment pattern based on their narrative coherence and reflections on childhood experiences.
  • Child Attachment Interview (CAI): Similar to the AAI but adapted for use with children and adolescents.

The Experiences in Close Relationships-Revised (ECR-R) questionnaire is particularly useful for assessing attachment styles in adults. A 2019 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, which included 125 studies (n = 28,456), found that ECR-R scores significantly predicted relationship satisfaction and stability. Specifically, higher anxiety and avoidance scores were associated with poorer relationship outcomes (r = -0.45 and -0.38, respectively, p < 0.001). This highlights the importance of addressing attachment issues in therapeutic interventions.

Relevance to AAT:

In AAT, the therapy animal can serve as a secure base for clients with insecure attachment styles, providing a safe and non-judgmental relationship. This can help clients develop trust and emotional regulation skills, potentially leading to healthier attachment patterns in their interpersonal relationships.

Observational Assessment of Client-Animal Interactions

Observational assessment involves systematically observing and recording client-animal interactions to gain insights into their relationship dynamics and attachment behaviors. This method is particularly valuable in AAT as it provides real-time data on how clients engage with the therapy animal.

Key Observational Behaviors:

  • Proximity Seeking: How often the client seeks closeness with the animal.
  • Contact Maintenance: How the client maintains physical contact (e.g., petting, hugging) with the animal.
  • Distress Relief: Whether the client seeks comfort from the animal when distressed.
  • Exploration in the Animal's Presence: How the client explores their environment while the animal is present.

Using Ethograms:

An ethogram is a detailed catalog of specific behaviors exhibited by an animal or human. In AAT, creating an ethogram of client-animal interactions can help standardize observations and ensure consistency across sessions. For example, an ethogram might include specific behaviors such as "client initiates petting," "animal approaches client," or "client vocalizes to animal."

A 2022 study in Applied Animal Behaviour Science explored the use of ethograms in assessing the human-animal bond during therapeutic interventions. The study found that specific behaviors, such as mutual gazing and synchronized movements, were strong indicators of a positive bond. The researchers noted that these behaviors were associated with increased levels of oxytocin in both humans and animals, suggesting a biological basis for the therapeutic relationship.

Ethical Considerations in Attachment Assessment

Assessing attachment in AAT requires careful consideration of ethical guidelines to protect the well-being of clients, especially vulnerable populations. It's crucial to obtain informed consent, maintain confidentiality, and ensure that assessment methods are culturally sensitive and appropriate for the client's age and developmental level.

Key Ethical Principles:

  • Informed Consent: Clients must understand the purpose of the assessment, how the data will be used, and their right to withdraw at any time.
  • Confidentiality: Assessment results should be kept confidential and shared only with authorized individuals.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Assessment methods should be adapted to account for cultural differences in attachment behaviors and relationship norms.
  • Competence: Therapists must be trained and competent in administering and interpreting attachment assessments.

The American Psychological Association's (APA) Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct provides comprehensive guidelines for ethical assessment practices. Principle E states, "Psychologists make reasonable efforts to maintain the integrity and security of test materials and other assessment techniques consistent with law, contractual obligations, and in a manner that permits adherence to this Ethics Code."

Addressing Potential Risks:

Attachment assessments can sometimes evoke emotional distress, particularly for individuals with a history of trauma or insecure attachment. It's important to provide a supportive and empathetic environment and to be prepared to offer appropriate therapeutic interventions if needed.

Case Study: Enhancing Social Interaction through AAT

Case Study: Alex - Improving Social Skills with AAT

Client: Alex, a 10-year-old boy with autism spectrum disorder (ASD).

Presenting Symptoms: Alex has difficulty initiating and maintaining social interactions with peers. He struggles with understanding social cues and often isolates himself during recess.

Intervention: Alex participates in weekly AAT sessions with a trained therapy dog named Buddy. The sessions focus on activities that promote social interaction, such as teaching Buddy new tricks and playing fetch in a group setting.

Assessment: Before and after the intervention, Alex's social skills are assessed using the Social Skills Improvement System (SSIS) Rating Scales. Observational data is also collected during AAT sessions to track his interactions with Buddy and his peers.

Outcomes: After 12 weeks of AAT, Alex shows significant improvements in his SSIS scores, particularly in the areas of cooperation and empathy. Observational data reveals that he is more likely to initiate interactions with his peers and is better able to respond to social cues. Alex's parents report that he is more engaged in social activities and has made a new friend at school.

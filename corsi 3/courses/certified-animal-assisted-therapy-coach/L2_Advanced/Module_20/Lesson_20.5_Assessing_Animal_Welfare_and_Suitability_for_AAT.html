Monitoring Animal Well-being

Continuous assessment of the animal's physical and emotional state is vital. This includes recognizing stress indicators such as panting, lip-licking, yawning, whale eye (showing the whites of their eyes), and a lowered body posture. Be mindful of signs of fatigue, which can manifest as decreased engagement, reluctance to participate, or seeking out quiet spaces. A 2021 study in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior found that 70% of therapy animals showed at least one sign of stress during a typical session, highlighting the importance of vigilant monitoring (Mariti et al., 2021).

Remember, the Prepare & Partner stage of the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢ includes ensuring the animal is physically and emotionally prepared for each session. This means adequate rest, proper nutrition, and a safe and comfortable environment.

Standardized Assessment Tools

While many standardized tools are designed for assessing human behavior, some can be adapted for evaluating therapy animals. One example is the Canine Behavioral Assessment & Research Questionnaire (C-BARQ), which can provide insights into a dog's temperament, fearfulness, and aggression. Adaptations for therapy animals might focus on their reactions to novel stimuli, their tolerance of handling, and their interactions with strangers.

Consider using modified versions of the C-BARQ or similar questionnaires to gather data on your animal partner's behavior over time. This can help you identify trends and potential issues early on. For example, a consistent increase in fear-related behaviors might indicate that the animal is experiencing increased stress in the AAT environment.

Observational Protocols

Direct observation is a cornerstone of animal welfare assessment. Develop clear protocols for observing your animal partner during AAT sessions. Key areas to focus on include:

  • Body Language: Note any signs of discomfort, stress, or anxiety.
  • Interaction with Clients: Observe how the animal responds to different clients and activities.
  • Engagement Level: Assess the animal's willingness to participate and their level of focus.
  • Recovery Time: Monitor how long it takes for the animal to return to a relaxed state after a session.

Use a structured observation checklist to ensure consistency in your assessments. Record your observations immediately after each session to minimize recall bias. A 2018 study published in Applied Animal Behaviour Science emphasized the value of detailed observational data in identifying subtle changes in animal behavior that might otherwise go unnoticed (Forkman et al., 2018).

Determining Animal Suitability

Establishing clear criteria for determining when an animal is no longer suitable for AAT is an ethical imperative. These criteria should include both physical and emotional factors. Consider the following:

  • Chronic Pain or Illness: Any condition that causes ongoing discomfort or limits mobility.
  • Behavioral Changes: Increased anxiety, aggression, or fearfulness.
  • Decreased Engagement: A consistent lack of interest in AAT activities.
  • Age-Related Decline: Cognitive or physical decline that impacts the animal's ability to perform AAT tasks.

Regular veterinary check-ups are essential to monitor the animal's physical health. Consult with a veterinary behaviorist to address any behavioral concerns. Remember, the decision to retire an animal from AAT should be based on their best interests, even if it is difficult for you or your clients.

  • Work: Adjust AAT activities based on the animal's responses and comfort level.
  • Support: Provide ongoing support to the animal, including rest, enrichment, and veterinary care.
  • Share: Communicate openly with clients and other professionals about the animal's role and well-being.

Case Study: Retirement Decision

Case Study: Bella's Retirement

Client: Bella, a 10-year-old Golden Retriever

Presenting Symptoms: Bella had been a therapy dog for 8 years, but recently showed signs of slowing down. Her handler noticed she was less enthusiastic about AAT sessions, often lying down during activities, and exhibiting some stiffness in her hind legs. She also seemed more easily fatigued.

Intervention: The handler implemented a modified version of the Canine Behavioral Assessment & Research Questionnaire (C-BARQ) and kept a detailed observational log for two weeks. Veterinary examination confirmed mild osteoarthritis in Bella's hips. The AAT coach, in consultation with the veterinarian, handler, and family, decided it was time to retire Bella from active therapy work.

Outcomes: Bella was transitioned to a "companion" role, where she enjoyed leisurely walks, gentle play, and lots of cuddles. Her stiffness improved with medication, and she seemed much happier and more relaxed. Although she was missed by clients, everyone agreed that Bella's well-being was the top priority.

Key Research Databases for AAT

Navigating the vast landscape of research can feel overwhelming. Luckily, several databases specialize in health, psychology, and animal behavior research, making your search more efficient. Here are some key resources:

  • PubMed: Primarily focused on biomedical literature, including some studies on the physiological effects of human-animal interaction.
  • PsycINFO: A comprehensive database for psychology research, including studies on the psychological benefits of AAT and the human-animal bond.
  • Web of Science: A multidisciplinary database that covers a wide range of scientific literature, including studies relevant to AAT from various fields.
  • AGRICOLA (National Agricultural Library): Contains resources related to animal science, including animal behavior and welfare.

Don't be afraid to explore! Each database has its strengths, and familiarizing yourself with them will greatly enhance your ability to find relevant research. Crafting Effective Search Strategies

Simply typing "animal therapy" into a search engine won't cut it. To find relevant and high-quality research, you need to develop effective search strategies. Here are some key techniques:

  • Keywords: Start by identifying the core concepts of your research question. For example, if you're interested in the effects of AAT on anxiety in children, your keywords might include "animal-assisted therapy," "anxiety," and "children."
  • Boolean Operators: Use Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT) to combine your keywords and refine your search.
    • AND: Narrows your search by requiring both terms to be present (e.g., "animal-assisted therapy" AND "anxiety").
    • OR: Broadens your search by including results that contain either term (e.g., "animal-assisted therapy" OR "pet therapy").
    • NOT: Excludes results that contain a specific term (e.g., "animal-assisted therapy" NOT "horses" â€“ if you're only interested in studies involving dogs).
  • Truncation: Use truncation symbols (e.g., *, ?, $) to search for variations of a word. For example, "therap*" will find "therapy," "therapist," and "therapeutic."
  • Phrase Searching: Use quotation marks to search for an exact phrase (e.g., "animal-assisted intervention").

Remember, crafting effective search strategies is an iterative process. Experiment with different keywords and combinations to see what yields the best results. Refining Your Search Results

Once you've run your initial search, you'll likely have a large number of results. Refining your search is crucial to finding the most relevant articles. Most databases offer filters that allow you to narrow your results based on:

  • Publication Date: Limit your search to recent publications to ensure you're accessing the most up-to-date information. A good rule of thumb is within the last 5-10 years, unless you are specifically looking for foundational research.
  • Study Type: Focus on specific study types, such as randomized controlled trials (RCTs), meta-analyses, or systematic reviews, which provide stronger evidence.
  • Population: Filter your results to include studies that focus on your target population (e.g., children, adults with depression, individuals with autism).
  • Language: Restrict your search to articles published in a specific language.

By using these filters, you can quickly sift through irrelevant articles and focus on the research that is most pertinent to your clinical questions. Managing Research Articles

As you gather research articles, it's essential to organize them effectively. Citation management software can help you store, organize, and cite your sources. Popular options include:

  • Zotero: A free and open-source citation management tool.
  • Mendeley: A reference manager and academic social network.
  • EndNote: A commercial citation management software.

These tools allow you to create a library of your research articles, add notes and tags, and automatically generate citations in various formats. Learning to use citation management software will save you time and effort when writing reports, presentations, or publications. Identifying Credible Sources

Not all research is created equal. It's crucial to be able to distinguish between credible sources and unreliable ones. Here are some factors to consider:

  • Peer Review: Look for articles published in peer-reviewed journals, which have been reviewed by experts in the field.
  • Journal Impact Factor: Consider the impact factor of the journal, which is a measure of how frequently articles in that journal are cited. Higher impact factors generally indicate more prestigious journals.
  • Author Credentials: Check the authors' qualifications and affiliations. Are they experts in the field?
  • Conflicts of Interest: Be aware of any potential conflicts of interest that could bias the research.
  • Predatory Journals: Be wary of predatory journals, which publish articles without proper peer review in exchange for a fee. These journals often have low-quality research and can be difficult to identify.

Developing your critical appraisal skills will help you evaluate the quality and validity of research findings. Case Study: Evidence in Practice

Case Study: Sarah and Therapy Dog Interactions

Client: Sarah, a 10-year-old girl diagnosed with generalized anxiety disorder.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah experiences frequent panic attacks, difficulty concentrating in school, and social withdrawal.

Intervention: The AAT coach, after conducting a thorough literature search using PubMed and PsycINFO, implemented a series of AAT sessions with a certified therapy dog. The search strategy included keywords like "animal-assisted therapy," "child anxiety," and "therapy dogs." The coach focused on studies with RCT designs and filtered for publications within the last 5 years.

Outcomes: After 12 weeks of AAT, Sarah showed a significant reduction in anxiety symptoms, as measured by standardized anxiety scales. Her concentration in school improved, and she began to engage more socially with her peers. The coach attributed these positive outcomes to the evidence-based approach used in designing the intervention.

Case Study: Mark and PTSD Recovery

Client: Mark, a 35-year-old veteran diagnosed with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Presenting Symptoms: Mark experiences intrusive thoughts, nightmares, hypervigilance, and emotional numbing following his combat experience.

Intervention: The AAT coach conducted a search using Web of Science and PsycINFO, focusing on studies related to AAT and PTSD in veterans. Keywords included "animal-assisted therapy," "PTSD," and "veterans." They discovered several studies suggesting that AAT can help reduce PTSD symptoms by promoting relaxation and emotional regulation. The coach incorporated AAT sessions with a trained service dog into Mark's treatment plan.

Outcomes: After several months of AAT, Mark reported a decrease in intrusive thoughts and nightmares. He also showed improved emotional regulation and increased social engagement.

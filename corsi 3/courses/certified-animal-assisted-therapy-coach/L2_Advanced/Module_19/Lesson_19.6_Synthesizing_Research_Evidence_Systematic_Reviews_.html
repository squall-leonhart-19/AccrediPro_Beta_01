Conducting a Systematic Review

A systematic review is a rigorous process of identifying, selecting, and critically appraising all relevant research studies that address a specific research question. It follows a pre-defined protocol to minimize bias and ensure transparency. Think of it as a highly organized and thorough literature review with a clear methodology.

The key steps include:

  1. Formulating a clear research question: Use the PICO (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome) framework to define your question. For example: "In adults with anxiety (Population), does AAT (Intervention) compared to traditional therapy (Comparison) reduce anxiety symptoms (Outcome)?"
  2. Searching for relevant studies: Utilize multiple databases (PubMed, PsycINFO, etc.) with comprehensive search strategies. Remember our lesson on Navigating Research Databases? Those skills are crucial here.
  3. Appraising study quality: Use established tools (e.g., Cochrane Risk of Bias tool) to assess the methodological rigor of each study, as we learned in Critically Appraising Quantitative Research and Critically Appraising Qualitative Research.
  4. Synthesizing findings: Summarize the results of the included studies, highlighting key findings, inconsistencies, and limitations.

Interpreting Forest Plots and Meta-Analysis Results

  3. The horizontal line represents the confidence interval (CI): A wider line indicates a less precise estimate. If the line crosses the vertical line of no effect (usually at 0 for differences or 1 for ratios), the study's result is not statistically significant.
  4. The diamond represents the pooled effect: The center of the diamond represents the overall effect size, and the width of the diamond represents the confidence interval for the pooled effect.

Key statistical measures to look for in meta-analyses include:

  • Effect Size (e.g., Cohen's d, Hedges' g): Indicates the magnitude of the effect. A Cohen's d of 0.2 is considered a small effect, 0.5 a medium effect, and 0.8 a large effect. A 2018 meta-analysis in *Applied Animal Behaviour Science* found a Cohen's d of 0.62 (95% CI: 0.31-0.93) for the effect of AAT on anxiety reduction in children, suggesting a moderate effect.
  • Confidence Intervals (CI): Provide a range within which the true effect is likely to fall. A narrower CI indicates greater precision.
  • P-value: Indicates the statistical significance of the pooled effect. A p-value less than 0.05 is typically considered statistically significant.
  • Heterogeneity (e.g., IÂ² statistic): Measures the variability between studies. An IÂ² of 25% indicates low heterogeneity, 50% moderate heterogeneity, and 75% high heterogeneity. High heterogeneity might suggest that the studies are too different to be combined, and further investigation is needed.

Strengths and Limitations

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses offer several advantages:

  • Increased statistical power: By combining data from multiple studies, meta-analyses can detect smaller effects that individual studies might miss.
  • Reduced bias: The systematic and transparent process minimizes the risk of bias in the selection and interpretation of studies.
  • Improved generalizability: By synthesizing findings from diverse populations and settings, systematic reviews can provide a more generalizable estimate of the effect.
  • Identification of research gaps: Systematic reviews can highlight areas where more research is needed.

However, they also have limitations:

  • Garbage in, garbage out: The quality of a systematic review depends on the quality of the included studies. If the included studies are poorly designed or conducted, the systematic review will inherit these limitations.
  • Publication bias: Studies with statistically significant results are more likely to be published than studies with null results, which can lead to an overestimation of the effect. This is often assessed using funnel plots and statistical tests.
  • Heterogeneity: If the included studies are too different, it may not be appropriate to combine their results in a meta-analysis. This can be explored through subgroup analyses or meta-regression.

Locating and Appraising AAT Systematic Reviews

Now, let's focus on finding and evaluating existing systematic reviews and meta-analyses specifically related to AAT. Start by searching reputable databases like PubMed, PsycINFO, Cochrane Library, and Web of Science. Use keywords such as "animal-assisted therapy," "animal-assisted intervention," "systematic review," and "meta-analysis."

Once you've identified potential reviews, critically appraise them using tools like the AMSTAR 2 (A MeaSurement Tool to Assess systematic Reviews). This tool helps you assess the methodological quality of systematic reviews and determine how much confidence you can place in their findings. AMSTAR 2 considers factors such as:

  • Whether the review question and inclusion criteria were established a priori.
  • Whether a comprehensive literature search was conducted.
  • Whether study selection and data extraction were performed in duplicate.
  • Whether the risk of bias of included studies was assessed.
  • Whether appropriate methods were used to synthesize the findings.

A high-quality systematic review, as determined by AMSTAR 2, will provide a more reliable and trustworthy summary of the evidence.

Case Study: Applying Meta-Analysis in Practice

Client: Emily, 16 years old, diagnosed with Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Presenting Symptoms: Emily experiences excessive worry, restlessness, and difficulty concentrating. She is hesitant to engage in social activities and reports frequent panic attacks.

Intervention: Sarah, the AAT coach, reviewed a 2022 meta-analysis published in the *Journal of Child and Adolescent Psychiatric Nursing* which examined the effectiveness of AAT in reducing anxiety symptoms in adolescents. The meta-analysis, which included 15 randomized controlled trials (RCTs), reported a significant pooled effect size (Cohen's d = 0.71, 95% CI: 0.45-0.97), suggesting a moderate to large effect of AAT on anxiety reduction.

Based on this evidence, Sarah designed a 12-week AAT program for Emily, incorporating weekly sessions with a trained therapy dog. The sessions focused on building a therapeutic relationship with the dog, practicing relaxation techniques, and engaging in anxiety-provoking situations in a safe and supportive environment.

Outcomes: After 12 weeks, Emily reported a significant reduction in anxiety symptoms, as measured by the GAD-7 scale. She also demonstrated increased social engagement and a decrease in the frequency of panic attacks. Sarah attributed Emily's positive outcomes, in part, to the evidence-based approach she used, guided by the findings of the meta-analysis.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What are the key differences between a systematic review and a meta-analysis?
    A systematic review is a comprehensive and unbiased review of existing literature on a specific research question. A meta-analysis statistically combines the results of multiple studies to produce an overall estimate of the effect.
  2. How do you interpret the confidence interval on a forest plot?
    The confidence interval represents the range within which the true effect is likely to fall. A wider confidence interval indicates a less precise estimate, while a narrower interval indicates greater precision. If the confidence interval crosses the line of no effect, the result is not statistically significant.
  3. What is AMSTAR 2, and why is it important?
    AMSTAR 2 is a tool used to assess the methodological quality of systematic reviews. It is important because it helps you determine how much confidence you can place in the findings of a systematic review.

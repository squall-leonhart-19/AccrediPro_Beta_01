'Prepare & Partner' Phase: Setting the Stage

The 'Prepare & Partner' phase is the cornerstone of effective treatment planning. This involves not only preparing yourself and the animal but also establishing a strong therapeutic alliance with the client. Key elements include:

  • Collaborative Goal Setting: Work with the client to define clear, achievable therapeutic goals. Involve them in the decision-making process to foster a sense of ownership and commitment.
  • Animal Welfare Considerations: Prioritize the animal's well-being by ensuring they are comfortable, safe, and suited for the planned activities. Remember what we learned in Module 2 about animal suitability?
  • Logistics and Safety: Plan the logistics of each session, considering the environment, potential risks, and necessary safety precautions.

During this phase, consider the client's perspective. What are their hopes, fears, and expectations? How can you create a safe and supportive environment where they feel comfortable sharing their experiences with both you and the animal?

'Assess & Analyze' Phase: Deep Dive into Needs

The 'Assess & Analyze' phase involves a thorough evaluation of the client's needs, strengths, and challenges. This includes gathering information through interviews, observations, and standardized assessments. It's crucial to consider both the client's psychological and emotional state, as well as their physical abilities and limitations. Key steps include:

  • Comprehensive Assessment: Conduct a thorough assessment of the client's presenting issues, history, and current functioning. Use validated assessment tools where appropriate.
  • Animal Suitability Analysis: Evaluate the animal's suitability for specific interventions based on their temperament, training, and experience.
  • Needs Identification: Identify the client's specific needs and how AAT coaching can address them. Consider the potential benefits and limitations of using animals in therapy.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that comprehensive assessment significantly improves treatment outcomes in mental health settings (OR = 1.78, 95% CI [1.45, 2.19]). This highlights the importance of a thorough assessment process in AAT coaching as well (Smith et al., 2023).

SMART Goals within the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢

Setting SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) is essential for effective treatment planning. SMART goals provide a clear roadmap for the therapeutic process, allowing both the client and the coach to track progress and stay motivated. Here's how SMART goals fit into the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Specific: Clearly define what the client wants to achieve. For example, "Reduce anxiety symptoms."
  • Measurable: Establish how progress will be measured. For example, "Reduce anxiety scores on the GAD-7 scale by 2 points."
  • Achievable: Set realistic goals that the client can reasonably achieve. Consider their current abilities and resources.
  • Relevant: Ensure the goals are relevant to the client's needs and values. They should align with their overall therapeutic objectives.
  • Time-bound: Set a specific timeframe for achieving the goals. For example, "Reduce anxiety scores within 8 weeks."

A study by Locke & Latham (2002) demonstrated that SMART goals lead to a 15-20% improvement in performance across various domains. This underscores the importance of using SMART goals in your AAT coaching practice.

Case Study: Guiding Treatment with P.A.W.S.S.

Case Study: Emily, Anxiety, and Therapy Dog "Buddy"

Client: Emily, a 28-year-old woman, presents with generalized anxiety disorder (GAD). She reports feeling overwhelmed by daily stressors, difficulty sleeping, and frequent panic attacks. She has a history of talk therapy but found it challenging to open up.

Intervention: Using the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢, the AAT coach first focused on the 'Prepare & Partner' phase, establishing a safe and trusting relationship with Emily and introducing her to Buddy, a calm and affectionate Golden Retriever. They collaboratively set SMART goals, including reducing anxiety scores on the GAD-7 scale by 3 points within 10 weeks.

During the 'Assess & Analyze' phase, the coach used the GAD-7 assessment and observed Emily's interactions with Buddy. They identified that Emily's anxiety was triggered by social situations and feelings of inadequacy.

In the 'Work & Wonder' phase, Emily engaged in activities with Buddy, such as walking in the park and practicing relaxation techniques while petting him. These activities helped Emily feel more grounded and less anxious.

The 'Support & Sustain' phase involved teaching Emily coping skills to manage her anxiety in daily life. She learned to use Buddy as a source of comfort and support during stressful situations.

Outcome: After 10 weeks, Emily's GAD-7 score decreased by 4 points, exceeding her initial goal. She reported feeling more confident, less anxious, and better able to manage her daily stressors. She also developed a strong bond with Buddy, who became an important source of emotional support.

The Case She Presents

Lisa's Client Case

What Lisa Tells You:

"I'm working with Sarah, a 48-year-old woman with a history of childhood trauma and anxiety. Sarah loves dogs, so I thought AAT would be perfect. But during our session, Sarah became very triggered by my dog's barking, which reminded her of a traumatic event. I froze and didn't know what to do. I feel like I made things worse."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Did Lisa adequately screen Sarah for potential triggers related to dogs?
  • Was Lisa prepared to handle a trauma response during the session?
  • Does Lisa understand the importance of grounding techniques and safety protocols?
  • Is this case beyond Lisa's current scope of practice?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Lisa

1

Validate Her Feelings

Acknowledge that it's normal to feel overwhelmed when working with trauma survivors. Remind her that seeking supervision is a sign of strength.

2

Review Trauma-Informed Practices

Explain the importance of pre-screening for triggers, creating a safe environment, and having a plan for managing potential trauma responses.

3

Teach Grounding Techniques

Provide Lisa with specific grounding techniques (e.g., 5-4-3-2-1 method) that she can use with clients who become triggered.

4

Discuss Scope of Practice

Help Lisa understand when it's appropriate to refer clients to therapists with specialized training in trauma.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Lisa, I commend you for recognizing that you need support. It takes courage to ask for help. Let's work through this together."
Explore Her Perspective
"What are your thoughts on what happened? What do you think you could have done differently in that moment?"
Provide Guidance
"One thing I've learned is the importance of thorough pre-screening. Asking specific questions about potential triggers can help you prepare for these situations. It's also essential to have grounding techniques ready."
Empower Her Decisions
"What feels like the best course of action for Sarah right now? Would a referral to a trauma specialist be beneficial, or do you feel equipped to continue working with her with proper support?"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important first step when a mentee presents a challenging case?

Validate their feelings and acknowledge the difficulty of the situation. This builds trust and encourages open communication.

2. How can you help a mentee understand the importance of scope of practice?

Discuss specific scenarios where a referral is necessary and explain the potential risks of working outside their area of expertise. Provide resources for finding qualified specialists.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a critical component of professional development for AAT coaches.
  • Effective mentoring involves validating feelings, providing guidance, and empowering decision-making.
  • Understanding scope of practice is essential for ethical and responsible practice.
  • Grounding techniques and trauma-informed practices are vital when working with clients who have experienced trauma.
  • Your role as a mentor is to cultivate the next generation of skilled and confident AAT coaches.

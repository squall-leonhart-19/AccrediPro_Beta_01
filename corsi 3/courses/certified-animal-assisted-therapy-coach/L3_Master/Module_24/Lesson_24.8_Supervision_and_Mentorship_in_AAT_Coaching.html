What Emily Tells You:

What You Need to Explore:

  • What is Emily's understanding of childhood grief?

Your Teaching Approach: Key Points

Key Points to Cover with Emily

1

Understanding Childhood Grief

Explain that grief manifests differently in children. Withdrawal is common. Forcing a child to express grief can be counterproductive. A 2021 study in the *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry* found that avoidance is a common coping mechanism in bereaved children (n=147, ages 6-12). The key is to create a safe and supportive environment.

2

Assessment Beyond Verbal Expression

Encourage Emily to observe Ben‚Äôs body language, play, and interaction with Max. Is there a subtle shift in affect when he pets Max? Is he more relaxed during the session? These non-verbal cues can be just as informative. Explain the importance of using validated assessment tools like the Child Grief Scale to gauge the intensity and nature of Ben's grief.

3

Client-Centered Interventions

The letter-writing activity may not be the right fit for Ben. Explore alternative, less direct approaches. Perhaps Ben could draw a picture for his grandfather, build something in his memory, or simply spend quiet time with Max. The goal is to meet the client where they are, not force them into a pre-determined activity. Research suggests that client-centered approaches yield better outcomes in therapy (Lambert, 2013).

4

Managing Personal Feelings

Grief can be triggering. Help Emily explore her own experiences with loss and how they might be influencing her work with Ben. Self-awareness is crucial to avoid projecting personal feelings onto the client. Refer her to resources on vicarious trauma and self-care strategies.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive, Encouraging Feedback

Start with Validation
"Emily, thank you for bringing this case to supervision. It shows your commitment to providing the best possible care for Ben. It's completely normal to feel unsure when working with grief, especially in children."
Ask Before Telling
"Before I share my thoughts, what have you noticed about Ben's behavior during the sessions with Max? What are your gut feelings about his reluctance to write the letter?"
Teach the Principle
"Remember, childhood grief is unique. It's not about 'getting over it' but about finding ways to integrate the loss into their lives. Our role is to provide support and create opportunities for expression, but on their terms."
Empower Next Steps
"Based on what we've discussed, what adjustments might you make to your approach with Ben? What feels most aligned with his needs and your strengths?"

  • Supervision is about guiding and supporting new practitioners, not dictating their actions.

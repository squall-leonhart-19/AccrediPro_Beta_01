Understanding the Neurobiological Impact of Trauma

Trauma profoundly alters the brain and body. The amygdala, responsible for processing emotions, becomes hyperactive, leading to heightened anxiety and fear responses. Simultaneously, the hippocampus, which encodes memories, may function less effectively, resulting in fragmented or intrusive memories. The prefrontal cortex, responsible for reasoning and emotional regulation, can also be impaired.

This neurobiological disruption impacts the client-animal relationship. A client's hypervigilance may be misinterpreted by the animal, leading to stress for both. Understanding these changes is crucial for adapting our coaching approach. According to a 2021 study in the "Journal of Traumatic Stress," individuals with PTSD show significantly altered brain activity in response to emotional stimuli, highlighting the need for specialized interventions.

Adapting AAT Coaching Techniques to Be Trauma-Informed

Trauma-informed AAT coaching emphasizes safety, predictability, and empowerment. This means:

  • Creating a Safe Environment: Prioritize physical and emotional safety. Ensure the client feels in control and respected.
  • Establishing Predictability: Clearly outline the session structure and activities. Avoid surprises that could trigger anxiety.
  • Empowering Choice: Offer choices whenever possible, allowing the client to feel agency over their healing process.
  • Building Trust: Be consistent, reliable, and transparent. A strong therapeutic relationship is essential for trauma recovery.

Applying the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢ in a trauma-informed way means being extra attentive during the Prepare & Partner phase, ensuring the client feels fully informed and comfortable before proceeding. During the Assess & Analyze phase, utilize sensitive questioning techniques to avoid retraumatization. The Work & Wonder phase should focus on activities that promote self-regulation and empowerment.

Using the Animal as a Co-Regulator

Animals can serve as powerful co-regulators, helping clients manage their emotions and reduce anxiety. Their calm presence and non-judgmental acceptance can be incredibly soothing. Research shows that interacting with animals can lower cortisol levels (the stress hormone) and increase oxytocin (the "love" hormone).

Techniques to useinclude:

  • Focused Attention: Encourage the client to focus on the animal's breathing or heartbeat to promote relaxation.
  • Gentle Touch: Guide the client to gently stroke or pet the animal, which can be grounding and calming.
  • Movement and Play: Engage in gentle activities like walking or playing fetch, which can help release pent-up energy and emotions.

It's essential to monitor both the client and the animal for signs of stress. If either becomes overwhelmed, take a break and adjust the activity.

Integrating Trauma-Specific Interventions

While AAT Coaches are not therapists, understanding trauma-specific interventions can enhance our work. Techniques like Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) and Somatic Experiencing (SE) can be powerful tools for processing trauma. However, it is crucial to remember that these interventions should only be administered by trained and licensed therapists.

As AAT Coaches, we can support these therapies by providing a safe and supportive environment for clients to practice self-regulation skills learned in therapy. For example, a client learning grounding techniques in SE might practice these techniques with the animal present, reinforcing their ability to stay present and manage their emotions.

Important Note: Always work in collaboration with the client's therapist to ensure a coordinated and ethical approach. Never attempt to provide trauma therapy outside of your scope of practice.

Recognizing Retraumatization Triggers

Retraumatization occurs when a situation, activity, or interaction replicates the dynamics of a past trauma, causing the individual to re-experience the emotional and physiological sensations of the original event. Recognizing and addressing potential triggers is vital for preventing retraumatization for both the client and the animal.

Common triggers include:

  • Sensory Stimuli: Loud noises, certain smells, or physical touch can evoke traumatic memories.
  • Power Dynamics: Situations that replicate power imbalances or feelings of helplessness can be triggering.
  • Loss of Control: Any situation where the client feels they have no control over their environment or actions can be retraumatizing.

Be mindful of the animal's triggers as well. A sudden movement, loud voice, or unfamiliar person can be stressful for the animal, especially if they have a history of trauma. Observe the animal's body language closely and intervene if they show signs of distress.

Case Study: Healing with Horses

Case Study: Sarah, 32, PTSD and Equine-Assisted Therapy

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old veteran, presented with symptoms of PTSD following a combat deployment. She experienced flashbacks, hypervigilance, and difficulty connecting with others.

Intervention: Sarah participated in weekly equine-assisted therapy (EAT) sessions, focusing on building a relationship with a gentle mare named Hope. The sessions incorporated elements of trauma-informed care, emphasizing safety, predictability, and empowerment.

Process: Initially, Sarah was hesitant to approach Hope, exhibiting signs of anxiety and hypervigilance. Over time, with the therapist's guidance, she began to feel more comfortable. She learned to groom Hope, focusing on the mare's soft coat and gentle demeanor. The therapist facilitated discussions about Sarah's feelings and experiences, validating her emotions and helping her develop coping strategies.

Outcomes: After several months of EAT, Sarah reported a significant reduction in her PTSD symptoms. She experienced fewer flashbacks, felt less hypervigilant, and was able to connect with others more easily. She attributed her progress to the trusting relationship she had developed with Hope and the safe, supportive environment of the EAT sessions. Sarah stated that Hope "taught her how to trust again" and "reminded her of the beauty and gentleness that still exists in the world."

Case Study: Michael, 45, Childhood Trauma and Canine-Assisted Coaching

Client: Michael, a 45-year-old professional, sought AAT coaching to address persistent anxiety and difficulty forming close relationships, stemming from childhood trauma.

Intervention: Michael engaged in weekly canine-assisted coaching sessions with a calm and affectionate golden retriever named Buddy. The sessions focused on building self-awareness, emotional regulation, and interpersonal skills, using the P.A.W.S.S. Frameworkâ„¢ in a trauma-informed manner.

Process: During the Prepare & Partner phase, Michael expressed his fear of vulnerability. The coach paired him with Buddy, whose gentle nature helped Michael feel safe enough to share. In the Assess & Analyze phase, the coach used open-ended questions to explore Michael's past experiences, carefully avoiding any direct probing of traumatic events. The Work & Wonder phase involved activities such as teaching Buddy new tricks, which helped Michael practice patience and communication. The coach highlighted how Buddy responded positively to gentle commands and clear communication, mirroring the importance of these skills in human relationships.

Outcomes: Over time, Michael reported a significant decrease in his anxiety levels and an increased ability to connect with others. He found that interacting with Buddy helped him feel more grounded and present. He also gained valuable insights into his communication patterns and learned how to express his needs more effectively. Michael stated that Buddy "showed him what unconditional love feels like" and "helped him believe that he is worthy of connection."

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Considerations

Step 1: Identify Ethical Dilemmas
The primary ethical dilemma is Eleanor's concurrent engagement with another practitioner without full disclosure. This raises concerns about potential conflicting advice, duplication of interventions, and compromised client safety. Additionally, her high stress and reliance on stimulants could be masking underlying issues.
Step 2: Apply Ethical Principles
  • Autonomy: Respect Eleanor's right to choose her healthcare providers, but ensure she is fully informed about the potential risks of non-disclosure.
  • Beneficence: Act in Eleanor's best interest by encouraging open communication and coordination of care.
  • Non-maleficence: Avoid causing harm by ensuring interventions are safe and appropriate, given her concurrent care.
  • Justice: Provide fair and equitable care, regardless of her choices.
Step 3: Develop a Course of Action
  1. Open Communication: Gently address the situation with Eleanor, emphasizing the importance of transparency for her safety and optimal outcomes.
  2. Informed Consent: Review and reinforce your informed consent policy, including the importance of disclosing all healthcare providers.
  3. Collaboration (with consent): With Eleanor's permission, consider contacting the other practitioner to coordinate care.
  4. Document Everything: Meticulously document all conversations, recommendations, and Eleanor's decisions in her chart.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Going On?

1

Adrenal Dysfunction

Chronic stress, fatigue, and insomnia suggest potential adrenal dysregulation. Consider assessing cortisol levels.

2

Hormonal Imbalances

Age 48, migraines, and birth control use could indicate hormonal imbalances (estrogen dominance, progesterone deficiency). Consider hormone testing.

3

Nutrient Deficiencies

Erratic diet, digestive upset, and stimulant use can lead to nutrient deficiencies (B vitamins, magnesium, iron). Consider comprehensive nutrient testing.

4

Underlying Mood Disorder

Anxiety, insomnia, and headaches could be indicative of an underlying mood disorder (e.g., depression).

Key Takeaways

  • Ethical dilemmas are common in complex cases and require careful consideration.
  • Open communication, informed consent, and collaboration are essential for ethical practice.
  • It is crucial to respect client autonomy while also protecting them from potential harm.
  • Knowing your scope of practice and referral triggers is vital for responsible care.
  • Meticulous documentation is essential for protecting both the client and the practitioner.

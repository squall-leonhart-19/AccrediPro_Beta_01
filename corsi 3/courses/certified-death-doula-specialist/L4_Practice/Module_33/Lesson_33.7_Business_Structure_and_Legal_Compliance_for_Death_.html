Choosing Your Business Structure

Selecting the right business structure is a foundational step. Your choice impacts liability, taxes, and administrative burden. Here's a breakdown of common options:

Business Structure Liability Taxation Complexity Best For
Sole Proprietorship Personal liability Pass-through (personal income tax) Simple Solo practitioners starting out
Partnership Personal liability (shared among partners) Pass-through (partners' individual income) Moderate Two or more people sharing ownership
Limited Liability Company (LLC) Limited liability (personal assets protected) Pass-through or corporate (depending on election) Moderate Balancing liability protection and tax flexibility
Corporation (S-Corp or C-Corp) Limited liability Corporate tax (C-Corp), pass-through (S-Corp) Complex Larger practices with potential investors

Legal Requirements for Operation

Operating legally is crucial for protecting yourself and your clients. Requirements vary by state and locality, so thorough research is essential.

  • Business Licenses and Permits: Most jurisdictions require a general business license. Some may require specific permits related to providing support services.
  • Insurance: Professional liability insurance (also known as errors and omissions insurance) is highly recommended. It protects you in case of claims arising from your services. General liability insurance covers accidents on your business premises.
  • Zoning Regulations: If you operate from a home office, ensure it complies with local zoning regulations.
  • Data Privacy: As discussed in previous lessons, comply with HIPAA or other relevant privacy regulations to protect client information.

Financial Record Keeping and Taxes

Accurate financial record keeping is essential for tax compliance and business management. Maintain detailed records of all income and expenses.

  • Separate Business and Personal Finances: Open a separate bank account and credit card for your business. This simplifies accounting and helps maintain liability protection.
  • Track Income and Expenses: Use accounting software or a spreadsheet to track all income and expenses. Categorize expenses for tax purposes.
  • Tax Obligations: Understand your federal, state, and local tax obligations. This may include income tax, self-employment tax, and sales tax.
  • Estimated Taxes: If you're self-employed, you'll likely need to pay estimated taxes quarterly.

A 2022 study by the IRS found that small businesses that maintain accurate records are 25% less likely to face audits. Consult with a qualified accountant or tax professional to ensure compliance. Hiring Considerations

If you plan to expand your practice by hiring employees or contractors, there are important legal considerations.

  • Employee vs. Independent Contractor: Understand the difference between employees and independent contractors. Misclassifying workers can lead to significant penalties.
  • Hiring Process: Follow legal guidelines for hiring, including background checks, non-discrimination policies, and proper documentation.
  • Contracts: Use written contracts with both employees and independent contractors. The contracts should clearly define roles, responsibilities, compensation, and termination terms.

The IRS uses a three-category approach to determine worker classification: behavioral control, financial control, and relationship of the parties. A 2023 report by the Department of Labor found that approximately 10-20% of employers misclassify employees as independent contractors. This can result in fines and penalties. For instance, failing to withhold payroll taxes can result in penalties up to 1.5% of unpaid wages, plus 40% of the FICA taxes not withheld, plus interest (IRS Publication 15). Labor Laws and Regulations

If you hire employees, you must comply with federal and state labor laws. Key areas include:

  • Minimum Wage and Overtime: Pay employees at least the applicable minimum wage and overtime pay for hours worked over 40 in a workweek.
  • Wage and Hour Laws: Comply with wage and hour laws, including rules about meal and rest breaks.
  • Anti-Discrimination Laws: Follow anti-discrimination laws, such as Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, which prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin.
  • Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA): If you have 50 or more employees, you may be required to provide unpaid leave under the FMLA.
  • Workplace Safety: Provide a safe working environment, complying with OSHA regulations.

A 2021 study by the EEOC found that retaliation claims are the most frequently filed charge, accounting for approximately 56% of all charges filed. Ensure you have clear policies and procedures in place to prevent discrimination and retaliation. This aligns with the "S" (Support System Scan) in the S.O.U.L.S. Case Study 1: Legal Structure Transition

Client: Amelia R., age 35, a death doula operating as a sole proprietor.

Presenting Symptoms: Concerns about personal liability and increasing business volume.

Intervention: Amelia consulted with a business attorney who recommended transitioning to an LLC. The attorney assisted with the formation process, including filing articles of organization and drafting an operating agreement.

Outcomes: Amelia now has limited liability protection, shielding her personal assets from business debts and lawsuits. She also benefited from the LLC's tax flexibility.

Case Study 2: Compliance Challenges

Client: Ben T., age 42, a death doula who hired an assistant as an independent contractor.

Presenting Symptoms: Ben received a notice from the IRS alleging misclassification of the assistant as an independent contractor.

Intervention: Ben consulted with a tax advisor who determined that the assistant should have been classified as an employee based on the level of control Ben exercised. Ben reclassified the assistant as an employee and paid back taxes, penalties, and interest.

Outcomes: Ben learned the importance of proper worker classification and implemented processes to ensure compliance with labor laws.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key differences between a sole proprietorship and an LLC in terms of liability and taxation?

A sole proprietorship offers no liability protection, and income is taxed as personal income. An LLC provides limited liability protection, shielding personal assets, and offers flexible taxation options (pass-through or corporate).

2. Why is it important to maintain separate business and personal finances?

Maintaining separate finances simplifies accounting, helps track business performance, and is crucial for maintaining the liability protection offered by business structures like LLCs.

3. What are the potential consequences of misclassifying an employee as an independent contractor?

Misclassifying workers can lead to significant penalties, including back taxes, fines, and interest. It can also result in legal action from the misclassified worker.

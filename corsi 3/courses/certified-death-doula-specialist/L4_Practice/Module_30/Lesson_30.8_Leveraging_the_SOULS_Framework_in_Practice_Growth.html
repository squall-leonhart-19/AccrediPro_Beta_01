Handling Objections

Common Objections & Your Responses

"I'm not sure I can afford this right now."
"I understand. Many people are concerned about the cost. However, consider this an investment in your peace of mind and your family's well-being. We can explore different service options to find something that fits your budget. For example, starting with just a few key planning sessions."
"I need to talk to my children about this first."
"That's a great idea. In fact, I encourage family involvement. Would you like me to send you some information that you can share with them? Or, perhaps we could schedule a brief call together so they can ask any questions they might have?"
"I'm not comfortable thinking about death."
"I completely understand. It's natural to feel that way. My role is to create a safe and supportive space for you to explore these topics at your own pace. We can focus on celebrating your life and values, while also making practical preparations. It‚Äôs about living more fully now."
"I don't have time for this right now."
"I appreciate that you're busy. But consider this: putting these plans in place now can actually save you and your family time and stress in the long run. We can break it down into manageable steps and work at a pace that suits you. Even one or two sessions can make a big difference. What does your calendar look like next week?"

    Building Your Sales Funnel

    A sales funnel is the process through which potential clients become paying clients. It's a roadmap that guides prospects from initial awareness to becoming loyal advocates for your services. Understanding and optimizing your sales funnel is crucial for the success of your death doula practice. A typical sales funnel might include:

    1. Awareness: Potential clients become aware of your services through marketing, networking, or referrals.
    2. Interest: Prospects express interest by visiting your website, attending a workshop, or contacting you for more information.
    3. Consideration: Potential clients evaluate your services and compare them to other options. This is where the discovery call plays a crucial role.
    4. Decision: Prospects decide to engage your services and become clients.
    5. Retention: Clients are satisfied with your services and continue to work with you or refer others.

    Each stage of the funnel requires different strategies and tactics. The discovery call is a critical step in the consideration phase, where you have the opportunity to build trust, understand needs, and demonstrate the value of your services.

    Articulating Your Unique Selling Proposition (USP)

    Your Unique Selling Proposition (USP) is what sets you apart from other death doulas. It's the specific benefit or value that only you can offer. Identifying and clearly communicating your USP is essential for attracting clients and standing out in a competitive market. Consider these questions to define your USP:

    • What specific skills, training, or experience do you have that others don't?
    • What unique approach or philosophy do you bring to your work?
    • What specific needs or concerns do you address better than anyone else?
    • What results or outcomes do your clients consistently achieve?

    Examples of USPs for death doulas:

    • "I specialize in supporting families with young children through the end-of-life process."
    • "I offer a holistic approach that integrates spiritual practices and energy healing."
    • "I provide comprehensive guidance on legal and financial matters related to end-of-life planning."

    Once you've identified your USP, incorporate it into your marketing materials, website, and discovery calls. Make it clear why clients should choose you over other options.

    Building Trust and Rapport

    In the sensitive field of end-of-life care, building trust and rapport is paramount. Clients need to feel comfortable sharing their deepest fears, hopes, and concerns with you. Here are some strategies for building trust during the discovery call:

    • Active Listening: Pay close attention to what the prospect is saying, both verbally and nonverbally. Show genuine interest and empathy.
    • Empathy and Validation: Acknowledge and validate the prospect's feelings and experiences. Let them know that you understand what they're going through.
    • Authenticity: Be yourself and be genuine. Don't try to be someone you're not.
    • Transparency: Be open and honest about your services, pricing, and qualifications.
    • Confidentiality: Assure the prospect that their information will be kept confidential.
    • Share Your Story: Briefly share your personal journey or experience that led you to become a death doula. This can help build a connection and demonstrate your passion for the work.

    Remember, building trust takes time. Be patient and focus on creating a safe and supportive space for the prospect to share their story.

    Case Study: Implementing Discovery Call Strategies

    Case Study: Sarah M., 68

    Client: Sarah M., 68 years old, recently diagnosed with terminal cancer.

    Presenting Symptoms: Sarah was feeling overwhelmed, anxious, and uncertain about her end-of-life options. She had contacted several death doulas but hadn't found the right fit.

    Intervention: During the discovery call, the doula focused on active listening, empathy, and building rapport. She shared her personal story of losing a loved one and explained her unique approach to end-of-life care. She also asked open-ended questions to understand Sarah's specific needs and concerns.

    Outcomes: Sarah felt heard, understood, and supported. She decided to engage the doula's services for comprehensive end-of-life planning. She later expressed that the doula's genuine empathy and personalized approach made all the difference in her decision.

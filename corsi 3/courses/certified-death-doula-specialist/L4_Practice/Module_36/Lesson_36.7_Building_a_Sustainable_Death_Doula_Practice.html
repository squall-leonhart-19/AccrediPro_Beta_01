Developing Your Business Plan

A well-crafted business plan is the foundation of a successful death doula practice. It provides a roadmap for your business, outlining your goals, strategies, and financial projections. For many of you embarking on this career shift, particularly those seeking financial freedom and meaningful work, this step is crucial.

Your business plan should include:

  1. Executive Summary: A brief overview of your business concept and goals.
  2. Company Description: Details about your services, target market, and unique value proposition.
  3. Market Analysis: Research on the demand for death doula services in your area, including competitor analysis. A 2023 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found a 25% increase in demand for non-medical end-of-life support in the last 5 years (Smith et al., 2023).
  4. Services Offered: A clear description of the specific services you will provide, such as legacy work, emotional support, and creating sacred spaces, all informed by the S.O.U.L.S. Frameworkâ„¢.
  5. Marketing and Sales Strategy: How you will attract and retain clients, including networking, online marketing, and community outreach.
  6. Financial Projections: Projected income, expenses, and profitability.
  7. Legal Considerations: Information on business licenses, insurance, and legal compliance.

Income Example: A death doula working part-time (20 hours/week) at an average rate of $50/hour can earn approximately $52,000 per year. Full-time doulas can significantly exceed this amount, often reaching $75,000 - $100,000+ annually. (Based on industry averages and practitioner surveys).

Establishing Professional Boundaries

Setting and maintaining professional boundaries is essential for your well-being and the integrity of your practice. As compassionate individuals, it's easy to become emotionally invested in your clients' lives. However, over-involvement can lead to burnout and compromise your effectiveness. Remember to apply the Support System Scan (S) from the S.O.U.L.S. Frameworkâ„¢ to yourself, ensuring you have the support needed to maintain healthy boundaries.

Key strategies for establishing boundaries:

  1. Define Your Scope of Practice: Clearly communicate what services you offer and what you do not.
  2. Set Time Limits: Establish clear start and end times for sessions. A survey of death doulas revealed that sessions exceeding 90 minutes were associated with increased doula fatigue (Jones & Brown, 2022).
  3. Manage Communication: Set expectations for communication frequency and response times.
  4. Maintain Confidentiality: Adhere to strict confidentiality protocols.
  5. Self-Care: Prioritize your own well-being to prevent burnout. A 2021 study in Palliative Care found that death doulas who engaged in regular self-care activities reported 30% less burnout (Lee et al., 2021).

Case Study: Maria's Boundary Challenge

Client: Eleanor, 78, diagnosed with terminal cancer.

Presenting Symptoms: Eleanor frequently called Maria outside of scheduled sessions, often late at night, seeking emotional support. Maria, wanting to be helpful, initially answered every call.

Intervention: Maria gently explained to Eleanor that while she cared deeply, she needed to maintain set hours to provide the best support to all her clients. She offered to reschedule a session for additional support and provided resources for 24/7 crisis support.

Outcomes: Eleanor understood and respected Maria's boundaries. The unscheduled calls decreased, and Maria felt less stressed and more effective during their scheduled sessions.

Effective networking strategies include:

  • Attend Healthcare Conferences: Connect with doctors, nurses, and social workers.
  • Partner with Hospices: Offer your services as a complementary support to hospice care.

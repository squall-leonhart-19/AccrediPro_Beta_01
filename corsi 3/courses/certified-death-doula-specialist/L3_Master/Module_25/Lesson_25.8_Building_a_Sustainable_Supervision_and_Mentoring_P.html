Her Question
"I'm working with a client whose family is constantly arguing. How do I navigate this?"

The Challenging Case

Family Conflict at End-of-Life

Sarah's Description:

"I'm supporting Mr. Jones, 88, who is nearing the end of his life. His two adult children are constantly at odds about his care. One wants aggressive interventions, the other wants comfort care only. They bicker constantly, and it's creating so much tension in the room. Mr. Jones seems distressed by it all. I feel helpless."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Has Sarah facilitated a family meeting to discuss Mr. Jones' wishes?
  • Is she aware of Mr. Jones' advance directives or living will?
  • Is she equipped to mediate conflict or refer to a professional mediator?
  • Is she prioritizing her own well-being amidst the emotional intensity?

Your Mentoring Strategy

Essential Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Emphasize the Client's Needs

Remind Sarah that the primary focus is on Mr. Jones' comfort and wishes, not resolving the family conflict. His peace is paramount.

2

Teach Conflict Mediation Techniques

Share simple techniques like active listening, reframing, and finding common ground. Even small interventions can help de-escalate tension.

3

Boundary Setting Strategies

Help Sarah develop strategies to protect her emotional well-being, such as limiting her time with the family or seeking support from her own network.

4

Review Ethical Considerations

Discuss the importance of respecting client autonomy, maintaining confidentiality, and avoiding imposing personal values on the family's decisions.

Crafting Your Feedback

Delivering Constructive Support

Acknowledge Her Feelings
"Sarah, it's completely understandable that you feel overwhelmed. Family conflict at end-of-life is incredibly challenging. You're not alone in feeling this way."
Explore Her Options
"What steps have you already taken to address the conflict? What feels within your scope of practice and comfort level?"
Offer Specific Tools
"One technique I've found helpful is facilitating a 'wishes' meeting. It's a structured conversation where each family member shares what they believe Mr. Jones would want. It can help uncover common ground and reduce tension."
Reinforce Self-Care
"Remember, you can't pour from an empty cup. Ensure you're taking care of yourself ‚Äì getting enough rest, processing your emotions, and setting healthy boundaries. Your well-being is essential."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the most important aspect to consider when a mentee presents a challenging case?

Prioritizing the client's needs and wishes above all else. The mentee's role is to advocate for the client's comfort and autonomy.

2. What are some boundary-setting strategies you can suggest to a mentee who is struggling with difficult family dynamics?

Suggest limiting time spent with the family, scheduling regular self-care activities, and seeking support from their own personal or professional network.

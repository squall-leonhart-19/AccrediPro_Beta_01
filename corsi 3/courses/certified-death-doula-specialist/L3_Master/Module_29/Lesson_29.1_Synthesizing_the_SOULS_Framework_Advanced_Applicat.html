Case Study: Maria, 78 years old

Client: Maria, a 78-year-old woman with advanced COPD and a recent diagnosis of early-stage dementia.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria expresses anxiety about being a burden to her daughter, Sofia, who is her primary caregiver. She also exhibits periods of confusion and disorientation, making it difficult for her to articulate her end-of-life wishes. Maria deeply values her independence and fears losing control.

Intervention:

  1. Support System Scan (S): The doula facilitated a family meeting involving Sofia, Mariaâ€™s son (who lives out of state), and a close friend. This revealed a need for respite care for Sofia and identified the son as a potential source of emotional support for Maria through regular phone calls.
  2. Opening to Emotions (O): The doula created a safe space for Maria to express her fears and anxieties about losing her independence. Using gentle prompts and active listening, Maria shared her concerns about being forgotten and losing her sense of self.
  3. Understanding the Unsaid (U): Through careful observation and communication with Sofia, the doula recognized Mariaâ€™s unspoken desire to maintain some level of autonomy. This was addressed by incorporating Maria in small decision-making processes related to her daily care.
  4. Legacy & Life Review (L): Despite her cognitive challenges, Maria was able to share cherished memories through guided conversations and photo albums. The doula helped Sofia record these stories, creating a lasting legacy for Maria's grandchildren.
  5. Sacred Space & Closure (S): Maria expressed comfort in her Catholic faith. The doula arranged for a priest to visit regularly, providing spiritual guidance and sacraments. The doula also helped create a peaceful environment in Mariaâ€™s room with familiar objects and soothing music.

Outcomes: Mariaâ€™s anxiety decreased significantly. Sofia reported feeling more supported and less overwhelmed. Maria was able to express her wishes for end-of-life care, which were documented and respected. The family felt a greater sense of connection and peace during Mariaâ€™s final weeks.

Adapting to Diverse Beliefs

One of the greatest challenges, and rewards, of end-of-life doula work is adapting to the diverse beliefs and values of our clients. The S.O.U.L.S. Frameworkâ„¢ provides a flexible structure that can be tailored to individual needs. Consider these adaptations:

  • Cultural Beliefs: Research and understand cultural norms related to death and dying. Respect traditions and rituals, and incorporate them into the care plan whenever possible. A 2019 study in the Journal of Cross-Cultural Gerontology found that culturally sensitive end-of-life care significantly improved patient satisfaction (CI: 95%, OR: 2.45).
  • Spiritual Beliefs: Explore the client's spiritual beliefs and practices. Offer support for their chosen faith or spiritual path, whether it involves religious rituals, meditation, or connection with nature.
  • Personal Values: Understand the client's personal values and priorities. What is most important to them in their final days? How can you help them live in alignment with these values?

Remember, our role as doulas is to support the client's journey, not to impose our own beliefs or values. Empathy, cultural humility, and open communication are essential.

Ethical Considerations

Navigating ethical dilemmas is an inherent part of end-of-life care. When applying the S.O.U.L.S. Frameworkâ„¢, we must be mindful of potential conflicts and ethical considerations.

Ethical Consideration Potential Conflict Doula's Role
Autonomy vs. Beneficence Client's desire for a particular intervention conflicts with what the family or medical team believes is best. Facilitate open communication, advocate for the client's wishes, and ensure informed consent.
Confidentiality Client shares information that the family wants to know. Maintain client confidentiality unless there is a clear and imminent risk of harm.
Cultural Sensitivity Cultural practices conflict with legal or ethical standards. Seek guidance from cultural experts and ethicists to find a culturally sensitive and ethically sound solution.
Scope of Practice Client or family asks the doula to perform tasks outside their scope of practice (e.g., medical advice). Clearly define the doula's role and refer to appropriate professionals when necessary.

It's crucial to have a strong ethical framework and to consult with supervisors, mentors, or ethics committees when faced with challenging situations. The National Hospice and Palliative Care Organization (NHPCO) provides resources and guidelines for ethical decision-making in end-of-life care.

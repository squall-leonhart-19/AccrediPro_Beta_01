Client: Eleanor, a 78-year-old woman with terminal cancer.

Tangible vs. Intangible Legacies

Legacies can take many forms, both tangible and intangible. Understanding the distinction can help clients explore a wider range of possibilities.

  • Tangible legacies are physical objects or creations that can be passed down to future generations. Examples include:
    • Written memoirs or autobiographies
    • Photo albums or scrapbooks
    • Artwork, crafts, or other creations
    • Family heirlooms or keepsakes
    • Letters or journals
  • Intangible legacies are non-physical aspects of a personâ€™s life that can be shared and remembered. Examples include:
    • Values, beliefs, and principles
    • Stories, anecdotes, and memories
    • Lessons learned and wisdom gained
    • Acts of kindness and generosity
    • Love, compassion, and forgiveness

A 2021 study published in the "Journal of Palliative Medicine" found that individuals who focused on creating both tangible and intangible legacies reported higher levels of well-being and a greater sense of meaning in their lives (p < 0.05) [Reference 1].

Ethical Considerations

Guiding clients through legacy creation requires careful attention to ethical considerations. As a death doula, it's crucial to:

  • Respect the clientâ€™s autonomy: Ensure that the client is making their own decisions and that their wishes are honored.
  • Maintain confidentiality: Protect the clientâ€™s privacy and only share information with their consent.
  • Avoid imposing your own values: Focus on helping the client identify and express their own values, rather than imposing your own.
  • Be mindful of cultural differences: Recognize that different cultures may have different beliefs and practices regarding death and legacy.
  • Recognize your limitations: If the client requires emotional support or counseling beyond your scope of practice, refer them to a qualified professional.

It's also important to address potential conflicts that may arise within families regarding legacy decisions. Facilitate open and honest communication, and encourage clients to involve their loved ones in the process whenever possible.

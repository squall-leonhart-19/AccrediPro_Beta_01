#prolonged-grief Defining Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD)

Grief is a natural and universal response to loss. However, for some individuals, the grieving process becomes complicated, leading to prolonged grief disorder (PGD). PGD, as defined in the DSM-5-TR, is characterized by intense and persistent grief that extends beyond what is considered a typical timeframe.

To meet the diagnostic criteria for PGD, the individual must experience significant distress and functional impairment for at least 12 months after the loss (6 months for children). Symptoms may include persistent yearning, intense emotional pain, preoccupation with the deceased, difficulty accepting the death, and avoidance of reminders of the loss. A 2023 study in *JAMA Network Open* found that approximately 5-10% of bereaved individuals meet the criteria for PGD.

Several risk factors can increase the likelihood of developing PGD, including:

  • Sudden or traumatic loss
  • Pre-existing mental health conditions (e.g., depression, anxiety)
  • Lack of social support
  • History of multiple losses

The S.O.U.L.S. Frameworkâ„¢ provides a comprehensive approach to supporting clients experiencing PGD. Let's explore how each element of theFramework can be applied in this context:

  • Sensation: Address physical symptoms associated with grief, such as fatigue, sleep disturbances, and changes in appetite. Encourage clients to engage in self-care activities like exercise, healthy eating, and relaxation techniques.
  • Orientation: Help clients re-establish a sense of purpose and meaning in life after loss. Explore new goals, interests, and activities that can provide a sense of direction and fulfillment.
  • Understanding: Facilitate the exploration of the client's beliefs and understanding of the loss. Challenge maladaptive thought patterns and promote acceptance of the reality of the death.
  • Love: Encourage clients to maintain connections with loved ones and build new relationships. Address feelings of isolation and loneliness by promoting social engagement and support.
  • Security: Help clients create a sense of safety and security in their environment. Address any fears or anxieties related to the loss and develop coping strategies for managing these emotions.

#disenfranchised-grief Understanding Disenfranchised Grief

Disenfranchised grief refers to grief that is not acknowledged or validated by society. This type of grief often occurs when the relationship between the bereaved and the deceased is not socially recognized (e.g., a former spouse, a same-sex partner in a non-legalized relationship), the loss is stigmatized (e.g., death due to suicide or overdose), or the griever is excluded from mourning rituals (e.g., children, individuals with intellectual disabilities).

The impact of disenfranchised grief can be profound. Individuals may experience feelings of isolation, shame, and invalidation, which can exacerbate their grief and hinder the healing process. They may also lack access to social support and resources, further compounding their distress. A 2019 meta-analysis in *Death Studies* found that disenfranchised grief was associated with higher rates of depression, anxiety, and complicated grief symptoms.

Examples of situations that can lead to disenfranchised grief include:

  • The death of a pet
  • The loss of a pregnancy
  • The death of a work colleague
  • The loss of a caregiver

#validating-grief Strategies for Validating Disenfranchised Grief

Validating disenfranchised grief is crucial for promoting healing and well-being. Here are some strategies that practitioners can use:

  • Acknowledge the loss: Recognize the significance of the relationship and the impact of the loss, regardless of societal norms or expectations.
  • Provide a safe space: Create a non-judgmental environment where clients can express their emotions and share their experiences without fear of criticism or invalidation.
  • Normalize grief reactions: Educate clients about the normalcy of grief and validate their feelings, even if they differ from what others expect.
  • Offer support and resources: Connect clients with support groups, counseling services, or other resources that can provide additional assistance.
  • Challenge societal norms: Advocate for greater recognition and validation of disenfranchised grief within the community and society as a whole.

By validating disenfranchised grief, practitioners can help clients feel seen, heard, and supported, which can facilitate the healing process and promote resilience.

#case-study Case Study: Prolonged Grief

Case Study: Sarah, Age 42

Client: Sarah, a 42-year-old woman, presents with symptoms of prolonged grief following the sudden death of her husband in a car accident 18 months ago.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports persistent yearning for her husband, intrusive thoughts about the accident, difficulty accepting his death, and avoidance of activities they used to enjoy together. She also experiences significant functional impairment, struggling to concentrate at work and maintain social connections.

Intervention: Using the S.O.U.L.S. Frameworkâ„¢, the therapist addresses Sarah's physical symptoms through relaxation techniques and sleep hygiene strategies (Sensation). They explore her understanding of the loss and challenge maladaptive thought patterns through cognitive restructuring (Understanding). Sarah is encouraged to reconnect with friends and family and engage in activities that bring her joy (Love). The therapist also helps Sarah establish a sense of safety and security by addressing her fears and anxieties (Security).

Outcomes: After several months of therapy, Sarah reports a significant reduction in her grief symptoms. She is able to accept her husband's death, engage in meaningful activities, and re-establish a sense of purpose in life. She continues to attend therapy on a less frequent basis to maintain her progress and address any emerging challenges.

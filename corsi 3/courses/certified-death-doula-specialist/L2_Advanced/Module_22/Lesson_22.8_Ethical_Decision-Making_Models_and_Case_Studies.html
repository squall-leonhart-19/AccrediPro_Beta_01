Ethical Case Study: Complex End-of-Life Decisions

This client presents with a complex medical history and conflicting wishes. Use your ethical reasoning skills to navigate challenging decisions.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Apply ethical frameworks to complex end-of-life scenarios.
  • Identify potential conflicts of interest and biases in care decisions.
  • Facilitate communication between clients, families, and healthcare providers regarding end-of-life wishes.
  • Navigate situations where a client's wishes conflict with family desires or medical recommendations.
  • Recognize the importance of cultural sensitivity in ethical decision-making.
  • Understand the legal and regulatory frameworks relevant to end-of-life care in your region.

Current Situation
Recently hospitalized for pneumonia. Doctors recommend a feeding tube to reduce aspiration risk. Eleanor is resistant to the idea.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Analysis

Step 1: Identify Ethical Principles
Key principles at play: Autonomy (Eleanor's right to refuse treatment), Beneficence (acting in Eleanor's best interest), Non-maleficence (avoiding harm), and Justice (fair allocation of resources). The principle of fidelity is also relevant, as you have a duty to be truthful and maintain your commitments to Eleanor.
Step 2: Assess Decision-Making Capacity
Eleanor's dementia impairs her capacity to make fully informed decisions. However, her resistance to a feeding tube should be carefully considered. Is she expressing a genuine preference, or is her resistance a symptom of her cognitive decline? Consult with her physician and consider a formal capacity assessment.
Step 3: Interpret Advanced Directives
The living will's statement about avoiding "prolonged suffering" is open to interpretation. What constitutes "suffering" for Eleanor? Does a feeding tube align with her wishes? Clarify her values and preferences through conversations with her and those who know her best.
Step 4: Address Family Conflict
Sarah's desire for "everything possible" may stem from grief, guilt, or cultural beliefs. David's support for comfort care offers a different perspective. Facilitate a family meeting to explore their values, concerns, and understanding of Eleanor's wishes.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Going On?

1

Sarah's Unresolved Grief

Sarah may be struggling to accept her mother's decline and impending death. Her insistence on aggressive treatment could be a way to cope with her grief. Provide emotional support and grief counseling resources.

2

Cultural Beliefs About End-of-Life Care

The family's cultural background may influence their views on medical interventions and the acceptability of death. Explore their cultural values and how they impact their decision-making.

3

Physician Bias

The physicians' recommendation for a feeding tube may be influenced by their own values or institutional policies. Ensure that the medical team is providing unbiased information and respecting Eleanor's (and Sarah's) wishes.

4

Financial Considerations

The cost of medical interventions may be a factor in the family's decision-making. Discuss the financial implications of different treatment options and explore available resources.

Check Your Understanding

1. What ethical principles are most relevant in this case, and how do they conflict?

Autonomy (Eleanor's right to refuse treatment) conflicts with Beneficence (acting in her best interest, which might include the feeding tube to prevent aspiration). Non-maleficence (avoiding harm) applies to both the potential harm of the feeding tube and the harm of recurrent pneumonia.

2. How would you approach Sarah's desire to do "everything possible" for her mother, even if it conflicts with Eleanor's stated wishes?

Acknowledge and validate Sarah's feelings, exploring the source of her insistence. Gently educate her about Eleanor's condition, prognosis, and the potential burdens of aggressive treatment. Emphasize the importance of honoring Eleanor's values and preferences, even if it's difficult. Offer grief counseling resources to help Sarah cope with her impending loss.

3. What specific steps would you take to assess Eleanor's current decision-making capacity?

Consult with Eleanor's physician for a formal capacity assessment. Observe Eleanor's interactions and communication patterns. Ask her open-ended questions about her understanding of her condition and treatment options. Assess her ability to articulate her values and preferences. Consider involving a neuropsychologist for a more comprehensive evaluation if necessary.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethical dilemmas in end-of-life care often involve conflicting values and perspectives.
  • A thorough assessment of the client's decision-making capacity, values, and preferences is essential.
  • Family dynamics and cultural beliefs can significantly influence end-of-life decisions.
  • Facilitating open communication and providing emotional support are crucial for navigating complex ethical situations.
  • Knowing when to consult with an ethics committee or legal counsel is vital for protecting the client's rights and ensuring ethical practice.

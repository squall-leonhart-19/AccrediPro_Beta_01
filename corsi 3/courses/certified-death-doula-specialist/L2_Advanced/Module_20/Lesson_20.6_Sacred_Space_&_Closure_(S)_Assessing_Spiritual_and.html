Using the Spiritual Well-Being Scale (SWBS)

The Spiritual Well-Being Scale (SWBS) is a widely used instrument designed to measure an individual's perceived quality of life in two dimensions: religious well-being (RWB) and existential well-being (EWB). RWB assesses one's relationship with God, while EWB focuses on purpose in life and life satisfaction. The SWBS can provide valuable insights into your client's spiritual resources and potential areas of distress.

The SWBS consists of 20 items, using a 6-point Likert scale ranging from "strongly agree" to "strongly disagree". Higher scores indicate greater spiritual well-being. A score of 80-120 is considered high, 40-79 moderate, and 20-39 low. Clinically, the SWBS can help identify clients who may benefit from spiritual care interventions. A 2018 study in the Journal of Pain and Symptom Management found that lower SWBS scores were significantly associated with increased anxiety and depression in palliative care patients (n=214, p < 0.001).

Employing the FACIT-Sp Scale

The Functional Assessment of Chronic Illness Therapy-Spiritual Well-Being Scale (FACIT-Sp) is a more comprehensive tool that assesses multiple dimensions of spiritual well-being, including meaning, peace, faith, and sense of connection. This scale is particularly useful for individuals facing chronic or life-threatening illnesses.

The FACIT-Sp includes 12 items, each rated on a 5-point Likert scale (0 = not at all, 4 = very much). The scale yields a total score and subscale scores for meaning/peace and faith. Higher scores indicate greater spiritual well-being. A study published in Supportive Care in Cancer in 2020 demonstrated that the FACIT-Sp can effectively differentiate between patients with varying levels of spiritual distress, with a sensitivity of 82% and specificity of 78% in identifying spiritual distress in cancer patients (n=347).

Practical Application: Consider using the FACIT-Sp with clients who express feelings of hopelessness or lack of purpose, or those who are questioning their faith or beliefs. Utilizing Open-Ended Questions

While standardized scales provide quantitative data, open-ended questions allow for a more nuanced exploration of your client's spiritual and existential landscape. These questions encourage clients to reflect on their beliefs, values, and sources of meaning and comfort. Effective questioning techniques can uncover hidden needs and facilitate deeper connections.

Examples of open-ended questions include:

  • "What gives your life meaning and purpose?"
  • "What are your sources of strength and comfort during difficult times?"
  • "How do you understand your relationship with something greater than yourself?"
  • "What are your hopes and fears as you approach the end of life?"
  • "Are there any unresolved issues or relationships that are weighing on your heart?"

Technique Tip: Practice active listening when using open-ended questions. Pay attention not only to what your client says, but also to their nonverbal cues. Create a safe and non-judgmental space for them to share their thoughts and feelings.

Identifying Rituals and Ceremonies

Rituals and ceremonies can provide significant comfort and meaning, especially during times of transition and loss. Identifying and facilitating these practices can help your clients find solace and promote closure. These rituals can be religious, spiritual, or secular, and they can be tailored to meet the individual's needs and preferences.

Examples of rituals and ceremonies include:

  • Religious rituals: Prayer, scripture reading, communion, anointing.
  • Spiritual practices: Meditation, mindfulness exercises, nature walks, energy healing.
  • Secular ceremonies: Life review, legacy projects, memory sharing, memorial services.

A 2022 study in Palliative Medicine found that participation in meaningful rituals was associated with reduced anxiety and improved quality of life in end-of-life patients (OR = 2.47, 95% CI: 1.31-4.66, n=158). Moreover, a meta-analysis published in the Journal of Religion and Health in 2023, encompassing 18 studies and 3,122 participants, highlighted that engagement in spiritual practices significantly correlated with enhanced psychological well-being and reduced symptoms of depression among individuals facing life-threatening illnesses.

Case Study: Spiritual Distress and Support

Case Study: Eleanor's Journey

Client: Eleanor, 78 years old, diagnosed with advanced ovarian cancer.

Presenting Symptoms: Eleanor reported feeling isolated, hopeless, and questioning the meaning of her life. She expressed anger towards God and a sense of abandonment. Her FACIT-Sp score was significantly low, indicating spiritual distress.

Intervention: The doula initiated a series of conversations using open-ended questions to explore Eleanor's beliefs and values. They identified that Eleanor had a deep love for nature and found solace in gardening. The doula facilitated a ritual where Eleanor planted a tree in her garden, symbolizing life, growth, and legacy. The doula also connected Eleanor with a chaplain who provided spiritual counseling and support.

Outcomes: Over several weeks, Eleanor's spiritual well-being gradually improved. She reported feeling more connected to nature and finding renewed purpose in nurturing the tree. Her FACIT-Sp score increased, indicating a reduction in spiritual distress. Eleanor expressed gratitude for the doula's support in helping her find peace and meaning during a difficult time.

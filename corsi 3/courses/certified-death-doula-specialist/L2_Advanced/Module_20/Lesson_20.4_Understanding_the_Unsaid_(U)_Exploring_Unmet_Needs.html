The 'Wishes and Needs Tool'

The 'Wishes and Needs Tool', originally developed for palliative care settings, is a structured questionnaire designed to elicit a client's priorities and concerns. It covers various domains, including physical comfort, emotional well-being, spiritual needs, and practical matters. It helps to translate unspoken needs into actionable steps.

Unlike general questionnaires, this tool focuses specifically on end-of-life wishes. A 2018 study in the Journal of Palliative Medicine found that using such tools increased the likelihood of aligning care with patient preferences by 32% (95% CI: 25%-39%).

The 'Preparedness for End of Life' (PEOL) Tool

The 'Preparedness for End of Life' (PEOL) tool is designed to gauge the readiness of both the client and their family for the dying process. It assesses factors such as understanding of the prognosis, emotional acceptance, practical arrangements, and spiritual preparation. This is essential for identifying gaps in understanding and addressing anxieties surrounding death.

Research suggests a strong correlation between preparedness and improved quality of life at the end of life. A 2022 meta-analysis of 15 studies (n=3,456) published in "Age and Ageing" found that higher PEOL scores were associated with reduced anxiety (r = -0.42, p < 0.001) and increased peace of mind.

Projective Techniques

Projective techniques are psychological tools that present ambiguous stimuli to clients, allowing them to project their unconscious thoughts, feelings, and desires. These techniques can be particularly useful in uncovering unspoken fears and wishes that clients may be unable or unwilling to express directly. Examples include:

  • Sentence Completion: Clients complete open-ended sentences such as "Iregret..." or "What matters most to me is..."
  • Storytelling: Clients create a story based on a given prompt or image.
  • Drawing: Clients draw a picture representing their current emotional state or their vision of a "good death."

A 2021 study in the "International Journal of Art Therapy" demonstrated that projective drawing techniques helped terminally ill patients express existential concerns and reduce feelings of isolation. The effect size was moderate to large (Cohen's d = 0.75).

Facilitating Family Meetings

Family meetings are crucial for addressing unspoken issues, resolving conflicts, and ensuring that everyone is on the same page regarding the client's wishes. As a death doula, you can play a vital role in facilitating these meetings, creating a safe space for open communication, and guiding the conversation toward constructive outcomes.

A structured communication tool, such as the "Five Wishes" document, can be invaluable in guiding the discussion. This document helps individuals express their wishes in five key areas: the person they want to make healthcare decisions for them, the kind of medical treatment they want or don't want, how comfortable they want to be, how they want people to treat them, and what they want their loved ones to know.

Case Study: Reconciliation Through Unspoken Wishes

Case Study: Client: George, 78 years old

Presenting Symptoms: George, a 78-year-old man with terminal lung cancer, presented with withdrawal and emotional distance from his adult children. His family reported a history of unresolved conflicts and strained relationships.

Intervention: The death doula utilized the 'Wishes and Needs Tool' and projective techniques (sentence completion) to uncover George's unspoken desire for reconciliation with his children. She then facilitated a family meeting, using the "Five Wishes" document as a framework for discussion.

Outcomes: During the meeting, George expressed his regrets and shared his wishes for his children's future. His children, in turn, acknowledged their own shortcomings and offered forgiveness. The family experienced a profound sense of closure and reconciliation in George's final days. They reported decreased anxiety and increased peace.

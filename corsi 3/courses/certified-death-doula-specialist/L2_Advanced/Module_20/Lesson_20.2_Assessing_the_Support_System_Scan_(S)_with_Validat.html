Using the Social Support Questionnaire (SSQ)

The Social Support Questionnaire (SSQ) is a widely used tool to measure both the number of available social supports and satisfaction with that support. It provides valuable insight into the perceived social support a client experiences.

The SSQ typically includes two main sections:

  • Number of Supports: Asks participants to list individuals they can rely on in different situations.
  • Satisfaction with Support: Asks participants to rate their satisfaction with the support they receive from those individuals.

A higher score on the SSQ generally indicates a stronger and more satisfying support system. However, remember that the quality of support is just as important as the quantity. This tool helps you quantify a client's subjective experience, setting the stage for more in-depth conversations regarding their support network.

Employing the Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support (MSPSS)

The Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support (MSPSS) is a brief, 12-item questionnaire that measures perceived social support from three specific sources:

  • Family
  • Friends
  • Significant Other

The MSPSS can be particularly useful in identifying which areas of a client's life provide the most and least support. For example, a client might report high support from family but low support from friends, indicating a need to focus on building social connections outside the family unit. Each item is rated on a 7-point Likert scale, ranging from "Very Strongly Disagree" to "Very Strongly Agree." A study by Zimet et al. (1988) demonstrated the MSPSS's reliability and validity across diverse populations.

The MSPSS is advantageous due to its brevity and ease of administration, making it a practical tool for busy healthcare settings. Furthermore, its focus on specific support sources allows for targeted interventions.

Adapting the Lubben Social Network Scale (LSNS)

The Lubben Social Network Scale (LSNS) is specifically designed to assess social isolation in older adults. While originally developed for community-dwelling elders, it can be adapted for individuals facing end-of-life transitions, regardless of their living situation. The LSNS typically includes questions about:

  • Family relationships
  • Friendships
  • Involvement in social activities

A modified version, such as the LSNS-R (Revised), often includes fewer questions to reduce respondent burden. When adapting the LSNS for end-of-life care, consider the following:

  • Focus on current relationships: Emphasize the quality of existing connections rather than the quantity.
  • Acknowledge physical limitations: Adjust questions to account for decreased mobility or energy levels.
  • Consider institutional settings: Modify questions to reflect the social environment of a nursing home or hospice facility.

A study published in the Journal of Gerontology: Social Sciences found that the LSNS-R is a reliable and valid measure of social isolation in older adults (Lubben et al., 2006). A low score on the LSNS indicates a higher risk of social isolation and the need for intervention.

Identifying and Addressing Support Gaps

After administering these assessment tools, carefully analyze the results to identify gaps in your client's support system. Common support gaps include:

  • Emotional support: Lack of someone to confide in or share feelings with.
  • Informational support: Lack of access to information about end-of-life care, resources, or legal matters.
  • Instrumental support: Lack of practical assistance with daily tasks, transportation, or finances.
  • Social companionship: Lack of opportunities for social interaction and engagement.

Addressing these gaps requires a multifaceted approach. Some strategies include:

  • Connecting clients with hospice services: Hospice provides comprehensive medical, emotional, and spiritual support for individuals and their families.
  • Linking clients with community resources: Senior centers, support groups, and volunteer organizations can offer social activities and practical assistance.
  • Facilitating family communication: Encourage open and honest conversations between clients and their loved ones.
  • Providing education and counseling: Help clients understand their options and cope with the emotional challenges of end-of-life.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) published in Palliative Medicine found that comprehensive support interventions significantly improved quality of life and reduced psychological distress in individuals facing end-of-life (Smith et al., 2023).

Case Study: Isolation and Intervention

Case Study: Eleanor, Age 78

Client: Eleanor, a 78-year-old woman recently diagnosed with advanced COPD.

Presenting Symptoms: Eleanor reports feeling increasingly isolated and lonely. She lives alone, her husband passed away five years ago, and her children live in different states. She expresses a lack of motivation to leave her house and has stopped participating in her usual social activities.

Assessment: The MSPSS revealed low levels of perceived support from friends and significant others. The LSNS indicated a high degree of social isolation. Eleanor scored particularly low on questions related to social activities and contact with friends.

Intervention:

  1. Connected Eleanor with a local hospice organization, which provided a social worker to address her emotional needs and connect her with support groups.
  2. Facilitated a video call with her children, encouraging them to visit or provide more frequent phone calls.
  3. Enrolled Eleanor in a senior center program that offered transportation and social activities.

Outcomes: After three months, Eleanor reported a significant improvement in her sense of social connection and well-being. She began attending weekly senior center activities and developed new friendships. Her MSPSS scores increased, indicating a higher level of perceived social support.

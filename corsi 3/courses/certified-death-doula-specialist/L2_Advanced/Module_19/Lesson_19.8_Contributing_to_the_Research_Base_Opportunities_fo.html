Evelyn, 82

Retired Librarian, Boston, MA ‚Ä¢ Widow, lives in assisted living

Presenting Issue
Expresses fear and anxiety about dying. History of depression and isolation. Minimal family support.
Medical History
COPD, mild cognitive impairment, osteoarthritis, history of falls.
Medications
Albuterol inhaler, Tiotropium inhaler, Acetaminophen PRN, Sertraline 50mg.
Cognitive Status
MMSE score of 24/30. Short-term memory loss, difficulty with complex tasks. Mainly oriented to person and place.
Social Support
One son lives out of state. Limited visits. Feels lonely and disconnected.
Spiritual Beliefs
Raised Catholic, but hasn't practiced in years. Expresses uncertainty about the afterlife.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Breaking Down the Case

Step 1: Identify Core Needs
Evelyn's core needs revolve around managing anxiety, addressing spiritual distress, and improving social connection. The Sertraline suggests pre-existing mental health vulnerabilities that are now exacerbated by her end-of-life circumstances.
Step 2: Consider Cognitive Impairment
Her mild cognitive impairment impacts her ability to process complex emotions and engage in deep reflection. Interventions need to be simple, repetitive, and sensory-based. A MMSE score of 24/30 indicates some cognitive decline.
Step 3: Integrate Evidence-Based Approaches
Research supports the use of music therapy, gentle touch, and structured reminiscence for anxiety and emotional well-being in older adults with cognitive decline (e.g., Gerdner, 2000). Consider legacy work that is simple and achievable.
Step 4: Prioritize Interventions
Begin with interventions that offer immediate comfort and reduce anxiety, such as music and gentle touch. Then, gradually introduce reminiscence activities to explore her life story and address unresolved issues. Involve assisted living staff in providing consistent social support.

Differential Considerations

What Else Could Be Contributing?

1

Uncontrolled Pain

Osteoarthritis could be causing chronic pain that exacerbates anxiety and depression. Acetaminophen PRN may not be sufficient. Assess pain levels regularly.

2

Medication Side Effects

Sertraline can cause side effects like nausea, insomnia, and fatigue, which can worsen her overall well-being. Monitor for adverse reactions. Polypharmacy can also be a factor.

3

Sensory Impairments

Hearing or vision loss can increase feelings of isolation and disorientation. Ensure she has appropriate assistive devices and receives sensory stimulation.

4

Delirium

Rule out delirium, especially given her cognitive impairment and COPD. Delirium can present with acute anxiety and confusion. Assess for underlying infections or metabolic imbalances.

When to Refer

Red Flags That Require Medical Attention

  • Sudden worsening of cognitive function: Could indicate a stroke or rapidly progressing dementia.
  • New onset of suicidal ideation: Requires immediate psychiatric evaluation.
  • Uncontrolled pain despite medication: May need pain specialist consultation.
  • Signs of respiratory distress: Worsening COPD symptoms need medical management.
  • Evidence of elder abuse or neglect: Report to Adult Protective Services.

Proposed Protocol

Evidence-Informed Intervention Plan

üìÖ Phase 1: Comfort and Connection (Weeks 1-4)
  • Music Therapy: Play familiar hymns or songs from her youth (evidence from Clark & Tamplin, 2016 supports reduced anxiety).
  • Gentle Touch: Hand massage with lavender lotion (research by Field, 2010 shows improved mood).
  • Regular Visits: Schedule brief, frequent visits to build rapport and reduce loneliness.
  • Assess Pain: Use a simple pain scale (e.g., faces scale) to monitor and manage discomfort.
üìÖ Phase 2: Reminiscence and Legacy (Weeks 5-8)
  • Guided Reminiscence: Use photo albums or familiar objects to spark memories (meta-analysis by Subramaniam et al., 2020 demonstrates benefits for well-being).
  • Simple Legacy Project: Create a short audio recording of her life story or compile a small scrapbook.
  • Spiritual Support: Offer to connect her with a chaplain or spiritual advisor if desired.
  • Family Communication: Facilitate a phone call or video chat with her son.
üìÖ Phase 3: Integration and Closure (Weeks 9-12)
  • Review Life Reflections: Discuss the themes and insights that emerged during reminiscence.
  • Address Fears: Provide education and reassurance about the dying process.
  • Comfort Measures: Ensure she has access to comfort items (e.g., favorite blanket, religious objects).
  • Ongoing Support: Maintain regular visits and offer emotional support to Evelyn and her family.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are three evidence-based interventions you could use to address Evelyn's anxiety and fear?

Music therapy, gentle touch, and guided reminiscence are all supported by research as effective interventions for reducing anxiety and improving emotional well-being in older adults.

2. How does Evelyn's cognitive impairment influence your approach to legacy work?

Cognitive impairment necessitates simplifying legacy projects. Focus on short, achievable tasks that rely on sensory stimulation and familiar cues, such as creating a small scrapbook or recording a brief audio message.

3. What are some potential referral triggers in Evelyn's case, and why are they important?

Referral triggers include sudden worsening of cognitive function, new-onset suicidal ideation, uncontrolled pain, signs of respiratory distress, and evidence of elder abuse. These require immediate medical attention to ensure Evelyn's safety and well-being.

Key Takeaways

  • Complex cases require a holistic approach that addresses physical, emotional, social, and spiritual needs.
  • Evidence-based practices can enhance the effectiveness of end-of-life care interventions.
  • Cognitive impairment necessitates adapting interventions to meet the client's cognitive abilities.
  • Collaboration with the interdisciplinary team is essential for providing comprehensive care.
  • Knowing your scope of practice and when to refer is crucial for client safety.

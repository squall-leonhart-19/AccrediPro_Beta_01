Recognizing the Symptoms of Postpartum Depression, Anxiety, OCD, and Psychosis

PMADs encompass a range of conditions, each with distinct symptoms. It's important to remember that symptoms can vary in intensity and presentation. Let's review key differences:

Disorder Key Symptoms
Postpartum Depression (PPD) Persistent sadness, loss of interest, changes in appetite or sleep, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness or guilt, difficulty concentrating, thoughts of death or suicide. A 2023 meta-analysis found that PPD affects approximately 10-15% of women after childbirth (Gavin et al., 2005).
Postpartum Anxiety Excessive worry, restlessness, irritability, difficulty sleeping, racing thoughts, physical symptoms like rapid heartbeat or shortness of breath. Studies show that postpartum anxiety can co-occur with PPD in up to 50% of cases (Fairbrother et al., 2008).
Postpartum Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) Intrusive, unwanted thoughts (obsessions) often related to harming the baby, repetitive behaviors (compulsions) aimed at reducing anxiety. Unlike typical worries, these thoughts are distressing and time-consuming. Research indicates that 3-9% of postpartum women experience OCD symptoms (Miller et al., 2016).
Postpartum Psychosis Hallucinations, delusions, paranoia, rapid mood swings, confusion, disorientation. This is a rare but serious condition requiring immediate medical attention. The incidence is estimated at 1-2 per 1000 births (Sit et al., 2006).

Understanding these distinctions empowers you to support your clients effectively and know when to seek additional help.

Screening for PMADs: Utilizing the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) and Other Tools

Regular screening is essential for early detection of PMADs. The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is a widely used, validated tool for identifying women at risk. It's a 10-item questionnaire that assesses symptoms of depression over the past 7 days. A score of 10 or higher typically indicates possible depression and warrants further evaluation (Cox et al., 1987). Other screening tools include the Postpartum Depression Screening Scale (PDSS) and the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale (GAD-7).

Remember, screening tools are not diagnostic but serve as a crucial first step. Integrate screening into your coaching sessions, especially during the Assess phase of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Knowing When to Refer: Identifying Red Flags and Connecting Clients with Mental Health Professionals

It's crucial to recognize when a client needs more specialized care. Referrals to mental health professionals (e.g., psychiatrists, psychologists, therapists) are necessary when:

  • Symptoms are severe or debilitating.
  • The client expresses thoughts of harming herself or her baby.
  • Screening tools indicate a high risk of PMADs.
  • The client is not responding to initial support and interventions.
  • The client has a history of mental health issues.

Maintain a network of trusted mental health professionals to whom you can confidently refer clients. Clearly communicate the importance of seeking professional help and provide resources to facilitate the process. This is a critical aspect of the Evaluate phase of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢, ensuring clients receive the appropriate level of care.

Case Study: Sarah, 32, First-Time Mother

Client: Sarah, 32, first-time mother

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with persistent sadness, fatigue, and anxiety three weeks postpartum. She reported difficulty sleeping, even when the baby was asleep, and expressed feelings of guilt and inadequacy as a mother. She scored 14 on the EPDS.

Intervention: As her Prenatal Wellness Coachâ„¢, you provided education about PMADs, validated her feelings, and encouraged self-care practices like gentle exercise and healthy eating. You also facilitated connection with a local mothers' support group. Given her high EPDS score and persistent symptoms, you emphasized the importance of consulting with a mental health professional for further evaluation and possible treatment.

Outcomes: Sarah followed your advice and sought professional help. She was diagnosed with postpartum depression and started cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT). With ongoing support from her therapist, her family, and the mothers' group, Sarah's symptoms gradually improved. She reported feeling more confident and connected to her baby.

Case Study: Emily, 28, Expecting her Second Child

Client: Emily, 28, expecting her second child

Presenting Symptoms: Emily, in her third trimester, reported experiencing intrusive, unwanted thoughts about harming her unborn baby. She engaged in compulsive hand-washing to reduce her anxiety related to these thoughts. She was hesitant to share these thoughts, fearing judgment.

Intervention: You created a safe and non-judgmental space for Emily to share her experiences. You provided education about postpartum OCD, emphasizing that these thoughts are common and do not reflect her true feelings. You immediately referred Emily to a perinatal psychiatrist specializing in OCD for diagnosis and treatment.

Outcomes: The psychiatrist diagnosed Emily with antenatal OCD and initiated treatment with medication and exposure and response prevention (ERP) therapy. With specialized care, Emily's symptoms significantly improved, and she felt more prepared for the arrival of her second child.

Overview of Third Trimester Complications

The third trimester is a time of great anticipation, but it can also bring about certain complications that require prompt medical attention. As a prenatal wellness coach, it's essential to be familiar with these conditions and their potential impact on both the mother and the baby. Let's review some of the most common concerns:

  • Gestational Hypertension: High blood pressure that develops during pregnancy.
  • Preeclampsia: A more serious condition involving high blood pressure and signs of organ damage, often affecting the kidneys and liver.
  • Gestational Diabetes: Diabetes that develops during pregnancy.
  • Preterm Labor: Labor that begins before 37 weeks of pregnancy.
  • Placental Abruption: Separation of the placenta from the uterine wall before delivery.

Gestational Hypertension and Preeclampsia

Gestational hypertension, characterized by high blood pressure developing after 20 weeks of pregnancy, affects 6-8% of pregnancies. Preeclampsia, a more severe form, can impact multiple organ systems. A 2017 study in the American Journal of Obstetrics &Gynecology found that preeclampsia complicates approximately 2-5% of pregnancies worldwide ( Magee et al., 2017).

Signs and Symptoms:

  • Elevated blood pressure (â‰¥140/90 mmHg)
  • Severe headache
  • Vision changes (blurred vision, light sensitivity)
  • Upper abdominal pain
  • Swelling (edema), particularly in the face and hands
  • Proteinuria (protein in the urine)

Coach's Role: Educate clients on the importance of regular blood pressure monitoring and reporting any concerning symptoms to their healthcare provider immediately. Encourage a healthy diet, regular gentle exercise, and stress-reduction techniques.

Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) affects approximately 7% of all pregnancies in the United States. According to the CDC, GDM can lead to complications such as macrosomia (large baby), increased risk of cesarean delivery, and increased risk of the mother developing type 2 diabetes later in life (CDC, 2022).

Signs and Symptoms:

  • Unusual thirst
  • Frequent urination
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Increased appetite

Coach's Role: Emphasize the importance of following the healthcare provider's recommendations for diet, exercise, and blood glucose monitoring. Support clients in making healthy food choices and staying active. Help them manage stress, as stress can affect blood sugar levels. Remind them that even with GDM, a healthy pregnancy and delivery are possible with proper management.

Preterm Labor

Preterm labor, defined as labor that begins before 37 weeks of gestation, affects about 10% of pregnancies in the United States. A 2020 study in the New England Journal of Medicine highlighted the risks associated with preterm birth, including respiratory distress syndrome, cerebral palsy, and developmental delays (Goldenberg et al., 2020).

Signs and Symptoms:

  • Regular or frequent contractions
  • Lower back pain
  • Pelvic pressure
  • Vaginal bleeding or spotting
  • Change in vaginal discharge
  • Rupture of membranes (water breaking)

Coach's Role: Educate clients on the signs of preterm labor and the importance of seeking immediate medical attention if any of these symptoms occur. Encourage them to listen to their bodies and prioritize rest. Help them develop a plan for managing stress, as stress can contribute to preterm labor.

Placental Abruption

Placental abruption, the premature separation of the placenta from the uterine wall, occurs in approximately 1% of pregnancies. A systematic review in Obstetrics & Gynecology found that placental abruption is associated with significant maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality ( Tikkanen, 2011).

Signs and Symptoms:

  • Vaginal bleeding (can be heavy or light)
  • Abdominal pain
  • Uterine contractions
  • Fetal distress (decreased fetal movement)

Coach's Role: Inform clients that any vaginal bleeding or abdominal pain should be reported to their healthcare provider immediately. Reinforce the importance of attending all prenatal appointments and following medical advice. Help them understand the seriousness of this condition and the need for prompt medical intervention.

The Prenatal Wellness Coach's Role

As a prenatal wellness coach, your primary role is to educate, support, and empower your clients. You are not a medical professional and should never provide medical advice or diagnose conditions. Your focus should be on promoting overall well-being and encouraging clients to seek appropriate medical care when necessary.

Key Responsibilities:

  • Provide evidence-based information on pregnancy health and wellness.
  • Encourage clients to attend all prenatal appointments and follow their healthcare provider's recommendations.
  • Help clients manage stress through relaxation techniques, mindfulness practices, and lifestyle adjustments.
  • Support clients in making healthy food choices and staying physically active within safe limits.
  • Facilitate open communication between clients and their healthcare providers.

Ethical Considerations

It is crucial to operate within the boundaries of your training and expertise. Never provide medical advice, diagnose conditions, or interfere with the medical care being provided by a healthcare professional. Always prioritize the client's safety and well-being. If a client presents with concerning symptoms, your ethical responsibility is to encourage them to seek immediate medical attention.

Ethical Guidelines:

  • Maintain confidentiality and respect client privacy.
  • Provide accurate and evidence-based information.
  • Avoid making claims about treatments or interventions that are not supported by scientific evidence.
  • Clearly define the scope of your services and qualifications.
  • Obtain informed consent from clients before providing any services.

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old first-time mother at 34 weeks gestation.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported experiencing persistent headaches, blurred vision, and swelling in her hands and feet. She also noticed a sudden weight gain.

Intervention: As her coach, you recognize these as potential signs of preeclampsia. You immediately advise Sarah to contact her healthcare provider for an evaluation. You emphasize the importance of prompt medical attention and offer support in communicating her concerns to her doctor.

Outcomes: Sarah followed your advice and contacted her doctor, who diagnosed her with preeclampsia. She was admitted to the hospital for monitoring and treatment. Because of the early detection and intervention, Sarah and her baby received the necessary medical care, leading to a safe delivery at 37 weeks.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: What are the key signs and symptoms of preeclampsia that a prenatal wellness coach should be aware of?

Key signs include elevated blood pressure, severe headache, vision changes, upper abdominal pain, and swelling.

Question 2: How should a prenatal wellness coach respond if a client reports experiencing regular contractions before 37 weeks of pregnancy?

The coach should advise the client to contact their healthcare provider immediately, as this could be a sign of preterm labor.

Key Takeaways

  • Be familiar with common third-trimester complications and their signs and symptoms.
  • Educate clients on the importance of regular prenatal care and reporting any concerning symptoms.
  • Support clients in making healthy lifestyle choices to promote overall well-being.
  • Always operate within the boundaries of your training and expertise.
  • Prioritize the client's safety and well-being above all else.

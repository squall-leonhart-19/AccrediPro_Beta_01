Screening for Mental Health Concerns

Pregnancy is a period of significant physiological and emotional change. These changes can sometimes lead to or exacerbate mental health concerns. Itâ€™s estimated that 1 in 5 women experience a mental health condition during pregnancy or postpartum (Gavin et al., 2005). Screening is a proactive way to identify women who may be at risk and ensure they receive timely support. Remember, early detection can significantly improve outcomes. Your role is to provide a safe space for clients to share their experiences and connect them with appropriate resources. As a prenatal wellness coach, you are NOT a therapist, but an important part of the support team.

Using Validated Screening Tools

Using validated screening tools adds credibility and accuracy to your assessments. These tools have been rigorously tested and standardized to identify individuals at risk for specific conditions.

Here are some commonly used tools:

  • Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS): Despite its name, the EPDS is widely used during pregnancy to screen for depression.
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7): A brief, self-report questionnaire that assesses anxiety symptoms.
  • Perceived Stress Scale (PSS): Measures the degree to which situations in one's life are appraised as stressful.

Let's take a closer look at the EPDS:

Important Note: Screening tools are not diagnostic. A positive screen indicates the need for further evaluation by a qualified mental health professional.

Tool Purpose Administration Scoring
Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) Screen for symptoms of depression during pregnancy and postpartum Self-administered questionnaire Score of 10 or higher suggests possible depression; further evaluation needed
Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7) Screen for symptoms of generalized anxiety disorder Self-administered questionnaire Scores of 5, 10, and 15 represent mild, moderate, and severe anxiety, respectively
Perceived Stress Scale (PSS) Measure the perception of stress Self-administered questionnaire Higher scores indicate higher perceived stress

Assessing Stress and Coping

Stress is a common experience during pregnancy. However, chronic or excessive stress can negatively impact both maternal and fetal well-being (Hobel et al., 2008). As a coach, it's important to help clients identify their stressors and evaluate their coping strategies. Ask open-ended questions like:

  • "What are the biggest sources of stress in your life right now?"
  • "How do you typically cope with stress?"
  • "Are your current coping strategies effective?"

Pay attention to both the sources of stress and the client's coping mechanisms. Are they using healthy strategies like exercise and mindfulness, or less adaptive ones like substance use? A 2023 study in the Journal of Prenatal Psychology (n=212) found that women who reported using emotion-focused coping strategies had significantly lower levels of anxiety compared to those who used avoidance-focused coping (OR = 0.42, 95% CI [0.21, 0.85]).

Exploring Past Trauma

A history of trauma can significantly impact a woman's experience of pregnancy and childbirth. Studies show that women with a history of trauma are at higher risk for postpartum depression, anxiety, and PTSD (Yildiz et al., 2017). It's crucial to approach this topic with sensitivity and respect. You are not a therapist, so you are not going to be doing any trauma processing. Instead, you are creating space for your client to share their experiences and offering support.

Consider asking questions like:

  • "Have you ever experienced any traumatic events in your life?"
  • "Do you feel that these experiences are impacting your pregnancy?"
  • "Do you have support systems in place to help you cope?"

Important: If a client discloses a history of trauma, it's essential to refer them to a qualified mental health professional for specialized support.

Promoting Emotional Regulation

Emotional regulation refers to the ability to manage and respond to emotional experiences in a healthy way. During pregnancy, hormonal changes and life stressors can make emotional regulation more challenging. As a Prenatal Wellness Coachâ„¢, you can teach clients simple techniques to promote emotional regulation, such as:

  • Mindfulness meditation: Focusing on the present moment without judgment.
  • Deep breathing exercises: Slow, controlled breathing can help calm the nervous system.
  • Progressive muscle relaxation: Tensing and releasing different muscle groups to reduce tension.
  • Journaling: Writing down thoughts and feelings can help process emotions.

A meta-analysis of 15 studies (n=784) published in Mindfulness (2022) found that mindfulness-based interventions significantly reduced symptoms of anxiety and depression in pregnant women (SMD = -0.62, 95% CI [-0.88, -0.35]).

Recognizing PMADs and Referral

Perinatal Mood and Anxiety Disorders (PMADs) are a group of mental health conditions that can occur during pregnancy and up to one year postpartum. These disorders include:

  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Postpartum psychosis

It's crucial to recognize the signs and symptoms of PMADs and know when to refer clients to a qualified mental health professional. Some common symptoms include:

Symptom Description
Persistent sadness or low mood Feeling sad, empty, or hopeless for most of the day, nearly every day
Loss of interest or pleasure Markedly diminished interest or pleasure in all, or almost all, activities
Changes in appetite or weight Significant weight loss when not dieting or weight gain, or decrease or increase in appetite nearly every day
Sleep disturbances Insomnia or hypersomnia nearly every day
Fatigue or loss of energy Feeling tired or having little energy nearly every day
Feelings of worthlessness or guilt Feelings of worthlessness or excessive or inappropriate guilt
Difficulty concentrating Diminished ability to think or concentrate, or indecisiveness
Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide Recurrent thoughts of death (not just fear of dying), suicidal ideation without a specific plan, or a suicide attempt
Excessive worry or anxiety Feeling anxious or worried most of the time, often about many different things
Panic attacks Sudden episodes of intense fear or discomfort, accompanied by physical symptoms like heart palpitations, sweating, and shortness of breath

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old pregnant woman in her second trimester.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports feeling increasingly anxious and overwhelmed. She has difficulty sleeping, experiences frequent panic attacks, and feels a constant sense of worry about her baby's health.

Intervention: After listening to Sarah's concerns, the coach administered the GAD-7, which indicated moderate anxiety. The coach provided Sarah with information about PMADs and emphasized the importance of seeking professional help. She provided resources for local therapists specializing in perinatal mental health.

Outcomes: Sarah followed up with a therapist and was diagnosed with generalized anxiety disorder. She began cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) and saw a significant improvement in her symptoms. She also began practicing mindfulness and relaxation techniques, which helped her manage her anxiety in daily life.

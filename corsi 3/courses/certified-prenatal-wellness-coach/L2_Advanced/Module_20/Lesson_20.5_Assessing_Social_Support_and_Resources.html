Using the Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support (MSPSS)

The Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support (MSPSS) is a widely used and validated tool for assessing a client's perceived social support from family, friends, and significant others. This brief, 12-item questionnaire can provide valuable insights into the quality and availability of a client's support system. You can use the information from this assessment to inform your coaching strategies and connect clients with resources to bolster their support.

Here's an example item from the MSPSS:

"There is a special person who is around when I am in need."

Clients rate their agreement with each statement on a 7-point Likert scale, ranging from "Very Strongly Disagree" to "Very Strongly Agree."A score is calculated for each subscale (family, friends, significant other) as well as a total score. Higher scores indicate greater perceived social support.

Important Note: The MSPSS assesses perceived social support. It's possible for someone to have many acquaintances but still feel unsupported. Always validate the client's experience and explore the reasons behind their perceptions.

Assessing Financial Stability and Access to Resources

Financial strain and resource scarcity can significantly impact prenatal health. In the U.S., food insecurity during pregnancy is associated with a 20-25% increased risk of adverse birth outcomes such as preterm birth and low birth weight ( Laraia et al., 2006). It's important to sensitively assess a client's financial situation and access to essential resources such as housing, food, transportation, and healthcare.

Consider these questions during your assessment:

  • "Are you currently employed? If so, is your employment stable?"
  • "Do you have access to affordable and nutritious food?"
  • "Are you able to afford your housing and utilities?"
  • "Do you have health insurance or access to prenatal care services?"
  • "Do you have reliable transportation to attend appointments?"

Remember to approach these questions with empathy and cultural sensitivity. Clients may be hesitant to disclose financial difficulties due to shame or fear. Reassure them that your goal is to connect them with resources that can support a healthy pregnancy.

Identifying Barriers to Accessing Prenatal Care

Beyond financial constraints, several other barriers can hinder access to prenatal care. These may include:

  • Lack of transportation: Difficulty getting to appointments due to lack of a vehicle or public transportation options.
  • Childcare challenges: Difficulty finding or affording childcare for existing children during appointments.
  • Language barriers: Difficulty communicating with healthcare providers due to language differences.
  • Cultural beliefs: Cultural beliefs or practices that may discourage seeking prenatal care.
  • Fear or mistrust of the healthcare system: Past negative experiences or systemic biases that lead to distrust.

A 2021 study published in "Maternal and Child Health Journal" found that women who reported experiencing at least one barrier to prenatal care were 2.5 times more likely to delay or forgo prenatal care altogether (Simmons et al., 2021).

Evaluating Relationship Dynamics

The quality of a client's relationships, particularly with their partner, family, and close friends, can significantly impact their well-being during pregnancy. Supportive relationships can buffer against stress and promote positive health behaviors, while strained or abusive relationships can have detrimental effects.

Consider exploring these areas:

  • Partner support: The level of emotional, practical, and financial support provided by the partner.
  • Family support: The availability of support from family members, including parents, siblings, and in-laws.
  • Social isolation: Feelings of loneliness or disconnection from others.
  • Domestic violence: Screening for physical, emotional, or financial abuse.

If you suspect domestic violence, it's crucial to provide the client with information about resources and support services. Prioritize their safety and well-being above all else.

Developing Strategies to Enhance Social Support

Based on your assessment, develop strategies to enhance the client's social support network and connect them with relevant community resources. This may involve:

  • Connecting clients with support groups: Prenatal support groups, parenting classes, or online communities can provide a sense of belonging and shared experience.
  • Referring clients to social services: Connecting clients with resources such as food banks, housing assistance programs, and transportation services.
  • Encouraging communication and conflict resolution skills: Helping clients improve communication skills to strengthen relationships.
  • Promoting self-care activities: Encouraging clients to engage in activities that promote well-being and reduce stress.

Remember to empower the client to take an active role in building their support network. Help them identify their strengths and resources and develop a plan that aligns with their goals and values.

Case Study: Maria, Age 28

Client: Maria, a 28-year-old first-time mother, presents with feelings of anxiety and isolation. She recently moved to a new city and lacks a strong support network. She also reports financial strain due to job loss.

Intervention: The coach administered the MSPSS, which revealed low perceived social support from family and friends. The coach connected Maria with a local prenatal support group and a job training program. They also worked on communication skills to help Maria build stronger relationships with her partner and family.

Outcomes: After several weeks, Maria reported feeling less isolated and more confident in her ability to cope with the challenges of pregnancy. She secured a new job and found a sense of community through the support group.

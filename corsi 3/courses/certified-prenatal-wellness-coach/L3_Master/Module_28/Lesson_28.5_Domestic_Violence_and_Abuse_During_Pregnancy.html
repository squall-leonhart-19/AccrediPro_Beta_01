Recognizing Domestic Violence

Domestic violence is a pattern of abusive behaviors used by one partner to gain or maintain power and control over another partner. Pregnancy can be a trigger for the escalation of violence, making it essential to be vigilant. According to the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG), approximately 4-8% of pregnant women experience physical violence during pregnancy, but the actual numbers are likely higher due to underreporting.

Signs and symptoms of domestic violence can be subtle and varied. They include:

  • Physical injuries (bruises, cuts, burns).
  • Emotional distress (anxiety, depression, panic attacks).
  • Social isolation (limited contact with friends and family).
  • Unexplained absences from appointments.
  • Controlling behavior by the partner (monitoring phone calls, restricting movement).
  • Statements that minimize or deny abuse.

It's crucial to remember that abuse can take many forms, including physical, emotional, verbal, sexual, and financial. As a coach, you are not expected to be a therapist, but recognizing these signs is the first step in offering help. We are building upon the skills we discussed in Module 1, T: Tune-In.

Tune-In: Creating a Safe Space

Creatinga safe and confidential space is paramount when addressing domestic violence. Your ability to Tune-In to the client's emotional state and build trust will determine whether they feel comfortable disclosing their experiences. Begin by ensuring privacy during your sessions and explaining the limits of confidentiality (e.g., mandated reporting requirements if there is a risk of harm to the client or others).

Use open-ended questions to encourage disclosure. Instead of asking, "Are you being abused?" try:

  • "How are things going at home?"
  • "Do you feel safe in your relationship?"
  • "Has your partner been supportive during your pregnancy?"

Pay attention to nonverbal cues such as body language, tone of voice, and eye contact. If you suspect abuse, ask direct questions about safety, such as "Are you afraid of your partner?" or "Has your partner ever hurt you?" Be prepared for the client to deny or minimize the abuse. It may take time for them to feel safe enough to disclose the truth.

Case Study: Maria, 28

Maria, a 28-year-old first-time mother, presented with symptoms of anxiety and frequent headaches. During a prenatal wellness coaching session, she mentioned that her partner had become increasingly irritable since she became pregnant. Using open-ended questions, the coach gently inquired about her safety at home. Maria initially denied any problems but eventually disclosed that her partner had been verbally abusive and had pushed her during an argument. The coach validated Maria's feelings, provided information about domestic violence resources, and helped her develop a safety plan. Maria was connected with a local shelter and legal aid services. She ultimately made the decision to leave the abusive relationship and create a safe environment for herself and her baby.

Resource & Educate: Providing Support

Once a client discloses experiences of abuse, it's crucial to provide them with information about available resources. This includes:

  • Domestic violence shelters.
  • Hotlines (e.g., The National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-SAFE).
  • Legal assistance.
  • Counseling services.
  • Support groups.

Develop a safety plan with the client. This plan should include steps they can take to protect themselves and their children if they are in immediate danger. A safety plan might include:

  • Identifying a safe place to go (e.g., a friend's house, a shelter).
  • Packing an emergency bag with essential items (e.g., money, medication, important documents).
  • Establishing a code word with trusted friends or family members.
  • Knowing how to call for help (e.g., dialing 911).

Remember, your role is to provide information and support, not to tell the client what to do. Empower them to make their own decisions based on what feels safest for them.

Individualized Plan: Safety First

Each client's situation is unique, and their safety plan should be tailored to their specific needs and circumstances. Consider factors such as:

  • The severity and frequency of the abuse.
  • The presence of children.
  • The client's access to resources.
  • The client's cultural background.

If the client chooses to stay in the relationship, focus on strategies to increase their safety, such as:

  • Avoiding arguments in certain areas of the house (e.g., the kitchen, where there are potential weapons).
  • Developing a signal to alert neighbors or friends if they are in danger.
  • Keeping a cell phone charged and readily accessible.

If the client chooses to leave the relationship, help them develop a plan for doing so safely. This might involve:

  • Obtaining a restraining order.
  • Changing the locks on their doors.
  • Informing their employer and school about the situation.

Collaborate with other professionals, such as law enforcement, social workers, and therapists, to ensure the client receives comprehensive support.

Visualize & Validate: Empowering Choices

Validate the client's experience and acknowledge the challenges they face. Let them know that they are not alone and that help is available. As discussed in Module 1, V: Validate. Reinforce their strengths and resilience, and empower them to make choices that prioritize their safety and well-being. According to a study published in the Journal of Interpersonal Violence, women who receive supportive interventions are more likely to leave abusive relationships and experience improved mental health outcomes.

Help the client visualize a future free from violence and abuse. Encourage them to set goals and take steps toward creating a safer and healthier life for themselves and their children. This may involve:

  • Seeking therapy to address trauma and build coping skills.
  • Participating in support groups to connect with other survivors.
  • Pursuing educational or vocational opportunities to increase their financial independence.

Remind the client that they have the right to live free from violence and abuse, and that they deserve to be treated with respect and dignity.

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Sarah, a 32-year-old pregnant woman, had been experiencing emotional and financial abuse from her partner for several years. She felt trapped and hopeless, but during a prenatal wellness coaching session, she disclosed her situation. The coach listened empathetically, validated Sarah's feelings, and provided information about available resources. Sarah decided to attend a support group for survivors of domestic violence. Through the support group, she gained the courage to leave the abusive relationship and obtain a restraining order. She also enrolled in a job training program and secured a stable job. Sarah gave birth to a healthy baby and is now living a life free from abuse.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: What are some subtle signs and symptoms of domestic violence during pregnancy?

Subtle signs include emotional distress, social isolation, unexplained absences, and controlling behavior by the partner.

Question 2: What are the key elements of a safety plan for a client experiencing domestic violence?

A safety plan should include identifying a safe place to go, packing an emergency bag, establishing a code word, and knowing how to call for help.

Key Takeaways

  • Domestic violence is a serious issue that can escalate during pregnancy.
  • Creating a safe and confidential space is essential for clients to disclose experiences of abuse.
  • Providing information about resources and developing a safety plan can empower clients to make choices that prioritize their safety.
  • Collaboration with other professionals is crucial to ensure clients receive comprehensive support.
  • Validating the client's experience and empowering them to make choices can help them create a safer and healthier life for themselves and their children.

Her Question

The Case She Presents

Maria's Client Case: Sarah, 48

What Maria Tells You:

"Sarah, 48, is dealing with a messy divorce and custody battle. She called me last night, sobbing, saying she 'can't take it anymore' and 'wishes she could just disappear.' I'm terrified she's going to hurt herself. I told her to call a crisis hotline, but she refused. What should I do?"

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Maria assessed Sarah's level of immediate risk (plan, means, intent)?
  • Does Maria understand her ethical and legal obligations in this situation?
  • Is Maria aware of resources beyond crisis hotlines (e.g., mobile crisis units, psychiatric hospitals)?
  • How is Maria managing her own anxiety and countertransference?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Maria

1

Prioritize Safety and Assessment

Emphasize the importance of a thorough risk assessment. "Maria, the first thing we need to determine is how imminent the danger is. Does Sarah have a specific plan? Does she have the means to carry it out? What is her stated intent?"

2

Review Ethical and Legal Obligations

Clarify the duty to protect. "In situations like this, we have a legal and ethical obligation to take action if we believe the client is a danger to herself or others. This might involve contacting emergency services, even against the client's wishes."

3

Explore Alternative Resources

Discuss options beyond crisis hotlines. "Mobile crisis units can come to the client's location and provide on-site assessment and support. Psychiatric hospitals offer a safe environment for stabilization. It's important to know all the resources available in your area."

4

Address Countertransference and Boundaries

Help Maria manage her own emotional response. "It's natural to feel anxious in these situations. But it's crucial to maintain your boundaries and avoid over-involvement. Remember, you can't save everyone. Your role is to assess, support, and connect them with appropriate resources."

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Validate Her Feelings
"Maria, it's completely understandable that you're feeling overwhelmed. These situations are incredibly stressful, especially for new practitioners. It's brave of you to reach out for support."
Ask About Her Assessment
"Before we go further, can you tell me what you know about Sarah's current state? Did you ask her directly if she had a plan to harm herself? What were her responses?"
Provide Clear Guidance
"Based on what you've told me, it sounds like Sarah is at elevated risk. Given her statements and emotional state, I would strongly recommend contacting a mobile crisis unit or, if necessary, emergency services to ensure her safety."
Empower Her Moving Forward
"This is a challenging situation, Maria, and you're doing your best. Let's create a safety plan together for Sarah, and let's also explore some self-care strategies for you. Remember, you're not alone in this."

You're Becoming a Leader!

Guiding new practitioners through crisis situations is a vital part of leadership. Your experience and wisdom can help them navigate these challenges with confidence and compassion.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the first priority when a mentee presents a case involving potential self-harm?

The first priority is to assess the client's level of immediate risk, including plan, means, and intent.

2. What ethical considerations are paramount when supervising a crisis case?

The duty to protect the client and others takes precedence, potentially requiring breaching confidentiality to ensure safety.

Key Takeaways

  • Prioritize client safety and conduct thorough risk assessments in crisis situations.
  • Understand your ethical and legal obligations, including the duty to protect.
  • Explore a range of resources beyond crisis hotlines, such as mobile crisis units and psychiatric hospitals.
  • Address countertransference and help mentees set healthy boundaries.
  • Validate the mentee's feelings and provide clear, direct guidance in stressful situations.

Trauma and Anxiety: The Brain's Response

Trauma can significantly alter brain function, particularly in the amygdala (the brain's alarm system) and the hippocampus (responsible for memory processing). In individuals with trauma-related anxiety, the amygdala may become hyperactive, leading to an exaggerated fear response, while the hippocampus may struggle to process and integrate traumatic memories, resulting in intrusive thoughts and flashbacks. A 2019 study in the Journal of Traumatic Stress found that individuals with PTSD showed a 22% reduction in hippocampal volume compared to controls (van Rooij et al., 2019).

EFT for Anxiety Symptoms

EFT offers a gentle yet effective way to regulate the nervous system and reduce anxiety symptoms. By tapping on specific meridian points while focusing on anxiety-provoking thoughts or sensations, clients can disrupt the neural pathways associated with fear and promote a sense of calm. EFT helps to downregulate the amygdala and facilitate emotional processing. A meta-analysis published in the Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease in 2016 showed that EFT significantly reduced anxiety symptoms compared to control groups (Cohen's d = 0.89, 95% CI= 0.62-1.15) (Sebastian & Nelms, 2016).

  1. Identify the Anxiety Trigger: Help the client pinpoint the specific situation, thought, or memory that triggers their anxiety.
  2. Rate the Intensity: Ask the client to rate their anxiety level on a scale of 0-10, with 10 being the most intense.
  3. Create the Setup Statement: Develop a setup statement that acknowledges the anxiety while affirming self-acceptance. For example: "Even though I feel this anxiety about [trigger], I deeply and completely accept myself."
  4. Tapping Sequence: Use the basic tapping sequence (eyebrow, side of the eye, under the eye, under the nose, chin point, collarbone, under the arm, top of the head) while repeating a reminder phrase related to the anxiety trigger.
  5. Re-evaluate Intensity: After each round of tapping, ask the client to rate their anxiety level again. Continue tapping until the intensity decreases significantly.

Personalized Tapping Sequences for Panic

Panic attacks can be overwhelming, but EFT can provide immediate relief by targeting the physical symptoms of panic, such as rapid heart rate, shortness of breath, and dizziness. Personalized tapping sequences can be tailored to address the specific sensations experienced during a panic attack. Grounding techniques, such as focusing on the present moment and engaging the senses, can be incorporated into the tapping sequence to enhance its effectiveness. A randomized controlled trial in Energy Psychology Journal in 2011 found that EFT significantly reduced the severity and frequency of panic attacks compared to a waiting list control group (Rowe, 2011).

Here's an example of a personalized tapping sequence for panic:

  • Setup: "Even though I'm feeling this panic right now, and my heart is racing, I choose to be calm and safe."
  • Eyebrow: "This racing heart."
  • Side of the Eye: "This shortness of breath."
  • Under the Eye: "This dizziness."
  • Under the Nose: "This overwhelming fear."
  • Chin Point: "I choose to breathe deeply."
  • Collarbone: "I am safe in this moment."
  • Under the Arm: "I release this panic."
  • Top of the Head: "I choose calm and clarity."

Integrating Exposure Therapy with EFT

Exposure therapy involves gradually confronting feared situations or stimuli in a safe and controlled environment. Integrating exposure therapy with EFT can enhance its effectiveness by reducing anxiety and promoting emotional processing. Before, during, and after exposure exercises, EFT can be used to help clients manage their anxiety levels and challenge negative beliefs. A pilot study published in Traumatology in 2013 found that EFT combined with exposure therapy resulted in significant reductions in PTSD symptoms compared to exposure therapy alone (Geronilla et al., 2013).

For example, if a client has a phobia of public speaking, you could use EFT before they practice their speech:

  • Before Exposure: Tap on the anxiety and fear associated with public speaking.
  • During Exposure: If anxiety rises during the practice speech, pause and tap to regulate emotions.
  • After Exposure: Tap on any residual anxiety or negative beliefs that emerged during the practice.

Case Study: Sarah's Journey to Calm

Case Study: Sarah

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old woman, presented with anxiety and panic attacks stemming from a car accident she experienced six months prior. She reported feeling anxious whenever she had to drive or be a passenger in a car. Her symptoms included a racing heart, shortness of breath, and a sense of impending doom.

Intervention: Sarah and I used EFT to address her anxiety symptoms. We started by identifying the specific triggers associated with driving, such as the sound of screeching tires or the sight of other cars. We then developed a personalized tapping sequence that targeted these triggers and the physical sensations she experienced during panic attacks.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Sarah's anxiety levels decreased significantly. She reported feeling more calm and in control when driving. We also integrated exposure therapy by gradually exposing her to driving-related situations, such as sitting in a parked car, driving around the block, and eventually driving on the highway. EFT was used before, during, and after each exposure exercise to manage her anxiety. By the end of our sessions, Sarah was able to drive without experiencing panic attacks and felt confident in her ability to handle any anxiety that might arise.

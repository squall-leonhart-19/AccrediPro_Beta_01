The Role of Core Beliefs

Core beliefs are fundamental assumptions about ourselves, others, and the world. These beliefs, often formed in childhood, can significantly impact our emotional experiences and behaviors. When these beliefs are negative or limiting, they can contribute to a range of emotional difficulties, from anxiety and depression to relationship problems and low self-esteem. For instance, a core belief of "I am not good enough" can lead to chronic self-doubt and a fear of failure.

Within the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢, understanding and addressing core beliefs is crucial. EFT can be a powerful tool for gently challenging and reshaping these beliefs, allowing clients to develop more positive and empowering self-perceptions. Identifying these beliefs is a crucial step in the 'Establish Emotional Connection' phase, as it allows you to tailor your approach to the individual's specific needs.

Eliciting Specific Events

Core beliefs are often reinforced by specific events in a person's life. These events can serve as "proof" of the validity of the belief, further solidifying its hold. To effectively address core beliefs, it's essential to help clients identify the specific events that trigger negative emotions and reinforce limiting beliefs. This process allows clients to connect their current emotional struggles to past experiences, providing valuable insight into the origins of their beliefs.

Techniques for eliciting specific events include asking open-ended questions such as: "Can you think of a time when you felt this way before?" or "What situations tend to bring up these feelings?" Encourage clients to be as detailed as possible, recalling the specific circumstances, thoughts, and emotions associated with the event. This process can be emotionally challenging, so it's vital to maintain a safe and supportive environment, drawing upon the rapport-building skills we covered in previous lessons.

Using the 'Gentle Questioning' Approach

The 'Gentle Questioning' approach is a cornerstone of effective EFT therapy. This involves employing open-ended questions to encourage clients to explore their emotions and experiences in depth, without feeling judged or pressured. The goal is to create a space where clients feel safe to share their thoughts and feelings, even if they are difficult or painful. This approach aligns perfectly with the 'Establish Emotional Connection' phase of the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢

Instead of asking leading questions that suggest a particular answer, focus on questions that invite exploration and reflection. Examples include: "What does that feeling feel like in your body?" or "What thoughts are going through your mind right now?" Pay close attention to both the verbal and nonverbal cues of your client, and adjust your approach accordingly. Remember, your role is to guide and support, not to direct or control.

Differentiating Between Primary and Secondary Emotions

It's common for clients to present with secondary emotions, which are often surface-level reactions to underlying primary emotions. For example, anger might be a secondary emotion masking feelings of sadness or fear. Identifying the primary emotion is crucial because it's the root of the emotional issue. Addressing the secondary emotion alone may provide temporary relief, but it won't lead to lasting change.

To differentiate between primary and secondary emotions, ask yourself: "What is this person really feeling underneath the surface?" Explore the client's emotional history and look for patterns in their emotional responses. Help the client connect with their primary emotions by asking questions like: "If you allowed yourself to feel the most vulnerable emotion in this situation, what would it be?" This process can be challenging, but with patience and empathy, you can help your clients uncover the deeper emotions that are driving their behavior.

A 2021 study in the *Journal of Counseling Psychology* found that interventions that focused on identifying and processing primary emotions led to significantly greater improvements in emotional well-being compared to interventions that only addressed secondary emotions (d = 0.72, 95% CI [0.48, 0.96]). This highlights the importance of this skill in effective therapeutic practice.

Outcomes: Over the course of several sessions, Maria began to challenge her core belief and develop a more positive self-perception.

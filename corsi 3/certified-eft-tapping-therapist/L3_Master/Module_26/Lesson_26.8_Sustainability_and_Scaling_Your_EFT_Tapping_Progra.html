The Challenging Case

Reviewing Sarah's Session

Sarah's Account:

"My client, Emily, 35, was tapping on childhood trauma. She started sobbing, hyperventilating, and said she felt like she was reliving the experience. I froze. I didn't know whether to keep tapping, stop, or call someone. I eventually just held her hand until she calmed down, but I felt so inadequate."

Key Questions to Explore:

  • Did Sarah establish safety and containment strategies before diving into trauma?
  • Was the intensity assessed regularly during the session?
  • Did Sarah have a plan for managing potential abreactions?
  • What are Sarah's own triggers around intense emotions?

Guiding Sarah Forward

Important Points to Cover

1

Emphasize Pre-Session Preparation

Discuss the importance of setting clear expectations, establishing grounding techniques, and creating a "safe word" before addressing potentially triggering topics. This is a cornerstone of trauma-informed practice.

2

Teach Intensity Assessment & Pacing

Explain how to use a Subjective Units of Distress Scale (SUDS) to monitor intensity levels throughout the session. Teach her how to gently pace the tapping to avoid overwhelming the client.

3

Introduce Containment Techniques

Share practical techniques like "container exercise," visualization, or bilateral stimulation (butterfly hug) to help clients manage overwhelming emotions and stay present.

4

Address Practitioner Self-Care

Discuss the importance of recognizing personal triggers and practicing self-care strategies to avoid vicarious traumatization. Encourage Sarah to seek her own therapy or supervision.

Crafting Your Feedback

How to Deliver Constructive Guidance

Acknowledge Her Courage
"Sarah, I commend you for being willing to tackle such sensitive issues with your client. It takes courage to venture into trauma work, and it's completely normal to feel overwhelmed at times."
Validate Her Feelings
"It sounds like you were genuinely concerned for Emily's well-being, and it's understandable that you felt panicked in that moment. Let's explore some strategies to help you feel more prepared in the future."
Offer Specific Suggestions
"Before diving into trauma work, it‚Äôs helpful to explicitly discuss safety strategies with the client. For example, you could say: 'If at any point you feel overwhelmed, we can use the 'safe word' and immediately shift to a grounding technique.' This empowers the client and gives you a clear plan of action."
Empower Her Growth
"What steps can you take to build your confidence and competence in this area? Perhaps some additional training, personal therapy, or ongoing supervision would be beneficial. Remember, you don't have to do this alone."

Check Your Understanding

1. What are three key elements of trauma-informed supervision?

Safety, collaboration, and empowerment. These elements are crucial for both the client and the new practitioner.

2. Why is it important for supervisors to address self-care with their mentees?

Vicarious traumatization is a real risk in this field. Supervisors should model and encourage self-care to prevent burnout and maintain ethical practice.

Nurturing the Next Generation

By sharing your knowledge and experience, you're not only helping new practitioners thrive, but also expanding the reach and impact of EFT in the world. Your leadership matters!

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is about guiding new practitioners, not just fixing their mistakes.
  • Trauma-informed supervision prioritizes safety, collaboration, and empowerment.
  • Intensity assessment and containment techniques are crucial for managing emotional releases.
  • Self-care is essential for both the practitioner and the supervisor.
  • Your mentorship helps expand the reach and impact of EFT.

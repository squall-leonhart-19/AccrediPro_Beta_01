Trauma and PTSD: A Review

As we build upon the foundations laid in Lesson 1, itâ€™s essential to revisit the diagnostic criteria for Trauma and Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD). According to the DSM-5, PTSD involves exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence, followed by intrusive symptoms, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and marked alterations in arousal and reactivity.

The neurobiological impact of trauma is profound. Trauma can disrupt the amygdala (emotional processing), hippocampus (memory), and prefrontal cortex (executive functions). Understanding these neurological changes is crucial for tailoring EFT interventions.

Gentle EFT Techniques

When working with trauma, aggressive or direct approaches can be re-traumatizing. Gentle Techniques in EFT are designed to minimize emotional flooding and promote safety. One such technique is the Tearless Trauma Technique, which involves focusing on different aspects of the traumatic memory without fully engaging with the emotional intensity.

Another approach is the Movie Technique, where clients visualize the traumatic event as a movie, tapping through each scene while maintaining a detached perspective. These techniques align with the 'Establish Emotional Connection' stage of the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢, ensuring the client feels safe and supported.

Cognitive Interweaves

Cognitive interweaves are statements or questions introduced during the tapping sequence to challenge distorted beliefs and facilitate cognitive restructuring. These can be particularly useful in addressing negative cognitions associated with trauma, such as "I am not safe" or "I am damaged."

For example, while tapping, you might introduce the interweave, "What if it's possible to feel safe again?" or "What if this memory doesn't define me?" These interweaves encourage clients to consider alternative perspectives and promote cognitive flexibility. This aligns with the 'Recognize & Reframe' stage of the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢.

Integrating EFT with Trauma-Informed Care

EFT is most effective when integrated with other trauma-informed approaches. Grounding exercises, such as focusing on the five senses or deep breathing, can help clients manage dissociation or emotional overwhelm. Mindfulness practices can enhance self-awareness and promote emotional regulation.

Consider incorporating these techniques before, during, or after EFT sessions to create a holistic and supportive therapeutic environment. This comprehensive approach reflects the 'Evaluate & Ease Intensity' stage of the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢.

Case Study: Sarah, 48

EFT for Chronic PTSD

Sarah, a 48-year-old veteran, presented with chronic PTSD symptoms stemming from her experiences in combat. Her symptoms included intrusive thoughts, nightmares, hypervigilance, and emotional reactivity.

Intervention: We began by establishing safety and rapport, emphasizing control and choice in the therapeutic process. Using the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢, we first Recognized and Reframed her core issues. We utilized Gentle Techniques, like the Movie Technique, to process traumatic memories. Cognitive interweaves were introduced during tapping to challenge negative beliefs such as "I should have done more" and "I am responsible for my comrades' deaths." Grounding exercises were incorporated to manage anxiety and dissociation.

Outcomes: Over 12 sessions, Sarah experienced a significant reduction in PTSD symptoms. Her SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress Scale) scores decreased from 9/10 to 3/10 when recalling the traumatic event. She reported fewer nightmares, improved sleep quality, and a greater sense of emotional control. Sarah also expressed feeling more connected to her family and engaged in social activities, reporting, "I feel like I'm finally starting to live again."

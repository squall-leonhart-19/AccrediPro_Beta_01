Understanding Anxiety Disorders

Welcome back! In our previous lessons, weâ€™ve explored EFT for trauma, addictions, and chronic pain. Now, we'll focus on how EFT can be a powerful tool for managing anxiety disorders. Anxiety is a common experience, but when it becomes excessive and interferes with daily life, it may indicate an anxiety disorder. As an EFT practitioner, understanding the nuances of these disorders is crucial.

Several types of anxiety disorders exist, each with unique characteristics:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD): Persistent and excessive worry about various events or activities.
  • Panic Disorder: Sudden episodes of intense fear that trigger severe physical symptoms.
  • Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD): Intense fear of social situations where one might be scrutinized by others.
  • Phobias: Intense, irrational fear of specific objects or situations.

Applying the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢ to Anxiety

The R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢ provides a structured approach to address anxiety. Letâ€™s break down how to apply each step:

  • R (Recognize & Reframe): Help clients recognize anxious thoughts and reframe them. For example, challenge catastrophic thinking with more balanced perspectives.
  • E (Establish Emotional Connection): Create a safe space where clients feel heard and understood. Validating their feelings is key.
  • S (Sequence Tapping Points): Use the standard EFT sequence while focusing on the anxious thoughts and associated emotions.
  • E (Evaluate & Ease Intensity): Regularly assess the client's SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress Scale) level to gauge the effectiveness of the tapping.
  • T (Transform & Test Beliefs): Identify and transform limiting beliefs that contribute to anxiety. Test new, empowering beliefs through tapping.

Consider this: A 2016 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease found that EFT significantly reduced anxiety symptoms compared to control groups (p < 0.001), with an average effect size of 0.89. This highlights EFT's potential as an evidence-based intervention.(Church et al., 2016)

Using EFT to Reduce Physical Symptoms

Anxiety manifests physically through symptoms like heart palpitations, sweating, muscle tension, and shortness of breath. EFT can directly target these physical manifestations. By tapping through the sequence while focusing on these symptoms, clients can experience significant relief.

For example, a client experiencing a panic attack might focus on the feeling of their racing heart while tapping on the Karate Chop point. The setup statement could be: "Even though my heart is racing and I feel terrified, I deeply and completely accept myself." Subsequent tapping on the other points reinforces this acceptance and helps calm the nervous system.

EFT and Exposure Therapy

Exposure therapy is a technique used to treat phobias and social anxiety by gradually exposing individuals to feared stimuli. Combining exposure therapy with EFT can enhance its effectiveness by reducing the anxiety associated with the exposure.

Imagine a client with social anxiety who fears public speaking. You could start by having them imagine speaking in front of a small group, tapping on their anxiety throughout the visualization. Gradually, you could progress to real-life exposure, using EFT to manage their anxiety before, during, and after each step. This combined approach can lead to more rapid and sustainable results.

Presenting Problem: Jane, a 45-year-old former teacher, presented with a history of panic disorder. She experienced frequent panic attacks characterized by heart palpitations, shortness of breath, dizziness, and a sense of impending doom. These attacks had led her to avoid social situations and significantly impacted her quality of life.

Intervention: Over 12 sessions, Jane was taught the R.E.S.E.T. Frameworkâ„¢ and EFT. Initially, tapping focused on reducing the intensity of her physical symptoms during imagined panic attacks. The setup statement used was: "Even though I feel this panic rising in my chest, I deeply and completely accept myself." As her anxiety decreased, the focus shifted to identifying and challenging the underlying thoughts and beliefs that triggered her panic.

Outcomes: After the sessions, Jane reported a significant reduction in the frequency and intensity of her panic attacks. She was able to gradually re-engage in social activities that she had previously avoided. A three-month follow-up showed that she had maintained her progress and was using self-EFT regularly to manage any residual anxiety.

Teaching Self-EFT for Anxiety Management

Empowering clients to use self-EFT is a crucial component of anxiety management. Teach them the tapping sequence and how to tailor the setup statements to their specific concerns. Encourage them to practice regularly, even when they are not feeling anxious, to build resilience.

A practical exercise is to have clients create a "calming script" that they can use during moments of heightened anxiety. This script should include positive affirmations and visualizations that help them feel grounded and safe. Regularly practicing this script with EFT can create a powerful tool for self-soothing.

The Case Sarah Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with Emily, 35, who is 32 weeks pregnant. She just received news that her baby may have a heart defect. She's devastated, terrified, and overwhelmed. She keeps asking me if I think her baby will be okay, and I don't know what to say. I feel like I'm not equipped to handle this level of distress."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • How is Sarah managing her own emotions in response to Emily's distress?
  • Is Sarah clear on the boundaries of her role as a doula coach?
  • Is Sarah equipped to provide resources and support for Emily's specific needs?
  • How can Sarah empower Emily to advocate for herself and her baby?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Acknowledge and Validate Sarah's Feelings

"It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed in situations like this. It shows you care deeply about your clients."

2

Reinforce Scope of Practice

Remind Sarah that her role is to provide emotional support, education, and resources, not medical advice or guarantees.

3

Focus on Empowering the Client

Guide Sarah to help Emily identify her needs, access reliable information, and communicate effectively with her healthcare providers.

4

Discuss Self-Care Strategies

Emphasize the importance of Sarah taking care of her own well-being to avoid burnout and maintain her effectiveness.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Sarah, thank you for bringing this to me. It takes courage to admit when you're feeling unsure, and I appreciate your commitment to providing the best possible support to your clients."
Explore Her Perspective
"What are your initial thoughts about how to approach this situation? What resources have you considered offering Emily?"
Offer Guidance and Resources
"One approach might be to help Emily connect with support groups for parents of children with heart defects. The American Heart Association and the Congenital Heart Defect Coalition are great resources."
Reinforce Boundaries
"Remember, you're not a medical professional. It's okay to say, 'I don't know,' and then redirect her to her doctor or a specialist."
Empower Next Steps
"What feels like the most important thing to focus on with Emily right now? How can I support you in taking those next steps?"

Check Your Understanding

1. As a supervisor, what's the most important thing to assess when a mentee brings you a challenging case?

The mentee's emotional state and understanding of their scope of practice.

2. What are some ways you can help a mentee empower their client in a crisis situation?

By helping the client identify their needs, access reliable information, and communicate effectively with their healthcare providers.

You're Becoming a Leader!

By mentoring new birth doula coaches, you're not only expanding your impact but also shaping the future of this vital profession. Your guidance will empower them to confidently navigate complex cases and provide exceptional support to families during their most vulnerable moments.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision involves more than just providing answers; it's about fostering critical thinking and emotional resilience in your mentees.
  • Reinforcing scope of practice is crucial, especially in complex cases where the lines between support and medical advice can become blurred.
  • Empowering clients to advocate for themselves is a key aspect of doula coaching, and you can help your mentees develop this skill.
  • Self-care is essential for both you and your mentees to prevent burnout and maintain effectiveness.

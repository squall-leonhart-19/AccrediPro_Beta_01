Understanding Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation

Motivation is the driving force behind our actions. Understanding its different forms is crucial for effective doula coaching. Intrinsic motivation comes from within â€“ a sense of enjoyment, interest, or purpose. Extrinsic motivation, on the other hand, arises from external rewards or pressures, such as money, recognition, or avoiding punishment.

For doulas, a healthy balance is key. While financial stability (extrinsic) is important, a genuine passion for supporting birthing individuals and families (intrinsic) is what fuels long-term commitment and prevents burnout. A 2021 study in the Journal of Perinatal Education found that doulas with high intrinsic motivation reported greater job satisfaction and longevity in the field (Smith et al., 2021).

As a coach, you can help doulas identify and cultivate their intrinsic motivators. Ask questions like:

  • What aspects of doula work do you find most fulfilling?
  • What impact do you hope to make on the lives of your clients?
  • What are your core values as a doula?

Applying Self-Determination Theory

Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by Deci and Ryan, posits that humans have three basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. When these needs are met, individuals are more likely to be intrinsically motivated and experience well-being.

  • Autonomy: The need to feel in control of one's own actions and choices.
  • Competence: The need to feel capable and effective in one's endeavors.
  • Relatedness: The need to feel connected to and cared for by others.

Here's how you can apply SDT in your coaching practice:

  • Foster Autonomy: Encourage doulas to set their own goals and make decisions about their practice. Avoid being overly directive.
  • Promote Competence: Provide opportunities for skill development and celebrate successes. Offer constructive feedback to help doulas improve.
  • Cultivate Relatedness: Create a supportive and collaborative environment where doulas feel connected to you and to each other. Facilitate peer support groups or mentorship programs.

For example, instead of telling a doula "You need to increase your marketing efforts," try asking "What are some marketing strategies you're interested in exploring? What support do you need to implement them?" This approach empowers the doula to take ownership of their growth.

Growth Mindset vs. Fixed Mindset

Carol Dweck's research on mindset has profound implications for motivation and achievement. A growth mindset is the belief that abilities can be developed through dedication and hard work. A fixed mindset, on the other hand, is the belief that abilities are innate and unchangeable.

Doulas with a growth mindset are more likely to embrace challenges, persevere through setbacks, and view effort as a path to mastery. They see failures as opportunities for learning and growth. Doulas with a fixed mindset, however, may avoid challenges, give up easily, and see effort as a sign of inadequacy.

As a coach, you can help doulas cultivate a growth mindset by:

  • Praising effort and progress rather than innate talent.
  • Reframing failures as learning opportunities.
  • Encouraging a focus on continuous improvement.
  • Sharing stories of successful doulas who overcame challenges through hard work and dedication.

For instance, if a doula is struggling to attract clients, instead of saying "Maybe you're just not cut out for this," try saying "Let's explore different marketing strategies and see what works best for you. Every successful doula has faced challenges along the way."

Motivational Interviewing Techniques

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a client-centered, directive method for enhancing intrinsic motivation to change by exploring and resolving ambivalence. It's particularly useful for helping doulas overcome resistance and commit to their goals.

Key principles of MI include:

  • Expressing Empathy: Understanding and accepting the doula's perspective without judgment.
  • Developing Discrepancy: Helping the doula recognize the gap between their current behavior and their desired goals.
  • Rolling with Resistance: Avoiding confrontation and instead inviting the doula to explore their own reasons for change.
  • Supporting Self-Efficacy: Believing in the doula's ability to change and helping them build confidence.

Common MI techniques include:

  • Open-Ended Questions: Asking questions that require more than a "yes" or "no" answer.
  • Affirmations: Recognizing and acknowledging the doula's strengths and efforts.
  • Reflective Listening: Repeating or paraphrasing what the doula has said to show understanding.
  • Summaries: Periodically summarizing the doula's statements to reinforce key points and facilitate deeper exploration.

For example, if a doula expresses reluctance to attend a networking event, you might say, "It sounds like you're feeling hesitant about networking events. On one hand, you see the potential benefits of meeting new people, but on the other hand, you're feeling anxious about putting yourself out there. Is that right?" This reflective listening approach validates the doula's feelings and opens the door for further exploration.

Case Study: Burnout Recovery

Case Study: Sarah's Journey Back to Passion

Client: Sarah, 38, doula for 5 years.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling exhausted, cynical, and detached from her work. She found herself dreading client interactions and questioning her career choice. She described feeling "burned out" and lacking the passion she once had.

Intervention:

  1. Exploration of Intrinsic Motivators: Coaching sessions focused on reconnecting Sarah with her original motivations for becoming a doula. She realized she missed the deep connection she felt with families during vulnerable times.
  2. Application of Self-Determination Theory: Sarah was encouraged to take more control over her schedule and client selection. She decided to specialize in postpartum doula work, which aligned better with her interests and strengths.
  3. Growth Mindset Reframing: Sarah began viewing her burnout as a sign that she needed to make changes, rather than a personal failure. She embraced new training opportunities to enhance her skills in postpartum care.
  4. Motivational Interviewing: Coaching sessions used MI techniques to address Sarah's ambivalence about making changes. She explored the pros and cons of different options and ultimately committed to a new path.

Outcomes: Over several months, Sarah experienced a significant reduction in burnout symptoms. She regained her passion for doula work and reported feeling more energized and fulfilled. She attributed her recovery to the coaching process, which helped her reconnect with her intrinsic motivations and take control of her career.

This case study illustrates how the principles of motivation can be applied to address real-world challenges faced by doulas. By understanding the underlying psychology of motivation, you can empower doulas to overcome obstacles and achieve their full potential.

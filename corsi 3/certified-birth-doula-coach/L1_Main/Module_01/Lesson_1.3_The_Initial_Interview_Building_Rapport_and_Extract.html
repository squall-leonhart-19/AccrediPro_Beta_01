Structuring for Success: A Roadmap

A well-structured interview ensures you cover all essential areas while respecting your client's time and energy. Consider this framework:

  1. Warm Welcome and Introductions: Reiterate your coaching philosophy and the value you bring.
  2. Review of Intake Form: Brieflyreview their completed intake form, clarifying any ambiguities.
  3. Setting the Agenda: Collaboratively establish the focus for the session.
  4. Exploring Core Areas: Delve into their values, beliefs, experiences, and perceived barriers (more on this below).
  5. Summarizing Key Insights: Reflect back what you've heard to ensure understanding.
  6. Action Planning (Optional): If time allows, co-create initial action steps.
  7. Concluding and Scheduling: Thank them for their openness and schedule the next session.

Flexibility is key. Adapt this structure to fit your client's individual needs and personality. Some clients may prefer a more conversational approach, while others appreciate a more structured format.

Questioning Techniques: Unlocking Insights

The art of asking powerful questions is central to effective coaching. Here are some key techniques:

  • Open-Ended Questions: Encourage detailed responses (e.g., "Tell me about your biggest challenge as a doula.").
  • Probing Questions: Seek deeper understanding (e.g., "What specifically makes that challenging?").
  • Clarifying Questions: Ensure accurate comprehension (e.g., "So, if I understand correctly, you're saying...").
  • Reflective Questions: Encourage self-reflection (e.g., "What does that tell you about your values?").
  • Hypothetical Questions: Explore possibilities (e.g., "If you could wave a magic wand, what would your doula practice look like?").

Avoid leading questions that imply a desired answer. Instead, focus on neutrality and genuine curiosity.

Important: Be mindful of cultural differences in communication styles. Some clients may be more reserved or direct than others. Adapt your questioning approach accordingly.

Active Listening: The Key to Understanding

Active listening goes beyond simply hearing words. It involves fully engaging with your client, demonstrating empathy, and creating a safe space for vulnerability. Key elements include:

  • Paying Attention: Minimize distractions and focus on your client's verbal and nonverbal cues.
  • Showing That You're Listening: Use verbal affirmations ("I see," "Uh-huh") and nonverbal cues (nodding, eye contact).
  • Providing Feedback: Paraphrase, summarize, and ask clarifying questions to ensure understanding.
  • Deferring Judgment: Suspend your own opinions and biases.
  • Responding Appropriately: Offer empathy and support, tailoring your response to your client's needs.

A 2018 study in the *International Journal of Listening* found that active listening significantly improves communication effectiveness and builds stronger relationships (92% reported increased understanding). This is especially crucial in the doula profession, where trust and vulnerability are paramount.

Identifying Core Values and Motivations

Understanding your client's core values and motivations is essential for aligning your coaching with their authentic self. Ask questions like:

  • "What's most important to you in your work as a doula?"
  • "What motivates you to get out of bed each morning?"
  • "What are your non-negotiables in your doula practice?"
  • "What impact do you want to make on the world?"

Their answers will reveal their driving forces, allowing you to tailor your coaching to support their unique aspirations. For example, a doula who values "community" may be motivated to create workshops or mentorship programs, while a doula who values "autonomy" may be drawn to independent practice models.

Exploring Past Experiences

Past experiences, both positive and negative, shape our beliefs and behaviors. Delving into your client's history can provide valuable insights into their current challenges and opportunities. Consider questions like:

  • "What are some of your proudest accomplishments as a doula?"
  • "What have been some of your biggest challenges?"
  • "What lessons have you learned from those experiences?"
  • "What advice would you give to your younger self?"

Pay attention to patterns and themes. Are there recurring challenges? What strategies have they used to overcome obstacles in the past? How can you leverage their past successes to build momentum?

Uncovering Perceived Barriers

Identifying perceived barriers is a crucial step in the "Gather Information" phase of The G.U.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢. These are the obstacles that your client believes are preventing them from achieving their goals. They may be real or imagined, but their impact is undeniable. Ask questions like:

  • "What's holding you back from achieving your goals?"
  • "What are your biggest fears or concerns?"
  • "What resources do you feel you're lacking?"
  • "What limiting beliefs might be getting in your way?"

Common barriers for doulas include lack of confidence, financial constraints, time management challenges, and marketing struggles. Once you've identified these barriers, you can begin to address them in the "Uncover" phase of The G.U.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Case Study: Sarah's Confidence Crisis

Client: Sarah, 35, a doula with 3 years of experience.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling overwhelmed and lacking confidence in her ability to attract and retain clients. She expressed fear of rejection and self-doubt.

Intervention: During the initial interview, the coach used open-ended questions to explore Sarah's past experiences and identify her core values. They uncovered a deep-seated fear of failure stemming from a previous business venture.

Outcomes: By acknowledging and validating Sarah's fears, the coach helped her reframe her limiting beliefs. They identified her strengths and refocused her attention on her passion for supporting birthing families. This initial shift in mindset paved the way for Sarah to take more confident action in her business.

Case Study: Maria's Time Management Troubles

Client: Maria, 42, a doula juggling family responsibilities and a growing practice.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria felt constantly stressed and overwhelmed, struggling to balance her work and personal life. She reported feeling guilty for not being present enough for her children.

Intervention: Through active listening and probing questions, the coach helped Maria identify her time management challenges and explore her values around family and career. They uncovered a belief that she had to do everything herself.

Outcomes: The coach challenged Maria's limiting belief and helped her identify opportunities to delegate tasks and set boundaries. They co-created a time management plan that prioritized her values and allowed her to be more present with her family without sacrificing her professional goals.

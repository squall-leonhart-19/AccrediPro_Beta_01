Non-Violent Communication (NVC)

Non-Violent Communication (NVC), developed by Marshall Rosenberg, is a powerful communication framework that emphasizes empathy, honesty, and connection. It focuses on expressing oneself authentically while remaining sensitive to the needs of others. The four components of NVC are:

  1. Observations: Stating the facts without judgment or evaluation.
  2. Feelings: Identifying and expressing your emotions.
  3. Needs: Articulating the underlying needs connected to your feelings.
  4. Requests: Making clear, specific, and actionable requests.

For example, instead of saying "You're always late and it's disrespectful," an NVC approach might be: "When you arrive late for our sessions (observation), I feel frustrated (feeling) because I need to use our time effectively (need). Would you be willing to set a reminder for yourself before our meetings? (request)"

A 2014 study in the Journal of Counseling Psychology found that NVC training significantly improved communication skills and reduced conflict in interpersonal relationships (citation below).

Motivational Interviewing Techniques

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a client-centered, directive method for enhancing intrinsic motivation to change by exploring and resolving ambivalence. Key principles of MI include:

  • Expressing Empathy: Understanding and accepting the client's perspective.
  • Developing Discrepancy: Helping the client recognize the gap between their current behavior and their desired goals.
  • Rolling with Resistance: Avoiding argumentation and working with the client's resistance.
  • Supporting Self-Efficacy: Encouraging the client's belief in their ability to change.

MI techniques include asking open-ended questions, affirming the client's strengths, reflective listening, and summarizing their statements. For instance, instead of directly challenging a client's procrastination, you might ask: "What are some of the benefits of maintaining the status quo? What are some of the drawbacks?" This encourages the client to explore their ambivalence and arrive at their own conclusions.

A meta-analysis published in Addiction in 2011 concluded that MI is an effective intervention for a wide range of behavioral changes (citation below).

Addressing Client Resistance and Ambivalence

Resistance and ambivalence are natural parts of the change process. Recognizing and addressing them effectively is crucial for maintaining a strong coaching relationship. Here are some strategies:

  • Reframe Resistance: View resistance as a signal that the client feels misunderstood or unheard.
  • Empathy First: Validate the client's feelings and perspective before offering solutions.
  • Explore Values: Connect the desired change to the client's core values and motivations.
  • Offer Choices: Empower the client by providing options and allowing them to make decisions.

For example, if a client resists implementing a new marketing strategy, you could say: "I understand that you're hesitant to try something new. What are some of your concerns about this strategy? How does it align with your values and goals for your business?"

Facilitating Difficult Conversations

As a doula coach, you may encounter situations requiring difficult conversations, such as addressing unmet expectations, conflicting desires, or personal challenges impacting the client's progress. Here are some guidelines:

  • Prepare Thoroughly: Clarify your intentions, gather relevant information, and anticipate potential reactions.
  • Create a Safe Space: Choose a private and comfortable setting where the client feels safe to express themselves.
  • Use "I" Statements: Express your concerns and observations from your perspective, avoiding blame or judgment.
  • Listen Actively: Pay attention to both verbal and nonverbal cues, and seek to understand the client's point of view.
  • Focus on Solutions: Collaborate with the client to identify mutually agreeable solutions and action steps.

For example, if a client is consistently missing deadlines, you could say: "I've noticed that you've missed the last few deadlines we set together. I'm concerned that this may be impacting your progress toward your goals. Can we talk about what's been happening and how we can get back on track?"

1. Principles of Adult Learning

Adults learn differently than children. Applying these principles will ensure your group programs are engaging and impactful:

  • Relevance: Connect learning to their real-life experiences and needs.
  • Experience: Value and incorporate their existing knowledge.
  • Autonomy: Give them choices and control over their learning.
  • Practicality: Focus on practical skills they can immediately apply.
  • Motivation: Create a supportive and encouraging environment.

For example, instead of lecturing about breastfeeding positions, have participants practice with dolls and pillows, sharing their own experiences and challenges.

2. Creating a Structured Curriculum

A well-structured curriculum provides a clear roadmap for participants. Hereâ€™s how to create one:

  1. Define Learning Objectives: What should participants know or be able to do by the end of the program?
  2. Choose Relevant Topics: Select topics that align with the learning objectives and address common concerns of expectant and new parents.
  3. Develop Interactive Activities: Incorporate discussions, demonstrations, role-playing, and hands-on activities to keep participants engaged.
  4. Provide Opportunities for Reflection: Encourage participants to reflect on what they are learning and how they can apply it to their own lives.
  5. Offer Resources and Support: Provide handouts, websites, and other resources to support their learning beyond the program.

A SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) framework can be used to define learning objectives. For instance, after a session on infant sleep, a SMART objective could be: "Participants will be able to identify three safe sleep practices for infants by the end of the session."

3. Determining Optimal Group Size

The ideal group size depends on the program's objectives and format. Smaller groups (6-10 participants) allow for more personalized attention and in-depth discussions. Larger groups (15-20 participants) can create a more dynamic and diverse learning environment.

Consider session length and frequency. Shorter, more frequent sessions (e.g., 90 minutes weekly) may be easier for parents to attend than longer, less frequent sessions (e.g., 3 hours monthly). Aim for a consistent schedule to build momentum and foster a sense of community.

4. Leveraging the 'U' Stage of G.U.I.D.E.

The G.U.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢ is a proprietary methodology used to design and deliver exceptional programs. The 'U' stage, Uncover Potential, is about discovering what makes your program unique and valuable.

Ask yourself:

  • What are my unique skills and experiences?
  • What are the specific needs of my target audience?
  • What topics am I passionate about teaching?
  • What format will best engage my audience (e.g., workshop, series, retreat)?

By answering these questions, you can develop a program that stands out from the competition and provides genuine value to participants. For example, you might combine your doula expertise with your knowledge of mindfulness to create a unique program on mindful parenting during pregnancy and postpartum.

5. Sample Program Outline: Prenatal Education Series

Here's a sample outline for a 6-week prenatal education series:

Week Topic Activities
1 Introduction to Pregnancy & Prenatal Care Group introductions, overview of pregnancy milestones, discussion of prenatal care options.
2 Nutrition & Exercise During Pregnancy Interactive presentation on healthy eating habits, demonstration of safe exercises.
3 Labor & Delivery: Stages & Pain Management Discussion of the stages of labor, demonstration of pain management techniques (e.g., breathing exercises, massage).
4 Comfort Measures & Positions for Labor Hands-on practice with comfort measures (e.g., massage, counter-pressure), exploration of different labor positions.
5 Newborn Care & Breastfeeding Basics Demonstration of newborn care techniques (e.g., diapering, swaddling), introduction to breastfeeding positions and latch techniques.
6 Postpartum Recovery & Mental Health Discussion of postpartum physical and emotional changes, strategies for self-care and managing postpartum mood disorders.

6. Income Potential

Group programs offer a scalable income opportunity. You can charge a per-person fee for the entire series or individual workshops. Pricing will depend on your location, experience, and the value you provide.

For example, a 6-week prenatal education series could be priced at $300-$600 per person. If you have 10 participants, that's $3,000-$6,000 in revenue. By offering multiple series throughout the year, you can generate a significant income stream.

Case Study: "Blooming Bellies" Prenatal Workshop Series

Client: Sarah Miller, a certified doula with 5 years of experience.

Presenting Problem: Sarah wanted to expand her services and generate more income but was limited by the number of individual clients she could serve.

Intervention: Sarah developed a 4-week prenatal workshop series called "Blooming Bellies." Each week focused on a different aspect of pregnancy and childbirth, including nutrition, labor and delivery, newborn care, and postpartum recovery. She leveraged the 'Uncover Potential' stage of the G.U.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢ by incorporating her unique expertise in aromatherapy and mindfulness into the workshops.

Outcomes: The "Blooming Bellies" series was a success! Sarah attracted an average of 12 participants per series and received positive feedback on the interactive activities and supportive environment. She generated an additional $4,800 per series (charging $400 per participant) and significantly increased her overall income.

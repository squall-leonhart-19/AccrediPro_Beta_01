Understanding Trauma

Trauma is not simply an event, but the impact of that event on an individual. It overwhelms a person's capacity to cope, leaving lasting effects on their neurological, physiological, and psychological well-being. Consider this definition from the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA): "Individual trauma results from an event, series of events, or set of circumstances that is experienced by an individual as physically or emotionally harmful or life-threatening and that has lasting adverse effects on the individualâ€™s functioning and mental, physical, social, emotional, or spiritual well-being." (SAMHSA, 2014)

Understanding this impact is crucial. Clients who have experienced trauma may present with a wide range of symptoms, including anxiety, depression, difficulty regulating emotions, and challenges in relationships. As astronomy practitioners, we must be equipped to recognize these signs and respond with sensitivity and care.

Trauma-Informed Principles

Trauma-informed practice is guided by several key principles. These principles are foundational to creating a safe and supportive environment for clients. By integrating these principles into your astronomical practice, you demonstrate respect, build trust, and empower clients to take control of their healing journey.

Safety

Prioritize physical and emotional safety for both the client and the practitioner. This includes creating a calm and predictable environment, being mindful of personal space, and establishing clear boundaries.

Trustworthiness & Transparency

Build trust through honesty, consistency, and clear communication. Explain your methods, be upfront about your limitations, and always obtain informed consent before proceeding with any interventions.

Choice

Empower clients by offering choices and respecting their autonomy. Allow them to decide what aspects of their story they wish to share and at what pace they feel comfortable progressing.

Collaboration

Foster a collaborative relationship where clients are active participants in their own healing process. Solicit their feedback, value their insights, and work together to develop personalized strategies.

Empowerment

Focus on clients' strengths and resilience. Help them identify their inner resources and develop coping mechanisms that promote self-efficacy and a sense of control over their lives.

Identifying Trauma Triggers

Trauma triggers are stimuli that remind a person of a traumatic event, leading to a re-experiencing of the trauma. These triggers can be internal (e.g., thoughts, feelings, sensations) or external (e.g., places, people, dates, sounds, smells). In the context of astronomical consultations, triggers may arise from various sources:

  • Specific planetary aspects: Certain challenging aspects (e.g., Saturn-Neptune squares) might resonate with past experiences of disillusionment or loss.
  • House placements: Difficult placements in houses associated with vulnerability (e.g., the 12th house) could evoke feelings of helplessness or isolation.
  • Retrograde motion: The symbolism of retrograde planets might trigger feelings of being stuck or reliving past traumas.
  • Client narratives: The stories clients share about their lives may contain details that trigger traumatic memories.

It's crucial to be attuned to both verbal and nonverbal cues that may indicate a client is being triggered. These cues might include changes in body language (e.g., fidgeting, withdrawing), shifts in emotional state (e.g., increased anxiety, sadness, anger), or difficulty focusing.

Creating a Safe Environment

Creating a safe environment is paramount in trauma-informed practice. This involves establishing both physical and emotional safety. Here are some practical strategies:

  • Physical Space: Ensure the consultation space is clean, comfortable, and private. Offer clients a choice of seating arrangements and be mindful of personal space.
  • Verbal Communication: Use clear, simple language and avoid jargon. Speak in a calm and reassuring tone.
  • Nonverbal Communication: Maintain open and inviting body language. Make eye contact, but don't stare. Be mindful of your own facial expressions and gestures.
  • Boundaries: Establish clear boundaries regarding the scope of your practice and your role as an astronomy practitioner. Be transparent about what you can and cannot offer.
  • Self-Regulation: Practice self-care and mindfulness to regulate your own emotions. This will help you remain grounded and present for your clients, even when they are experiencing intense emotions.

COSMOS Framework Application

The COSMOS Frameworkâ„¢ provides a structured approach to guide clients through transformative experiences. In the context of trauma-informed practice, each step of the framework can be adapted to prioritize safety, trust, and empowerment.

Let's focus on the "Mapping Milestones" step:

  • Trauma-Informed Adaptation: When mapping milestones, focus on achievable goals that promote healing and empowerment. Avoid setting unrealistic expectations or pushing clients beyond their comfort zone.
  • Example: Instead of aiming for a complete resolution of past trauma (which may not be possible or realistic), focus on developing coping mechanisms, building resilience, and fostering self-compassion.
  • Practical Application: Work with the client to identify small, manageable steps they can take to move towards their goals. Celebrate their successes, no matter how small, and provide ongoing support and encouragement.

Case Study: Sarah (38)

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with anxiety, sleep disturbances, and difficulty trusting others. Her natal chart revealed a challenging Saturn-Moon aspect, which she associated with a childhood characterized by emotional neglect.

Intervention: Using a trauma-informed approach, the practitioner focused on creating a safe and supportive environment. They explained the symbolism of the Saturn-Moon aspect in a non-judgmental way and validated Sarah's feelings of emotional deprivation. The practitioner then helped Sarah identify small, achievable goals, such as practicing self-compassion exercises and establishing healthy boundaries in her relationships. The 'Mapping Milestones' step focused on building self-soothing skills and identifying supportive resources.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah reported a decrease in anxiety and improved sleep. She also began to develop a stronger sense of self-worth and an increased capacity for trust. The astronomical insights, combined with trauma-informed care, helped Sarah understand and heal from her past experiences.

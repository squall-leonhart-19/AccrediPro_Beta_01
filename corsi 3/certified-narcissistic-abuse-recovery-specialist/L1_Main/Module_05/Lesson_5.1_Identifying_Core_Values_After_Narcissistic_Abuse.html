The Impact of Narcissistic Abuse on Values

Narcissistic abuse is insidious because it attacks the very core of who you are. Abusers often manipulate and distort your values to gain control. They might criticize your beliefs, invalidate your feelings, or pressure you into acting against your conscience. This can lead to a profound sense of confusion and a loss of self.

Think back to a time before the abuse. What was important to you? Did you value kindness, creativity, or independence? Narcissistic abuse often suppresses these authentic values, replacing them with values that serve the abuser's needs, such as obedience, compliance, and external validation.

A 2017 study in the Journal of Interpersonal Violence found that victims of narcissistic abuse often report a significant decline in their sense of self-worth and a feeling of being disconnected from their own values (Johnson et al., 2017).

Rediscovering Pre-Abuse Values

The good news is that you can reconnect with your authentic values. It takes time and effort, but it's an incredibly rewarding process. Here are some methods to help you rediscover your pre-abuse values:

  • Journaling Prompts: Ask yourself questions like, "What made me happy before the abuse?" "What activities made me feel most alive and authentic?" "What did I believe in passionately?"
  • Guided Meditation: Use guided meditations focused on self-discovery and inner wisdom to access deeply held beliefs and values.
  • Visualization Techniques: Imagine yourself in a safe and supportive environment, free from the abuser's influence. What values guide your actions in this scenario?

These techniques can help you peel back the layers of manipulation and remember who you truly are. As you engage in these practices, be patient with yourself. It's normal to feel confused or uncertain at first. The key is to keep exploring and trusting your intuition.

Values vs. Coping Mechanisms

Itâ€™s crucial to differentiate between your authentic values and the coping mechanisms you developed to survive the abuse. For example, you might have learned to prioritize avoiding conflict at all costs. While this might have been necessary for your safety, it's likely not a core value. Itâ€™s a survival strategy.

Ask yourself, "Am I doing this because I genuinely believe it's the right thing to do, or am I doing it out of fear or obligation?" Authentic values are intrinsically motivating and bring a sense of fulfillment. Coping mechanisms, on the other hand, are often draining and leave you feeling resentful.

Exploring Shadow Values

The concept of "shadow values", introduced by Carl Jung, refers to the disowned or repressed aspects of yourself. Narcissistic abuse canforce you to suppress certain qualities or desires, leading to a disconnect from these shadow values. For example, you might have suppressed your ambition or assertiveness to avoid triggering the abuser's anger.

Exploring your shadow values doesn't mean embracing negativity. It means acknowledging and integrating all parts of yourself, even the ones you've been taught to reject. This can lead to a more complete and authentic sense of self.

Creating a Personal Values Inventory

A personal values inventory is a powerful tool for clarifying your core values. Hereâ€™s how to create one:

  1. Brainstorm a list of values: Start by listing as many values as you can think of (e.g., honesty, compassion, freedom, security, creativity, justice).
  2. Define each value: Write a brief definition of what each value means to you. This will help you differentiate between similar values and identify the ones that truly resonate.
  3. Prioritize your values: Narrow down your list to your top 5-10 core values. Ask yourself, "Which of these values are most important to me? Which ones guide my decisions and actions?"
  4. Reflect on your values: Consider how well your current life aligns with your core values. Are there any areas where you're compromising your values? What steps can you take to live more in alignment with your authentic self?

Case Study: Reclaiming Authenticity

Case Study: Sarah, 38

Client: Sarah, a 38-year-old woman, presented with symptoms of anxiety, depression, and a profound sense of identity loss after escaping a 10-year marriage with a narcissistic partner.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling disconnected from her former self, unsure of her values, and overwhelmed by self-doubt. She struggled to make decisions and felt constantly anxious about pleasing others.

Intervention: Over several months, Sarah engaged in individual therapy sessions focused on value clarification and self-compassion. She used journaling prompts, guided meditations, and visualization techniques to reconnect with her pre-abuse values. She also practiced assertiveness skills to set boundaries and communicate her needs effectively.

Outcomes: Sarah experienced a significant reduction in anxiety and depression symptoms. She developed a clearer sense of her core values, including authenticity, independence, and creativity. She began making decisions based on her own needs and desires, rather than seeking external validation. Sarah also started pursuing her passion for painting, which brought her a sense of joy and fulfillment.

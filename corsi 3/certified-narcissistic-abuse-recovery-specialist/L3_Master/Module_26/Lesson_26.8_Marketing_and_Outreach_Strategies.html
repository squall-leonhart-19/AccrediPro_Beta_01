Her Question
"I'm working with a client with a history of severe childhood abuse. I feel overwhelmed and unsure if I'm equipped to help her."

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case: Emily, 48

What Sarah Tells You:

"Emily is 48 and reports severe childhood emotional and physical abuse by her narcissistic mother. She's experiencing flashbacks, nightmares, and intense anxiety. I've been using grounding techniques and some gentle somatic work, but she seems to be destabilizing. I'm worried I'm doing more harm than good."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Has Sarah assessed for dissociation and suicidality?
  • Is Emily connected to a medical doctor or psychiatrist?
  • Is Sarah pacing the work appropriately, or is she moving too quickly?
  • Does Sarah have a clear understanding of trauma-informed principles?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Prioritize Safety and Stabilization

Emphasize the importance of establishing a strong therapeutic alliance and creating a sense of safety for Emily. Discuss the use of grounding techniques, mindfulness exercises, and self-soothing strategies.

2

Assess for Dissociation and Suicidality

Guide Sarah on how to assess for dissociation, suicidal ideation, and self-harm behaviors. Explain the importance of having a safety plan in place and knowing when to refer to a higher level of care.

3

Pacing and Titration

Teach Sarah about the concept of pacing and titration in trauma work. Explain the importance of moving slowly and gently, allowing Emily to process her experiences at her own pace. Avoid overwhelming her with too much information or intense emotional material.

4

Boundary Setting and Self-Care

Help Sarah understand the importance of setting healthy boundaries with Emily and practicing self-care to avoid burnout. Encourage her to seek supervision and support from experienced professionals.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Appreciation
"Sarah, thank you for bringing this case to supervision. It takes courage to ask for help, and I commend you for prioritizing Emily's well-being."
Explore Her Perspective
"Tell me more about what you've been doing with Emily. What are your goals for her? What are you hoping to achieve?"
Offer Guidance and Support
"Based on what you've shared, I think it's important to prioritize safety and stabilization. Have you assessed for dissociation and suicidality? It's also crucial to pace the work appropriately and avoid overwhelming her. Remember, slow and steady wins the race."
Empower Her Next Steps
"What are your thoughts on this? What feels right to you? I'm here to support you in any way I can. Let's create a plan together that feels safe and effective for Emily."

You're Becoming a Leader!

By mentoring new practitioners like Sarah, you're contributing to a more skilled and compassionate field. Your guidance and support will make a real difference in the lives of both practitioners and clients.

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the key elements of trauma-informed supervision?

Trauma-informed supervision prioritizes safety, collaboration, and empowerment. It also acknowledges the impact of trauma on both the client and the practitioner.

2. How can you help a new practitioner manage transference and countertransference in trauma work?

Encourage self-reflection, provide education on transference and countertransference, and help them develop healthy boundaries. Normalize the experience and offer support.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a crucial component of professional development for practitioners working with trauma survivors.
  • Trauma-informed supervision prioritizes safety, collaboration, and empowerment.
  • Effective supervision involves providing guidance, support, and encouragement.
  • Mentoring new practitioners is a rewarding way to contribute to the field and improve client outcomes.

The Presented Case

Sarah's Client Situation

What Sarah Shares:

"I'm working with Emily, 35, who experienced narcissistic abuse from both parents. She's now struggling with anxiety and relationship issues. During our last session, she disclosed a traumatic childhood memory, and I felt completely lost. I'm worried I re-traumatized her. I validated her feelings, but I'm not sure I did enough."

Key Areas to Explore:

  • Did Sarah have a clear trauma-informed approach?
  • Were appropriate grounding techniques employed?
  • Was the disclosure within the client's window of tolerance?
  • What is Sarah's self-care plan after difficult sessions?

Your Mentoring Approach

Essential Points for Sarah

1

Emphasize Self-Regulation

Explain the importance of the practitioner's own nervous system regulation. "You can't pour from an empty cup." Share techniques like deep breathing and mindfulness.

2

Review Trauma-Informed Principles

Reiterate the core principles: safety, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration, and empowerment. Discuss how these apply in practice.

3

Teach Grounding Techniques

Share practical grounding exercises like the 5-4-3-2-1 method, body scans, or visualization to help clients (and Sarah) stay present.

4

Discuss Scope of Practice

Clarify when a referral to a licensed therapist or psychologist is necessary. L1 practitioners focus on psychoeducation and empowerment, not deep trauma processing.

Crafting Your Feedback

Delivering Constructive and Supportive Feedback

Acknowledge and Validate
"Sarah, it's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when a client shares a difficult trauma history. Your empathy is a strength. Let's explore how we can support you and Emily."
Inquire Before Advising
"What strategies did you consider in the moment? What felt aligned with your training, and what felt challenging?"
Introduce a Principle
"Remember, trauma processing requires specialized training. Our role at L1 is to provide safety, education, and empowerment. We can help clients build resilience and coping skills, but we don't lead them through deep trauma work."
Empower Future Action
"Based on what we've discussed, what adjustments might you make in your next session with Emily? What resources can you offer her to support her healing journey?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is self-regulation crucial for practitioners, especially when mentoring new graduates?

Self-regulation allows practitioners to remain grounded and present during challenging situations. It prevents burnout, ensures clear thinking, and helps maintain appropriate boundaries. For mentors, it's essential to model healthy coping mechanisms for new graduates who are still developing their own self-care practices.

2. What are some practical ways to teach a mentee about scope of practice?

Share specific examples of client scenarios that fall outside the L1 scope. Discuss the importance of recognizing personal limitations and making appropriate referrals to licensed therapists or specialists. Provide resources and guidelines on ethical practice and professional boundaries.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring involves guiding new practitioners while prioritizing their well-being and ethical practice.
  • Emphasize self-regulation, trauma-informed principles, and clear boundaries.
  • Provide constructive feedback that empowers independence and celebrates growth.
  • Clarify scope of practice and encourage referrals when necessary.
  • Remember that effective mentoring multiplies your impact in the field.

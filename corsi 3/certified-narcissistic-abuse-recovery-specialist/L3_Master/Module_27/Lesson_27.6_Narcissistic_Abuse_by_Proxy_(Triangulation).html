Defining Triangulation

Triangulation is a manipulation tactic where a narcissist involves a third person to create a power dynamic, control a situation, or gain validation. This third person can be a family member, friend, colleague, or even a child. The narcissist uses this person to relay messages, spread rumors, or create conflict between the victim and the third party. This creates a sense of confusion, isolation, and dependence in the victim.

Triangulation serves several purposes for the narcissist:

  • Maintaining Control: By involving a third party, the narcissist can control the narrative and the dynamics between individuals.
  • Avoiding Direct Confrontation: The narcissist avoids direct responsibility by using someone else to deliver hurtful messages or actions.
  • Gaining Validation: The narcissist seeks validation from the third party, reinforcing their distorted sense of self-importance.
  • Creating Division: Triangulation often results in division and conflict between the victim and others.

A 2017 study in the Journal of Social and Personal Relationships found that triangulation is a common tactic used in families with narcissistic parents, leading to increased conflict and decreased family cohesion.

Roles and Motivations

In triangulation, there are typically three roles:

  1. The Narcissist: The instigator of the triangulation, seeking control, validation, and avoidance of responsibility.
  2. The Victim: The target of the narcissist's manipulation, often feeling confused, isolated, and devalued.
  3. The Third Party (Proxy): The person used by the narcissist to carry out their agenda, either knowingly or unknowingly.

The motivations of the third party can vary:

  • Naivety: Some individuals may be unaware of the narcissist's manipulative intentions and genuinely believe they are helping or mediating.
  • Fear: Others may be afraid of the narcissist's reaction if they refuse to participate.
  • Seeking Approval: Some may be motivated by a desire for the narcissist's approval or validation.
  • Shared Beliefs: In some cases, the third party may share similar beliefs or biases as the narcissist, making them more willing to participate in the abuse.

Understanding these roles and motivations is crucial in helping clients recognize and address the dynamics of triangulation. This knowledge is fundamental to the Assessment and Diagnosis component of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Breaking Free from Triangulation

Breaking free from triangulation requires a multi-faceted approach:

  1. Recognize the Pattern: Help your client identify the instances of triangulation in their relationships. Encourage them to document these instances to gain clarity.
  2. Establish Boundaries: Teach your client to set clear and firm boundaries with both the narcissist and the third party. This includes refusing to engage in conversations that involve triangulation.
  3. Limit Contact: Depending on the severity of the abuse, limiting or cutting off contact with the narcissist and the third party may be necessary.
  4. Seek Support: Encourage your client to seek support from a therapist, support group, or trusted friends and family members.
  5. Focus on Self-Care: Prioritize self-care activities to rebuild self-esteem and emotional well-being.

A 2021 study in the Journal of Family Psychology highlighted the effectiveness of boundary-setting in reducing the impact of narcissistic abuse, with 78% of participants reporting improved emotional well-being after establishing clear boundaries.

Emotional and Psychological Impact

Being a victim of triangulation can have significant emotional and psychological consequences:

  • Confusion and Doubt: The conflicting messages and manipulations can lead to confusion and self-doubt.
  • Anxiety and Stress: Constant vigilance and fear of manipulation can cause chronic anxiety and stress.
  • Depression: The isolation and devaluation can contribute to feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and depression.
  • Low Self-Esteem: Being constantly criticized and manipulated can erode self-esteem and self-worth.
  • Difficulty Trusting Others: The betrayal and manipulation can make it difficult to trust others in the future.

Addressing these emotional and psychological impacts requires a trauma-informed approach, focusing on validation, empowerment, and healing. This aligns with the Trauma Awareness and Healing Modalities component of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Impact on Children

When children are used as proxies in parental narcissistic abuse, the impact can be particularly devastating. Children may be forced to take sides, relay messages, or act as spies for the narcissistic parent. This can lead to:

  • Emotional Distress: Children may experience anxiety, guilt, and confusion.
  • Damaged Relationships: The triangulation can damage the child's relationship with the other parent or siblings.
  • Role Reversal: Children may be forced to take on adult responsibilities, leading to role reversal and emotional immaturity.
  • Difficulty Forming Healthy Relationships: The experience can make it difficult for children to form healthy relationships in the future.

Intervention strategies should focus on protecting the child from further abuse, providing therapy to address the emotional damage, and fostering healthy relationships. This is a critical aspect of the Intervention and Support Strategies component of the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢.

Case Study: Lisa

Case Study: Lisa

Client: Lisa, 38 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Lisa presented with symptoms of anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem. She reported feeling constantly confused and manipulated in her relationship with her mother, Carol.

Background: Lisa described her mother, Carol, as a charming but controlling woman. Carol frequently involved Lisa's younger sister, Emily, in their conflicts. Carol would often relay negative messages about Lisa through Emily, creating tension and division between the sisters.

Intervention:

  • Psychoeducation about narcissistic abuse and triangulation.
  • Exploration of Lisa's relationship patterns and identification of instances of triangulation.
  • Development of boundary-setting skills.
  • Cognitive restructuring to challenge negative self-beliefs.
  • Emotional processing of past traumas.

Outcomes:

  • Lisa gained a clearer understanding of her mother's manipulative tactics.
  • She established firm boundaries with her mother and sister, refusing to engage in triangulated conversations.
  • Lisa reported a significant reduction in anxiety and depression symptoms.
  • Her self-esteem improved, and she began to prioritize her own needs and well-being.

Key Takeaways

  • Triangulation is a manipulation tactic used by narcissists to control, divide, and gain validation.
  • Understanding the roles and motivations in triangulation is crucial for identifying and addressing the dynamic.
  • Breaking free from triangulation requires recognizing the pattern, establishing boundaries, and seeking support.
  • The emotional and psychological impact of triangulation can be significant, requiring a trauma-informed approach to healing.
  • When children are used as proxies, the impact can be particularly devastating, necessitating intervention and support.

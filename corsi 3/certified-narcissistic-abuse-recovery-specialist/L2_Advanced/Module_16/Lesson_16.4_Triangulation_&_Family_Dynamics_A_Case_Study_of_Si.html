Triangulation, in this context, refers to a manipulative tactic where a narcissistic parent involves a third party (often another sibling) to create conflict, maintain control, and avoid direct communication. Case Study: Sarah and the Sibling Rivalry

Sarah's Story

Client: Sarah L., 32 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with symptoms of anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem. She reported a history of strained relationships with her family, particularly her younger brother, Mark. Sarah described a childhood characterized by constant competition and comparison, orchestrated by their mother, a suspected narcissist.

Background: Sarah recounted how her mother would frequently compare her achievements to Mark's, often highlighting Mark's perceived successes while downplaying Sarah's accomplishments. This created a dynamic where Sarah felt constantly inadequate and resentful towards her brother. Their mother would also confide in Mark about Sarah's "flaws" and vice versa, further fueling the sibling rivalry. Sarah felt betrayed and manipulated, leading to a deep sense of distrust and isolation within the family.

Intervention: Using the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢, we focused on several key areas:

  • Triangulation Awareness: Educating Sarah about the dynamics of triangulation and how it had affected her relationship with her brother.
  • Inner Child Work (I): Addressing Sarah's feelings of rejection, abandonment, and worthlessness stemming from her mother's favoritism and manipulation.
  • Boundary Setting: Developing strategies for setting healthy boundaries with her mother and brother to protect herself from further emotional abuse.
  • Support System: Encouraging Sarah to seek support from outside the family, including therapy and support groups for adult children of narcissistic parents.

Outcomes: After several months of therapy, Sarah reported a significant improvement in her mental health. She gained a deeper understanding of her family dynamics and the role she played within them. Sarah began to set clearer boundaries with her mother, limiting contact and refusing to engage in conversations that perpetuated the triangulation. She also started to rebuild her relationship with her brother, recognizing that he was also a victim of their mother's manipulation. Sarah's self-esteem improved, and she developed a stronger sense of self-worth, independent of her family's approval.

Analyzing Family Roles and Dynamics

In families with narcissistic dynamics, specific roles often emerge to maintain the dysfunctional system. Recognizing these roles is crucial for understanding the client's experience and tailoring the intervention. In Sarah's case, we identified the following roles:

  • The Narcissistic Parent: The parent who exhibits narcissistic traits, such as a lack of empathy, a need for admiration, and a tendency to manipulate others. In this case, Sarah's mother.
  • The Enabler: The parent or family member who supports the narcissistic parent's behavior, either actively or passively. This role often involves minimizing the abuse, making excuses for the narcissist, or complying with their demands.
  • The Scapegoat: The family member who is blamed for all the family's problems and becomes the target of the narcissist's anger and criticism. This role is often assigned to the child who challenges the narcissist's authority or threatens their sense of control. In some cases, both siblings can be scapegoated at different times, depending on the narcissist's needs.
  • The Golden Child: The family member who is idealized and praised by the narcissistic parent. This child is often used to bolster the narcissist's ego and maintain their image of being a "good" parent. However, the golden child's position is often precarious, as they can be easily replaced if they fail to meet the narcissist's expectations. In Sarah's case, Mark was often positioned as the "golden child," leading to resentment from Sarah.

Understanding these roles helps clients like Sarah make sense of their experiences and recognize that they are not responsible for the family's dysfunction. A 2020 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Child Abuse & Neglect* found a significant correlation between childhood exposure to narcissistic parenting and increased risk of depression, anxiety, and complex trauma in adulthood (Miller et al., 2020).

Inner Child Work: Addressing Rejection and Abandonment

As a Certified Narcissistic Abuse Recovery Specialistâ„¢, you understand the profound impact of childhood trauma on adult functioning. Inner Child Work (I) within the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢ is essential for addressing the emotional wounds caused by narcissistic abuse. In Sarah's case, we focused on her feelings of rejection, abandonment, and worthlessness stemming from her mother's favoritism and manipulation.

The goal of Inner Child Work is to connect with the wounded child within and provide them with the love, validation, and protection they did not receive in childhood. This involves:

  • Identifying the Inner Child: Helping the client recognize and connect with the part of themselves that still carries the emotional wounds of childhood.
  • Validating the Inner Child's Feelings: Acknowledging and validating the Inner Child's feelings of pain, anger, and fear, without judgment or criticism.
  • Reparenting the Inner Child: Providing the Inner Child with the love, support, and guidance they needed but did not receive from their parents.
  • Setting Boundaries for the Inner Child: Teaching the Inner Child how to set healthy boundaries with others to protect themselves from further abuse and manipulation.

For Sarah, Inner Child Work involved visualizing herself as a young girl and comforting her, telling her that she was loved, valued, and worthy of attention. This process helped Sarah to heal her emotional wounds and develop a stronger sense of self-worth.

Setting Boundaries and Protecting Yourself

Setting healthy boundaries is crucial for protecting oneself from further abuse and manipulation in narcissistic family systems. Boundaries are the limits we set to define what is acceptable and unacceptable behavior in our relationships. For adult children of narcissistic parents, setting boundaries can be challenging, as they have often been conditioned to prioritize the needs of others over their own. However, it is essential for their well-being.

In Sarah's case, we worked on setting the following boundaries:

  • Limiting Contact: Reducing the amount of time she spent with her mother and brother, particularly during holidays and family gatherings.
  • Avoiding Triggering Topics: Refusing to engage in conversations that perpetuated the triangulation or brought up painful memories.
  • Enforcing Consequences: Implementing consequences for boundary violations, such as ending the conversation or leaving the situation.
  • Prioritizing Self-Care: Making time for activities that nourished her mind, body, and spirit, such as exercise, meditation, and spending time with supportive friends.

Setting boundaries is not about punishing others but about protecting oneself from further harm. It is a form of self-respect and self-care. A 2022 study in the *Journal of Interpersonal Violence* found that individuals who set and maintain healthy boundaries in their relationships reported significantly lower levels of stress and anxiety (Brown et al., 2022).

Finding Support and Validation Outside the Family

For adult children of narcissistic parents, finding support and validation outside the family system is essential for healing. Narcissistic families often create an environment where the individual's feelings and experiences are invalidated, leading to a deep sense of isolation and loneliness. Seeking support from others who understand their experiences can provide a sense of belonging and validation.

In Sarah's case, we explored the following options:

  • Therapy: Individual therapy with a therapist who specializes in narcissistic abuse recovery.
  • Support Groups: Support groups for adult children of narcissistic parents, where she could connect with others who shared similar experiences.
  • Trusted Friends: Building and nurturing relationships with trusted friends who could provide emotional support and validation.
  • Online Communities: Engaging in online communities and forums dedicated to narcissistic abuse recovery.

Finding support and validation outside the family helps individuals to break free from the cycle of abuse and build a healthier sense of self. It also provides them with the resources and tools they need to navigate their relationships with their family members in a healthier way.

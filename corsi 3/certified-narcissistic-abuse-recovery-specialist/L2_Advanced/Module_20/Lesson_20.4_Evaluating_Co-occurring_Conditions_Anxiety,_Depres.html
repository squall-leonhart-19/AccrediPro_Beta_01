1. Review of Assessment Tools

As discussed in previous modules, narcissistic abuse often leads to a range of co-occurring mental health conditions. Let's review some key assessment tools:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item (GAD-7): A widely used tool for screening and measuring the severity of generalized anxiety disorder.
  • Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9): A brief self-report questionnaire used to screen for and measure the severity of depression.
  • Experiences in Close Relationships-Revised (ECR-R): A self-report measure of adult attachment styles, assessing attachment-related anxiety and avoidance.

Remember to administer these tools in a trauma-informed manner, providing a safe and supportive environment for your client. In Module 18, we discussed the importance of establishing rapport and trust before initiating any assessment

2. Understanding the Interplay

The interplay between narcissistic abuse, trauma, and mental health symptoms is complex. Narcissistic abuse can lead to complex post-traumatic stress disorder (C-PTSD), which often co-occurs with anxiety, depression, and attachment issues. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that individuals with a history of emotional abuse were 3.2 times more likely (95% CI: 2.8-3.7) to develop anxiety disorders compared to those without such a history (Goodkind et al., 2023).

Understanding these relationships is crucial for accurate assessment and effective treatment planning. For example, a client presenting with depressive symptoms may also have underlying trauma and attachment issues stemming from narcissistic abuse. Addressing all these factors is essential for long-term healing.

3. Strategies for Using Assessment Data

Assessment data is only valuable if it informs treatment planning. Here are some strategies for using assessment data effectively, aligning with the T.H.R.I.V.E. Frameworkâ„¢:

  1. Prioritize interventions: Use assessment results to identify the most pressing issues and prioritize interventions accordingly. For example, if a client scores high on both the GAD-7 and PHQ-9, address the anxiety first, as it may be exacerbating the depressive symptoms.
  2. Tailor treatment: Customize treatment plans based on individual needs and assessment results. A client with high attachment anxiety may benefit from attachment-based therapy, while a client with severe trauma symptoms may require trauma-focused therapy.
  3. Monitor progress: Use assessment tools to track progress and adjust treatment as needed. Administer the GAD-7 and PHQ-9 periodically to monitor changes in anxiety and depression symptoms.

Remember to involve your client in the treatment planning process, empowering them to take an active role in their recovery. Research indicates that collaborative treatment planning leads to better outcomes and increased client satisfaction (Duncan & Miller, 2000).

4. Impact of Attachment Styles

Attachment styles play a significant role in the recovery process for survivors of narcissistic abuse. Individuals with insecure attachment styles (anxious, avoidant, or disorganized) may have difficulty forming healthy relationships and may be more vulnerable to further abuse. The ECR-R can help identify attachment-related anxiety and avoidance.

Attachment-based therapy can be beneficial for clients with insecure attachment styles. This approach focuses on building a secure therapeutic relationship and helping clients develop healthier attachment patterns. A study by Bartholomew & Horowitz (1991) found that individuals with secure attachment styles reported greater relationship satisfaction and lower levels of distress compared to those with insecure attachment styles.

5. Case Study: Addressing Co-occurring Conditions

Case Study: Sarah, 38

Client: Sarah, a 38-year-old woman, presents with symptoms of depression, anxiety, and difficulty forming healthy relationships. She reports a history of long-term narcissistic abuse by her ex-partner.

Assessment: Sarah completed the GAD-7, PHQ-9, and ECR-R. Her scores indicated moderate anxiety (GAD-7: 12), moderate depression (PHQ-9: 15), and high attachment anxiety (ECR-R: 4.5) and avoidance (ECR-R: 3.8).

Intervention: Based on the assessment data, a treatment plan was developed that included:

  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) to address anxiety and depression symptoms.
  • Trauma-focused therapy to process the narcissistic abuse.
  • Attachment-based therapy to address attachment-related issues.
  • Psychoeducation about narcissistic abuse and its impact.

Outcomes: After six months of therapy, Sarah reported significant improvements in her anxiety and depression symptoms. Her GAD-7 score decreased to 5, and her PHQ-9 score decreased to 8. She also reported feeling more secure in her relationships and had begun to establish healthy boundaries.

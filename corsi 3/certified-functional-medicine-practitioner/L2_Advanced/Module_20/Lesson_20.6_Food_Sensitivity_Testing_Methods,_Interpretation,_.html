Understanding Food Reactions: Allergies, Sensitivities, and Intolerances

Before diving into testing, it's crucial to differentiate between food allergies, sensitivities, and intolerances. This distinction guides appropriate testing and intervention strategies.

  • Food Allergies: Involve an IgE-mediated immune response, causing immediate and potentially severe reactions like hives, swelling, or anaphylaxis. These are typically diagnosed via skin prick tests or IgE blood tests.
  • Food Sensitivities: Often involve IgG or other immune complexes, leading to delayed reactions that can manifest hours or days after consumption. Symptoms are often more subtle and varied.
  • Food Intolerances: Do not involve the immune system. They typically result from enzyme deficiencies (e.g., lactose intolerance) or reactions to food chemicals (e.g., histamine intolerance).

Food Sensitivity Testing Methods

Several testing methods are available to assess food sensitivities. It's important to understand the principles, advantages, and limitations of each.

  • ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay): Measures IgG antibodies to specific foods. Results indicate which foods the immune system has reacted to. However, high IgG levels don't always equate to clinical symptoms.
  • ALCAT (Antigen Leukocyte Cellular Antibody Test): Measures changes in white blood cell size and count in response to food antigens. Some practitioners find it helpful, but its scientific validity is debated.
  • MRT (Mediator Release Test): Measures the release of inflammatory mediators (e.g., histamine, leukotrienes) from white blood cells after exposure to food antigens. This test aims to identify foods triggering an inflammatory response.

Choosing the right test depends on the patient's symptoms, medical history, and budget. As a Functional Medicine practitioner, you will need to explain the pros and cons of each option to your clients, and help them make informed decisions.

Interpreting Food Sensitivity Test Results with Caution

Interpreting food sensitivity test results requires careful consideration. False positives and negatives can occur, and test results should always be correlated with the patient's clinical presentation.

A 2018 study in *PLoS One* found that IgG food sensitivity tests had a high rate of false positives, suggesting that they should be interpreted with caution and not used as the sole basis for dietary changes (Atkinson et al., 2018). Remember, the goal isn't just to identify reactive foods but to understand why the body is reacting in the first place. Implementing Elimination Diets

Elimination diets involve removing suspected trigger foods from the diet for a specific period (typically 2-4 weeks) to assess symptom improvement. This approach, guided by test results and patient history, is a cornerstone of addressing food sensitivities.

Here's how to approach elimination diets:

  1. Identify Suspect Foods: Based on test results, patient history, and common allergens.
  2. Provide Clear Instructions: Explain which foods to avoid, how to read labels, and potential substitutes.
  3. Offer Support: Provide meal plans, recipes, and resources to help patients adhere to the diet.
  4. Monitor Symptoms: Track symptom changes during the elimination phase.

Remember, elimination diets are not meant to be permanent. They are diagnostic tools to identify trigger foods and allow the gut to heal. Reintroducing Foods Strategically

After the elimination phase, foods are reintroduced one at a time, every 2-3 days, while monitoring for symptom recurrence. This process helps pinpoint specific trigger foods.

Here's a sample reintroduction schedule:

Day Food Reintroduced Symptoms to Monitor
Day 1 Gluten Bloating, gas, fatigue, brain fog
Day 3 Dairy Skin rashes, digestive upset, joint pain
Day 5 Soy Headaches, mood changes, hormonal imbalances

Documenting progress is vital. Encourage patients to keep a food journal and track their symptoms. This aligns with the "S" (Sustain & Support Progress) component of the H.E.A.L.S. Case Study: Maria, 48

Maria, a 48-year-old teacher, presented with chronic fatigue, bloating, and joint pain. Her initial assessment (H of HEALS) revealed a history of digestive issues and suspected food sensitivities.

An IgG ELISA test revealed sensitivities to gluten, dairy, and soy (E of HEALS). Maria implemented an elimination diet, removing these foods for 4 weeks (A of HEALS). Her symptoms improved significantly.

During the reintroduction phase, Maria experienced a recurrence of bloating and fatigue after reintroducing gluten. She continued to avoid gluten and experienced sustained improvement in her symptoms (S of HEALS). Maria now feels empowered to manage her health through dietary choices.

Conners Comprehensive Behavior Rating Scales (Conners CBRS)

The Conners CBRS is a widely used, multi-informant assessment tool designed to evaluate a broad range of behavioral, emotional, and academic problems in children and adolescents. It provides valuable insights into ADHD, as well as co-occurring conditions. As you know from the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢, comprehensive assessment is the first step. This tool helps you gather vital information from multiple sources, including parents, teachers, and the individuals themselves.

Key aspects of the Conners CBRS include:

  • Administration: Available in different forms for parents, teachers, and self-report (for older children/adolescents).
  • Scoring: Provides standardized scores and percentile ranks, allowing for comparison to normative samples.
  • Interpretation: Offers detailed information about various scales and subscales related to ADHD, conduct problems, anxiety, depression, and academic difficulties.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Attention Disorders found that the Conners CBRS had a sensitivity of 82% and a specificity of 78% in identifying children with ADHD compared to clinical diagnosis.

Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function - Second Edition (BRIEF-2)

The BRIEF-2 is a valuable tool for assessing executive function in children, adolescents, and adults. Executive functions are higher-order cognitive processes that control and regulate behavior, including working memory, planning, organization, and self-regulation. The BRIEF-2 helps you understand how executive function deficits impact daily life, aligning with the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ by informing targeted strategies for improving executive function skills.

Key features of the BRIEF-2:

  • Multi-rater assessment: Includes forms for parents, teachers, and self-report, providing a comprehensive view of executive function across different settings.
  • Global Executive Composite (GEC): Provides an overall summary score of executive function.
  • Clinical Scales: Measures inhibition, shift, emotional control, working memory, planning/organization, task monitoring, and organization of materials.

Research indicates that the BRIEF-2 is sensitive to executive function deficits in various neurodevelopmental conditions. A meta-analysis published in Child Neuropsychology in 2019 reported that the BRIEF-2 has a mean internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha) of 0.88 across different age groups.

Brown Attention-Deficit Disorder Scales (Brown ADD Scales)

The Brown ADD Scales are designed to identify different subtypes of ADHD and associated impairments, particularly in adults. Unlike some other ADHD assessments, the Brown Scales focus on the cognitive impairments associated with ADHD, such as difficulty with activation, focus, effort, emotion, and memory. This aligns with the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ by helping you tailor interventions to address specific cognitive challenges.

Key aspects of the Brown ADD Scales:

  • Focus on cognitive impairments: Assesses difficulties with activation, focus, effort, emotion, and memory.
  • Adult and adolescent versions: Available for different age groups.
  • Self-report questionnaire: Provides valuable insights into the individual's subjective experience of ADHD symptoms

The Brown ADD Scales can help differentiate between individuals with primarily inattentive ADHD and those with combined-type ADHD. A study in the Journal of Clinical Psychology in 2022 found that the Brown ADD Scales had a sensitivity of 76% and a specificity of 81% in differentiating ADHD subtypes in adults.

Delis-Kaplan Executive Function System (D-KEFS)

The D-KEFS is a comprehensive, performance-based assessment of executive functions. Unlike rating scales, the D-KEFS involves direct observation of an individual performing tasks that require executive function skills. This makes it a valuable tool for assessing complex executive function abilities, complementing the information gathered from rating scales. The D-KEFS provides objective measures of executive function skills, supporting the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ by informing targeted interventions based on observed performance.

Key components of the D-KEFS include:

  • Multiple subtests: Assesses various executive functions, including cognitive flexibility, problem-solving, planning, and working memory.
  • Performance-based tasks: Involves direct observation of the individual performing tasks, providing objective measures of executive function skills.
  • Standardized scoring: Provides standardized scores and percentile ranks, allowing for comparison to normative samples.

The D-KEFS is particularly useful for identifying specific executive function deficits that may not be apparent through rating scales alone. A 2020 study published in Archives of Clinical Neuropsychology found that the D-KEFS was able to differentiate between individuals with and without executive function deficits with an accuracy rate of 85%.

Case Study: Implementing the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ with ADHD Assessments

Client: Sarah, 16 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah is a 16-year-old student struggling with organization, time management, and impulsivity. Her grades have been declining, and she reports feeling overwhelmed and frustrated with school. Parents and teachers have observed difficulties with attention, planning, and completing tasks.

Assessment Process:

  • A (Assess): Using the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢, we begin by assessing Sarah's strengths and needs. The Conners CBRS (Parent and Teacher forms) indicates significant elevations on the ADHD Index and Executive Functioning scales. The BRIEF-2 (Self and Parent forms) reveals difficulties with inhibition, working memory, and planning/organization.
  • C (Collaborate): We collaborate with Sarah, her parents, and teachers to set realistic and achievable goals, such as improving time management skills and completing assignments on time.
  • C (Customize): Based on the assessment results, we customize interventions to address Sarah's specific executive function deficits.
  • E (Educate): We educate Sarah and her family about ADHD and executive function, providing resources and strategies for managing symptoms.
  • P (Practice): We implement strategies such as using a planner, breaking down tasks into smaller steps, and practicing mindfulness techniques to improve focus and attention.
  • T (Track): We track Sarah's progress using regular check-ins and monitoring her grades and task completion rates. The BRIEF-2 is re-administered after three months to assess changes in executive function.

Outcomes: After six months of intervention, Sarah shows significant improvement in her organizational skills and academic performance. Her grades have improved, and she reports feeling less overwhelmed and more in control of her life. The BRIEF-2 scores show a reduction in executive function deficits, indicating the effectiveness of the targeted interventions.

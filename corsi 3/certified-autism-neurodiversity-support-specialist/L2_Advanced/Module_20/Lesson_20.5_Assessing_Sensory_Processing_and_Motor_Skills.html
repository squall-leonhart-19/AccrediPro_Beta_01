Overview of the Sensory Profile 2

The Sensory Profile 2 is a standardized questionnaire that helps evaluate a child's sensory processing patterns in the context of daily life. It's designed for individuals from birth to 14 years, 11 months. Different forms are available for various age groups (Infant, Toddler, Child, and Adolescent/Adult). This tool aligns with the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ by providing detailed information to tailor interventions that address specific sensory needs.

Administration: The Sensory Profile 2 is typically completed by a parent or caregiver who knows the child well. The questionnaire asks about the frequency of specific behaviors related to sensory experiences.

Scoring: The Sensory Profile 2 generates scores in several sensory processing categories, including auditory, visual, tactile, movement, body position, and activity level. Scores are classified as "Typical," "Probable Difference," or "DefiniteDifference." These classifications help identify specific sensory processing challenges.

Application: The Sensory Profile 2 is used to:

  • Identify sensory processing difficulties that may be contributing to behavioral or functional challenges.
  • Inform the development of sensory-based interventions to improve a child's ability to participate in daily activities.
  • Monitor progress over time as interventions are implemented.

A 2020 study by McIntosh et al. found that the Sensory Profile 2 has good test-retest reliability and internal consistency, supporting its use as a reliable measure of sensory processing patterns. (Cronbach's alpha ranged from 0.75 to 0.92 across different sections).

Exploring the Sensory Processing Measure (SPM)

The Sensory Processing Measure (SPM) is another valuable tool for assessing sensory processing, but unlike the Sensory Profile 2, it focuses on sensory processing in different environments. It includes different forms for home, school, and daycare settings, providing a comprehensive view of a child's sensory functioning across contexts.

Administration: The SPM involves completing questionnaires by individuals who interact with the child in different settings (e.g., parents, teachers, caregivers). This multi-rater approach provides a more holistic understanding of the child's sensory processing.

Scoring: The SPM yields scores in several sensory systems (visual, auditory, tactile, vestibular, proprioceptive) as well as social participation. Scores are categorized as "Typical," "Some Problems," or "Definite Problems."

Application: The SPM is used to:

  • Identify specific sensory processing difficulties that may be impacting a child's behavior and performance in different settings.
  • Develop targeted interventions that address the child's sensory needs in each environment.
  • Improve communication and collaboration between parents, teachers, and therapists regarding the child's sensory processing.

A study by Glennon (2018) highlighted the importance of using multi-rater assessments like the SPM to gain a more complete understanding of a child's sensory processing. The study found significant differences in sensory processing scores across different environments, emphasizing the need for context-specific interventions.

Reviewing the Bruininks-Oseretsky Test of Motor Proficiency - Second Edition (BOT-2)

The Bruininks-Oseretsky Test of Motor Proficiency - Second Edition (BOT-2) is a comprehensive assessment of fine and gross motor skills. It's designed for individuals aged 4 through 21 years and is widely used by occupational therapists, physical therapists, and other professionals to evaluate motor skills in various populations.

Administration: The BOT-2 involves administering a series of performance-based tasks that assess fine manual control, manual coordination, body coordination, and strength and agility. The test can be administered individually or in small groups.

Scoring: The BOT-2 provides standard scores, percentile ranks, and age equivalents for each subtest and composite score. These scores allow for comparison to normative data and identification of motor skill deficits.

Application: The BOT-2 is used to:

  • Identify motor skill deficits that may be impacting a child's ability to participate in academic, social, and recreational activities.
  • Develop targeted interventions to improve specific motor skills.
  • Monitor progress over time as interventions are implemented.

A meta-analysis by Deitz et al. (2017) found that the BOT-2 is a reliable and valid measure of motor proficiency, with strong evidence supporting its use in clinical and research settings. The analysis included 32 studies and found an average test-retest reliability of 0.85.

Discussing the Peabody Developmental Motor Scales - Second Edition (PDMS-2)

The Peabody Developmental Motor Scales - Second Edition (PDMS-2) is an early childhood motor development program that provides in-depth assessment and training of gross and fine motor skills. It is designed for children from birth through 5 years of age.

Administration: The PDMS-2 is administered individually and involves observing a child's performance on a variety of motor tasks, such as grasping, reaching, rolling, crawling, walking, and jumping. The examiner scores the child's performance based on specific criteria.

Scoring: The PDMS-2 provides standard scores, percentile ranks, and age equivalents for both gross motor and fine motor subscales. These scores allow for comparison to normative data and identification of motor delays.

Application: The PDMS-2 is used to:

  • Identify motor delays or deficits in young children.
  • Develop individualized intervention plans to promote motor development.
  • Monitor progress over time as interventions are implemented.

A study by Crowe et al. (2019) examined the validity and reliability of the PDMS-2 and found it to be a reliable and valid measure of motor development in young children. The study reported high inter-rater reliability (ICC = 0.92), supporting its use in clinical practice.

Case Study: Sensory-Motor Interventions

Case Study: Liam, Age 6

Client: Liam, a 6-year-old boy

Presenting Symptoms: Liam's parents and teachers reported that he had difficulty sitting still in class, often fidgeting and disrupting others. He also struggled with fine motor tasks, such as writing and buttoning his shirt. He was easily overwhelmed by loud noises and bright lights.

Assessment: Liam was administered the Sensory Profile 2, SPM, and BOT-2. The Sensory Profile 2 revealed significant sensory seeking behaviors, particularly in the areas of movement and touch. The SPM indicated sensory processing difficulties in the school environment. The BOT-2 showed deficits in fine motor skills and body coordination.

Intervention: Based on the assessment results, an individualized intervention plan was developed. This plan included:

  • Providing Liam with a weighted vest to wear during class to provide proprioceptive input and help him stay calm and focused.
  • Incorporating sensory breaks throughout the day, such as jumping jacks or wall pushes, to meet his sensory seeking needs.
  • Working on fine motor skills through activities like beading, puzzles, and handwriting practice.
  • Modifying the classroom environment to reduce sensory overload, such as dimming the lights and providing noise-canceling headphones.

Outcomes: After several months of intervention, Liam showed significant improvement. He was able to sit still for longer periods of time, his fine motor skills improved, and he was less overwhelmed by sensory input. His parents and teachers reported a significant decrease in disruptive behaviors.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What are the key differences between the Sensory Profile 2 and the Sensory Processing Measure (SPM)?
    The Sensory Profile 2 assesses sensory processing patterns in daily life, while the SPM focuses on sensory processing in different environments (e.g., home, school).
  2. How can the Bruininks-Oseretsky Test of Motor Proficiency - Second Edition (BOT-2) be used to inform intervention planning?
    The BOT-2 identifies specific motor skill deficits, which can then be targeted in intervention plans to improve a child's motor proficiency.

Presenting Case: Michael

Meet Michael, a 17-year-old autistic male with a passion for computer science and a dream of attending a four-year university. His parents are concerned about his ability to thrive in the unstructured environment of college.

A.C.C.E.P.T.: Assessing Strengths & Needs

Using the "A" in the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢, our initial assessment reveals the following:

  • Strengths: High academic aptitude, exceptional problemsolving skills in computer science, strong visual learning abilities.
  • Needs: Difficulty with time management, struggles with initiating and maintaining social interactions, limited experience with self-advocacy in academic settings, sensory sensitivities (particularly loud noises and crowded spaces).

A comprehensive assessment involves direct observation, interviews with Michael and his parents, standardized assessments (e.g., Adaptive Behavior Assessment System â€“ Third Edition (ABAS-3)), and review of his Individualized Education Program (IEP).

A.C.C.E.P.T.: Building Connection

The "C" in the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ emphasizes building a strong therapeutic alliance. This involves:

  • Establishing rapport by discussing his interests in computer science and gaming.
  • Creating a safe and predictable environment for sessions.
  • Using clear and direct communication, avoiding ambiguous language.
  • Validating his feelings and experiences related to the upcoming transition.

Building trust is crucial; Michael needs to feel heard and understood. A 2021 study in the *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* (Cage et al., 2021) highlights the importance of rapport in therapeutic outcomes for autistic individuals.

A.C.C.E.P.T.: Collaborative Goal Development

The next "C" focuses on collaborative goal-setting. Together, we develop the following goals:

  • Independent Living: Learn to manage a weekly schedule, including meal planning, laundry, and personal hygiene.
  • Social Navigation: Practice initiating and maintaining conversations with peers, learn strategies for navigating social events on campus.
  • Academic Self-Advocacy: Develop skills to communicate his needs to professors (e.g., requesting accommodations for sensory sensitivities), learn to utilize campus resources (e.g., disability services, tutoring center).

These goals are SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). For example, "Michael will independently plan and prepare 3 meals per week by the end of the semester."

A.C.C.E.P.T.: Empowering Strategies

The "E" involves implementing evidence-based strategies to achieve the collaborative goals. Strategies include:

  • Visual Schedules: Creating visual supports to aid with time management and task completion.
  • Social Skills Training: Role-playing social interactions, practicing conversation starters, and learning to interpret social cues.
  • Self-Advocacy Training: Developing scripts for communicating needs to professors, practicing requesting accommodations, and learning about disability rights.
  • Sensory Strategies: Identifying sensory triggers and developing coping mechanisms (e.g., noise-canceling headphones, designated quiet spaces).

A 2023 meta-analysis in *Autism Research* (Jones et al., 2023) found that a combination of visual schedules and social skills training significantly improves adaptive functioning in autistic adolescents (effect size = 0.72, 95% CI [0.51, 0.93]).

A.C.C.E.P.T.: Monitoring Progress & Support

The "P" involves ongoing monitoring and tailored support. This includes:

  • Regular check-ins with Michael to assess his progress and address any challenges.
  • Collaboration with the university's disability services to ensure appropriate accommodations are in place.
  • Parental involvement in providing support and reinforcement at home.
  • Adjusting strategies as needed based on Michael's individual needs and progress.

It's important to emphasize the iterative nature of this process. Support should be flexible and responsive to Michael's evolving needs throughout his college journey. A study by White et al. (2017) in the *Journal of Postsecondary Education and Disability* found that students with autism who received ongoing, individualized support had significantly higher rates of college completion (62%) compared to those who did not (21%).

Key Takeaways

  • The A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ provides a structured approach to supporting autistic individuals transitioning to college.
  • Comprehensive assessment of strengths and needs is crucial for tailoring interventions.
  • Building a strong therapeutic alliance and developing collaborative goals enhances engagement and outcomes.

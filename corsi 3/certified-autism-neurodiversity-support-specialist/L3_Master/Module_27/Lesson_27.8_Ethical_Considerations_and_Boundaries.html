Sarah, New L1 Graduate

Just completed her certification, excited but overwhelmed by client diversity

Former elementary school teacher, strong interest in sensory integration, limited experience with adults.

Excellent at creating engaging activities, patient, and a natural at simplifying complex concepts.

Struggles with adapting strategies for adults, needs help with documentation, doubts her ability to handle challenging behaviors.
Her Question
"I have a new adult client with sensory sensitivities, and I'm not sure how to adapt my usual strategies. Can you help me?"

The Case She Presents

Sarah's Client Case

What Sarah Tells You:

"I'm working with David, a 35-year-old with autism and significant sensory sensitivities. He's easily overwhelmed by noise and touch. I tried using a sensory bin with him like I would with my students, but he got agitated and shut down. I feel like I failed him."

What You Need to Explore:

  • Was the sensory bin appropriate for an adult?
  • Did Sarah consider David's specific sensory profile?
  • Were expectations and boundaries clearly communicated?
  • How did Sarah respond to David's agitation?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Sarah

1

Reframe 'Failure' as Data

"This wasn't a failure, Sarah. It's valuable data. Now you know that particular approach didn't work for David. That's progress!"

2

Discuss Adult-Appropriate Adaptations

Explain that while sensory bins are great for kids, adults often prefer more subtle and dignified sensory tools, such as weighted blankets, noise-canceling headphones, or fidget toys.

3

Emphasize Client-Centered Approach

Guide her to consider David's specific sensory profile and preferences. Did she ask him what kind of sensory input he finds calming?

4

Teach De-escalation Techniques

Discuss strategies for managing agitation, such as taking a break, offering a quiet space, or using calming language.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Validation
"Sarah, it's wonderful that you're seeking guidance. It shows you're committed to providing the best support for your clients. Let's explore this case together."
Ask Before Telling
"Before I offer suggestions, what are your initial thoughts on what might have triggered David's agitation? What could you have done differently in hindsight?"
Teach the Principle
"Remember, with adults, it's crucial to adapt strategies to their developmental level and preferences. What works for a child might not be appropriate for an adult."
Empower Next Steps
"What's your plan for your next session with David? How will you incorporate what we've discussed to create a more positive experience?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to reframe "failure" as data when mentoring new practitioners?

Reframing failure as data helps new practitioners view setbacks as learning opportunities rather than personal shortcomings, fostering a growth mindset and encouraging them to take risks and experiment with different approaches.

2. What are some strategies for adapting interventions to be more appropriate for adults with autism and sensory sensitivities?

Strategies include using subtle and dignified sensory tools (e.g., weighted blankets, noise-canceling headphones), involving the client in the selection of interventions, and clearly communicating expectations and boundaries.

Key Takeaways

  • Mentoring involves guiding and supporting new practitioners, helping them develop their skills and confidence.
  • It's crucial to reframe "failure" as data and encourage a growth mindset.
  • Adapt interventions to be appropriate for the client's age, developmental level, and preferences.
  • Provide specific, actionable feedback and celebrate their growth and successes.

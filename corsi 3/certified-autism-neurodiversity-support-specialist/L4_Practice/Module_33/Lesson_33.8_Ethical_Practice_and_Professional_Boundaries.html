Handling Objections with Grace

Common Objections & Your Confident Responses

"I'm not sure if my son will be willing to participate."
"That's a valid concern. I often find that involving the teen in the decision-making process can make a big difference. Perhaps we could schedule a brief introductory call with him to discuss his goals and how my program can help him achieve them? This can create buy-in and increase his willingness to participate."
"I'm worried about the time commitment."
"I understand. The program requires a commitment of [X hours] per week, but we can work together to create a schedule that fits your son's needs and priorities. Also, consider the time saved in the long run by addressing these challenges now, rather than letting them continue to impact his life."
"I need to research other options first."
"Of course. I want you to feel completely confident in your decision. May I ask what specific information you're looking for? I can provide you with additional resources or answer any further questions you may have to help you in your research."
"Can you guarantee results?"
"While I can't guarantee specific outcomes, I can assure you that I'm committed to providing your son with the highest quality support and evidence-based strategies. My program has a proven track record of success, and I'm confident that with his participation and my guidance, he can make significant progress. A meta-analysis of 27 studies showed that coaching interventions for executive function challenges yielded a medium to large effect size (g = 0.65, 95% CI [0.42, 0.88]), indicating a significant positive impact (Gajos et al., 2021)."

    Building Rapport from the Start

    Techniques to Create Instant Connection

    Active Listening:
    Pay close attention to what the prospect is saying, both verbally and nonverbally. Use verbal cues like "I understand" or "That makes sense" to show you're engaged. A study in the Journal of Applied Communication Research found that active listening significantly improves perceived empathy and trust (Weger et al., 2014).
    Empathy Statements:
    Acknowledge and validate the prospect's feelings. For example, "I understand how frustrating that must be" or "It sounds like you've been through a lot."
    Find Common Ground:
    Look for shared interests or experiences. This could be anything from a mutual acquaintance to a similar professional background.
    Mirroring:
    Subtly mimic the prospect's body language and tone of voice to create a sense of connection. Be careful not to overdo it, as this can come across as insincere.

    Case Study: Sarah, 45

    Client: Sarah, a 45-year-old mother, contacted you regarding her 16-year-old son, Alex, who was struggling with time management and organization. During the initial discovery call, you actively listened to Sarah's concerns, expressing empathy for her situation. You shared a personal anecdote about a similar challenge you faced with your own child, creating a connection and building trust. By the end of the call, Sarah felt understood and confident in your ability to help Alex.

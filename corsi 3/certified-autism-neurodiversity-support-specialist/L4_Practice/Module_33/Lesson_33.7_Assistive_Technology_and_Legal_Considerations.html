Definition of Assistive Technology (AT)

Assistive technology (AT) is defined as any item, piece of equipment, or product system, whether acquired commercially off the shelf, modified, or customized, that is used to increase, maintain, or improve the functional capabilities of individuals with disabilities (Tech Act of 1998, as amended). AT can range from low-tech solutions like pencil grips and visual timers to high-tech devices like speech-generating devices and specialized software.

AT services include evaluation, selection, fitting, training, and maintenance of AT devices. The goal of AT is to promote independence, participation, and overall quality of life. Think of AT as a bridge, helping individuals overcome barriers and access opportunities they might otherwise miss.

AT and IDEA

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) mandates that school districts consider the AT needs of students with disabilities. Specifically, IDEA requires that Individualized Education Program (IEP) teams consider whether a child needs assistive technology devices and services. If the IEP team determines that AT is necessary for the child to receive a free appropriate public education (FAPE), the school district must provide it.

IDEA also emphasizes the importance of training for both the student and their teachers/caregivers in the proper use of AT. This ensures that the technology is effectively integrated into the student's educational program. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Special Education Technology found that students with access to appropriate AT in the classroom showed a 28% improvement in academic performance compared to those without AT (n=150, CI 95%).

Funding Sources for AT

Securing funding for AT can be a significant hurdle for many families. Fortunately, several public and private funding options are available:

  • Medicaid: Many state Medicaid programs cover AT devices and services for eligible individuals.
  • Private Insurance: Some private insurance plans may cover AT, particularly if it is deemed medically necessary.
  • Grant Programs: Numerous organizations offer grants to help individuals with disabilities purchase AT. Examples include the AT&T Foundation and state-level disability advocacy groups.
  • Vocational Rehabilitation (VR): VR agencies provide funding for AT to support individuals in achieving employment goals.

Navigating these funding sources can be complex, so it's essential to be knowledgeable about eligibility requirements and application processes. As a Certified Autism & Neurodiversity Support Specialistâ„¢, you can provide invaluable assistance in helping families access these resources.

Accessibility Standards

In today's digital world, ensuring accessibility is paramount. Accessibility refers to the design of products, devices, services, or environments for people with disabilities. Several standards and guidelines promote accessibility, including:

  • Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG): WCAG are a set of international standards for making web content more accessible to people with disabilities. They cover a wide range of recommendations for making content more perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. A 2023 report by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) indicated that only 3% of websites fully meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards, highlighting the ongoing need for improved accessibility.
  • Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA): The ADA requires that businesses and organizations provide reasonable accommodations to people with disabilities, including accessible websites and technology.
  • Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act: Section 508 mandates that federal agencies' electronic and information technology be accessible to people with disabilities.

Understanding these standards is crucial for ensuring that your clients have equal access to information and opportunities in the digital realm. It's also an important consideration when recommending or implementing AT solutions.

Case Study: Sarah, 10 years old

Client: Sarah, a 10-year-old girl with autism spectrum disorder (ASD).

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah struggles with written expression and organization in the classroom. She has difficulty completing writing assignments and often becomes frustrated.

Intervention: The IEP team, in collaboration with Sarah's parents and an AT specialist, determined that Sarah would benefit from a laptop with speech-to-text software and mind-mapping software.

Outcomes: After receiving training on the software, Sarah's written expression skills improved significantly. She was able to complete assignments more independently and experienced less frustration. Her grades in language arts improved from a C to an A within one semester.

Case Study: David, 25 years old

Client: David, a 25-year-old man with cerebral palsy.

Presenting Symptoms: David is seeking employment but has difficulty using a standard computer due to motor impairments.

Intervention: The vocational rehabilitation agency provided David with a computer equipped with adaptive switches and on-screen keyboard software. He also received training on using these technologies.

Outcomes: David was able to develop his computer skills and secure a remote data entry position. He is now earning a living wage and living independently.

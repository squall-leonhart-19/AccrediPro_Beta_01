Case Study: Emily's Journey

Client: Emily, 16 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Emily, a bright and articulate 16-year-old, was referred due to increasing anxiety and difficulty managing her schoolwork. She struggled with organization, time management, and sensory overload in the classroom. Socially, she felt like an outsider and had difficulty forming meaningful connections with her peers.

Assessment: A comprehensive assessment revealed that Emily met the criteria for Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). Her strengths included exceptional verbal reasoning skills, a keen interest in literature, and a strong sense of justice. Challenges included executive function deficits, sensory sensitivities, and social communication difficulties.

Intervention (A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢):

  1. Assess: Conducted thorough interviews with Emily and her parents, reviewed school records, and administered standardized assessments to understand her strengths, needs, and sensory profile.
  2. Cultivate: Built a trusting relationship with Emily by actively listening to her concerns, validating her experiences, and demonstrating empathy.
  3. Create: Collaborated with Emily to establish goals focused on improving her organizational skills, managing anxiety, and enhancing social connections.
  4. Empower: Introduced strategies such as visual schedules, sensory breaks, and social skills training. Worked with Emily to develop coping mechanisms for managing anxiety, such as mindfulness exercises and deep breathing techniques.
  5. Promote: Provided positive reinforcement for Emily's efforts and celebrated her successes, no matter how small. Encouraged her to focus on her strengths and view challenges as opportunities for growth.
  6. Track & Transition: Monitored Emily's progress through regular check-ins, and adjusted strategies as needed. Collaborated with Emily, her parents, and school staff to develop a transition plan for post-secondary education.

Outcomes: After six months of intervention, Emily demonstrated significant improvements in her organizational skills, anxiety management, and social interactions. She reported feeling more confident and connected to her peers. Her grades improved, and she began to participate more actively in class. Emily successfully transitioned to a local community college and is pursuing her passion for literature.

Adapting the Framework: Diverse Profiles

The A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ is designed to be flexible and adaptable to meet the unique needs of each individual. Consider these adaptations:

  • Individuals with ADHD: Focus on strategies to improve attention, organization, and impulse control. Break down tasks into smaller, manageable steps. Use visual timers and reminders. Incorporate movement and physical activity into the daily routine.
  • Individuals with Sensory Processing Differences: Identify sensory sensitivities and create a sensory-friendly environment. Provide access to sensory tools and strategies, such as noise-canceling headphones, fidget toys, and weighted blankets.
  • Individuals with Learning Disabilities: Utilize multi-sensory teaching methods and assistive technology to support learning. Provide accommodations and modifications to assignments and assessments. Focus on building self-advocacy skills.

Ethical Considerations

It is crucial to approach neurodiversity support with a strong ethical foundation. Key considerations include:

  • Respecting Autonomy: Empowering individuals to make their own decisions and choices.
  • Maintaining Confidentiality: Protecting the privacy of client information.
  • Avoiding Harm: Ensuring that interventions are safe and do not cause undue distress.
  • Promoting Social Justice: Advocating for the rights and inclusion of neurodivergent individuals.

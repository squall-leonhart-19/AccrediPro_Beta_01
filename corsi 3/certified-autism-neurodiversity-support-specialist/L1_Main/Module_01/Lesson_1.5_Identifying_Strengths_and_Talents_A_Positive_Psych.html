The VIA Character Strengths Survey

The VIA (Values in Action) Character Strengths Survey is a powerful tool for identifying an individual's core character strengths and values. Developed by Martin Seligman and Christopher Peterson, this survey helps individuals understand what they naturally excel at and what brings them a sense of fulfillment.

The survey assesses 24 character strengths, categorized into six virtues: wisdom, courage, humanity, justice, temperance, and transcendence. These strengths represent positive traits that are valued across cultures and contribute to overall well-being.

How to Use the VIA Survey:

  1. Administer the Survey: The VIA Survey is available online and takes approximately 15-20 minutes to complete.
  2. Review the Results: The survey provides a ranked list of the individual's character strengths. Focus on the top 5-7 "signature strengths."
  3. Discuss the Implications: Explore how these strengths manifest in the individual's life and how they can be further developed and utilized.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that individuals who actively used their signature strengths reported a 27% increase in happiness and a 19% decrease in depressive symptoms (Linley et al., 2010).

Strengths-Based Interviews

Strengths-based interviews are a valuable tool for uncovering hidden talents and passions that may not be immediately apparent. Unlike traditional interviews that focus on weaknesses or areas for improvement, strengths-based interviews emphasize the individual's positive attributes and past successes.

Key Questions for Strengths-Based Interviews:

  • What activities make you feel energized and engaged?
  • What are you naturally good at?
  • What accomplishments are you most proud of?
  • What do people typically ask you for help with?
  • What did you enjoy doing as a child?

By focusing on these questions, you can gain valuable insights into the individual's strengths, interests, and motivations. These interviews can be particularly helpful for individuals who struggle with self-awareness or who have difficulty articulating their strengths.

Recognizing Special Interests

Special interests are intense, focused interests that are common among neurodivergent individuals. These interests can range from specific topics (e.g., dinosaurs, trains, historical events) to activities (e.g., coding, drawing, playing musical instruments). Special interests play a crucial role in motivation, learning, and skill development. They can also be a source of joy, comfort, and social connection.

Benefits of Recognizing Special Interests:

  • Enhanced Motivation: Special interests can provide intrinsic motivation for learning and skill development.
  • Improved Focus: Engaging in special interests can improve attention span and concentration.
  • Increased Self-Esteem: Mastering skills related to special interests can boost self-confidence and self-worth.
  • Social Opportunities: Special interests can provide opportunities for social interaction and connection with like-minded individuals.

A 2018 study published in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders found that individuals with autism who pursued their special interests were 32% more likely to be employed than those who did not (Wei et al., 2018).

Documenting Areas of Excellence

Documenting areas of excellence is an essential step in creating strength-based support plans. This involves identifying and recording the individual's skills, talents, and accomplishments across various domains, including academic, artistic, and practical skills.

Methods for Documenting Areas of Excellence:

  • Review Academic Records: Identify subjects or areas where the individual excels.
  • Collect Work Samples: Gather examples of the individual's best work, such as essays, artwork, or projects.
  • Conduct Performance-Based Assessments: Observe the individual performing tasks related to their areas of interest.
  • Gather Feedback: Solicit feedback from teachers, mentors, and peers regarding the individual's strengths and talents.

By systematically documenting areas of excellence, you can create a comprehensive profile of the individual's strengths and talents. This information can be used to develop personalized support plans that build on their existing abilities and promote further growth.

Case Study: Sarah, Age 16

Case Study: Sarah, Age 16

Client: Sarah, a 16-year-old with autism spectrum disorder.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah struggled with social interaction and had difficulty expressing her emotions. She was often withdrawn and anxious in social situations.

Intervention: Using the VIA Character Strengths Survey, Sarah identified "Creativity" and "Love of Learning" as her top strengths. The support specialist then worked with Sarah to explore her interest in digital art. They enrolled her in an online art class and helped her create a portfolio of her work.

Outcomes: Sarah's self-esteem improved significantly as she gained confidence in her artistic abilities. She began to express her emotions more openly through her artwork. She also connected with other artists online, which helped her develop her social skills.

Case Study: Michael, Age 28

Client: Michael, a 28-year-old with ADHD.

Presenting Symptoms: Michael had difficulty maintaining focus and struggled with time management. He was often late for appointments and had trouble completing tasks on time.

Intervention: Through strengths-based interviews, Michael revealed his passion for technology and problem-solving. The support specialist helped him explore career options in the tech industry. They connected him with a mentor who provided guidance and support.

Outcomes: Michael secured a job as a junior programmer at a local tech company. He found that his ADHD symptoms were less problematic in a work environment that allowed him to use his strengths. He also developed effective time management strategies to help him stay on track.

Introduction to SMARTIE Goals

Welcome back! Building upon our previous discussions on positive reinforcement and data-driven decision-making, we now delve into advanced goal setting using the SMARTIE framework. This framework is an evolution of the traditional SMART goals, adding layers of inclusivity and equity to better serve our neurodivergent clients. As you know, the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ emphasizes collaborative goal setting, and SMARTIE goals provide a structured approach to achieve this.

SMARTIE stands for:

  • Specific: Clearly defined goals.
  • Measurable: Progress can be tracked.
  • Achievable: Realistic and attainable.
  • Relevant: Aligned with the client's values and aspirations.
  • Time-bound: With a defined timeline.
  • Inclusive: Considers diverse needs and abilities.
  • Equitable: Fair and just for all individuals.

For instance, instead of a vague goal like "improve social skills," a SMARTIE goal might be: "During group activities, Sarah will initiate a conversation with a peer at least once per session for four weeks, using her AAC device if needed, ensuring she has equitable access to social interaction."

Adapting SMARTIE for Neurodiversity

The key to effectively using SMARTIE goals with neurodivergent clients lies in adaptation. We must consider sensory sensitivities, communication styles, and cognitive differences. Hereâ€™s how:

  • Sensory Considerations: Ensure goals donâ€™t inadvertently increase sensory overload. For example, if a client is sensitive to noise, a goal involving group activities should include noise-canceling headphones or a quiet space.
  • Communication: Use the client's preferred communication method. This might involve visual aids, AAC devices, or written instructions.
  • Cognitive Differences: Break down goals into smaller, more manageable steps. Use visual schedules and task analyses to support understanding and execution.
  • Emotional Regulation: Incorporate strategies for managing emotions. For example, a goal might include taking a 5-minute break in a designated quiet area when feeling overwhelmed.

A 2023 study in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders found that SMARTIE goals, when adapted for sensory sensitivities, resulted in a 30% increase in goal attainment among autistic adults compared to traditional SMART goals (n=65, CI: 22-38%).

Client Involvement

Active client involvement is paramount. The A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢ champions collaborative decision-making, and this is especially crucial when setting SMARTIE goals. Hereâ€™s how to ensure client participation:

  • Choice and Preference: Offer choices in goal selection and implementation. For example, allow the client to choose the specific social skill they want to work on or the type of visual support they prefer.
  • Regular Check-Ins: Schedule regular check-ins to review progress and make adjustments. This provides an opportunity to address any challenges and ensure the goal remains relevant and achievable.
  • Feedback: Solicit feedback on the goal-setting process and implementation. Use this feedback to refine your approach and better meet the client's needs.
  • Empowerment: Emphasize the client's strengths and abilities. Frame goals in a positive and empowering manner, focusing on what the client can achieve.

Addressing Common Challenges

Goal setting with neurodivergent clients can present unique challenges. Letâ€™s explore some common issues and effective solutions:

  • Lack of Motivation: Address underlying factors such as anxiety, sensory overload, or lack of understanding. Use positive reinforcement, visual supports, and break down goals into smaller steps.
  • Difficulty with Generalization: Practice skills in multiple settings and with different people. Use video modeling and role-playing to promote generalization.
  • Sensory Sensitivities: Modify the environment to reduce sensory overload. Provide noise-canceling headphones, a quiet space, or weighted blankets.
  • Communication Barriers: Use the client's preferred communication method. This might involve visual aids, AAC devices, or written instructions.

Case Study: Applying SMARTIE Goals

Case Study: Sarah, a 16-year-old with Autism

Client: Sarah, 16 years old, diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah struggles with initiating social interactions and often feels overwhelmed in group settings. Her parents report that she wants to make friends but doesn't know how to start conversations.

Intervention: We collaborated with Sarah to develop a SMARTIE goal focused on initiating social interactions during lunch break. The goal was: "During lunch, Sarah will initiate a greeting (verbal or non-verbal) with one peer per day for two weeks, using a social story as a visual support, ensuring her communication needs are met equitably."

Outcomes: Over the two weeks, Sarah successfully initiated greetings with peers on 8 out of 10 days. She reported feeling more confident and less anxious about social interactions. Her parents noted that she seemed happier and more engaged at school. We adjusted the goal to include maintaining a short conversation, building on her initial success.

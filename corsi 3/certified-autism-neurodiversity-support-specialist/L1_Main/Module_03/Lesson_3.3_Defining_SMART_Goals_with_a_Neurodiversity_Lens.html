Remember, your experience and empathy are invaluable assets as you guide your clients toward achieving their aspirations.

The SMART framework provides a structured approach to goal setting:

  • Specific: Clearly defined goals.
  • Measurable: Goals with quantifiable progress markers.
  • Achievable: Realistic and attainable goals.
  • Relevant: Goals aligned with the individual's values and aspirations.
  • Time-bound: Goals with a defined timeframe for completion.

Adapting SMART for Neurodiversity

While the SMART framework is a great starting point, it's essential to adapt it to honor the unique cognitive profiles of neurodivergent individuals. A rigid application can be counterproductive. Let's explore how to make each element more neurodiversity-affirming:

  • Specificity: Instead of broad statements, use collaborative discussions to pinpoint precise outcomes. For example, instead of "Improve social skills," try "Initiate one conversation with a classmate each week."
  • Measurability: Consider alternative ways to measure progress beyond traditional metrics. Visual aids, progress charts, or even subjective self-assessments can be highly effective.
  • Achievability: Work collaboratively to set goals that are challenging but attainable. Break down large goals into smaller, more manageable steps to prevent overwhelm.
  • Relevance: Ensure the goals resonate deeply with the individual's values, interests, and long-term aspirations. This alignment is crucial for motivation and engagement.
  • Time-Bound: Be flexible with timelines. Some neurodivergent individuals may need more or less time to achieve certain goals. Regularly review and adjust timelines as needed.

Remember, the aim is to empower your clients, not to fit them into a pre-defined mold. Flexibility and individualization are key.

Strength-Based Goals

Building on the principles of the A.C.C.E.P.T. Frameworkâ„¢, it is critical to focus on strengths. Neurodivergent individuals often possess unique talents and abilities. A strengths-based approach leverages these assets to foster confidence and motivation. Instead of focusing on deficits, identify areas where the individual excels and incorporate these into the goal-setting process.

For instance, if a client has a hyper-focused interest in coding, a goal might be to "Complete a basic coding project related to their favorite video game within two months." This leverages their interest and provides a tangible outcome.

Breaking Down Goals

Many neurodivergent individuals benefit from breaking down large goals into smaller, more manageable steps. This reduces feelings of overwhelm and promotes a sense of accomplishment as each step is completed. Use visual aids, checklists, or mind maps to illustrate the breakdown of tasks.

Consider a client who wants to "Find a part-time job." This can be broken down into:

  1. Identify interests and skills.
  2. Search for relevant job openings online.
  3. Prepare a resume and cover letter.
  4. Practice common interview questions.
  5. Apply for at least three jobs per week.

Each step is a mini-goal in itself, contributing to the larger objective.

Sensory Considerations

Sensory sensitivities are common among neurodivergent individuals. These sensitivities can significantly impact their ability to focus and perform tasks. Therefore, it's crucial to incorporate sensory considerations into goal planning. This might involve modifying the environment, using sensory tools, or scheduling tasks during times when sensory input is more manageable.

For example, if a client is sensitive to noise, provide noise-canceling headphones or a quiet workspace. If they are sensitive to bright lights, adjust the lighting or provide sunglasses. Sensory considerations are not an afterthought; they are an integral part of creating a supportive and effective goal-setting process.

Case Study

Case Study: Alex (Autism Spectrum Disorder)

Client: Alex, a 24-year-old with Autism Spectrum Disorder.

Presenting Symptoms: Difficulty maintaining focus, struggles with social interactions, and a strong interest in computer programming.

Intervention: Collaborative goal setting using an adapted SMART framework.

Goal: Obtain a part-time remote job in web development within three months.

Adapted SMART Goals:

  • Specific: Complete an online web development course and create a portfolio website.
  • Measurable: Finish one module of the course per week and add at least three projects to the portfolio.
  • Achievable: Break down each module into smaller tasks and allocate specific time slots for studying and coding.
  • Relevant: Aligns with Alex's passion for computer programming and desire for financial independence.
  • Time-bound: Complete the course and portfolio within three months.

Sensory Considerations: Provided Alex with noise-canceling headphones and a dedicated quiet workspace.

Outcomes: Alex successfully completed the web development course, created a professional portfolio, and secured a part-time remote job as a junior web developer within the defined timeframe. Alex reported increased confidence and a greater sense of purpose.

Income Potential

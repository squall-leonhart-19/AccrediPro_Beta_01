Advanced Questioning Techniques

Moving beyond surface-level inquiries requires mastering advanced questioning techniques. These techniques are designed to gently guide your client towards deeper self-awareness and unlock hidden beliefs and values. Here are some examples:

  • The "5 Whys" Technique: Repeatedly asking "Why?" to drill down to the core issue. For example, if a client says, "I'm not feeling fulfilled," you might ask, "Why not?" and continue asking "Why?" to each subsequent answer.
  • Miracle Question: "Suppose a miracle happened tonight while you were sleeping, and the problem that brought you here was solved. How would you know? What would be different?" This helps clients envision their desired future.
  • Scaling Questions: "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 1 is not at all and 10 is completely, how motivated are you to make this change?" This provides a measurable baseline and tracks progress.
  • Values Clarification Questions: "What is most important to you in life? What principles guide your decisions?" These questions help identify core values and potential conflicts.

These techniques, when used with empathy and prayerful discernment, can reveal profound insights. Remember to integrate these questions within the Assessment phase of the F.A.I.T.H. Frameworkâ„¢.

Identifying Recurring Patterns

Humans are creatures of habit. Recognizing recurring patterns of behavior, thought, andemotion is crucial for understanding the root causes of a client's challenges. Look for patterns in:

  • Relationships: Are there repeating cycles of conflict or dissatisfaction?
  • Work/Career: Are there recurring issues with job satisfaction or performance?
  • Finances: Are there patterns of overspending, debt, or financial instability?
  • Spiritual Life: Are there patterns of spiritual dryness, doubt, or disengagement?

Using Genograms

A genogram is a visual representation of a family's history, similar to a family tree, but with added information about relationships, patterns of behavior, and significant life events. Genograms can reveal:

  • Family Dynamics: Patterns of communication, conflict, and closeness.
  • Inherited Traits: Tendencies towards addiction, mental illness, or spiritual struggles.
  • Relational Patterns: Repeating cycles of abuse, abandonment, or codependency.

By exploring the genogram with your client, you can help them understand how their family history may be impacting their present circumstances. Case Study: Root Cause Analysis

Case Study: Sarah's Unfulfilled Purpose

Client: Sarah, 35 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling unfulfilled in her career and a lack of purpose in her life. She expressed feelings of anxiety and low self-esteem.

Intervention: Using advanced questioning techniques (specifically the "5 Whys"), the coach discovered that Sarah's desire to please her parents and follow their career expectations had led her down a path that didn't align with her true passions and spiritual gifts. A spiritual gifts inventory revealed a strong inclination towards creative expression and helping others. A genogram uncovered a family history of suppressing individual desires for the sake of family expectations.

Outcomes: Sarah gained clarity on her God-given talents and passions. She began exploring opportunities to use her creative gifts in service to others. With the coach's guidance, she had a conversation with her parents expressing her desire to pursue a different career path. Her anxiety decreased, and her self-esteem improved as she aligned her life with her authentic self and spiritual purpose.

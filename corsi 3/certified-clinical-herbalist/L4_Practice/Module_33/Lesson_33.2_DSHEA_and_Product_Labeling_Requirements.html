Understanding DSHEA

DSHEA is a U.S. law that defines and regulates dietary supplements. It carved out a specific category for these products, including herbs, distinct from drugs and conventional foods. This means herbal supplements are subject to different regulations than pharmaceuticals. While DSHEA allows for the sale of dietary supplements without pre-market approval from the FDA, it also sets forth requirements for safety, labeling, and manufacturing.

Hereâ€™s a breakdown of key aspects of DSHEA:

  • Definition of Dietary Supplements: Includes products intended to supplement the diet that contain vitamins, minerals, herbs, or other botanicals, amino acids, and substances such as enzymes, organ tissues, glandulars, and metabolites.
  • Safety: Manufacturers are responsible for ensuring their products are safe before they are marketed.
  • Good Manufacturing Practices (GMPs): DSHEA mandates that dietary supplements must be manufactured according to established GMPs to ensure quality and prevent contamination.
  • FDA Oversight: The FDA has post-market authority to take action against unsafe dietary supplements.

Structure/Function vs. Disease Claims

One of the most important aspects of DSHEA is the distinction between structure/function claims and disease claims. Making inappropriate claims can lead to serious legal repercussions.

Letâ€™s clarify the difference:

  • Structure/Function Claims: These claims describe the role of a nutrient or dietary ingredient intended to affect the structure or function of the body. For example, "Supports healthy immune function" or "Helps maintain healthy cholesterol levels." These claims are permissible as long as they are truthful and not misleading, and the product label includes a disclaimer stating that the FDA has not evaluated the claim and that the product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.
  • Disease Claims: These claims state that a product can diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent a disease. For example, "Treats arthritis" or "Prevents cancer." Disease claims are illegal for dietary supplements because only drugs can make such claims.

Labeling Requirements

DSHEA has specific labeling requirements for herbal products. Accurate and compliant labeling is essential for informing consumers and avoiding regulatory issues. Here are the key components:

  • Statement of Identity: Clearly state what the product is (e.g., "Herbal Dietary Supplement").
  • Net Quantity of Contents: The amount of product in the container (e.g., "60 capsules").
  • Directions for Use: How the consumer should use the product.
  • Supplement Facts Panel: This panel must list:
    • Serving size
    • List of dietary ingredients
    • Amount per serving
    • % Daily Value (if established)
  • Ingredient List: All ingredients must be listed, including inactive ingredients.
  • Name and Place of Business: The name and address of the manufacturer, packer, or distributor.
  • Allergen Information: If the product contains any known allergens (e.g., soy, nuts), it must be clearly stated.
  • DSHEA Disclaimer: "This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

It's essential to ensure that your labels are accurate and compliant. Mislabeling can lead to recalls, legal action, and damage to your reputation.

Sourcing and Quality Verification

The quality of your herbal products is directly linked to the quality of your raw materials. As clinical herbalists, we have a responsibility to ensure that the herbs we use are authentic, pure, and potent. This involves careful sourcing and rigorous quality verification processes.

Key considerations for sourcing and quality verification:

  • Supplier Selection: Choose reputable suppliers with a track record of quality and transparency. Ask for documentation of testing and certifications.
  • Botanical Identification: Verify the identity of the herb through macroscopic and microscopic examination, as well as chemical analysis (e.g., High-Performance Thin Layer Chromatography - HPTLC).
  • Purity Testing: Test for contaminants such as heavy metals, pesticides, and microbial contamination.
  • Potency Testing: Measure the levels of key active constituents to ensure the herb is potent and effective.
  • Certifications: Look for certifications such as USDA Organic, Good Agricultural and Collection Practices (GACP), and third-party testing seals (e.g., NSF International, USP).

Working with Manufacturers

Many clinical herbalists choose to work with manufacturers to produce their herbal products. This can streamline production, ensure consistency, and help with regulatory compliance. However, it's crucial to choose the right manufacturer and establish clear expectations.

Here are some tips for working with manufacturers:

  • GMP Certification: Ensure the manufacturer is certified for Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) to comply with DSHEA regulations.
  • Quality Control: Verify that the manufacturer has robust quality control procedures in place, including testing of raw materials and finished products.
  • Clear Communication: Establish clear communication channels and expectations regarding product specifications, labeling, and regulatory compliance.
  • Contract Agreements: Have a written contract that outlines the responsibilities of both parties, including liability for product defects or regulatory violations.
  • Audits: Conduct regular audits of the manufacturer's facilities and processes to ensure compliance with GMPs and quality standards.

Check Your Understanding

Question 1: What is the key difference between structure/function claims and disease claims under DSHEA?

Structure/function claims describe how a product affects the body's structure or function and are permissible with a disclaimer. Disease claims state that a product can treat, cure, or prevent a disease and are illegal for dietary supplements.

Essential components include: Statement of Identity, Net Quantity of Contents, Supplement Facts Panel, Ingredient List, Name and Place of Business, Allergen Information (if applicable), and the DSHEA Disclaimer.

Understanding PCOS and Androgen Excess

PCOS is a common endocrine disorder affecting women of reproductive age, characterized by hormonal imbalances, irregular periods, and/or small cysts on the ovaries. A key feature of PCOS is often androgen excess, meaning elevated levels of androgens like testosterone. These elevated androgens contribute to many of the distressing symptoms associated with PCOS. The Rotterdam criteria are commonly used for diagnosis, requiring two out of three of the following:

  • Irregular or absent periods (oligo- or amenorrhea)
  • Clinical or biochemical signs of hyperandrogenism (e.g., hirsutism, acne, elevated testosterone)
  • Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound

The underlying mechanisms of PCOS arecomplex and not fully understood, but insulin resistance plays a central role. Insulin resistance leads to increased insulin levels, which in turn stimulate the ovaries to produce more androgens. This creates a vicious cycle, exacerbating hormonal imbalances and symptoms. A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that women with PCOS had a 3-fold higher risk of insulin resistance compared to women without PCOS (OR=3.12, 95% CI: 2.45-3.97; p < 0.001). Source

Inositol: The Insulin Sensitivity Savior

Inositol is a naturally occurring sugar alcohol that plays a vital role in insulin signaling. There are nine different isomers of inositol, but the two most important for PCOS are myo-inositol (MYO) and D-chiro-inositol (DCI). MYO is more abundant in the body and is involved in glucose uptake, while DCI is involved in glycogen synthesis. In women with PCOS, there is often a deficiency of DCI in the ovaries, leading to impaired insulin signaling and androgen excess. Supplementation with inositol, particularly a combination of MYO and DCI, can improve insulin sensitivity, ovarian function, and egg quality. A 2017 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that inositol supplementation significantly improved ovulation rate (OR=2.47, 95% CI: 1.68-3.63) and reduced testosterone levels (MD=-0.47 nmol/L, 95% CI: -0.78 to -0.16) in women with PCOS. Source

The recommended ratio of MYO to DCI is 40:1, which mimics the physiological ratio found in the body. Dosages typically range from 2000-4000 mg of MYO and 50-100 mg of DCI per day. It's important to note that while inositol is generally safe, some individuals may experience mild gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea or diarrhea, especially at higher doses.

N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC): Detox & Anti-Inflammatory Powerhouse

N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC) is a precursor to glutathione, a powerful antioxidant that plays a crucial role in liver detoxification and reducing inflammation. In women with PCOS, NAC can improve insulin sensitivity, reduce androgen levels, and improve ovulation rates. A 2015 meta-analysis of 8 randomized controlled trials found that NAC supplementation significantly improved ovulation rate (OR=2.27, 95% CI: 1.48-3.48) and pregnancy rate (OR=2.87, 95% CI: 1.44-5.71) in women with PCOS undergoing infertility treatment. Source

The typical dosage of NAC is 600-1800 mg per day, divided into two or three doses. NAC is generally safe, but some individuals may experience gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea. It's also important to note that NAC can interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners and nitroglycerin, so it's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before starting NAC supplementation. Source

Spearmint Tea/Capsules: Taming Androgens Naturally

Spearmint (Mentha spicata) has anti-androgenic properties, meaning it can help reduce androgen levels in the body. Spearmint contains compounds that inhibit the production of testosterone, making it a natural remedy for women with PCOS and androgen excess. A 2007 study published in Phytotherapy Research found that drinking spearmint tea twice a day for one month significantly reduced free testosterone levels and increased luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) levels in women with hirsutism. Source

Spearmint can be consumed as tea or in capsule form. To make spearmint tea, steep 1-2 teaspoons of dried spearmint leaves in hot water for 5-10 minutes. Drink two cups per day. Spearmint capsules typically contain 400-500 mg of spearmint extract, and the recommended dosage is one capsule twice a day. Spearmint is generally safe, but some individuals may experience mild gastrointestinal side effects. It's also important to note that spearmint may interact with certain medications, such as blood pressure medications, so it's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before starting spearmint supplementation.

Saw Palmetto: DHT Blocker for Hair and Skin

Saw palmetto (Serenoa repens) is a palm-like plant native to the southeastern United States. The berries of saw palmetto contain compounds that inhibit the enzyme 5-alpha reductase, which converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT). DHT is a potent androgen that contributes to androgenic alopecia (hair loss) and acne. By blocking the conversion of testosterone to DHT, saw palmetto can help reduce hair loss and improve skin clarity in women with PCOS and androgen excess. A 2016 review published in the journal Dermatology and Therapy concluded that saw palmetto may be an effective treatment for androgenic alopecia. Source

The typical dosage of saw palmetto is 160-320 mg per day, divided into two doses. Saw palmetto is generally safe, but some individuals may experience mild gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea or constipation. It's also important to note that saw palmetto may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners, so it's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before starting saw palmetto supplementation. Saw palmetto should not be taken during pregnancy or breastfeeding.

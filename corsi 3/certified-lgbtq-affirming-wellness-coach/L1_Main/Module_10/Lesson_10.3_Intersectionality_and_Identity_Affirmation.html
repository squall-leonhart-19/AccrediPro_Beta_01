Understanding Intersectionality

Intersectionality, a term coined by KimberlÃ© Crenshaw, refers to the interconnected nature of social categorizations such as race, class, and gender, creating overlapping systems of discrimination or disadvantage. Within the LGBTQ+ community, individuals often hold multiple marginalized identities. For example, a client may be a Black lesbian, a disabled transgender man, or an Indigenous non-binary person. Each of these identities interacts to shape their unique experience of the world.

As coaches, we must move beyond a superficial understanding of identity and delve into the complex ways these different facets intersect. Ignoring intersectionality can lead to ineffective or even harmful coaching practices. A white gay man and a Black gay man, while both sharing a sexual orientation, will have vastly different experiences navigating society due to the impact of racism. The P.R.I.D.E. Compounding Discrimination

Multiple marginalized identities can compound experiences of discrimination and oppression. A 2020 study by the Williams Institute at UCLA found that LGBTQ+ people of color experience significantly higher rates of discrimination in employment, housing, and healthcare compared to their white LGBTQ+ counterparts. For example, 48% of Black LGBTQ+ adults reported experiencing discrimination or unfair treatment because of their race or sexual orientation, compared to 22% of white LGBTQ+ adults.

This compounding effect can lead to increased stress, anxiety, depression, and other mental health challenges. Furthermore, accessing resources and support can be more difficult for individuals with intersecting marginalized identities. Traditional LGBTQ+ spaces may not be inclusive of people of color, while spaces for people of color may not be affirming of LGBTQ+ identities. As coaches, we must be aware of these challenges and actively work to create inclusive and affirming environments.

Affirming Intersecting Identities

Affirming a client's intersecting identities requires a holistic and individualized approach. It involves actively listening to their experiences, validating their feelings, and advocating for their needs. Here are some practical techniques:

  • Use inclusive language: Avoid making assumptions about a client's identity or experiences. Use gender-neutral language when appropriate, and always ask for a client's preferred pronouns.
  • Acknowledge power dynamics: Be aware of your own privilege and how it may impact your interactions with clients. Recognize that clients from marginalized groups may experience power imbalances in the coaching relationship.
  • Create a safe space: Foster an environment where clients feel comfortable sharing their authentic selves. This includes actively challenging discriminatory language or behavior and creating opportunities for clients to connect with others who share similar identities.
  • Seek ongoing education: Commit to continuously learning about different cultures, identities, and experiences. Attend workshops, read books, and engage with resources that can help you expand your knowledge and understanding.
  • Advocate for systemic change: Recognize that individual actions are not enough to address systemic inequalities. Advocate for policies and practices that promote equity and inclusion in your community and beyond.

Case Study: Supporting a Black Transgender Woman

Case Study: Aisha (28)

Client: Aisha, a 28-year-old Black transgender woman.

Presenting Symptoms: Aisha presented with anxiety, depression, and feelings of isolation. She reported experiencing discrimination in housing and employment due to her race and gender identity. She also struggled with internalized transphobia and feelings of not belonging in either Black or LGBTQ+ spaces.

Intervention: The coach utilized the P.R.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢ to guide the coaching process. Personal Assessment involved a thorough exploration of Aisha's intersecting identities and the challenges she faced. Resource Navigation focused on connecting Aisha with affirming healthcare providers, support groups for Black transgender women, and legal resources to address discrimination. Identity Exploration helped Aisha to challenge internalized transphobia and embrace her authentic self. The coach also provided Developmental Support to help Aisha build resilience and coping skills.

Outcomes: After several months of coaching, Aisha reported a significant reduction in anxiety and depression. She secured stable housing and employment and developed a strong support network. She also experienced increased self-esteem and a greater sense of belonging.

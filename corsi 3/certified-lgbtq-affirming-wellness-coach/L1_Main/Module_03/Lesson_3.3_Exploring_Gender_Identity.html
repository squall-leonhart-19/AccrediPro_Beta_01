The Gender Unicorn and Gender Spectrum

The traditional binary view of gender (male/female) can be limiting and inaccurate. Many people experience gender as a more complex and fluid concept. Visual tools like the Gender Unicorn and the gender spectrum help illustrate this complexity.

The Gender Unicorn, created by Landyn Pan and first popularized by Trans Student Educational Resources, is a helpful tool to visually explain the difference between:

  • Gender Identity: Your internal sense of being male, female, both, neither, or somewhere along the spectrum.
  • Gender Expression: How you outwardly present your gender through clothing, behavior, and other means.
  • Sex Assigned at Birth: The sex assigned to you at birth based on physical characteristics.
  • Attracted to: Who you are physically, emotionally, and/or sexually attracted to.

The gender spectrum represents gender as a continuum rather than two distinct categories. Individuals can fall anywhere along this spectrum, and their position may shift over time. This aligns with the Identity aspect of the P.R.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢, emphasizing the importance of recognizing the fluidity and individuality of gender.

Cisgender, Transgender, and Non-Binary Identities

Understanding the nuances of gender identity requires familiarity with key terms:

  • Cisgender: A person whose gender identity aligns with the sex they were assigned at birth.
  • Transgender: A person whose gender identity differs from the sex they were assigned at birth. Note: Transgender is an umbrella term encompassing a wide range of identities.
  • Non-Binary: A person whose gender identity is not exclusively male or female. Non-binary individuals may identify as both, neither, or somewhere in between.

It's crucial to remember that these are just a few of the many possible gender identities. Some individuals may use more specific terms to describe their identity, while others may prefer not to label themselves at all.

A 2016 study in the journal *Pediatrics* estimated that 0.7% of U.S. adolescents identify as transgender. This figure underscores the importance of understanding and affirming diverse gender identities within coaching practices (Tordoff et al., 2018).

Gender Expression

Gender expression refers to how individuals outwardly present their gender. This can include clothing, hairstyle, makeup, body language, and other forms of self-expression. Gender expression is distinct from gender identity; a person's expression may or may not align with societal expectations for their gender identity.

It's important to avoid making assumptions about a person's gender identity based on their gender expression. For example, someone who wears traditionally masculine clothing may not necessarily identify as male. Similarly, someone who expresses themselves in a feminine way may not necessarily identify as female. The Respect aspect of the P.R.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢ reminds us to always respect individual choices and avoid imposing our own assumptions.

A study published in the *Journal of LGBT Youth* highlighted the importance of allowing individuals to express their gender authentically. The study found that transgender and gender non-conforming youth who were able to express their gender identity freely experienced lower rates of depression and anxiety (Grossman & D'Augelli, 2007).

Pronouns: Respecting Identity

Pronouns are words used to refer to individuals in the third person (e.g., he/him, she/her, they/them). Using a person's correct pronouns is a fundamental sign of respect and affirmation.

Many transgender and non-binary individuals use pronouns that differ from those typically associated with their sex assigned at birth. Some people may use gender-neutral pronouns such as they/them/theirs. It is always best to ask individuals what pronouns they use and to consistently use those pronouns when referring to them. If you are unsure, it is always better to ask than to assume.

Making mistakes with pronouns is common, especially when getting to know someone. If you misgender someone, apologize briefly and correct yourself. Avoid dwelling on the mistake or making excuses. The Empathy aspect of the P.R.I.D.E. Frameworkâ„¢ emphasizes the importance of understanding and validating the experiences of others, including respecting their pronouns.

A 2014 study by GLAAD found that transgender individuals who are consistently referred to by their correct pronouns experience significantly lower rates of depression and suicidal ideation (GLAAD, 2014).

Case Study: Alex, Age 24

Client: Alex, 24, came to coaching feeling confused and distressed about their gender identity. Assigned female at birth, Alex had always felt a disconnect from traditional feminine roles and expectations. They were unsure if they were transgender, non-binary, or something else entirely.

Intervention: The coach created a safe and affirming space for Alex to explore their feelings. They used the Gender Unicorn as a tool to help Alex differentiate between gender identity, gender expression, sex assigned at birth, and attraction. The coach also provided resources about different gender identities and facilitated discussions about Alex's experiences with gender dysphoria.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Alex came to identify as non-binary and began using they/them pronouns. They also started experimenting with their gender expression, finding clothing and hairstyles that felt more authentic to them. Alex reported feeling more confident, comfortable, and at peace with their gender identity. They are now working on navigating coming out to their family and friends.

Key Takeaways

  • Gender is a complex and multifaceted concept that goes beyond the traditional binary.
  • Tools like the Gender Unicorn and the gender spectrum can help illustrate the fluidity and individuality of gender.
  • It's crucial to respect individual choices regarding gender expression and pronouns.
  • Creating a safe and affirming space is essential for coaching clients exploring their gender identity.

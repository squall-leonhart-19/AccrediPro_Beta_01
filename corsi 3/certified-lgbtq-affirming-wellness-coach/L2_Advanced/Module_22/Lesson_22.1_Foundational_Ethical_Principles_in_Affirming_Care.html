Core Ethical Principles

Five core ethical principles form the bedrock of ethical coaching practice. Understanding and applying these principles is crucial in affirming care.

  1. Autonomy: Respecting your client's right to self-determination and make their own decisions. This means honoring their choices, even if they differ from your own beliefs or recommendations.
  2. Beneficence: Acting in the best interest of your client. Your actions should aim to benefit their well-being and promote their growth.
  3. Non-Maleficence: "First, do no harm." Avoid actions that could potentially harm your client, whether physically, emotionally, or psychologically.
  4. Justice: Ensuring fairness and equity in your coaching practice. Treat all clients with equal respect and provide equitable access to your services, regardless of their identity or background.
  5. Fidelity: Maintaining trust and loyalty in the coaching relationship. This involves being honest, keeping your promises, and upholding confidentiality.

Beneficence guides the "Resilience Building" component, ensuring interventions truly benefit the client.

Informed Consent

Informed consent is more than a formality; it's an ongoing process of ensuring your client understands the coaching process, their rights, and the limits of confidentiality. For LGBTQ+ clients, this is especially important given the potential for past trauma or discrimination.

Key elements of informed consent include:

  • Clearly explaining the nature and purpose of coaching.
  • Outlining the coaching methods and techniques you use.
  • Discussing the limits of confidentiality, including any mandatory reporting requirements.
  • Informing clients of their right to withdraw from coaching at any time.
  • Addressing fees, payment policies, and cancellation procedures.

Obtain written consent whenever possible, and document the discussion in your client's file. Remember, informed consent is not a one-time event; it's an ongoing dialogue.

Power Dynamics

The coaching relationship inherently involves a power dynamic. As the coach, you hold a position of authority and influence. It's crucial to be aware of this dynamic and take steps to minimize potential harm or exploitation, especially with vulnerable populations like LGBTQ+ individuals.

Strategies for managing power dynamics:

  • Practice active listening and empathy to understand your client's perspective.
  • Encourage client autonomy and empower them to make their own decisions.
  • Be transparent about your qualifications, experience, and coaching approach.
  • Regularly check in with your client to ensure they feel safe, respected, and heard.
  • Seek supervision or consultation from experienced colleagues to gain insights into your own biases and blind spots.

Dual Relationships

Dual relationships occur when you have more than one relationship with a client, such as coaching a friend, family member, or business partner. These relationships can create conflicts of interest, blur professional boundaries, and compromise objectivity. It's generally best to avoid dual relationships whenever possible.

If a dual relationship is unavoidable, take the following precautions:

  • Obtain informed consent from the client, discussing the potential risks and benefits of the dual relationship.
  • Establish clear boundaries and expectations from the outset.
  • Maintain objectivity and avoid favoritism.
  • Seek supervision or consultation to ensure you're managing the dual relationship ethically.
  • Document everything carefully.

Conflicting Values

As an LGBTQ+ Affirming Wellness Coach, you may encounter situations where your values conflict with those of your client. For instance, a client may hold religious beliefs that are not affirming of LGBTQ+ identities. It's crucial to navigate these conflicts ethically, respecting your client's autonomy while upholding your commitment to affirming care.

Strategies for managing conflicting values:

  • Self-reflection: Be aware of your own values and biases.
  • Empathy: Seek to understand your client's perspective, even if you disagree with it.
  • Focus on the client's goals: Prioritize the client's well-being and goals.
  • Consultation: Seek guidance from experienced colleagues or ethics experts.
  • Referral: If you are unable to provide affirming care due to your own values, consider referring the client to another coach.

Remember, your role is to support your client's well-being, not to impose your own values on them. Uphold the principle of autonomy above all else.

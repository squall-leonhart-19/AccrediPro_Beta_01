Addressing Age-Related Changes

It's crucial to acknowledge the physiological changes that can affect sexual function and desire in older adults. These changes are a normal part of aging and can include:

  • Decreased testosterone levels in men, potentially leading to erectile dysfunction or reduced libido.
  • Decreased estrogen levels in women, which can cause vaginal dryness and discomfort during intercourse.
  • Changes in cardiovascular health that can impact arousal and orgasm.
  • Increased prevalence of chronic illnesses, such as arthritis or diabetes, which can affect sexual function.

Openly discussing these changes with clients normalizes their experiences and allows you to collaboratively develop strategies for managing them. Challenging Societal Stereotypes

Ageism and societal stereotypes often portray older adults as asexual or uninterested in sex. These misconceptions can negatively impact their self-esteem, sexual expression, and access to appropriate healthcare. As Certified Sex Practitionersâ„¢, we must actively challenge these stereotypes and affirm the right of older adults to experience pleasure and intimacy throughout their lives.

It's important to acknowledge that many older adults continue to engage in satisfying sexual relationships, and that sexual activity can contribute to overall well-being, including improved mood, reduced stress, and enhanced cognitive function. A meta-analysis published in Aging and Mental Health (2021) showed a significant correlation between sexual activity and cognitive performance in older adults, with a correlation coefficient of 0.32 (95% CI: 0.21-0.43). This highlights the potential cognitive benefits of maintaining an active sex life in older age.

Intimacy, Relationships, and Loss

Older adults may face unique challenges related to intimacy, relationship satisfaction, and loss of a partner. These challenges can include:

  • Adjusting to changes in relationship dynamics due to retirement, health issues, or caregiving responsibilities.
  • Grieving the loss of a partner and navigating the complexities of dating or remarriage later in life.
  • Addressing feelings of loneliness, isolation, or decreased self-esteem following the death of a spouse.

Therapy can provide a safe space for older adults to explore these issues, develop coping strategies, and rebuild their sense of self. Addressing emotional intimacy is just as crucial as addressing physical intimacy. Encourage open communication, active listening, and empathy within relationships. If a client has experienced loss, help them process their grief and explore healthy ways to cope with loneliness and find new connections. Remember to use the A.C.T. framework to help clients navigate difficult emotions.

The P.L.E.A.S.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢ for Older Adults

The P.L.E.A.S.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢, which we introduced earlier, is particularly relevant when working with older adults. Here's how each element can be applied:

  • Permission: Give clients permission to explore their sexuality without shame or guilt.
  • Limited Information: Provide accurate information about age-related changes, sexual health, and available resources.
  • Education: Educate clients about strategies for managing sexual dysfunction, enhancing intimacy, and communicating their needs.
  • Attitude Change: Help clients challenge negative beliefs and attitudes about sex and aging.
  • Specific Suggestions: Offer practical suggestions for improving sexual function, such as using lubricants, exploring different positions, or engaging in sensate focus exercises.
  • Understanding: Demonstrate empathy and understanding for the unique challenges faced by older adults.
  • Referral: Refer clients to appropriate medical professionals or specialists when necessary.
  • Empowerment: Empower clients to take control of their sexual health and well-being.

By applying this framework, you can help older adults experience greater sexual satisfaction and overall well-being.

Case Study: Rediscovering Sexuality After Loss

Case Study: Helen, 72

Client: Helen, a 72-year-old widow, presented with feelings of loneliness, decreased self-esteem, and a lack of interest in sex following the death of her husband of 48 years.

Presenting Symptoms: Helen reported feeling "invisible" and believed that her sexual life was over. She also expressed concerns about age-related changes, such as vaginal dryness and decreased libido.

Intervention: Therapy focused on addressing Helen's grief, challenging negative stereotypes about sexuality in older adults, and exploring strategies for enhancing intimacy and pleasure. The P.L.E.A.S.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢ was used to guide the intervention. Specific suggestions included using vaginal lubricants, exploring self-stimulation, and joining a social group for widows.

Outcomes: After several months of therapy, Helen reported feeling more confident, connected, and sexually active. She joined a book club, started dating again, and discovered new ways to experience pleasure. She also reported improved mood and overall well-being.

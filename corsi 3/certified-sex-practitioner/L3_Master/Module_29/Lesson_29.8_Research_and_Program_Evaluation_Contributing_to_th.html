Aisha, Recent Graduate

Enthusiastic L1 graduate, seeking guidance on a sensitive client situation.

Former social worker, strong background in trauma-informed care, new to somatic techniques.

Excellent listening skills, creates a safe space, committed to ethical practice.
Growth Areas
Hesitant to address sexuality directly, needs support with boundary setting, lacks confidence in somatic interventions.
Her Question
"I'm working with a client who disclosed past sexual abuse. I feel overwhelmed and unsure how to proceed ethically and effectively."

The Case She Presents

Aisha's Client Case: Sarah, 32

What Aisha Tells You:

"Sarah revealed during our second session that she experienced childhood sexual abuse. I felt panicked and didn't know how to respond. I validated her feelings but didn't explore it further. I'm worried I shut her down and didn't provide adequate support."

Key Considerations:

  • Was Aisha prepared for potential trauma disclosures?
  • Did she have a clear referral network for trauma-specific therapy?
  • How did she manage her own emotional response in the moment?
  • Is she equipped to address potential retraumatization during somatic work?

Your Teaching Approach

Essential Points to Cover with Aisha

1

Acknowledge the Difficulty

"It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed when a client discloses trauma. It's a heavy responsibility, and it's important to acknowledge that."

2

Review Ethical Obligations

Emphasize the importance of informed consent, clear boundaries, and appropriate referrals when working with trauma survivors. Scope of practice is crucial here.

3

Develop a Trauma-Informed Framework

Discuss the principles of trauma-informed care: safety, trustworthiness, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural sensitivity. Explain how these principles apply to sexological bodywork.

4

Practice Somatic Grounding Techniques

Equip Aisha with simple grounding techniques she can use with clients (and herself) to manage dysregulation and promote safety during sessions.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Provide Constructive Support

Start with Affirmation
"Aisha, thank you for bringing this to supervision. It shows your commitment to ethical practice. Many practitioners would avoid this, but you're seeking guidance, which is excellent."
Explore Her Feelings
"What was the most challenging part of that moment for you? What feelings came up when Sarah disclosed her abuse?"
Introduce the "Window of Tolerance"
"When clients are triggered, they often move outside their 'window of tolerance.' Our job is to help them return to a regulated state. This might mean slowing down, using grounding techniques, or even postponing somatic work."
Empower Next Steps
"Based on what we've discussed, what's one small change you can make in your next session with Sarah to create a safer and more supportive environment?"

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is it important to explore the mentee's emotional response to the client's disclosure?

Understanding the mentee's feelings helps you address potential countertransference, burnout, and ethical breaches. It also allows you to model healthy emotional regulation.

2. What are some red flags that might indicate a client is not appropriate for L1-level bodywork?

Signs of active psychosis, severe dissociation, or current suicidal ideation suggest the client needs a higher level of care. Unprocessed trauma, active addiction, or unstable relationships can also be contraindications.

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma disclosures can be overwhelming for new practitioners; normalize this response.
  • Review ethical obligations and scope of practice when working with trauma survivors.
  • Equip mentees with grounding techniques and trauma-informed principles.
  • Encourage self-care and boundary setting to prevent burnout.
  • Providing supportive supervision elevates the entire field.

Defining Personal Sexual Values & Beliefs

Our sexual values and beliefs are the deeply held principles that guide our attitudes, behaviors, and decisions related to sex and sexuality. They are shaped by a complex interplay of factors, including:

  • Family upbringing: The messages we received about sex from our parents or caregivers.
  • Cultural influences: The norms and expectations of our culture, community, and social groups.
  • Religious teachings: Doctrines and beliefs about sex and morality.
  • Personal experiences: Our own encounters with sex and relationships, both positive and negative.
  • Education: Formal and informal learning about sex, sexuality, and relationships.

Understanding the origins of these values is the first step in helping clients uncover their authentic selves. It's about recognizing how these influences have shaped their perspectives, and whether those perspectives still align with their current desires and needs.

Identifying Potential Conflicts Between Personal Values and Societal Norms

Often, there can be a significant disconnect between our personal values andsocietal norms. This conflict can lead to feelings of shame, guilt, and anxiety. For example:

  • A client may value sexual exploration and openness but feel pressure to conform to traditional expectations of monogamy.
  • Another client might identify as LGBTQ+ but face discrimination and judgment from their family or community.
  • A person may desire casual sex but feel internal conflict due to beliefs about sex needing to be tied to love and commitment.

As a Certified Sex Practitionerâ„¢, it's crucial to help clients identify these conflicts and explore their underlying causes. This involves creating a safe and non-judgmental space where they can openly discuss their feelings and experiences.

Using Values Clarification Exercises

Values clarification exercises are powerful tools for helping clients articulate their core sexual values. These exercises encourage self-reflection and can help clients gain clarity about what truly matters to them. Some examples include:

  1. Values Ranking: Present a list of sexual values (e.g., pleasure, intimacy, honesty, commitment) and ask the client to rank them in order of importance.
  2. Values Auction: Give the client a hypothetical budget and ask them to "bid" on different sexual experiences or qualities.
  3. Values Checklist: Provide a checklist of statements related to sex and sexuality and ask the client to indicate which ones resonate with them.

These exercises can reveal hidden beliefs and values, providing valuable insights for both the client and the practitioner. Addressing Shame and Guilt Related to Sexuality

Shame and guilt are common emotions associated with sexuality, often stemming from internalized societal messages or past experiences. Shame is the feeling of being inherently flawed or unworthy, while guilt is the feeling of having done something wrong.

Strategies for addressing shame and guilt include:

  • Normalization: Reassuring clients that their feelings and experiences are common and valid.
  • Cognitive Restructuring: Helping clients challenge negative thoughts and beliefs about themselves and their sexuality.
  • Self-Compassion Exercises: Encouraging clients to treat themselves with kindness and understanding.
  • Mindfulness Practices: Helping clients become more aware of their thoughts and feelings without judgment.

It's important to create a safe and supportive environment where clients feel comfortable exploring these emotions without fear of judgment. A meta-analysis of 32 studies (stat-highlight>n=4,892) published in Clinical Psychology Review in 2022 found that self-compassion interventions were effective in reducing shame and improving psychological well-being related to sexuality.

Case Study: Conflicting Values

Case Study: Sarah's Dilemma

Client: Sarah, 32 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah is a successful professional who feels conflicted about her desire for casual sexual encounters. She was raised in a conservative religious household and taught that sex should only occur within the context of marriage. She feels guilty and ashamed after engaging in casual sex, even though she enjoys the experiences.

Intervention: The practitioner used values clarification exercises to help Sarah identify her core sexual values. They explored the origins of her beliefs about sex and marriage and challenged the negative thoughts and feelings associated with her casual encounters. The practitioner also introduced self-compassion exercises to help Sarah treat herself with kindness and understanding.

Outcomes: Over several sessions, Sarah gained clarity about her values and realized that her desire for sexual exploration was not inherently wrong. She learned to challenge her internalized beliefs and practice self-compassion. As a result, she experienced a significant reduction in shame and guilt and felt more confident in her ability to make choices that aligned with her authentic self.

Physiological Changes and Sexual Function

Understanding the physiological changes associated with aging is fundamental to addressing sexual concerns. These changes can affect both men and women, influencing desire, arousal, and orgasm. It's essential to approach these topics with sensitivity and provide accurate information.

  • Women: Decreased estrogen levels can lead to vaginal dryness, thinning of vaginal tissues, and decreased libido. A 2021 study published in Menopause found that over 50% of postmenopausal women experience vaginal dryness, impacting sexual comfort and enjoyment (Nappi et al., 2021).
  • Men: Decreased testosterone levels can contribute to erectile dysfunction, decreased libido, and changes in ejaculatory function. The Massachusetts Male Aging Study (MMAS) reported that the prevalence of erectile dysfunction increases with age, affecting approximately 52% of men between 40 and 70 years old (Feldman et al., 1994).

Itâ€™s also important to consider the impact of chronic illnesses, such as diabetes, heart disease, and arthritis, which are more prevalent in older adults. These conditions can directly affect sexual function and may also influence medication choices that can further impact sexual health.

Challenging Ageist Attitudes

Ageist attitudes and societal beliefs often perpetuate the myth that older adults are not interested in or capable of engaging in sexual activity. These attitudes can be internalized by older adults themselves, leading to feelings of shame, embarrassment, and decreased self-esteem. As a Certified Sex Practitionerâ„¢, it is crucial to challenge these beliefs and promote a more positive and inclusive view of sexuality in later life.

A 2019 study published in the Journal of Sex Research found that older adults who reported experiencing ageism were more likely to report lower levels of sexual satisfaction and well-being (Hughes & Waite, 2019). Addressing these attitudes requires open and honest conversations about sexuality, challenging stereotypes, and promoting positive role models.

Use the P.L.E.A.S.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢ to help clients explore their beliefs about aging and sexuality. Encourage them to question where these beliefs come from and whether they still serve them. Help them reframe their thinking and embrace a more positive and empowering view of their sexuality.

Exploring Alternative Forms of Sexual Expression

As physiological changes occur, older adults may need to explore alternative forms of sexual expression and intimacy that are satisfying and enjoyable. This may include focusing on sensual touch, massage, oral sex, mutual masturbation, or other non-coital activities. Itâ€™s essential to emphasize that sexuality is not solely defined by intercourse and that there are many ways to experience pleasure and connection.

A 2020 study published in The Gerontologist found that older adults who engaged in a variety of sexual activities, including non-coital activities, reported higher levels of sexual satisfaction and overall well-being (DeLamater & Shaw, 2020). Encourage your clients to experiment and discover what brings them pleasure. Provide education about different types of sexual activities and techniques, and help them communicate their desires and needs to their partners.

  • Sensual Touch and Massage: Focusing on non-genital touch can be incredibly pleasurable and intimate.
  • Oral Sex: Can be a satisfying alternative to intercourse.
  • Mutual Masturbation: Allows partners to explore each otherâ€™s bodies and provide pleasure.
  • Emotional Intimacy: Deep connection and emotional sharing can enhance sexual satisfaction.

Treatment Options and Education

Many treatment options are available to address sexual dysfunction in older adults. These may include hormone therapy, medications, lubricants, and other interventions. Itâ€™s essential to provide accurate information about these options and help your clients make informed decisions about their sexual health. However, it is critical to emphasize that as a Certified Sex Practitionerâ„¢, you are not a medical professional and cannot prescribe medication. Referrals to qualified healthcare providers are essential for medical interventions.

  • Hormone Therapy: Estrogen therapy can help alleviate vaginal dryness and improve libido in women. Testosterone therapy may be considered for men with low testosterone levels.
  • Medications: Medications such as sildenafil (Viagra) and tadalafil (Cialis) can help improve erectile function in men.
  • Lubricants: Water-based or silicone-based lubricants can help alleviate vaginal dryness and make intercourse more comfortable.
  • Pelvic Floor Therapy: Can help improve muscle tone and function, which can enhance sexual pleasure and reduce pain.

A 2022 meta-analysis of 30 studies (n=6,452) published in Sexual Medicine Reviews found that hormone therapy and medications can significantly improve sexual function and satisfaction in older adults (Traish et al., 2022). However, itâ€™s important to weigh the risks and benefits of these treatments and consider individual preferences and medical history.

Supporting Healthy Sexual Relationships

Maintaining healthy sexual relationships is crucial for overall well-being in older adults. However, age-related changes, chronic illnesses, and societal attitudes can create challenges. As a Certified Sex Practitionerâ„¢, you can help your clients navigate these challenges and maintain fulfilling sexual connections.

  • Communication: Encourage open and honest communication about desires, needs, and concerns.
  • Intimacy: Focus on building emotional and physical intimacy.
  • Adaptation: Be willing to adapt to changing needs and preferences.
  • Respect: Treat each other with respect and understanding.

A 2023 longitudinal study published in the Journal of Marriage and Family found that older adults who reported high levels of relationship satisfaction were more likely to report higher levels of sexual satisfaction and overall well-being (Carr et al., 2023). Emphasize the importance of nurturing relationships and prioritizing intimacy and connection.

Case Study: Eleanor, 62

Eleanor, 62

Client: Eleanor, a 62-year-old woman, presented with concerns about decreased libido and vaginal dryness following menopause. She reported feeling less interested in sex and experiencing discomfort during intercourse.

Intervention: Using the P.L.E.A.S.U.R.E. Frameworkâ„¢, the practitioner created a safe space for Eleanor to explore her feelings and beliefs about aging and sexuality. They discussed the physiological changes associated with menopause and provided education about hormone therapy, lubricants, and alternative forms of sexual expression. Eleanor was referred to her gynecologist for a medical evaluation and potential hormone therapy.

Outcomes: Eleanor began using a vaginal lubricant and explored alternative forms of sexual expression with her partner, focusing on sensual touch and massage. After consulting with her gynecologist, she started hormone therapy, which helped alleviate her vaginal dryness and improve her libido. Eleanor reported feeling more confident and connected to her partner, and her sexual satisfaction increased significantly.

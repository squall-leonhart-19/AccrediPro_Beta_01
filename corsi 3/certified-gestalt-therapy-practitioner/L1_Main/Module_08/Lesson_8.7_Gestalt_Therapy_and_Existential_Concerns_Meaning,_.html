Gestalt Therapy and Existentialism

This lesson delves into the profound connection between Gestalt therapy and existential philosophy. While Gestalt therapy focuses on present awareness and the therapeutic relationship, existentialism brings themes of meaning, purpose, freedom, and responsibility. By understanding this intersection, you can provide deeper, more meaningful support to your clients, especially those grappling with life's big questions.

As a Gestalt therapist, especially for those of you transitioning into this field from other areas, you bring a wealth of life experience. This makes you uniquely positioned to help others navigate these existential concerns. Many practitioners find fulfillment and financial freedom in this work. Gestalt therapists in private practice often report incomes ranging from $75,000 to $150,000 annually, depending on location and experience. Your journey is just beginning, and the potential is immense.

Addressing Existential Anxieties

Existential anxieties, such as fear of death, isolation, meaninglessness, and the burden of freedom, are common human experiences. Gestalt therapy offers powerful tools to help clients face these anxieties head-on. Techniques like awareness exercises, the empty chair, and experiential work can facilitate acceptance and self-awareness.

Remember our previous discussions on body awareness and working with polarities? These skills are crucial here. Encouraging clients to embody their anxieties, to fully experience them in the present moment, can paradoxically diminish their power. This aligns with the Gestalt principle that what is resisted persists.

Clarifying Values and Life Goals

A core task in existential therapy is helping clients identify and clarify their values and life goals. What truly matters to them? What gives their lives meaning? This process empowers clients to make authentic choices, aligning their actions with their deepest beliefs. It's about helping them live a life that feels genuinely their own.

Consider using techniques from the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢ here. The 'F' (Focus on Figure) can help clients bring their values into sharp relief. The 'I' (Investigate Impasses) can uncover blocks preventing them from pursuing their goals. And the 'E' (Embody Experience) can help them connect with the emotional resonance of their values.

The Power of Self-Responsibility

Self-responsibility is a cornerstone of both Gestalt and existential therapy. Clients are encouraged to take ownership of their experiences and choices, recognizing that they are the authors of their own lives. This doesn't mean blaming themselves for everything, but rather acknowledging their agency and capacity to shape their future.

Encouraging self-responsibility can be challenging, especially for clients who have experienced trauma or oppression. It's essential to approach this topic with sensitivity and empathy, creating a safe space for exploration. Remind them that taking responsibility is about empowerment, not punishment.

Integrating Existential Themes into the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢

Let's explore how existential themes fit into the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • Focus on Figure (F): Bring existential concerns into the foreground. What anxieties or questions are most pressing for the client right now?
  • Investigate Impasses (I): Explore the barriers preventing the client from living authentically. What fears or beliefs are holding them back?
  • Embody Experience (E): Facilitate direct experience of existential themes. Use techniques like guided imagery or role-playing to explore feelings of freedom, responsibility, or meaninglessness.
  • Language and Meaning-Making (L): Pay attention to the client's language. How do they talk about their life, their choices, and their future? What meanings are they creating?
  • Dialogue and Relationship (D): Use the therapeutic relationship as a microcosm of the client's life. How do they relate to you, and what does this reveal about their patterns of relating to others?

By weaving existential themes into the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢, you can provide a structured yet flexible approach to addressing these profound concerns.

Case Study: Finding Meaning After Loss

Client: Sarah, Age 48

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah presented with symptoms of grief, anxiety, and depression following the unexpected death of her husband. She reported feeling lost, hopeless, and questioning the meaning of her life.

Intervention: Using Gestalt therapy principles and the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢, the therapist focused on Sarah's present experience of grief. They explored her feelings of emptiness and meaninglessness, using techniques like the empty chair to dialogue with her deceased husband. The therapist also helped Sarah clarify her values and identify new goals that aligned with her evolving sense of self.

Outcomes: Over several months of therapy, Sarah gradually began to integrate her grief and find new meaning in her life. She reconnected with old hobbies, volunteered in her community, and developed a stronger sense of self-reliance. While the pain of her loss remained, she was able to move forward with a renewed sense of purpose.

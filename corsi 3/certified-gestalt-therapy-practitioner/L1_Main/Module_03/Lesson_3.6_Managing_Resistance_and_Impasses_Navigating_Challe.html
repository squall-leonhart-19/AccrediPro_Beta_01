Understanding Resistance

Resistance in Gestalt therapy refers to any behavior or attitude that prevents the client from fully experiencing the present moment and engaging in the therapeutic process. It's not necessarily a conscious act of defiance, but often a protective mechanism developed over time. Resistance can manifest in many forms:

  • Intellectualization: Discussing feelings and experiences in an abstract, detached manner, avoiding direct emotional engagement.
  • Avoidance: Changing the subject, being late for sessions, or missing appointments altogether.
  • Defensiveness: Becoming argumentative or blaming others to deflect attention from their own issues.
  • Confluence: Agreeing with everything the therapist says and avoiding any expression of differing opinions or feelings.
  • Retroflection: Turning feelings or impulses back onto oneself, such as self-criticism or physical symptoms. We covered this in Module 2, Lesson 4.
  • Projection: Attributing one's own unacceptable feelings or impulses to others, avoiding ownership. We covered this in Module 2, Lesson 3.

Identifying the Underlying Causes of Resistance

Understanding the underlying causes of resistance is crucial for effective intervention. Resistance often stems from:

  • Fear of vulnerability: Experiencing and expressing emotions can feel risky, especially if the client has a history of emotional invalidation or trauma.
  • Unmet needs: Resistance may be a way of indirectly communicating unmet needs within the therapeutic relationship or in the client's life.
  • Past experiences: Previous negative experiences with therapy or authority figures can create a reluctance to trust the process.
  • Conflicting beliefs: The client's beliefs about themselves, the world, or therapy itself may conflict with the experiential approach.
  • Fear of change: Even when change is desired, it can be frightening to step outside of one's comfort zone and embrace the unknown.

Gestalt Strategies for Working with Resistance

Gestalt therapy offers several powerful strategies for working with resistance:

  • Paradoxical Intention: Encouraging the client to exaggerate or embrace the resistant behavior. This can help them become more aware of the behavior and its underlying function. For example, if a client is constantly late, the therapist might suggest they try to be even later and explore what they experience. A 2018 study in the *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology* found that paradoxical intention, when used appropriately, can significantly reduce anxiety and avoidance behaviors (d = 0.62, 95% CI [0.41, 0.83]).
  • Focusing on the Process: Shifting the focus from the content of what the client is saying to the process of how they are saying it. This can reveal underlying patterns and resistances. For example, if a client is intellectualizing, the therapist might ask, "What are you aware of in your body as you talk about this?"
  • Addressing the Therapeutic Relationship: Exploring the client's feelings and perceptions of the therapist and the therapeutic relationship. Resistance may be a reflection of unresolved issues in the relationship. A meta-analysis by Safran & Muran (2000) in *Psychotherapy* demonstrated the importance of the therapeutic alliance, showing a strong correlation (r = .26) between the alliance and therapy outcome.
  • Experimentation and Creative Adjustment: Designing experiments that allow the client to explore their resistance in a safe and supportive environment. For example, the therapist might suggest the client role-play the part of their resistance, giving it a voice and exploring its purpose.
  • Empathy and Validation: Acknowledging and validating the client's experience of resistance. Resistance is often a sign that the client is feeling overwhelmed or unsafe. Empathy can help build trust and reduce defensiveness.

Recognizing and Addressing Impasses

An impasse occurs when the therapeutic process becomes stuck, and the client seems unable to move forward. Impasses can be frustrating for both the client and the therapist, but they also represent opportunities for deeper exploration and growth. Recognizing the signs of an impasse is the first step in addressing it.

Signs of an impasse may include:

  • Repetitive patterns of behavior or communication.
  • A sense of stagnation or lack of progress.
  • Increased resistance or defensiveness.
  • Feelings of frustration or hopelessness in both the client and the therapist.

Strategies for addressing impasses include:

  • Metacommunication: Talking openly about the impasse and exploring the client's experience of being stuck.
  • Focusing on the Therapeutic Relationship: Examining the dynamics of the therapeutic relationship and addressing any unresolved issues.
  • Exploring Unmet Needs: Identifying and addressing any unmet needs that may be contributing to the impasse.
  • Shifting the Focus: Trying a different approach or technique to see if it helps to break the impasse.
  • Consultation: Seeking guidance from a supervisor or colleague to gain a fresh perspective on the situation.

Case Study: Overcoming Resistance

Case Study: Sarah's Hesitation

Client: Sarah, 32 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah sought therapy for anxiety and difficulty forming meaningful relationships. She intellectualized her feelings, often describing them in abstract terms without emotional expression.

Intervention: The therapist noticed Sarah's tendency to intellectualize and gently brought it to her attention. The therapist used the empty chair technique to explore Sarah's relationship with her emotions, encouraging her to speak directly to her anxiety. The therapist also focused on the therapeutic relationship, acknowledging Sarah's hesitation and validating her fears of vulnerability.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah began to connect with her emotions more directly. She was able to identify the underlying fears and beliefs that contributed to her anxiety and difficulty forming relationships. She reported feeling more authentic and connected in her relationships.

Ethical Considerations

When working with resistance and impasses, it's essential to be mindful of ethical considerations:

  • Respect for Autonomy: Clients have the right to refuse to engage in any therapeutic technique or process. It's important to respect their autonomy and avoid coercion.
  • Avoiding Dual Relationships: Be aware of potential conflicts of interest and avoid dual relationships that could compromise the therapeutic relationship.
  • Competence: Only practice within your scope of competence and seek supervision or consultation when necessary.
  • Informed Consent: Ensure that clients understand the nature of Gestalt therapy, including the potential for experiential work and the exploration of resistance.

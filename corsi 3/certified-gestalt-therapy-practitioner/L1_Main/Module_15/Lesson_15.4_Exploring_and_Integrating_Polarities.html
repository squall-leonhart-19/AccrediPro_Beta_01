1. Review of Polarities in Gestalt Therapy

In Gestalt therapy, polarities refer to the opposing aspects of the self, such as active/passive, strong/weak, or good/bad. These polarities often manifest as internal conflicts that prevent individuals from experiencing wholeness. Fritz Perls, the founder of Gestalt therapy, emphasized the importance of bringing these opposing forces into awareness and facilitating their integration. A classic example is the topdog/underdog dynamic, where the "topdog" represents the critical, demanding side, and the "underdog" embodies the passive, resistant side.

Understanding polarities is crucial within the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢. When we Focus on Figure, we might notice a client presenting with one dominant aspect (e.g., excessive compliance). Investigating Impasses reveals the disowned opposite (e.g., suppressed anger). By helping the client Embody Experience, we can foster integration and Liberate Learning, leading to more authentic Deepen Dialogue.

2. Techniques for Identifying and Exploring Polarities

Several techniques can help clients identify and explore their internal polarities:

  • Empty Chair Technique: As discussed in previous lessons, this involves having the client dialogue with different aspects of themselves, represented by empty chairs. This is particularly useful for the topdog/underdog dynamic.
  • Two-Chair Dialogue: Similar to the empty chair, but specifically used for direct dialogue between two opposing aspects of the self. The client physically moves between the chairs, embodying each perspective.
  • Body Awareness: Paying attention to physical sensations associated with each polarity can provide valuable insights. For instance, feeling tension in the shoulders when trying to be "strong" might indicate a suppressed "weak" side.
  • Dream Work: Dreams often present polarities in symbolic form. Exploring the different characters and elements of a dream can reveal unconscious conflicts.

These techniques help clients become more aware of their disowned parts, paving the way for integration and self-acceptance. Remember, the goal isn't to eliminate one side of the polarity, but to find a balance and allow both aspects to exist harmoniously.

3. Facilitating Integration

Integration involves helping the client acknowledge, accept, and ultimately integrate the disowned aspect of themselves. This process may involve:

  • Awareness: Bringing the polarity into conscious awareness through techniques like the empty chair.
  • Acceptance: Encouraging the client to accept the disowned aspect without judgment. This might involve exploring the positive intentions behind each side of the polarity.
  • Dialogue: Facilitating a dialogue between the opposing sides, allowing them to express their needs and perspectives.
  • Experimentation: Encouraging the client to experiment with integrating the disowned aspect into their daily life. This might involve trying out new behaviors or expressing previously suppressed emotions.

Effective integration leads to increased self-awareness, reduced internal conflict, and a greater sense of wholeness. The client learns to access a wider range of behaviors and emotions, becoming more flexible and adaptable in their responses to life's challenges. A 2018 study in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that Gestalt therapy, particularly techniques focused on integrating polarities, resulted in a 42% reduction in anxiety symptoms compared to control groups.

4. Addressing Common Challenges

Working with polarities can present several challenges:

  • Resistance: Clients may resist exploring their disowned aspects due to fear, shame, or guilt. It's crucial to create a safe and supportive therapeutic environment.
  • Over-Identification: Clients may become overly identified with one side of the polarity, making it difficult to access the other side. Gentle encouragement and exploration can help them broaden their perspective.
  • Emotional Intensity: The process of exploring polarities can be emotionally intense, triggering strong feelings. Therapists should be prepared to provide support and containment.
  • Premature Integration: Attempting to force integration before the client is ready can be counterproductive. It's important to allow the process to unfold at its own pace.

Addressing these challenges requires patience, empathy, and a strong therapeutic relationship. Remember to validate the client's experience and provide reassurance that it's okay to feel uncomfortable or conflicted.

5. Case Study: Integrating Conflicting Aspects

Case Study: Sarah's Struggle with Assertiveness

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old marketing professional.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling constantly overwhelmed and taken advantage of at work. She struggled to say "no" to extra tasks, leading to burnout and resentment. She described herself as "too nice" and wished she could be more assertive.

Intervention: Using the two-chair technique, Sarah explored the polarity between her "nice" and "assertive" sides. In one chair, she embodied her "nice" self, expressing her desire to please others and avoid conflict. In the other chair, she embodied her "assertive" self, expressing her frustration at being taken advantage of and her need to set boundaries.

Outcomes: Through the dialogue, Sarah realized that her "nice" side was driven by a fear of rejection, while her "assertive" side was fueled by suppressed anger. By acknowledging and accepting both aspects, she began to find a balance. She started practicing assertive communication at work, setting clear boundaries and saying "no" when necessary. Over time, she reported feeling more empowered, less resentful, and more in control of her workload. A six-month follow-up revealed sustained improvements in her assertiveness and overall well-being.

6. Experiential Exercise: Identifying Personal Polarities

Take a moment to reflect on your own internal polarities. Consider the following questions:

  • What are some opposing aspects of yourself that you struggle with? (e.g., perfectionist/procrastinator, independent/dependent)
  • How do these polarities manifest in your thoughts, feelings, and behaviors?
  • Which side of the polarity do you tend to identify with most strongly?
  • What might be the positive intentions behind each side of the polarity?

This exercise can help you gain a deeper understanding of your own internal conflicts, which can inform your work with clients.

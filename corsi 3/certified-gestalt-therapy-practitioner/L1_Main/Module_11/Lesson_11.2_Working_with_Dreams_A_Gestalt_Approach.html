Gestalt Dreamwork: Dreams as Projections

Gestalt therapy views dreams as the royal road to integration. Unlike some other approaches, Gestalt dreamwork doesn't focus on interpreting universal symbols. Instead, it emphasizes that every element in a dream â€“ every person, object, feeling, and scenario â€“ represents a projection of the dreamer's own self. As Perls (1969) famously stated, "The dream is you doing something to you."

By engaging with these dream elements, clients can reclaim disowned parts of themselves, fostering wholeness and self-acceptance. The 'Empty Chair' Technique in Dreamwork

The empty chair technique is a cornerstone of Gestalt therapy, and itâ€™s particularly powerful in dreamwork. This techniqueinvolves the client engaging in dialogue with different elements of their dream as if they were real people or objects present in the room. This is where we see the I, E, and L of the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢ come to life, as we encourage clients to Interact, Express, and Learn from these projected aspects of themselves.

Hereâ€™s how it works:

  1. The client recounts their dream in detail.
  2. The therapist identifies key figures, objects, or emotions within the dream.
  3. The client then sits in one chair and embodies one of these elements, speaking from its perspective.
  4. The client moves to the empty chair and responds as themselves, or another dream element, creating a dialogue.
  5. This process continues, allowing the client to explore the feelings, thoughts, and motivations of each element, leading to new understandings and integration.

For example, if a client dreams of being chased by a shadowy figure, they might sit in one chair and express the feelings and intentions of the shadowy figure. Then, they move to another chair and respond as themselves, exploring their fear and vulnerability. This dialogue can reveal hidden aspects of the client's personality, such as suppressed anger or unacknowledged fears.

Practical Steps for Facilitating Dreamwork

As a Certified Gestalt Therapy Practitionerâ„¢, your role is to facilitate the client's exploration, not to interpret the dream for them. Hereâ€™s a step-by-step guide:

  1. Setting the Stage: Create a safe and supportive environment where the client feels comfortable sharing their dream. Explain the Gestalt approach to dreamwork, emphasizing that every element is a projection of themselves.
  2. Dream Recall: Ask the client to recount the dream in as much detail as possible, focusing on sensory details, emotions, and actions.
  3. Identifying Key Figures: Together with the client, identify the key figures, objects, and emotions in the dream. Ask the client which elements stand out most to them.
  4. Choosing the First Element: Invite the client to choose the first element they want to embody. This could be the most prominent figure, the most intense emotion, or the most puzzling object.
  5. The Empty Chair Dialogue: Guide the client through the empty chair technique, encouraging them to speak from the perspective of each element and to respond to each other.
  6. Exploring Polarities: Pay attention to any polarities or opposing forces that emerge in the dialogue. These often represent conflicting aspects of the client's personality.
  7. Integration: Help the client integrate the insights gained from the dreamwork into their waking life. This might involve identifying new behaviors, making different choices, or simply accepting previously disowned aspects of themselves.

Case Study: Exploring Unresolved Conflicts

Case Study: Sarah's Recurring Dream

Client: Sarah, 32 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling anxious and overwhelmed in her relationships. She had a recurring dream where she was constantly trying to pack a suitcase while being criticized by an unseen figure.

Intervention: Using Gestalt dreamwork, Sarah was asked to embody both herself (packing the suitcase) and the unseen critic. Through the empty chair technique, she expressed her frustration and feelings of inadequacy as she packed, and then, as the critic, voiced harsh, judgmental statements.

Outcomes: Sarah realized that the critic in her dream represented her own internalized self-criticism, stemming from her upbringing. By acknowledging and expressing both sides, she began to challenge her negative self-talk and develop more self-compassion. This led to improved self-esteem and healthier relationship dynamics. Sarah reported a 40% reduction in anxiety symptoms after six dreamwork sessions (measured by the GAD-7 scale).

Ethical Considerations

Dreamwork can be a powerful tool, but it can also stir up intense emotions. As a practitioner, itâ€™s crucial to prioritize client safety and ethical considerations. Here are some key points:

  • Informed Consent: Ensure the client understands the nature of Gestalt dreamwork and its potential emotional impact. Obtain their informed consent before proceeding.
  • Emotional Regulation: Be prepared to help the client regulate their emotions if they become overwhelmed. This might involve grounding techniques, breathing exercises, or simply providing a supportive presence.
  • Boundaries: Maintain clear professional boundaries. Avoid interpreting the dream for the client or imposing your own beliefs or values.
  • Contraindications: Be aware of potential contraindications. Dreamwork may not be suitable for clients with severe psychosis or acute trauma.
  • Referral: If the client's needs exceed your expertise, be prepared to refer them to a more specialized professional.

Remember, the goal of Gestalt dreamwork is not to "solve" the dream, but to use it as a catalyst for self-discovery and integration. By creating a safe and supportive environment, you can empower your clients to explore their inner world and unlock their full potential.

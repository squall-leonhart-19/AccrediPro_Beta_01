The Phenomenological Approach

The phenomenological approach in Gestalt therapy is rooted in the idea that each individual's experience is unique and valid. Instead of trying to fit the client into a pre-existing diagnostic category, we focus on understanding their world as they perceive it. This involves suspending our own assumptions and biases to truly listen to and understand the client's subjective reality.

This approach aligns perfectly with the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢, as it allows us to enter the client's "Field" of experience with genuine curiosity and openness. By focusing on the "Focus on Figure" element of F.I.E.L.D., we're able to perceive the figure as it emerges from the client's background, rather than imposing our own preconceived notions.

Attending to the 'What' and 'How'

In Gestalt therapy, we pay close attention to both the 'what' (the content of the client's experience) and the 'how' (the process or manner in which they experience it). The 'what' refers to the specific details of the client's thoughts, feelings, and sensations. The 'how' refers to the way these experiences are organized and expressed.

For example, a client might say, "I feel anxious." The 'what' is the anxiety itself. The 'how' might be that they describe the anxiety as a tight knot in their stomach, accompanied by rapid breathing and a sense of impending doom. By attending to both the 'what' and 'how,' we gain a richer understanding of the client's experience and can tailor our interventions accordingly.

A 2018 study published in the Journal of Humanistic Psychology (n=67) found that therapists who explicitly attended to both the 'what' and 'how' of client experience reported significantly higher levels of therapeutic alliance and client-reported outcomes (d = 0.72, 95% CI [0.45, 0.99]).

Exploring Experience with Open-Ended Questions

Open-ended questions are a powerful tool for exploring the client's present experience and facilitating figure formation. These questions invite the client to elaborate and explore their thoughts and feelings without being constrained by a specific answer. Instead of asking "Are you feeling anxious?", we might ask "What are you experiencing right now?" or "How are you experiencing that feeling?"

Here are some examples of open-ended questions that can be used in Gestalt therapy:

  • "What are you aware of in this moment?"
  • "How are you experiencing that feeling in your body?"
  • "What is your attention drawn to right now?"
  • "What is it like for you to say that?"
  • "What do you want right now?"

Creating a Safe and Supportive Environment

A safe and supportive environment is essential for the client to explore their experience openly and honestly. This involves creating a space where the client feels accepted, understood, and free from judgment. As therapists, we can cultivate this environment by demonstrating empathy, active listening, and unconditional positive regard.

According to a meta-analysis of 52 studies (n=4,389) published in the Journal of Counseling Psychology in 2022, a strong therapeutic alliance, characterized by empathy and trust, accounted for approximately 17% of the variance in therapy outcomes (r = .41, 95% CI [.36, .46]).

Bracketing: Understanding the Client's Perspective

Bracketing is a technique used to suspend our own beliefs, assumptions, and biases in order to understand the client's experience from their perspective. This involves consciously setting aside our own judgments and interpretations so that we can truly listen to and understand the client's subjective reality. This is crucial for effective phenomenological inquiry.

Bracketing isn't about emptying our minds completely, but rather about being aware of our own perspectives and consciously setting them aside so that we can fully engage with the client's experience. It's an ongoing process that requires self-awareness and a commitment to understanding the client's world.

Case Study: Sarah's Journey

Sarah, 32, Anxiety and Career Uncertainty

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah, a 32-year-old marketing professional, presented with persistent anxiety and uncertainty about her career path. She described feeling "stuck" and overwhelmed by the pressure to make the "right" decision.

Intervention: Using a phenomenological approach, the therapist focused on exploring Sarah's present experience. The therapist asked open-ended questions such as, "What are you aware of in your body right now as you talk about feeling stuck?" and "How are you experiencing the pressure to make the 'right' decision?". The therapist also practiced bracketing, consciously setting aside their own assumptions about career success and focusing on Sarah's subjective experience.

Outcomes: Through this exploration, Sarah began to identify the underlying fears and beliefs that were contributing to her anxiety. She realized that she was afraid of failure and that she had internalized her parents' expectations for her career. By bringing these experiences into awareness, Sarah was able to challenge her limiting beliefs and explore new possibilities. Over time, Sarah reported a significant reduction in her anxiety and a greater sense of clarity about her career goals.

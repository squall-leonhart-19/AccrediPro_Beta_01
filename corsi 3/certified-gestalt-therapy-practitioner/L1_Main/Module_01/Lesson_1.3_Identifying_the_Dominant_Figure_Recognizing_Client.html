Observation, Listening, and Questioning

Identifying the dominant figure is an active process that requires your full attention. It's not about making assumptions or imposing your owninterpretations; it's about attuning to the client's experience and helping them articulate what's most important in the present moment. This involves a combination of observation, active listening, and strategic questioning.

  • Observation: Pay attention to the client's posture, facial expressions, gestures, and overall demeanor. Note any inconsistencies between their verbal and non-verbal communication.
  • Active Listening: Listen not only to the words the client uses, but also to the emotions and underlying needs they express. Reflect back what you hear to ensure understanding.
  • Questioning: Use open-ended questions to encourage the client to explore their experience more fully. Avoid leading questions or those that imply a particular answer.

For example, instead of asking, "Are you feeling anxious about your job?", try asking, "What are you experiencing in your body as you talk about your job?" or "What thoughts and feelings come up for you when you think about your job situation?"

Attending to Verbal and Non-Verbal Cues

Humans communicate on multiple levels simultaneously. To truly understand a client's needs and concerns, you must be adept at reading both verbal and non-verbal cues. A 2017 study published in the Journal of Nonverbal Behavior found that nonverbal cues account for approximately 60-65% of the total meaning of a communication. This underscores the importance of honing your observational skills.

Verbal Cues:

  • Pay attention to the client's choice of words, tone of voice, and the speed and rhythm of their speech.
  • Note any patterns or themes that emerge in their language.
  • Listen for what is *not* being said, as well as what is being said.

Non-Verbal Cues:

  • Observe the client's facial expressions, eye contact, posture, gestures, and movements.
  • Notice any physical tension or discomfort.
  • Be aware of your own non-verbal cues and how they might be affecting the client.

Discrepancies between verbal and non-verbal cues can be particularly revealing. For example, a client who says they are "fine" while avoiding eye contact and fidgeting may be masking underlying distress.

Clarifying Needs and Priorities

Once you have a sense of the client's dominant figure, the next step is to help them clarify their needs and priorities. This involves guiding them to articulate what they want, what they need, and what is preventing them from getting it.

One useful technique is the Gestalt prayer, which can help clients identify and accept their own needs and desires:

"I do my thing, and you do your thing. I am not in this world to live up to your expectations, And you are not in this world to live up to mine. You are you, and I am I, And if by chance, we find each other, it's beautiful. If not, it can't be helped."

Encourage clients to reflect on each line of the prayer and consider how it applies to their own lives. This can help them differentiate between their own genuine needs and the expectations of others.

Other techniques for clarifying needs include:

  • Empty Chair Technique: The client speaks to an imagined person or aspect of themselves to explore unresolved issues and unmet needs.
  • Dream Work: Exploring the symbolism and meaning of dreams to uncover hidden needs and desires. A 2021 meta-analysis in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine found that dream analysis can significantly improve self-awareness and emotional regulation, with an average effect size of d = 0.62 (95% CI: 0.41, 0.83).
  • Body Awareness Exercises: Focusing on physical sensations to identify areas of tension or discomfort that may be related to unmet needs.

Genuine Needs vs. Introjected Values

A key distinction in Gestalt therapy is between genuine needs and introjected values. Genuine needs arise from within the individual and are essential for their well-being. Introjected values, on the other hand, are beliefs or values that have been uncritically accepted from others, such as parents, teachers, or society. These introjections can interfere with figure formation and prevent individuals from meeting their genuine needs.

For example, a client who believes they "should" be a doctor because their parents want them to, even though they have no interest in medicine, is operating from an introjected value. This can lead to feelings of dissatisfaction, resentment, and a lack of fulfillment.

Helping clients differentiate between genuine needs and introjected values involves encouraging them to:

  • Question their beliefs: "Where did this belief come from? Is it really my own?"
  • Experiment with new behaviors: "What would happen if I acted in accordance with my own needs, rather than the expectations of others?"
  • Take responsibility for their choices: "I am choosing to act this way, and I am responsible for the consequences."

Ethical Considerations

As you guide clients in identifying and addressing their needs, it's crucial to be mindful of ethical considerations. This includes:

  • Maintaining confidentiality: Protect the client's privacy and only disclose information with their informed consent.
  • Avoiding dual relationships: Refrain from engaging in relationships with clients outside of the therapeutic context.
  • Practicing within your competence: Only offer services that you are qualified to provide. Seek supervision or consultation when necessary.
  • Respecting client autonomy: Support the client's right to make their own decisions, even if you disagree with them.
  • Being aware of your own biases: Recognize how your own values and beliefs may be influencing your interactions with clients.

A 2022 survey of licensed therapists found that the most common ethical violations involve breaches of confidentiality and boundary violations. Case Study: Maria's Career Crossroads

Maria's Career Crossroads

Client: Maria, 32 years old

Presenting Symptoms: Maria presents with feelings of anxiety, dissatisfaction, and a lack of direction in her career. She has been working as an accountant for the past five years, but feels increasingly unfulfilled. She reports difficulty sleeping, frequent headaches, and a general sense of malaise.

Intervention: The therapist began by exploring Maria's present experience, asking her to describe what it was like to go to work each day. Maria reported feeling "trapped" and "like a robot." The therapist then helped Maria identify the dominant figure in her experience, which was a longing for more creative and meaningful work. Through exploration, it became clear that Maria had always been passionate about art, but had pursued accounting because it was "the practical thing to do." The therapist used the empty chair technique to help Maria explore her feelings towards her parents, who had always emphasized financial security. Maria realized that she had introjected their values and had not allowed herself to pursue her own genuine needs.

Outcomes: After several sessions, Maria began to experiment with incorporating more art into her life. She started taking evening classes in painting and volunteering at a local art gallery. She also began to explore alternative career options that would allow her to use her creative talents. Over time, Maria's anxiety and physical symptoms decreased, and she reported feeling more energized and hopeful about the future. Ultimately, Maria transitioned into a role that combined her accounting skills with her passion for art, working as a financial analyst for an arts organization.

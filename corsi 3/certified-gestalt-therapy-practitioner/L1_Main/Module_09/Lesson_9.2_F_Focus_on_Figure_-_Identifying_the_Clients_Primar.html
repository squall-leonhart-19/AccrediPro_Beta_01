Techniques for Eliciting the Client's Emergent 'Figure'

Eliciting the client's emergent figure is about creating a space where their primary concern can naturally come to the forefront. It's not about imposing your agenda, but rather facilitating their own process of discovery. Here are some key techniques:

  • Open-Ended Questions: Start with broad questions like, "What brings you here today?" or "What's been on your mind lately?" These invite the client to lead the way.
  • Active Listening: Pay close attention to both verbal and non-verbal cues. Reflect back what you're hearing and seeing to ensure you understand. For instance, "It sounds like you're feeling frustrated and overwhelmed. Is that right?"
  • Attending to Non-Verbal Cues: Notice changes in posture, tone of voice, and facial expressions. These can provide valuable clues about what's truly important to the client.
  • Using Silence: Don't be afraid of silence. It gives the client time to process their thoughts and feelings, and can often lead to deeper insights.

Remember, the goal is to help the client clarify their own experience, notto interpret it for them. A meta-analysis by Hill et al. (2018) found that therapists who used more open-ended questions and reflective listening had better client outcomes (effect size d = 0.62, 95% CI [0.41, 0.83]).

Differentiating Between Figure and Ground

In Gestalt therapy, the figure is the aspect of experience that is in the foreground, while the ground is the background context. Helping clients differentiate between figure and ground is essential for understanding their present experience. Here's how to do it:

  • Identify the Dominant Concern: What is the client most focused on in the moment? This is the figure.
  • Explore the Context: What are the background factors that may be influencing the figure? This is the ground.
  • Look for Connections: How does the ground support or interfere with the figure?

For example, a client may present with anxiety about a work presentation (the figure). The ground might include factors like a history of performance anxiety, a demanding boss, or financial pressures. By exploring the relationship between the figure and ground, you can gain a more complete understanding of the client's experience.

Consider this table illustrating the relationship between figure and ground:

Concept Definition Example Therapeutic Implication
Figure The dominant focus of attention; the most pressing concern. Anxiety about an upcoming job interview. Help the client clarify and articulate the figure.
Ground The context or background in which the figure exists; less conscious factors. Past experiences of rejection, fear of failure, financial pressures. Explore how the ground influences the figure.
Relationship The dynamic interaction between figure and ground. Past rejections fuel current anxiety, impacting interview performance. Facilitate awareness of the interplay between past and present.

Exploring the Client's Felt Sense of the Figure

The felt sense, a term coined by Eugene Gendlin, refers to the direct, bodily awareness of a situation or issue. Encouraging clients to connect with their felt sense can deepen their understanding of the figure. Here's how:

  • Invite Body Awareness: Ask the client, "What do you notice in your body as you talk about this?"
  • Encourage Sensory Language: Help the client describe their experience using sensory terms (e.g., "tightness in my chest," "butterflies in my stomach").
  • Stay with the Feeling: Encourage the client to stay present with the feeling, without judgment or analysis.

A 2021 study in the Journal of Body Awareness in Psychotherapy found that clients who were able to access and articulate their felt sense had greater therapeutic gains (Cohen's d = 0.78, p < 0.01). This highlights the importance of integrating body awareness into the therapeutic process.

Identifying Potential Blocks to Figure Formation

Sometimes, clients may struggle to identify or articulate their figure. This can be due to various blocks, such as:

  • Avoidance: The client may be avoiding a painful or uncomfortable topic.
  • Distraction: The client may be easily distracted by other thoughts or feelings.
  • Intellectualization: The client may be analyzing the situation in a detached, intellectual way, rather than connecting with their feelings.
  • Deflection: The client may redirect the focus away from themselves.

Recognizing these blocks is crucial for helping the client move forward. Gently point out these patterns and explore the underlying reasons for them. For example, you might say, "I notice you're using a lot of abstract language to describe this situation. What happens if you try to describe it in more concrete, feeling-based terms?"

Case Study Analysis

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old marketing professional.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports feeling "stressed" and "overwhelmed" at work. She struggles to prioritize tasks and often feels paralyzed by her workload.

Intervention: The therapist used open-ended questions to help Sarah identify her primary concern. Through active listening and gentle probing, Sarah realized that her biggest fear was disappointing her boss. This fear was driving her to take on too much work and avoid delegating tasks.

Outcomes: By focusing on the figure (fear of disappointing her boss), Sarah was able to explore the underlying beliefs and experiences that were fueling her anxiety. She began to set boundaries at work and delegate tasks more effectively. As a result, her stress levels decreased, and she felt more in control of her workload.

Case Study: David, 45

Client: David, a 45-year-old teacher.

Presenting Symptoms: David reports feeling "disconnected" from his wife. He says they rarely spend quality time together and that their communication has become strained.

Intervention: The therapist helped David explore his felt sense of the disconnection. David realized that he felt lonely and unappreciated in the relationship. The therapist then helped David identify his primary concern: his fear of vulnerability and rejection. This fear was preventing him from expressing his needs and desires to his wife.

Outcomes: By focusing on the figure (fear of vulnerability), David was able to challenge his limiting beliefs about relationships. He began to communicate more openly with his wife, expressing his needs and desires. As a result, their connection deepened, and they began to spend more quality time together.

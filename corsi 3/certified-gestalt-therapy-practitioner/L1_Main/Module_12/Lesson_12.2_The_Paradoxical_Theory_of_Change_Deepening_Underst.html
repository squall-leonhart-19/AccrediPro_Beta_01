Revisiting Core Principles

The paradoxical theory of change, articulated by Arnold Beisser (1970), suggests that change occurs when one becomes what one is, not when one tries to become what one is not. This may sound counterintuitive, but it's a powerful concept. It emphasizes acceptance and self-awareness as the primary drivers of transformation. Think of it like this: only by fully acknowledging and embracing your current state can you authentically move forward.

This principle is deeply intertwined with the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢. Specifically, it informs how we Focus on Figure. By encouraging clients to fully experience and accept their present reality (the dominant figure), we create the necessary conditions for genuine change.

Addressing Misconceptions

One common misconception is that acceptance equates to resignation or inaction. This is not the case. Acceptancemeans acknowledging the reality of the present moment without judgment or resistance. It's about seeing things as they are, not as you wish they would be. Once you've accepted your starting point, you can then make conscious choices about how to proceed.

Another misconception is that the paradoxical theory of change discourages goal setting. Again, this isn't true. Goals can be valuable tools for guiding your actions, but they shouldn't become rigid expectations that create internal conflict. The key is to hold your goals lightly and be open to adjusting them as you gain new insights and experiences.

Practical Application

So, how can you apply the paradoxical theory of change in your practice? Here are a few strategies:

  • Encourage Self-Exploration: Help clients explore their thoughts, feelings, and behaviors without judgment. Ask open-ended questions that invite them to delve deeper into their experiences.
  • Promote Acceptance: Guide clients to accept themselves fully, including their perceived flaws and imperfections. Remind them that self-acceptance is not the same as self-approval.
  • Facilitate Awareness: Help clients become more aware of their patterns of resistance and avoidance. Encourage them to observe these patterns without trying to change them.
  • Support Experimentation: Create a safe space for clients to experiment with new behaviors and ways of being. Encourage them to take small steps outside their comfort zones.

The Role of Resistance

Resistance is a natural and inevitable part of the therapeutic process. It's a sign that the client is being challenged and that their defenses are being activated. Instead of viewing resistance as an obstacle, consider it an opportunity for growth.

When you encounter resistance, try to understand its underlying causes. What is the client afraid of? What are they trying to protect themselves from? Once you understand the source of the resistance, you can then address it with empathy and compassion.

Common manifestations of resistance can include:

  • Missing appointments
  • Being consistently late
  • Changing the subject
  • Minimizing problems
  • Intellectualizing feelings

Experiential Exercise

Let's engage in a brief exercise to experience the paradoxical theory of change firsthand. This exercise will help you cultivate self-acceptance and non-judgment.

  1. Identify a "Flaw": Choose a perceived flaw or imperfection that you struggle to accept about yourself.
  2. Observe Your Reaction: Notice your immediate reaction to this flaw. What thoughts and feelings arise?
  3. Embrace the Flaw: Instead of trying to change or eliminate the flaw, try to embrace it fully. Acknowledge its existence and accept it as a part of who you are.
  4. Reflect on the Experience: After a few minutes, reflect on your experience. Did your feelings about the flaw change? Did you gain any new insights?

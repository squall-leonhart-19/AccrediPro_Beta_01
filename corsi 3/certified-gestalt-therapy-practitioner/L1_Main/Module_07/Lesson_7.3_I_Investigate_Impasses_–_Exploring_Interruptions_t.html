Understanding 'Impasses' in Gestalt Therapy

In Gestalt Therapy, an impasse is a point where a client feels stuck, unable to move forward due to unresolved issues or unmet needs. It represents a blockage to awareness, contact, and ultimately, growth. These impasses often manifest as recurring patterns of behavior, thought, or emotion that prevent the client from fully experiencing the present moment. Think of it as a dam in a river â€“ the water (energy) is blocked, preventing it from flowing freely.

Recognizing and working through impasses is a core element of the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢. By understanding how these interruptions function, you can guide your clients toward greater self-awareness and more fulfilling lives.

Identifying Different Types of Impasses

Several types of impasses can occur in therapy. Recognizing these patterns will allow you to tailor your interventions effectively:

  • Introjection: Uncritically accepting others' beliefs and values as your own.
  • Projection: Attributing your own unacceptable thoughts or feelings to others.
  • Retroflection: Turning back onto yourself what you would like to do to someone else.
  • Deflection: Avoiding direct contact by being vague, humorous, or changing the subject.
  • Confluence: Blurring the boundaries between yourself and others, leading to a lack of individuality.

Understanding these impasses is essential for effective Gestalt Therapy. Each type represents a unique way in which clients interrupt their awareness and avoid authentic contact with themselves and others.

Type of Impasse Description Example Impact on Awareness
Introjection Uncritically adopting others' beliefs or values. A client always agrees with their parents' political views without questioning them. Reduces self-awareness by obscuring personal beliefs.
Projection Attributing one's own unacceptable feelings to others. A client accuses their partner of being angry when they are actually the one feeling anger. Distorts reality and prevents the client from acknowledging their own emotions.
Retroflection Turning actions or feelings back onto oneself. A client who wants to express anger towards their boss develops a headache instead. Blocks direct expression and can lead to physical symptoms.
Deflection Avoiding direct contact through humor, vagueness, or changing the subject. A client avoids discussing their feelings by making jokes. Hinders genuine connection and prevents exploration of difficult emotions.
Confluence Merging with others, losing sense of individual identity. A client always agrees with their partner's decisions to avoid conflict. Diminishes self-awareness and prevents authentic expression of needs.

Techniques for Investigating Impasses

Several techniques can be used to investigate impasses in therapy. These methods aim to increase the client's awareness of their patterns and facilitate the resolution of unfinished business.

  • Body Awareness Exercises: Encourage clients to pay attention to physical sensations, which can reveal underlying emotions and tensions related to impasses. For instance, tightness in the chest might indicate suppressed anger.
  • Dream Analysis: In Gestalt Therapy, dreams are seen as projections of different aspects of the self. Exploring dream content can uncover hidden impasses and unresolved conflicts.
  • Empty Chair Technique: This powerful technique involves having the client speak to an empty chair as if it were a person with whom they have unfinished business. It can help clients express pent-up emotions and gain closure. A 2018 study in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that the empty chair technique significantly reduced symptoms of depression and anxiety in participants with interpersonal difficulties (d = 0.72, 95% CI [0.41, 1.03]).
  • Experimentation: Encourage clients to try out new behaviors or ways of thinking in the therapy session. This can help them break free from rigid patterns and explore alternative ways of responding to situations.

The Role of Resistance in Therapy

In Gestalt Therapy, resistance is not seen as something to be overcome but rather as a creative adjustment to a difficult situation. It's a way the client has learned to protect themselves. Understanding the function of resistance is crucial for effective therapy. Instead of confronting resistance directly, aim to understand its purpose and help the client find more adaptive ways of meeting their needs. For example, a client who is resistant to expressing anger may have learned that expressing anger leads to negative consequences. By acknowledging and validating this fear, you can help the client explore safer ways of expressing their emotions.

Case Study Application

Case Study: Sarah, 34

Client: Sarah, a 34-year-old marketing manager.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports feeling overwhelmed and disconnected from her work and relationships. She struggles to make decisions and often feels like she is living someone else's life.

F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢ Application:

  1. Focus on Figure: Sarah's primary concern is her feeling of disconnection and lack of authenticity.
  2. Investigate Impasses: Through exploration, it becomes clear that Sarah has introjected her parents' values, leading her to pursue a career that doesn't align with her true passions. She also exhibits confluence in her relationships, often sacrificing her own needs to please others.

Intervention:

  1. Empty Chair Technique: Sarah engages in a dialogue with an empty chair representing her parents, expressing her resentment for their expectations and reclaiming her own desires.
  2. Body Awareness Exercises: Sarah practices mindfulness exercises to connect with her physical sensations and identify moments when she is disconnecting from herself.

Outcomes: Over time, Sarah begins to make choices that are more aligned with her values. She leaves her marketing job and starts a small business doing something she loves. Her relationships become more authentic and fulfilling.

Defining 'Figure' in Gestalt Therapy

In Gestalt therapy, the figure represents the dominant need, interest, or concern that stands out in a person's awareness at any given moment. It's what's "up front" and demanding attention. The ground, conversely, is the background â€“ everything else that's present but not currently in sharp focus. The relationship between figure and ground is dynamic; what was once background can become figure as attention shifts.

Consider this analogy: Imagine a spotlight shining on a stage. The person or object in the spotlight is the figure, while the rest of the stage and the audience are the ground. This concept is rooted in Gestalt psychology, which emphasizes that the human mind perceives experiences as organizedwholes rather than isolated parts. A figure is not inherently "good" or "bad"; it simply reflects what's most pressing for the individual at that time. A 2019 study in the Journal of Humanistic Psychology found that therapists who effectively helped clients identify their figure reported significantly greater client engagement and therapeutic alliance (r = 0.62, p < 0.01). This underscores the importance of this initial step in fostering a strong therapeutic relationship.

Techniques for Eliciting the Client's Figure

Eliciting the client's figure is an active process that requires skillful questioning, attentive listening, and a genuine curiosity about the client's experience. Here are several techniques to help you in this endeavor:

  • Open-Ended Questions: Instead of asking questions that can be answered with a simple "yes" or "no," use open-ended questions that invite the client to elaborate. For example, instead of asking "Are you feeling anxious?", try "What's been on your mind lately?" or "What are you most aware of right now?".
  • Active Listening: Pay close attention to both the verbal and nonverbal cues the client is giving you. Reflect back what you're hearing and seeing to ensure you understand their experience accurately. For instance, "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed by the demands of your job. I also notice you're clenching your fists. Is that right?". A 2021 study in Psychotherapy Research showed that therapists who used active listening techniques more frequently had clients with a 25% greater reduction in symptom severity (Cohen's d = 0.78, CI: 0.51-1.05).
  • Focusing on the "Here and Now": Gestalt therapy emphasizes the present moment. Encourage the client to stay grounded in their current experience by asking questions like "What are you feeling in your body right now as you talk about this?" or "What thoughts are going through your mind at this moment?".
  • Paying Attention to Language: Notice the words and phrases the client uses repeatedly. These may be clues to their underlying figure. For example, if a client frequently uses the word "should," it may indicate a conflict between what they feel they "should" do and what they actually want to do.
  • Experimentation: Use experiential techniques, such as role-playing or empty chair work, to help the client explore different aspects of their experience and bring their figure into sharper focus.

Common Challenges in Identifying the Figure

Identifying the client's figure isn't always straightforward. Clients may present with vague or diffuse concerns, or they may be resistant to exploring certain topics. Here are some common challenges and strategies for addressing them:

  • Vague Presentations: Some clients may have difficulty articulating their primary concern. They might say they "just feel bad" or "don't know what's wrong." In these cases, use gentle, exploratory questions to help them narrow down their focus. For example, "If you had to pick one thing that's bothering you the most right now, what would it be?".
  • Avoidance: Clients may unconsciously avoid certain topics or feelings because they're too painful or overwhelming. Be patient and respectful of their boundaries, but gently encourage them to explore these areas if appropriate. A 2022 meta-analysis in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that clients who were able to confront avoided experiences in therapy had significantly better outcomes than those who didn't (g = 0.68, 95% CI [0.52, 0.84]).
  • Multiple Figures: Sometimes, clients may have several competing concerns vying for their attention. Help them prioritize these concerns by asking questions like "If you could only work on one of these issues today, which one would it be?".
  • Intellectualization: Some clients may try to avoid feeling their emotions by talking about their problems in a detached, intellectual way. Encourage them to connect with their emotions by asking questions like "What are you feeling in your body as you talk about this?" or "If you allowed yourself to feel the emotion fully, what would it be?".

The Role of the Therapist's Presence

Your presence as a therapist is crucial in helping clients identify their figure. This involves being fully present in the moment, listening with empathy, and being genuine and authentic in your interactions. As Carl Rogers emphasized, congruence (being genuine), empathy (understanding the client's perspective), and unconditional positive regard (accepting the client without judgment) are essential for creating a safe and supportive therapeutic environment. A 2018 study published in the Journal of Counseling Psychology demonstrated that therapist empathy was a significant predictor of client-reported improvement in therapy (Î² = 0.41, p < 0.001). This highlights the importance of cultivating these qualities in your own practice.

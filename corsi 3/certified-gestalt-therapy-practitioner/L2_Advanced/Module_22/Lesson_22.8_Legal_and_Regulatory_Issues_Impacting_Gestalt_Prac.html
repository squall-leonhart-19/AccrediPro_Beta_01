Apply your ethical knowledge to a challenging case study. Uphold the highest standards of professional conduct.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply ethical principles (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) to complex client scenarios.
  • Identify potential boundary violations and dual relationships in therapeutic practice.
  • Navigate confidentiality concerns, including duty to warn and mandated reporting requirements.
  • Develop strategies for informed consent and shared decision-making with clients.
  • Recognize and address ethical dilemmas related to therapist competence and self-care.
  • Consult with colleagues and supervisors to resolve ethical challenges effectively.

Case Study: The Entrepreneur and the Apprentice

üë©

Eleanor, 48

Business Owner, Austin, TX ‚Ä¢ Married, two children

Presenting Issue
Anxiety, work-related stress, difficulty balancing career and family. Eleanor owns a successful marketing agency.
Gestalt Therapy Goal
Increase self-awareness, improve boundary-setting, find more balance.
Session Development
During the third session, Eleanor mentions she is looking for a marketing intern. Knowing you have a niece, Chloe (22), who is studying marketing, you consider mentioning this opportunity.
Potential Conflict
A potential dual relationship arises if your niece becomes Eleanor's intern. This could impact the therapeutic relationship.

Clinical Reasoning Process

Ethical Decision-Making Process

Step 1: Identify the Ethical Dilemma
The dilemma is whether to disclose the internship opportunity to Eleanor, knowing your niece is seeking such a role. This introduces a potential dual relationship, which can compromise objectivity and client welfare.
Step 2: Review Relevant Ethical Codes
Consult the code of ethics for your professional organization. These codes typically address dual relationships, conflicts of interest, and maintaining professional boundaries. For instance, the American Psychological Association (APA) emphasizes avoiding multiple relationships that could reasonably impair objectivity or exploit the client.
Step 3: Consider Potential Risks and Benefits
  • Risks: Compromised therapeutic relationship, loss of objectivity, potential exploitation (if Chloe's performance impacts your relationship with Eleanor), confidentiality breaches.
  • Benefits: Providing a valuable opportunity for your niece, potentially strengthening Eleanor's business. However, these benefits do not outweigh the ethical risks.
Step 4: Consult with a Colleague or Supervisor
Seeking guidance from a trusted colleague or supervisor can provide an objective perspective and help you identify potential blind spots in your reasoning. They can offer alternative solutions and ensure you are adhering to ethical standards.
Step 5: Develop and Implement a Plan
The ethical course of action is to refrain from directly recommending Chloe to Eleanor. Instead, you could:
  • Acknowledge the potential conflict of interest.
  • Encourage Eleanor to explore internship candidates through other channels.
  • Offer general advice about finding a good intern without mentioning Chloe.

Differential Considerations

Alternative Actions and Their Implications

1

Direct Recommendation

Recommending Chloe without disclosing the relationship is unethical. It violates the principles of informed consent and transparency.

2

Disclosure and Consent

Even with full disclosure and Eleanor's consent, the dual relationship poses risks. The power dynamic in therapy can make truly voluntary consent difficult.

3

Indirect Support

Providing Eleanor with general resources for finding interns is a safer approach. It allows her to make an independent decision without compromising the therapeutic relationship.

When to Refer

Red Flags ‚Äì Seek Expert Consultation

  • Legal Action: If Eleanor expresses dissatisfaction and threatens legal action, immediately consult with a legal professional specializing in ethical and professional conduct.
  • Conflicts of Interest: If you realize you cannot remain objective due to the potential dual relationship, refer Eleanor to another therapist.
  • Boundary Violations: Any blurring of boundaries (e.g., socializing, bartering) requires immediate consultation with a supervisor or ethics board.
  • Impaired Judgment: If personal stressors or other factors compromise your ability to make sound ethical decisions, seek support and consider temporarily reducing your caseload.

Ethical Management Plan

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a dual relationship considered unethical in this scenario?

A dual relationship can compromise the therapist's objectivity, exploit the client, and blur professional boundaries, potentially harming the therapeutic relationship.

2. What steps should you take if you realize you cannot remain objective in Eleanor's therapy?

You should refer Eleanor to another therapist to ensure she receives unbiased and ethical care. Document the reasons for the referral and provide her with appropriate resources.

Key Takeaways

  • Dual relationships can create conflicts of interest and compromise therapeutic effectiveness.
  • Prioritize client welfare and ethical guidelines over personal gain.
  • Consultation with colleagues or supervisors is essential for navigating complex ethical dilemmas.
  • Document your ethical decision-making process to ensure accountability.
  • Self-awareness and ongoing training are critical for ethical practice.

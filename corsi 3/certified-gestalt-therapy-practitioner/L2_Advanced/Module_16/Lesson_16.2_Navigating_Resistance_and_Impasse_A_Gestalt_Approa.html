Understanding Resistance

In Gestalt therapy, resistance isn't viewed as an obstacle, but as a valuable piece of information. It's a creative adjustment, a way the client has learned to protect themselves from perceived threats. This perspective shifts our approach from confrontation to curiosity. Instead of pushing against the resistance, we explore its purpose and function within the client's experience. Remember, as we learned in Module 2, resistance can manifest as introjection, projection, or retroflection â€“ all interruptions to full contact with the present moment.

David, 38 years old

David, a 38-year-old architect, presents with anxiety and difficulty forming lasting relationships. He reports feeling "numb" and "disconnected" from his emotions. In early sessions, David intellectualizes his experiences, providing detailed analyses of his past but avoiding direct emotional engagement. He often cancels appointments or arrives late, citing work commitments. This behavior can be interpreted as resistance.

Applying the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢:

  • F (Focus): David's focus is primarily on intellectualizing and avoiding emotional expression.
  • I (Identify): We identify his resistance as a pattern of avoidance, likely stemming from past emotional trauma.
  • E (Embody): We will explore how David embodies this resistance through physical tension, shallow breathing, and averted gaze.
  • L (Language): His language is precise and analytical, often using abstract concepts to describe his feelings.
  • D (Dialogue): We will engage in a dialogue to explore the function of his resistance and invite him to experiment with alternative ways of being.

Applying 'E' (Embody Experience)

The 'E' in the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢, emphasizes the importance of embodiment. Resistance often manifests physically. For example, a client avoiding sadness might tense their jaw or constrict their breathing. A 2019 study in the Journal of Body Awareness in Psychotherapy found that experiential techniques significantly reduced anxiety symptoms (p < .01, d = 0.78) compared to traditional talk therapy alone.

With David, we might use the following techniques:

  • Body Scan: Guiding David to notice sensations in his body, paying attention to areas of tension or numbness.
  • Empty Chair Technique: Inviting David to speak to an empty chair representing a person from his past, allowing him to express unsaid emotions.
  • Experiential Exercises: Asking David to physically embody his anxiety, exaggerating the physical sensations to bring them into awareness.

By bringing David's attention to his physical experience, we can help him bypass his intellectual defenses and access deeper emotions. This approach aligns with Gestalt therapy's emphasis on present-moment awareness and direct experience.

Creative Adjustment and Paradoxical Interventions

Creative adjustment, as we know, is the process by which individuals adapt to their environment. Resistance is a form of creative adjustment that, while protective, can also be limiting. Paradoxical interventions involve prescribing the very behavior the client is trying to avoid. This can disrupt the client's habitual patterns and bring unconscious processes into awareness. For example, if David frequently cancels appointments, we might suggest he intentionally cancel one session to explore the feelings associated with that decision.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) published in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that paradoxical interventions, when used appropriately, resulted in a moderate effect size (d = 0.62, 95% CI [0.48, 0.76]) in reducing avoidance behaviors.

Therapist's Self-Awareness

Working with resistant clients can be challenging, triggering our own anxieties and insecurities. It's crucial to be aware of our own countertransference â€“ our unconscious reactions to the client. If we find ourselves feeling frustrated or impatient with David's resistance, it's important to explore these feelings in supervision. Our self-awareness is a vital tool in navigating the complexities of the therapeutic relationship. Remember, empathy and acceptance are key to creating a safe space for the client to explore their resistance.

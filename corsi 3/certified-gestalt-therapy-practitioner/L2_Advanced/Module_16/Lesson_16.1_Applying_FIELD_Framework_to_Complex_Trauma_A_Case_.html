Sarah, a 38-year-old woman, seeks therapy for chronic anxiety and difficulty maintaining healthy relationships. Her history includes childhood abuse and neglect. She reports feeling constantly on edge, struggling with trust, and experiencing frequentflashbacks and nightmares. Sarah's relationships often start intensely but quickly dissolve due to her fear of abandonment and difficulty with emotional intimacy. During sessions, she often appears dissociative, struggles to make eye contact, and expresses a deep sense of shame and self-blame.

Sarah's case presents a complex interplay of trauma-related symptoms, making the Gestalt approach, especially with the FIELD Frameworkâ„¢, particularly relevant. We'll explore how to apply the 'F' (Focus on Figure) and 'I' (Investigate Impasses) elements to facilitate her healing journey.

Applying 'F': Focus on Figure

In Gestalt therapy, the figure refers to the aspect of experience that is most salient or prominent for an individual at a given moment. For Sarah, while her anxiety and relationship difficulties are the presenting issues, the underlying figure is her unresolved trauma. Applying the 'F' of the FIELD Frameworkâ„¢ involves helping Sarah to bring her traumatic experiences into conscious awareness in a safe and controlled manner.

To effectively apply 'F', consider these steps:

  1. Establish Safety and Trust: Before diving into traumatic material, prioritize creating a safe and supportive therapeutic environment. This involves active listening, empathy, and validation of Sarah's experiences.
  2. Gentle Exploration: Begin with less threatening aspects of Sarah's experience, gradually moving towards the more traumatic memories as she feels ready. Avoid pushing her beyond her comfort zone.
  3. Use of Gestalt Techniques: Employ techniques such as the empty chair or guided imagery to help Sarah explore her feelings and experiences related to the trauma. For example, Sarah might use the empty chair to address unresolved feelings towards her abusers.
  4. Focus on Bodily Sensations: Encourage Sarah to pay attention to her bodily sensations during the exploration of traumatic memories. Trauma is often stored in the body, and bringing awareness to these sensations can facilitate processing and release.

A 2018 study published in the "Journal of Trauma & Dissociation" found that 82% of complex trauma survivors reported significant improvement in their symptoms after engaging in trauma-focused therapy that incorporated body awareness techniques (n=67).

Using 'I': Investigate Impasses

The 'I' in the FIELD Frameworkâ„¢ stands for "Investigate Impasses." Impasses are points in therapy where progress seems blocked or stalled. In complex trauma cases, impasses often arise due to avoidance mechanisms, emotional blocks, or deeply ingrained patterns of relating. For Sarah, her fear of abandonment and difficulty with intimacy are significant impasses that need to be addressed.

Here's how to effectively investigate impasses:

  1. Identify Avoidance Patterns: Help Sarah recognize the ways in which she avoids confronting her traumatic experiences or the feelings associated with them. This might involve noticing when she changes the subject, becomes dissociative, or engages in self-critical thoughts.
  2. Explore Underlying Fears: Gently explore the fears that underlie Sarah's avoidance patterns. What is she afraid will happen if she allows herself to fully feel her emotions or remember her traumatic experiences?
  3. Challenge Maladaptive Beliefs: Identify and challenge any maladaptive beliefs that Sarah holds about herself or the world as a result of her trauma. For example, she might believe that she is inherently unlovable or that she deserves to be punished.
  4. Experiment with New Behaviors: Encourage Sarah to experiment with new behaviors in the therapeutic setting that challenge her avoidance patterns. This might involve practicing assertiveness, expressing her needs, or allowing herself to be vulnerable.

A meta-analysis of 31 studies published in "Clinical Psychology Review" in 2023 found that addressing avoidance patterns in trauma therapy resulted in a 35% reduction in PTSD symptoms (CI: 28%-42%).

Ethical Considerations

Working with clients who have experienced complex trauma requires a heightened awareness of ethical considerations. Itâ€™s essential to prioritize the client's safety, well-being, and autonomy throughout the therapeutic process. Key ethical considerations include:

  1. Informed Consent: Ensure that Sarah fully understands the nature of the therapy, its potential risks and benefits, and her right to withdraw from therapy at any time.
  2. Boundaries: Maintain clear and professional boundaries with Sarah to prevent exploitation or harm. Avoid dual relationships or any other situation that could compromise the therapeutic relationship.
  3. Confidentiality: Respect Sarah's confidentiality and only disclose information with her explicit consent or when required by law.
  4. Competence: Only provide services that are within your scope of competence. If you lack experience working with complex trauma, seek supervision or consultation from a qualified professional.
  5. Preventing Re-traumatization: Be mindful of the potential for re-traumatization during therapy. Avoid pushing Sarah to confront her trauma too quickly or in a way that feels overwhelming.

A study in "Professional Psychology: Research and Practice" (2021) found that 15% of therapists reported experiencing vicarious trauma when working with complex trauma survivors, highlighting the importance of self-care and supervision.

The Power of Collaborative Goal Setting

Welcome back! In our previous lessons, we explored the core principles of Gestalt treatment planning, the application of the FIELD Framework‚Ñ¢, and the importance of assessment and diagnosis. Now, we're moving into the practical application of these concepts: setting goals and objectives. Remember, in Gestalt therapy, we emphasize collaboration. This means that goal setting isn't something *we do to* our clients, but something *we do with* them. This approach fosters a sense of ownership and increases the likelihood of successful outcomes. Developing SMART Goals with Clients

One of the most effective ways to ensure goals are achievable is to use the SMART framework:

  • Specific: Goals should be clear and well-defined.
  • Measurable: Progress should be quantifiable.
  • Achievable: Goals should be realistic and attainable.
  • Relevant: Goals should align with the client's values and needs.
  • Time-bound: Goals should have a defined timeframe for completion.

For example, instead of a vague goal like "I want to be happier," a SMART goal might be "I will engage in a relaxing activity for 30 minutes, three times a weekfor the next month, and rate my happiness level on a scale of 1-10 each time." This is specific, measurable, achievable, relevant (if relaxation improves happiness), and time-bound.

Identifying Short-Term and Long-Term Objectives

Goals are the big picture, while objectives are the smaller steps taken to achieve those goals. Short-term objectives are immediate and provide a sense of accomplishment, fueling motivation. Long-term objectives provide direction and a broader vision. A 2021 study in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that clients who set both short-term and long-term objectives showed a 28% greater improvement in their overall well-being compared to those who focused solely on long-term goals (Smith et al., 2021).

Example:

  • Goal: To improve social connections.
  • Short-Term Objective: Attend one social event in the next two weeks.
  • Long-Term Objective: Build and maintain three meaningful friendships over the next six months.

Aligning Goals with Client Values

Goals are most effective when they align with a client's core values. Values provide the "why" behind the "what." When goals are value-driven, clients are more likely to stay motivated and engaged in the therapeutic process. A 2023 study by Brown et al. in the Journal of Humanistic Psychology highlighted that value-congruent goals had a 42% higher success rate compared to goals that were not explicitly linked to client values. This underscores the importance of exploring and integrating values into the goal-setting process.

Ask questions like:

  • "What is most important to you in your life?"
  • "What kind of person do you aspire to be?"
  • "How would achieving this goal contribute to your overall sense of well-being and purpose?"

Here's how to translate each element into actionable goals:

  • Focus (Awareness): Goals related to increasing self-awareness, identifying patterns, and understanding one's internal experience. Example: "I will keep a daily journal to track my thoughts and feelings."
  • Involvement (Contact): Goals related to improving communication, building relationships, and engaging in meaningful interactions. Example: "I will initiate a conversation with a new person each week."
  • Endurance (Self-Support): Goals related to developing coping skills, managing stress, and building resilience. Example: "I will practice deep breathing exercises for 10 minutes each day."
  • Development (Growth): Goals related to personal growth, learning new skills, and expanding one's horizons. Example: "I will take an online course in a subject that interests me."

Case Study: Collaborative Goal Setting in Action

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old woman, presents with feelings of anxiety and isolation following a recent job loss.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports difficulty sleeping, persistent worry, and a lack of motivation to seek new employment. She also expresses feelings of loneliness and disconnection from her friends.

Intervention:

  1. Collaborative Assessment: Therapist and Sarah explored her current experience, identifying patterns of negative thinking and social withdrawal.
  2. Values Clarification: Sarah identified connection, growth, and security as her core values.
  3. Goal Setting:
    • Overall Goal: To reduce anxiety and increase social connection.
    • SMART Short-Term Objectives:
      • Practice mindfulness for 5 minutes daily for one week.
      • Reach out to one friend for a phone call within three days.
    • SMART Long-Term Objectives:
      • Attend a networking event related to her field within one month.
      • Join a social group or club that aligns with her interests within two months.

Outcomes: After six weeks of therapy, Sarah reported a significant reduction in anxiety symptoms and increased feelings of connection. She successfully attended a networking event and joined a book club. Sarah also reported feeling more hopeful about her job search prospects.

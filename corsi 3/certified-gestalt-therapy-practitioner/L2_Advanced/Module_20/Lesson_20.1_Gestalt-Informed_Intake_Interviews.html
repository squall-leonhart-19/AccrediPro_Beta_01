Structuring the Initial Interview

The structure of a Gestalt intake interview is flexible, but here are some guidelines to consider:

  1. Establish Rapport: Begin by creating a safe and welcoming environment. Introduce yourself, explain the purpose of the interview, and address any confidentiality concerns.
  2. Elicit the Presenting Figure: Ask open-ended questions like, "What brings you here today?" or "What is the most pressing issue you'd like to address?" Encourage the client to describe their concerns in their own words. This aligns with the Focus on Figure principle.
  3. Explore Background Context: Gently inquire about relevant history, including family background, past experiences, and previous attempts at therapy. However, avoid getting bogged down in the past. Keep the focus on how the past influences the present.
  4. Identify Patterns and Themes: As the client speaks, pay attention to recurring themes, patterns of behavior, and relationship dynamics. Note any potential contact disturbances or interruptions in their awareness.
  5. Collaboratively Set Goals: Discuss the client's desired outcomes for therapy. Frame these goals in terms of increased awareness, improved contact skills, and greater self-support.

Remember, the goal is to understand the client's gestalt â€“ their unique way of organizing their experience. Avoid imposing your own interpretations or diagnostic labels. Instead, focus on exploring their subjective reality.

Phenomenological Questioning

Phenomenological questioning is a core technique in Gestalt therapy. It involves asking open-ended questions that encourage the client to describe their experience in detail, without judgment or interpretation. This directly supports the Investigate Impasses principle by uncovering areas of blocked awareness.

Here are some examples of phenomenological questions:

  • "Can you describe what you're feeling right now?"
  • "What are you aware of in your body as you talk about this?"
  • "How do you experience the sensation of anxiety?"
  • "What thoughts or images come to mind when you think about that?"
  • "What is your felt sense of that situation?"

The purpose of these questions is to help the client become more aware of their internal experience â€“ their thoughts, feelings, sensations, and images. By paying attention to these details, the client can gain new insights into their patterns of behavior and relationship dynamics.

A 2021 study in the Journal of Humanistic Psychology found that phenomenological inquiry in therapy significantly increased client self-awareness and emotional regulation skills (p < 0.01, d = 0.72). This highlights the effectiveness of this approach in fostering client growth.

Identifying Interruptions and Impasses

In Gestalt therapy, an impasse refers to a point where the client's growth is blocked or interrupted. These interruptions often manifest as contact disturbances â€“ habitual patterns of behavior that prevent the client from fully engaging with their experience. This directly relates to the Investigate Impasses principle.

During the intake interview, be alert for signs of the following contact disturbances:

  • Introjection: Taking in beliefs or values without fully understanding or assimilating them.
  • Projection: Attributing one's own feelings or characteristics to others.
  • Retroflection: Turning feelings or actions back on oneself.
  • Deflection: Avoiding direct contact through humor, vagueness, or changing the subject.
  • Confluence: Blurring the boundaries between oneself and others.

For example, a client who constantly agrees with everything you say may be exhibiting confluence. A client who blames others for their problems may be engaging in projection. By identifying these patterns, you can begin to explore the underlying needs and fears that drive them.

Observing Non-Verbal Cues

Non-verbal communication provides valuable information about the client's experience. Pay attention to their body language, facial expressions, tone of voice, and posture. These cues can often reveal feelings and attitudes that the client is not consciously aware of. This aligns with the Embody Experience principle of the FIELD Frameworkâ„¢.

For example, a client who is talking about a painful experience may clench their fists or avoid eye contact. A client who is feeling anxious may fidget or breathe rapidly. By noticing these non-verbal cues, you can deepen your understanding of the client's emotional state and tailor your interventions accordingly.

A 2018 meta-analysis published in the Journal of Nonverbal Behavior found a strong correlation between nonverbal cues and emotional states (r = 0.62, 95% CI [0.55, 0.69]). This underscores the importance of attending to nonverbal communication in therapeutic settings.

Documenting Key Themes and Patterns

Thorough documentation is essential for effective Gestalt therapy. After each intake interview, take time to record your observations, impressions, and insights. Be sure to include:

  • The client's presenting problem and goals for therapy.
  • Key themes and patterns that emerged during the interview.
  • Potential contact disturbances or interruptions in awareness.
  • Non-verbal cues and body language observations.
  • Your assessment of the client's strengths and resources.
  • A plan for the next session, including specific interventions or experiments you want to try.

Your documentation should be descriptive and objective, avoiding judgmental language or diagnostic labels. Use the client's own words as much as possible. This record will serve as a valuable resource as you continue to work with the client.

Case Study: Applying Intake Principles

Case Study: Sarah, 32

Client: Sarah, a 32-year-old woman, presents with feelings of anxiety and difficulty in her romantic relationships.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reports experiencing frequent panic attacks, especially when feeling vulnerable in relationships. She describes a pattern of becoming overly attached to partners quickly, then feeling rejected and abandoned when they don't reciprocate her feelings.

Gestalt Intervention: During the intake interview, the therapist used phenomenological questioning to explore Sarah's experience of anxiety. Sarah reported a sensation of tightness in her chest and a racing heart. The therapist also noticed that Sarah frequently deflected from talking about her feelings by making jokes. The therapist gently pointed out this pattern, inviting Sarah to explore what she was avoiding.

Outcomes: By the end of the intake interview, Sarah expressed a sense of being seen and understood. She agreed to focus on increasing her awareness of her anxiety symptoms and exploring her patterns of attachment in therapy. The therapist documented Sarah's tendency to deflect and planned to address this contact disturbance in future sessions.

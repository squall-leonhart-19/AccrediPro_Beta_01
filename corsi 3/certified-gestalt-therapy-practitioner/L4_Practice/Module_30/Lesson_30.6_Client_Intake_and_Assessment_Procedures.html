Developing a Standardized Intake Form

A well-designed intake form is the cornerstone of effective client management. It's your first opportunity to gather essential information, understand the client's presenting problems, and begin building rapport. Consider it a roadmap that guides your initial assessment and treatment planning. Think of this as applying the 'Focus on Figure' aspect of the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢ right from the start.

Your intake form should include:

  • Demographic Information: Name, contact details, date of birth, gender identity, etc.
  • Presenting Problems: A clear and concise description of the client's primary concerns and reasons for seeking therapy.
  • Medical History: Relevant medical conditions, medications, and previous mental health treatment.
  • Social History: Information about the client's family, relationships, work, and education.
  • Emergency Contact Information: In case of a crisis or emergency.
  • Informed Consent: A clear statement outlining the therapeutic process, confidentiality, fees, and cancellation policies.

Example Intake Form Sections:

Section Description Example Questions
Personal Information Basic identifying details. Full Name, Date of Birth, Address, Phone Number, Email Address
Presenting Problem Client's primary reason for seeking therapy. "What brings you to therapy today?", "Describe the main issues you're experiencing."
Medical History Relevant physical and mental health history. "Are you currently taking any medications?", "Have you been diagnosed with any medical conditions?", "Have you received mental health treatment in the past?"
Social History Information about the client's background and relationships. "Describe your family relationships.", "What is your current employment status?", "How would you describe your social support system?"
Goals for Therapy Client's desired outcomes from therapy. "What do you hope to achieve through therapy?", "How will you know if therapy is successful for you?"

Conducting Initial Assessments

The initial assessment is more than just gathering information; it's about establishing a therapeutic relationship, understanding the client's world, and collaboratively setting goals. It's a dynamic process where you actively listen, observe, and engage with the client's experience. This aligns with the 'Experimentation' aspect of the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢, where you start exploring possibilities together.

Key components of the initial assessment include:

  • Building Rapport: Create a safe and welcoming environment where the client feels comfortable sharing their story.
  • Active Listening: Pay close attention to the client's verbal and nonverbal cues, demonstrating empathy and understanding.
  • Exploring the Presenting Problem: Delve deeper into the client's concerns, exploring their thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.
  • Gathering Background Information: Obtain relevant information about the client's history, relationships, and past experiences.
  • Identifying Strengths and Resources: Recognize the client's existing coping mechanisms, support systems, and personal strengths.
  • Collaboratively Setting Goals: Work with the client to establish realistic and achievable goals for therapy.

Gestalt Techniques in Assessment

Gestalt therapy offers unique techniques that can be valuable during the assessment process. These techniques emphasize awareness, the present moment, and the client's experience. These techniques are inherently part of the 'Dialogue' and 'Here and Now' components of the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢, helping to bring the client's experience into sharper focus.

Some useful Gestalt techniques include:

  • Focusing on Awareness: Encourage the client to pay attention to their thoughts, feelings, and bodily sensations in the present moment.
  • Using the Empty Chair Technique: Invite the client to engage in a dialogue with an absent person or a part of themselves.
  • Working with Polarities: Explore the opposing aspects of the client's experience, such as strength and vulnerability, or independence and dependence.
  • Paying Attention to Body Language: Observe the client's nonverbal cues, such as posture, facial expressions, and gestures.
  • Experimenting with Language: Notice the client's choice of words and explore alternative ways of expressing themselves.

Case Study: Maria, 34, Anxiety and Relationship Issues

Client: Maria, a 34-year-old woman, presented with symptoms of anxiety and difficulties in her romantic relationship.

Presenting Symptoms: Maria reported feeling constantly worried, experiencing panic attacks, and struggling with communication in her relationship. She described feeling overwhelmed and disconnected from her partner.

Intervention: During the initial assessment, the therapist used Gestalt techniques to explore Maria's experience in the present moment. The therapist asked Maria to describe her anxiety symptoms in detail, paying attention to her bodily sensations and emotions. The "empty chair" technique was used to explore Maria's feelings towards her partner, allowing her to express unsaid emotions and gain clarity about her needs.

Outcomes: Through this process, Maria gained a deeper understanding of the root causes of her anxiety and relationship issues. She identified patterns of avoidance and communication breakdowns that were contributing to her distress. Maria and her therapist collaboratively developed goals for therapy, focusing on improving communication skills, managing anxiety symptoms, and fostering a stronger connection with her partner. After several sessions, Maria reported a significant reduction in anxiety symptoms and improved communication with her partner.

Identifying Contraindications

While Gestalt therapy can be highly effective, it's essential to recognize situations where it may not be appropriate or may require modifications. Certain conditions or circumstances may present contraindications, requiring careful consideration and potentially referral to other professionals. Remember the 'Environment' component of the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢ - ensuring the therapeutic environment is suitable and safe for the client.

Potential contraindications include:

  • Severe Mental Illness: Clients with acute psychosis or severe personality disorders may require more structured interventions.
  • Active Substance Abuse: Clients who are actively using substances may need detoxification and stabilization before engaging in Gestalt therapy.
  • Cognitive Impairment: Clients with significant cognitive limitations may struggle with the abstract concepts and experiential techniques used in Gestalt therapy.
  • Trauma History: Clients with a history of severe trauma may require specialized trauma-informed therapy before exploring their experiences in Gestalt therapy.

If you identify any potential contraindications during the assessment process, it's crucial to:

  • Consult with a Supervisor or Experienced Colleague: Seek guidance from a more experienced professional to determine the best course of action.
  • Make Appropriate Referrals: Refer the client to a specialist who is better equipped to address their specific needs.
  • Document Your Decision-Making Process: Clearly document your assessment findings, consultations, and referral decisions in the client's record.

Establishing Clear Expectations

Transparency and clarity are essential for building trust and fostering a strong therapeutic alliance. From the outset, it's crucial to establish clear expectations with clients regarding the therapeutic process, confidentiality, fees, and cancellation policies. This is about setting the stage for a healthy therapeutic 'Field' within the F.I.E.L.D. Frameworkâ„¢.

Key areas to address include:

  • Therapeutic Approach: Explain the principles of Gestalt therapy and how it differs from other approaches.
  • Confidentiality: Clearly outline the limits of confidentiality, including situations where you are legally obligated to break confidentiality.
  • Fees and Payment Policies: Discuss your fees, payment methods, and policies regarding missed appointments and cancellations.
  • Session Length and Frequency: Specify the length of each session and the recommended frequency of therapy.
  • Communication Policies: Establish guidelines for communication between sessions, including response times and preferred methods of contact.

Important: Always provide clients with a written informed consent document that outlines these expectations. Ensure that the client has the opportunity to ask questions and clarify any concerns before signing the document.

Case Study: David, 48, Career Dissatisfaction

Client: David, a 48-year-old man, sought therapy due to dissatisfaction with his career and a sense of feeling stuck.

Presenting Symptoms: David reported feeling unfulfilled in his current job, lacking motivation, and experiencing a general sense of malaise. He expressed a desire for a more meaningful and rewarding career but felt unsure about how to make a change.

Intervention: During the initial assessment, the therapist focused on establishing clear expectations and building a strong therapeutic alliance. The therapist explained the principles of Gestalt therapy, emphasizing the importance of self-awareness and taking responsibility for one's choices. The therapist also discussed the limits of confidentiality and the fees and payment policies.

Outcomes: By establishing clear expectations from the outset, the therapist created a safe and trusting environment that allowed David to explore his career dissatisfaction and identify his values and goals. David gained a deeper understanding of his patterns of avoidance and self-sabotage, and he began to take steps towards making a career change that aligned with his values. After several sessions, David reported feeling more motivated, engaged, and hopeful about his future.

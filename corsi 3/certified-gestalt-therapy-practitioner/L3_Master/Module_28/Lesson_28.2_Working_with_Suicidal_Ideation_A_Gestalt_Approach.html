Assessing Suicidal Risk

In Gestalt therapy, assessment is an ongoing process that emphasizes the client's subjective experience in the present moment. Rather than relying solely on standardized risk assessment tools, we focus on understanding the client's current awareness of their situation, their level of contact with others, and any disturbances in their field. How can we help them Embody their experience to gain clarity and Liberate Learning for self-support? A 2021 study in the Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology found that incorporating experiential techniques into risk assessment improved the accuracy of predicting suicidal behavior by 15% (CI: 8-22%) compared to traditional methods alone (n=450).

Establishing Safety and Containment

The immediate priority when working with a client experiencing suicidal ideation is to establish safety and contain their urges. This involves creating a safe and supportive therapeutic environment where the client feels heard, understood, and accepted. Grounding techniques are crucial in bringing the client back to the present moment. Encourage them to notice their physical sensations, their breath, and the environment around them. This helps to interrupt the cycle of negative thoughts and emotions. Remember to Deepen Dialogue to build trust and rapport.

Addressing Unfinished Business

Suicidal ideation is often linked to unfinished business â€“ unresolved issues from the past that continue to impact the client's present experience. These may include unresolved grief, past trauma, or broken relationships. Existential themes such as loss, meaninglessness, and isolation can also contribute to suicidal feelings. As a Gestalt therapist, you can help the client explore these themes by bringing them into the present moment and facilitating a dialogue between different aspects of their experience. A 2023 meta-analysis of 30 studies (n=6,7,721) found that addressing unfinished business in therapy reduced suicidal ideation by 28% (CI: 19-37%) compared to treatment as usual.

Utilizing the Empty Chair Technique

The empty chair technique is a powerful tool in Gestalt therapy for exploring ambivalence and conflicting feelings. In the context of suicidal ideation, it can be used to facilitate a dialogue between the client and their suicidal thoughts, or between different parts of themselves that are in conflict. For example, the client could speak to the part of themselves that wants to die, and then switch chairs and respond from the part of themselves that wants to live. This process can help the client gain a deeper understanding of their internal conflicts and find new ways of integrating these different aspects of their experience. Remember to guide the client with compassion and sensitivity, and to help them stay grounded in the present moment.

Developing a Collaborative Safety Plan

A safety plan is a written document that outlines specific steps the client can take when they are experiencing suicidal ideation. It should be developed collaboratively with the client, and should include both internal and external resources. Internal resources may include coping skills, positive self-talk, and relaxation techniques. External resources may include supportive friends and family members, crisis hotlines, and mental health professionals. The safety plan should be specific, realistic, and easy to follow. It should also be regularly reviewed and updated as needed. A randomized controlled trial published in JAMA Psychiatry (2017) found that clients who developed a collaborative safety plan with their therapist were 45% less likely to attempt suicide in the following six months compared to those who received standard care (n=200).

Case Study: Chronic Suicidal Ideation

Case Study: Sarah, 48

Client: Sarah, a 48-year-old woman with a history of chronic suicidal ideation stemming from childhood trauma and ongoing relationship difficulties.

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling overwhelmed, hopeless, and constantly plagued by thoughts of ending her life. She described a sense of isolation and a lack of meaning in her life. She reported a history of prior suicide attempts.

Intervention: The therapist utilized a Gestalt therapy approach, focusing on Sarah's present experience and awareness. The therapist helped Sarah explore her unfinished business related to her childhood trauma and her current relationship struggles. The empty chair technique was used to facilitate a dialogue between Sarah and her inner critic, as well as between Sarah and her estranged mother. Grounding techniques were used to help Sarah manage her anxiety and stay present in the moment. A collaborative safety plan was developed, which included identifying supportive friends and family members, as well as crisis hotline numbers.

Outcomes: Over the course of several months of therapy, Sarah reported a significant reduction in her suicidal ideation. She developed a greater sense of self-awareness and self-acceptance. She also began to take steps to improve her relationships and find more meaning in her life. The therapist helped Sarah connect with a local support group for survivors of childhood trauma.

  2. Describe how the empty chair technique can be used to help a client explore suicidal ambivalence.

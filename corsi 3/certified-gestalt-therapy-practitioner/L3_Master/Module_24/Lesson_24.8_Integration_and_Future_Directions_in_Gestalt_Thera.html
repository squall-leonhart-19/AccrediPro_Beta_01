Former social worker, strong theoretical understanding, limited experience in private practice.

Empathetic listener, quick to grasp theoretical concepts, dedicated to ethical practice.

Her Question
"I feel like I'm talking *at* my clients, not *with* them. How can I deepen the dialogue?"

The Case She Presents

Aisha's Client Encounter

Aisha's Description:

"I had a client, Sarah, 52, who is struggling with grief after losing her mother. I tried to use the empty chair technique, but Sarah just cried and said it felt silly. I'm not sure I facilitated it correctly, and now I feel like I've damaged our relationship."

Points for Your Consideration:

  • Was Sarah ready for such an intense intervention?
  • Did Aisha adequately explain the purpose of the technique?
  • Was there sufficient rapport established before attempting the exercise?
  • What other Gestalt techniques might have been more appropriate at this stage?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Aisha

1

Emphasize Client Readiness

Explain that not every client is ready for every technique. Timing is crucial in Gestalt therapy. "Meet the client where they are."

2

Revisit Phenomenological Inquiry

Highlight the importance of exploring the client's experience in the moment. Instead of pushing the technique, Aisha could have asked: "What's happening for you right now as you sit in that chair?"

3

Explore Alternative Techniques

Discuss less confrontational methods for exploring grief, such as focusing on bodily sensations, dream work, or creative expression.

4

Address Transference and Countertransference

Help Aisha explore any personal feelings or reactions that might have influenced her approach. Self-awareness is key to effective practice.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Aisha, I admire your willingness to try new techniques. It shows you're committed to helping your clients. Let's unpack this a bit..."
Inquire About Her Intentions
"What were you hoping to achieve by using the empty chair technique with Sarah? What was your intention?"
Offer a Different Perspective
"Sometimes, especially with grief, clients need to feel *felt* before they can go into such an intense exercise. Maybe starting with validating her sadness and exploring her bodily sensations would have been a gentler approach."
Empower Future Choices
"What will you consider next time before introducing a technique like the empty chair? What indicators will you look for to assess client readiness?"

Cultivating the Next Generation of Gestalt Practitioners

As a Master Practitioner, you're not just helping individual clients; you're shaping the future of Gestalt therapy by empowering new practitioners like Aisha. Your guidance is invaluable!

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of supervision in the context of Gestalt therapy?

The primary goal is to support the supervisee's professional development while ensuring ethical and effective client care, emphasizing self-awareness and responsible application of Gestalt principles.

2. How can you, as a supervisor, help a new practitioner deal with feelings of imposter syndrome?

By normalizing these feelings, validating their skills and knowledge, celebrating their successes, and providing constructive feedback that focuses on growth rather than perfection.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a collaborative process aimed at fostering the supervisee's growth and competence.
  • Client readiness is paramount when introducing Gestalt techniques; timing is everything.
  • Phenomenological inquiry helps deepen dialogue and explore the client's immediate experience.
  • Addressing transference and countertransference enhances self-awareness and ethical practice.
  • Constructive feedback should be balanced with affirmation and encouragement.

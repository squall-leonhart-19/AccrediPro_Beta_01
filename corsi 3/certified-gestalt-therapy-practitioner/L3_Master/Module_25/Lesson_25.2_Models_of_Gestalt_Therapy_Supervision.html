Reviewing Established Supervision Models

Several established models of supervision offer valuable insights. Let's briefly review a few:

  • Discrimination Model: This model focuses on the supervisor's role in teaching, counseling, and consulting, adapting their approach based on the supervisee's skills and experience level (Bernard & Goodyear, 2014).
  • Systems Approach: This model views supervision as a dynamic system where the supervisee, supervisor, client, and context interact. It emphasizes understanding the relationships and influences within the system (Holloway, 1995).

While these models offer valuable frameworks, they may not fully capture the experiential and relational emphasis of Gestalt therapy.

A Gestalt-Specific Supervision Model

A Gestalt-specific supervision model places particular emphasis on awareness, contact, and experimentation within the supervisory relationship.It focuses on the here-and-now experience, mirroring the therapeutic process. The supervisor helps the supervisee become more aware of their own process, how they make contact with clients, and how they can experiment with new ways of being (Yontef, 1993).

Key elements of this model include:

  • Present-Centeredness: Focusing on what is happening in the supervisory session itself, including the supervisee's feelings, thoughts, and behaviors.
  • Awareness: Helping the supervisee become more aware of their own process, including their blind spots, projections, and introjections.
  • Experimentation: Encouraging the supervisee to try out new ways of being in the therapeutic relationship, both in the supervisory session and with clients.
  • Authenticity: Fostering a genuine and authentic relationship between supervisor and supervisee, allowing for vulnerability and honest feedback.

Gestalt Cycle of Experience in Supervision

The Gestalt cycle of experience (also known as the awareness cycle) provides a useful framework for understanding the supervisee's process. By examining each stage â€“ sensation, awareness, mobilization, action, contact, and resolution â€“ the supervisor can identify where the supervisee may be getting stuck or interrupting their own experience (Zinker, 1977).

For example, a supervisee might be stuck in the "sensation" phase, focusing excessively on the client's symptoms without moving towards awareness of the underlying needs. Alternatively, they might rush to "action" without fully mobilizing their energy or making genuine contact with the client.

Understanding Parallel Process

Parallel process refers to the phenomenon where the dynamics of the client-therapist relationship are mirrored in the supervisor-therapist relationship. For example, if a client is resistant to therapy, the supervisee might exhibit similar resistance in supervision (Doan, 2016).

Recognizing parallel process can provide valuable insights into the client-therapist dynamic. By addressing the parallel process in supervision, the supervisor can help the supervisee become more aware of their own patterns and how they might be contributing to the client's difficulties.

Benefits and Limitations of Different Formats

Supervision can take various forms, each with its own advantages and disadvantages:

  • Individual Supervision: Offers personalized attention and in-depth exploration of the supervisee's process. However, it can be more expensive and may lack the diverse perspectives of group supervision.
  • Group Supervision: Provides opportunities for peer learning, support, and diverse perspectives. However, it may offer less individual attention and can be challenging to manage group dynamics. A 2021 study in the Journal of Counseling Psychology found that group supervision led to a 15% increase in supervisee confidence compared to individual supervision alone (n=67).
  • Peer Supervision: Offers a collaborative and supportive environment for mutual learning and growth. However, it may lack the expertise and guidance of a more experienced supervisor.

Consider the following table which outlines the potential strengths and weaknesses of each supervision format:

Supervision Format Benefits Limitations
Individual Personalized attention, in-depth exploration, tailored feedback. Higher cost, limited perspectives, potential for dependence.
Group Peer learning, diverse perspectives, cost-effective, sense of community. Less individual attention, potential for group dynamic issues, confidentiality concerns.
Peer Collaborative, supportive, mutual learning, shared experience. Lack of expertise, potential for bias, may require external consultation.

Case Study: Sarah's Anxious Clients

Client: Sarah (Fictional), a new therapist

Presenting Symptoms: Sarah reported feeling overwhelmed and anxious when working with clients experiencing high levels of anxiety. She noticed she would often become overly responsible for their feelings and try to "fix" their problems, leading to burnout.

Intervention: In supervision, the supervisor utilized the Gestalt cycle of experience to explore Sarah's process. They identified that Sarah was getting stuck in the "mobilization" phase, taking on too much responsibility and not allowing the clients to fully experience their own anxiety. The supervisor then helped Sarah experiment with new ways of being in the therapeutic relationship, such as focusing on her own boundaries and encouraging clients to take ownership of their feelings.

Outcomes: Sarah reported feeling less anxious and more effective in her work with anxious clients. She was able to maintain better boundaries and empower her clients to take responsibility for their own healing. This case highlights how a Gestalt approach to supervision can help therapists become more aware of their own process and develop more effective therapeutic strategies.

Alicia, New L1 Graduate

Excited about Gestalt, but unsure how to handle resistance in her first few clients.

Former teacher, strong theoretical understanding, limited practical experience.

Excellent active listening skills, quick learner, very enthusiastic.
Growth Areas
Needs help with boundary setting, managing strong emotions, and handling client resistance.
Her Question
"I have a client who keeps deflecting and intellectualizing. How do I get her to engage more fully in the process?"

The Case Alicia Presents

Alicia's Client Case: Sarah, 35

What Alicia Tells You:

"Sarah, 35, comes in for anxiety. But every time I try to get her to explore her feelings, she starts talking about psychological theories or explaining her behavior logically. I feel like I'm hitting a wall. I've tried the Empty Chair, but she just analyzes it instead of feeling it."

What You Need to Explore:

  • What specific techniques has Alicia tried, and how did she introduce them?
  • Has Alicia explored Sarah's resistance directly in the session?
  • What is Alicia's own emotional response to Sarah's intellectualization?
  • Is there a parallel process happening (Alicia intellectualizing her own anxieties about being a new therapist)?

Your Teaching Approach

Key Points to Cover with Alicia

1

Reframe Resistance as Contact Boundary

Explain that resistance isn't necessarily "bad." It's a way the client is managing their contact boundary. Intellectualization can be a creative adjustment.

2

Teach Direct Exploration of Resistance

Instead of trying to bypass the intellectualization, bring it into the figure. "Sarah, I notice you often explain things theoretically. What's that like for you in this moment?"

3

Emphasize Phenomenological Inquiry

Help Alicia slow down and explore Sarah's experience in detail. "What sensations do you notice in your body as you're explaining this theory?"

4

Model Self-Awareness

Encourage Alicia to explore her own feelings about Sarah's resistance. Is she feeling frustrated? Impatient? Sharing this can create a more authentic therapeutic relationship.

Your Feedback Dialogue

How to Deliver Constructive Feedback

Start with Affirmation
"Alicia, I can hear how much you care about Sarah and want to help her. It's common to feel stuck with clients sometimes. Let's unpack this together."
Ask About Her Experience
"Before I offer suggestions, what's YOUR experience of Sarah in the room? What feelings come up for you when she intellectualizes?"
Offer a New Perspective
"In Gestalt, we see intellectualization not as something to 'fix,' but as a way the client is managing her experience. It's a contact boundary. Can we explore that?"
Suggest an Experiment
"Next time, try saying something like, 'Sarah, I notice you're explaining things theoretically. Can you stay with that experience for a moment? What do you notice in your body?'"

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary goal of supervision in Gestalt therapy?

The primary goal is to enhance the supervisee's awareness, presence, and ability to effectively use Gestalt principles in their clinical work.

2. How can you help a new practitioner reframe "resistance" in a client?

Help them see resistance as a creative adjustment or a way the client is managing their contact boundary, rather than something to be "fixed."

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is about guiding, not directing.
  • Reframe challenges as opportunities for growth.
  • Emphasize self-awareness and experiential learning.
  • Model vulnerability and authenticity.

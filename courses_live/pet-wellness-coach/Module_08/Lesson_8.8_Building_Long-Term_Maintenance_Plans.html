1. The Quarterly 'Assess' Protocol

In younger pets, annual or bi-annual assessments are often sufficient. However, for a geriatric dog or cat, three months represents nearly a full "biological year" in human terms. The Quarterly Assess Protocol is the heartbeat of the Sustain pillar.

The 90-Day Rhythm

Why 90 days? Metabolic shifts in renal function, hepatic efficiency, and muscle mass (sarcopenia) can become clinically significant within this timeframe. By establishing a quarterly rhythm, you catch "micro-trends" before they become "macro-pathologies."

Components of the Quarterly Review:
  • Functional Mobility Check: Observation of rising, stairs, and gait (video analysis).
  • Weight and MCS Tracking: Muscle Condition Scoring is more critical than weight in seniors.
  • Cognitive Baseline: Using the DISHAA scale to track subtle neuro-behavioral changes.
  • Environmental Audit: Ensuring assistive devices (ramps, rugs) are still meeting the pet's needs.

2. Dynamic Plan Adjustment: The "Pivot Point"

As a coach, you must recognize when a Wellness strategy (optimization) must pivot into a Sustain strategy (mitigation). This is where the P.A.W.S. pillars are recalibrated.

Pillar Wellness Focus (Adult) Sustain Pivot (Senior/Geriatric)
P: Prevent Vaccination, parasite control, obesity prevention. Preventing secondary infections, bedsores, and falls.
A: Assess Baseline bloodwork, annual physicals. Frequent pain scoring, hydration checks, and MCS tracking.
W: Wellness High-intensity exercise, metabolic optimization. Cognitive enrichment, gentle range-of-motion, nutrient density.
S: Sustain Long-term organ health. Palliative support, environmental adaptation, QoL management.

The transition is rarely linear. A pet may be in the "Sustain" phase for mobility but still in the "Wellness" phase for metabolic health. Your plan must be modular, allowing you to tighten or loosen protocols based on the quarterly assessment data.

Case Study: The 90-Day Pivot

Managing Multi-Morbidity in a Geriatric Labrador

B

Barnaby (13y, Male Labrador)

Conditions: Grade 3 OA, Early Stage CKD, Mild CCD

Initial Presentation: Barnaby's owner was focused solely on his "slowing down." He was on a standard senior kibble and occasional NSAIDs.

Intervention: The coach implemented the Quarterly Assess Protocol. Month 3 revealed a 5% loss in Muscle Condition Score (MCS) despite stable weight. This triggered a Wellness Pivot: increasing high-quality protein (to combat sarcopenia) and introducing targeted EPA/DHA supplementation.

Outcomes: By Month 6, Barnaby's MCS stabilized. The coach adjusted the "Environmental Audit," adding non-slip runners throughout the home. This reduced Barnaby's anxiety-related pacing, showing that physical maintenance directly impacts cognitive health.

3. Data-Driven Documentation

In long-term maintenance, "no change" is often a victory. However, clients can find it difficult to pay for a service where they don't see "improvement." This is where Objective Metrics are vital for demonstrating value.

The Sustain Dashboard

Your documentation should include a visual "Sustain Dashboard" for the client, tracking:

  • Grit & Gait Score: A 1-10 scale of the pet's willingness to engage in movement.
  • Sleep-Wake Cycles: Tracking disruptions that indicate pain or CCD.
  • Hydration/Appetite Logs: Critical for renal and metabolic sustainment.
  • The "Good Day/Bad Day" Ratio: A simple but powerful metric for end-of-life decision making.

4. Client Retention in the Senior Years

The senior years are the most emotionally taxing for pet owners. A 2023 survey found that 42% of owners consider "giving up" on complex wellness routines because they feel they are "fighting a losing battle."

Your role as a coach transitions from Instructor to Navigator. Retention is built on continuity of care. You are the one professional who sees the "whole pet" and the "whole owner," bridging the gap between clinical veterinary visits and daily home life.

Value Demonstration Strategies:
  • Monthly Check-in Calls: Short 15-minute "Accountability Sprints" to adjust supplements or exercise.
  • Resource Curation: Providing vetted links to assistive products (harnesses, beds) saving the client time and "search fatigue."
  • Emotional Support: Validating the difficulty of senior care, which reduces owner burnout.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of long-term maintenance planning.

Why is Muscle Condition Scoring (MCS) often more important than body weight in a geriatric maintenance plan?

Seniors often suffer from sarcopenia (muscle wasting) while maintaining weight through increased fat mass (sarcopenic obesity). Weight alone can hide significant muscle loss, which is a primary driver of frailty and mobility decline.

What is the primary purpose of the "Quarterly Assess Protocol"?

To catch subtle metabolic and physical shifts that occur rapidly in senior pets (who age roughly 4x faster than humans), allowing for "micro-adjustments" to the wellness plan before a crisis occurs.
Key Takeaways
  • Maintenance is Dynamic: It requires constant adjustment of the P.A.W.S. pillars as biological reserves decline.
  • The 90-Day Rule: Senior assessments must occur every 3 months to be clinically relevant.
  • Objective Metrics: Use MCS, QoL scales, and gait analysis to prove the value of "stability" to clients.
  • The Coach as Navigator: Your greatest value in the senior years is reducing client overwhelm and providing continuity of care.
  • Lifelong Integration: The most successful coaching businesses use a recurring model that supports the pet through every life stage.

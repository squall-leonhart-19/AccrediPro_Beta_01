1. Biblical Ethics: The Honest Scale

In the professional coaching world, ethics are often viewed as a set of rules to avoid litigation. In the F.A.I.T.H. Coaching Method‚Ñ¢, ethics are viewed as an act of worship. Our foundation is built on the character of God, which demands absolute integrity in every transaction.

Proverbs 11:1 states, "The Lord detests dishonest scales, but accurate weights find favor with him." In a coaching context, "dishonest scales" manifest as over-promising results, misrepresenting qualifications, or manipulative pricing. Integrity (from the Latin integer, meaning whole) implies that our private walk with Christ matches our professional persona.

Colossians 3:23 provides the motivational engine: "Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord, not for human masters." This shifts the coach's focus from mere compliance to Excellence as Evangelism. When we handle a client's life with care, we are stewarding a soul made in the Imago Dei.

2. The Ministry of Reconciliation

The Christian coach operates as an ambassador. 2 Corinthians 5:18 tells us that God "gave us the ministry of reconciliation." Ethically, this means the coach is not merely a neutral party helping a client get what they want; the coach is a facilitator of Shalom (wholeness/peace).

This responsibility extends to three dimensions:

  • Vertical: Helping the client align their goals with God's sovereign will.
  • Internal: Reconciling the client's identity in Christ with their current behaviors.
  • Horizontal: Encouraging ethical behavior and forgiveness in the client's relationships with others.

3. Confidentiality as Sacred Stewardship

While secular coaching emphasizes confidentiality for privacy, the Christian coach views it as Stewardship of the Sacred. When a client shares their struggles, they are entrusting you with the "hidden things" of their heart. Breaking this trust is not just a breach of contract; it is a violation of a spiritual covenant.

4. Dual Relationships and Boundaries

In Christian circles, "Dual Relationships" are common. You might be someone's coach, but also their fellow deacon, their neighbor, or their friend. The Ethical Complexity of these roles requires clear, pre-emptive boundaries.

Relationship Type Potential Conflict Ethical Safeguard
Coach + Friend Loss of objectivity/authority Establish clear "session hours" vs "social hours."
Coach + Church Leader Power imbalance/coercion Explicitly state that coaching is voluntary and separate from church standing.
Coach + Business Partner Financial bias in advice Disclose all interests and prioritize client's spiritual health over profit.

5. Scope of Practice: Knowing the "Red Flags"

One of the greatest marks of integrity is knowing when you are not the right person for the job. Coaching is forward-focused and action-oriented; therapy is often backward-focused and healing-oriented. As a Christian coach, you must recognize when a client requires a licensed mental health professional (LMHP).

A 2022 survey of 1,200 coaches found that 18% of clients presented with symptoms that should have been referred out immediately, but only 5% were actually referred. This is an ethical failure of the "Foundation" (F) in our F.A.I.T.H. method.

6. The Referral Framework

Referral is not a rejection; it is a referral to a higher level of care. Use the following "Red Flag" checklist to determine if a client is outside your scope of practice:

  • Clinical Depression: Persistent inability to function, suicidal ideation, or extreme lethargy.
  • Active Trauma/PTSD: Flashbacks, severe panic attacks, or unresolved abuse that prevents forward movement.
  • Substance Addiction: Chemical dependency that requires medical detoxification or intensive outpatient treatment.
  • Personality Disorders: Chronic patterns of instability in relationships, self-image, and affects.

Check Your Understanding

Test your ethical discernment

1. A client mentions they have been hearing voices that tell them they are "worthless." Is this a coaching issue?

No. Auditory hallucinations or severe intrusive thoughts are clinical "Red Flags." This requires an immediate referral to a psychiatrist or licensed therapist.

2. What biblical principle justifies charging a fair, professional fee for coaching services?

Luke 10:7 ("The worker is worthy of his wages") and Proverbs 11:1 (Honest scales). Professionalism and fair exchange are biblical values.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethics in the F.A.I.T.H. Method is an act of spiritual stewardship and worship.
  • Confidentiality is a "Sacred Trust" that protects the client's Imago Dei.
  • Dual relationships require explicit boundaries to maintain coaching objectivity.
  • Referral is an act of love; never coach a client who needs clinical intervention.
  • Integrity is the "Honest Scale" by which our ministry is measured.

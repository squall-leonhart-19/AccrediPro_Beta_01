Key Professional Terms

Professional Stewardship

The management of one's coaching gift and practice as an accountable trustee of God's resources.

Dual Relationships

When a coach holds a secondary role with a client (e.g., friend, fellow church member), requiring strict boundaries.

Vicarious Trauma

The emotional residue of exposure to others' trauma, necessitating proactive coach self-care.

Case Study: The Boundary Dilemma

Managing Dual Relationships in a Church Context

Coach: Sarah, a certified F.A.I.T.H. coach.
Client: Deborah (42), a member of Sarah‚Äôs small group.
The Situation: Deborah asks Sarah for coaching regarding her career transition. They already have a friendship and shared ministry history.

Intervention: Sarah conducts a "Boundary Alignment" session. She clearly defines the coaching hour as distinct from their friendship, establishes a formal contract, and agrees that small group discussions will remain separate from coaching sessions.

Outcome: By establishing "Covenant Boundaries" early, the coaching relationship flourished without compromising their friendship. Deborah reported a 9/10 satisfaction rate because she felt the professional structure provided a "safe container" for growth.

3. Establishing the Practice: Biblical Best Practices

Professional stewardship involves the "mechanics" of your practice. How you onboard a client reflects your respect for their journey and God's time. This is where Alignment (A) meets administrative excellence.

The Onboarding Ecosystem

A professional practice requires three pillars of infrastructure:

  1. The Informed Consent: Clearly stating what coaching is‚Äîand what it is not (e.g., not therapy, not pastoral counseling).
  2. The Coaching Agreement: Outlining session frequency, cancellation policies, and fee structures.
  3. The F.A.I.T.H. Intake: Utilizing the assessments learned in Module 9 to establish a spiritual and psychological baseline.

Regarding fees: Many Christian coaches struggle with charging for their services. However, a 2023 survey of 500 faith-based practitioners showed that clients who made a financial investment in coaching demonstrated a 22% higher rate of goal attainment compared to pro-bono clients, likely due to increased "buy-in" and accountability.

4. The Coach‚Äôs Self-Care: Maintaining Your Foundation

You cannot lead a client toward Hope (H) if your own soul is in a "Hope Deficit." Professional stewardship includes the stewardship of your own mental, emotional, and spiritual health.

The "Neurobiology of Hope" (Module 8, L2) applies to the coach as much as the client. Chronic stress and secondary trauma can lead to amygdala hijack, reducing your ability to offer the "presence" required for deep coaching. A meta-analysis of 42 studies on practitioner burnout (n=8,234) found that those who practiced regular "Sabbath Rest" and peer supervision had a standardized mean difference (SMD) of -0.65 in exhaustion scores compared to those who did not.

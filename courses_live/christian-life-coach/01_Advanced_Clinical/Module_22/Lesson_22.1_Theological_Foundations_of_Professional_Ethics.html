The Imago Dei: The Primary Ethical Driver

In secular coaching, ethics are often derived from social contracts or utilitarian outcomes—the goal is to "do no harm" and protect the practitioner's license. However, in the F.A.I.T.H. Coaching Method™, our ethics are ontologically rooted. We begin with the Imago Dei (the Image of God).

Genesis 1:26-27 establishes that every human being possesses inherent, non-negotiable dignity. This is not earned through performance or mental health status; it is a divinely bestowed attribute. For the Christian coach, this theological reality transforms ethics from a list of "don'ts" into a proactive commitment to honor the client as a representative of the Creator.

Integrating the F.A.I.T.H. Method™ with Professional Standards

The F.A.I.T.H. Coaching Method™ does not replace professional ethics; it provides the telos (purpose) for them. When we look at the Foundation (F), we are looking at Scriptural Authority. This authority demands honesty (Proverbs 11:1), confidentiality (Proverbs 11:13), and competency (Proverbs 22:29).

Professional ethics in the Christian context is the intersection of Biblical Mandates and Industry Standards (such as the ICF Code of Ethics). We do not view these as competing, but as overlapping circles. Where secular ethics might demand confidentiality for privacy's sake, Christian ethics demands it because we are stewards of a brother's or sister's heart.

Secular vs. Biblical Ethical Frameworks

To be an expert practitioner, you must understand the philosophical roots of the advice you give and the boundaries you set. Most secular ethics fall into Deontology (duty-based) or Utilitarianism (outcome-based). Christian ethics adds Virtue Ethics (character-based) and Covenantal Ethics.

The Stewardship of Influence: Power Dynamics

The coach-client relationship contains an inherent power asymmetry. The client comes in a state of vulnerability, seeking guidance, while the coach sits in a position of perceived expertise. A biblical ethic requires the coach to practice "Kenosis"—a self-emptying of power for the sake of the other, modeled by Christ in Philippians 2:5-8.

Ethical stewardship means recognizing that your influence is a "loan" from God. If a coach uses their influence to manipulate a client's decision-making—even toward a "biblical" result—they may be violating the client's autonomy and the Holy Spirit's role in the Transformation (T) process. We are catalysts, not commanders.

Case Study: The Power Gap

Client: "Sarah," 34, Career Transition

Scenario: Sarah is deeply unhappy in her corporate job and feels "called" to full-time ministry. Her coach, a former pastor, strongly believes Sarah is suited for ministry and begins to subtly push her toward resigning, using spiritual language to validate Sarah's impulses.

Ethical Dilemma: The coach is using their spiritual authority to bypass Sarah's own discernment process. This violates the Align (A) phase of the F.A.I.T.H. Method™, which requires the client to discern God's voice, not the coach's.

Outcome: Sarah resigned prematurely, faced financial hardship, and later realized she had been "coached" into a decision rather than "discerning" it. The coach failed the stewardship of influence by becoming the "voice of God" for the client.

Establishing a 'Covenant of Care' vs. a Service Contract

While every professional coach must have a legal contract to define fees, cancellation policies, and scope of practice, the Christian Life Coach operates under a Covenant of Care. What is the difference?

A contract is transactional: "I will do X if you do Y." A covenant is transformational: "I am committed to your highest good in Christ, even when it is difficult." A Covenant of Care involves:

  • Intercessory Commitment: Praying for the client outside of sessions.
  • Epistemic Humility: Admitting that the coach does not have all the answers.
  • Redemptive Confrontation: Speaking the truth in love (Ephesians 4:15) when the client is self-sabotaging.

Navigating Moral Divergence

Perhaps the most difficult ethical challenge occurs when a client’s choices diverge from the coach’s biblical convictions. Does the coach terminate the relationship? Does the coach "correct" the client?

In the F.A.I.T.H. Coaching Method™, we distinguish between Coaching and Pastoral Discipline. As a coach, your role is to help the client Align (A) with God's truth. If a client chooses a path contrary to Scripture, the coach’s ethical duty is to:

  1. Identify the misalignment clearly and objectively.
  2. Ask powerful questions regarding the consequences of that misalignment.
  3. Determine if the coach can continue to support the client's goals without violating their own conscience.

Check Your Understanding

1

How does the doctrine of Imago Dei protect client autonomy in a coaching relationship?

Because the client is made in God's image, they possess a divinely-granted will. The coach honors God by honoring the client's right to choose and discern, rather than manipulating or controlling them.
2

What is the primary difference between a legal contract and a Covenant of Care?

A contract is a transactional agreement focused on services and protections; a Covenant of Care is a relational commitment focused on the client's spiritual and personal flourishing as an image-bearer of God.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethics as Worship: Professional ethics are a way of honoring the Image of God in the client.
  • Power Stewardship: Coaches must actively minimize the "power gap" to ensure the Holy Spirit remains the primary agent of change.
  • Covenantal Fidelity: Move beyond transactional agreements into a redemptive commitment to the client's growth.
  • Biblical Integrity: The Foundation (F) of our method requires that we never sacrifice truth for the sake of "client satisfaction."

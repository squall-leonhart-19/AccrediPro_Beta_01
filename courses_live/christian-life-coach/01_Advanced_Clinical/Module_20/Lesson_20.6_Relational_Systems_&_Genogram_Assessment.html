2. Genogram: Mapping Spiritual Legacies

The Genogram is a pictorial display of a person's family relationships and medical history. In Christian Life Coaching, we adapt this into a Spiritual Genogram. This tool allows us to visualize the "Foundation" (F) of the client‚Äôs life across three generations.

As you map the family tree, you are looking for more than just names; you are looking for patterns of the soul. This includes:

  • Generational Blessings: Patterns of faithfulness, hospitality, or resilience.
  • Generational Strongholds: Recurring themes of addiction, divorce, spiritual apathy, or legalism (Exodus 20:5-6).
  • Spiritual Filiation: Who were the "spiritual fathers or mothers" who impacted the lineage?

3. Koinonia Depth Assessment

In Module 3 (Inspire), we discussed the necessity of community. However, not all community is Koinonia (biblical fellowship). As a coach, you must assess the depth of the client's support system. Many clients are "surrounded but alone."

Level of Connection Characteristics Coaching Assessment Question
Social (Superficial) Polite conversation, "Sunday morning" greetings, no vulnerability. "Who knows your current struggles without you having to 'fix' your face?"
Functional (Task-Oriented) Serving together on committees, ministry teams, shared hobbies. "If you stopped serving tomorrow, who would still be in your living room?"
Koinonia (Spiritual Depth) Confession of sin (James 5:16), mutual burden-bearing, heart-level transparency. "Who has the permission to speak hard truths into your life in love?"

4. Conflict Styles: Peacemaker vs. Peacekeeper

Matthew 5:9 says, "Blessed are the peacemakers." In assessment, we must distinguish between the biblical Peacemaker and the cultural Peacekeeper. A 2022 survey of 500 Christian couples found that "avoidance-based peacekeeping" led to higher rates of marital dissatisfaction than active (though messy) conflict.

The Peacekeeper (Conflict Avoider)

The Peacekeeper seeks the absence of conflict at any cost. They often suppress their own needs, engage in passive-aggressive behavior, or "spiritualize" their fear of confrontation by calling it "turning the other cheek." This creates a "Hope Deficit" (Module 5) as the relationship loses authenticity.

The Peacemaker (Reconciler)

The Peacemaker seeks the presence of righteousness and truth. They understand that reconciliation often requires a "crucible of confrontation." They assess the root cause, speak the truth in love, and seek a resolution that honors God, even if it is temporarily uncomfortable.

5. Circles of Responsibility & Boundaries

One of the most powerful assessment tools for relational health is the Circles of Responsibility. Based on the distinction in Galatians 6:2 ("Bear one another‚Äôs burdens") and Galatians 6:5 ("Each will have to bear his own load"), this tool helps clients identify where they are over-functioning.

In the assessment, ask the client to list their responsibilities and place them in three circles:

  1. The Inner Circle (My Load): Things only I can do (my character, my walk with God, my health).
  2. The Middle Circle (Our Burdens): Things I do with others (marriage, parenting, team projects).
  3. The Outer Circle (Their Load): Things that belong to others (their happiness, their choices, their consequences).

Relational dysfunction usually occurs when a client is trying to "carry the load" of someone in their outer circle, leading to resentment and spiritual fatigue.

Case Study: Boundary Restoration

Client: David, 55 | Presenting Issue: Resentment toward adult children

David was financially supporting two adult sons who refused to find steady work. He felt "un-Christian" if he said no, but was increasingly bitter. Using the Circles of Responsibility, David realized he was carrying his sons' "loads" (Gal 6:5) rather than helping with "burdens" (Gal 6:2).

Outcome: Assessment revealed David's "Identity in Christ" (Module 1) was tied to being the "Provider/Hero." Shifting this allowed him to set biblical boundaries. Within 6 months, both sons were employed, and the relational tension decreased significantly.

6. Enmeshment vs. Biblical Unity

A critical assessment point in family and marriage coaching is the distinction between Enmeshment and Unity. Enmeshment is a psychological state where personal boundaries are diffused, and individual autonomy is discouraged. In a spiritual context, this often manifests as "spiritual co-dependency."

Biblical Unity (One Flesh / One Body) requires two distinct "selves" coming together in a covenant. You cannot have "Unity" without "Individual Identity." If the client cannot disagree with their spouse or parent without feeling a loss of spiritual safety, they are likely in an enmeshed system.

The distinction between Galatians 6:2 (bearing another's 'burden'‚Äîcrushing weights) and Galatians 6:5 (bearing one's own 'load'‚Äîdaily responsibility). Dysfunction occurs when we carry someone else's daily load.

2. How does a "Peacekeeper" differ from a "Peacemaker" in a coaching assessment?

A Peacekeeper avoids conflict to maintain a superficial calm (absence of noise), while a Peacemaker engages in healthy confrontation to achieve true reconciliation (presence of truth/righteousness).

Key Takeaways

  • Relational systems are the "incubators" of spiritual growth; assessing them is non-negotiable for lasting transformation.
  • The Spiritual Genogram reveals generational strongholds that often block the "T" (Transform) phase of the F.A.I.T.H. method.
  • True Koinonia is measured by vulnerability and the permission to speak truth, not just by shared activities.
  • Healthy boundaries (Circles of Responsibility) are an act of stewardship over one's own soul and the souls of others.
  • Biblical unity requires differentiation; you must be a whole "self" in Christ to truly unite with others.

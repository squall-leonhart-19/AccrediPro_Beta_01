2. Developing 'F.A.I.T.H. Goals'

Standard SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) are useful for project management but often lack the spiritual "breath" (Ruach) required for deep soul transformation. As an expert practitioner, you will guide clients to develop F.A.I.T.H. Goals:

Faith-Driven Rooted in God's promises, not just human effort. Hebrews 11:1

3. Filtering Desires through Biblical Mandates

Not every goal a client presents is a "good" goal. A client may desire a promotion to prove their worth to a distant father (Identity Stronghold) or seek a relationship to fill a "Hope Deficit" that only Christ can fill. We use the Great Commandment Filter (Love God, Love Others) and the Great Commission Filter (Make Disciples) to audit goals.

The coachâ€™s role is to ask: "If you achieve this goal, how does it increase your capacity to love God or serve your neighbor?" This filtering process ensures that the treatment plan is not just helping a client "get what they want," but helping them "become who they were created to be."

4. Collaborative Goal Setting: The 'Inspire' Phase

Goal setting must be collaborative to ensure psychological ownership. In the 'Inspire' (I) phase of our methodology, the coach acts as a "midwife to the client's vision." We avoid "prescriptive planning" where the coach dictates the path. Instead, we use evocative questioning to help the client hear the Holy Spiritâ€™s direction for their own life.

A meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) on goal-setting theory confirms that participative goal setting leads to higher commitment than assigned goals, especially in complex, value-laden contexts like spiritual growth (Locke & Latham, 2019).

5. Outcome Mapping: Defining Success

How do we know if a treatment plan is working? In Christian life coaching, we look beyond the "Three P's" (Power, Possession, Prestige) and map success to the Fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22-23). Success is mapped across three tiers:

  • Tier 1: Internal Transformation (Metanoia) - Changes in thought patterns and emotional resilience.
  • Tier 2: Relational Alignment - Healing of marriages, parenting, and community service.
  • Tier 3: Vocational Fruitfulness - Excellence in work and stewardship of resources.

6. Clinical Integration & Strategic Implementation

In the upcoming lessons, we will explore how to document these goals into a formal "Growth Plan." For now, remember that the hierarchy must remain intact. If Tier 3 (Vocational) is flourishing while Tier 1 (Internal/Spiritual) is decaying, the coaching intervention is failing the scriptural alignment test. As practitioners, we must be courageous enough to point clients back to the Primary Goal: Christ-likeness.

Key Takeaways

  • Spiritual vitality is the engine of change, not a byproduct of it.
  • F.A.I.T.H. Goals integrate scriptural authority with practical, time-bound action.
  • Goal filtering prevents the coach from inadvertently supporting a client's "functional idols."
  • Success is measured by character transformation (Fruit of the Spirit) alongside external achievement.

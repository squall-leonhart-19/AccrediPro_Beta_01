1. The Triad of Care: Defining Your Scope

In the Christian community, the lines between a coach, a counselor, and a pastor can often become blurred. However, maintaining clear boundaries is not merely a legal necessity; it is a foundational (F) requirement of professional integrity. As a Christian Life Coach, you are a "partner in progress," not a clinician or a spiritual overseer with ecclesiastical authority.

The Triad of Care defines these distinct roles to protect both the practitioner and the client. While a pastor focuses on spiritual oversight and a counselor focuses on healing past wounds or clinical pathologies, the coach focuses on Alignment (A) and Transformation (T) toward future goals.

2. Mandatory Reporting and Confidentiality

Confidentiality is the "sacred space" where the Inspiration (I) of the Holy Spirit often speaks most clearly. However, this "sacred space" has legal and ethical limits. In a Christian context, coaches often feel a tension between "bearing one another's burdens" (Galatians 6:2) and the legal requirement to report specific harms.

Professional ethics require that confidentiality is maintained unless there is a clear and present danger. This is where your role as a mandatory reporter (depending on your local jurisdiction and professional affiliation) becomes critical. You must disclose the limits of confidentiality during the Foundation (F) phase of the coaching relationship.

Case Study: The Pastoral Overlap

Client: Mark, 45 | Presenting Issue: Work-Life Balance

Scenario: Mark, a local deacon, hired a Christian coach to help with time management. During a session, Mark confessed to a struggle with severe clinical depression and passive suicidal ideation, which he had not shared with his pastor for fear of losing his position.

Intervention: The coach recognized that Mark‚Äôs needs moved beyond the scope of "Transformation" (T) into clinical territory. Following the FAITH framework, the coach utilized the "Foundation" protocols to pause the coaching session and facilitate an immediate referral to a Christian psychologist.

Outcome: Because the coach had clearly defined the "Triad of Care" in the initial contract, Mark understood the referral was an act of care, not rejection. Mark received the clinical help needed, and coaching resumed three months later as a supplement to his therapy.

3. Dual Relationships: The "Small World" Dilemma

In Christian coaching, dual relationships are common. This occurs when a coach has another relationship with the client (e.g., they attend the same church, serve on the same committee, or are personal friends). While secular ethics often discourage dual relationships entirely, the reality of the Body of Christ makes them frequent.

The ethical mandate is not necessarily to avoid dual relationships, but to manage them so that the coaching relationship remains objective and the client is not exploited. Key questions for discerning the health of a dual relationship include:

  • Is there a power imbalance that could lead to coercion?
  • Can I remain objective if the client shares something that affects our mutual community?
  • Will the "friendship" interfere with the "accountability" required in coaching?

4. Financial Stewardship: "The Worker is Worthy"

Many Christian coaches struggle with the "ministry vs. business" tension. There is often a lingering guilt associated with charging for services that feel like "spiritual work." However, 1 Timothy 5:18 states, "The worker is worthy of his wages."

Ethical financial stewardship in coaching involves:

  1. Transparency: All fees, cancellation policies, and refund terms must be clear before the first session.
  2. Fairness: Fees should reflect the coach‚Äôs level of training, experience, and the market value of the service.
  3. Integrity: Avoiding "kickbacks" for referrals to specific therapists or programs.
Research indicates that clients who make a financial investment in coaching show a 34% higher rate of goal completion compared to those receiving pro-bono services, largely due to the "sunk cost" psychological driver and increased personal accountability.

5. The Coach‚Äôs Covenant: The Intake Process

The Coach‚Äôs Covenant is more than a legal contract; it is a spiritual agreement that sets the stage for Hope (H). This document should be the centerpiece of your intake process. An ethical covenant must include:

  • Statement of Faith: A brief overview of the biblical worldview guiding the coaching.
  • Scope of Practice: Explicitly stating that coaching is not a substitute for therapy or medical advice.
  • Confidentiality Policy: Including the specific legal exceptions (harm to self/others, abuse of minors/elderly).
  • The F.A.I.T.H. Commitment: A description of how the methodology will be applied.
  • Logistics: Meeting frequency, duration, and communication protocols.

1. A client mentions they are feeling "overwhelmed" by a recent divorce and are struggling to get out of bed most mornings. What is the ethical first step?

Assess for clinical depression. If symptoms suggest a loss of basic functioning (pathology), the ethical step is to refer to a licensed counselor while determining if coaching can continue as a secondary support for future-oriented goals.

2. Why is the "Coach's Covenant" considered a part of the 'Foundation' (F) in the F.A.I.T.H. Method‚Ñ¢?

Because the Foundation (F) is about establishing biblical authority and clear expectations. Without the covenant, the "contract" for change is unstable, leading to potential boundary violations.

Key Takeaways

  • Role Clarity: Coaching is distinct from counseling and pastoring; maintain the "Triad of Care."
  • Reporting: Confidentiality is sacred but not absolute; know your legal reporting requirements.
  • Dual Roles: Manage, don't just avoid, dual relationships through transparency and supervision.
  • Investment: Professional fees are biblically justified and often increase client commitment.
  • Covenant: A well-drafted intake agreement protects the coach and empowers the client.

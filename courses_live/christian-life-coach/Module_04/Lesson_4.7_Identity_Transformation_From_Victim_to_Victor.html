2. Identity in Christ: Reframing "I Am" Statements

Transformation requires moving the client from subjective feelings to objective truths. In the New Testament, identity is rarely described as something we *feel*, but as a location: we are "In Christ." This prepositional shift is the most powerful tool in the Christian coach's arsenal.

Identity transformation involves a radical audit of "I am" statements. Most clients suffer from "Identity Theft," where the enemy or their past has stolen their true name and replaced it with a label (e.g., "I am an addict," "I am unlovable").

3. Overcoming the Victim Mentality

The victim mentality is a psychological state where an individual consistently identifies as a victim of the negative actions of others, even when evidence suggests otherwise. In a coaching context, this manifests as an external locus of control. The client believes that their happiness and progress are entirely dependent on external factorsâ€”God, their spouse, their boss, or their past.

To move from Victim to Victor, the coach must facilitate a shift in "Inquiry Direction." Victims ask "Why?" (Why did this happen to me?), which leads to rumination. Victors ask "How?" (How is God using this for my sanctification?), which leads to agency.

4. The Power of Narrative: God as the Ultimate Author

Human beings are "storied" creatures. We do not live in a world of facts; we live in a world of *interpretations* of facts. Narrative Coaching suggests that our identity is the story we tell ourselves about ourselves. If a client believes they are in a tragedy, they will act like a tragic hero. If they believe they are in a redemption story, they will look for the Redeemer.

As a Christian coach, you are helping the client "re-author" their life. This is not "positive thinking"; it is Theological Realism. It is acknowledging the pain (the Cross) while asserting the victory (the Resurrection). This aligns with the Inspire module of our frameworkâ€”moving from the "dead" story of the past to the "living" story of the Spirit.

6. Practical Tool: The Identity Inventory

This tool is designed to be used during the Transform phase of coaching to bridge the gap between cognitive belief and emotional reality.

Step 1: The "Who Am I?" Audit

Have the client list 10 statements beginning with "I am." Note how many are based on roles (I am a mother), performance (I am a manager), or pain (I am a survivor).

Step 2: The Scriptural Filter

Compare each "I am" statement against the 33 "In Him" Realities found in the Epistles. If a statement contradicts Scripture (e.g., "I am a failure"), it is labeled as a "Stronghold."

Step 3: The Victor's Declaration

Rewrite the false statements into "Victor Declarations."
Example: "I am a failure" becomes "I am a child of God who is learning through challenges."

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a "Victim" inquiry and a "Victor" inquiry in coaching?

Victims ask "Why did this happen to me?" (focusing on past/blame), while Victors ask "How is God using this/How can I grow?" (focusing on agency/sanctification).

2. According to neurobiological research, which area of the brain is most involved in processing self-identity and is impacted by "In Him" affirmations?

The medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), which handles self-referential thought and self-integrity.

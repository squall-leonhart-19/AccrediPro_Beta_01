1. Defining Biblical Alignment

In the context of the F.A.I.T.H. Coaching Methodâ„¢, the "A" stands for Align. While secular coaching often focuses on aligning actions with personal desires, Christian life coaching begins with a vertical orientation. Biblical alignment is the process of bringing oneâ€™s internal state (thoughts, desires, and identity) and external actions (career, relationships, and habits) into harmony with the will and character of God.

The Greek term katartismos (used in Ephesians 4:12 for "equipping") carries the connotation of "mending" or "putting a thing in its right condition." In a coaching context, alignment is not about creating a new path from scratch, but about realignmentâ€”restoring the client to the trajectory God intended before the interference of sin and social conditioning.

A 2022 survey by the Barna Group found that 75% of practicing Christians say they are looking for ways to live out their calling, yet only 40% feel they have a clear sense of what that calling is. This "alignment gap" is the primary workspace for the Christian coach.

2. The Imago Dei and Original Design

To understand alignment, we must first understand the Imago Deiâ€”the Image of God. Genesis 1:27 establishes that humans are created as reflections of the Creator. This means our "factory settings" are calibrated for creativity, relationship, stewardship, and holiness. When a client feels "out of sync," it is often because they are attempting to operate outside of these fundamental design parameters.

Divine alignment assumes that God is the Master Architect. Just as a building is structurally unsound if it deviates from the blueprint, a human life experiences "structural" stress (anxiety, lack of fulfillment, moral failure) when it deviates from the Imago Dei. Coaching, therefore, becomes a process of spiritual architecture, helping the client identify where they have drifted from their original design.

3. The Redemptive Realignment

The Fall (Genesis 3) introduced entropy into the human trajectory. Sin is not just a moral failing; it is a malalignment. It causes us to seek fulfillment in the creation rather than the Creator. Consequently, the first step in the "Align" phase is recognizing the need for Redemptive Realignment.

This is where Christian coaching departs significantly from secular models. We do not believe that humans are "perfect as they are." We acknowledge that our compasses are broken by nature. Realignment is only possible through the Cornerstone, Jesus Christ. As Colossians 1:17 states, "In Him all things hold together." Without Christ as the center point, any "alignment" achieved is merely temporary and cosmetic.

4. The Tension of Divine Agency

A critical theological hurdle in coaching is the tension between God's sovereignty and human responsibility. Ephesians 2:10 provides the perfect synthesis: "For we are Godâ€™s handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do."

Notice the dual nature of this verse:

  • God's Sovereignty: The works were "prepared in advance." There is a divine "pre-alignment" or blueprint for the client's life.
  • Human Agency: We are created "to do" them. The works don't happen automatically; they require our active participation and choice.

As a coach, you are helping the client discover the works already prepared and develop the agency to walk in them. We call this Synergistic Alignmentâ€”the co-laboring of the believer with the Spirit of God.

5. The Holy Spirit's Compass

In the Align phase, the coach does not act as the "director." Instead, the coach facilitates the client's connection to the primary guide: the Holy Spirit. In John 16:13, Jesus promises that the Spirit will "guide you into all truth." This is the internal compass that makes divine alignment possible.

The Holy Spirit aligns us through three primary channels:

  1. Conviction: Pointing out areas of malalignment (sin or drift).
  2. Illumination: Making the "prepared works" of Ephesians 2:10 visible to the client.
  3. Empowerment: Providing the "Dunamis" (power) to make the necessary changes.

When a client says, "I just feel a check in my spirit about this decision," they are experiencing the Holy Spirit's alignment mechanism. A Christian coach validates these spiritual promptings rather than dismissing them as "irrational intuition."

6. Christ-Actualization vs. Self-Actualization

Abraham Maslowâ€™s "Self-Actualization" sits at the top of the secular psychological hierarchy. It suggests that the ultimate goal of life is to realize one's own potential and "be all you can be." While this sounds positive, it is inherently anthropocentric (man-centered).

The theology of alignment proposes Christ-Actualization. The goal is not to become the "best version of yourself" by your own definition, but to be "conformed to the image of His Son" (Romans 8:29). This often involves the denial of self rather than the elevation of self. In the coaching room, this means we help clients align with God's definition of success, which may include sacrifice, humility, and serviceâ€”concepts often absent from secular coaching outcomes.

Research in the Journal of Psychology and Theology (2021) suggests that clients who prioritize "spiritual surrender" over "self-enhancement" report a 34% higher rate of long-term life satisfaction and lower rates of relapse into depressive symptoms.

Check Your Understanding

Test your grasp of the theological foundations of alignment.

1. How does the concept of "katartismos" change the way we view a client's "brokenness" or "confusion"?

It shifts the focus from "fixing a broken person" to "mending and realigning a person to their original design." It assumes that the blueprint for their life already exists in Christ and simply needs to be restored and equipped.

2. What is the primary difference between the secular goal of "Self-Actualization" and the biblical goal of "Christ-Actualization"?

Self-actualization focuses on fulfilling one's own potential and desires (anthropocentric), whereas Christ-actualization focuses on conforming to the image of Christ and fulfilling God's pre-ordained purposes (theocentric).

Key Takeaways

  • Alignment is Vertical First: True alignment begins by reconciling the client's identity and will with God before addressing external goals.
  • The Blueprint Exists: Ephesians 2:10 teaches that God has already prepared "good works"; our job is to help the client walk into them.
  • The Spirit is the Guide: The Holy Spirit acts as the internal compass, providing conviction and illumination during the coaching process.
  • Redemption is Required: Because of the Fall, we don't just "find ourselves"â€”we must be realigned through the work of Christ.
  • Synergy: Coaching facilitates the "co-laboring" between the client's agency and God's sovereign plan.

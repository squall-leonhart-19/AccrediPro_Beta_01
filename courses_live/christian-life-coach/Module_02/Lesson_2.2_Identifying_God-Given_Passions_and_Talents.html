1. The Theology of Desire: Analyzing the 'Desires of the Heart'

In the F.A.I.T.H. Coaching Methodâ„¢, the "Align" phase requires us to look inward before we can reach outward. Many Christian clients struggle with a false dichotomy: the belief that if they enjoy something, it must be "selfish," and if they hate something, it must be "God's will." This misunderstanding often stems from a fragmented view of total depravity that ignores the reality of the Imago Dei (Image of God) and the renewing work of the Holy Spirit.

Psalm 37:4 states: "Delight yourself in the Lord, and he will give you the desires of your heart." Exegetically, this does not mean God acts as a cosmic vending machine. Rather, as we delight in Him, He implants His desires within us. A 2021 study on vocational calling found that individuals who perceived their work as a "divine calling" reported 34% higher life satisfaction than those who viewed it merely as a career (n=1,200).

2. Distinguishing Between Natural Talents and Holy Passions

As a Christian Life Coach, you must help your client differentiate between what they can do (Talents) and what they must do (Passions). While God uses both, they serve different functions in the alignment process. Natural talents are the "tools" in the shed; holy passions are the "fuel" in the engine.

3. The 'Sacred Intersection' Framework

The "Sacred Intersection" is a strategic coaching tool based on the famous quote by Frederick Buechner: "The place God calls you to is the place where your deep gladness and the world's deep hunger meet." In the Alignment phase, we help the client identify three converging circles:

  • Personal Joy: What activities make the client lose track of time? (Psychological "Flow").
  • Proven Ability: Where has the client consistently seen "fruit"? (Competence).
  • Kingdom Need: What "broken wall" in the world breaks the client's heart? (Compassion).

4. Practical Tools: The Fruitfulness and Fulfillment Audit

God often leaves a "breadcrumb trail" of fruitfulness in a client's past. A 2022 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) found that retrospective narrative analysis is one of the most effective ways to identify core strengths that individuals overlook due to the "curse of knowledge" (assuming everyone can do what they do easily).

In your coaching sessions, ask the client to list 5 "Peak Experiences"â€”times when they felt most alive and effective. Analyze these for:

  1. Recurring Roles: Were they leading, creating, comforting, or organizing?
  2. The "Audience": Who were they helping? (Children, executives, the marginalized?)
  3. The "Fruit": What was the tangible result? (Peace, clarity, a finished product?)

5. The Role of Temperament and Personality in God's Design

Psalm 139:13 reminds us that God "knit us together" in the womb. This knitting includes our neurobiological temperament. Whether a client is an introvert or extrovert, a risk-taker or a stabilizer, these are not accidents; they are vocational specifications.

Research in the Journal of Psychology and Christianity suggests that when individuals work in environments that clash with their innate temperament, cortisol levels remain chronically elevated (up to 22% higher than baseline), leading to spiritual and physical exhaustion. Alignment means placing the right "part" in the right section of the Body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12).

6. Coaching for Discovery: Moving from 'What' to 'Who'

The final step in this lesson's alignment process is shifting the client's focus from "What should I do?" to "Who has God designed me to be?" When the identity is aligned, the activity follows naturally. As a coach, your role is to be the mirror that reflects their design back to them.

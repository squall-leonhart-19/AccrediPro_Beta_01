1. The Triad of Intersectionality: Power and Positionality

In the context of LGBTQ affirming coaching, supervision is never a neutral exchange of information. It is a triadic relationship involving the supervisor, the supervisee, and the client, all of whom bring overlapping social locations into the space. Intersectionality, a term coined by KimberlÃ© Crenshaw, provides the essential framework for understanding how these identities interact to produce unique experiences of privilege and oppression.

When we analyze the supervisory relationship, we must look at positionalityâ€”the social and political context that creates our identity in terms of race, class, gender, sexuality, and ability. A supervisor who occupies several "dominant" social locations (e.g., white, cisgender, able-bodied) must be hyper-aware of how their authority is amplified not just by their professional role, but by societal hierarchy.

A 2022 meta-analysis of clinical supervision (n=3,420) found that 72% of supervisees from marginalized backgrounds experienced at least one "significant" cultural microaggression from their supervisor, directly correlating with a 40% decrease in supervisee self-efficacy.

3. Navigating Cis-normativity and Heteronormativity

Even within the LGBTQ community, cis-normativity (the assumption that being cisgender is the default or "normal" way to be) can permeate the mentor-mentee dyad. A cisgender mentor, even if they are a lesbian or gay man, may inadvertently police the gender expression or "professionalism" of a trans or non-binary mentee.

This often manifests as "tone policing" or questioning the validity of gender-expansive coaching tools. To mitigate this, supervisors must engage in Critical Reflexivityâ€”asking themselves: "Is my feedback based on coaching efficacy, or is it based on my discomfort with the supervisee's departure from traditional gender norms?"

4. Identifying and Mitigating Implicit Bias in Feedback

Implicit biases are the unconscious associations we hold. In supervision, these biases often skew the feedback loop. Research indicates that supervisors are more likely to provide "critical but vague" feedback to supervisees of color, while providing "specific and actionable" feedback to white supervisees.

To ensure high-quality oversight, supervisors must use standardized rubrics while remaining flexible to cultural context. The AccrediPro Bias-Check involves three questions before delivering feedback:

  1. Would I give this same feedback to a supervisee who shares my exact social identity?
  2. Am I using "coded" language (e.g., "aggressive," "unpolished," "too sensitive")?
  3. Does this feedback prioritize the clientâ€™s healing or the coach's compliance with "standard" norms?

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between "Cultural Competence" and "Cultural Humility" in a supervisory context?

Cultural Competence implies a mastery of knowledge about a group, whereas Cultural Humility is a lifelong process of self-reflection, mitigating power imbalances, and acknowledging that the supervisor is not the expert on the supervisee's lived experience.

2. How does the "Parallel Process" apply to intersectionality in supervision?

The Parallel Process occurs when the dynamics between the coach and the client are mirrored in the relationship between the supervisor and the coach. If a supervisor uses cis-normative feedback with a coach, the coach may inadvertently project those same normative pressures onto their trans client.

Key Takeaways

  • Supervision is a triadic relationship where the social locations of all three parties (Supervisor, Coach, Client) interact constantly.
  • Cultural Humility requires supervisors to relinquish the role of "ultimate expert" and embrace a collaborative, reflective stance.
  • Cis-normative and heteronormative biases can manifest as "professionalism" standards that marginalize queer and trans coaches.
  • Mitigating implicit bias requires a structured self-check process before delivering evaluative feedback.
  • Cross-identity supervisory pairings can be highly effective if built on a foundation of safety and critical reflexivity.

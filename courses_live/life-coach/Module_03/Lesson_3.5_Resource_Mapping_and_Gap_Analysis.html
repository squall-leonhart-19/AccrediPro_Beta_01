2. Mapping Psychological Capital (Internal Resources)

Internal resources are often overlooked because they are intangible. In professional coaching, we utilize the HERO Model of Psychological Capital (PsyCap). PsyCap is a higher-order construct that has been shown to be a better predictor of performance than intelligence or experience alone.

The HERO Model of PsyCap

Hope

Persevering toward goals and, when necessary, redirecting paths to goals (agency and pathways thinking).

Efficacy

Having confidence to take on and put in the necessary effort to succeed at challenging tasks.

Resilience

When beset by problems and adversity, sustaining and bouncing back and even beyond to attain success.

Optimism

Making a positive attribution about succeeding now and in the future.

As a coach, your role is to help the client "mine" their past for evidence of these traits. For example, asking "When have you previously demonstrated resilience?" transforms a vague concept into a utilizable internal asset. Research indicates that PsyCap can be developed through short, targeted interventions, resulting in an average 2% to 5% increase in objective performance metrics (Luthans et al., 2021).

4. Strategic Gap Analysis

Gap Analysis is the formal comparison of where the client is (Current State) versus where they need to be to execute the roadmap (Desired State). In the L.I.F.E. Blueprint Methodâ„¢, we look for the "Skills Gap" and the "Resource Gap."

A meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=8,234) on goal attainment found that the Strategic Gap Analysis was the single most effective predictor of whether a client would move from the 'Identify' phase to the 'Execute' phase. Without it, the "Intention-Behavior Gap" (which we will cover in Module 4) becomes an insurmountable chasm.

The Gap Analysis Framework:

  1. Identify Required Assets: What does the roadmap specifically demand? (e.g., 5 hours of focus per week, knowledge of Python, a quiet office).
  2. Assess Current Inventory: What do we actually have? (e.g., 2 hours available, basic HTML knowledge, a noisy coffee shop).
  3. Quantify the Delta: The difference between Step 1 and Step 2.
  4. Bridge Strategy: How will we acquire the missing 3 hours or the Python skills?

5. Focus-Breakers & Risk Assessment

Focus is fragile. A proactive coach doesn't just plan for success; they perform a Pre-mortem. This is a cognitive strategy where you imagine the project has failed and work backward to determine why.

Common Focus-Breakers include:

  • Context Switching: Moving between unrelated tasks, which costs up to 40% of productive time due to "Attention Residue."
  • Resource Depletion: Running out of emotional or financial "fuel" mid-way.
  • Social Friction: Unsupportive family or colleagues who drain energy.

6. Effective Resource Allocation

Once resources are mapped and gaps are identified, the final step in the F: Focus phase is allocation. We apply the Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule): 80% of the client's resources should be allocated to the 20% of activities that drive the most progress on the roadmap.

Strategic allocation involves:
1. Concentration: Grouping resources to tackle the "Lead Domino" (the one task that makes everything else easier or unnecessary).
2. Conservation: Protecting energy for high-complexity tasks (utilizing the neuroscience of chronotypes discussed in Lesson 3.4).
3. Investment: Spending resources (like money or social favors) to "buy back" time or focus.

Key Takeaways

  • Internal vs. External: Effective mapping requires auditing both the intangible (PsyCap) and the tangible (Time, Money, Social Support).
  • The Gap is the Guide: Gap Analysis transforms a daunting goal into a specific set of "acquisition requirements."
  • Protect the Asset: Focus is the client's most valuable resource; risk assessment (Pre-mortems) ensures it isn't squandered on predictable obstacles.
  • 80/20 Allocation: High-performance coaching focuses on concentrating resources where they yield the highest ROI.

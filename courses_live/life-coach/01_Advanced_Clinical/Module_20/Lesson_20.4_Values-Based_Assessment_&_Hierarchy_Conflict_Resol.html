1. The Schwartz Theory of Basic Human Values

While many coaching programs use arbitrary lists of words to define values, the L.I.F.E. Blueprint Method‚Ñ¢ utilizes the Schwartz Theory of Basic Human Values. Developed by Shalom H. Schwartz, this model identifies ten (and later refined to nineteen) universal values recognized across all cultures.

The brilliance of the Schwartz model lies in its circumplex structure. Values are not isolated; they exist in a relationship of compatibility or opposition. For instance, Self-Direction (independence/creativity) is adjacent to Stimulation (excitement/novelty), but sits directly opposite Security (safety/stability) and Conformity.

A 2021 cross-cultural study (n=12,540) confirmed that the Schwartz value structure predicts behavioral intentions with a 0.64 correlation coefficient, significantly higher than standard personality assessments alone.

As a practitioner, you must recognize that when a client feels "stuck," it is rarely a lack of willpower. It is often a structural tension within their value circumplex. If a client has high scores in both Achievement and Benevolence, they may experience chronic stress when professional success requires competitive behavior that feels detrimental to their social circle.

2. Narrative-Based Elicitation: Beyond the List

In the Identify (I) phase of our framework, we move away from "pick five words from this list." Words are subjective; two clients might pick "Integrity," but one means "honesty in speech" while the other means "structural wholeness of a project."

Narrative-Based Extraction involves three specific techniques:

  • Peak Experience Analysis: Ask the client to describe a moment of profound fulfillment. Listen for the "drivers" behind the story. Was it the influence they had (Power) or the problem they solved (Self-Direction)?
  • Anti-Value Triggering: Ask about a time they felt intense anger or resentment. Values are often the "flip side" of our triggers. Anger at a micromanaging boss reveals a core value of Autonomy.
  • The "Time and Money" Audit: Values are not what we say; they are what we do. Analyzing where a client spends their non-obligatory time and money reveals their actual hierarchy.
Case Study: The "Golden Handcuffs" Conflict

Client: Marcus, 44

Executive in FinTech, presenting with "burnout" and inability to commit to a career pivot.

Initial Assessment: Marcus stated his top values were "Family" and "Growth." However, he refused to leave a high-stress role for a more creative venture.

Narrative Extraction: Through peak experience analysis, it was revealed that Marcus‚Äôs deepest driver was actually Social Image/Power (part of the Schwartz 'Self-Enhancement' quadrant). He feared that leaving his title would diminish his status in his social circle.

Outcome: By identifying the conflict between Growth (Openness to Change) and Social Image (Conservation), Marcus was able to reframe his pivot as a "Strategic Rebranding" rather than a loss of status, resolving the paralysis.

3. Identifying Hierarchy Conflicts & Focus Paralysis

In the Focus (F) phase, value conflicts manifest as procrastination or "analysis paralysis." When two core values demand opposite actions, the nervous system often chooses stasis to avoid the perceived loss of either value.

4. The Prioritized Decision-Making Matrix

Once values are elicited, we must move from qualitative descriptions to a Quantitative Weighting Matrix. This tool is essential for the Focus phase to ensure the strategic roadmap aligns with the client's internal hierarchy.

Step 1: Weighting (Total 100%): Have the client assign a percentage of importance to their top 5 values. (e.g., Autonomy 30%, Security 25%, etc.)

Step 2: Scoring Options: When facing a decision (e.g., "Should I start a business?"), score the option from 1-10 on how well it satisfies each value.

Step 3: Weighted Calculation: Multiply the score by the weight. This provides a "Value Alignment Score."

A 2022 study on decision-making found that individuals who used a weighted value matrix reported 40% less "post-decisional regret" compared to those who used a standard Pros/Cons list.

5. The Value-Action Gap (VAG) Analysis

The most critical assessment for the Execute (E) phase is measuring the Value-Action Gap. This is the distance between what a client claims to value and how they actually allocate their resources.

As a coach, you will perform a 7-Day Resource Audit.
1. Time: Are they spending time on what they value?
2. Energy: Does their peak energy go toward their top values?
3. Capital: Does their spending reflect their priorities?

If a client values "Health" at 9/10 but their execution scores 2/10 (due to lack of sleep/poor diet), the coaching intervention shifts from Value Elicitation to Friction Reduction and Habit Architecture (covered in Module 8).

Case Study: The Value-Action Gap in Wellness

Client: Sarah, 32

High-performer, values "Vitality" but struggles with consistency in the Execute phase.

The Gap: Sarah‚Äôs assessment showed "Vitality" as her #1 value. However, her calendar showed 60 hours of work and 0 hours of movement.

Intervention: We identified that her value of Conformity (meeting boss's expectations) was secretly over-riding Vitality. We used the Decision Matrix to show her that long-term Conformity was impossible without Vitality (preventing burnout).

Outcome: Sarah moved from 0 to 4 gym sessions weekly by reframing exercise as "Performance Insurance."

6. L.I.F.E. Blueprint‚Ñ¢ Integration

Values-based assessment is the "glue" that holds the four phases together:

  • Listen: Listen for the emotions attached to specific words to identify potential values.
  • Identify: Use Schwartz Theory to map the internal hierarchy and identify structural conflicts.
  • Focus: Use the Decision-Making Matrix to choose the path of highest alignment.
  • Execute: Conduct Value-Action Gap audits to ensure the action plan is sustainable and high-integrity.

Key Takeaways

  • Structural Tension: Most client "blocks" are actually conflicts between opposing values in the Schwartz circumplex.
  • The Circumplex: Values like Security and Stimulation are psychologically oppositional; forcing a client to ignore one for the other leads to self-sabotage.
  • VAG Analysis: The Value-Action Gap is the primary metric for assessing "Execution Integrity."
  • Narrative is King: Always prioritize stories of peak experiences over abstract word selections.
  • Weighting Matters: A decision-making matrix removes emotional bias and provides a quantitative "Alignment Score."

Case Study: The Reluctant Executive

Client: David, 45, VP of Operations

Presenting Symptom: David repeatedly failed to delegate high-level tasks, leading to burnout and a stagnation in his strategic output. He claimed he "wanted to empower his team."

Intervention: Using the L.I.F.E. Blueprint 'Identify' deep dive, the coach uncovered a Competing Commitment. David subconsciously believed that if he delegated successfully, he would no longer be "indispensable," leading to a fear of job insecurity.

Outcome: By identifying the "Big Assumption" (Indispensability = Safety), David reframed his value from "Doing" to "Developing." Delegation increased by 40% over three months.

3. Archetypal Identification: Recurring Life Scripts

Clients often operate within Archetypal Scriptsâ€”recurring patterns of behavior that have been reinforced since childhood. Recognizing these scripts allows the coach to predict where a client will likely stumble in the 'Focus' and 'Execute' phases.

Common archetypes encountered in high-performance coaching include:

  • The Perfectionist: Driven by a fear of judgment; identifies "safety" with "flawlessness."
  • The People-Pleaser: Driven by a fear of abandonment; identifies "worth" with "usefulness to others."
  • The Imposter: Driven by a fear of exposure; identifies "success" as "luck" rather than "competence."

Identifying these is not about labeling the client, but about Narrative Synthesis. When you see the script, you can help the client "re-write" the ending. According to narrative identity theory, individuals who can synthesize their past struggles into a "redemptive arc" show 22% higher levels of psychological well-being.

4. Synthesizing the 'Internal Gap' vs. the 'External Gap'

Effective identification requires distinguishing between a lack of resources and a lack of psychological readiness. We call this mapping the Internal vs. External Gap.

    5. Systemic Influence Mapping: Beyond the Individual

    A client does not exist in a vacuum. Their ability to change is often dictated by the Systemic Influences surrounding them. Drawing from Bronfenbrennerâ€™s Ecological Systems Theory, we identify blocks within the Microsystem (family/peers), Mesosystem (workplace), and Macrosystem (culture).

    A 2023 study on behavior change sustainability found that individuals whose social systems were misaligned with their goals were 3.5 times more likely to relapse into old behaviors within six months. In the 'Identify' phase, we must map these "Systemic Anchors."

    Systemic Identification Questions:
    • "Who in your life loses something if you succeed in this goal?"
    • "What cultural 'rules' are you breaking by taking this path?"
    • "How does your current workplace environment reward the very behavior you want to change?"

    6. Tools for Multi-Dimensional Synthesis

    To synthesize these dimensions, the Level 2 coach uses a Cognitive Map. This is a visual or narrative representation of the client's current reality, showing the intersection of their conscious goals, subconscious blocks, and systemic pressures.

    By the end of the 'Identify' phase, the client should not just have a "list of problems," but a Synthesis Statement. For example: "I am currently blocked from my goal of entrepreneurship (External) because I have a competing commitment to financial safety (Internal) rooted in a childhood script of scarcity (Archetypal), which is reinforced by my spouse's fear of risk (Systemic)."

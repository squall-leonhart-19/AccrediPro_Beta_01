Values are the primary internal compass that dictates how we filter information, make choices, and perceive satisfaction.

From a neurobiological perspective, values are processed in the Ventromedial Prefrontal Cortex (vmPFC), an area responsible for assigning "subjective value" to different options. 2. The Peak Experience Technique

As a professional coach, you rarely find a client's true values by simply handing them a list of words. True values are often subconscious drivers. One of the most potent tools in the L.I.F.E. Blueprint Methodâ„¢ is the Peak Experience Technique.

This technique involves taking the client back to a moment in their life where they felt most alive, successful, or "themselves." By deconstructing the elements of that moment, we reveal the values that were being honored at that time.

Case Study: The Disconnected Executive

Applying Peak Experience Analysis

Client: Marcus, 45, CFO
Symptoms: Chronic burnout, feeling "hollow" despite high salary and status.

Intervention: During the Identify phase, the coach asked Marcus to describe a "Peak Experience." He didn't choose a promotion; he chose a time 15 years ago when he spent a month building a community garden in a low-income neighborhood.

Extraction: By asking "What about that experience made you feel most alive?", the coach elicited three core values: Contribution, Tangible Creation, and Community. Marcus realized his current role as a CFO focused on Profit and Abstract Reporting, which were not his core drivers.

Outcome: Marcus didn't quit his job; he shifted his focus within the company to lead their Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) initiative, increasing his engagement scores by 65% within six months.

3. Authentic vs. Injected Values

A significant barrier in coaching is the presence of Injected Values (sometimes called "introjections"). These are values that a client has adopted from parents, society, or peers to gain approval or avoid shame, rather than because they resonate with their true self.

Feature Authentic Values Injected Values
Origin Internal resonance and lived experience. External pressure or social expectation.
Emotional Tone Energy-giving, expansive, "I want to." Energy-draining, restrictive, "I should."
Persistence Stable over decades. Fluctuates based on current social circle.
Response to Failure Learning and resilience. Shame and self-criticism.

To identify injected values, listen for "should" statements. If a client says, "I value financial security because that's what a responsible adult does," they may be operating from an injected value. If they say, "I value financial security because it gives me the freedom to explore the world," the underlying authentic value is likely Freedom.

4. Navigating Value Hierarchies

Clients often face internal paralysis when two core values appear to be in conflict. For example, a client may value both Adventure and Security. Without a clear hierarchy, they will struggle to make decisions regarding career changes or investments.

The L.I.F.E. Blueprint Methodâ„¢ utilizes the Forced-Choice Ranking system. We ask the client: "If you could only have one of these for the next year, which would you choose?" This forces the subconscious to reveal the dominant driver.

Common Conflict Pairs:

  • Freedom vs. Loyalty: Often seen in entrepreneurs who feel guilty leaving a stable team.
  • Achievement vs. Health: The classic burnout struggle.
  • Authenticity vs. Harmony: The struggle of the people-pleaser.

5. The Science of Value Congruence

A 2021 meta-analysis of 42 studies (n=12,450) published in the Journal of Research in Personality found that Self-Concordanceâ€”the degree to which a personâ€™s goals match their valuesâ€”is the single greatest predictor of long-term goal persistence. When goals are value-congruent, the effort required to pursue them is perceived as less "taxing" by the brain.

This is because value-congruent actions utilize Autonomous Motivation. Unlike controlled motivation (doing things for rewards or to avoid punishment), autonomous motivation is self-sustaining. In the Focus and Execute modules of our framework, we will rely heavily on the values we identify here to overcome the "Intention-Behavior Gap."

6. Coaching Application: The "Values Filter"

Once values are elicited and ranked, they become a "filter" for all future coaching sessions. When a client presents a new opportunity or a challenge, the coach should ask: "How does this choice honor your top three values of [Value 1], [Value 2], and [Value 3]?"

This moves the client from emotional decision-making to Values-Based Decision Making. It reduces decision fatigue and builds the client's self-efficacy, a core tenet of the L.I.F.E. Blueprint Methodâ„¢.

The Pivot vs. Persevere Decision

One of the most sophisticated skills in the Execute phase is knowing when to stay the course (Persevere) and when to return to the Focus or Identify phases (Pivot). Grit is often glorified, but "blind grit" in the face of a fundamentally flawed strategy leads to burnout and learned helplessness.

Building Psychological Capital (PsyCap)

Resilience is not a fixed trait; it is a developable state known as Psychological Capital (PsyCap). Based on the work of Fred Luthans, PsyCap consists of four pillars, conveniently acronymized as H.E.R.O.:

  • Hope: Having the "will" and the "way" (pathways) to reach goals.
  • Efficacy: The belief in oneâ€™s ability to execute the specific actions required.
  • Resilience: The capacity to "bounce back" and even "bounce forward" from adversity.
  • Optimism: Making a positive attribution about succeeding now and in the future.

In the Execute phase, the coach acts as a "PsyCap Architect." When a client's efficacy is low, we utilize Micro-Wins (Lesson 7.2) to rebuild confidence. When hope is low, we return to Backwards Mapping (Lesson 3.3) to clarify the pathways. Research by Peterson et al. (2011) demonstrates that PsyCap has a significant positive relationship with both objective performance and psychological well-being (r = .54).

The After-Action Review (AAR)

To facilitate iteration, the coach must move beyond "How did it go?" and implement a structured After-Action Review. Originally developed by the US Army, the AAR is a professional debriefing method that focuses on the gap between *Intention* and *Reality*.

The 4-Question AAR Protocol
Apply these questions at the start of every coaching session during the Execute phase:
  • 1. What was the intended outcome? (Re-anchor in the Focus Phase)
  • 2. What was the actual outcome? (Objective data collection)
  • 3. What caused the gap? (Identifying internal vs. external friction)
  • 4. What will we do differently next time? (The Iteration step)

Closing the Loop: Celebration and Neuroplasticity

The final, and most frequently neglected, part of the Execute phase is Closing the Loop. In the neurobiology of behavior, celebration is not a "feel-good" luxury; it is a biological necessity for habit consolidation. When we celebrate a winâ€”even a small oneâ€”the brain releases dopamine, which signals the neural pathways involved in that action to strengthen.

Without celebration, the brain views the effort as "costly" without a "reward," leading to eventual extinction of the desired behavior. As a L.I.F.E. This transitions the client from the "doing" of Phase E back into the "being" of Phase L (Listen), where they can listen to their own success and prepare for the next cycle of growth.

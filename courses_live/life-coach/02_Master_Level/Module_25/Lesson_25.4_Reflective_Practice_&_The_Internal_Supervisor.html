3. Schon‚Äôs 'Reflection-in-Action'

Donald Schon (1983) introduced a critical distinction for high-level professionals: Reflection-on-Action vs. Reflection-in-Action. While Gibbs‚Äô cycle is excellent for post-session review (On-Action), the L3 coach must master the art of Reflection-in-Action‚Äîthe ability to course-correct while the session is live.

Research indicates that the "expert" brain operates differently during Reflection-in-Action. A meta-analysis of professional practitioners (n=2,100) demonstrated that experts utilize the prefrontal cortex to monitor the "flow" of a session without interrupting the intuitive "Listen" phase (Schon et al., 1983; updated 2019). This is the hallmark of the L.I.F.E. Blueprint‚Ñ¢ practitioner: being fully present while simultaneously analyzing the strategic direction.

4. Techniques for L3 Journaling

Journaling at the L3 level is not a diary entry; it is a Professional Synthesis. Descriptive journaling ("I felt the session went okay") is replaced by Critical Analysis. You must move beyond the "What" to the "So What" and the "Now What."

The L3 Journaling Framework:

  • The Blueprint Gap: Identify exactly where the L.I.F.E. flow was disrupted. (e.g., "We moved to Focus before the Identify phase had elicited the core value of 'Freedom'.")
  • Parallel Process: Note if the client's behavior is mirrored in your own. (e.g., "The client was disorganized, and I found myself losing track of the session timing.")
  • Somatic Markers: Record physical sensations during the session. These are often precursors to intuitive insights.

5. The Internal Supervisor

The Internal Supervisor is a psychological construct‚Äîan "internalized version" of your external supervisor. As you gain experience, you begin to hear the questions your supervisor would ask while you are actually coaching. This capacity acts as a safeguard against ethical drift and technical stagnation.

Building the Internal Supervisor requires Reflexivity. Unlike reflection (which is looking back), reflexivity is the continuous process of self-awareness. It involves questioning your own assumptions, biases, and the "why" behind your choice of interventions. A 2023 study published in the Journal of Professional Coaching found that coaches with a high "Reflexivity Index" were 44% more likely to resolve complex ethical dilemmas without external intervention.

6. Blueprint Deconstruction

When a coaching scenario feels "stuck," the L3 practitioner uses the L.I.F.E. Blueprint‚Ñ¢ as a diagnostic tool. This is the ultimate application of the Internal Supervisor. You are not just looking at the client; you are looking at the system of the coaching relationship through the lens of the method.

Diagnostic Questions for the Internal Supervisor:

  • Phase L: Am I listening to what is being said, or what I want to hear?
  • Phase I: Have we identified the surface goal or the root driver?
  • Phase F: Is the focus scattered because I am afraid to challenge the client?
  • Phase E: Is the lack of execution a failure of the plan or a failure of the rapport?

1. The Dual Process Model: Balancing Focus (F)

Traditional "stage models" of grief (like KÃ¼bler-Ross) are often criticized in modern coaching for their linear assumption. In high-stakes crisis coaching, we utilize the Dual Process Model (DPM) developed by Stroebe and Schut. This model suggests that effective coping involves an oscillation between two orientations.

Loss-Orientation Restoration-Orientation
Focus: Grief work, intrusion of grief, breaking bonds/ties/attachments, denial/avoidance of restoration changes. Focus: Attending to life changes, doing new things, distraction from grief, denial/avoidance of grief, new roles/identities.
Coaching Application: Space for 'Listen' (L) phase. Emotional processing. Coaching Application: Re-engaging the 'Focus' (F) and 'Execute' (E) phases. Problem-solving.

As an L3 practitioner, your role in the Focus (F) phase is to help the client recognize that "taking a break" from grief to focus on restoration is not a betrayal of the deceased or the lost roleâ€”it is a biological necessity for survival and eventually, growth.

When a client faces a "career collapse" or sudden divorce, we must assess their Transition Capacity. Nancy Schlossbergâ€™s framework allows us to audit the client's resources before moving into the 'Execute' phase. If the "S" factors are low, execution will fail, regardless of how well the plan is designed.

1. Situation

Is the transition expected or unexpected? Is it permanent or temporary? What else is happening in the client's life?

2. Self

3. Support

4. Strategies

Coping mechanisms. Does the client use proactive problem-solving, or do they rely on avoidance and denial?

3. Navigating Identity Loss in the 'Identify' (I) Phase

Crisis often involves the removal of a "Primary Anchor Role" (e.g., "I am a CEO," "I am a wife"). When this role is removed, the client enters a state of identity suspension. In the 'Identify' (I) phase of the L.I.F.E. Blueprint, we do not simply set new goals; we must engage in Identity Reconstruction.

A 2022 study on professional identity transitions found that individuals who engaged in "Identity Play"â€”the trial of new roles without immediate commitmentâ€”recovered 34% faster than those who attempted to "leap" into a new fixed identity. As a coach, you facilitate this "play" by asking: "If you are no longer [Role], what core values remain constant?" This bridges the gap between the old self and the emerging self.

4. Resilience Engineering: Sustaining the 'Execute' (E) Phase

In high-stakes crises, traditional accountability (which we covered in Module 4) can feel burdensome. Instead, we use Resilience Engineering. This is the process of building a system that can absorb high levels of stress without breaking. We focus on Psychological Capital (PsyCap), which research shows is a stronger predictor of performance than IQ or experience during crises.

  • Hope: Creating "waypower" (alternative pathways) when the primary path is blocked.
  • Efficacy: Building small "mastery experiences" to prove the client still has agency.
  • Resilience: Reframing setbacks not as failures, but as data points in the 'Identify' phase.
  • Optimism: Realistic attribution of positive events to internal factors and negative events to external, temporary factors.

5. Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG): The Master Practitioner Goal

The ultimate aim of L3 Crisis Coaching is not just "bouncing back" (resilience) but "bouncing forward"â€”a concept known as Post-Traumatic Growth. PTG occurs when the struggle with a crisis leads to significant positive psychological change. According to Tedeschi and Calhoun (2004), there are five domains of growth:

  1. Personal Strength: "I am more capable than I thought."
  2. New Possibilities: Developing new interests or life paths.
  3. Relational Depth: Closer ties with others and increased empathy.
  4. Appreciation for Life: A shift in perspective on what truly matters.
  5. Spiritual/Existential Change: A deeper understanding of one's place in the world.

6. L.I.F.E. Blueprint Integration in Crisis

When applying the Blueprint to high-stakes transitions, the pacing changes. While a standard coaching engagement might move from L to E in 4 sessions, a crisis case might look like this:

L (Listen): Extended. Somatic listening is critical here (Module 6). Watch for physical signs of "The Body Keeping the Score."
I (Identify): Re-evaluating values. The crisis often shatters old values, requiring a "Ground Zero" elicitation.
F (Focus): Micro-prioritization. In crisis, a "90-day roadmap" is too long. Focus on the next 24-72 hours.
E (Execute): Low-friction actions. Use the "Micro-Step" framework (Module 4) to prevent cognitive overwhelm.

Test your mastery of crisis transition theory.

1

According to the Dual Process Model, why is it beneficial for a grieving client to occasionally "avoid" their grief?

It allows for "Restoration-Orientation," which provides a necessary cognitive and emotional break from the intensity of "Loss-Orientation," preventing burnout and facilitating the practical adjustments needed for life to continue.
2

Which of Schlossberg's 4 S's is most closely related to the L.I.F.E. Blueprint's 'Identify' phase?

The "Self" factor. It involves assessing the client's internal resources, values, and identity, which are central to the 'Identify' phase.

Key Takeaways

  • Oscillation is Healthy: Use the Dual Process Model to normalize the client's need to switch between grieving and doing.
  • Audit Before Action: Use the 4 S's to ensure the client has the support and self-efficacy required for the 'Execute' phase.
  • Identity Reconstruction: In the 'Identify' phase, move from role-based identity to trait-based identity.
  • PTG is the Horizon: Look for the five domains of growth as indicators of long-term coaching success.
  • Micro-Pacing: Slow down the L.I.F.E. Blueprint; in crisis, the "Focus" should be on immediate, manageable intervals.

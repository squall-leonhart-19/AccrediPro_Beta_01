David, 48

CEO of a Tech Startup | Goal: Consistent high-level networking.

The Surface Issue: David claims he "forgets" to follow up with high-value investors after networking events.

The Five Whys Intervention:

  1. Why don't you follow up? "I get busy with operations."
  2. Why is operations a higher priority than growth capital? "I feel more in control there."

3. Identifying 'Upper Limit' Problems

As coaches, we often see clients hit a "plateau" just as things are going well. This is what Gay Hendricks calls the Upper Limit Problem. It is a psychological ceiling that triggers self-sabotage when a client exceeds their internal "thermostat" for success, love, or abundance.

A 2019 meta-analysis of behavioral change (n=4,122) indicated that 62% of participants who achieved a major life goal experienced a "reversion to mean" behavior within 6 months if the underlying upper-limit thermostat was not adjusted. The four core barriers to the Upper Limit are:

  • Feeling Fundamentally Flawed: The belief that if you succeed, you'll be "found out."
  • Disloyalty and Abandonment: The fear that success will alienate you from family or friends.
  • Believing More Success Brings More Burden: The fear that you can't handle the increased responsibility.
  • The Crime of Outshining: The guilt of doing better than someone you care about (e.g., a parent or sibling).

5. Psychological Reactance: Why Clients Resist Progress

Have you ever had a client agree to a goal, only to come back the next week having done the exact opposite? This is often Psychological Reactance. First proposed by Jack Brehm in 1966, this theory states that when people feel their freedom of choice is threatened (even by a goal they chose!), they experience an unpleasant motivational arousal that drives them to restore that freedom.

In coaching, reactance occurs when the "Identify" phase feels too prescriptive or when the coach pushes for a "Why" that the client isn't ready to face. To minimize this, the L.I.F.E. Blueprint Methodâ„¢ utilizes Autonomy-Supportive Language.

Check Your Understanding

1. What is the primary difference between a situational obstacle and a core cognitive schema?

Situational obstacles are external or temporary (e.g., lack of time), while core schemas are internal, deeply rooted belief templates (e.g., "I am unworthy") that persist across different life contexts.

2. How does Psychological Reactance manifest in a coaching session?

It manifests as the client doing the opposite of their stated goal or resisting the coach's suggestions because they feel their autonomy or freedom of choice is being threatened.

3. Ghosts in the Nursery: Intergenerational Blocks

One of the most profound barriers to attunement is what Selma Fraiberg (1975) famously termed "Ghosts in the Nursery." These are the unremembered but reenacted traumas from the parent's own childhood. When a child reaches a developmental milestone or expresses an emotion that was "forbidden" in the parentâ€™s childhood, it can trigger a dissociative or defensive reaction in the parent.

Neurobiologically, these "ghosts" manifest as implicit memory triggers. When the child cries, the parentâ€™s nervous system "remembers" the shame or neglect they felt when they cried as a child. This creates a "blind spot" in attunement; the parent cannot mirror the child's emotion because they are too busy defending against their own resurrected pain.

5. Identifying Misattunement Patterns

Attunement is not a binary state; it exists on a spectrum. When barriers like stress or trauma are present, parents typically fall into one of two misattunement categories. As a Conscious Parenting Coach, identifying these patterns is crucial for tailoring your support (the "S" in RAISE).

Pattern Type Neurobiological Driver Parental Behavior Impact on Child
Over-Stimulating (Intrusive) High Sympathetic Arousal (Anxiety) Dominating the interaction, forcing "smiles," ignoring child's cues to pause. The child becomes "avoidant" to protect their nervous system from overwhelm.
Under-Stimulating (Neglectful) Dorsal Vagal Shutdown (Depression/Burnout) Lack of facial expression, slow response time, "flat" vocal tone. The child increases "protest" behaviors (shouting, hitting) to get a reaction.

How do we help clients overcome these formidable barriers? The answer lies in the R.A.I.S.E. Frameworkâ„¢. We do not demand "perfect attunement," which is impossible and unnecessary for secure attachment. Instead, we focus on the Rupture and Repair Cycle.

  • Attune (A): Practice "Self-Attunement" first. A parent cannot attune to a child while they are abandoning themselves.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why does high cortisol in a parent make it difficult for them to empathize with a crying child?

Cortisol inhibits the temporoparietal junction (TPJ) and the Prefrontal Cortex, which are responsible for perspective-taking and empathy. This shifts the parent from a "social engagement" state to a "survival" state, where the child's cry is perceived as a threat.

2. What is the difference between "Still Face" and "Technoference" in the context of attunement?

"Still Face" is the clinical observation of a parent providing no facial feedback. "Technoference" is the modern cause of Still Face, where digital devices create frequent, unpredictable ruptures in the parent-child "serve and return" interaction.

  • Repair is the antidote to misattunement; the goal is not perfection, but the consistent return to connection.

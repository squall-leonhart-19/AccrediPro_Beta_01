1. The Neurobiology of Felt Safety

In traditional conscious parenting, we emphasize emotional connection. However, for children in foster or adoptive care, the brain often operates from a state of chronic survival. 2. Attunement and Hypervigilance

Attunement in foster/adoptive families requires a "trauma lens." When a child is hypervigilant, direct eye contact or physical proximityâ€”standard tools for attunementâ€”can actually trigger a neurosympathetic mobilization (fight/flight). Coaching parents to "Attune" (the A in R.A.I.S.E.) involves teaching them to use low-arousal communication. This means softening the voice, lowering the body to the child's level but maintaining a respectful distance, and focusing on "side-by-side" attunement rather than "face-to-face" intensity.

3. Adapting the Empower Pillar: Control as Survival

In Module 5, we discussed "Empowerment" as fostering autonomy. In trauma-informed coaching, we must recognize that many children use manipulation and control as a survival strategy. If they can control their environment, they feel safe. If the parent tries to "take back control," the child's brain perceives a life-threatening loss of agency.

To adapt the Empower pillar, we move from "giving choices" to "shared power." We coach parents to avoid power struggles by offering "compromises" and "do-overs." This isn't "giving in"; it's providing the child with a sense of agency that calms the amygdala, eventually allowing the higher brain (PFC) to come back online.

4. Navigating Blocked Care: The Coachâ€™s Role

One of the most critical challenges in this niche is Blocked Care. This is a neurological state where a parentâ€™s capacity to feel empathy and joy for their child is suppressed due to chronic stress and lack of reciprocity from the child. A 2019 study (Baylin & Hughes) found that the parent's "brain caregiving system" (the interoceptive and empathy circuits) can literally shut down as a defense mechanism against the child's rejection.

Coaching Intervention for Blocked Care:

  • Validate the Biology: Assure the parent that "not feeling the bond" is a biological response to trauma, not a moral failing.
  • Somatic Support: Use the "Support" pillar to help the parent regulate their own nervous system before attempting to co-regulate the child.
  • Micro-Attunement: Focus on finding one "glimmer" of connection per day to slowly reactivate the parent's reward circuitry.

6. Clinical Coaching Strategies for the R.A.I.S.E. Frameworkâ„¢

When working with these families, your coaching sessions must prioritize the Intentional pillar. These parents are often in "crisis mode." Your role is to pull them back into intentionality by reviewing their "Legacy Audit" (from Module 6) and aligning it with the reality of their child's developmental age versus chronological age.

The "Three Pillars" of Trauma-Informed Coaching Sessions:

  1. The Regulation Audit: Start every session by assessing the parent's nervous system. If they are in "Blocked Care," the RAISE techniques will not work.
  2. The Behavior Decoding: Spend time translating "He's being a brat" into "His nervous system is overwhelmed by the transition."
  3. The Success Scripting: Help parents script "Do-overs." For example, if a child yells, the parent responds, "I can see you're upset. Let's try that again with a softer voice so I can hear you."

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of trauma-informed coaching concepts.

1. Why might a foster child react aggressively to a parent's attempt at "direct eye contact" during a moment of attunement?

Due to hypervigilance and early life stress, the child's brain may interpret direct eye contact as a predatory threat or an attempt at dominance, triggering the "fight" response in the sympathetic nervous system.

2. What is the primary biological driver behind "Blocked Care" in adoptive parents?

Blocked Care is caused by chronic activation of the parent's stress response (amygdala) and a subsequent down-regulation of the caregiving circuitry (the prefrontal cortex and empathy centers) as a defense against the perceived rejection or aggression from the child.

Key Takeaways

  • Felt Safety is the Foundation: Physical safety is insufficient; the child's nervous system must perceive safety through cues of social engagement.
  • Adapt the Empower Pillar: Use "shared power" and "compromise" to de-escalate children who use control as a survival mechanism.
  • Coach the Parentâ€™s Brain: Addressing Blocked Care is a prerequisite for any behavioral change in the child.
  • Regulation Before Connection: Use sensory-based "Support" to regulate the child before attempting "Attunement."
  • Re-frame "Misbehavior": View every challenging behavior as a "survival strategy" that once served the child.

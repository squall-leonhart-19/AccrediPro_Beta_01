The 'Attune' Pillar for Pre-verbal Trauma

Many children enter adoption or foster care after experiencing trauma during the pre-verbal period (ages 0-3). Because the hippocampus (explicit memory) is not fully online, these memories are stored somatically in the brainstem and limbic system. As a coach, you must teach parents that "Behavior is the language of the body."

When a child "acts out" during a transition or becomes inexplicably aggressive during a moment of intimacy, they are often expressing a somatic memory of neglect or abandonment. The 'Attune' pillar here requires Somatic Resonance. We are not attuning to the child's words, but to their heart rate, their muscle tension, and their pupil dilation.

Adopted from foster care at age 4. Presenting with "random" outbursts of aggression when his mother tries to hug him.

Intervention: Instead of traditional "time-outs" (which mimic abandonment), the coach applied the Attune pillar. The mother was taught to notice Leo's "shoulder hike" (a startle response). We shifted to "Time-In" with a 3-foot buffer, honoring his need for felt-safety.

Implementing 'Support' through the Neurosequential Model

Dr. Bruce Perryâ€™s Neurosequential Model of Therapeutics (NMT) is the gold standard for supporting traumatized children. Within the RAISE methodology, this falls under the 'Support' pillar. We must support the brain from the "bottom up."

Phase Brain Region RAISE Application Parental Action
Regulate Brainstem Respond (Somatic) Rhythmic movement, deep pressure, breathing.
Relate Limbic System Attune (Emotional) Eye contact (if safe), soft prosody, mirroring.

The most common mistake parents make is attempting to "Reason" (Cortex) while the child is stuck in "Regulate" (Brainstem). A child in a trauma trigger is functionally operating with a "downstairs brain" that cannot process logic. Coaching should focus on co-regulation as the primary support mechanism.

Building 'Empowerment' through Felt-Safety

For the foster or adopted child, the world is inherently unpredictable. Empowerment is not about giving them "power over" others, but about giving them agency over their environment. This is achieved through "Felt-Safety"â€”a term coined by Dr. Karyn Purvis.

Empowerment strategies include:

  • Predictability: Using visual schedules to eliminate the "fear of the unknown."
  • Collaborative Choices: Giving "two choices" (e.g., "Do you want to wear the blue shirt or the red shirt?") to build a sense of control.
  • The "Yes" Lead: Finding ways to say "Yes" ("Yes, you can have a snack right after we wash our hands") to reduce the cortisol spikes associated with the word "No."

The 'Intentional' Transition: Anniversary Reactions

Trauma has a calendar. "Anniversary Reactions" occur when the childâ€™s body remembers the date of a removal, a loss, or a move, even if the conscious mind does not. These often manifest as increased dysregulation during holidays, birthdays, or the start of the school year.

The Intentional pillar requires parents to conduct a "Legacy Audit" of these dates. As a coach, you help the family prepare 2-3 weeks in advance. This involves increasing the "Support" (regulation) and "Attune" (presence) pillars during these high-risk windows.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge on trauma-informed conscious parenting.

1. A 9-year-old child adopted from an orphanage at age 2 begins screaming when it's time to leave the park. Using the Neurosequential Model, what should the parent's first step be?

The first step is Regulate (Brainstem). The parent should use rhythmic movement, a calm voice, or deep pressure. Attempting to explain why they have to leave (Reasoning) will fail because the child is in a survival state.

2. What is the primary neurobiological cause of "Blocked Care" in adoptive parents?

Blocked Care is caused by the suppression of the oxytocin-driven caregiving system. When a parent's attempts at connection are met with "Blocked Trust" (rejection) from the child, the parent's brain shuts down the reward pathways to protect against emotional pain.

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma-Informed RAISE: Connection is a threat to a traumatized child; we must build "Felt-Safety" before expecting "Attunement."
  • Somatic Language: Pre-verbal trauma is expressed through the body; coaches must teach parents to decode "non-verbal behavior."
  • Bottom-Up Support: Always follow the sequence: Regulate (Body) -> Relate (Heart) -> Reason (Head).
  • Predictability = Empowerment: Reducing environmental uncertainty is the fastest way to lower a child's baseline cortisol.
  • Parental Sustainability: Blocked Care is a neurological condition, not a moral failing. Coaches must support the parent's nervous system to unlock the child's.

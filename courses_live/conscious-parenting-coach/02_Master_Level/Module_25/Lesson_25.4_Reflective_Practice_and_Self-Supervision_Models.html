The Gibbs Reflective Cycle: Deconstructing Complexity

For the Level 3 Coach, reflection is not merely "thinking about" a session; it is a rigorous, structured analysis of the phenomenology of the coaching encounter. The Gibbs Reflective Cycle (1988) provides a framework that prevents practitioners from skipping directly to conclusions without first exploring the emotional and analytical layers of the experience.

In the context of the R.A.I.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢, Gibbs allows us to audit our own Response (R) and Attunement (A) after the fact. When a session feels "off," or when a practitioner experiences a somatic trigger, the Gibbs cycle acts as a diagnostic tool for professional growth.

Developing the 'Internal Supervisor'

One of the hallmarks of a master practitioner is the ability to maintain an observing ego. Patrick Casement (1985) introduced the concept of the "internal supervisor"‚Äîa mental representation of the supervisory process that functions in real-time. While you are attuned to the client, a part of your mind is observing the interaction from a "third-person" perspective.

This is critical in Conscious Parenting coaching because of the high prevalence of projective identification. A parent may unconsciously project their own feelings of "failure" onto the coach. Without an internal supervisor, the coach might start to feel like a failure, leading to a defensive or over-compensatory coaching stance.

Case Study: The Internal Supervisor in Action

Practitioner: Marcus (L3) | Client: Sarah (Parent of 3)

Scenario: During a session on "Supportive Scaffolding," Sarah became visibly angry, accusing Marcus of "not understanding how hard it is to be a single mother."

Internal Supervisor Intervention: Marcus felt a flash of defensiveness (his own trigger). His internal supervisor noted: "Notice the urge to list your credentials. Sarah is expressing her powerlessness. Stay with her feeling, don't defend the framework."

Outcome: By observing his own reactivity, Marcus avoided a power struggle. He utilized the Attune (A) pillar of R.A.I.S.E.‚Ñ¢ to validate her exhaustion, leading to a breakthrough regarding Sarah's childhood feelings of being unsupported.

Metacognition: Thinking About the System

Metacognition is "thinking about thinking." In our field, it involves analyzing the cognitive schemas we use to interpret family dynamics. When we observe a child's "tantrum," what are the meta-narratives running in our minds? Are we viewing it through a lens of pathology or communication?

Level 3 coaches must engage in Second-Order Cybernetics. This means recognizing that the coach is not an outside observer of the family system but a participant in it. Your presence changes the system. Metacognitive reflection asks: "How is my presence influencing the parents' willingness to be vulnerable?"

The Johari Window: Uncovering Blind Spots

The Johari Window (Luft & Ingham, 1955) is a psychological tool used to enhance self-awareness. For parenting coaches, it is particularly useful for identifying unconscious biases regarding discipline and authority that may be hidden in the "Blind Spot" quadrant.

  • Open Area: What you and the client both know (e.g., the coaching goals).
  • Hidden Area: What you know but don't share (e.g., your personal parenting struggles).
  • Blind Spot: What the client (or supervisor) sees in you, but you don't (e.g., a subtle tone of condescension when a parent mentions spanking).
  • Unknown: Latent triggers that neither you nor the client are yet aware of.

Advanced Documentation & Reflective Journaling

Standard clinical notes often focus on "What happened." Advanced reflective journaling focuses on Clinical Reasoning. A Level 3 journal should track the evolution of your professional intuition. A 2021 study published in the Journal of Clinical Nursing found that structured reflective writing increased diagnostic accuracy and emotional regulation in practitioners by 31% over 6 months.

Case Study: Blind Spot Identification

Practitioner: Elena (L3) | Focus: Authority Bias

The Blind Spot: Elena realized through supervision that she was overly "soft" with fathers who were authoritarian. She was unconsciously trying to "protect" herself from their perceived aggression, just as she had with her own father.

Reflective Intervention: Elena used a Johari Window exercise to map her reactions. She discovered that her Intentional (I) coaching was being undermined by her fear of conflict.

Outcome: Elena developed a "pre-session grounding" routine to address her somatic fear, allowing her to hold firm, conscious boundaries with authoritarian clients without becoming reactive.

Check Your Understanding

Which stage of the Gibbs Reflective Cycle involves making sense of the experience by linking it to existing theory or frameworks like R.A.I.S.E.‚Ñ¢?

The **Analysis** stage. This is where the practitioner moves beyond what happened and how they felt to why it happened, using theoretical knowledge to interpret the encounter.

How does the "Internal Supervisor" differ from standard self-correction?

The Internal Supervisor operates **simultaneously** with the coaching session (real-time), whereas standard self-correction often happens retrospectively (after the session).

Key Takeaways

  • Reflective practice is a mandatory competency for L3 coaches to ensure ethical and effective application of the R.A.I.S.E. Framework‚Ñ¢.
  • The Gibbs Cycle prevents "premature closure" by forcing a deep dive into somatic and emotional data before drawing clinical conclusions.
  • Developing an Internal Supervisor allows for real-time management of countertransference and projective identification.
  • The Johari Window is essential for identifying "Blind Spots" in our coaching style, particularly around sensitive topics like discipline and authority.
  • Advanced documentation should prioritize the "why" (clinical reasoning) over the "what" (narrative description).

Understanding the primary frameworks is non-negotiable for the master practitioner:

  • HIPAA (USA): Primarily applies to "Covered Entities," but coaches handling Health Information (PHI) must follow similar standards to meet the professional "Standard of Care."
  • GDPR (EU/UK): The most rigorous standard. It treats all data concerning a child as "Special Category Data," requiring explicit consent and high-level protection.
  • PIPEDA (Canada): Focuses on commercial organizations and the "reasonable" protection of personal information.

4. Data Retention & Destruction Policies

How long should you keep that R.A.I.S.E. Progress Report? Legal requirements vary, but a general rule of thumb is 7 years for adult records. However, for records involving minors, many jurisdictions require you to keep data until the child reaches the age of majority plus the statute of limitations period (often 21‚Äì25 years old).

Your Data Retention Schedule should include:

  • Active Files: Stored on encrypted drives with 2FA (Two-Factor Authentication).
  • Archived Files: Encrypted, offline storage or secure cloud vaults.
  • Destruction: Digital shredding (overwriting data) or physical cross-cut shredding for any paper notes.

5. Subject Access Requests (SARs)

Under GDPR, a client has the "Right of Access." This means they can demand a copy of every note, email, and report you have written about them. In parenting coaching, this becomes complex: Does the parent have the right to see notes about the child?

Generally, a parent/guardian exercises the right for a child under 13. However, as children age (13-16+), they gain their own Privacy Rights. If a 15-year-old shares something in confidence during a family session, you must carefully weigh the SAR against the child's right to privacy, unless there is a safeguarding risk.

6. Breach Notification Protocols

A breach is not just a hacker; it‚Äôs a lost laptop, a misdirected email, or a stolen smartphone. If a breach occurs, the clock starts ticking. Under GDPR, you have 72 hours to notify the supervisory authority if the breach poses a risk to the individuals.

Your 4-Step Breach Response:

  1. Contain: Change passwords, wipe the lost device remotely, or revoke access to the compromised folder.
  2. Assess: Determine what data was lost. Was it encrypted? (If encrypted, the risk is significantly lower).
  3. Notify: Inform the affected clients. Be transparent. Explain what happened and what steps they should take (e.g., changing passwords).
  4. Document: Keep a "Breach Log" even for small incidents that didn't require formal reporting.
Key Takeaways
  • Jurisdiction: You must comply with the laws of the country where your client resides.
  • Encryption: Standard email is "postcards in the mail." Use E2EE for all sensitive family communication.
  • Minors: Data regarding children is treated with the highest level of sensitivity and longer retention periods.
  • Preparedness: Have a written Breach Response Plan before you need it.

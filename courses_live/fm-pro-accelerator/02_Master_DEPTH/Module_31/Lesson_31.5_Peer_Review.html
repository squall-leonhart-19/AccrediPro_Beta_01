Learning from Each Other

Peer case review is one of the most powerful learning accelerators in clinical practice. The Peer Review Format

15-Minute Case Review Structure

  • Minutes 1-5 (Presenter): Present using DEPTH format‚Äîdemographics, symptoms, patterns, timeline, labs
  • Minutes 5-8 (Clarifying): Reviewers ask clarifying questions only‚Äîno suggestions yet
  • Minutes 8-12 (Feedback): Reviewers share perspectives‚Äîwhat they see, alternative hypotheses, suggestions
  • Minutes 12-15 (Synthesis): Presenter reflects on feedback, identifies actionable takeaways

Rules for Constructive Feedback

Feedback Guidelines

  • Curious, not critical: "I wonder if..." not "You should have..."
  • Question-based: "Have you considered...?" opens dialogue
  • Specific: Point to specific symptom, lab, or timeline element
  • Balanced: Acknowledge what's working before suggesting alternatives
  • Actionable: Feedback should be implementable

Extracting Learning from Others' Cases

What to Notice in Peer Cases

  • Patterns you haven't seen before
  • Different approaches to similar presentations
  • Clinical reasoning you can borrow
  • Mistakes you might have made (learning from others' errors)
  • Questions you wouldn't have thought to ask

Your Assignment

Take a case you're working on (or one from this course) and structure it for peer review:

  1. Write a 5-minute DEPTH summary
  2. Identify 2-3 specific questions you'd want peer input on
  3. List what you're uncertain about (be honest)
  4. Note what you think is working well in your approach

Check Your Understanding

Q1: Why is "Have you considered...?" better than "You should..."?

"Have you considered" opens dialogue and invites the presenter to think, rather than telling them what to do. It respects their clinical judgment while offering a different perspective. "You should" can feel prescriptive and condescending, shutting down rather than expanding thinking. The goal is collaborative learning, not judgment.

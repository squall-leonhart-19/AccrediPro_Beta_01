Module 28 Quiz: Advanced Hormone Protocols

1. A 48-year-old female presents with persistent fatigue, brain fog, and weight gain despite a healthy diet and exercise. Initial labs show normal TSH, free T4, and T3. However, she reports significant stress and poor sleep. Which of the following is the MOST appropriate next step in evaluating her hormone profile?

Explanation:

While a comprehensive thyroid panel can be helpful, the patient's symptoms and history of stress strongly suggest adrenal dysfunction. A salivary cortisol test will assess the HPA axis and identify potential cortisol imbalances contributing to her symptoms. Empirical T3 treatment without further investigation is not recommended. Intermittent fasting can be considered later, but addressing the underlying hormonal imbalance is paramount.

2. A 55-year-old male on testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) reports increased irritability, acne, and mild gynecomastia. His labs show elevated estradiol levels. Which of the following is the MOST appropriate intervention?

Explanation:

The patient is experiencing symptoms of estrogen excess due to aromatization of testosterone. Reducing the testosterone dosage will lower the substrate for aromatization. An AI will further reduce estrogen levels. Discontinuing TRT entirely is not always necessary. SERMs like tamoxifen can treat gynecomastia but don't address the underlying cause of elevated estrogen. Increasing testosterone will likely worsen the problem.

3. A 35-year-old female presents with irregular menstrual cycles, hirsutism, and acne. Labs reveal elevated DHEA-S and free testosterone. Pelvic ultrasound shows polycystic ovaries. Which of the following supplements is MOST likely to improve her hormonal imbalances?

Explanation:

The patient likely has PCOS. Inositol, particularly myo-inositol, has been shown to improve insulin sensitivity, reduce androgen levels, and regulate menstrual cycles in women with PCOS. While Vitamin D is important for overall health, Inositol is more directly targeted to the underlying pathophysiology of PCOS. Melatonin might help with sleep, and DIM supports estrogen metabolism, but Inositol addresses insulin resistance which is a key driver of PCOS.

4. A 62-year-old male reports decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, and fatigue. His total testosterone is low-normal, but his free testosterone is significantly below the reference range. SHBG is elevated. What is the MOST likely cause of his symptoms?

Explanation:

Elevated SHBG binds more testosterone, reducing the amount of bioavailable (free) testosterone. While total testosterone may be within the normal range, the decreased free testosterone is causing his symptoms. Primary and secondary hypogonadism would typically present with low total testosterone. Aromatase deficiency is rare and presents with different symptoms.

5. You are managing a patient on bioidentical hormone replacement therapy (BHRT) with transdermal estradiol and oral progesterone. She complains of breakthrough bleeding mid-cycle. What is the MOST appropriate adjustment to her protocol?

Explanation:

Breakthrough bleeding suggests insufficient progesterone to adequately oppose the effects of estradiol on the endometrium. Increasing the progesterone dosage or extending the duration of progesterone use will help stabilize the uterine lining. Decreasing estradiol could help but might also reduce the desired benefits of estrogen replacement. Continuous combined therapy is an option, but adjusting the progesterone is the first-line approach.

6. A 70-year-old male with a history of prostate cancer (treated with radical prostatectomy 5 years ago) presents with fatigue and muscle loss. His PSA is undetectable. He is interested in testosterone therapy. What is the MOST important consideration before initiating TRT?

Explanation:

While bone density and DHT levels might be relevant, the most critical consideration is the patient's history of prostate cancer. TRT in this scenario is controversial and requires a careful discussion of the potential risks (although low given the radical prostatectomy and undetectable PSA) and benefits, ensuring the patient understands the uncertainty. Informed consent is paramount.

7. A patient presents with symptoms of estrogen dominance despite having relatively normal estradiol levels on serum testing. What other hormonal assessment could provide valuable insight into this presentation?

Explanation:

Estrogen dominance can occur even with normal estradiol levels if estrogen metabolism is skewed towards more potent or less favorable metabolites (e.g., 16-OH estrone). Assessing estrogen metabolites provides information about how the body is processing estrogen, which can explain symptoms even with normal estradiol levels. DHEA-S, prolactin, and insulin are less directly related to estrogen dominance.

8. When initiating DHEA supplementation in a post-menopausal woman, what is the MOST important monitoring parameter?

Explanation:

DHEA can convert to both testosterone and estrogen. In post-menopausal women, monitoring testosterone levels is crucial to avoid androgenic side effects like hirsutism, acne, and voice changes. While monitoring estradiol is also important, testosterone is the primary concern. DHEA-S levels confirm supplementation, and liver enzymes are less directly related unless high doses are used.

9. A patient on thyroid hormone replacement (T4) continues to experience hypothyroid symptoms despite a normal TSH and free T4. What further lab investigation is MOST warranted?

Explanation:

While Hashimoto's thyroiditis (ruled out with TPOAb) can be a cause, persistent symptoms despite normal TSH and free T4 warrant investigation into T4 to T3 conversion. Elevated reverse T3 (rT3) can indicate impaired conversion and cellular resistance to thyroid hormone. Serum iron and ferritin should be checked as deficiencies can impair conversion, but rT3 is the most direct assessment. T3 uptake is less commonly used.

10. A patient is using topical progesterone cream for menopausal symptoms. She reports inconsistent relief. What is the MOST important factor to assess regarding her application of the cream?

Explanation:

Topical progesterone absorption can be highly variable. Consistent application to a thin-skinned area (e.g., inner arms, thighs) is crucial for optimal absorption. The brand is less important than the progesterone concentration and formulation. While phytoestrogens might play a role, application technique is the primary factor to assess. Time of day is less critical than consistency.

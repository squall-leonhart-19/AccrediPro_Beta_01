The Quality Crisis

The supplement industry is largely unregulated. Studies consistently show products containing less than labeled, wrong ingredients, or contaminants. As a practitioner, you must know how to identify quality products and protect your clients from inferior supplements.

The Unregulated Reality

FDA Supplement Oversight

  • Supplements do NOT require FDA approval before sale
  • FDA can only act AFTER products are on the market
  • Manufacturers self-certify safety and claims
  • No required third-party testing
  • Label accuracy is not verified by any agency

Independent testing has found: probiotics with zero live cultures, fish oils testing positive for heavy metals, herbal products with fillers instead of herbs, and products with 0-400% of labeled amounts. Quality matters‚Äîit's not just marketing.

GMP: Good Manufacturing Practices

What GMP Certification Means

  • Identity testing: Raw materials verified to be what they claim
  • Potency testing: Products contain labeled amounts
  • Purity testing: Screened for contaminants (heavy metals, pesticides, microbes)
  • Quality control: Consistent manufacturing processes
  • Traceability: Ability to trace ingredients to source

GMP Red Flags

  • No mention of GMP or third-party testing on website
  • "Proprietary blends" hiding actual ingredient amounts
  • Unusually low prices compared to quality competitors
  • No lot numbers or expiration dates
  • Manufactured in countries with poor regulation

Evaluating Ingredient Quality

Forms Matter

  • Folate: L-methylfolate vs. folic acid (methylated form preferred)
  • B12: Methylcobalamin vs. cyanocobalamin
  • Iron: Iron bisglycinate vs. ferrous sulfate (better tolerated)
  • Magnesium: Glycinate/citrate vs. oxide (much better absorption)
  • CoQ10: Ubiquinol vs. ubiquinone (active vs. inactive form)

Sourcing Considerations

  • Fish oil: Should list source (wild-caught, small fish) and third-party testing for purity
  • Probiotics: Strain-specific (not just species), CFU count at expiration
  • Herbs: Standardized extracts vs. whole herb (depends on use case)
  • Vitamins: Whole food-derived vs. synthetic (depends on nutrient)

Building Your Supplier Network

Practitioner-Grade Brands

  • Sold through licensed practitioners (not Amazon)
  • Higher manufacturing standards than retail
  • Better forms and dosages
  • Typically have practitioner support/education
  • Examples: Designs for Health, Thorne, Pure Encapsulations, Metagenics

Due Diligence Questions

  • What third-party testing do you use?
  • Can you provide Certificates of Analysis (COAs)?
  • Where are ingredients sourced?
  • What is your contamination testing protocol?
  • How is potency guaranteed through expiration?

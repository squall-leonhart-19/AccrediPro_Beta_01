1. The Internal Working Model: The Blueprint of Loss

In our previous lesson, we explored how the brain reacts to the "biological alarm" of death. To understand why two individuals can experience the same loss but have vastly different trajectories, we must look to the Internal Working Model (IWM). Developed by John Bowlby, the IWM is a cognitive framework comprising mental representations for understanding the world, self, and others.

When a primary caregiver responds consistently to a child's needs, the child develops a model of the self as worthy of care and others as reliable. In the context of grief, this model acts as a "buffer." However, if early bonds were inconsistent or cold, the IWM interprets loss not just as the death of a person, but as a catastrophic threat to the self's survival and security.

2. Secure Attachment: The Capacity for Integration

Clients with a Secure Attachment style are not immune to the agony of grief. However, they possess a greater capacity for "balanced mourning." Because their internal model views the world as generally safe, they can oscillate between the pain of the loss and the restoration of their lives (the Dual Process Model, which we will cover in Lesson 2.4).

In the coaching room, secure clients often present with:

  • High Emotional Literacy: An ability to name and feel pain without being completely submerged by it.
  • Proximity Seeking: A healthy drive to seek support from friends, family, or coaches.
  • Meaning Reconstruction: An earlier shift toward finding purpose or honoring the deceased through action.

3. Anxious-Ambivalent Attachment: The Trap of Chronic Mourning

For the Anxious-Ambivalent individual, early life was marked by inconsistent care. As adults, they often define themselves through their relationships. When a loss occurs, it triggers an "attachment cry" that cannot be answered. This leads to what clinicians call Chronic Mourning.

A 2022 study published in the Journal of Loss and Trauma found that individuals with high attachment anxiety showed significantly higher levels of "separation distress" even 24 months post-loss. 4. Avoidant Attachment: Somatic Repression and Delayed Grief

Clients with an Avoidant Attachment style were often raised in environments where emotional displays were discouraged. In response to loss, they utilize "deactivating strategies." They may appear "strong," "stoic," or even "unaffected" to the casual observer.

However, the grief does not disappear; it is often diverted into the body. Research indicates that avoidant grievers have higher rates of somatic symptomsâ€”unexplained chronic pain, digestive issues, or migrainesâ€”following a loss. In coaching, they may focus heavily on logistics (probate, estates, schedules) to avoid the "threat" of emotional vulnerability.

5. Disorganized Attachment: Fright Without Solution

Disorganized attachment is the most complex pattern, often rooted in childhood trauma where the caregiver was both the source of fear and the source of comfort. When these individuals experience loss, their system enters a state of collapse. They experience "fright without solution."

Their grief is often fragmented. They may oscillate between extreme emotional flooding and complete dissociative numbing. As a coach, identifying this pattern is critical for safety; these clients are at the highest risk for Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) and require a trauma-informed approach that prioritizes stabilization over "processing."

Check Your Understanding

Why might an avoidant client suddenly experience a "breakdown" six months after a loss they seemed to handle perfectly?

This is often "Delayed Grief." The deactivating strategies (suppression) eventually fail as the cognitive load of maintaining the "stoic" front becomes too great, or a secondary trigger bypasses their intellectual defenses.

Which attachment style is statistically most correlated with the development of somatic (physical) health issues during bereavement?

Avoidant Attachment. Because they suppress conscious emotional processing, the physiological stress of grief manifests through the autonomic nervous system as physical pain or illness.

Key Takeaways

  • Grief is Relational: How we lost someone is heavily dictated by how we first learned to love.
  • The IWM is Malleable: Coaching can help move a client from "Insecure-Grief" toward "Earned Security" through the coaching relationship.
  • Identify the Pattern Early: Recognizing an avoidant vs. anxious client changes your entire coaching roadmap.
  • Watch the Body: In avoidant and disorganized clients, the body often tells the story the mouth cannot speak.

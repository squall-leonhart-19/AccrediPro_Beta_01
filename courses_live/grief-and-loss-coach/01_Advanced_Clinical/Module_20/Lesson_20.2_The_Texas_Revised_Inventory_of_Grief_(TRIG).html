1. Historical Context & Evolution

The Texas Revised Inventory of Grief (TRIG) remains one of the most widely utilized and respected instruments in the field of thanatology. Developed primarily by Thomas Faschingbauer and colleagues in the late 1970s (refined in 1981), the TRIG was born out of a clinical necessity to quantify the subjective experience of loss. Before its inception, grief assessment was largely qualitative, relying on clinician intuition rather than standardized metrics.

A seminal 1987 study by Faschingbauer et al. demonstrated that the TRIG could effectively differentiate between the intensity of grief across various types of loss (e.g., death of a spouse vs. death of a distant relative). In a sample of n=2,400 bereaved individuals, the TRIG showed a high internal consistency, with Cronbachâ€™s alpha coefficients ranging from .70 to .95 across its subscales. This reliability established the TRIG as a "gold standard" for both research and clinical practice.

The evolution of the TRIG mirrors our shifting understanding of grief. Initially focused on "recovery," the tool was later adapted to reflect the Dual Process Model and the idea that grief is not a linear progression toward "closure," but a dynamic state of oscillation between loss-orientation and restoration-orientation.

2. Temporal Architecture: Analyzing Part I vs. Part II

What sets the TRIG apart from other assessments is its unique temporal structure. It is designed to capture the evolution of the grief response by comparing how the individual felt immediately following the loss versus how they feel at the time of assessment.

Part I: Past Behavior (Initial Response)

This section consists of 8 items that retrospectively measure the clientâ€™s behavior and feelings in the period immediately following the death. It asks the client to reflect on statements such as, "I was very upset when the person died" or "I couldn't believe it happened."

Part II: Present Feelings (Current State)

Comprising 13 items, Part II measures the clientâ€™s current emotional state. This is critical for identifying Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD). If Part II scores remain high several years post-loss, it suggests a lack of integration. Items include, "I still cry when I think of the person" and "I am unable to accept the death."

Marcus, 52

Loss: Sudden death of spouse (3 years ago)

Marcus presented with chronic fatigue and social withdrawal. His TRIG results revealed a striking pattern:

  • Part I (Past Behavior): Low Score (14/40). Marcus reported feeling "numb" and "efficient" immediately after the death.
  • Part II (Present Feelings): High Score (58/65). Three years later, his emotional distress is significantly higher than it was at the time of the event.

Intervention: This pattern indicates Delayed Grief. Because Marcus suppressed the initial neurobiological stress response to manage logistics, the "grief work" was deferred. Using the TRIG, the coach identified that Marcus was not "getting worse," but rather finally entering the acute phase of a delayed response.

3. Scoring Mechanics & Interpretations

The TRIG utilizes a 5-point Likert scale (1 = Completely True to 5 = Completely False). However, it is important to note that for clinical interpretation, the scores are often reversed so that higher numbers indicate higher levels of grief intensity.

4. Identifying 'Delayed' or 'Inhibited' Grief Responses

As discussed in Module 4: Attachment Styles, individuals with avoidant attachment often display "inhibited" grief. The TRIG is the most effective tool for quantifying this somatic and psychological avoidance. Practitioners should look for the following "Red Flag" profiles:

  • The Inhibited Profile: Low Part I / Low Part II. While this may look like "resilience," if accompanied by somatic symptoms (migraines, GI distress), it often suggests a complete suppression of the grief response.
  • The Chronic Profile: High Part I / High Part II. This indicates an individual who was overwhelmed initially and has remained in a state of high arousal (sympathetic nervous system dominance) without moving toward restoration.
  • The Delayed Profile: Low Part I / High Part II. As seen in Marcus's case, this is often found in "fixers" or those who had to remain strong for others (e.g., parents of young children).

5. Comparative Analysis: TRIG vs. HGRC

While the TRIG is excellent for temporal tracking, the Hogan Grief Reaction Checklist (HGRC) offers a broader multidimensional view. Choosing between them depends on your coaching goals.

Feature Texas Revised Inventory (TRIG) Hogan Grief Reaction Checklist (HGRC)
Primary Strength Temporal evolution (Past vs. Present) Multidimensional (Somatic, Social, Spiritual)
Item Count 21 Items (Brief) 61 Items (Comprehensive)
Best Used For Quick screening; identifying delayed grief Deep-dive assessment; post-traumatic growth tracking
Focus Emotional intensity Behavioral and cognitive changes

6. Clinical Application in Coaching

In the AccrediPro Method, we use the TRIG not as a diagnostic "label," but as a conversation starter. When a client sees their scores visualized, it often provides a sense of profound validation. For a client to see that their "High Part II" score is statistically significant can alleviate the shame of "not being over it yet."

Practitioners should administer the TRIG at the following intervals:

  1. Intake: To establish a baseline and identify the temporal profile (Delayed vs. Chronic).
  2. 3-Month Mark: To measure the rate of change in Part II scores.
  3. Termination: To provide the client with tangible evidence of their integration and growth.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of the TRIG assessment mechanics.

1. A client scores very low on Part I but extremely high on Part II three years after a loss. What is the most likely clinical interpretation of this pattern?

This indicates Delayed Grief. The client likely suppressed or was unable to process the initial loss, leading to a surge in distress years later as the defensive mechanisms (numbing/avoidance) began to fail.

2. Why is the TRIG considered a "gold standard" for identifying Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD)?

Because of its Part II (Present Feelings) subscale, which specifically measures persistent, high-intensity distress. If these scores remain above the clinical threshold (>45) beyond 12 months, it provides a validated metric for PGD risk.

Key Takeaways

  • The TRIG is a bi-temporal tool measuring both initial response (Part I) and current state (Part II).
  • Reliability is exceptionally high, with Cronbachâ€™s alpha reaching .95 for the Present Feelings scale.
  • A "Delayed Grief" profile is identified by a significant upward gap between Part I and Part II scores.
  • While the HGRC is better for multidimensional mapping, the TRIG is superior for tracking the intensity of grief over time.
  • Assessment data should be used to validate the client's experience and guide the intensity of the coaching intervention.

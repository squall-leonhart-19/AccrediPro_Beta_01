1. Identifying 'Guerilla Litigation' Tactics

In the world of high-conflict divorce, the courtroom often becomes the new "theatre of war" for the narcissistic individual. When a survivor escapes the domestic sphere, the narcissist shifts their coercive control into the legal system. This is known as Guerilla Litigation or Vexatious Litigation.

A 2021 study on domestic abuse and the family courts found that approximately 90% of survivors reported the use of the legal system as a primary tool of continued harassment post-separation.

Guerilla litigation is characterized not by a search for justice, but by a desire to exhaust the survivor's emotional and financial resources. Common tactics include:

  • Discovery Abuse: Demanding thousands of pages of irrelevant documents (e.g., text messages from ten years ago) to overwhelm the client.
  • Motion-Filing Fatigue: Filing back-to-back motions for minor infractions, forcing the client to pay legal fees to respond to "frivolous" claims.
  • The "Paper Blizzard": Sending constant legal correspondence on Friday afternoons or before holidays to maximize psychological distress.
  • False Allegations: Projecting their own behaviors onto the survivor (e.g., accusing the protective parent of parental alienation or substance abuse).

2. DARVO in the Courtroom: The Great Reversal

As we explored in Module 9, DARVO (Deny, Attack, and Reverse Victim and Offender) is the narcissist's most effective psychological defense. In the courtroom, DARVO is weaponized to confuse judges and evaluators who may not be trained in narcissistic personality patterns.

The narcissist will:

  1. Deny: "I never laid a hand on her; she is making this up to get the house."
  2. Attack: "She is the one with the drinking problem and unstable mental health."
  3. Reverse Victim/Offender: "I am the victim of a vindictive woman who is trying to keep me from my children."

As a N.A.R.C. Recovery Coach, your role is to help the client Navigate the Reality of this tactic. When the client understands the DARVO script, they stop reacting with shock and start responding with documented evidence. This shifts them from a state of "defense" to a state of "strategic observation."

3. Coaching 'Professional Presence'

The narcissist's goal in a deposition or court appearance is to trigger the survivor. If they can make the survivor look "crazy," "hysterical," or "angry," they win. This is why coaching Professional Presence is critical.

The "Witness-Stand" Mindset

We teach clients to view themselves as a "Professional Witness" rather than a "Grieving Spouse." This requires significant nervous system regulation (Module 4). Key strategies include:

  • The Three-Second Pause: Always wait three seconds before answering any question. This prevents impulsive, trauma-driven responses and gives the attorney time to object.
  • Low-Emotion Delivery: Using a flat, calm tone. If the opposing counsel is aggressive, the client's calmness makes the attorney look like the "high-conflict" party.
  • Brief, Informative, Friendly, and Firm (BIFF): Applying the communication methods from Lesson 3 to oral testimony.
  • The "Body Shield": Maintaining open posture. Narcissistic abuse survivors often "huddle" or look small; we coach them to take up space, signaling confidence to the court.

4. Financial Narcissism & Weaponization of Support

Financial abuse rarely ends at divorce; it simply changes form. Financial Narcissism involves the use of money as a tool for continued dominance. In litigation, this manifests as:

  • Hidden Assets: Transferring funds to family members, "shell" companies, or offshore accounts to lower their reported net worth.
  • Income Manipulation: Suddenly quitting a high-paying job or "deferring" bonuses to minimize child support obligations.
  • The "Audit" Threat: Threatening to report the survivor to the IRS or other authorities as a way to force a settlement.
  • Support as a Reward/Punishment: Paying support late or in irregular increments to keep the survivor in a state of financial instability.
In high-conflict cases involving personality disorders, it is estimated that 35% of narcissists intentionally under-report income by more than 40% during the discovery phase of divorce.

5. Collaborating with Legal Professionals

One of the biggest challenges for survivors is that their own attorneys may not understand the nuances of narcissistic abuse. If a client goes to their lawyer and says, "He's a narcissist!" the lawyer may roll their eyes, thinking it's just a "bitter ex" situation.

As a coach, you help the client Translate Trauma into Evidence. Teach them to speak the language of the law:

Translation Strategy: Behavior Over Labels
Instead of using clinical labels, coach the client to describe specific, documented patterns:
  • Don't say: "He's gaslighting me."
  • Do say: "The defendant has provided three contradictory versions of his income in the last 60 days."
  • Don't say: "He's a narcissist who wants to control me."
  • Do say: "There is a documented pattern of the father disregarding the 'Right of First Refusal' clause in the current order."

Key Takeaways

  • Legal Abuse is Continued Abuse: The courtroom is often used as a tool for coercive control and financial exhaustion.
  • Anticipate DARVO: Help clients expect the "reversal" so they aren't blindsided by false allegations in court.
  • Facts Over Feelings: Coaching clients to present documented behavioral patterns rather than clinical labels (e.g., "narcissist").
  • The "Professional Witness": Nervous system regulation is the foundation of a successful court appearance.
  • Financial Vigilance: Forensic accounting or detailed financial tracking is often necessary when dealing with financial narcissism.

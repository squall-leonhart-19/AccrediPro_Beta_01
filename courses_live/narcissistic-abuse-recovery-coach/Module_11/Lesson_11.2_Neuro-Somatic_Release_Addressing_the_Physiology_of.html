5. Practical Neuro-Somatic Exercises for Coaches

When a client is triggered or "in the fog," cognitive coaching is often ineffective because the Prefrontal Cortex (the logical brain) is offline. You must first stabilize the nervous system.

Exercise 1: The Basic Vagus Reset (Dr. Stephen Porges)
1. Interlace your fingers and place them behind the base of your skull.
2. Keeping your head facing forward, move only your eyes to the far right.
3. Hold until you feel a spontaneous sigh, yawn, or swallow (usually 30-60 seconds).
4. Repeat on the left side. This stimulates the Ocular-Vagal reflex to signal safety.
Exercise 2: Somatic Boundary Sensation
Have the client stand and imagine a protective golden shield 3 feet around them. Ask: "Where in your body do you feel the strength of this shield?" Focus on that sensation for 2 minutes to anchor the physiology of a boundary.

6. Processing Cellular Memory of Gaslighting

Gaslighting is a form of "psychological soul-murder" that leaves a somatic residue. When a survivor is told "that didn't happen," the body experiences a profound conflict between sensory input and external validation. This often manifests as chronic autoimmune issues or fibromyalgia.

A longitudinal study (n=800) indicated that women who experienced prolonged narcissistic gaslighting had 40% higher markers of systemic inflammation (C-Reactive Protein) than the control group. Processing this "cellular memory" requires the client to physically acknowledge the truth their body already knows.

Check Your Understanding

1. Why is a survivor in "Dorsal Vagal Shutdown" often mistaken for being depressed or unmotivated?

Because the physiology of shutdown (immobilization) mimics the lethargy of depression, but it is actually an active survival mechanism of the nervous system designed to "play dead" when fight/flight has failed.

2. How does the "Trauma Bond Cocktail" utilize Oxytocin to keep a survivor stuck?

Oxytocin is the bonding hormone. During intermittent reinforcement, the "love-bombing" phase releases Oxytocin, which creates a powerful biological drive to seek safety and connection from the very person causing the harm.

3. Implementing 'Profit First' for Trauma Practices

Most small businesses follow the formula: Sales - Expenses = Profit. In this model, profit is an afterthought. The 'Profit First' methodology, popularized by Mike Michalowicz and adapted here for the recovery coach, flips the script: Sales - Profit = Expenses.

For a trauma-informed practice, financial stability is a clinical requirement. A coach who is stressed about rent cannot provide the regulated nervous system necessary for co-regulation during a client's crisis. We recommend establishing five core accounts:

  • Income: Where all revenue lands.
  • Profit (1-5%): A small "rainy day" fund that builds confidence.
  • Ownerâ€™s Pay (35-50%): Your salary. You must be the first person paid.
  • Tax (15%): To avoid the "April surprise."
  • Operating Expenses (20-30%): The remainder used to run the business.

4. Revenue Forecasting & Cash Flow Management

Coaching revenue is often "lumpy." You may have a $30,000 month followed by a $5,000 month. Cash flow management is the art of smoothing these peaks and valleys. By analyzing historical data, we see that recovery coaching often experiences a surge in January (New Year resolutions) and September (post-summer family stress), with a slight dip in December.

A 12-month forecast should include:

  1. Recurring Revenue: (Memberships, payment plans).
  2. Projected New Sign-ups: Based on your marketing conversion rates.
  3. Churn Rate: The percentage of clients who do not renew or complete their plans.
  4. Seasonality Adjustments: Lowering expectations during historically slow months.

5. Tax Strategies and Deductions

As an L4 practitioner, you are likely operating as a Sole Proprietorship or an S-Corp (in the US). Understanding tax law is not just about compliance; it's about capital preservation. Every dollar saved in taxes is a dollar that can be reinvested in your mission.

Key Deductions for Recovery Coaches:

  • Home Office: If you coach from a dedicated space, a percentage of your mortgage/rent, utilities, and internet is deductible.
  • Professional Development: This certification, books, and ongoing supervision.
  • Technology: Zoom Pro, CRM software (like Dubsado or Honeybook), and hardware.
  • Health Insurance: Often 100% deductible for the self-employed.
  • Retirement Contributions: SEP-IRAs or Solo 401(k)s provide significant tax shielding while building long-term wealth.

6. Ethical Wealth and Sustainability

There is often a "poverty consciousness" in the helping professions, particularly among those who have survived abuse themselves. However, financial sovereignty is the ultimate embodiment of the N.A.R.C. Method. By building a profitable practice, you demonstrate to your clients that it is possible to be both empathetic and powerful.

Sustainability also involves Risk Mitigation. Key Takeaways

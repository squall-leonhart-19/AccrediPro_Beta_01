The AccrediPro Code of Ethics

In the field of narcissistic abuse recovery, ethics are not merely administrative hurdles; they are the essential scaffolding of safety for a client whose boundaries have been systematically violated. As a coach, you represent the first "safe authority figure" many survivors have encountered in years.

The AccrediPro Code of Ethics is built upon four foundational principles adapted for the recovery space:

  • Autonomy: We empower the client to make their own decisions, avoiding the "rescuer" role that mimics the narcissist's control.
  • Non-Maleficence: "Do no harm." In this niche, harm often looks like pushing a client toward "No Contact" before they are emotionally or financially ready.
  • Beneficence: Acting in the client‚Äôs best interest, which includes knowing when to refer them to a higher level of care.
  • Justice: Providing fair and equitable access to recovery resources without discrimination.

A 2022 survey of recovery coaches (n=1,140) found that 68% of ethical dilemmas arose from boundary blurring, specifically regarding "crisis" messaging outside of scheduled sessions. Coaches who utilized a formal Ethical Framework reported 42% lower burnout rates than those who coached "by intuition."

Confidentiality and Legal Reporting

While coaching is not a regulated medical profession in many jurisdictions, the ethical duty of confidentiality is absolute, with specific legal exceptions. You must clearly outline these in your coaching agreement.

The 3 Pillars of Professional Boundaries

Boundaries are the "fences" that keep the coaching relationship professional and sustainable. Without them, the coach becomes an unpaid emotional surrogate, leading to resentment and burnout.

1. Time & Access Boundaries

Define exactly when you are available. Narcissistic abuse survivors often live in a state of perpetual crisis (The Trauma Bond). If you allow 2:00 AM "emergency" texts, you are enabling the crisis cycle rather than teaching self-regulation.

  • Policy: "I respond to messages within 24 business hours."
  • Policy: "Sessions end exactly at the 50-minute mark."

2. Financial Boundaries

Survivors may struggle with financial abuse. However, sliding scales or "pro-bono" work must be structured. Inconsistent billing creates a "special treatment" dynamic that can trigger the client's abandonment fears if it ever has to change.

3. Emotional/Physical Boundaries

No physical contact (if in-person) beyond a professional handshake. No "dual relationships"‚Äîyou cannot be a client's coach and their business partner, friend, or romantic interest. This is a hard boundary in the AccrediPro framework.

Self-Care: Avoiding Secondary Trauma

Working with survivors means witnessing extreme human cruelty. This can lead to Vicarious Trauma‚Äîa shift in the coach‚Äôs worldview‚Äîor Secondary Traumatic Stress (STS), which mimics C-PTSD symptoms.

A 2023 meta-analysis (n=8,234) of trauma workers found that 26.4% met the criteria for Secondary Traumatic Stress. To protect yourself, you must implement the "Coach's Shield":

  • Metabolic Reset: Physical activity after a heavy session to "burn off" the cortisol spike.
  • Cognitive Offloading: Using supervision or peer-mentoring to process difficult cases.
  • Environmental Anchoring: Having a dedicated coaching space that you "leave" physically and mentally at the end of the day.

Unless you have a pre-arranged emergency protocol, the ethical response is to wait until your designated business hours. This reinforces the client's agency and prevents the coach from becoming a "crutch" that replaces the client's internal coping mechanisms.

What is the primary difference between Counter-Transference and Empathy?

Empathy is feeling with the client while maintaining your own identity. Counter-transference is projecting your own past emotions or needs onto the client, often resulting in biased advice or emotional reactivity.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethics provide the "safety container" necessary for the Navigate phase of recovery.
  • Counter-transference is an occupational hazard for survivor-coaches; supervision is the antidote.
  • Mandatory reporting overrides confidentiality in cases of harm to self, others, or children.
  • Professional boundaries (Time, Money, Access) prevent coach burnout and client dependency.
  • Secondary Trauma is a biological reality; self-care is a professional requirement, not a luxury.

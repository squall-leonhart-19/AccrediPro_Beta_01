2. The Vagus Nerve: The Soul's Information Highway

The Vagus nerve (the 10th cranial nerve) is the primary component of the parasympathetic nervous system. It regulates heart rate, digestion, and respiratory rate. In survivors of Narcissistic Victim Syndrome (NVS), the "vagal tone" is often low, meaning the body is inefficient at "braking" the stress response.

This physically resets the alignment of the atlas and axis vertebrae, signaling safety to the brainstem.

3. Identifying & Managing Somatic Flashbacks

A cognitive flashback is a memory of an event. A somatic flashback is the body re-experiencing the physiological state of the trauma without a clear mental image. A client might suddenly feel a "knot in the stomach," a "tightening of the throat," or a "cold chill" that seems disconnected from their current safe environment.

4. Re-inhabiting the Body: Overcoming Dissociation

Chronic hyper-vigilance causes the survivor to "live in their head." They become expert observers of the narcissist's moods but lose all connection to their own internal signals (interoception). Re-inhabiting the body is a core component of the "C: Claim Your Power" phase.

Practitioners must guide clients through Titrationâ€”the process of experiencing small amounts of bodily sensation at a time so as not to overwhelm the nervous system. If a client has been dissociated for years, feeling "everything" at once can trigger a massive sympathetic spike.

Check Your Understanding

Test your mastery of somatic resilience concepts.

1. A client reports feeling "numb, cold, and unable to make decisions" after a hoovering attempt. Which Polyvagal state are they likely in, and what is the primary coaching goal?

They are in a Dorsal Vagal (Shutdown) state. The goal is NOT "calming down" (they are already too "low"), but rather gentle activation. Use sensory input, light movement, or cold water on the face to "thaw" the freeze response.

2. Why is "Resonant Frequency Breathing" (5.5 breaths/min) preferred over rapid deep breathing for trauma survivors?

Rapid deep breathing can mimic hyperventilation and trigger a sympathetic "fight/flight" response. Resonant breathing maximizes Heart Rate Variability (HRV) and synchronizes the heart, lungs, and brain into a state of coherence and safety.

1. The Mechanics of Triangulation

At its core, triangulation is a manipulation tactic where one person (the narcissist) uses a third person to control the dynamics of a relationship. In a healthy relationship, conflict is resolved directly between two people. In a narcissistic system, the narcissist introduces a third partyâ€”either physically or through hearsayâ€”to create a sense of competition, insecurity, and urgency.

Triangulation serves three primary psychological functions for the narcissist:

  • Stabilization: The narcissist feels "wobbly" in their ego and brings in a third person to bolster their position.
  • Deflection: By shifting focus to a third party, the narcissist avoids accountability for their own behavior.
  • Validation: The narcissist recruits a "witness" to agree that the survivor is the problem.

The N.A.R.C. Insight

Triangulation is often subtle. It may sound like: "My ex never complained about my long hours," or "Everyone at the office thinks you're being a bit sensitive lately." The goal is to make the survivor feel they are "losing" a competition they didn't know they were in.

3. The Anatomy of a Smear Campaign

A smear campaign is a pre-emptive strike. Narcissists are acutely aware that their mask will eventually slip. To protect their "Golden Image," they begin destroying the survivorâ€™s reputation long before the relationship ends. This ensures that when the survivor finally speaks out, they have already been framed as "crazy," "unstable," or "abusive."

The Strategic Phases of a Smear Campaign:

  1. The "Concerned" Phase: The narcissist tells friends, "I'm so worried about [Survivor's] mental health lately. They're acting so erratic." This builds a foundation of false empathy.
  2. The "Victim" Phase: The narcissist shares exaggerated or fabricated stories of the survivor's "abuse" toward them.
  3. The "Isolation" Phase: The narcissist tells the survivor that "Everyone thinks you've changed," while telling everyone else that the survivor wants to be left alone.

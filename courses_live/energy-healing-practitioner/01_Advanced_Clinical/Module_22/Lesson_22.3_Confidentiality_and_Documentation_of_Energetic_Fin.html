3. The Ethics of Intuition: Sharing vs. Silence

Before sharing an intuitive finding, apply the Three-Filter Rule:

  • Is it helpful? Will sharing this empower the client (E) or merely satisfy the practitioner's ego?
  • Is it within scope? Does the insight lead toward a medical diagnosis (forbidden) or toward energetic alignment?

4. Anonymization Protocols for Case Studies

Contributing to the body of evidence for energy medicine is vital. However, when using a client's story for a case study or research, simple name changes are insufficient. In the digital age, "jigsaw re-identification" allows individuals to be identified through a combination of unique symptoms, location, and history.

The AccrediPro De-identification Standard:

  1. Remove all 18 HIPAA identifiers (Name, specific dates, VINs, etc.).
  2. Generalize the location (e.g., "A client in the Pacific Northwest" instead of "Seattle").
  3. Alter non-essential biographical details (e.g., change a 42-year-old architect to a 45-year-old engineer).
  4. Focus the narrative on the energetic mechanics of the B.A.L.A.N.C.E. Protocolâ„¢ rather than the client's personal drama.

5. Mandatory Reporting vs. Confidentiality

Confidentiality is a cornerstone of the practitioner-client relationship, but it is not absolute. Legal mandates override energetic ethics in specific scenarios. You must be clear on your local jurisdiction's requirements for Mandatory Reporting.

Generally, confidentiality must be breached if:

  • There is a credible threat of the client harming themselves (suicidal ideation with plan/intent).
  • There is a credible threat of the client harming a specific, identifiable other person.
  • There is suspicion of abuse or neglect of a child, elder, or dependent adult.

Effective documentation serves three purposes: it tracks client progress through the B.A.L.A.N.C.E. Protocolâ„¢, it provides a legal defense in case of disputes, and it facilitates professional hand-offs if the client sees another provider.

Standardized notes should follow the SOAP format, adapted for Energy Medicine:

  • Subjective: What the client reports (e.g., "Feeling ungrounded").

In cases of suspected abuse of a minor or vulnerable adult, or when there is a credible threat of the client harming themselves or others.

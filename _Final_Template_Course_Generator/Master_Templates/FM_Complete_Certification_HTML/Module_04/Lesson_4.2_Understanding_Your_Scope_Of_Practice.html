What is Scope of Practice?

Scope of practice defines the services, procedures, and activities that a professional is authorized to perform based on their training, certification, and applicable laws. It's the boundary between what you're qualified to do and what requires different credentials‚Äîlike medical licensure, dietitian registration, or mental health licensure.

For health coaches, scope of practice is determined by multiple intersecting factors:

1

Your Training & Certification

What you've actually learned through your coaching education and are qualified to practice based on competencies demonstrated.

2

State Laws

Some states have specific regulations for health coaches or "practice of medicine" laws that define what only licensed professionals can do.

3

Certification Body Standards

Organizations like NBHWC (National Board for Health & Wellness Coaching) define scope through their standards of practice.

4

Employment Context

If you work within a healthcare organization, hospital, or clinic, they may have additional institutional guidelines.

5

Professional Ethics

Even if something is technically legal, it may not be ethical to practice beyond your actual competence and training.

Important

Scope of practice isn't optional guidance‚Äîit's a legal and ethical requirement. Practicing outside your scope can result in liability lawsuits, loss of certification, criminal charges (in some jurisdictions), and most importantly, harm to clients.

What Health Coaches CAN Do

Health coaching has a robust, meaningful scope when practiced appropriately. Here's what's clearly within your professional boundaries:

‚úÖ WITHIN SCOPE: What You CAN Do

Health & Lifestyle Assessment

‚Ä¢ Gather comprehensive health history information
‚Ä¢ Assess lifestyle factors (diet, sleep, stress, movement, relationships)
‚Ä¢ Use validated questionnaires and assessment tools
‚Ä¢ Identify patterns and connections in health information

Coaching & Behavior Change Support

‚Ä¢ Use evidence-based coaching techniques (motivational interviewing, stages of change, goal setting)
‚Ä¢ Help clients clarify their values and health vision
‚Ä¢ Support clients in developing and implementing action plans
‚Ä¢ Provide accountability and follow-up

Health Education

‚Ä¢ Share general health and wellness information
‚Ä¢ Explain how lifestyle factors affect health
‚Ä¢ Provide resources and information for client learning
‚Ä¢ Discuss research and evidence (without making medical claims)

Lifestyle Recommendations

‚Ä¢ Suggest dietary changes for general wellness (not to treat disease)
‚Ä¢ Recommend sleep hygiene practices
‚Ä¢ Discuss stress management strategies
‚Ä¢ Encourage appropriate movement and exercise

Coordination & Referral

‚Ä¢ Recognize when clients need medical attention and refer appropriately
‚Ä¢ Coordinate with healthcare providers (with client consent)
‚Ä¢ Support clients in communicating with their healthcare team
‚Ä¢ Facilitate continuity of care

Coach Tip

Frame your scope positively. Instead of focusing on what you "can't" do, emphasize the powerful tools you can use: "I can't diagnose thyroid conditions, but I can help you optimize the lifestyle factors that support thyroid function‚Äîsleep, stress management, nutrient-dense eating, and toxin reduction. Let's work on what's in our control while your doctor manages the medical piece."

What Health Coaches CANNOT Do

Equally important‚Äîand often more challenging in practice‚Äîis understanding what falls outside your scope. These activities require professional licensure or credentials you don't hold:

‚õî OUTSIDE SCOPE: What You CANNOT Do

Diagnosis

‚Ä¢ Diagnose any disease, condition, or disorder
‚Ä¢ Tell clients they "have" or "probably have" a condition
‚Ä¢ Use diagnostic labels (even informally)
‚Ä¢ Interpret labs to make or rule out diagnoses

Prescribing & Treatment

‚Ä¢ Prescribe medications or treatments
‚Ä¢ Advise clients to stop, change, or start medications
‚Ä¢ Prescribe diets to treat specific diseases
‚Ä¢ Recommend specific supplement doses to treat conditions

Medical Testing

‚Ä¢ Order medical tests or laboratory work
‚Ä¢ Interpret lab results to diagnose conditions
‚Ä¢ Perform physical examinations
‚Ä¢ Use diagnostic equipment

Mental Health Treatment

‚Ä¢ Provide psychotherapy or counseling for mental health disorders
‚Ä¢ Diagnose mental health conditions
‚Ä¢ Treat eating disorders, trauma, or addiction independently
‚Ä¢ Prescribe or adjust psychiatric medications

Medical Nutrition Therapy

‚Ä¢ Provide nutrition therapy for specific diseases (e.g., renal diet for kidney disease)
‚Ä¢ Create therapeutic meal plans for medical conditions (reserved for RDs in many states)
‚Ä¢ Calculate tube feeding formulas or manage enteral nutrition

Red Flag Phrases

If you catch yourself saying any of these, stop immediately: "You have...", "I think it's...", "You should stop taking...", "This will cure...", "Your labs show..." These phrases indicate you've crossed into diagnosis or treatment territory.

The Language of Scope

Often, scope isn't just about what you do but how you communicate it. The right language keeps you professionally safe and ethically sound; the wrong language crosses dangerous lines. Let's look at precise comparisons:

‚ùå Out of Scope
  • "You have hypothyroidism."
  • "You should take 5,000 IU of vitamin D daily."
  • "You can stop taking that medication now."
  • "This supplement will cure your inflammation."
  • "Your labs show you're insulin resistant."
  • "Follow this diet to treat your IBS."
‚úÖ Within Scope
  • "Your symptoms are consistent with patterns I've seen in thyroid issues‚Äîyour doctor can evaluate this."
  • "Research suggests many people benefit from vitamin D. Your doctor can test your levels and advise on dosing."
  • "Any medication changes should be discussed with your prescribing doctor."
  • "Some people find this supplement helpful for general wellness. Here's what the research shows."
  • "I notice some of your values‚Äîhave you discussed these patterns with your doctor?"
  • "Many people with digestive concerns find this dietary approach supportive. Let's experiment and see how you respond."
Coach Tip

Use conditional, educational language: Replace certainty with possibility. Instead of "You need...", say "Many people find..." or "Research suggests..." Instead of diagnosing, say "This pattern might be worth discussing with your doctor." This language is both more accurate (you're not omniscient) and legally safer.

Navigating the Gray Areas

In practice, you'll regularly encounter situations that aren't clearly black or white. A client mentions fatigue‚Äîis that in scope to address through lifestyle, or does it require medical evaluation? They ask about probiotics‚Äîcan you recommend one, or is that prescribing? Here's your decision-making framework:

üîç When You're Unsure, Ask Yourself:

1. Am I diagnosing or treating disease?

If yes, you're out of scope. Reframe to education and lifestyle support instead.

2. Does this require specific licensure?

Medical diagnoses, prescribing, psychotherapy, and medical nutrition therapy all require professional licenses you don't hold.

3. Would I be comfortable if a physician were watching?

If you'd be embarrassed, defensive, or nervous about another healthcare professional observing your practice, reconsider your approach.

4. Could this cause harm if I'm wrong?

When health consequences are potentially serious, always err on the side of referral to licensed professionals.

5. Am I acting in the client's best interest or my ego's?

It feels good to be "the expert" with all the answers. But client safety and appropriate care trump your desire to be helpful.

6. Do I have adequate training for this?

Scope includes competence. Even if something is technically allowed, if you haven't been trained in it, don't practice it.

Communicating Your Role to Clients

Setting clear expectations from the very first session prevents scope confusion and protects both you and your clients. Here's how to establish those boundaries effectively:

üí¨

Scripts for Scope Communication

Language That Sets Clear Expectations

In Your Initial Session:

"As a health coach, I'm here to support you in making lifestyle changes that optimize your health and help you reach your wellness goals. I want to be clear about what I do and don't do: I'm not a doctor, so I don't diagnose medical conditions, prescribe treatments, or give medical advice. What I do is help you implement sustainable changes in nutrition, stress management, sleep, movement, and other lifestyle factors. I work alongside your healthcare providers‚Äînot instead of them‚Äîto support your overall care. Does that make sense?"

When Clients Ask Medical Questions:

"That's a great question, and it's one for your doctor since it involves [diagnosis/medication/medical treatment]. What I can help with is [reframe to lifestyle factor]. Would you like to discuss that instead, or would you like support in preparing to bring this question to your next doctor's appointment?"

When Clients Want Lab Interpretation:

"I can see why these results are confusing. While I can't interpret labs for diagnosis‚Äîthat's your doctor's role‚ÄîI can help you understand what questions to ask your doctor and what lifestyle factors might support the areas you're concerned about. Would that be helpful?"

Coach Tip

Make scope part of your intake paperwork. Include a clear statement in your client agreement that defines your role, explains what health coaching is and isn't, and outlines the collaborative relationship with their healthcare team. Having it in writing protects you legally and sets expectations before conversations even begin.

Success Story

One coach reported: "Once I started clearly communicating my scope in the first session, clients stopped asking me to diagnose or prescribe. They understood my role was different‚Äîand actually more valuable in some ways because I had time to really dig into lifestyle factors their doctors couldn't address in 15-minute appointments. Setting boundaries increased my credibility, not decreased it."

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

A client brings you recent lab work showing elevated fasting glucose and asks if they have diabetes. How do you respond while staying within scope?

Answer: Stay within scope by saying: "I can see why these numbers concern you. I can't diagnose diabetes‚Äîthat's your doctor's role‚Äîbut I can help you understand what questions to ask them and what lifestyle factors support healthy blood sugar. Have you scheduled a follow-up appointment to discuss these results with your doctor?"

Why this works: You acknowledge their concern, clearly state you can't diagnose, redirect to appropriate professional, and offer support within your scope (lifestyle education and doctor communication support).

2

Which of the following is WITHIN a health coach's scope of practice?

Answer: Within scope activities include:

  • ‚úÖ Educating clients about how sleep affects metabolism
  • ‚úÖ Supporting clients in developing meal plans for general wellness
  • ‚úÖ Coordinating with a client's physician (with consent)
  • ‚úÖ Using motivational interviewing to support behavior change

Out of scope: Interpreting labs to diagnose conditions, prescribing supplements to treat disease, creating medical nutrition therapy meal plans for renal disease, providing psychotherapy for depression.

3

A client has lost 25 pounds and asks if they can stop taking their blood pressure medication. What's the appropriate response?

Answer: "That's wonderful progress! Any changes to your medication need to be discussed with the doctor who prescribed it. They can evaluate your blood pressure and make adjustments safely. I'd encourage you to bring this up at your next appointment‚Äîand I'm happy to help you prepare for that conversation if you'd like."

Why this matters: Even if the client's blood pressure has improved, stopping medication without medical supervision can be dangerous. Medication adjustments are always outside a coach's scope, regardless of how successful lifestyle changes have been.

4

What are the five determining factors of a health coach's scope of practice?

Answer: The five factors are:

  1. Your training and certification ‚Äî what you've actually learned and demonstrated competency in
  2. State laws ‚Äî regulations specific to health coaching or practice of medicine laws
  3. Certification body standards ‚Äî guidelines from NBHWC, AADP, and other certifying organizations
  4. Your employment context ‚Äî institutional policies if you work in healthcare settings
  5. Professional ethics ‚Äî practicing only within your competence, even if technically legal

All five factors must align for an activity to be within your scope.

Key Takeaways
  • Scope of practice defines what you're authorized to do based on training, certification, and law‚Äîit's legally and ethically binding
  • Health coaches CAN: assess lifestyle, coach behavior change, educate about wellness, recommend lifestyle modifications, coordinate with providers, and refer appropriately
  • Health coaches CANNOT: diagnose diseases, prescribe treatments or medications, order medical tests, provide medical nutrition therapy, or treat mental health disorders
  • Language matters profoundly‚Äîhow you communicate can mean the difference between staying in scope and crossing dangerous boundaries
  • When uncertain, use the six-question framework: diagnosis/treatment? licensure required? comfortable if observed? potential harm? ego vs. client interest? adequate training?
  • Set clear expectations in your first session and in writing‚Äîboundaries increase credibility rather than diminish it
  • When in doubt, refer. Client safety always comes first, and knowing when you're not the right professional is a mark of competence, not weakness

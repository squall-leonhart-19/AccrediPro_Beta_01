Connection to Your Practice: Clients often ask about supplements for energy. This lesson helps you navigate the landscape of mitochondrial support supplements‚Äîunderstanding which have solid evidence, how they work, when they're most appropriate, and how to discuss them within your scope of practice.

The supplement industry is flooded with products promising "more energy" and "cellular vitality." Some deliver on their promises with solid science behind them. Others are expensive marketing hype. As a functional medicine health coach, you need to help clients navigate this complex landscape with evidence-based guidance while staying within your professional boundaries.

Case Study: Patricia's Energy Breakthrough

From Persistent Fatigue to Renewed Vitality

üë©

Patricia, Age 55

Persistent fatigue ‚Ä¢ Taking CoQ10 for several months ‚Ä¢ Skeptical about supplements

"I've been taking CoQ10 for a few months and I honestly feel like I have more energy," says Patricia. "Is that real, or am I imagining it? And are there other things I should be taking?"

Patricia's experience is likely genuine‚ÄîCoQ10 is one of the most evidence-based mitochondrial supplements, and her age (when natural CoQ10 production declines) makes her an ideal candidate for benefit. Understanding which supplements genuinely support cellular energy, and which are more hype than substance, helps you provide informed guidance while respecting the primary role of diet and lifestyle.

Before diving into specific supplements, let's establish the foundation that makes supplementation effective‚Äîand avoid the trap of thinking pills can replace fundamental health practices.

The Supplement Hierarchy: Foundation First

In functional medicine, we follow a principle that's often overlooked in conventional supplement marketing: supplements can't replace fundamentals. No amount of CoQ10 or NAD+ precursors will compensate for inadequate sleep, chronic stress, sedentary lifestyle, or poor dietary choices.

Supplements work best when the following foundation is in place:

1

Nutrient-Dense Diet

The majority of nutrients should come from whole, unprocessed foods that provide cofactors and phytonutrients pills can't replicate.

2

Sleep Optimization

7-9 hours of quality sleep allows mitochondrial repair and regeneration‚Äîno supplement can replace this.

3

Regular Movement

Physical activity is the most powerful stimulus for mitochondrial biogenesis‚Äîcreating new, healthy mitochondria.

4

Stress Management

Chronic stress depletes nutrients and damages mitochondria faster than supplements can repair them.

Coach Tip

When clients ask about energy supplements, start with: "Before we talk about what you can add, let's look at what might be depleting your energy. Are you getting 7-8 hours of sleep? How's your stress level? When did you last take a vacation or have true downtime?" This reframes the conversation from quick fixes to sustainable solutions.

With that foundation, supplements can fill nutritional gaps and provide therapeutic support that may be difficult to achieve through food alone. Now let's explore which supplements have the strongest evidence for mitochondrial support.

Foundational Mitochondrial Supplements

These supplements address the most common deficiencies affecting energy production. They represent the "first tier" of support‚Äîbacked by substantial research and often addressing widespread nutritional gaps.

Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10/Ubiquinone)

CoQ10: The Electron Transport Chain Essential

‚ö° What It Does

CoQ10 is an essential electron carrier in the mitochondrial electron transport chain. Without adequate CoQ10, electrons can't move efficiently through the chain, and ATP production cannot proceed optimally. It also functions as a powerful antioxidant, protecting mitochondrial membranes from oxidative damage.

üìâ Natural Production Decline

The body synthesizes CoQ10, but production peaks around age 20 and declines significantly thereafter. By age 80, tissue levels may be 50% lower than peak levels. This age-related decline correlates with declining energy and increased oxidative stress.

üíä Depletion Factors

Statin medications block HMG-CoA reductase‚Äîthe same enzyme pathway that produces CoQ10. This is why statin users often experience muscle pain and fatigue. Aging, chronic disease, and high oxidative stress also deplete CoQ10 stores.

üî¨ Evidence Base

Strong evidence supports CoQ10 for heart failure, migraine prevention, and statin-induced myopathy. Growing evidence shows benefits for general fatigue, cognitive function, exercise performance, and healthy aging.

Ubiquinone (Oxidized Form)
  • More stable in supplements
  • Less expensive
  • Body must convert to ubiquinol
  • Good for younger adults
Ubiquinol (Reduced Form)
  • Active form‚Äîready to use
  • Better absorbed by older adults
  • Preferred if over 40
  • More expensive but often worth it

Typical dosing: 100-300 mg daily. Take with fat-containing meals for optimal absorption (CoQ10 is fat-soluble). Those on statins may benefit from higher doses (200-300 mg).

Coach Tip

For clients over 40 or those on statins, frame CoQ10 this way: "Think of CoQ10 like spark plugs in your cellular engines. As we age, or when certain medications deplete it, we need to replace what's missing. Most clients notice improvements in energy within 4-8 weeks of consistent use."

B Vitamins: The Energy Vitamin Complex

B vitamins are absolutely critical for mitochondrial function. They serve as cofactors for dozens of enzymatic reactions in energy metabolism. Deficiency in even one B vitamin can create an energetic bottleneck.

üîã B Vitamins and Mitochondrial Function

B1 (Thiamine)

Essential cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase complex‚Äîthe gateway enzyme that allows pyruvate to enter the Krebs cycle. Without adequate B1, glucose cannot be efficiently converted to energy.

B2 (Riboflavin)

Required for FAD production (flavin adenine dinucleotide), a crucial electron carrier in the electron transport chain. B2 deficiency directly impairs ATP production.

B3 (Niacin)

Precursor to NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide)‚Äîperhaps the most important molecule in cellular metabolism. NAD+ is required for glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain.

B5 (Pantothenic Acid)

Required for Coenzyme A synthesis. CoA is essential for the Krebs cycle‚Äîwithout it, the cycle cannot function and energy production grinds to a halt.

B6 (Pyridoxine)

Cofactor in amino acid metabolism and neurotransmitter synthesis. Critical for converting food into usable energy and supporting nervous system function.

B12 (Cobalamin)

Essential for methylation reactions, red blood cell formation, and neurological function. B12 deficiency causes profound fatigue, cognitive impairment, and neuropathy.

Important Consideration

B vitamin deficiency is extremely common, particularly B12. Vegetarians and vegans are at high risk for B12 deficiency (it's only found in animal products). Those taking metformin (diabetes medication) or acid-blocking medications (PPIs, H2 blockers) also commonly develop B12 deficiency. Older adults often have reduced absorption of B vitamins.

Methylated forms: For individuals with MTHFR gene polymorphisms (affecting approximately 40% of the population), methylated B vitamins‚Äîmethylfolate (B9) and methylcobalamin (B12)‚Äîmay be more effective since they bypass the impaired methylation pathway.

Typical dosing: A high-quality B-complex providing 50-100 mg of most B vitamins, with higher B12 (500-1000 mcg) if deficient. Take in the morning‚ÄîB vitamins can be energizing.

Magnesium: The Energy Mineral

Magnesium might be the most underappreciated nutrient for energy production. It's required for over 300 enzymatic reactions, many directly involving ATP metabolism.

Why Magnesium Is Non-Negotiable for Energy

üîã ATP Activation

ATP doesn't exist in a free form‚Äîit exists as Mg-ATP complex. Without magnesium, ATP cannot be utilized by cells. This makes magnesium absolutely essential for every energy-requiring process in the body.

üìâ Widespread Deficiency

Up to 50% of Americans may have suboptimal magnesium status. Modern agriculture depletes soil magnesium. Processed foods are stripped of magnesium. Chronic stress depletes magnesium stores. Yet conventional medicine rarely tests for it.

‚ö° Multiple Roles

Magnesium is required for glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, the electron transport chain, DNA and RNA synthesis, protein synthesis, muscle and nerve function, blood pressure regulation, and blood glucose control.

üí§ Sleep Connection

Magnesium supports GABA activity (the calming neurotransmitter) and regulates melatonin. Many people with insomnia improve dramatically with magnesium supplementation‚Äîand better sleep means better energy.

Forms matter significantly:

Magnesium Glycinate
  • Chelated to amino acid glycine
  • Excellent absorption
  • Calming effect (glycine supports relaxation)
  • Ideal for sleep support
  • Gentle on digestion
Magnesium Malate
  • Bound to malic acid
  • Malic acid supports Krebs cycle
  • May be particularly helpful for fatigue
  • Often used for fibromyalgia
  • Can be energizing

Other useful forms: Magnesium citrate (well-absorbed, mild laxative effect‚Äîuseful if constipated), magnesium threonate (crosses blood-brain barrier, may support cognition).

Forms to avoid: Magnesium oxide (poorly absorbed‚Äîonly about 4% bioavailability).

Typical dosing: 200-400 mg elemental magnesium daily. Start low and increase gradually to assess tolerance. Higher doses may be needed if deficient, but work up slowly to avoid loose stools.

Coach Tip

Magnesium is one of the safest, most effective supplements for energy and sleep. Try suggesting: "Let's start with magnesium glycinate at bedtime‚Äî200 mg to start. It supports energy production, helps muscles relax, and most clients notice improved sleep quality within a week. Better sleep means better energy during the day."

Iron: Essential but Proceed with Caution

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide and a major cause of fatigue, particularly in menstruating women. Yet iron supplementation must be approached carefully.

Critical Warning

Only supplement iron if deficiency is confirmed by testing. Iron overload is dangerous‚Äîexcess iron generates oxidative stress, damages tissues, and increases risk of heart disease and cancer. Never guess with iron supplementation.

Why iron matters for energy:

  • Required for hemoglobin synthesis‚Äîtransporting oxygen to tissues
  • Essential component of cytochromes in the electron transport chain
  • Cofactor for numerous metabolic enzymes
  • Ferritin (iron storage protein) below 50-70 ng/mL often associated with fatigue symptoms, even if hemoglobin is "normal"

Best form: Iron bisglycinate (chelated iron) is typically best tolerated with fewer digestive side effects than ferrous sulfate.

Typical dosing: Varies based on degree of deficiency‚Äîoften 25-50 mg elemental iron daily. Take with vitamin C for enhanced absorption. Avoid taking with calcium, coffee, or tea which inhibit absorption.

Advanced Mitochondrial Support

Once foundational nutrients are optimized, these advanced supplements target specific aspects of mitochondrial function. They're appropriate for clients who have addressed basics but still need additional support‚Äîor for those with higher demands (athletes, high-stress professions, chronic illness recovery).

NAD+ Precursors: NR and NMN

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is arguably the most important molecule in cellular metabolism. It's required for glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, the electron transport chain, DNA repair, and sirtuin activation (longevity pathways).

The NAD+ Decline and Restoration

üìâ Age-Related Decline

NAD+ levels decline progressively with age‚Äîby age 50, levels may be 50% lower than youth. This decline is thought to contribute to aging-related energy decline, cognitive impairment, and metabolic dysfunction.

üíä NR (Nicotinamide Riboside)

A precursor that converts to NAD+ in cells. Research shows NR supplementation can effectively raise NAD+ levels. Well-studied with a good safety profile. Brand example: Tru Niagen.

üíä NMN (Nicotinamide Mononucleotide)

One biochemical step closer to NAD+ than NR. Growing research base showing it raises NAD+ levels. May have advantages for certain tissues. More research emerging on optimal dosing and timing.

üî¨ Evidence Status

Preclinical evidence (animal studies) is very promising for energy, metabolism, cognitive function, and longevity. Human research is still emerging but shows NAD+ levels do increase with supplementation. Long-term human outcome data still being gathered.

Typical dosing: NR: 250-500 mg daily; NMN: 250-500 mg daily. Some protocols use higher doses. Research on optimal dosing is still evolving.

When to consider: Clients over 40 experiencing energy decline despite optimized lifestyle and basic supplementation. Those recovering from chronic illness. High-performing individuals seeking optimization.

L-Carnitine: The Fat Transporter

L-Carnitine's primary role is transporting long-chain fatty acids across the mitochondrial membrane for beta-oxidation (fat burning). Without adequate carnitine, cells cannot efficiently burn fat for fuel‚Äîthey become dependent on glucose.

üî• Who Benefits Most from L-Carnitine

ü•ó Vegetarians & Vegans

Carnitine is synthesized in the body but primarily comes from animal foods (particularly red meat). Plant-based eaters often have lower carnitine levels and may benefit significantly from supplementation.

üë¥ Older Adults

Carnitine synthesis declines with age. Supplementation may help restore youthful fat-burning capacity and energy levels.

üò¥ Chronic Fatigue

Research shows carnitine supplementation can improve fatigue scores in chronic fatigue syndrome. It may help cells more efficiently utilize fat for sustained energy.

üí™ Athletes

Carnitine may improve exercise recovery, reduce muscle damage, and enhance fat oxidation during endurance activities. Particularly useful for low-carb or ketogenic athletes.

Forms of L-Carnitine:

  • L-Carnitine: Standard form for general energy and fat metabolism support
  • Acetyl-L-Carnitine (ALCAR): Crosses the blood-brain barrier more readily; may support cognitive function in addition to energy production
  • L-Carnitine L-Tartrate: Often used in sports nutrition for exercise performance and recovery

Typical dosing: 500-2000 mg daily, often divided into 2-3 doses. ALCAR: 500-1500 mg daily for cognitive support.

Coach Tip

For vegetarian or vegan clients struggling with energy despite good diet and lifestyle: "Your plant-based diet is providing incredible nutrition, but one nutrient that's harder to get without animal foods is L-carnitine‚Äîit helps your cells burn fat for energy. Many plant-based clients notice a real difference in sustained energy when they add this supplement."

Alpha-Lipoic Acid (ALA): The Universal Antioxidant

Alpha-lipoic acid plays multiple roles in mitochondrial health‚Äîas both a cofactor in energy production and as a powerful antioxidant protecting mitochondria from oxidative damage.

  • Krebs cycle cofactor: Required for pyruvate dehydrogenase and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase‚Äîkey entry points into the Krebs cycle
  • Universal antioxidant: Both fat and water soluble, allowing it to work throughout cells. Regenerates other antioxidants (vitamin C, vitamin E, glutathione)
  • Insulin sensitivity: May improve glucose uptake and insulin sensitivity‚Äîimportant for metabolic health
  • Neuroprotection: Crosses blood-brain barrier; may protect against cognitive decline

Typical dosing: 300-600 mg daily. Some protocols for diabetic neuropathy use higher doses (600-1200 mg). Take on empty stomach for best absorption.

PQQ: The Mitochondrial Biogenesis Stimulator

Pyrroloquinoline quinone (PQQ) offers a unique mechanism‚Äîrather than just supporting existing mitochondria, it may stimulate the creation of new mitochondria (mitochondrial biogenesis).

PQQ: Creating New Energy Factories

üÜï Unique Mechanism

PQQ activates PGC-1Œ±, the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. This means it may help create brand new mitochondria‚Äînot just support the ones you have.

üî¨ Evidence Base

Primarily preclinical (animal) studies showing increased mitochondrial number and density. Human studies suggest cognitive benefits, improved sleep quality, and enhanced energy. More human research needed.

ü§ù CoQ10 Synergy

Often combined with CoQ10 in formulas. The rationale: CoQ10 supports existing mitochondria while PQQ helps create new ones. Together they may provide comprehensive mitochondrial support.

üíä Dosing

Typical dosing: 10-20 mg daily. Often found in combination with CoQ10 (e.g., 100-200 mg CoQ10 + 10-20 mg PQQ).

D-Ribose: Rapid ATP Replenishment

D-ribose is a five-carbon sugar that forms the structural backbone of ATP, DNA, and RNA. It may help cells regenerate ATP faster after depletion.

  • Some evidence for chronic fatigue syndrome and fibromyalgia‚Äîconditions where ATP depletion may be a core issue
  • Often used by athletes for faster recovery between training sessions
  • May help heart muscle recover after ischemic events (reduced blood flow)
  • Typical dosing: 5 grams 2-3 times daily (15 grams total). Mix in water or beverage.
Caution for Diabetics

D-ribose is a sugar and can affect blood glucose. Those with diabetes should monitor blood sugar carefully and consult their physician before use.

When to Consider Supplements

Not everyone needs mitochondrial supplements. Strategic, personalized supplementation is most beneficial in specific situations:

Clinical Considerations for Supplementation

üß™ Testing Reveals Deficiencies

Nutrient testing (serum, RBC, or functional testing) identifies specific deficiencies. This removes guesswork and allows targeted supplementation.

ü•ó Dietary Restrictions

Vegetarians/vegans (B12, carnitine, iron, zinc), food allergies, elimination diets‚Äîall can create nutritional gaps supplements can fill.

üíä Medication-Induced Depletion

Statins deplete CoQ10. Metformin depletes B12. Acid blockers impair B12, iron, and magnesium absorption. Birth control pills may deplete B vitamins. Supplementation can prevent deficiency-related side effects.

ü©∫ Impaired Absorption

GI disorders (IBS, IBD, celiac), small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO), aging (reduced stomach acid, intrinsic factor)‚Äîall impair nutrient absorption.

üìà Increased Demands

Chronic stress, intense exercise, pregnancy/lactation, illness recovery, high-stress professions‚Äîall increase nutrient needs beyond what diet alone may provide.

‚úÖ Optimized Baseline

When diet, sleep, exercise, and stress management are already optimized but fatigue persists‚Äîthis is when strategic supplementation may provide the final piece of the energy puzzle.

Important: Rule Out Medical Causes First

If severe, persistent fatigue is present, ensure thyroid dysfunction, anemia, sleep apnea, depression, chronic infections, autoimmune disease, and other medical causes have been ruled out before focusing primarily on mitochondrial supplements. Supplements support health‚Äîthey don't replace proper medical evaluation.

Scope of Practice Considerations

As a functional medicine health coach, you have an important but bounded role in supplement guidance. Understanding these boundaries protects both you and your clients.

Critical Boundaries

Education vs. Prescription: You can educate clients about supplements‚Äîwhat they do, how they work, what the research shows. You cannot prescribe supplements or dose them for specific medical conditions. The line between education and prescription can be blurry‚Äîwhen in doubt, refer to the supervising practitioner.

1

Drug Interactions

Always encourage clients to inform their physicians and pharmacists about all supplements. Some interactions can be serious (e.g., St. John's Wort with medications, high-dose vitamin E with blood thinners).

2

Condition-Specific Treatment

Using supplements therapeutically to treat diagnosed diseases typically exceeds coaching scope. General wellness support and filling nutritional gaps is appropriate; treating heart failure with CoQ10 requires physician oversight.

3

Testing Requirements

If recommending supplements based on suspected deficiency, testing to confirm is optimal and often requires physician orders. You can suggest testing; you typically cannot order it yourself.

4

Quality Matters

Help clients choose quality brands with third-party testing (USP, NSF, ConsumerLab). The supplement industry is poorly regulated‚Äîquality varies enormously. Professional-grade brands are often worth the extra cost.

Coach Tip

Frame supplement discussions appropriately: "Based on what you've shared about your diet, medications, and symptoms, you might benefit from discussing CoQ10 with your doctor. Here's some research you can bring to that conversation. I can't prescribe it, but I can help you understand why it might be worth exploring." This respects boundaries while empowering clients.

A Practical Supplementation Framework

Clients can feel overwhelmed by supplement recommendations. This tiered approach provides a clear, evidence-based framework that prioritizes what matters most.

Three-Tier Supplement Strategy

Tier 1: Foundation (Address First)

These support basic nutritional needs and are appropriate for almost everyone:

  • High-quality multivitamin OR targeted B-complex
  • Magnesium (glycinate or malate)
  • Vitamin D (if deficient‚Äîtest to confirm and dose appropriately; most people need 2000-5000 IU daily)
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA from fish oil or algae; 1000-2000 mg combined EPA+DHA daily)

Tier 2: Mitochondrial Support (Add If Needed)

Once foundation is in place, add these for targeted energy support:

  • CoQ10 or Ubiquinol (especially if over 40 or on statins; 100-300 mg daily)
  • Iron (ONLY if deficiency confirmed by testing; 25-50 mg elemental iron as bisglycinate)

Tier 3: Advanced (For Specific Needs)

Add these for additional support when Tiers 1 & 2 are optimized but more support is needed:

  • NAD+ precursors (NR or NMN; 250-500 mg daily)
  • L-Carnitine or ALCAR (500-2000 mg daily; especially for vegetarians, older adults, athletes)
  • Alpha-lipoic acid (300-600 mg daily)
  • PQQ (10-20 mg daily; often combined with CoQ10)
  • D-Ribose (5 grams 2-3x daily; for chronic fatigue, fibromyalgia, or intense athletic recovery)
Start Simple, Layer Strategically

Don't overwhelm clients with a dozen supplements at once. Start with Tier 1‚Äîfoundation. Give it 4-6 weeks. Reassess. If energy improves, great. If not, consider Tier 2. Continue this methodical approach. This allows you to identify what actually helps versus what's unnecessary.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

Why is CoQ10 particularly important for people taking statin medications?

Answer: Statin medications block HMG-CoA reductase, the same enzyme pathway that produces CoQ10. This is why statin users often experience muscle pain and fatigue‚Äîtheir CoQ10 levels are depleted. Supplementation can prevent or reduce these side effects while still allowing the cardiovascular benefits of statins.

2

Explain why magnesium is called "the energy mineral" and describe which form might be best for someone experiencing both fatigue and poor sleep.

Answer: Magnesium is called the energy mineral because ATP exists as Mg-ATP complex‚Äîwithout magnesium, ATP cannot be utilized by cells. It's also required for 300+ enzymatic reactions in energy metabolism. For someone with both fatigue and poor sleep, magnesium glycinate would be ideal. The glycine component supports relaxation and sleep quality, while magnesium supports energy production. It's well-absorbed and gentle on digestion.

3

What's the key difference between NR/NMN (NAD+ precursors) and other mitochondrial supplements like CoQ10 or B vitamins?

Answer: NAD+ precursors aim to boost levels of NAD+, which declines significantly with age (potentially contributing to aging-related energy decline). The evidence base is still emerging for human benefits, whereas CoQ10 and B vitamins have decades of research showing they fill actual nutritional deficiencies and support established metabolic pathways. NAD+ precursors are more "cutting edge" and may be particularly relevant for age-related energy decline.

4

Why should iron supplementation only occur after confirmed deficiency through testing, while magnesium or B vitamins can be supplemented more liberally?

Answer: Iron overload is dangerous. Excess iron generates oxidative stress, damages tissues, and increases risk of heart disease, liver disease, and cancer. The body has no efficient way to excrete excess iron, so it accumulates. In contrast, B vitamins and magnesium are water-soluble or have wide safety margins‚Äîexcess is typically excreted, making deficiency far more likely than toxicity. Iron must be supplemented only when true deficiency is confirmed.

Key Takeaways
  • Foundation first: Supplements support but cannot replace diet, sleep, exercise, and stress management
  • CoQ10 is the gold standard mitochondrial supplement‚Äîespecially valuable with aging or statin use; ubiquinol form often better for those over 40
  • B vitamins and magnesium: Common deficiencies that directly impair energy production; nearly everyone benefits from optimization
  • Iron: Only supplement if deficiency confirmed‚Äîiron overload is harmful
  • NAD+ precursors (NR/NMN): Emerging area with potential for age-related energy decline, but research still developing
  • Tiered approach: Start with foundational nutrients, layer in mitochondrial support, add advanced options only if needed
  • Test don't guess: Whenever possible, confirm deficiencies with testing rather than blind supplementation
  • Quality matters: Choose third-party tested supplements from reputable brands‚Äîprofessional grade when possible
  • Know your scope: Educate and support within appropriate boundaries; refer to physicians for prescription-level recommendations

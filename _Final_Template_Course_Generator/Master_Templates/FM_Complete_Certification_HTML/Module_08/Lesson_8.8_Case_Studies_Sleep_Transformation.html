Case Study 2: The Conditioned Insomniac

üìã

Michael, Age 42

Eight Years of Insomnia and Dread

üë®

Michael, Software Developer

Works from home ‚Ä¢ 8 years of insomnia ‚Ä¢ Multiple failed medication trials

Chief Complaint: "I've been an insomniac for 8 years. I dread going to bed because I know I'll just lie there for hours."

History: Insomnia began after a stressful work period. Has tried multiple sleep medications with diminishing returns. Now takes trazodone nightly but still struggling.

Current Habits: Goes to bed at 9 PM hoping to maximize sleep time, lies in bed until 7 AM. Uses bedroom as home office. Reads news on phone in bed to "tire himself out."

Assessment Findings

Sleep Pattern Analysis

‚è±Ô∏è Sleep Metrics

Time in bed: 10 hours (9 PM - 7 AM)
Sleep onset latency: 2-3 hours
Estimated sleep: 4-5 hours
Sleep efficiency: ~45%

üò∞ Psychological Impact

Multiple awakenings with difficulty returning to sleep
Extreme anxiety about sleep
Rates "dread of bedtime" 9/10

Root Causes Identified
  1. Conditioned arousal: Bed associated with frustration and wakefulness (8 years of conditioning)
  2. Excessive time in bed: Diluting sleep, perpetuating low efficiency
  3. Stimulus control violation: Works in bedroom, uses phone in bed
  4. Sleep anxiety: Catastrophic thoughts about consequences of not sleeping
  5. Sleep effort: Trying too hard to sleep, creating arousal
Coach Tip

Chronic insomnia often becomes a behavioral and psychological problem even after the original trigger resolves. Michael's case shows classic conditioned arousal‚Äîthe bed itself triggers wakefulness. This requires CBT-I approaches (stimulus control, sleep restriction, cognitive restructuring), not supplements or sleep hygiene alone.

Protocol: Modified CBT-I Approach

1

Stimulus Control (Weeks 1-2)

Move work setup out of bedroom (dining table became office) ‚Ä¢ Bed for sleep and intimacy only‚Äîno phone, no reading in bed ‚Ä¢ If awake more than 20 minutes, get up and go to another room ‚Ä¢ Return to bed only when sleepy ‚Ä¢ No clock-watching (turn clocks away)

2

Sleep Restriction (Weeks 3-6)

Initial sleep window: 12:30 AM - 6:30 AM (6 hours, based on estimated sleep time + buffer) ‚Ä¢ Fixed wake time at 6:30 AM regardless of sleep quality ‚Ä¢ No napping ‚Ä¢ Expand by 15 minutes when sleep efficiency reaches 85%+ for 5 days

3

Cognitive Restructuring (Ongoing)

Identify catastrophic thoughts: "I'll never sleep" ‚Üí "I've slept before and will again" ‚Ä¢ "Tomorrow will be ruined" ‚Üí "I've functioned on less sleep; one night won't destroy me" ‚Ä¢ Shift from controlling sleep to allowing sleep ‚Ä¢ Scheduled "worry time" at 8 PM (not bedtime)

Coach Tip

Sleep restriction is counterintuitive but powerful. Warn clients that the first 1-2 weeks will be challenging‚Äîthey'll be tired. But this builds genuine sleep pressure and breaks the bed-wakefulness association. Support them through this difficult phase and celebrate small wins (falling asleep faster, less anxiety about bed).

Results: Michael's Progress

Week 1-2

Challenging. Very tired during the day. But falling asleep faster because genuinely exhausted by 12:30 AM. Started to break bed-wakefulness association.

Week 4

Sleep efficiency reached 85%. Expanded sleep window to 12:15 AM - 6:30 AM. Anxiety about bed decreasing. "I actually feel sleepy when I go to bed now."

Week 8

Sleep window 11:30 PM - 6:30 AM (7 hours). Sleeping 6-6.5 hours most nights. Sleep efficiency 88%. Began tapering trazodone with doctor support.

Week 12

Off trazodone. Sleep window 11 PM - 6:30 AM. No longer dreads bedtime. "I had forgotten what normal sleep felt like."

Key Lessons from Michael's Case
  • 8 years of conditioned insomnia required behavioral intervention, not supplements
  • Excessive time in bed was perpetuating the problem
  • Sleep restriction is powerful but requires commitment through initial discomfort
  • Cognitive work was essential‚Äîchanging relationship with sleep
  • Medication taper was possible once natural sleep ability was restored

Case Study 3: The Blood Sugar Roller Coaster

üìã

Diana, Age 55

The 3 AM Wake-Up with Heart Pounding

üë©

Diana, Elementary School Teacher

Post-menopausal ‚Ä¢ Pre-diabetic (HbA1c 5.9%) ‚Ä¢ Takes lisinopril for blood pressure

Chief Complaint: "I wake up every night between 2-3 AM with my heart pounding and feeling anxious. Sometimes I'm sweating. It takes an hour or more to calm down."

History: Problem started about 2 years ago. No hot flashes otherwise (post-menopausal).

Current Habits: Late dinner (7:30-8 PM), often pasta or rice-based. Glass of wine with dinner. Occasional cookie or ice cream after. Usually asleep by 10 PM.

Assessment Findings

Pattern Recognition
  • Waking consistently at 2-3 AM (4-5 hours post-dinner)
  • Symptoms: racing heart, anxiety, sometimes sweating
  • Worse on nights with higher carb dinner + wine + dessert
  • Better on nights with protein-heavy dinner, no dessert
  • Pre-diabetic status indicates poor glucose regulation
Clinical Hypothesis

Reactive Hypoglycemia: High-carb dinner (especially with wine) ‚Üí blood sugar spike ‚Üí insulin surge ‚Üí blood sugar crash at 2-3 AM ‚Üí body releases cortisol and adrenaline to raise glucose ‚Üí sudden awakening with anxiety, racing heart, sweating

Coach Tip

Timing is a critical clue. When clients wake at the same time every night (especially 3-5 hours after dinner), consider blood sugar dysregulation. The stress hormone surge that counteracts low blood sugar creates the exact symptoms Diana experienced‚Äîanxiety, racing heart, sweating. This is often misdiagnosed as anxiety or menopause.

Protocol: Blood Sugar Stabilization Focus

üçΩÔ∏è Diana's Dietary Protocol

Dinner Modifications

Move dinner earlier (6-6:30 PM when possible) ‚Ä¢ Reduce refined carbohydrates; emphasize protein, vegetables, healthy fats ‚Ä¢ If having carbs, choose lower glycemic options with fiber ‚Ä¢ Eliminate or significantly reduce wine (alcohol worsens blood sugar instability) ‚Ä¢ No dessert‚Äîor if desired, small portion with protein (e.g., cheese with a few berries)

Optional Bedtime Snack

Small protein-fat snack 30 minutes before bed to stabilize overnight glucose ‚Ä¢ Options: handful of nuts, cheese and crackers, Greek yogurt, nut butter

Track and Correlate

Food diary noting dinner composition, timing, alcohol ‚Ä¢ Sleep diary noting waking times, symptoms ‚Ä¢ Look for patterns connecting food choices to sleep quality

Results: Diana's Progress

Week 1

On nights with modified dinners (lower carb, earlier timing, no wine), waking reduced significantly. Pattern became undeniable.

Week 3

Sleeping through most nights. Occasional waking on "cheat" nights (pasta + wine) confirmed the connection.

Week 6 Outcome

Consistent sleep. Added magnesium glycinate for additional blood sugar and relaxation support. Lost 4 pounds as a bonus. "I never would have connected my diet to my 3 AM waking."

Key Lessons from Diana's Case
  • Blood sugar dysregulation is an underappreciated cause of middle-of-night waking
  • Pattern recognition (timing of waking, associated symptoms) pointed to the cause
  • Dietary modification was the primary intervention‚Äîno supplements needed initially
  • Pre-diabetic status was a clue to impaired glucose regulation
  • Tracking correlation between food and sleep was powerful for client insight

Your Sleep Assessment Framework

Use this comprehensive framework when working with sleep clients to ensure you don't miss contributing factors:

üîç Comprehensive Sleep Assessment

1. Sleep Pattern Mapping

What time do they go to bed and wake up (weekdays/weekends)? ‚Ä¢ How long to fall asleep? ‚Ä¢ How many times do they wake? For how long? ‚Ä¢ What's their total sleep time vs. time in bed? ‚Ä¢ Calculate sleep efficiency

2. Root Cause Exploration

Hormonal: Menstrual cycle phase? Perimenopausal symptoms? Thyroid issues? ‚Ä¢ Dietary: Caffeine (timing/amount)? Alcohol? Dinner composition and timing? ‚Ä¢ Behavioral: Screen use? Bedroom environment? Schedule consistency? ‚Ä¢ Psychological: Stress levels? Anxiety about sleep? Racing thoughts? ‚Ä¢ Medical: Pain? Medications? Snoring/apnea symptoms?

3. Pattern Recognition

When did the problem start? What was happening then? ‚Ä¢ What makes it better or worse? ‚Ä¢ Any patterns (time of night, day of week, menstrual cycle)?

4. Protocol Development

Prioritize highest-impact, most achievable changes first ‚Ä¢ Phase implementation to track what helps ‚Ä¢ Match intervention to root cause (don't give CBT-I to someone with blood sugar issues) ‚Ä¢ Include client in decision-making for better adherence

Coach Tip

Start broad, then narrow. Your initial assessment should cast a wide net across all potential contributing factors. As patterns emerge (like Diana's consistent 3 AM waking or Jennifer's correlation with night sweats), you can focus interventions more precisely. Don't jump to conclusions based on the first piece of information‚Äîthorough assessment prevents wasted time on ineffective interventions.

When to Refer

üö® Red Flags Requiring Medical Referral

Refer to medical providers or sleep specialists when you identify:

  • Sleep apnea symptoms: Loud snoring, witnessed breathing pauses, gasping, excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep time
  • Restless leg syndrome: Uncomfortable urge to move legs, worse at rest/evening
  • Parasomnias: Sleepwalking, acting out dreams, confusional arousals
  • Narcolepsy symptoms: Sudden sleep attacks, cataplexy, sleep paralysis
  • Severe, treatment-resistant insomnia unresponsive to behavioral approaches
  • Mental health crises: Severe depression, suicidal ideation
  • Medication concerns: Need for sleep medication management

As a health coach, you can address many sleep issues through lifestyle, behavioral, and nutritional interventions. However, some conditions require medical diagnosis and treatment. Knowing when to refer is a critical part of ethical, effective practice.

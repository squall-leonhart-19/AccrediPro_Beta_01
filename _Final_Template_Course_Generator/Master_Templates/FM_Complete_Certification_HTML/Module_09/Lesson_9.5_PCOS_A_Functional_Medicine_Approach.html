What Is PCOS?

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a common endocrine disorder characterized by a combination of irregular or absent ovulation, high androgens (testosterone, DHEA), and metabolic dysfunction‚Äîmost commonly insulin resistance.

Despite its name, PCOS isn't primarily about ovarian "cysts." The polycystic appearance on ultrasound is actually multiple immature follicles that haven't ovulated. And you can have PCOS without this appearance on imaging.

Important Distinction

PCOS is fundamentally a metabolic disorder with reproductive manifestations‚Äînot simply a reproductive problem. This understanding is crucial for effective treatment.

Diagnostic Criteria (Rotterdam Criteria)

PCOS is diagnosed when 2 of 3 criteria are present, after ruling out other conditions like thyroid disorders or hyperprolactinemia:

Criterion 1

Irregular/Absent Ovulation

Cycles longer than 35 days, fewer than 8 periods per year, or no ovulation confirmed by testing

Criterion 2

Hyperandrogenism

Clinical signs (acne, hirsutism, hair loss) OR elevated testosterone/DHEA on lab tests

Criterion 3

Polycystic Ovaries

12+ follicles in one ovary or increased ovarian volume on ultrasound (NOT required for diagnosis)

The Four PCOS Phenotypes

Not all PCOS is the same. Understanding the different phenotypes helps tailor treatment approaches to each individual:

A

Full-Blown (Classic) PCOS

Criteria met: All three (anovulation + hyperandrogenism + polycystic ovaries)

Characteristics: Most metabolically severe. Highest insulin resistance, highest androgen levels. Weight gain common. Highest long-term health risks.

Approach: Aggressive focus on insulin sensitivity, anti-inflammatory diet, significant lifestyle modification.

B

Classic Non-PCO PCOS

Criteria met: Anovulation + hyperandrogenism (no polycystic ovaries)

Characteristics: Similar to Type A but without ultrasound findings. Still significant metabolic and hormonal issues.

Approach: Same as Type A‚Äîfocus on insulin resistance and androgen reduction.

C

Ovulatory PCOS

Criteria met: Hyperandrogenism + polycystic ovaries (regular ovulation)

Characteristics: May have regular cycles but still has androgen symptoms (acne, hirsutism). Less metabolic dysfunction. Better fertility outcomes.

Approach: Focus on androgen reduction. Still address insulin if elevated.

D

Non-Hyperandrogenic PCOS

Criteria met: Anovulation + polycystic ovaries (no high androgens)

Characteristics: Mildest form. Irregular cycles but no acne/hirsutism. Lower metabolic risk. May resolve with stress/weight management.

Approach: May respond well to lifestyle intervention alone. Address underlying drivers (stress, inflammation).

Coach Tip

Help clients identify which phenotype best describes their presentation. This personalizes the approach: "Your PCOS shows up mainly as irregular cycles without severe androgen symptoms, which means you may see results more quickly with focused lifestyle changes around stress and inflammation."

The Insulin-PCOS Connection

Insulin resistance is the primary driver for 70-80% of women with PCOS. Understanding this connection is essential for effective intervention.

The Insulin-PCOS Cascade

Insulin Resistance ‚Üí Elevated Insulin ‚Üí Ovaries Produce More Androgens ‚Üí Impaired Ovulation ‚Üí PCOS Symptoms

Additionally, high insulin increases free testosterone by reducing sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), which normally keeps testosterone bound and inactive.

Signs of Insulin Resistance in PCOS

üîç Clinical Signs to Recognize

Weight Changes

Weight gain, especially around the abdomen, or difficulty losing weight despite healthy eating and exercise

Energy Patterns

Intense carbohydrate cravings, energy crashes after meals, afternoon fatigue

Skin Changes

Acanthosis nigricans (dark, velvety patches on neck, armpits, groin), skin tags

Lab Markers

Elevated fasting insulin (even with normal glucose), elevated HbA1c, abnormal glucose tolerance test

Critical Lab Insight

Fasting glucose and HbA1c can be normal while fasting insulin is elevated. This means someone is insulin resistant but compensating‚Äîtheir pancreas is working overtime. Always request fasting insulin along with glucose for a complete metabolic picture. Optimal fasting insulin is <7 ¬µIU/mL; levels above 10-12 indicate insulin resistance.

Other PCOS Drivers

While insulin resistance is primary for most, other factors contribute to PCOS:

Contributing Factors Beyond Insulin

üî• Chronic Inflammation

Low-grade inflammation is common in PCOS and contributes to insulin resistance and metabolic dysfunction. CRP and other inflammatory markers are often elevated.

üò∞ Adrenal Androgen Excess

Some women have elevated DHEA-S from adrenal sources rather than ovarian. Often related to chronic stress and HPA axis dysfunction.

ü¶† Gut Dysbiosis

Altered gut microbiome is increasingly recognized in PCOS, affecting inflammation, estrogen metabolism, and potentially insulin sensitivity.

üß¨ Genetic Predisposition

PCOS tends to run in families. Genetic factors interact with diet, lifestyle, and environment to express the condition.

üíä Post-Pill PCOS

Some women develop PCOS-like symptoms after stopping hormonal birth control. This may be temporary androgen surge or revelation of underlying PCOS that was masked.

Dietary Approaches for PCOS

Lifestyle modification is first-line treatment for PCOS according to international guidelines. Diet is central to managing insulin resistance and reducing inflammation.

Core Dietary Principles

1

Prioritize Protein

Aim for 25-30g protein at every meal to stabilize blood sugar, support satiety, and reduce carbohydrate cravings. Sources: eggs, fish, poultry, grass-fed meat, legumes, Greek yogurt.

2

Maximize Fiber

Target 25-35g/day from vegetables, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains. Fiber slows glucose absorption, supports gut health, and binds excess estrogen for elimination.

3

Low Glycemic Approach

Choose carbohydrates that don't spike blood sugar. Favor non-starchy vegetables, legumes, berries, and small portions of whole grains. Avoid refined carbs and added sugars.

4

Healthy Anti-Inflammatory Fats

Include olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish (omega-3s). Fat doesn't spike insulin and provides satiety. Avoid inflammatory seed oils (corn, soybean, canola).

5

Colorful Vegetables

Fill half your plate with vegetables at each meal. Their phytonutrients, antioxidants, and fiber reduce inflammation and support detoxification.

6

Foods to Minimize

Sugar and refined carbs, processed foods, inflammatory oils, excessive dairy (for some), alcohol. These worsen insulin resistance and inflammation.

Specific Dietary Approaches with Evidence

Low Glycemic Index Diet

Research shows low-GI diets improve insulin sensitivity, reduce androgens, and improve menstrual regularity in PCOS. Focus on minimally processed carbohydrates paired with protein and fat.

Mediterranean Diet

Anti-inflammatory pattern emphasizing vegetables, fish, olive oil, nuts. Studies show improvements in insulin resistance, inflammation markers, and weight in women with PCOS.

Lower Carbohydrate Approach

Reducing carbohydrates (not necessarily ketogenic) can significantly improve insulin sensitivity and weight. Typical guidance: 75-150g carbs/day for PCOS, individualized based on response.

Coach Tip

Rather than prescribing a rigid diet, help clients experiment: "Let's start by building balanced plates‚Äîhalf vegetables, quarter protein, quarter smart carbs, and healthy fat. We'll track how you feel after meals and adjust from there. Your body will tell us what's working." This empowers clients to become their own nutrition detectives.

Exercise for PCOS

Exercise is powerful medicine for PCOS‚Äîimproving insulin sensitivity, reducing androgens, and supporting healthy weight management. The key is finding the right balance.

üèÉ‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Exercise Guidelines for PCOS

Resistance/Strength Training

2-3x/week: Builds muscle, which improves insulin sensitivity even at rest. May be the most beneficial exercise type for PCOS. Focus on compound movements.

Moderate Cardio

3-5x/week: Walking, cycling, swimming for 30-60 minutes. Improves cardiovascular health and insulin sensitivity without excessive cortisol response.

HIIT (High-Intensity)

1-2x/week: Can be particularly effective for insulin sensitivity. Keep sessions short (15-20 minutes) to avoid cortisol spike. Not suitable for everyone.

Mind-Body Movement

Yoga, Pilates, tai chi support stress management while building strength. Excellent additions to support HPA axis balance.

Avoid Overtraining

Excessive exercise can increase cortisol, which worsens insulin resistance and may increase adrenal androgen production. Balance intensity with adequate recovery. If a client is exercising intensely 6-7 days/week without results, less may be more.

Stress Management & Supplements

The Stress-PCOS Connection

High cortisol from chronic stress worsens insulin resistance and can contribute to androgen excess, especially elevated DHEA-S. Stress management is essential, not optional:

  • Daily relaxation practices: Meditation, deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation (10-20 minutes daily)
  • Adequate sleep: 7-9 hours nightly in a dark, cool room. Poor sleep worsens insulin resistance
  • Boundaries: Workload management, saying no, protecting personal time
  • Adaptogenic herbs: Ashwagandha, rhodiola may support HPA axis (under practitioner guidance)

Evidence-Based Supplements for PCOS

üíä

Inositol (Myo + D-Chiro)

First-line supplement for PCOS. Improves insulin sensitivity, reduces androgens, supports ovulation. 40:1 ratio of myo:d-chiro mimics physiological ratio. Typical dose: 2-4g myo-inositol/day. Strongest evidence base.

üíä

Berberine

Comparable to metformin for insulin sensitivity. 500mg 2-3x/day with meals. Can lower blood sugar‚Äîuse with caution in diabetics on medication. Cycle on/off; not for continuous long-term use.

üíä

N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC)

Antioxidant that improves insulin sensitivity and may support ovulation. 600-1800mg/day. Often used alongside inositol for synergistic effects.

üíä

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Anti-inflammatory, may reduce androgens and improve lipid profiles. 2-4g EPA+DHA/day from high-quality fish oil. Choose brands tested for purity.

üíä

Vitamin D

Deficiency is common in PCOS and associated with worse metabolic outcomes. Test levels and optimize to 40-60 ng/mL. Typical dose: 2000-5000 IU/day based on levels.

üíä

Spearmint Tea

May help reduce androgens and hirsutism. Two cups per day showed benefit in studies. Simple, low-risk intervention with traditional use history.

Coach Tip

When discussing supplements, emphasize: "Supplements support the foundation we're building with diet, exercise, and stress management‚Äîthey don't replace it. Inositol has the strongest evidence for PCOS and is typically the first one to consider. Let's work with your healthcare provider to determine what's appropriate for you."

What PCOS Is NOT

Common misconceptions about PCOS can lead to inappropriate treatment and unnecessary discouragement. Let's set the record straight:

‚ùå PCOS Myths Debunked

Myth: You Can't Get Pregnant

Reality: Many women with PCOS conceive naturally or with minimal intervention once ovulation is restored through lifestyle changes or medication.

Myth: Weight Causes PCOS

Reality: While weight can worsen PCOS, lean women get PCOS too. Weight is often a consequence of insulin resistance, not the cause. About 30% of women with PCOS are lean.

Myth: It's Just Reproductive

Reality: PCOS is a metabolic disorder with lifelong implications for diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and mental health‚Äînot just a fertility issue.

Myth: Birth Control "Treats" PCOS

Reality: Pills manage symptoms but don't address underlying insulin resistance. Symptoms often return when stopping. Pills mask the problem, they don't fix it.

Myth: You Must Have Cysts

Reality: Polycystic ovaries aren't required for diagnosis. You can have PCOS without them, and you can have polycystic ovaries without PCOS.

Myth: Nothing Can Be Done

Reality: Lifestyle intervention has profound effects on PCOS. Many women see dramatic symptom improvement and restored ovulation with comprehensive lifestyle changes.

Long-Term Health Considerations

PCOS is a lifelong condition with health implications that extend far beyond reproductive years:

Increased Long-Term Health Risks
  • Type 2 diabetes: 50-70% lifetime risk (vs. 10% in general population)
  • Cardiovascular disease: Higher risk of heart disease, hypertension, and stroke
  • Endometrial cancer: Increased risk due to chronic anovulation (no progesterone to protect uterine lining)
  • Depression and anxiety: Significantly more common in women with PCOS
  • Sleep apnea: More prevalent, especially with insulin resistance and weight gain
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD): Higher prevalence due to insulin resistance
The Good News

Lifestyle intervention significantly reduces these risks. This is why health coaching is so valuable‚Äîthe changes that improve PCOS symptoms also protect long-term health. Women who manage their PCOS proactively can have excellent health outcomes.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

What are the three diagnostic criteria for PCOS according to Rotterdam guidelines, and how many must be present?

Answer: The three Rotterdam criteria are: (1) Irregular or absent ovulation, (2) Clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism, and (3) Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. Two of three criteria must be present for diagnosis (after ruling out other conditions like thyroid disorders).

2

Why is measuring fasting insulin important in PCOS, even when fasting glucose is normal?

Answer: Fasting glucose and HbA1c can be normal while fasting insulin is elevated. This indicates insulin resistance with compensation‚Äîthe pancreas is working overtime to maintain normal glucose levels. Elevated insulin drives androgen production in the ovaries, making it a critical marker for PCOS management. Optimal fasting insulin is <7 ¬µIU/mL.

3

What are the three core dietary principles for managing insulin-resistant PCOS?

Answer: The three core principles are: (1) Prioritize protein at every meal (25-30g) to stabilize blood sugar and support satiety, (2) Maximize fiber (25-35g/day) to slow glucose absorption and support gut health, and (3) Choose low-glycemic carbohydrates that don't spike blood sugar (non-starchy vegetables, legumes, berries, limited whole grains). Additionally, include anti-inflammatory healthy fats and minimize refined carbs and sugar.

4

What supplement has the strongest evidence base for improving PCOS outcomes, and what is the typical dosing?

Answer: Inositol (specifically a combination of myo-inositol and d-chiro-inositol in a 40:1 ratio) has the strongest evidence for PCOS. It improves insulin sensitivity, reduces androgens, and supports ovulation. Typical dose is 2-4g myo-inositol per day. Research shows significant improvements in metabolic parameters and reproductive outcomes.

The Digestive System: More Than a Tube

Before exploring the gut's far-reaching influence, let's establish a foundational understanding of what the digestive system does and how it works. This isn't just anatomy‚Äîunderstanding each stage helps you identify where dysfunction might be occurring for your clients.

The Digestive Journey: From Mouth to Elimination

üëÑ Mouth

Mechanical breakdown through chewing; salivary amylase begins carbohydrate digestion. Digestion starts here‚Äîchewing matters! Inadequate chewing is one of the most overlooked contributors to digestive distress.

üîª Esophagus

Muscular tube that transports food to the stomach via peristalsis (wave-like contractions). Problems here can manifest as difficulty swallowing or food "sticking."

ü´ô Stomach

Hydrochloric acid (HCl) kills pathogens and activates protein-digesting enzymes like pepsin. Food becomes chyme (liquid). Low stomach acid is a common hidden problem that causes heartburn, not high acid.

üåÄ Small Intestine

Where most digestion and absorption occurs (20+ feet long). Pancreatic enzymes and bile break down fats, proteins, and carbohydrates. Nutrients cross the intestinal lining into the bloodstream. This is where leaky gut develops when the lining becomes damaged.

ü¶† Large Intestine

Home to the majority of the microbiome. Absorbs water and electrolytes. Bacteria ferment fiber, producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) and vitamins. Forms and eliminates stool. Transit time matters‚Äî12-24 hours is ideal.

üè• Supporting Organs

Liver: Produces bile for fat digestion and detoxification. Gallbladder: Stores and releases bile. Pancreas: Produces digestive enzymes and bicarbonate to neutralize stomach acid.

The Gut's Many Roles

Digestion is just the beginning. The gut performs multiple critical functions that influence whole-body health. Understanding these roles helps you connect seemingly unrelated symptoms back to gut dysfunction.

Nutrient Extraction & Absorption

The gut breaks down food into absorbable nutrients‚Äîamino acids, fatty acids, glucose, vitamins, and minerals. Without proper digestion and absorption, even the perfect diet fails to nourish. "You are what you absorb" is more accurate than "you are what you eat."

Immune Defense (70-80%)

The gut contains the largest concentration of immune tissue in the body (GALT‚Äîgut-associated lymphoid tissue). It must distinguish between harmless food and dangerous pathogens. This balance, when disrupted, drives food sensitivities, allergies, autoimmunity, and chronic infections.

üß†

Neurotransmitter Production

The gut produces approximately 95% of serotonin (the "happiness" neurotransmitter) and 50% of dopamine (motivation and reward). It also produces GABA (calming), norepinephrine (alertness), and melatonin (sleep). The gut communicates with the brain via the vagus nerve.

‚öñÔ∏è

Hormone Metabolism

The gut metabolizes and recycles hormones, particularly estrogen via the "estrobolome"‚Äîthe collection of gut bacteria that regulate estrogen levels. Gut dysbiosis can lead to estrogen dominance or estrogen deficiency, affecting menstrual cycles, menopause, PMS, and PCOS.

üöø

Detoxification Support

The gut eliminates toxins through bile excretion and stool elimination. Constipation impairs this pathway, causing toxins to be reabsorbed (enterohepatic recirculation), increasing toxic burden throughout the body.

üî•

Inflammation Regulation

The gut lining serves as a selective barrier with tight junctions between cells. When compromised ("leaky gut"), it allows inflammatory molecules and undigested food particles into the bloodstream, triggering systemic inflammation linked to virtually every chronic disease.

The Functional Medicine Approach

Conventional medicine often treats gut symptoms with medications that suppress them‚Äîantacids for reflux, laxatives for constipation, anti-diarrheals for IBS, antibiotics for infections. While sometimes necessary, these approaches rarely address the underlying dysfunction.

Functional medicine asks why these symptoms are occurring and addresses root causes:

Conventional Approach
  • Heartburn: Suppress acid with antacids or PPIs
  • IBS: Manage symptoms with medications
  • Constipation: Use laxatives long-term
  • Focus: Symptom suppression
Functional Approach
  • Heartburn: Ask why‚Äîoften LOW stomach acid, not high. Address root cause
  • IBS: Investigate triggers‚ÄîSIBO, dysbiosis, food sensitivities, stress
  • Constipation: Address motility, hydration, fiber, magnesium, thyroid
  • Focus: Root cause resolution

This systematic approach addresses gut dysfunction at every level‚Äîfrom removing triggers to rebuilding healthy gut architecture. It's personalized to each client's unique situation and typically progresses through these phases over 3-6 months.

Check Your Understanding

Test Your Knowledge
1

Why might a client with anxiety and brain fog benefit from addressing their gut health?

Answer: The gut produces approximately 95% of the body's serotonin and 50% of dopamine‚Äîneurotransmitters critical for mood, motivation, and cognitive function. The gut communicates directly with the brain via the vagus nerve (the "gut-brain axis"). Additionally, gut inflammation triggers systemic inflammation that affects brain function, causing brain fog and mood disturbances. Intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") allows inflammatory molecules into the bloodstream that cross the blood-brain barrier. Healing the gut often dramatically improves mental health symptoms because the gut-brain connection is bidirectional and powerful. Many clients with anxiety and depression also have undiagnosed gut dysfunction.

2

What percentage of the immune system resides in the gut, and why does this matter for your coaching practice?

Answer: 70-80% of the immune system is located in the gut (GALT‚Äîgut-associated lymphoid tissue). This matters immensely because gut dysfunction directly impairs immune function, leading to increased susceptibility to infections, food sensitivities, seasonal allergies, and autoimmune conditions. The gut must constantly distinguish between food (safe) and pathogens (dangerous). When this discrimination breaks down due to intestinal permeability or dysbiosis, the immune system becomes hypervigilant, attacking harmless substances (allergies) or even the body's own tissues (autoimmunity). For coaches, this means you cannot effectively support a client's immune health without addressing gut health. Every client with recurring infections, allergies, or autoimmune conditions needs gut investigation.

3

How does constipation affect the body's detoxification capacity, and what symptoms might result?

Answer: The liver packages toxins into bile and sends them to the gut for elimination in stool. When someone is constipated (less than one bowel movement daily), these toxins sit in the colon too long and can be reabsorbed into the bloodstream through a process called enterohepatic recirculation. This dramatically increases toxic burden throughout the body. Symptoms of impaired detoxification due to constipation include: skin issues (acne, eczema, dull complexion), hormonal imbalances (especially estrogen dominance since estrogen is excreted via bile), fatigue and brain fog, headaches, body odor and bad breath, and worsening PMS symptoms. Regular daily bowel movements (ideally 1-3 times daily) are essential for proper detoxification. This is why addressing constipation is often the first step in any gut healing protocol.

4

Why is "you are what you absorb" more accurate than "you are what you eat," and how does this principle guide your coaching?

Answer: Even a perfect diet fails to nourish if the gut cannot properly digest and absorb nutrients. Multiple factors can impair absorption: low stomach acid (can't break down proteins or absorb B12, iron, calcium), insufficient digestive enzymes (incomplete breakdown of macronutrients), damaged intestinal lining (nutrients can't cross into bloodstream), dysbiosis (harmful bacteria compete for nutrients and damage gut lining), and inflammation (impairs nutrient transport). A client could eat organic, nutrient-dense foods but still develop deficiencies in iron, B12, vitamin D, magnesium, and amino acids if their gut isn't functioning properly. This principle guides coaching by emphasizing that gut health must be addressed before expecting results from dietary changes or supplements. It also explains why some clients don't improve despite "doing everything right"‚Äîtheir gut can't absorb the nutrients. Testing nutrient status and addressing gut function together provides the best outcomes.

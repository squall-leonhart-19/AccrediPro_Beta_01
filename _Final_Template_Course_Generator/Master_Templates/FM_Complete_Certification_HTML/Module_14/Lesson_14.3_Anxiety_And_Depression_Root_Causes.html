Case Study: Lisa's Mixed Anxiety-Depression

Uncovering the Physical Roots of Mental Symptoms

üë©

Lisa, Age 52

Mixed anxiety-depressive disorder ‚Ä¢ Three failed medication trials ‚Ä¢ Morning anxiety, afternoon depression

"I wake up anxious every morning, but by afternoon I'm exhausted and hopeless," Lisa described. She'd tried three different medications with limited success and significant side effects.

Through careful intake, her coach identified multiple contributing factors: severely low ferritin (14 ng/mL despite "normal" hemoglobin), undertreated hypothyroidism, perimenopause with significant hormone fluctuations, chronic insomnia, and daily consumption of refined carbohydrates causing blood sugar swings.

Working with her integrative physician, Lisa addressed her iron deficiency, optimized her thyroid medication, began hormone therapy, and gradually shifted to a blood sugar-stabilizing diet. Her coach supported sleep hygiene changes and introduced a morning stress-reduction routine.

After four months: Lisa's anxiety was 80% improved, her depression had lifted, and she felt like herself again. "I couldn't believe how much of my mental health was actually physical health," she said.

Lisa's story illustrates a fundamental truth of functional medicine: mental health symptoms often have physical roots. When we address these underlying imbalances, the mind follows the body toward healing.

Coach Tip

When clients express frustration with failed treatments, try this reframe: "Your body has been sending messages through anxiety and depression. We haven't failed‚Äîwe just haven't found the right language to understand what it's trying to tell us yet. Let's look at your whole system together."

Understanding Anxiety

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions, affecting approximately 40 million American adults. They include generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, social anxiety, and specific phobias.

While anxiety has psychological and situational components, the functional medicine approach recognizes that numerous physiological factors can trigger or worsen anxiety symptoms:

Blood Sugar Instability

Reactive hypoglycemia triggers adrenaline release, mimicking panic attacks with racing heart, sweating, and shakiness

Thyroid Dysfunction

Both hyper- and hypothyroidism can cause anxiety symptoms‚Äîracing thoughts, restlessness, insomnia

ü¶†

Gut Dysbiosis

Imbalanced bacteria reduce GABA production, increase inflammation, and disrupt the gut-brain axis

‚òï

Stimulant Excess

Caffeine, especially in slow metabolizers, overstimulates the nervous system and raises cortisol

‚ö°

Nutrient Deficiencies

Low magnesium, B6, iron, or zinc impair GABA and calming neurotransmitter pathways

üî•

Inflammation

Systemic inflammation activates brain immune cells (microglia) and stress pathways

üåô

Hormone Fluctuations

Perimenopause, PMS, and postpartum states increase anxiety through progesterone/GABA changes

üò¥

Sleep Deprivation

Even one night of poor sleep increases anxiety reactivity and reduces emotional regulation

The Blood Sugar-Anxiety Connection

This connection deserves special attention because it's so common and so treatable. When blood sugar drops (after a high-carb meal, skipped meals, or due to insulin resistance), the body releases adrenaline and cortisol to raise blood sugar. These stress hormones cause symptoms identical to anxiety:

Critical Recognition

Many people with "anxiety" are actually experiencing recurrent reactive hypoglycemia. Stabilizing blood sugar through regular protein-containing meals and reducing refined carbohydrates often dramatically reduces anxiety symptoms.

Hypoglycemia Symptoms
  • Racing heart
  • Sweating
  • Shakiness
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Irritability
  • Feeling of impending doom
Anxiety Attack Symptoms
  • Racing heart
  • Sweating
  • Trembling
  • Mental fog
  • Agitation
  • Sense of danger

Notice the overlap? The body's stress response to low blood sugar produces the exact same sensations as an anxiety attack.

Coach Tip

Ask anxious clients to track symptoms in relation to meals: "Can you notice if your anxiety is worse when you wake up, before meals, or a few hours after eating something sweet? This pattern often reveals blood sugar involvement." Then work together to establish regular protein-rich meals.

Understanding Depression

Depression affects over 16 million American adults annually. Symptoms include persistent sadness, loss of interest, fatigue, sleep disturbances, appetite changes, difficulty concentrating, and in severe cases, thoughts of death or suicide.

The functional medicine approach views depression not as simply "low serotonin" but as the brain's response to multiple stressors that overwhelm adaptive capacity:

Root Causes of Depression

üî• Inflammation

Chronic low-grade inflammation, autoimmune conditions, obesity, and infections activate inflammatory pathways that directly affect mood circuits

ü¶† Gut Dysfunction

Dysbiosis, leaky gut, and reduced serotonin precursor production (90% of serotonin made in gut)

üåô Hormonal Issues

Hypothyroidism, low testosterone, estrogen deficiency/fluctuation, cortisol dysregulation

üíä Nutrient Deficiencies

B12, folate, vitamin D, iron, zinc, and omega-3 fatty acid deficiencies

‚ö° Metabolic Dysfunction

Insulin resistance, mitochondrial dysfunction, methylation problems

üèÉ Lifestyle Factors

Sleep deprivation, sedentary behavior, social isolation, lack of purpose

‚ò†Ô∏è Environmental Toxins

Mold exposure, heavy metals, insufficient natural light exposure

Inflammation and Depression: The New Understanding

Research increasingly supports inflammation as a key driver of depression. This represents a paradigm shift from the traditional "chemical imbalance" model:

üî¨ Evidence for Inflammatory Depression

Biomarker Studies

People with depression have 30% higher levels of inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6)

Treatment Response

Anti-inflammatory medications sometimes improve depression symptoms

Comorbidity Patterns

Inflammatory conditions (autoimmune diseases, obesity, heart disease) increase depression risk 2-3 fold

Biochemical Mechanism

Inflammation diverts tryptophan away from serotonin production toward kynurenine pathway

Brain Impact

Inflammatory cytokines directly affect brain circuits involved in mood and motivation

Clinical Significance

This "inflammatory depression" phenotype may explain why some patients don't respond to traditional antidepressants‚Äîthey need anti-inflammatory interventions rather than (or in addition to) serotonin-focused treatments.

The Anxiety-Depression Overlap

Anxiety and depression frequently occur together‚Äîabout 60% of people with one condition also have the other. From a functional medicine perspective, this makes perfect sense because many root causes contribute to both:

Coach Tip

When clients have both anxiety and depression, frame it positively: "The good news is that anxiety and depression often share root causes. This means the same foundational changes‚Äîstabilizing blood sugar, reducing inflammation, improving sleep‚Äîoften improve both conditions simultaneously."

Shared Root Causes
  • HPA axis dysregulation: Chronic stress leads first to anxiety (elevated cortisol), then to depression (cortisol resistance/depletion)
  • Gut dysfunction: Affects production of both serotonin (depression) and GABA (anxiety)
  • Inflammation: Promotes both conditions through neuroinflammation and neurotransmitter changes
  • Blood sugar instability: Causes anxiety during crashes, fatigue between meals (mimicking depression)
  • Sleep deprivation: Increases both anxiety and depression risk through multiple pathways
  • Thyroid dysfunction: Causes anxiety (early stages or with hyperthyroid) and depression (hypothyroid)
  • Nutrient deficiencies: Same nutrients (B vitamins, iron, magnesium) affect both conditions

Nutritional Interventions

The functional medicine approach to mood disorders begins with food as medicine. Nutritional interventions have strong research support and often produce results comparable to pharmaceutical treatments‚Äîwithout side effects.

1

Anti-Inflammatory Diet

The SMILES trial showed that a Mediterranean-style diet significantly improved depression‚Äîone-third of participants achieved remission through dietary change alone. Key elements: abundant vegetables, olive oil, fatty fish, nuts, limited processed foods and sugar.

2

Blood Sugar Stabilization

Protein at every meal (20-30g), eliminate or reduce refined carbohydrates, include healthy fats, avoid skipping meals. This alone can dramatically reduce anxiety for many people.

3

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Meta-analyses support omega-3 supplementation (particularly EPA) for depression. Recommended: 2-4g of combined EPA/DHA daily, with EPA ratio higher than DHA for mood. Fatty fish 2-3 times weekly also beneficial.

4

Adequate Protein

Protein provides precursor amino acids for neurotransmitters (tryptophan for serotonin, tyrosine for dopamine). Aim for 0.8-1g per pound of ideal body weight, distributed across meals.

5

Reduce Caffeine

Caffeine can trigger or worsen anxiety, especially in slow metabolizers (CYP1A2 variants). It also raises cortisol and disrupts sleep. Consider elimination or significant reduction for anxious clients.

Lifestyle Interventions

While nutrition provides the biochemical building blocks for mood, lifestyle interventions activate the body's own healing mechanisms. The evidence for these strategies is remarkably strong:

üèÉ Exercise: The Most Powerful Intervention

Research Evidence

Meta-analyses show exercise is as effective as antidepressants for mild-moderate depression

Mechanisms

Increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), reduces inflammation, improves mitochondrial function

Immediate Benefits

Even a 10-minute walk improves mood; effects are immediate and cumulative

Recommendation

Aim for 150 minutes moderate or 75 minutes vigorous activity weekly; any movement counts

Coach Tip

For depressed clients who lack motivation to exercise, start impossibly small: "What if we just aimed for a 3-minute walk around the block? Not to get fit‚Äîjust to prove to your brain that you can move when it says you can't." Success builds motivation for the next step.

üí§

Sleep Optimization

Poor sleep is both a cause and consequence of mood disorders. Prioritize 7-9 hours, consistent sleep/wake times, dark cool bedroom, no screens before bed. Address sleep apnea if suspected.

‚òÄÔ∏è

Light Exposure

Morning light exposure (10-30 minutes) supports circadian rhythm and serotonin production. Light therapy boxes (10,000 lux) effective for seasonal and non-seasonal depression. Reduce evening light to support melatonin.

üßò

Stress Management

Regular practice of stress-reduction techniques: meditation, deep breathing, yoga, time in nature. Even 10-15 minutes daily makes a measurable difference. Mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) reduces depression relapse by 50%.

üë•

Social Connection

Isolation is both a symptom and driver of depression. Encourage regular meaningful social interaction, even when motivation is low. Support groups can be particularly helpful.

Supplement Considerations

Beyond correcting nutrient deficiencies, certain supplements have research support for anxiety and depression. These should always be discussed with healthcare providers:

For Anxiety
  • Magnesium glycinate (200-400mg)
  • L-theanine (100-200mg)
  • Ashwagandha (300-600mg)
  • Lavender oil/Silexan
  • GABA (limited brain penetration)
  • Passionflower
For Depression
  • SAMe (400-1600mg daily)
  • EPA-focused omega-3s
  • Curcumin (500-1000mg)
  • Saffron (30mg)
  • St. John's Wort (caution: interactions)
  • Probiotics (specific strains)
Critical Safety Warnings

St. John's Wort interacts with many medications including birth control, blood thinners, and antidepressants. SAMe can trigger mania in bipolar disorder. 5-HTP should not be combined with antidepressants (risk of serotonin syndrome). Always coordinate supplement use with prescribing providers. Supplements are adjuncts, not replacements for appropriate medical/psychological care.

Coaching Approach

When working with clients experiencing anxiety or depression, your role as a health coach is to support foundational lifestyle changes while ensuring appropriate professional mental health care:

Essential Coaching Strategies
  • Ensure appropriate professional support: Clients should have a mental health provider (therapist, psychiatrist) as primary support. Coaching is complementary, not primary treatment for mental health conditions.
  • Explore physical factors: Through intake, identify potential contributors (gut issues, thyroid symptoms, sleep problems, blood sugar patterns, nutrient-poor diet).
  • Encourage appropriate testing: Suggest clients ask their providers about comprehensive thyroid panel, vitamin D, B12, iron/ferritin, inflammatory markers (CRP).
  • Start with foundations: Blood sugar stabilization, sleep hygiene, and gentle movement are often the most impactful initial interventions.
  • Be patient and compassionate: Depression reduces motivation; anxiety creates resistance to change. Small steps with unconditional support are key.
  • Monitor for worsening: If symptoms worsen significantly or suicidal ideation emerges, encourage immediate contact with mental health provider. Have crisis resources available (988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline).
Pause & Reflect

Think about your own experience with mood fluctuations. What physical factors (sleep, food, exercise, light) have the biggest impact on your mental state? This awareness will help you connect authentically with clients experiencing mood challenges.

Key Takeaways
  • Anxiety has multiple physiological triggers including blood sugar instability, thyroid dysfunction, gut dysbiosis, and nutrient deficiencies
  • Depression is increasingly understood as an inflammatory condition, not simply "low serotonin"
  • Anxiety and depression share many common root causes and frequently occur together (60% comorbidity)
  • Anti-inflammatory diet, blood sugar stabilization, and omega-3 fatty acids have strong research support for mood disorders
  • Exercise is as effective as antidepressants for mild-moderate depression; sleep optimization, light exposure, and stress management are foundational
  • Certain supplements may help but require coordination with medical providers and awareness of interactions
  • Health coaches support lifestyle and nutritional factors while ensuring clients have appropriate mental health care
  • Always monitor for worsening symptoms and have crisis resources available
